       <header>
        <h1>
         <a href="http://www.neatorama.com/2013/01/23/Postal-Experiments/">
          Postal Experiments
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <footer class="byline">
        <a class="userlink author-name" href="http://www.neatorama.com/whois/miss-cellania/" itemprop="author" rel="author" title="Profile for Miss Cellania - Member Since Aug 4th, 2012">
         Miss Cellania

       <section class="postbody">
        <div class="postbody-text">
         <p class="author">
          The following is an article from
          <a href="http://www.improbable.com/" target="_blank">
           The Annals of Improbable Research
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p class="author">
          <em>
           by Jeff Van Bueren, San Francisco, California
          </em>
         </p>
         <p class="author" style="text-align: center;">
          <img src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788796-0.jpg" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" width="600"/>
          (Image credit: Flickr user
          <span class="name" id="yui_3_7_3_3_1358788354672_1186">
           <strong class="username">
           </strong>
           <span class="realname" id="yui_3_7_3_3_1358788354672_1185">
            <span class="photo_navi_contact" id="yui_3_7_3_3_1358788354672_1190">
             <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/91201697@N00/4198716319/" target="_blank">
              Matt Hintsa
             </a>
             )
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          Having long been genuine admirers of the United States Postal Service (USPS),        which gives amazingly reliable service especially compared with many other        countries, our team of investigators decided to test the delivery limits        of this immense system. We knew that an item, say, a saucepan, normally        would be in a package because of USPS concerns of entanglement in their        automated machinery. But what if the item were not wrapped? How patient        are postal employees? How honest? How sentimental? In short, how eccentric        a behavior on the part of the sender would still result in successful mail        delivery?
         </p>
         <h4>
          Testing the Limits
         </h4>
         <p>
          We sent a variety of unpackaged items to U.S. destinations, appropriately        stamped for weight and size, as well as a few items packaged as noted. We        sent items that loosely fit into the following general categories: valuable,        sentimental, unwieldy, pointless, potentially suspicious, and disgusting.        We discovered that although some items were never delivered, most of the        objects of even highly unusual form did get delivered, as long as the items        had a definitely ample value of stamps attached. The Postal Service appears        to be amazingly tolerant of the foibles of its public and seems occasionally        willing to relax specific postal regulations.
         </p>
         <h4>
          <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788796-1.jpg" style="float: right; margin:0 0 1em 1em;"/>
          Procedures
         </h4>
         <p>
          Our research staff began the project by obtaining and reviewing relevant        information on USPS regulations and discussing, in a limited and very hypothetical        manner, the planned project with USPS 800 number personnel. A group of mailable        objects was then assembled, stamped with abundant postage by weight and        size, and mailed at public postal collection boxes (when possible to cram        the object through the aperture) or at postal stations (if possible). A        card was strapped to the object with duct tape or stranded strapping tape,        and postage was affixed to the card, except as otherwise noted below.
         </p>
         <p>
          Senders and receivers were interchangeable; the mailings were double-masked        to conceal the identity of our mailing specialists, and gloves were used        to prepare the mailings (to avoid fingerprints). In no case was a return        address given -each object either went forward to its destination or was        lost to follow-up. An object was considered lost if it was not received        within the 180-day study parameter. All objects were sent first class using        five-digit ZIP codes to actual domestic addresses, and the number of days        to delivery were recorded (excluding postal holidays). The condition of        the object upon receipt was also recorded, if it had changed, as was any        unusual communication, verbal or written, from the postal carrier or counter        clerk.
         </p>
         <h4>
          Materials and Findings
         </h4>
         <p>
          The items we mailed fall into several broad classifications, which are        described here.
         </p>
         <a name="more">
         </a>
         <p>
          ***
         </p>
         <h5>
          <strong>
           VALUABLE ITEMS. These were items that seemed stealable or had some        apparent business worth.
          </strong>
         </h5>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Letter with stamp placed at top left corner (incorrect stamp location).
          </strong>
          Formal business-style letter, to formal business name, in high-quality envelope.        Days to delivery, 21. The stamp was crossed out by hand; the top right corner        of the envelope was stamped with the following: EVIDENCE POSTAGE WAS AFFIXED,        ONE RATE OK'D.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788796-2.jpg" style="float: left; margin:0 1em 1em 0;"/>
           $1 bill.
          </strong>
          Sealed in clear plastic, with label attached with address        and postage. Days to delivery, 6.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           $20 bill.
          </strong>
          Days to delivery, 4.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Football.
          </strong>
          Days to delivery, 6. Male postal carrier was talkative        and asked recipient about the scores of various current games. Carrier noted        that mail must be wrapped.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Pair of new, expensive tennis shoes.
          </strong>
          Strapped together with duct        tape. Days to delivery, 7. When shoes were picked up at station, laces were        tied tightly together with difficult-to-remove knot. Clerk noted that mail        must be wrapped.
         </p>
         <h5>
          <strong>
           SENTIMENTAL ITEMS
          </strong>
         </h5>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Rose.
          </strong>
          Postage and address were attached to a card that was tied        to the stem. Delivery at doorstep, 3 days, beat up but the rose bud was        still attached.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Wooden postcard.
          </strong>
          Dimension 4" x 6" x 3/8", showing a moose and mailed        from Maine. Postage used was 20 cents in spite of the added weight, because        investigators strongly expected successful delivery in this case. Days to        delivery, 6.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Molar tooth.
          </strong>
          Mailed in clear plastic box. Made a nice rattling sound.        Repackaged in padded mailer by unknown individual; the postage and address        had been transferred to the outside of the new packaging. A handwritten        note in a woman's writing inside read, "Please be advised that human remains        may not be transported through the mail, but we assumed this to be of sentimental        value, and made an exception in your case." Days to delivery, 14.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Sound-emitting toy.
          </strong>
          A monkey-in-box toy that, upon shaking, shouted,        "Let me out of here! Help! Let me out of here!" Addressed in big letters        to LITTLE JOHNNIE. Sound toy was equipped with a new battery. Delivery at        doorstep, 6 days.
         </p>
         <h5>
          <strong>
           UNWIELDY ITEMS.
          </strong>
          These were items that would be a challenge to handle.
         </h5>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788796-3.jpg" style="float: right; margin:0 0 1em 1em;"/>
           Hammer.
          </strong>
          Card was strapped to hammer handle; extra-large amount of postage        was attached. Never received.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Feather duster.
          </strong>
          The card with postage and address was attached by        wire to the handle. Days to notice of delivery, 6. Clerk at station commented        that mail must be wrapped.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Ski.
          </strong>
          A large amount of postage was affixed to a card that was attached        to the ski. The ski was slipped into a bin of postage that was being loaded        into a truck behind a station (a collaborating staff member created a verbal        disturbance up the street to momentarily distract postal workers' attention).        Notice of postage due received, 11 days. Upon pickup at the station, the        clerk and supervisor consulted a book of postage regulations together for        2 minutes and 40 seconds before deciding on additional postage fee to assess.        Clerk asked if mailing specialist knew how this had been mailed; our recipient        said she did not know. Clerk also noted that mail must be wrapped.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Nev
          </strong>
          <strong>
           <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788796-4.jpg" style="float: left; margin:0 1em 1em 0;"/>
          </strong>
          <strong>
           er-opened small bottle of spring water
          </strong>
          . We observed the street        corner box surreptitiously the following day upon mail collection. After        puzzling briefly over this item, the postal carrier removed the mailing        label and drank the contents of the bottle over the course of a few blocks        as he worked his route.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Helium balloon
          </strong>
          . The balloon was attached to a weight. The address        was written on the balloon with magic marker; no postage was affixed. Our        operative argued strongly that he should be charged a negative postage and        refunded the postal fees, because the transport airplane would actually        be lighter as a result of our postal item. This line of reasoning merely        received a laugh from the clerk. The balloon was refused; reasons given:        transportation of helium, not wrapped.
         </p>
         <h5>
          <strong>
           POINTLESS ITEMS.
          </strong>
          These were items that looked like a prank.
         </h5>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788945-0.jpg" style="float: right; margin:0 0 1em 1em;"/>
           Can of soup.
          </strong>
          Never received.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coconut.
          </strong>
          Fresh green coconut containing juice, mailed in Hawaii.        Delivery at doorstep, 10 days.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Brick.
          </strong>
          Mailed at street corner box with ample postage for weight.        Never received.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Lemon.
          </strong>
          Never received.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Small bag of kitty litter.
          </strong>
          Never received.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Bald tire.
          </strong>
          The card with postage was strapped to the tire. Refused        at station.
         </p>
         <h5>
          <strong>
           SUSPICIOUS ITEMS.
          </strong>
          For reasons given.
         </h5>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Sound-emitting toy.
          </strong>
          Same toy as under "Sentimental" above, wrapped        securely in brown paper. Never received.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Street sign.
          </strong>
          Conceivably a stolen item, or illegal possession. Notice        of attempted delivery received, 9 days. Handed over at station with comment        that mail must be wrapped.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Box of sand.
          </strong>
          Packaged in transparent plastic box to be visible to        postal employees. Sent to give an impression of potentially hiding something.        The plastic box had obviously been opened before delivery and then securely        taped shut again. Delivery without comment at doorstep, 7 days.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788945-1.jpg" style="float: left; margin:0 1em 1em 0;"/>
           Wrapped coconut.
          </strong>
          Wrapped in brown paper. Made ample sloshing sound,        and round shape seemed suspicious. Attempted mailing at station. Clerk requested        identification of object. When told it was a coconut, clerk informed our        mailing specialist that a certificate from the US Department of Agriculture        would be required before it could cross state lines. Not mailed.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Wrapped brick.
          </strong>
          Wrapped in brown paper; posted in street corner box        with same amount of postage as was strapped to unwrapped brick. Extreme        weight for size made package seem suspicious. Notice of attempted delivery        received, 16 days. Upon pickup at station, our mailing specialist received        a plastic bag containing broken and pulverized remnants of brick. Inside        was a small piece of paper with a number code on it. Our research indicates        that this was some type of US Drug Enforcement Agency release slip. The        clerk made our mailing specialist sign a form for receipt.
         </p>
         <h5>
          <strong>
           DISGUSTING ITEMS.
          </strong>
          These items were malicious, potentially infectious,        smelly, etc.&gt;
         </h5>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Deer tibia
          </strong>
          . Our mailing specialist received many strange looks from        both postal clerks and members of the public in line when he picked it up        at the station, 9 days. The clerk put on rubber gloves before handling the        bone, inquired if our researcher were a "cultist," and commented that mail        must be wrapped.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Large wheel of cheese.
          </strong>
          The cheese was already extremely ripe (rancid)        at the time of mailing. Mailed in cardboard box. The cheese had oiled its        way through the bottom of the cardboard box by the time of pickup, 8 days.        The box had been placed in a plastic bag.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Dead fish, old seaweed, etc.
          </strong>
          Mailed in cardboard box. Notice to        pick up at station, 7 days. The postal supervisor warned our mailing specialist        that he could be fined for mail service abuse, even as a recipient, should        this happen again.
         </p>
         <p>
          ***
         </p>
         <h4>
          <img alt="" src="http://uploads.neatorama.com/images/posts/672/57/57672/1358788945-2.jpg" style="float: right; margin:0 0 1em 1em;"/>
          Summary and Concluding Remarks
         </h4>
         <p>
          First, this experiment yielded a 64% delivery rate (18/28), an almost two-thirds        success rate. (For our purposes, "delivery" constituted some type of independent        handling by the USPS and subsequent contact regarding the object, regardless        of whether we got to see or keep the object or whether it arrived whole.)        This is astounding, considering the nature of some of the items sent. This        compares with a 0% rate of receipt of fully wrapped packages from certain        countries of the developing world, such as Peru, Turkey, and Egypt. Admittedly,        those were international mailings, and thus not totally comparable; nevertheless,        the disparity is striking.
         </p>
         <p>
          Second, the delivery involved the collusion of sequences of postal workers,        not simply lone operatives. The USPS appears to have some collective sense        of humor, and might in fact here be displaying the rudiments of organic        bureaucratic intelligence.
         </p>
         <p>
          Finally, our investigation team felt remorse for some of its experimental        efforts, most particularly the category "Disgusting," after the good faith        of the USPS in its delivery efforts. We sought out as many of the USPS employees        who had (involuntarily) been involved in the experiment as we could identify,        and gave them each a small box of chocolate.
         </p>
         <p>
          We, and all scientists, owe a debt of gratitude to these civil servants.        Without them, we would have had but little success in pushing the envelope.
         </p>

           <div class="comm-content">
            <div class="comm-body">
             His twin brother manages my nearest post office! The guy won't let you mail anything if it's not inside an official USPS box (which you have to buy there) and won't let you mail anything without a 'proper' return address, including your name, address, city, state, and ZIP. I normally exclude writing city and state anywhere, because there is already a unique ZIP for each city-state. I pointed this out to him and he said it's required because "the computers needed all that information". I told him the computers should (and can) get by with nothing more than an address and a ZIP, but it was like arguing with a wookiee turd.
