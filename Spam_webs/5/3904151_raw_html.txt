        <h1>
         How We Increased Conversion Rates 294% in 3 Weeks
        </h1>
       </a>
       <div class="info">
        <div class="left">
         <a class="author" href="/how-we-increased-conversion-rates-294-in-3-weeks/">
          <img alt="author" class="minigravatar" src="/content/images/2014/Jun/top-img-1.png"/>
          Jake Taylor
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="right">

          <i class="fa fa-clock-o">
          </i>

         </a>

       <h3 id="weknewwehadaconversionproblembutasimplefixsurprisedus">
        We knew we had a conversion problem but a simple fix surprised us
       </h3>
       <p>
        <img alt="postimage" src="/content/images/2014/Jun/top-img2.png"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        We work with an online retailer selling a range of beauty products, a notoriously aggressive category with relatively low conversion rates. The cost per acquisition was nearing $18 which together with low conversion rates led to a loss ($) for traffic coming in from PPC. The client was however "addicted" to the additional revenue and made up for the loss by supplementing it with profitable traffic from other channels. The Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) was opaque at best. I was worried that this was simply a waste of money. Why make a loss on a sale for the low probability of a future sale? Our past experience showed that only a tiny percentage of existing customers would place a subsequent order.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="Conversion" src="/content/images/2014/Jun/Conversion_rate_low-1.png"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Conversion rates were stuck around the 1.5% to 2% range.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Average conversion rates are around 2% for our industry.
       </p>
       <p>
        I designed 4 potential new product page templates with great calls to action (and most common low hanging fruit improvements). We then A/B (C/D) tested these new designs. To my shock the existing product page design far exceeded the tested pages (by as much as 50 to 70%). It was back to the drawing board.
       </p>
       <p>
        We had a look at the conversion funnel which discovered that the checkout sequence  was highly optimized. Roughly 75% of people who started the checkout process completed it. These numbers far exceed other online retailers that I have worked with.
       </p>
       <p>
        This was the first step in discovering the "blockage" in conversions. The process of elimination continued. If the majority of customers complete the checkout process successfully the problem must be in the steps before checkout? But its not the product page. What could it be?
       </p>
       <p>
        I then had an epiphany.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Give us a tweet:
         </strong>
         Sometimes the most obvious things are not obvious at all #Trunkery
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        I went back to basics.
       </p>
       <p>
        The site from our research looked to be already highly optimized. Only very small incremental improvements were possible after 3 weeks of work. By this point, I was getting very frustrated. Whats the problem with this damn site? Maybe ~2% is all it will ever achieve because of the industry average conversion rate?
       </p>
       <p>
        Then I showed a friend the site I was working now. It was on her laptop (so it wasn't cached).... We waited and waited and waited. It took literally 8 seconds to load the page. How could I not notice that?
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         We set up our CDN account and within literally 15 minutes of setting everything up there were noticeabe improvements.
        </strong>
        We opted for Akamai after researching some of the other providers such as MaxCDN, Fast.ly, AWS Cloudfront and Limelight Networks. All the other CDN's had their unique benefits but for us it was hard to beat the mammoth Akamai network that consists of thousands of servers worldwide.
       </p>
       <h3 id="thisonelittletechnicalchangeincreasedourconversionratesby294">
        This one little technical change increased our conversion rates by 294%
       </h3>
       <p>
        <img alt="Conversion Improvement" src="/content/images/2014/Jun/Conversion_rate_increase.png"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        It just goes to show the importance of site speed. It also shows that when looking to optimize a website - KISS! (Keep it simple stupid).
       </p>
       <p>
        This is just the beginning when it comes to increasing
        <strong>
         <em>
          performance
         </em>
        </strong>
        there is heaps of room for improvement. Minify CSS and Javascript, lowering image file size and evaluating if we actually need the numerous JS tracking scripts (are they actually hurting us instead of helping?).
       </p>
       <p>
        A few years ago Amazon announced that for every 100 millisecond decrease in site speed, they saw a 1% increase in sales. Shopzilla increased revenue 12% by lowering average page speed from 6 to 1.2 seconds. The proof is in the pudding. And what a delicious pudding it is, nothing is sweeter then achieving tangible conversion improvements.
       </p>
       <p>
        The other great thing we noticed is the cost of paid traffic started to reduce significantly.
        <strong>
         The faster the site the lower the cost of paid search traffic.
        </strong>
        This can have a fantastic effect on the profitability of an order, especially if its a low margin industry. It can also create a compelling competitive advantage, in terms of customer experience and by extracting the most bang out of your ad spend buck.
       </p>
       <p>
        We have proven in just 3 weeks that site speed is perhaps the most powerful conversion improvement trick possible. Improve site speed and you will increase conversions.
       </p>
       <h3 id="lessonslearnt">
        Lessons Learnt
       </h3>
       <ol>
        <li>
         Go back to basics
        </li>
        <li>
         Fast is better then slow
        </li>
        <li>
         Big isn't better then small (well, in terms of file size!).
        </li>
       </ol>
