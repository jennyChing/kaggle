     <p>
      A
      <b>
       jetboat
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">
       boat
      </a>
      propelled by a jet of water ejected from the back of the craft. Unlike a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powerboat" title="Powerboat">
       powerboat
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Motorboat" title="Motorboat">
       motorboat
      </a>
      that uses a
      <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">
       propeller
      </a>
      in the water below or behind the boat, a jetboat draws the water from under the boat into a
      <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">
       pump
      </a>
      inside the boat, then expels it through a nozzle at the
      <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">
       stern
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Jetboats were originally designed by
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Hamilton_(engineer)" title="Bill Hamilton (engineer)">
       Sir William Hamilton
      </a>
      (who developed a waterjet in 1954) for operation in the fast-flowing and shallow rivers of
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      , specifically to overcome the problem of propellers striking rocks in such waters.
     </p>
     <p>
      Previous attempts at waterjet propulsion had very short lifetimes, generally due to the inefficient design of the units and the fact that they offered few advantages over conventional propellers. Unlike these previous waterjet developments, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Secondo_Campini" title="Secondo Campini">
       Campini's
      </a>
      and the Hanley Hydrojet, Hamilton had a specific need for a propulsion system to operate in very-shallow water, and the waterjet proved to be the ideal solution. From this, the popularity of the jet unit and jetboat increased rapidly. Through further developments, it was found the waterjet offered several other advantages over propellers for a wide range of vessel types, and as such, waterjets are used widely today for many high-speed vessels, including passenger ferries, rescue craft, patrol boats and offshore supply vessels.
     </p>
     <p>
      Jetboats are highly maneuverable, and many can, from full speed, be reversed and brought to a stop within little more than their own length, in a maneuver known as a "crash stop". The well known
      <i>
       Hamilton turn
      </i>
      or "jet spin" is a high-speed maneuvre where the boat's engine throttle is cut, the steering is turned sharply and the throttle opened again, causing the boat to spin quickly around with a large spray of water.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is no engineering limit to the size of jetboats, though the validity of their use depends a lot on the type of application. Classic prop-drives are generally more efficient and economical at low speeds, up to about 20 knots (37km/h; 23mph), but as boat speed increases beyond this, the extra
      <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">
       hull
      </a>
      resistance generated by struts,
      <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">
       rudders
      </a>
      , shafts (etc.) means waterjets are more efficient in the 20-50 knot range (up to 90km/h; 60mph). Also, in situations with very large propellers turning at slow speeds (such as tug boats), the equivalent size waterjet would be too big to be practical. For these reasons, the vast majority of waterjet units are installed in high-speed vessels and in particular situations where shallow draught, maneuverability, and load flexibility are main concerns.
     </p>
     <p>
      The biggest jet-driven vessels are found in military use or the high-speed passenger/car ferry industry. South Africa's
      <a href="/wiki/Valour-class_frigate" title="Valour-class frigate">
       Valour-class
      </a>
      frigates (approximately 120 metres or 390 feet long) and the United States Littoral Combat Ship (127 meters or 417 feet long) are among the biggest jet-propelled vessels so far. Even these vessels are capable of performing "crash stops".

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Function">
       Function
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jetboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg/220px-Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg/330px-Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg/440px-Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rogue_jetboat_M_D_Vaden.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Jetboat on the Rogue River by Grants Pass, Oregon.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A conventional screw propeller works within the body of water below a boat hull, effectively "screwing" through the water to drive a vessel forward by generating a difference in pressure between the forward and rear surfaces of the propeller blades and by
      <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">
       accelerating
      </a>
      a mass of water rearward. By contrast, a waterjet unit delivers a high-pressure "push" from the stern of a vessel by accelerating a volume of water as it passes through a specialised pump mounted above the waterline inside the boat hull. Both methods yield thrust due to
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
       Newton
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">
       third law
      </a>
       every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a jetboat, the waterjet draws water from beneath the hull, where it passes through a series of
      <a href="/wiki/Impeller" title="Impeller">
       impellers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Stator" title="Stator">
       stators
      </a>
      - known as stages - which increase the velocity of the waterflow. Most modern jets are single-stage, while older waterjets may have as many as three stages. The tail section of the waterjet unit extends out through the
      <a href="/wiki/Transom_(nautical)" title="Transom (nautical)">
       transom
      </a>
      of the hull, above the waterline. This jetstream exits the unit through a small nozzle at high velocity to push the boat forward. Steering is accomplished by moving this nozzle to either side, or less commonly, by small gates on either side that deflect the jetstream. Because the jetboat relies on the flow of water through the nozzle for control, it is not possible to steer a conventional jetboat without the engine running.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shotover_Jet,_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons,_Queenstown,_New_Zealand.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Shotover_Jet%2C_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons%2C_Queenstown%2C_New_Zealand.jpg/220px-Shotover_Jet%2C_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons%2C_Queenstown%2C_New_Zealand.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Shotover_Jet%2C_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons%2C_Queenstown%2C_New_Zealand.jpg/330px-Shotover_Jet%2C_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons%2C_Queenstown%2C_New_Zealand.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Shotover_Jet%2C_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons%2C_Queenstown%2C_New_Zealand.jpg/440px-Shotover_Jet%2C_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons%2C_Queenstown%2C_New_Zealand.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shotover_Jet,_Jet_Boating_the_Shotover_River_Canyons,_Queenstown,_New_Zealand.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A jetboat on
        <a href="/wiki/Shotover_River" title="Shotover River">
         Shotover Canyon
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
         New Zealand
        </a>
        , the country for which jetboats were originally invented.
       </div>

     </div>
     <p>
      Unlike conventional propeller systems where the rotation of the propeller is reversed to provide astern movement, a waterjet will continue to pump normally while a deflector is lowered into the jetstream after it leaves the outlet nozzle. This deflector redirects thrust forces forward to provide reverse thrust. Most highly developed reverse deflectors redirect the jetstream down and to each side to prevent recirculation of the water through the jet again, which may cause aeration problems, or increase reverse thrust. Steering is still available with the reverse deflector lowered so the vessel will have full maneuverability. With the deflector lowered about halfway into the jetstream, forward and reverse thrust are equal so the boat maintains a fixed position, but steering is still available to allow the vessel to turn on the spot - something which is impossible with a conventional single propeller.
     </p>
     <p>
      Unlike
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrofoil" title="Hydrofoil">
       hydrofoils
      </a>
      , which use underwater
      <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">
       wings
      </a>
      or struts to lift the vessel clear of the water, standard jetboats use a conventional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planing_(sailing)" title="Planing (sailing)">
       planing hull
      </a>
      to ride across the water surface, with only the rear portion of the hull displacing any water. With the majority of the hull clear of the water, there is reduced drag, greatly enhancing speed and maneuverability, so jetboats are normally operated at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planing_(sailing)" title="Planing (sailing)">
       planing
      </a>
      speed. At slower speeds with less water pumping through the jet unit, the jetboat will lose some steering control and maneuverability and will quickly slow down as the hull comes off its planing state and hull resistance is increased. However, loss of steering control at low speeds can be overcome by lowering the reverse deflector slightly and increasing throttle - so you increase thrust and thus control without increasing boat speed itself. A conventional river-going jetboat will have a shallow-angled (but not flat-bottomed) hull to improve its high-speed cornering control and stability, while also allowing it to traverse very shallow water. At speed, jetboats can be safely operated in less than 7.5cm (3inches) of water.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the most significant breakthroughs, in the development of the waterjet, was to change the design so it expelled the jetstream
      <i>
       above
      </i>
      the water line, contrary to many people's intuition. Hamilton discovered early on that this greatly improved performance, compared to expelling below the waterline, while also providing a "clean" hull bottom (i.e. nothing protruding below the hull line) to allow the boat to skim through very shallow water. It makes no difference to the amount of thrust generated whether the outlet is above or below the waterline, but having it above the waterline reduces hull resistance and draught. Hamilton's first waterjet design had the outlet below the hull and actually in front of the inlet. This probably meant that disturbed water was entering the jet unit and reducing its performance, and the main reason why the change to above the waterline made such a difference.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Queenstown,_New_Zealand" title="Queenstown, New Zealand">
       Queenstown, New Zealand
      </a>
      , where jetboats are used extensively for
      <a href="/wiki/Adventure_travel" title="Adventure travel">
       adventure tourism
      </a>
      , claims to be the jetboat capital of the world, and jetboats are very common for many coastal and riverine tourism activities in the country, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Excitor" title="Excitor">
       Excitor
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Islands" title="Bay of Islands">
       Bay of Islands
      </a>
      .
     </p>

        </div>
        USMC
        <a href="/wiki/Expeditionary_Fighting_Vehicle" title="Expeditionary Fighting Vehicle">
         Expeditionary Fighting Vehicle
        </a>
        . Note the bow, which is extended into
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroplaning_(tires)" title="Hydroplaning (tires)">
         hydroplaning
        </a>
        position.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Applications for jetboats include most activities where conventional propellers are also used, but in particular passenger ferry services, coastguard and police patrol, navy and military, adventure tourism (which is becoming increasingly popular around the globe), pilot boat operations,
      <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(rescue)" title="Lifeboat (rescue)">
       surf rescue
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">
       farming
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">
       fishing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Exploration">
       exploration
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pleasure_boating&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pleasure boating (page does not exist)">
       pleasure boating
      </a>
      , and other water activities where motor boats are used. Jetboats can also be raced for sport, both on rivers and on specially designed racecourses. Recently there has been increasing use of jetboats in the form of
      <a href="/wiki/Rigid-hulled_inflatable_boat" title="Rigid-hulled inflatable boat">
       rigid-hulled inflatable boats
      </a>
      and as
      <a href="/wiki/Luxury_yacht_tender" title="Luxury yacht tender">
       luxury yacht tenders
      </a>
      . Many jetboats are small enough to be carried on a trailer and towed by car.
     </p>
     <p>
      One very important feature of the jetboat is the fact that it has no external rotating parts; it is thus safer for swimmers and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_life" title="Marine life">
       marine life
      </a>
      , though these can still be struck by the hull. The safety benefit itself can sometimes be reason enough to use this type of propulsion.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1977, Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Hillary" title="Edmund Hillary">
       Edmund Hillary
      </a>
      led a jetboat expedition, titled "Ocean to Sky", from the mouth of the Ganges River to its source. One of the jetboats was sunk by a friend of Hillary.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">
       Drawbacks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jetboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The fuel efficiency and performance of a jetboat can be affected by anything that disrupts the smooth flow of water through the jet unit. For example, a plastic bag sucked onto the jetunit's intake grill can have quite an adverse effect.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another disadvantage of jetboats appears to be that they are more sensitive to engine/jetunit mismatch, compared with the problem of engine/propeller mismatch in propeller-driven craft.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      If the jet-propulsion unit is not well-matched to the engine performance, inefficient fuel consumption and poor performance can result.
     </p>
