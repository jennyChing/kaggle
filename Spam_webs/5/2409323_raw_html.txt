     <p>
      Every known living
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Kakapo" title="Kakapo">
        kakapo
       </a>
      </b>
      , except some young chicks, has been given a name by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kakapo_Recovery_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kakapo Recovery Programme (page does not exist)">
       Kakapo Recovery Programme
      </a>
      officials. Many of the older birds were given
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English language
      </a>
      names, but more recent chicks have been given
      <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Mori language">
       Mori
      </a>
      names. Some Kakapo, such as Richard Henry and Moorhouse, are named after people who have provided assistance to the preservation efforts.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_population_changes">
       Recent population changes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_kakapo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recent population changes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After a good breeding year in 2002, the population remained at 86 until 2004, when three two-year-old female Kakapo died from infections by the soil bacterium
      <a href="/wiki/Erysipelas" title="Erysipelas">
       erysipelas
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-illness_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-illness-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gartrell_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gartrell-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Four chicks survived from the 2005 breeding season. The male Gunner died of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aflatoxicosis" title="Aflatoxicosis">
       aflatoxicosis
      </a>
      during the New Zealand winter of 2005, bringing the population back to 86.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In April 2008, the population grew to 93
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with the hatching of seven chicks,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRP_2008-04-08_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KRP_2008-04-08-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      then fell to 92 due to the death of adult Bill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRP_2008-04-01_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KRP_2008-04-01-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of the seven 2008 chicks died soon after hatching,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRP_2008-03-24_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KRP_2008-03-24-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      bringing the total down to 91, and on 28 October 2008 male Lee died, bringing the population down to 90.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRP_2008-11-11_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KRP_2008-11-11-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Male Rangi was re-discovered in February 2009 after spending 21 years living on Codfish Island.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRP_2009-02-08_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KRP_2009-02-08-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2009 there was another good breeding season, with 33 chicks (20 male and 13 female) bringing the population up to 124.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124_Kakapo_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-124_Kakapo-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A number of older birds died during 2010, followed by the significant loss of the only remaining Fiordland Kakapo, Richard Henry, announced during January 2011. His Fiordland genes survive through his three progeny.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richardhenry2010_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-richardhenry2010-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A breeding season in 2011 produced 11 chicks (8 females, 3 male), which all survived, bringing the population to 131. However, the deaths of several birds followed and with no breeding season in 2012, the population is down to 124.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recoveryprogrammefront_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-recoveryprogrammefront-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of May 2014, with the hatching of six eggs (the first hatchings since 2011) the total population is 126
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kakapo_count_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kakapo_count-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      living individuals.
     </p>
