        <div class="headline_area">
         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Nucleophilicity vs. Basicity
         </h1>
         <p class="headline_meta">
          by
          <span class="author vcard">
           <span class="fn">
            James
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p class="headline_meta">
          in
          <span>
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/alkyl-halides-2/" rel="category tag">
            Alkyl Halides
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/organic-chemistry-1/" rel="category tag">
            Organic Chemistry 1
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/understanding-electron-flow/" rel="category tag">
            Understanding Electron Flow
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="format_text entry-content">
         <p>
          Following up on
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/2012/06/05/nucleophiles-and-electrophiles/">
           Nucleophiles and Electrophiles
          </a>
          , heres a common question students have about nucleophilicity:
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Whats the difference between nucleophicity and basicity?
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Great, great question.
         </p>
         <p>
          First of all, remember that basicity is a
          <em>
           subset
          </em>
          of nucleophilicity. All nucleophiles are Lewis bases; they donate a lone pair of electrons. A base (or, Brnsted base) is just the name we give to a nucleophile when its forming a bond to a proton (H+). To summarize, when were talking about basicity and nucleophilicity, were talking about these two types of events.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Basicity
          </span>
          : nucleophile attacks hydrogen
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Nucleophilicity
          </span>
          : nucleophile attacks any atom other than hydrogen. Because were talking about organic chemistry here, for our purposes, this is going to mean carbon most of the time.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Understanding Basicity
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          So how do reactions of nucleophiles at hydrogen differ from reactions of nucleophiles at carbon? Well, theyre more easily reversible, for one thing. We can measure acidity (and, by extension, basicity) through the measure known as pKa, which is a reflection of the position of the equilibrium between an acid and its conjugate base.
         </p>
         <p>
          Lets put it up in a graphic:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/06/1-base-nucleophile1.png">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5240" height="306" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/06/1-base-nucleophile1.png" title=" " width="483"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Because we can measure the equilibrium constants for reversible acid-base reactions, we can get a fairly good idea of the relative strengths of acids and bases. Theres some complications; solvent effects can play a role in stabilities, for instance* but overall,
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/2010/06/18/know-your-pkas/">
           the pKa table is our friend
          </a>
          . Its a great reactivity ladder to hang our hats on.
         </p>
         <p>
          We talked about what made species strong bases a few posts ago  its
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/2012/04/25/walkthrough-of-acid-base-reactions-3-acidity-trends/">
           here
          </a>
          . The bottom line is that the more unstable a lone pair of electrons is, the more basic it will be (and vice versa).
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Understanding Nucleophilicity
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          And then theres nucleophilicity. How is nucleophilicity different from basicity? Well, since its not limited to simply forming a bond to hydrogen anymore, this leads to some extra complications. Lets just talk about the measurement problem first.
         </p>
         <p>
          Many reactions of nucleophiles are not reversible. A bond forms, a bond breaks, and thats the end of the reaction. The problem with this from a measurement standpoint is that we often cant determine an equilibrium constant for a reaction. And if we cant do that, then we cant develop a reactivity scale based on equilibria.
         </p>
         <p>
          If we cant measure equilibria, then what do we do? Well, we usethe next best measurement available: to measure reaction
          <strong>
           rates
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Theres one important thing to remember with reaction rates. They dont
          <em>
           always
          </em>
          reflect overall stability. There are a few more variables at play here.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Factor #1: Steric hindrance.
          </strong>
          Reactions where nucleophiles attack carbon-based electrophiles are significantly more sensitive to steric effects, because empty orbitals on carbon are not as accessible.
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/2011/07/18/steric-hindrance-is-like-a-fat-goalie/">
           Steric hindrance is like a fat goalie
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Factor #2: Solvents
          </strong>
          . The medium (solvent) in which a reaction takes place can greatly affect the rate of a reaction. Specifically, the solvent can greatly attenuate (reduce) the nucleophilicity of some Lewis bases through hydrogen bonding.
         </p>
         <p>
          Again, lets go to the graphic:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/06/2-base-nucleophile.png">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5241" height="550" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/06/2-base-nucleophile.png" title=" " width="564"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Heres another question that comes up. How do you know when something is going to act as a base, and when its going to act as a nucleophile? Another great question!
         </p>
         <p>
          Like the prototypical three-handed economist, Ill tell you that it depends upon what type of reaction youre talking about.
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/2012/05/21/acid-base-reactions-are-fast/">
           Acid-base reactions tend to be fast
          </a>
          , relatively speaking. But it will greatly depend on the situation. More soon, I assure you.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Next:
          </strong>
          So what, specifically, makes something a good nucleophile?
         </p>

         </p>
         <p>
          * Note (for more advanced readers) For instance, alcohols are dramatically less acidic in dimethyl sulfoxide [DMSO] than they are in water, and since many pKa values for less acidic species are given in DMSO, its easy to misjudge the acidity of alcohols relative to other species.
