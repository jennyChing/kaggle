                 <h2 class="date-header">
                  <span>
                   25 May 2015
                  </span>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="date-posts">
                  <div class="post-outer">
                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-RfgtpS9b4Xo/VWMeVfhYQLI/AAAAAAAAAxI/7DHlcwVcaho/s640/My%2BResponse%2BTo%2B-Veganism%2BIs%2Ba%2BFirst.png" itemprop="image_url"/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://theveganscholar.blogspot.com.au/2015/05/my-response-to-veganism-is-first-world.html">
                      My Response To "Veganism Is a First World Luxury. There, I've said it."
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-5347569503207565566" itemprop="articleBody">
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-RfgtpS9b4Xo/VWMeVfhYQLI/AAAAAAAAAxI/7DHlcwVcaho/s1600/My%2BResponse%2BTo%2B-Veganism%2BIs%2Ba%2BFirst.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="536" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-RfgtpS9b4Xo/VWMeVfhYQLI/AAAAAAAAAxI/7DHlcwVcaho/s640/My%2BResponse%2BTo%2B-Veganism%2BIs%2Ba%2BFirst.png" width="640"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <br/>
                      I'm sure most of you have seen the Mamamia article currently doing the rounds on Facebook. It's popped up all over my feed over the past few days. Interestingly enough, I've only seen it shared by vegans who want to discuss how ridiculous it is - I haven't actually seen any posts
                      <i>
                       agreeing
                      </i>
                      with the author. I suppose that goes to show just how incorrect Maya's argument is.
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      First off, I think it's important to mention that we shouldn't get too worked up about this article. It's just one person's (uniformed) opinion. It doesn't change the fact that veganism is rapidly becoming part of mainstream culture, it doesn't change the fact that new vegan brands, stores and restaurants are popping up, it seems, each and every day.
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      <br/>
                      Essentially, this article is click-bait (as many of Mamamia's articles are). It was intentionally written to stir controversy - and here I am, jumping on the bandwagon and adding more fuel to the fire. Oops!
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      We should take articles like this with a grain of salt. Although the writer may have been expressing her honest opinion, it's clear that the title and tone of the article were skewed towards two main audiences: vegans and 'anti-vegans'. It was written specifically to ignite maximum "share-worthiness". Vegans will share the post around to encourage other vegans to dispute Maya's claims, while 'anti-vegans' will share the post around to let everyone know
                      <i>
                       just how hypocritical those crazy vegans are!
                      </i>
                      <br/>
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                      <div style="text-align: center;">
                       <span style="color: #b6d7a8; font-size: large;">
                        <b>
                         <i>
                          "Although the writer may have been expressing her honest opinion, it's clear that the title and tone of the article were skewed towards two main audiences: vegans and 'anti-vegans'."
                         </i>
                        </b>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      And so, to reiterate: don't take click-bait articles too seriously. If you're happy to take the time to dispute this article like I am about to, go ahead, but it's also totally fine to completely ignore silly stuff like this.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; text-align: center;">
                       <tbody>
                        <tr>
                         <td>
                          <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-FgRBLHV0rn0/VWMc3kcKtGI/AAAAAAAAAw8/0smiif0MF64/s1600/green-juice-769129_1280.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                           <img border="0" height="426" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-FgRBLHV0rn0/VWMc3kcKtGI/AAAAAAAAAw8/0smiif0MF64/s640/green-juice-769129_1280.jpg" width="640"/>
                          </a>
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                        <tr>
                         <td class="tr-caption" style="font-size: 12.8000001907349px;">
                          Should we eat meat instead of greens because we're privileged?
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                       </tbody>
                      </table>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: justify;">
                      <br/>
                      Now I've got that out of the way - let's discuss the content of the article itself.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Although the tone of the article is unfairly accusing, it does hold
                      <i>
                       some
                      </i>
                      merit. Veganism is indeed a first world luxury. Severely impoverished people have little choice in what they eat and drink. Sometimes they
                      <i>
                       must
                      </i>
                      use animal products or risk death. Most of us more privileged folk, on the other hand, have the wonderful benefit of being able to choose what we eat. Now, the author of the Mamamia article seems to suggest that we should feel ashamed about our ability to choose; that we should
                      <i>
                       not
                      </i>
                      choose to do what we feel is right because some people don't have that privilege. Honestly, this is a really silly way to look at things. Why shouldn't we take complete advantage of the choice we have to make the world a better place?
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Having enough money to make donations and enough energy to run charity marathons could also be considered "first world luxuries", but the Mamamia article conveniently makes no mention of this. Charity work certainly isn't something we should stop doing simply because we must be privileged in order to do it.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      All of this tells us that Mamamia's article is simply a poor attempt to take a stab at vegans. It's a troll article, it's full of misleading information, and we shouldn't concern ourselves too much with it. And I haven't even begun to touch on the fact that the grains used to fatten up farm animals for Western consumption could instead feed the starving people of the world. But that's a different story.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Now, here's an adorable picture of a piglet to lighten the mood:

                 <h2 class="date-header">
                  <span>
                   30 Apr 2015
                  </span>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="date-posts">
                  <div class="post-outer">
                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-eJ2IFnIxzik/VUIDv0MbXhI/AAAAAAAAAv4/G45qz47jBqE/s1600/Tumblr%2BUser%2BSays%2B-Vegans%2BShould%2BEat.png" itemprop="image_url"/>
                    <meta content="8339427540555488088" itemprop="blogId"/>
                    <meta content="6022450447536187745" itemprop="postId"/>

                    </a>
                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://theveganscholar.blogspot.com.au/2015/04/tumblr-user-says-vegans-should-eat-honey.html">
                      Tumblr User Says "Vegans Should Eat Honey"
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6022450447536187745" itemprop="articleBody">
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-eJ2IFnIxzik/VUIDv0MbXhI/AAAAAAAAAv4/G45qz47jBqE/s1600/Tumblr%2BUser%2BSays%2B-Vegans%2BShould%2BEat.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="536" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-eJ2IFnIxzik/VUIDv0MbXhI/AAAAAAAAAv4/G45qz47jBqE/s1600/Tumblr%2BUser%2BSays%2B-Vegans%2BShould%2BEat.png" width="640"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     I don't know much about honey production. I don't know much about honey at all, because it's never really been included in my diet. I don't eat honey for two reasons: one, because it's an animal product, and I'm against the use of animal products; and two, because it tastes absolutely disgusting. I've never enjoyed eating honey.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     What I
                     <i>
                      do
                     </i>
                     know is that, in general, honey isn't considered vegan. Avoiding animal products is a very important part of the vegan lifestyle, and honey, by definition, is an animal product. In that sense, it's not vegan and vegans shouldn't consume it.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     But sometimes we have to look deeper. We have to think logically and practically about how to stop harming animals. Is it possible that honey production could be
                     <i>
                      saving
                     </i>
                     the bees rather than hurting them?
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     That's what Tumblr user
                     <a href="http://itslittl3red.tumblr.com/">
                      <i>
                       itslitt3red
                      </i>
                     </a>
                     (who is, according to their Tumblr page, vegan) has suggested. Here's a screenshot of the post in question, if you're interested:
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-tCrcHbQW1lg/VUHl14CMrVI/AAAAAAAAAvo/-jkHNNyEG-I/s1600/11173335_362711890582931_5765455037441048591_n.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="392" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-tCrcHbQW1lg/VUHl14CMrVI/AAAAAAAAAvo/-jkHNNyEG-I/s1600/11173335_362711890582931_5765455037441048591_n.jpg" width="640"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     If you can't read the text in the screenshot, here's a transcript:
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      "As I'm sure most people know by now, bees are disappearing at alarming rates. Simply put, our entire species could not survive without them. This is due to a syndrome called Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD).
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      <br/>
                     </i>
                     <i>
                      Many vegans opt to use agave nectar instead of honey, because agave nectar is plant based. BUt harvesting of agave nectar is threatening the existence of two other endangered species: Mexican long-nosed bats (who live strictly off of nectars - primarily agave nectar) and the Jaguarundi (a solitary feline who basically looks like a love child between a jungle cat and a ferret). Approximately 113,126 acres of these animals' habitat were destroyed from 1991 to 2000, and more has been destroyed since.
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      <br/>
                     </i>
                     <i>
                      On the other hand, beekeepers are essential to increasing bee population. They monitor the bees' health and help protect them from dangerous parasites and pesticides that are suspected to cause CCD. In addition, well-kept bees never need to use the amount of honey they produce; honey is made by the bees to consume only when there is not enough food for them outside the hive. In the care of a good beekeeper, this will only happen during the winter months, and the keeper will leave enough honey for the bees to thrive until it's spring again.
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      <br/>
                     </i>
                     <i>
                      It's best to buy local, organic honey if at all possible. Local beekeepers will not use dangerous factory-farming methods, and it helps maintain your local bee population! If you want to help bees in a more active way than buying local honey, you can plant a bee garden or even become a small-scale beekeeper! (I don't have a link for this, it's best to check out local resources. Maybe even ask the person selling honey at your farmer's market!)"
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     It's a long piece of text, but it's worth reading. I found the arguments in this post interesting, but I wasn't really sure what to think because I know very little about honey production and beekeeping. This post outlines a variety of possible benefits of beekeeping but doesn't touch on any possible drawbacks. That's why I enlisted
                     <a href="http://theveganscholar.blogspot.com.au/">
                      my Facebook page
                     </a>
                     's audience to find out more.
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      <br/>
                     </i>
                     My Facebook audience gave their educated opinions on this issue and I compiled their comments. In this post, I'll give a summary of opinions from both sides of the fence, allowing you to make your own decision about whether or not vegans should eat honey.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The responses from my Facebook audience were divided - some agreed entirely, some disagreed entirely, and some both agreed and disagreed with aspects of
                     <i>
                      itslittl3red'
                     </i>
                     s post.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Those who disagreed with
                     <i>
                      itslittl3red
                     </i>
                     offered a variety of different arguments. The most popular comment, offered by Cem, said that veganism is about rejecting the commodity status of animals. In Cem's eyes, this is an standard of veganism that shouldn't be compromised under any circumstances. To quote them directly: "
                     <i>
                      Sustainability is not a vegan concern since it's only about ethics."
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      <br/>
                     </i>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-W9ypZRmVUZQ/VUIvPq5UugI/AAAAAAAAAwI/1WGFsy1tTGU/s1600/bee-515023_1280.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="442" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-W9ypZRmVUZQ/VUIvPq5UugI/AAAAAAAAAwI/1WGFsy1tTGU/s1600/bee-515023_1280.jpg" width="640"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Several other commenters appeared to agree with Cem's arguments. Debby, for example, noted that vegans don't use or harm animals unless it's necessary - honey bees included. Samuel offered a similar yet harsher argument, stating that bees are raped, gassed and killed in honey production.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Chelsea offers an interesting perspective:
                     <i>
                      "Honey bees are not native to the U.S. anyway, and they compete with native pollinators (who are up to 2-3 times more efficient at pollinating), which is one more reason why the honey industry shouldn't be supported."
                     </i>
                     To back up this claim, Chelsea cited
                     <a href="http://news.cornell.edu/stories/2011/10/native-bees-are-better-pollinators-honeybees">
                      this link
                     </a>
                     ,
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Another reply to my Facebook post offered an opposing perspective. Cheyenne, who buys raw, local honey from a beekeeper, has no ethical hangups with this form of honey production. Cheyenne supports a local beekeeper and is happy with the way they treat their bees. This contrasts with Cem's argument which suggested that honey should
                     <i>
                      never
                     </i>
                     be used, regardless of how well the bees are treated.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <span style="color: #b6d7a8; font-size: large;">
                       <b>
                        "All things considered, honey is not vegan - at least not by definition."
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     Jane, who misses the taste of honey but doesn't eat it, sees honey as what it fundamentally is:
                     <i>
                      bee vomit!
                     </i>
                     Jane has a hive in their garden, but won't take any honey from the hive for one main reason. To quote Jane: "
                     <i>
                      The bees aren't making it for me."
                     </i>
                     Jane's comment reiterates what Cem said about rejecting the commodity status of animals (I should add veganism, as defined by Wikipedia, is indeed about rejecting the commodity status of animals - but is there more to it than this?).
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Another commenter, Rhiannon, thinks
                     <i>
                      itslitt3red
                     </i>
                     has some valid points. They suggest a Netflix documentary called
                     <i>
                      <a href="http://vanishingofthebees.com/">
                       Vanishing of the Bees
                      </a>
                      (2009),
                     </i>
                     which details the sudden disappearance of honey bees from beehives around the world. According to this documentary, CCD (or Colony Collapse Disorder, the same disorder mentioned in
                     <i>
                      itslittl3red's
                     </i>
                     post)is noted as a primary cause of this disappearance, and pesticide use is said to contribute to CCD. According to
                     <i>
                      itslitt3red's
                     </i>
                     post, beekeepers help protect honey bees from these pesticides (in turn combating CCD).
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     All things considered, honey is not vegan - at least not by definition. In the most general terms, veganism is about rejecting the commodity status of animals and avoiding the use of animal products. In this sense, using honey goes against the 'rules of veganism'. On the other hand, it's important to think outside the box. It's a similar concept to my article about
                     <a href="http://theveganscholar.blogspot.com/2015/04/a-vegan-moral-dilemma-should-we-eat-non.html">
                      food wastage
                     </a>
                     - there are other things that harm animals besides animal product use. Deforestation, habitat removal and pollution are some common examples. In my opinion, you should take all of this into account before you decide whether or not to use honey.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     P.S: A big 'thank you' to everyone who responded to my Facebook post - I really appreciate your efforts!
