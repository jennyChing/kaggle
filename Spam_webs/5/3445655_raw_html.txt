      <h1 class="article-title">
       U.S. companies hoard record amount of cash
      </h1>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="row">
     <div class="column">
      <!-- main -->
      <div class="share-tools pull-left" id="js-sharebar-main">
      </div>
      <div class="pull-right">
       <span class="cnnbyline js-vid-byline-">
        <span class="byline">
         By
         <a href="/author/matt-egan/index.html">
          Matt Egan
         </a>
        </span>
        <a class="soc-twtname" href="https://twitter.com/intent/user?screen_name=mattmegan5">
         @mattmegan5
        </a>
       </span>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
   </header>
   <div class="row two-columns-fixed-rr">
    <section class="column">
     <article class="module">
      <div id="storycontent">
       <!--storytext-->
       <div id="storytext">
        <figure id="ie_dottop">
         <img alt="cash capital spending" border="0" height="439" src="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/money/dam/assets/150320125723-cash-capital-spending-780x439.jpg" width="780"/>
        </figure>
        <div class="share-tools share-tools--floater" id="js-sharebar-floater">
        </div>
        <h2>
         Corporate America has so much cash sitting in the bank that it could purchase the Dallas Cowboys 437 times without borrowing a dime.
        </h2>
        <p>
         Or if these titans of business really love House of Cards they could splurge by acquiring
         <span>
          Netflix
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=NFLX&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            NFLX
           </a>
          </span>
          ,
          <a href="http://money.cnn.com/technology/tech30/index.html">
           Tech30
          </a>
          )
         </span>
         53 times. They could even buy
         <span>
          Apple
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=AAPL&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            AAPL
           </a>
          </span>
          ,
          <a href="http://money.cnn.com/technology/tech30/index.html">
           Tech30
          </a>
          )
         </span>
         ,
         <span>
          Facebook
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=FB&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            FB
           </a>
          </span>
          ,
          <a href="http://money.cnn.com/technology/tech30/index.html">
           Tech30
          </a>
          )
         </span>
         and Warren Buffett's
         <span>
          Berkshire Hatahway
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=BRKA&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            BRKA
           </a>
          </span>
          )
         </span>
         and still have cash to play with.
        </p>
        <div id="ie_column">
         <div id="ie_column">
          <div id="quigo220">
           <!-- ADSPACE: technology/quigo/ctr.220x200 -->
           <div id="ad_ns_btf_03">
           </div>
           <!-- ADSPACE-END -->
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         In other words, big American companies literally have more cash than they know what to do with.
        </p>
        <p>
         The mountain of cash in corporate vaults climbed to a record-high of $1.4 trillion during the fourth quarter, according to a FactSet analysis of
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/data/markets/sandp/">
          S&amp;P 500
         </a>
         companies.
        </p>
        <p>
         The good news is that means companies like
         <span>
          Microsoft
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=MSFT&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            MSFT
           </a>
          </span>
          ,
          <a href="http://money.cnn.com/technology/tech30/index.html">
           Tech30
          </a>
          )
         </span>
         and
         <span>
          Oracle
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=ORCL&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            ORCL
           </a>
          </span>
          ,
          <a href="http://money.cnn.com/technology/tech30/index.html">
           Tech30
          </a>
          )
         </span>
         have extremely healthy balance sheets. That's useful if another crisis comes along.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2015/03/20/investing/nasdaq-5000-alltime-high-record/index.html?iid=HP_LN">
          <span class="inStoryHeading">
           Related: Nasdaq near all-time high. Is it another bubble?
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         The bad news is that at least some of these corporate giants still aren't willing to spend their cash on new equipment, new hires and new research. It would be a big economic boost if companies went on a spending spree. Shareholders would also like bigger dividends given all this cash companies have on hand.
        </p>
        <p>
         A large part of the problem is the energy sector. The dramatic plunge in oil prices has caused Big Energy CEOs to scale back on spending. Capital expenditures at S&amp;P 500 companies are projected to decline 3.5% over the next 12 months, FactSet said.
        </p>
        <p>
         "Since we've come out of the recession, Corporate America has become much more cautious about spending," said Mark Litzerman, co-head of real asset strategy at Wells Fargo Investment Institute.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2015/03/18/investing/oil-price-plunge-100000-layoffs/index.html">
          <span class="inStoryHeading">
           Related: Cheap oil is costing 100,000 jobs
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Oil fallout hits spending:
         </strong>
         Companies like
         <span>
          Chevron
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=CVX&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            CVX
           </a>
          </span>
          )
         </span>
         and
         <span>
          ConocoPhillips
         </span>
         <span>
          (
          <span class="inlink_chart">
           <a class="inlink" href="http://money.cnn.com/quote/quote.html?symb=COP&amp;source=story_quote_link">
            COP
           </a>
          </span>
          )
         </span>
         have disclosed plans to
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2014/12/08/investing/shale-oil-spending-america-opec/index.html">
          slash spending
         </a>
         this year to offset shrinking cash flows. Many oil producers are simply
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2015/01/29/investing/oil-prices-drilling-supply/index.html">
          pulling the plug on drilling rigs
         </a>
         in response to the oil glut.
        </p>
        <p>
         That's why analysts believe the energy sector will slash capital spending by 13.5% over the next 12 months, easily the biggest drop in the S&amp;P 500. That's a very big deal because energy accounted for nearly one-third of S&amp;P 500 capex over the past decade and during the fourth quarter it jumped by double-digits.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2015/03/17/investing/oil-price-opec-us-shale/index.html">
          <span class="inStoryHeading">
           Related: Why OPEC can't kill the U.S. oil boom
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Not everyone is hoarding:
         </strong>
         The picture looks a bit better when you look beyond the oil patch. Six sectors in the S&amp;P 500 are projected to boost spending for the next 12 months, led by technology and health care.
        </p>
        <p>
         U.S. corporations more broadly are expected to increase spending between 3.5% and 6%, according to Liz Ann Sonders, chief investment strategist at Charles Schwab.
        </p>
        <p>
         "We've gone through two cycles where capex is growing but not sharply. It's not been incredibly robust," Sonders said referring to the recovery from the 2001 recession.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2015/03/20/investing/nike-earnings-stock/index.html">
          <span class="inStoryHeading">
           Related: Just buy it? Nike stock soars to all-time high
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Stuff is getting old:
         </strong>
         There is a silver lining here: At some point companies will be forced to go on a shopping spree to replace their aging stuff.
        </p>
        <p>
         At the same time, there's no secret that the country's infrastructure is badly in need of a face lift. Now that state and local budgets are in better shape, spending on roads and bridges may finally be in the works.
        </p>
        <p>
         "We think the next recession is still a ways away largely because recessions come when you've built up excesses. It's easy to argue there's not a heck of a lot of excess built up in this economy," Sonders said.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://money.cnn.com/2014/10/16/investing/companies-hoarding-cash-us-economy/index.html?sr=fbmoney101614hoardingcash1000story">
          <span class="inStoryHeading">
           Related: When will companies stop hoarding cash?
          </span>
         </a>
        </p>
