           <h3 style="text-align: center;">
            <em>
             No trees for that hammock? No problem.
            </em>
           </h3>
           <p>
            This is irony: Im a hammock camping
            <em>
             fanatic
            </em>
            , surrounded by thelargest contiguous Ponderosa Pine forest in North America, yet I moved into a neighborhood devoid of trees. My entire 2-acre lot is barren, and while Ive planted a few trees, it will be years before Ill be able to hang anything more than a bird feeder. To make matters worse, I live in the American Southwest, a region known more for red rocks and sand dunes than forests and trees.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/elcos_hammock/" rel="attachment wp-att-35535">
             <img alt="elcos_hammock" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35535" height="300" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/elcos_hammock.jpg" width="400"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            So for the past few years Ive collected and even built a variety of hammock stands out of necessity. Like any good recreationist, I know the value of testing gear in the backyard before proving it in the field. But these hammock stands serve a more practical purpose beyond the casual backyard test. Ive often found myself in areas where pitching a hammock was a challenge, and where having a portable hammock stand would have been ideal. Here are a few examples:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Improved campgrounds where camping is restricted to specific plots and trees are not reachable
            </li>
            <li>
             Hanging hammocks on trees is not allowed (in certain national and state parks, for example)
            </li>
            <li>
             At group camping events (e.g., bicycle touring; local or national scouting camporees or jamborees) where you must camp in large fields
            </li>
            <li>
             On river trips where camping is restricted to sandy riverbanks
            </li>
            <li>
             At locations where there is no vegetationno trees, no nothing (e.g., much of the American Southwest: Arches, Escalante, Grand Canyon, etc.)
            </li>
            <li>
             Above the tree line
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Ive learned many (often expensive and even painful) lessons during this collection process. First, most traditional backyard spreader bar hammock stands are not designed for most camping hammocks, nor are they engineered to accommodate a tarp. Some portable hammock stands are simply too heavy and bulky for anything but car camping, and some stands are too complicated or require unique handling to work correctly. Other portable stands require significant anchoring (and more real estate) to work properly, while others are free-standing.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/hammock-stand-airfield/" rel="attachment wp-att-35536">
             <img alt="hammock-stand-airfield" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35536" height="241" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/hammock-stand-airfield.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            Good or bad, some of the best stands for camping hammocks are do-it-yourself projects, some more complicated to construct than others. If youre comfortable with do-it-yourself projects and have the necessary tools and know-how, these stands can be a fun to make. But dont let the DIY label scare you  some of these stands can be assembled without any tools in just a few minutes.
           </p>
           <p>
            After spending a lot of time buying and building stands, Ive gravitated to the following criteria when evaluating them for hammock camping:
           </p>
           <ol>
            <li>
             Is it truly portable (e.g., it can break down and fit in my sedan)?
            </li>
            <li>
             Is it easy to assemble in the field (especially with just one person)?
            </li>
            <li>
             Is it affordable?
            </li>
            <li>
             Can it accommodate a tarp without flexing?
            </li>
            <li>
             Is it structurally sound and sturdy?
            </li>
            <li>
             Can it hold more than one hammock?
            </li>
           </ol>
           <p>
            Items one through three are arguably very subjective. What you can afford in terms of cost, assembly time, and transportation are as variable as the user. Ill argue that items four and five are mandatory, as you dont want to compromise your health or safety with a stand that fails or cannot hold a tarp taut in severe weather. Number six is a bonus item, and not often applicable, but its handy if, like me, you often camp with friends or family.
           </p>

            The following list of stands isnt meant to be exhaustive and is not ordered by any means, but it illustrates some of the options that fit my criteria, within reason.

            : This is one of the more popular do-it-yourself hammock camping stands, probably because it can be built in the field if materials are readily available. The rigid top rail prevents the stand from flexing, making it possible to hang a taut tarp above the hammock. This stand can be constructed a variety of ways, including using wood/bamboo struts with a
            <a href="http://www.animatedknots.com/lashtripod/" rel="nofollow">
             tripod lashing
            </a>
            and a top rail, or using hinges, bolts, and steel beams.
           </p>
           <p>
            Hennessy Hammock
            <a href="http://www.freepatentsonline.com/7243383.pdf" rel="nofollow">
             patented a variation
            </a>
            of this stand that theyve used at multiple Boy Scout World Jamborees. The beauty of this tripod-based stand is that it can grow to accommodate multiple hammocks, creating a hammock village.
           </p>

            </a>

            </a>

           <h3>
            <a href="https://www.hammockforums.net/forum/showthread.php?t=8758" rel="nofollow">
             24 Hammock Stand
            </a>
            (DIY)
           </h3>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/2x4-stand-folded/" rel="attachment wp-att-35528">
             <img alt="2x4-stand-folded" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35528" height="412" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/2x4-stand-folded.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Portable
            </strong>
            : Requires tools to unfasten bolts, but can be disassembled into as few as 3 pieces. Some can fold into just one piece.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Field Assembly
            </strong>
            : 1 person. Easy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Affordable
            </strong>
            : $100+
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Fits a tarp
            </strong>
            : Possible. Depending on how you build this stand. Longer stands will support tarps better.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Flex
            </strong>
            : No flex.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Sturdy
            </strong>
            : Sturdy construction, depending on materials used.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Comments
            </strong>
            : A lot of people have gotten very creative with fencing pipe, poles, and wood struts, creating hammock stands to suit their own needs. This is one of the more popular examples using construction materials to assemble the stand. The 24 stand can be built to fold down and then carried in the bed of a truck. Its not as portable than other stands due to its pack size and weight.
           </p>

           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/handy-hammock-kilimanjaro/" rel="attachment wp-att-35537">
             <img alt="handy-hammock-kilimanjaro" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35537" height="288" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/handy-hammock-kilimanjaro.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>

           <h3>
            <a href="http://handyhammock.co.uk/" rel="nofollow">
             Handy Hammock Stand
            </a>
           </h3>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/handy-hammock-one-strut/" rel="attachment wp-att-35538">
             <img alt="handy-hammock-one-strut" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35538" height="326" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/handy-hammock-one-strut.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Portable
            </strong>
            : Folds up and is backpacker-friendly.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Field Assembly
            </strong>
            : 1 person. Moderately easy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Affordable
            </strong>
            : $99
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Fits a tarp
            </strong>
            : Possible. The struts can be pulled apart to accommodate a tarp.

            <strong>
             Flex
            </strong>
            : No flex.

            <strong>
             Sturdy
            </strong>
            : Very sturdy. Uses tensegrity to maintain structure. Requires good staking.

            <strong>
             Comments
            </strong>
            : This is the only backpacker-friendly stand on the market. At approximately 1 kg, this stand can be easily portaged, carried, and packed to nearly any destination. For beaches, Handy Hammock sells longer beach stakes that keep the stand sturdy in soft sand.
           </p>

            For more photos and modifications, check out my

             Handy Hammock review
            </a>
            .

           <h3>
            <a href="http://theultimatehang.com/2013/04/3-person-hammock-stand/">
             3-Person Hammock Stand
            </a>
            (DIY)
           </h3>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Portable
            </strong>
            : Breaks down to three struts, rope, and stakes. Hard to fit in a sedan trunk.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Field Assembly
            </strong>
            : 1-2 person. Moderately easy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Affordable
            </strong>
            : $40
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Fits a tarp
            </strong>
            : Yes.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Flex
            </strong>
            : Slight flex, depending on rope used.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Sturdy
            </strong>
            : The design is based on tensegrity, which channels compressive forces through the struts. It is remarkably sturdy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Comments
            </strong>
            : I used the instructions from
            <a href="http://www.bweebweebwee.com/tensegrity/" rel="nofollow">
             bweebweebwee.com
            </a>
            and found the design to be rock-solid, even with cheap rope. This stand takes up a good amount of real estate, but it is a great center piece at a camporee and can double as a group kitchen area and lounge. This is the only stand that can easily accommodate more than one hammock by design.
           </p>

           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/anchored-pole-stand/" rel="attachment wp-att-35530">
             <img alt="anchored-pole-stand" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35530" height="552" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/anchored-pole-stand.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <h3>
            <a href="https://www.hammockforums.net/forum/showthread.php?t=34776" rel="nofollow">
             Anchored Pole Stand
            </a>
            (DIY)
           </h3>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/anchored-pole-stand-blackbird-and-tarp/" rel="attachment wp-att-35531">
             <img alt="anchored-pole-stand-Blackbird-and-tarp" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35531" height="381" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/anchored-pole-stand-Blackbird-and-tarp.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Portable
            </strong>
            : Breaks down to two struts/pipes, rope, and stakes. Can be hard to fit in a sedan trunk if the struts are too long (put the seats down!).
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Field Assembly
            </strong>
            : 1-2 person. Moderately easy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Affordable
            </strong>
            : $40+
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Fits a tarp
            </strong>
            : Yes.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Flex
            </strong>
            : Slight flex, depending on rope used.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Sturdy
            </strong>
            : The design is similar to the Handy Hammock design but the force distribution varies depending on construction.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Comments
            </strong>
            : This is a fairly simple design, but solid anchoring is very important. If the design is tweaked so the struts are at a 60-degree angle, more compressive forces are placed on the struts instead of pulling directly up on the stakes.
           </p>
           <p>
            This anchored pole design can be used when you only have one tree or support to anchor your hammock. A commercial variation of this is the
            <a href="http://www.avantlink.com/click.php?tt=app&amp;ti=1019&amp;mi=11469&amp;pw=3816&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.cabelas.com%2Fproduct%2FByer-of-Mainereg-Madera-Hammock-Stand%2F1587151.uts%3FNtk%3DAllProducts%26Ntt%3DByer%2Bof%2BMaine%2BMadera%2Bstand%26searchPath%3D%252Fcatalog%252Fsearch.cmd%253Fform_state%253DsearchForm%2526N%253D0%2526fsch%253Dtrue%2526Ntk%253DAllProducts%2526Ntt%253DByer%252Bof%252BMaine%252BMadera%252Bstand%2526x%253D10%2526y%253D6%2526WTz_l%253DHeader%25253BSearch-All%252BProducts">
             Byer of Maine Madera stand
            </a>
            (below). It uses two struts and one tendon/anchor, but it works much in the same way.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/byer-of-maine-madera-stand/" rel="attachment wp-att-35533">
             <img alt="byer-of-maine-madera-stand" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35533" height="541" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/byer-of-maine-madera-stand.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            Let me reiterate the importance of good anchoring with these stands. Depending on the angle of the strut/pole, you can exert more load on the anchors than they are designed for. I recommend getting 2 ft (61 cm) or longer rebar as stakes and pounding them in at a 60-degree angle away from the struts for maximum support.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a name="a8">
            </a>
           </p>
           <hr/>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/?attachment_id=35541" rel="attachment wp-att-35541">
             <img alt="new-tensegrity-6" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35541" height="480" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/new-tensegrity-6.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <h3>
            <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/home/how-to-plans/lawn-garden/how-to-build-a-three-person-hammock">
             3-Pole, 3-Person Tensegrity Stand
            </a>
            (DIY)
           </h3>
           <p>
            <a href="http://sectionhiker.com/portable-hammock-stands-for-camping-by-derek-hansen/tensegrity-stand-camporee-001/" rel="attachment wp-att-35545">
             <img alt="tensegrity-stand-camporee-001" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-35545" height="384" src="http://assets.sectionhiker.com/wp-content/uploads/thumbskeep/2013/04/tensegrity-stand-camporee-001.jpg" width="512"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Portable
            </strong>
            : Breaks down to three (or more) strut/pipe sections, rope, and stakes. Can be hard to fit in a sedan trunk if the struts are too long.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Field Assembly
            </strong>
            : 2 person. Moderately challenging.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Affordable
            </strong>
            : $100+
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Fits a tarp
            </strong>
            : Yes.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Flex
            </strong>
            : Slight flex, depending on rope/tendons used.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Sturdy
            </strong>
            : Tensegrity can be very strong and sturdy, but this stand is not exactly free-standing in every respect and requires stake-outs, especially if only one person is hanging.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Comments
            </strong>
            : This is one of those physics-defying stands that doesnt look like it should work. When I first saw it,
            <a href="https://www.hammockforums.net/gallery/showimage.php?i=10879" rel="nofollow">
             I had to try it
            </a>
            . I will say that this stand is complicated to build. There are much easier stands to construct and assemble in the field. However, this one gets a lot of oh-my-gosh-this-is-cool points, even if it takes 30+ minutes to finally assemble.
           </p>
           <p>
            When this design was first attempted, a lot of people wondered if it would be free-standing. In practice, it isnt. The stand requires some good anchoring to prevent tipping, especially with one occupant. I tried making this stand using sections of fence pipe. The fence coupling I used wasnt strong enough and eventually bent, collapsing this stand on top of me. If you use pole sections, be sure the sections have strong couplings and do not bend.
           </p>
