         <h3 class="entry-header">
          Handmade Vs. Machine-made cigars
         </h3>
         <div class="entry-content">
          <div class="entry-body">
           <img alt="15750166428_d8ca318c62_z (1)" height="282" src="http://ic.pics.livejournal.com/cigarweddinggif/72556702/1856/1856_original.jpg" title="15750166428_d8ca318c62_z (1)" width="400"/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The main difference between handmade and
           <a href="http://cigars.about.com/od/handrolledormachinemade/a/0061402.htm" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            machine-made cigars
           </a>
           is the fact that most machine-made cigars are not made with long fillers, which often means that the quality of the cigars is inferior in terms of burn and temperature. In other words, they burn faster and get hotter. For some machine brands, such as Bering Dominican Cigars for example, a longfiller is used, making them better than the rest, but still less than handmade cigars.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The quality of the cigar wrapper in machine-made ones is also usually inferior compared to the improved hand-made cigars. For the inexpensive mass market, a blend of filler rods is created, as with cigarettes, and covered with a continuous sheet wrapper. The rods are sealed and put to length. The wrapper is applied and the cigars are trimmed.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           How do you see the difference?
           <br/>
           <br/>
           It's pretty easy to tell the difference between hand-made and machine-made cigars: the cap of machine-made cigars is often much more pointed, the cigars feel less smooth and the wrapper is usually coarser, often with overlying veins.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           If the cigar has no cap, you can rest assured that it is a cheap machine-made cigar. A cellophane wrapper may also be a sign, in particular in Cuban cigars, but of the non-Cuban hand-made cigars, many good ones are wrapped in cellophane as well. Cuba recently introduced the concept of hand-finished cigars, which are basically machine-made, but finished by hand,such as the famous brand Quintero for example.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           These cigars are made of lightweight filler, have a fairly good wrapper and the caps are the same as those of the handmade ones. They can approach the smoking pleasure of a handmade cigar in taste, although the experienced smoker can notice the difference. Handmade cigars are so much more expensive because the production process takes more time, is labor intensive and because expensive leaves are used. When making hand-made cigars, there is also more waste.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The cigar box
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Cigars were originally sold in bundles with pig bladder on top (with a few vanilla pods for the smell). Then came big cedar chests where multiple layers of cigars were able to fit in. In 1830 bankers started shipping cigars to the directors in London, packed in sealed cedar wooden boxes with the logo of the bank on it. When the bank decided to focus on the cigar business, the cedar boxes were used as packaging for all major Havana brands and handmade cigars (although small amounts nowadays are packed in cardboard boxes, while loose cigars of many brands are packed in aluminum tubes with a cedar cladding). With cedar cigars do not dry out as fast and it promotes the aging process, which in turn will improve the flavor.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Labels on cigar boxes
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The idea of colored labels, which are now used for all handmade brands, comes from Ramon Allones, a immigrant in Cuba. He took them to the brand that he founded in 1837. When the industry began to grow in the mid 19th century , so did the need to separate the brands. Labels or illustrations were also used on the inside of the lids of boxes of Havanas and many other brands. The boxes usually have a colorful decorated border. The cedar chest is called 'bote nature'. Paper, usually colored, is usually stuck to the inside of the crate so that it covers the cigars contained in it.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           After being filled and checked the box is properly boarded and sealed with a green and white label (a habit that dates back to 1912), indicating that there are genuine Havanas inside. Nowadays such a label with similar colors and lettering for most handmade cigars is used, Cuban or not.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The shape of the package that is referred to as 8-9-8, is used for some cigars such as Partagas and Ramon Allones. These are shiny boxes with rounded corners which contain 25 cigars, arranged in two layers of 8 and in between a layer of 9.
           <br/>
           Cigar factories in Havana today look almost exactly like in the middle of the 19th century, when the art of cigar making was standardized and an industry was born.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Eight cigar factories in Cuba
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Nowadays, there are only eight farms in Cuba where cigars are made in the factory (at the beginning of the century, there were still 120). The factories were given an ideologically correct name after the revolution, but are still known by their name at that time and still have their old signs.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The procedure is essentially the same in all plants, even though the size and feel of the factories is different. The large EI Laguito for example, occupies three buildings in the Italian style from 1910 in an upscale residential area, formerly the residence of Marquez's Pinar del Rio. The rather gloomy three-storied building of Partagas, in the center of Havana, was built in 1845 and is more in a businesslike style. Laguito was the first company that hired female rollers, and to this day, the majority of the rollers is still female.
