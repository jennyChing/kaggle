     <p>
      A
      <b>
       scone
      </b>
      is a single-serving
      <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">
       cake
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Quick_bread" title="Quick bread">
       quick bread
      </a>
      . They are usually made of
      <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">
       wheat
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">
       barley
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Oatmeal" title="Oatmeal">
       oatmeal
      </a>
      , with
      <a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">
       baking powder
      </a>
      as a
      <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">
       leavening agent
      </a>
      , and are baked on
      <a href="/wiki/Sheet_pan" title="Sheet pan">
       sheet pans
      </a>
      . They are often lightly sweetened and are occasionally
      <a href="/wiki/Glaze_(cooking_technique)" title="Glaze (cooking technique)">
       glazed
      </a>
      . The scone is a basic component of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cream_tea" title="Cream tea">
       cream tea
      </a>
      or Devonshire tea. It differs from a
      <a href="/wiki/Teacake" title="Teacake">
       teacake
      </a>
      and other
      <a href="/wiki/Sweet_roll" title="Sweet roll">
       sweet buns
      </a>
      , which are made with
      <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker's yeast">
       yeast
      </a>
      . A scone is in some senses a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Pastry" title="Pastry">
       pastry
      </a>
      since it is made with essentially the same ingredients as shortcrust, though with different proportions of fat to flour.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buttermilk-Scones-batch.jpg">

        scones

     <p>
      The pronunciation of the word within the United Kingdom varies. According to one academic study, two-thirds of the British population pronounce it
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'o' in 'body'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      with the preference rising to 99% in the Scottish population. Similarly, in the Republic of Ireland, they pronounce it 'scon'. Experts say this is the correct pronunciation of the term as Ireland is the birthplace of the scone. This is also the pronunciation of Australians, Canadians and New Zealanders. Others, particularly inhabitants of the United States, pronounce the word as
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/o/ long 'o' in 'code'">
           o
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      , as spelt. British dictionaries usually show the "con" form as the preferred pronunciation, while recognising that the "cone" form also exists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Oxford Dictionaries explain that there are also regional and class differences in England connected with the different pronunciations:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       There are two possible pronunciations of the word
       <b>
        scone
       </b>
       : the first rhymes with
       <b>
        gone
       </b>
       and the second rhymes with
       <b>
        tone
       </b>
       . In US English the pronunciation rhyming with
       <b>
        tone
       </b>
       is more common. In British English the two pronunciations traditionally have different regional and class associations, with the first pronunciation associated with the north of England and the northern working class, while the second is associated with the south and the middle class.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The difference in pronunciation is alluded to in the poem which contains the lines:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <div class="poem">
       <p>
        I asked the maid in dulcet tone
        <br/>
        To order me a buttered scone;
        <br/>
        The silly girl has been and gone
        <br/>
        And ordered me a buttered scone.
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
      </i>
      reports that the first mention of the word was in 1513. Origin of the word
      <i>
       scone
      </i>
      is obscure and may, in fact, derive from different sources. That is, the classic Scottish
      <i>
       scone
      </i>
      which, according to Sheila MacNiven Cameron in
      <i>
       The Highlander's Cookbook
      </i>
      , originated as a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bannock_(food)" title="Bannock (food)">
        bannock
       </a>
      </i>
      cut into pieces; and the Dutch
      <i>
       schoonbrood
      </i>
      or "spoonbread" (very similar to the drop scone); and possibly other, similar and similarly named quick breads, may have made their way onto the British tea table, where their similar names merged into one.
     </p>
     <p>
      Thus,
      <i>
       scone
      </i>
      may derive from the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">
       Middle Dutch
      </a>
      <i>
       schoonbrood
      </i>
      (fine white bread), from
      <i>
       schoon
      </i>
      (pure, clean) and
      <i>
       brood
      </i>
      (bread),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and/or it may also derive from the Scots Gaelic term
      <i>
       sgonn
      </i>
      meaning a shapeless mass or large mouthful. The
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">
       Middle Low German
      </a>
      term
      <i>
       Schnbrot
      </i>
      meaning fine bread may also have played a role in the origination of this word. And if the explanation put forward by Sheila MacNiven Cameron be true, the word may also be based on the town of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scone,_Scotland" title="Scone, Scotland">
       Scone
      </a>
      (

         </span>

         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ) (
      <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
       Scots
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="sco" xml:lang="sco">
       <i>
        Scuin
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">
       Scottish Gaelic
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd">
       <i>
        Sgin
       </i>
      </span>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      , the ancient capital of that country where Scottish monarchs were still crowned, even after the capital was moved to Perth, then to Edinburgh (and on whose
      <a href="/wiki/Stone_of_Scone" title="Stone of Scone">
       Scone Stone
      </a>
      the monarchs of the United Kingdom are still crowned today).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The original scone was round and flat, usually as large as a medium-sized plate. It was made with unleavened oats and baked on a griddle (or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Griddle" title="Griddle">
        girdle
       </a>
      </i>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
       Scots
      </a>
      ), then cut into triangular sections for serving. Today, many would call the large round cake a
      <a href="/wiki/Bannock_(food)" title="Bannock (food)">
       bannock
      </a>
      , and call the triangles
      <i>
       scones
      </i>
      . In Scotland, the words are often used interchangeably.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ingram_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ingram-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When
      <a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">
       baking powder
      </a>
      became available to the masses, scones began to be the oven-baked, well-leavened items we know today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delia_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Delia-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Modern scones are widely available in British and Irish
      <a href="/wiki/Bakery" title="Bakery">
       bakeries
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Grocery_store" title="Grocery store">
       grocery stores
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">
       supermarkets
      </a>
      . A 2005 market report estimated the UK scone market to be worth 64m, showing a 9% increase over the previous five years. The increase is partly due to an increasing consumer preference for impulse and convenience foods.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scones sold commercially are usually round, although some brands are
      <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">
       hexagonal
      </a>
      as this shape may be
      <a href="/wiki/Tessellation" title="Tessellation">
       tessellated
      </a>
      for space efficiency. When prepared at home, they may take various shapes including triangles, rounds and squares.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Baking scones at home is often closely tied to heritage baking. They tend to be made using family recipes rather than recipe books, since it is often a family member who holds the "best" and most-treasured recipe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldman_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldman-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">
       Varieties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Varieties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scone_varieties.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1381" data-file-width="1814" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Scone_varieties.jpg/220px-Scone_varieties.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Scone_varieties.jpg/330px-Scone_varieties.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Scone_varieties.jpg/440px-Scone_varieties.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scone_varieties.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Clockwise from bottom: Hot buttered tattie scones next to a cheese scone, shiny and flat treacle scones, and a milk scone above a fruit scone
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scone_and_Cafe.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Scone_and_Cafe.jpg/220px-Scone_and_Cafe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Scone_and_Cafe.jpg/330px-Scone_and_Cafe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Scone_and_Cafe.jpg/440px-Scone_and_Cafe.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scone_and_Cafe.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Scones with coffee
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      British scones are often lightly sweetened, but may also be
      <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">
       savoury
      </a>
      . They frequently include
      <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">
       raisins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Zante_currant" title="Zante currant">
       currants
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">
       cheese
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Date_palm" title="Date palm">
       dates
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">
       Ulster
      </a>
      , savoury varieties of scone include
      <i>
       soda scones
      </i>
      , also known as soda
      <a href="/wiki/Farl" title="Farl">
       farls
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Potato_scone" title="Potato scone">
       potato scones
      </a>
      , normally known as
      <i>
       tattie scones
      </i>
      , which resemble small, thin savoury
      <a href="/wiki/Pancake" title="Pancake">
       pancakes
      </a>
      made with
      <a href="/wiki/Flour#Other_flours" title="Flour">
       potato flour
      </a>
      . Potato scones are most commonly served fried in a
      <a href="/wiki/Full_breakfast" title="Full breakfast">
       full Scottish breakfast
      </a>
      or an
      <a href="/wiki/Full_breakfast#Ulster" title="Full breakfast">
       Ulster fry
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Griddle_scone" title="Griddle scone">
       griddle scone
      </a>
      (or "girdle scone" in
      <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
       Scots
      </a>
      ) is a variety of scone which is cooked on a griddle (or girdle) on the stove top rather than baked in the oven. This usage is also common in
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      where scones of all varieties form an important part of traditional colonial
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_cuisine" title="New Zealand cuisine">
       New Zealand cuisine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Other common varieties include the
      <i>
       dropped scone
      </i>
      , or
      <i>
       drop scone
      </i>
      , like a pancake, after the method of dropping the batter onto the griddle or frying pan to cook it, and the
      <i>
       lemonade scone
      </i>
      , which is made with lemonade and cream instead of butter and milk. There is also the fruit scone or fruited scone, which contains currants, sultanas, peel and glac cherries, which is just like a plain round scone with the fruit mixed into the dough.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some countries one may also encounter
      <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">
       savoury
      </a>
      varieties of scone which may contain or be topped with combinations of
      <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">
       cheese
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">
       onion
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bacon" title="Bacon">
       bacon
      </a>
      , etc.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variations">
       Regional variations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Regional variations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">
       Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Scones were chosen as the
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">
       Republic of Ireland
      </a>
      representative for
      <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_Europe" title="Caf Europe">
       Caf Europe
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austrian
      </a>
      Presidency of the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      in 2006, while the United Kingdom chose
      <a href="/wiki/Shortbread" title="Shortbread">
       shortbread
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Hungary, a pastry very similar to the British version exists under the name "
      <a href="/wiki/Poga%C4%8Da" title="Pogaa">
       pogaa
      </a>
      ". The name has been adopted by several neighbouring nations' languages. (E.g. Pogatsche in German.) Pogcsa is almost always savoury and served with varied seasonings and toppings, like dill and cheese.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">
       Australia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Australia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">
       Pumpkin
      </a>
      scones, made by adding mashed cooked pumpkin to the dough mixture, had increased exposure during the period when
      <a href="/wiki/Florence_Bjelke-Petersen" title="Florence Bjelke-Petersen">
       Florence Bjelke-Petersen
      </a>
      was in the public eye.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Date scones, which contain chopped dried dates, can also be found in Australia. Another old style of cooking scones, generally in the colder months, is to deep-fry or deep pan-fry them in dripping or oil, when they are called "
      <a href="/wiki/Puftaloon" title="Puftaloon">
       puftaloons
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">
       Americas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Americas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1556" data-file-width="2089" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG/220px-UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG/330px-UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG/440px-UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UU_Scones_with_strawberries_on_a_silver_plate.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        North American style scones with strawberries
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Round British scones can resemble North American
      <a href="/wiki/Biscuit_(bread)" title="Biscuit (bread)">
       biscuits
      </a>
      in appearance, but scones traditionally rely on cold butter, while biscuits are more often made with other kinds of animal fat or vegetable shortening.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldman_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldman-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, while scones are frequently (but not always) sweet, and served with coffee and tea, biscuits are served more as a bread, often with breakfast in the South.
     </p>
     <p>
      In recent years, scones under that name have begun to appear in
      <a href="/wiki/Category:Coffee_houses_of_the_United_States" title="Category:Coffee houses of the United States">
       coffee houses
      </a>
      . They are universally sweet, often containing
      <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">
       fruit
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Blueberry" title="Blueberry">
       blueberries
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">
       raspberries
      </a>
      , or else such flavorings as
      <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">
       cinnamon
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">
       Utah
      </a>
      , the bread products locally called "scones" are similar to Native American
      <a href="/wiki/Frybread" title="Frybread">
       frybread
      </a>
      and are made from a sweet yeast dough, with buttermilk and baking powder and/or soda added, and they are fried rather than baked. They are customarily served with butter and either honey or maple syrup.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scones are quite popular in
      <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
       Argentina
      </a>
      as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
       Uruguay
      </a>
      . They were brought there by Irish, English and Scottish immigrants and by Welsh immigrants in Patagonia (Britons are the third largest foreign community in Argentina).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They are usually accompanied by tea, coffee or
      <a href="/wiki/Mate_(beverage)" title="Mate (beverage)">
       mate
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_usage">
       Other usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other usage">

       </span>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
       Scots
      </a>
      the verb
      <i>
       scon
      </i>
      means to crush flat or beat with the open hand on a flat surface, and "scon-cap" or "scone-cap" refers to a man's broad flat cap or "bunnet".
     </p>
