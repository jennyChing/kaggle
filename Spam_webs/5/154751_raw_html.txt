      <p>
       Engel and his Field Museum colleagues collected 30 of the birds that had died close to shore, most of them red-breasted merganser and white-winged scoter ducks. Some were floating and others were sealed in ice; all of them were emaciated.

    <div class="mod-chitribarticletextwithadcpc mod-chitribarticletext mod-articletext" id="mod-a-body-after-first-para">
     <p>
      Its sort of hard to go out and see  you can practically see them dying, said Engel, who is also an ornithologist.
     </p>
     <p>
      Tens of thousands of diving ducks and other waterfowl spend their winters in the southwest part of Lake Michigan each year, said Douglas Stotz, conservation ecologist and ornithologist at the Field Museum. Most years, they fly from places like Canada and Alaska and spend their time here diving for mollusks and fish without major problems.
     </p>
     <p>
      But then came Chicagos frigid winter, which as recently as last weekend had blanketed about 93 percent of Lake Michigan in ice.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hundreds of waterfowl likely have died as a result, experts say, many more are near starvation and a winter storm  the likes of which Chicago experienced Tuesday night  could put even more at risk.
     </p>
     <p>
      Theyre built to survive and they do survive, Bob Fisher, president of the Illinois Ornithological Society, said of the affected birds, which also include loons and grebes. Under normal conditions, they can handle it, but this year... there just isnt enough access to open water.
     </p>
     <p>
      Elsewhere in the Midwest, illness is another factor being considered. A sudden onset of disease caused by ducks' close quarters in open water is a possibility, Wisconsin wildlife officials recently told the Milwaukee Journal-Sentinel.
     </p>
     <p>
      Whatever the cause or causes, Illinois birding experts and enthusiasts say they havent seen Lake Michigan water-fowl starvation on the scale that theyve seen this season, especially in the past few weeks. But they arent the only ones who have noticed.
     </p>
     <p>
      The problem has shocked recent visitors at sites such as Montrose Point, said Engel, where some people have gone so far as to throw fish out to birds that are still alive.
     </p>
     <p>
      Making matters worse, said Fisher of the state Ornithological Society, is that dead birds that have become noticeable in recent days may have died weeks ago. The remains of more will likely be exposed in the next few weeks, he said.
     </p>
     <p>
      For every one you see, there might be 10 buried in the ice, Fisher said. When the lake kind of really releases a lot of its ice, you may see a lot of duck remains washing up on shore. But then again, a lot of them may become waterlogged and sink to the bottom, and you never see them.
     </p>

    <div class="mod-chitribarticletextwithadcpc mod-chitribarticletext mod-articletext" id="mod-a-body-after-second-para">
     <p>
      Of the dead waterfowl Engel has seen in recent weeks, three-quarters were red-breasted mergansers. Most of the others he saw were the white-winged scoters, which are not usually seen along the lakefront. The scoters typically stay north of Chicago and away from the shore but probably reached the citys harbors in search of open water, said Judy Pollock, director of bird conservation at Audubon Chicago Region.
     </p>
     <p>
      It was kind of a treat to see them this winter, Pollock said of the white-winged scoter, except when some of them started dying.
     </p>
     <p>
      Usually the birds can swim between chunks of ice, but this year more of those gaps froze together and left smaller spaces of open water, Pollock said. That, in turn, prevented them from getting the running start on open water that they need to fly away  something they struggle to do on ice or other solid surfaces  and search for food elsewhere.
     </p>
     <p>
      Several waterfowl traveled inland this winter, staying in the Chicago River, the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal near Lockport and near the Edens Expressway. Their displacement has extended as far as the U.S. South, according to Pollock, who said some birds that typically winter in the Midwest have been spotted along the coast of Louisiana.
     </p>
     <p>
      Inland is often where their troubles can deepen, as the birds may mistake puddles or frozen-over asphalt for small bodies of water, according to Scott Diehl, wildlife manager of the Wisconsin Humane Societys Wildlife Rehabilitation Center in Milwaukee.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometimes the weak, starving birds, in desperation start looking away from Lake Michigan for a new food source, and they mistakenly land on these hard surfaces, Diehl said.
     </p>
     <p>
      The birds are so well adapted to the water  several have legs situated far back on their body  That they struggle to walk on land and cant take off. Each winter people bring five or six such ducks to Diehls rehabilitation center. Since the end of last December, he said, the center has received 88, most 30 or 40 percent below their body weight.
     </p>
