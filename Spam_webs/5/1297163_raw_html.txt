        <div style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/07/14/google-eddystone-bluetooth-beacons/">
          <img alt="" data-credit="Google" data-mep="883818" data-original="http://o.aolcdn.com/hss/storage/midas/5092fba27a38d3979f34349df776e6ce/202301771/google.jpg"/>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="home-loader-img">
         Loading...
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        Using low-energy Bluetooth beacons to serve up location-based info to customers isn't a new development. We've seen more than a few implementations of Apple's
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2014/09/15/macys-stores-get-ibeacons/">
         iBeacon tech
        </a>
        . Google has a new project that employs a similar setup, and it's called
        <a href="http://googledevelopers.blogspot.com/2015/07/lighting-way-with-ble-beacons.html">
         Eddystone
        </a>
        . How does it differ from Apple's Bluetooth initiative? The effort is a "new and open format" that "anyone" can implement. In addition to working alongside a mobile app to beam info to your phone or tablet when you're in a specified location, Eddystone can also broadcast a URL when an app isn't an option. Rather than missing out, a company can choose to send a web link instead. And it works with both Android and iOS devices, too. There are also two location options, meaning developers can choose one of two APIs that will either find and ping a nearby beacon (like in a museum) or send info when you visit a specific location (e.g., latitude/longitude).

       <p>
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2007/09/09/south-park-wins-emmy-for-world-of-warcraft-episode/">
         Emmy voters
        </a>
        won't have to worry about keeping up with the remote for their DVD players for much longer. The Television Academy is teaming up with Google to replace the truckload of physical media sent out each year by giving each eligible member a
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/05/24/chromecast-redesigned-device-view/">
         Chromecast
        </a>
        . "The initiative represents the latest move by the Academy to evolve and enhance the viewing and voting procedures for the Emmy Awards with state-of-the-art technology and methodology," the press release explains. Opting for

Instead of only being able to make selections in four "blue ribbon panels," members can cast their vote for any of the major programming categories alongside professional categories that align with their experience. As you might expect, the streaming will be handled through from the Television Academy's members-only web and mobile apps.

        </a>
        this week, Comcast announced the start of a

        </a>

         here
        </a>

        </a>

        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/07/01/the-cable-box-might-solve-the-internet-of-things-biggest-problem/">
         cable box as a the center of your connected home
        </a>
        . Now, let's see if we can get
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/06/16/shenmue-3-is-really-happening/">
         Yu Suzuki
        </a>
        to launch a Sega Channel Kickstarter.

          <img alt="Google" data-caption="Logotipo de Google en la pared de la recepcin del edificio Gas Works de Dubln." data-credit="Carlos Luna/Flickr" data-mep="883619" data-original="http://o.aolcdn.com/dims-shared/dims3/GLOB/crop/3000x2000+0+0/resize/960x640!/format/jpg/quality/85/http://hss-prod.hss.aol.com/hss/storage/midas/c30ed50159d41c492023e8b73c2e2d27/200131117/2856173673_98ab05e317.jpg"/>

       <p>
        The Internet is unforgiving. Web search engines like
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/06/12/france-cnil-google-right-to-be-forgotten/">
         Google
        </a>
        neatly index the most embarrassing moments, traumatic histories and criminal activities. In May last year, the
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2014/11/29/eu-right-to-be-forgotten-guidelines/">
         European Court of Justice
        </a>
        asked the
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/05/14/google-ico-right-forgotten/">
         web giant
        </a>
        to remove website links that were no longer relevant to people's lives. The ruling recognized that archiving people's lives often took their personal moments out of context, creating "detailed but
        <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/technology/2015/feb/18/the-right-be-forgotten-google-search">
         selective profiles
        </a>
        ". Since the sweeping decision did not exclude killers or even terrorists from the "right to be forgotten", it was largely believed that the requests that poured in were from criminals or public figures looking to erase their pasts. But
        <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/technology/2015/jul/14/google-accidentally-reveals-right-to-be-forgotten-requests?CMP=twt_technology-gdntech">
         <em>
          The Guardian
         </em>
        </a>
        recently discovered data in Google's
        <a href="http://www.google.com/transparencyreport/removals/europeprivacy/?hl=en-US">
         transparency report
        </a>
        that was never meant to be public. An analysis of the source code reveals that 95 percent of the requests came in from ordinary people looking to delist personal information that is irrelevant or is just plain embarrassing.
       </p>

       <p>
        Sony wants you to want
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2014/01/07/playstation-now-hands-on/">
         PS Now
        </a>
        , its Netflix-like game-streaming service. The problem is, the company doesn't seem to know how to build that desire. Though the
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/07/07/playstation-now-subscription-app/">
         recent app relaunch
        </a>
        has seen the service's user interface improve considerably, the same can't be said for the user
        <em>
         experience.
        </em>
        I used PS Now on the
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2013/11/14/sony-playstation-4-review/">
         PlayStation 4
        </a>
        almost exclusively for a week and by the time my seven-day trial was set to expire, I still couldn't find a compelling reason to pay for the service -- let alone recommend it to a friend. With Netflix, the value is apparent: $8 per month for on-demand access to thousands of movies and TV shows. Sure the visual and audio quality of that streaming library might not be on par with its Blu-ray counterparts, but the convenience outweighs any cons. For PS Now, that same trade-off isn't quite worth it.

       <p>
        Hideo Kojima revolutionized the stealth video game genre in 1987 with the release of the original
        <em>
         Metal Gear
        </em>
        , published by Konami. Under Kojima's leadership, the
        <em>
         Metal Gear
        </em>
        franchise exploded in popularity, and it gained fame as a complex, quirky and endlessly innovative series. However,
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/05/20/konami-silent-hills-pt-mega64/">
         recent reports
        </a>
        suggest that Kojima and Konami will sever ties after the release of
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/03/04/metal-gear-solid-v-release-date/">
         <em>
          Metal Gear Solid V: The Phantom Pain
         </em>
        </a>
        in September. A new series of
        <em>
         Phantom Pain
        </em>
        box-art images add fuel to this claim: Konami's initial mock-ups included a credit to Kojima, but
        <a href="https://www.konami.com/download/mgs5thephantompain.php">
         new official images
        </a>
        show the words "A Hideo Kojima Game" have been removed from the top of the box. Plus, the logo for Kojima's studio, Kojima Productions (a subsidiary of Konami), is no longer on the front of the box.

       <p>
        Have one of TiVo's DVRs in the living room, but would rather not get another one of its devices just to watch recordings in the basement? If you use Apple devices at home, you no longer have to. TiVo has updated its
        <a href="https://itunes.apple.com/app/tivo/id401673976?mt=8">
         iOS app
        </a>
        with (long, long overdue) AirPlay mirroring support, so you can send your recorded shows to an
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/03/09/apple-tv-gets-a-new-price-69/">
         Apple TV
        </a>
        as long as everything is on the same local network. You'll need a Premiere, Roamio Plus,
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2013/08/20/tivo-roamio-plus-review/">
         Roamio Pro
        </a>
        or
        <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2012/09/04/tivo-stream-transcoder-review/">
         Stream
        </a>
        device for AirPlay to even be an option, so don't get your hopes up if you're hanging on to old hardware. Still, this a big step forward -- you now have an easy way to catch up on missed episodes without having a TiVo-based set-top attached to your TV of choice.
