             There are a lot of factors that affect the handling of a motorcycle. In addition to design faults by the manufacturer, poor maintenance can turn a reasonable handling bike into a white knuckle ride! And a bad set of tires can transform any bike into a crash without a date!
            </p>
            <p>
             Compiling a list of ten bad handling bikes is easy, but putting them in order is impossible. It would also be doing the manufacturer a disservice as the riders weight/size can make a big difference--especially to a small bike. Nonetheless, the following bikes stand head and shoulders above their contemporaries as evil handling, not for the faint of heart, rides.
            </p>
           </div>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <figure class="content-img content-img-small right-gutter">
             <div class="content-img-wrapper">
              <img alt="Number one on the list of: The 10 Worst Handling Motorcycles of All Time - Image courtesy http://motorbike-search-engine.co.uk" class=" addPinit" data-description="The 10 Worst Handling Motorcycles of All Time: Kawasaki 750 Triple 1V and H2" data-pinit-img-url="http://f.tqn.com/y/classicmotorcycles/1/S/G/5/-/-/Kawasaki-H2-457x309.png" height="202" itemprop="image" src="http://f.tqn.com/y/classicmotorcycles/1/L/G/5/-/-/Kawasaki-H2-457x309.png" width="300"/>
             </div>
             <figcaption class="content-img-subtitle">
              Kawasaki H2 750.
              <span class="credit">
               Image courtesy http://motorbike-search-engine.co.uk
              </span>
             </figcaption>
            </figure>
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              1.
Kawasaki 750 Triple 1V and H2
             </h3>
             <p>
              Easily coming in as number one on the list of the 10 worst handling motorcycles of all time is the Kawasaki 750 Triple 1V and H2. These 748 cc three cylinder 2-strokes were the fastest street bikes of their time in a straight line. Unfortunately, the brakes and handling were considered to be amongst the worst ever designed. The bikes became known as the
              <em>
               widow maker
              </em>
              . Introduced in 1972, the model was dropped from Kawasakis line-up in 1976.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="2" class="adsense-slot adsense" id="adsense1">
           </div>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              2.
Kawawaki 500 H1
             </h3>
             <p>
              Introduced in 1969, these bikes shared common points with their bigger cousins: the later 750s. Poor handling, very powerful, and inadequate brakes; in particular, the power came in on these bikes in a rush. Below 4500 rpm the power was moderate. Above this figure and the front wheel could be elevated in the first three gears!
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              3.
Honda C50, 70, 90, 110
             </h3>
             <p>
              The step through chassisd
              <a href="/od/designersandentrepreneurs/ss/Honda-The-Early-Years.htm">
               Honda
              </a>
              is the best selling bike of all time. First offered in 1958, over 60 million
              <a href="/od/photogalleries/ss/Honda-Classic-Motorcycles.htm">
               Honda C
              </a>
              ubs have been sold since. However, the three speed automatic transmission found on the earlier versions was prone to locking the rear wheel if the rider changed down too quickly. The suspension was also very soft on the earlier versions with poor damping resulting in a pogo stick effect on long bumpy corners.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="2" class="adsense-slot adsense" id="adsense2">
           </div>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              4.
Honda CX 500
             </h3>
             <p>
              This bike suffered from low speed maneuverability problems due to its top heavy design. Produced from 1978 to 1983, the CX 500 became a favorite with many owners. However early UK versions were plagued with a major manufacturing faultthe crankshaft main bearing specifications were incorrect resulting in a major recall. Besides the top heavy handling characteristics, these machines also suffered from major crankshaft rotation related quirks. For instance, if the throttle was closed quickly (in an emergency, for instance) the bike would lean to the right. In addition, the rear wheel on these shaft drive bikes could be easily locked if the rider changed down too quickly.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              5.
Moto Guzzi
             </h3>
             <p>
              Manufacturers have tried endless ways of stopping vibrations from the engine reaching the riderfrom rubber mounted engines (
              <a href="/od/historicaldevelopment/ss/Norton-The-Later-Years.htm">
               Norton
              </a>
              Commando) to handlebar plugs that changed the vibration frequency. To stop this transmission of vibration, Moto Guzzi incorporated a rubber mounting for the
              <a href="/od/historicaldevelopment/ss/Motorcycle-Handlebars.htm">
               handlebars
              </a>
              on some of their earlier models. Unfortunately, any bikes fitted with high-rise handlebars became very unstable. The movement in the mounting gave a vagueness to the steering that made the bike feel like it was wandering.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense" id="adsense3">
           </div>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              6.
Ariel Arrow
             </h3>
             <p>
              Produced from 1958 to 1965, the
              <a href="/od/historicaldevelopment/a/The-Ariel-Leader-And-Arrow.htm">
               Ariel Arrow
              </a>
              was 2-stroke twin with trailing link
              <a href="/od/historicaldevelopment/ss/Development-Of-Motorcycle-Front-Suspension.htm">
               front forks
              </a>
              and a steel pressed back-bone style frame/chassis. Although the Arrow offered reasonable handling, the low mounted mufflers greatly restricted the ground clearance. Riders would often find they were running out of road as the mufflers stopped the bike from being leaned over sufficiently.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              7.
Suzuki GT380/550/750
             </h3>
             <p>
              Sold from 1972 to 1980 (in some countries), the
              <a href="/od/historicaldevelopment/a/Suzuki-GT750.htm">
               GT series from Suzuki
              </a>
              had three problems: they had poor ground clearance due to the muffler location and engine width, the later front disc brakes had poor performance (almost non-existent in the wet) and a very flexible swing arm. Also, the front end tended to oscillate from side to side (tank slappers) under acceleration. The shocks also had soft damping giving the inevitable pogo stick handling effect.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              8.
Husqvarna 250 MX, 1970
             </h3>
             <p>
              Husqvarna produced fast bikes from the beginning, but the handling on some of their MX bikes left a lot to be desired. The 250 of 1970 was fast in a straight line, had state-of-the-art brakes (adequate) but a weak swing arm with poor shocks. The rear end of the bike would be flicked from side to side at the slightest provocation. But possibly the worst design from Husqvarna at this time was the crotch pad. This leather device was designed to stop the rider sliding up the gas tank under heavy breaking; something it accomplished at the expense of extreme pain in delicate areas! Combined with burnt left legs from poor exhaust routing, the Husqvarna experience was truly painful.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              9.
Greeves
             </h3>
             <p>
              Any of the leading link front fork models had one problem: the front tried to come up as the front break was applied. Besides altering the steering geometry in the wrong direction, the front end would lose all of its suspension during breaking. Any large bumps (when riding the MX or
              <a href="/od/classicmotorcycleracing/a/Motorcycle-Trials-For-Vintage-And-Classic-Bikes.htm">
               trial
              </a>
              s bikes, for instance) would be transmitted through the bars to the rider.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
           <section class="list-unit group">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <h3 class="item-heading">
              10.
Harley Davidson Sportster, 1981
             </h3>
             <p>
              With long forks set at a steep angle and a top heavy weight displacement, the Sportsters were fine in a straight line (in fairness, they were designed primarily for this) but lacked handling ability in long corners due to poor suspension. Low speed maneuverability was impaired, too, with the fork/steering geometry.
             </p>
            </div>
           </section>
