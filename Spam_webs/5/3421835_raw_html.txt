       <div class="title-with-sep single-title">
        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         The Pros and Cons of being a World Traveler: Visas
        </h1>
       </div>
      </header>
      <div class="entry-media">
      </div>
      <div class="wrapper">
       <div class="grids">
        <div class="grid-8 column-1">
         <div class="single-box clearfix entry-content" itemprop="articleBody">
          <a id="dd_start">
          </a>
          <p>
           Yesterday I arrived to
           <a href="http://journeywonders.com/is-japan-expensive/" target="_blank" title="Is Japan Expensive? How to travel in Japan on a budget">
            Osaka, Japan
           </a>
           to begin the last leg of my Asian journey and, for the third time in my life as a world traveler, I had to spend more than one hour explaining all of my stamps to the officer in charge of immigration. While some people might find the experience a little bit humiliating and invasive, I enjoyed re-telling the story of the my last few weeks of travel to the officer and his superior. Since my mother doesnt speak English fluently, I was the one talking, explaining and (eventually) showing through my Photos of Wonders, the amazing journey that Ive been having in these years as a world-traveler.
          </p>
          <p>
           During the long train journey from the Osaka Kensai Airport to the city center of Kyoto, I began to reflect about the pros and cons that come with a life of travel, especially those referring to the practical side of traveling, namely, visas, immigration and the good ol scams.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_1051" style="width: 810px">
           <img alt="Autumn Foliage at Kyoto" class="wp-image-1051 size-full" height="600" src="http://cdn.journeywonders.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/img_2909.jpg" width="800"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Autumn Foliage at Kyoto
           </p>
          </div>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #3366ff;">
            <strong>
             Pros: Obtaining Visas
            </strong>
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           As a rule of thumb, the more visas you have on your passport, the less trouble youll have in obtaining visas for other countries. The reason for this is very simple: Foreign countries DO NOT want you to overdo your stay because doing so probably implies that you have somehow found a source of (undeclared and non-taxable) revenue inside their country that allows you to afford staying in it.
          </p>
          <p>
           In these particular cases, your visa-filled passport is your best weapon to prove that you are not planning on working as an illegal in the country and that your interests lie only in sightseeing and learning from their culture. This December Ill return to Mexico and one of the first things Ill do is obtain my second passport since I am extremely close to completing all of its 32 pages and because of a particular stamp I have that might (and will) cause me a lot of trouble when visiting Central Asian countries. You probably already know which country Im talking about, right?
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_649" style="width: 810px">
           <img alt="The Temple Mount" class="wp-image-649 size-full" height="600" src="http://cdn.journeywonders.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/p1160041.jpg" title="The Temple Mount" width="800"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Visiting this place is totally worth all the hassle
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Yes, you guessed it! The only exception to this visa rule is
           <a href="http://journeywonders.com/jerusalem-holy-city/" target="_blank" title="The non-religious guide to Jerusalem">
            Israel and some of its Middle East neighbors
           </a>
           such as Syria, Iran, Lebanon, Yemen, Sudan, Saudi Arabia and Libya. There is absolutely no way in hell (no pun intended) that youll get a visa to these countries if you have an Israel stamp in your passport. Theyll even double-check if you have ever been to Egypt and Jordan since both countries have a land border with Israel and it was a common practice for people to ask the Israel immigration officer to stamp a loose page instead of the passport itself.
          </p>
          <p>
           Note: If you are from a Third-World Latin American country (such as my dear Mexico), it is generally a bad idea to start the paperwork for an U.S.A. Visa if you have no foreign stamps on your passport. A friend from Peru got her U.S.A. Visa denied twice for this same reason. The agent even told her the second time Why dont you visit Rio de Janeiro instead? Shocking indeed.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_3404" style="width: 910px">
           <img alt="The girls of Ipanema beach" class="size-full wp-image-3404" height="675" src="http://cdn.journeywonders.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/The-girls-of-Ipanema-beach.jpg" width="900"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            The girls of Ipanema beach
           </p>
          </div>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #3366ff;">
            <strong>
             Cons: Lengthy Interrogations upon Arrival
            </strong>
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           Even if youre not from a country that is a member of the European Union, traveling within Europe is extremely easy and hassle free. Flights from within EU countries are treated as domestic flights, meaning that you dont have to go through immigrations when traveling, lets say, from Germany to Portugal. The same applies for all sorts of land transportation. Even when traveling from Greek to Turkey by land, the process was extremely simple: You get off the bus, you pay the equivalent of 15 USD for your one-entry Turkish visa, the immigration officer stamps it and you get back to the bus. As easy as that!
          </p>
          <p>
           So, having being used to this, you could probably understand my shock when I was detained for almost two hours at Eilat, Israel after attempting to cross the land border from Egypt (specially since I had to do cross it twice in order to go to
           <a href="http://journeywonders.com/travel-petra-jordan/" target="_blank" title="Petra, the Red Rose city as old as time itself">
            Petra, Jordan
           </a>
           and eventually travel all the way back to Cairo!). Have you ever imagined being asked if your Spanish last name had any Arabic roots? Has it ever occurred to you that the decision of whether accepting you into their country or not relied on answering religious questions correctly? Did you ever dream that your journey to the Middle East would end up requiring you to strip naked in front of the Israel army forces?
          </p>
          <p>
           I kind of wish that someone would include Israel Border: The Ride unto a theme park. I guess it cant be worse that waiting for hours to ride Dumbo once more at Disneyland.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_667" style="width: 810px">
           <img alt="Giza Pyramid Complex" class=" wp-image-667 size-full" height="600" src="http://cdn.journeywonders.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/photo.jpg" title="Giza Pyramid Complex" width="800"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            At the end of the Israel Border, The Ride youll be forced to ride an Egyptian camel for three hours while hordes of souvenir sellers harass you.
           </p>
          </div>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #3366ff;">
            <strong>
             Pros: Awareness of border scams and frauds
            </strong>
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           While this is a negative stereotype, you should always be aware at land borders of Third-World countries. These are, more often than not, understaffed and not so strictly as regulated as the immigration offices found at international airports. The lack of regulation easily allows for corruption to prosper. It is not unusual for them to perform random inspections on obviously foreign tourists and confiscate their personal belongings (cameras, laptops, cellphones) because they forgot to declare them. Of course, a few hundred dollars later and they will look the other way. This happened to me in the land border between Peru and Bolivia this year and, having already read about this particular scam I called them out on it.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Bolivian agents panicked and started to lower the fine. In the end, I paid them the equivalent of 10 USD and rushed to the other side of the border to catch the last bus to Puno. My heart goes out to those innocent tourists that have been the victims of these unscrupulous agents.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_1050" style="width: 810px">
           <img alt="The Bolivian-Peru Land/Sea border" class="wp-image-1050 size-full" height="600" src="http://cdn.journeywonders.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/img_5911.jpg" width="800"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            The Bolivian-Peru Land/Sea border
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           As I said before, this scam is extremely rare (and almost non-existent) at airports due to the strict regulation that can be found there. That is why I really panicked when I was detained for questioning at Santiago, Chile since I only had four hours to catch my connecting flight to Easter Island and I knew that money alone would not help me out this time. Turns out that my South America itinerary was sketchy to the eyes of the immigration agent so I was secluded in a special room for further questioning.
          </p>
          <p>
           I spent one hour explaining how I started traveling from Peru to Bolivia by land, how I flew to Rio de Janeiro, Brazil to relax at its beaches before eventually crossing the triple border of Iguazu, arriving to
           <a href="http://journeywonders.com/goodbye-buenos-aires/" target="_blank" title="How to exchange USD in Buenos Aires and more">
            Buenos Aires, Argentina
           </a>
           for my advertising internship (the fact that such internships did not qualify for a work resident visa sure raised some red flags!) and now heading to Easter Island for a one week vacation. In the end, the agent believed my story but gave me a harsh ultimatum: Either I agree to a full (and frankly, quite invasive) body search or I had to wait 12 hours for her supervisor to arrive.
          </p>
          <p>
           I guess you probably know which option I chose.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_3311" style="width: 910px">
           <img alt="Hanga Roa and the Moais" class="size-full wp-image-3311" height="675" src="http://cdn.journeywonders.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/Hanga-Roa-and-the-Moais.jpg" width="900"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Hanga Roa and the Moais
           </p>
          </div>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #3366ff;">
            <strong>
             A Foreword on the Pros and Cons
            </strong>
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           In the end, I completely understand why the Israeli, Chilean and Japanese immigration officers detained me in each separate occasion: World Travelers are a rare breed. For them, it is very common to receive tourists with a round-trip air ticket from neighboring countries but receiving someone who is traveling on a RTW ticket with sketchy and suspicious countries on its itinerary? Red flag alert!
          </p>
          <p>
           Their main concern is obviously the safety of their own country and they want to avoid any risk of drug smuggling. And no, to this day, I do not believe I have ever been the victim of racial profiling because of my Mexican origin, saying that is an insult to all of the honest immigration officers who are just doing their job because of the love they have for their country.
          </p>
          <p>
           The three occasions that I was detained for questioning had more to do with the itinerary itself, the many foreign stamps in an alphabet other than ours and, of course, the fact that my passport is so worn out by now that the golden logo of the Mexican eagle has vanished, leaving a completely black passport that, lets admit it, looks extremely sketchy.
          </p>
          <p>
           What are your thoughts about this pros and cons? Agree, disagree? Share your opinion in the comments section and participate in the debate!
          </p>

           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            The Gaudi influence in Barcelona
