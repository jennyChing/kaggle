                   BySlade SohmeronJune 2, 2014
                  </h5>
                  <hr/>
                  <div class="author_social_link">
                   <a href="http://twitter.com/sladehv" rel="me nofollow" target="_blank" title="My Twitter">
                    <img alt="Twitter" height="32" src="http://cdn.hypervocal.com/wp-content/plugins/author-box-with-different-description/image/twitter.png" width="32"/>

                   <a href="http://www.youtube.com/sladeny" rel="me nofollow" target="_blank" title="My YouTube">

                 <p>
                  People hate Twitter Clickbait. But we click. We reward. We always click.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Since Twitter became an organized tool for media companies to push out content, many social media editors either have been directed by bosses or have taken a go-getter approach to composing short, punchy tweets with as little information as possible, followed by a link that may or may not answer the question it poses. Frustrating. A bit dickish. Life in 2014.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  To combat this 140-character plague, weve seen the rise of the heroes Twitter deserves, but not the one it needs right now: accounts like
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/HuffPoSpoilers" target="_blank">
                   @HuffPoSpoilers
                  </a>
                  and
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/UpworthySpoiler" target="_blank">
                   @UpworthySpoiler
                  </a>
                  . Now theres a new kid, and it just may be the best yet, with a pointed ferocity and multiple targets:
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick" target="_blank">
                   <strong>
                    @SavedYouAClick
                   </strong>
                  </a>
                  .
                 </p>
                 <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" data-conversation="none" lang="en">
                  <p>
                   Phone, PIN, SSNyou know all of these. RT
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/ABC">
                    @ABC
                   </a>
                   : The 8 numbers identity thieves want to steal from you:
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    Saved You A Click (@SavedYouAClick)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick/statuses/473201148107960320">
                    June 1, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" data-conversation="none" lang="en">
                  <p>
                   A piano. RT
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/HuffingtonPost">
                    @HuffingtonPost
                   </a>
                   : You won't believe what just washed up in the East River
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    Saved You A Click (@SavedYouAClick)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick/statuses/473227552237305858">
                    June 1, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Between the time I interviewed
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick" target="_blank">
                   @SavedYouAClick
                  </a>
                  s white knight,
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/JakeBeckman" target="_blank">
                   Jake Beckman
                  </a>
                  , a Content Catalyst at RebelMouse, the spoiler account picked up more than 9,000 new followers (in two hours). Beckman says he woke up on Sunday morning with about 650 @SavedYouAClick followers and went to bed dreaming of 20,000 as the stock began to rise  it hit that goal today.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  So now that @SavedYouAClick is well on its way to becoming a force, I asked Beckman to expand on why he thinks people are so taken with what hes doing. After Beckman, I circled back with @HuffPoSpoilers founder Alex Mizrahi, who is also Social Media Associate at Blue State Digital, for updates and whether he considers Beckman to be competition. I reached out for comment from a few social media editors too, they weigh in below. Enjoy.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <a class="twitter-follow-button" data-show-count="false" href="https://twitter.com/SladeHV">
                   Follow @SladeHV
                  </a>

                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   Slade Sohmer: What makes a good spoiler tweet? Is it as simple as providing the answer in the clickbait? Or is it more about choosing which clickbait tweet to spoil?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   Jake Beckman:
                  </strong>
                  The first thing I look for is a tweet that poses a question or leaves the reader with a cliffhanger. Usually those can be answered or resolved with just a few words, often as simple as Yes or No. A good spoiler tweet gives the reader the key information that the clickbait tweet lacked, and it does so in a way that shows just how easy it would have been to actually write an informative tweet in the first place.
                 </p>
                 <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
                  <p>
                   He thought Jobs was too domineering" RT
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/businessinsider">
                    @businessinsider
                   </a>
                   : Why the man who invented email attachments refused to work for Steve Jobs
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    Saved You A Click (@SavedYouAClick)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick/statuses/473278737081856000">
                    June 2, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: As we saw with
                   <a href="http://twitter.com/HuffPoSpoilers" target="_blank">
                    @HuffPoSpoilers
                   </a>
                   , people love when an account comes along to spoil these clickbait tweets. But clearly theres a market for clickbait, otherwise they wouldnt be done. Whats your overall take on the issue?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  JB: Twitter is an incredibly effective means of distribution for publishers, but the real beauty of the platform is that
                  <em>
                   its designed for effective communication
                  </em>
                  . Twitter is intended for you to get your point across succinctly and then offer your followers a link if they want more information. Clickbait, on the other hand, gives the reader as little actual information as possible, which makes clicking on the link the only way to actually understand whats being reported.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The reason accounts like @SavedYouAClick and @HuffPoSpoilers are so popular is because clickbait is one of the few remaining truly shared experiences on social media. It doesnt matter who you are or who you follow, youve definitely encountered and been annoyed by clickbait in the past.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The worst clickbait is the misleading or manipulative kind  promising one thing in the tweet and then delivering something only tangentially related in the article. Often, the answer to clickbait tweets can be found right in the headline, which could have fit in the tweet, but publishers feel its more effective to lure readers to their site by posing the headline as a question that begs to be answered.
                  <em>
                   My goal with @SavedYouAClick is to answer those questions for readers, so they dont have to click themselves.
                  </em>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: Whats been the best Twitter reaction so far to your efforts?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  JB: Ive been surprised and humbled by the love being shown for the account. Last night someone said that I was going to destroy the entire business of online media, but it will be so worth it.
                 </p>
                 <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
                  <p>
                   Love
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick">
                    @SavedYouAClick
                   </a>
                   going to destroy the entire business model of online media, but it will be so worth it.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    James Poniewozik (@poniewozik)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/poniewozik/statuses/473257585584635904">
                    June 2, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I dont want to destroy the business, but I do want to help it avoid its more disingenuous tendencies. @xeni said the account was full of benevolent spoilers  I like that.
                 </p>
                 <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
                  <p>
                   Benevolent spoilers:
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick">
                    @SavedYouAClick
                   </a>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    Xeni Jardin (@xeni)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/xeni/statuses/473282196371812352">
                    June 2, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: What was behind the jump from 600 followers to 14,000 overnight?
                   <em>
                    (ed note: The account as of publication jumped to 24K)
                   </em>
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  JB: The jump in followers overnight was really incredible. Ive had some fun and success with a couple hundred followers since starting the account, but to gain thousands and thousands of followers in just a few hours was truly amazing. I think it starts with strong content that people can relate to. A few accounts with tens of thousands of followers somehow found the account yesterday and urged their audience to follow, and it just snowballed from there. At one point I was getting 50 or 60 new followers per minute from all over the world  I was floored.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: Any sites from which youve saved me a click respond yet?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  JB: Not really. Mostly the news organizations ignore what Im doing, I think because its shaming many of their social media practices. Except for this one, just this morning. This was pretty cool, but a genuine mistake on their end:
                 </p>
                 <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
                  <p>
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/SavedYouAClick">
                    @SavedYouAClick
                   </a>
                   OOPS! Geuine mistake
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    HuffPostUK Pictures (@HuffPostUKPics)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/HuffPostUKPics/statuses/473426896609628160">
                    June 2, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: Can you keep this up? Can this go on forever? Have the stamina?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  JB: Ill keep this up as long as publishers keep tweeting clickbait. Im on Twitter every day and follow hundreds of news accounts from both @SavedYouAClick and my personal
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/jakebeckman" target="_blank">
                   @jakebeckman
                  </a>
                  account. Theres a lot of material, and Im just getting started.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  We here at HyperVocal have been
                  <a href="https://twitter.com/hypervocal/status/463771254479413248" target="_blank">
                   on board since Day One
                  </a>
                   So @SavedYouAClick started today. Its like @HuffPoSpoilers, but for everything, and with more wanting to watch the world burn  and having known both Beckman and
                  <a href="http://twitter.com/HuffPoSpoilers" target="_blank">
                   @HuffPoSpoilers
                  </a>
                  creator Mizrahi for some time, I was fortunate enough to watch the creation of this back in March.
                 </p>

                    HuffPo Spoilers (@HuffPoSpoilers)
                   <a href="https://twitter.com/HuffPoSpoilers/statuses/473476659648405504">
                    June 2, 2014
                   </a>
                  </p>
                 </blockquote>
                 <p>
                  <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                  </script>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: When did you start HPS? Has anything changed since the inception?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   Alex Mizrahi:
                  </strong>
                  Started it in August 2012, had 19 followers on the morning of April 25th 2013, 3,000 by the end of the day, 10,000 by the end of the weekend. I dont know who first found it that afternoon but it quickly spread. It was quite the whirlwind weekend.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: Have you heard from any news orgs who dont like it?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  AM: Nope. I had a conversation with someone at HuffPost a few months ago who said he or she [playing it safe here] liked it, but that as an organization, they hated it; this, however, has not been verified. HuffPost did write about it on that day it blew up, and again more recently, so I dont think there was a company-wide edict to avoid me. Then again, they have 4 million+ followers and Im at 35,000, so Im just a gnat to them.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: What is it about clickbait that both drives people insane and actually works?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  AM: I understand the inherent value and necessity of getting people to click on your articles. But theres a reason spoiler Twitter feeds for sites like Gawker, the NY Times, NPR, and yes, even HyperVocal, havent caught on  they all put thought and personality into their tweets, not relying on the same few formulas and tropes the way HP does (
                  <a href="https://medium.com/i-m-h-o/a-glossary-of-words-and-phrases-the-huffington-post-needs-to-ban-from-its-twitter-feed-51eab44b5859" target="_blank">
                   see here for a glossary of these
                  </a>
                  ).
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   SS: How do you feel about other accounts like Jakes? All in this together, all doing good deeds, or is there some competition?
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  AM: I think what Jakes doing is great, were absolutely on the same team fighting the same fight. My @HuffPoSpoilers feed right now is dominated by people saying things along the lines of if you like @huffpospoilers check out @savedyouaclick or conversely, people responding to others tweets about @savedbyaclick with If you like @savedyouaclick, check out @huffpospoilers. His success right now is raising awareness of mine, if tangentially.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The difference between our feeds is, I come at this from a more institutional direction. While over the past few months Ive branched out a bit to spoil the more egregious tweets from other news organizations (@Time, @Slate, @NYTimes, among others), I for the most part stick to Huffington Post, the biggest and most prolific Clickbaiter.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Im not gonna lie though, I am a bit jealous of Jake. One, because @HuffPoSpoilers broke in a time before
                  <a href="http://bits.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/09/13/twitters-magic-recs-experiment-personalizes-what-you-follow/" target="_blank">
                   Magic Recs
                  </a>
                   Ive already had coworkers today say they found out about @SYAC because of that. And two, Ive thought about expanding more and knew that I should  it would be the next logical step  but I never did. And Im not sure how long Id even be able to keep that going before getting burnt out; even just with Huffington Post, Ive had stretches where Ive wondered why I even bothered to keep going. So I hope hes able to stick with it.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  With @SYAC blowing up over the weekend, Im a bit afraid that Im going to end up the Pixies to his Nirvana, but Im also proud that I can claim a minor part in its formation (as can you!). And I kind of like the idea of passing the mantle on and letting him run with it. Well see where things go from here.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <a href="http://cdn.hypervocal.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/jake-alex.jpg">
                   <img alt="jake alex" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-137826" height="621" src="http://cdn.hypervocal.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/jake-alex.jpg" width="800"/>
                  </a>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I also reached out to some social media editors for comment, none of which wanted to speak on the record. All three seemed to enjoy spoilers on a personal note, and not care all that much about the business ramifications, but there was one piece of criticism that from a high-profile corporate media tweeter that did ring true: I get the point of it, but its annoying that they DONT keep the link in their the tweets they spoil, this editor told me. Like the more information I get, I just want to click it more  a decent story will never be replaced by a spoilery tweet. But Im probably not their target audience.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Beckman responded to me:
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I take out the original links for two reasons: one, for space (140 characters plus a link isnt a lot of room); but more importantly, I take it out because I dont want to reward news organizations with increased reach on, and engagement with, the content. My idea is that you can effectively convey the information in a tweet without forcing the user to click out to learn more. Its a 100% conscious decision on my part. I bet theyd like me to tweet out their links with context, but Id prefer it if they just did that in the first place.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  With any other trend, spoiler accounts will come, and go, and ebb, and flow, and trend pieces will be written like this, and again when the next one rises, but you better believe youll be seeing Clickbait for the rest of your lives.
                 </p>
