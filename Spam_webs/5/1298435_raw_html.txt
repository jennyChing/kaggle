     <p>
      <b>
       Apocalyptic fiction
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenre" title="Subgenre">
       subgenre
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
       science fiction
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">
       horror fiction
      </a>
      that is concerned with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_catastrophic_risks" title="Global catastrophic risks">
       end of human civilization
      </a>
      . This apocalypse is typically portrayed as being due to a potentially
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existential_risk" title="Existential risk">
       existential catastrophe
      </a>
      such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zombie_apocalypse" title="Zombie apocalypse">
       zombie apocalypse
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">
       nuclear warfare
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">
       pandemic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Alien_invasion" title="Alien invasion">
       extraterrestrial attack
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">
       impact event
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cybernetic_revolt" title="Cybernetic revolt">
       cybernetic revolt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">
       technological singularity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dysgenics" title="Dysgenics">
       dysgenics
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernatural_phenomena" title="Supernatural phenomena">
       supernatural phenomena
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Eschatology" title="Eschatology">
       divine judgment
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">
       runaway climate change
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">
       resource depletion
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_collapse" title="Ecological collapse">
       ecological collapse
      </a>
      , or some other
      <a href="/wiki/Disaster" title="Disaster">
       general disasters
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Post-apocalyptic fiction
      </b>
      is set in a world or civilization after such a disaster that ruins the world. The time frame may be immediately after the catastrophe, focusing on the travails or psychology of survivors, or considerably later, often including the theme that the existence of pre-catastrophe civilization has been forgotten (or mythologized). Post-apocalyptic stories often take place in a non-technological future world, or a world where only scattered elements of society and technology remain.
     </p>
     <p>
      The genre gained popularity after
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , when the possibility of global annihilation by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">
       nuclear weapons
      </a>
      entered the public consciousness. However, recognizable apocalyptic novels had existed since the first quarter of the 19th century, when
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">
       Mary Shelley
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Man" title="The Last Man">
        The Last Man
       </a>
      </i>
      was published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLM_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TLM-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_predecessors">
       Ancient predecessors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apocalyptic_and_post-apocalyptic_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient predecessors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Numerous societies, including the Babylonian and Judaic, had produced
      <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_literature" title="Apocalyptic literature">
       apocalyptic literature
      </a>
      and mythology which dealt with the end of the world and of human society.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoAF9_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EoAF9-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">
       Epic of Gilgamesh
      </a>
      , written ca. 20001500 BC, details a myth where the angry gods send floods to punish humanity, but the ancient hero
      <a href="/wiki/Utnapishtim" title="Utnapishtim">
       Utnapishtim
      </a>
      and his family are saved through the intervention of the god
      <a href="/wiki/Enki" title="Enki">
       Ea
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">
       scriptural
      </a>
      story of
      <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">
       Noah
      </a>
      and his Ark describes the end of a corrupt civilization and its replacement with a remade world. Noah is assigned the task to build the Ark and save the lifeforms so as to reestablish a new post-flood world.
     </p>
     <p>
      A similar story about the Genesis flood narrative is found in Sura 71 of the
      <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">
       Quran
      </a>
      , where the Islamic counterpart of Noah, N ( ), builds the ark and rebuilds humanity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even in the
      <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">
       Hindu
      </a>
      Dharmasastra, the apocalyptic deluge plays a prominent part. According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Matsya_Purana" title="Matsya Purana">
       Matsya Purana
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Matsya" title="Matsya">
       Matsya
      </a>
      avatar of Lord
      <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">
       Vishnu
      </a>
      , informed the King Manu of an all-destructive deluge which would be coming very soon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MATSYA_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MATSYA-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The King was advised to build a huge boat (ark) which housed his family, nine types of seeds, pairs of all animals and the
      <a href="/wiki/Saptarishi" title="Saptarishi">
       Saptarishis
      </a>
      to repopulate the Earth, after the deluge would end and the oceans and seas would recede. At the time of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deluge_myth" title="Deluge myth">
       deluge
      </a>
      , Vishnu appeared as a horned fish and
      <a href="/wiki/Shesha" title="Shesha">
       Shesha
      </a>
      appeared as a rope, with which
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaivasvata_Manu" title="Vaivasvata Manu">
       Vaivasvata Manu
      </a>
      fastened the boat to the horn of the fish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Variants of this story also appear in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">
       Buddhist
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jain" title="Jain">
       Jain
      </a>
      scriptures.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first centuries AD saw the creation of various apocalyptic works; the best known is the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
       Book of Revelation
      </a>
      (from which the word
      <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse" title="Apocalypse">
       apocalypse
      </a>
      originated, meaning "revelation of secrets"), which is filled with prophecies of destruction, as well as luminous visions. In the first chapter of Revelation, the author St. John the Divine explains his divine errand: Write the things which thou hast seen, the things which are, and the things which shall be hereafter (Rev. 1:19). He takes it as his mission to conveyto revealto Gods kingdom His promise that justice will prevail and that the suffering will be vindicated (Leigh). The apocalyptist provides a beautific vision of Judgement Day, revealing Gods promise for redemption from suffering and strife.
      <i>
       Revelation
      </i>
      describes a New Heaven and a New Earth, and its intended audience (Christians under the persecution of the Roman Empire, for whom John wrote the letters that constitute the book) were enchanted and inspired, rather than terrified by visions of Judgment Day. These Christians believed themselves chosen for Gods salvation, and so such apocalyptic sensibilities inspired optimism and nostalgia for the end times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Shelley" title="Mary Shelley">
       Mary Shelley
      </a>
      's 1826 novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Man" title="The Last Man">
        The Last Man
       </a>
      </i>
      , is often considered the first work of modern apocalyptic fiction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TLM_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TLM-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The story follows a group of people as they struggle to survive in a plague-infected world. The story centers on a male protagonist as he struggles to keep his family safe but is inevitably left as the last man alive. However, Shelley's novel is predated by
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Cousin_de_Grainville" title="Jean-Baptiste Cousin de Grainville">
       Jean-Baptiste Cousin de Grainville
      </a>
      's French novel
      <a href="/wiki/Le_Dernier_Homme" title="Le Dernier Homme">
       Le Dernier Homme
      </a>
      (English:
      <i>
       The Last Man
      </i>
      (1805)). Published after his death in 1805, de Grainville's novel follows the character of Omegarus, the titular "last man," in what is essentially a retelling of the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
       Book of Revelation
      </a>
      , combined with themes of the story of
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">
       Adam and Eve
      </a>
      . Unlike most apocalyptic tales, de Grainville's novel approaches the end of the world not as a cautionary tale, or a tale of survival, but as both an inevitable, as well as necessary, step for the spiritual resurrection of mankind.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">
       Edgar Allan Poe
      </a>
      's 1839 short story
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Conversation_of_Eiros_and_Charmion" title="The Conversation of Eiros and Charmion">
        The Conversation of Eiros and Charmion
       </a>
      </i>
      follows the conversation between two souls in the afterlife as they discuss the destruction of the world. The destruction was brought about by a comet that removed nitrogen from Earth's atmosphere; this left only oxygen and resulted in a worldwide inferno. The 1885 novel
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/After_London" title="After London">
        After London
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Jefferies" title="Richard Jefferies">
       Richard Jefferies
      </a>
      can best be described as genuine post-apocalyptic fiction. After a sudden and unspecified catastrophe has depopulated England, the countryside reverts to nature and the few survivors return to a quasi-medieval way of life. The first chapters consist solely of a description of nature reclaiming England: fields becoming overrun by forest, domesticated animals running wild, roads and towns becoming overgrown,
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      reverting to lake and poisonous swampland. The rest of the story is a straightforward adventure/quest set many years later in the wild landscape and society, but the opening chapters set an example for many later science fiction stories.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.G._Wells" title="H.G. Wells">
       H.G. Wells
      </a>
      wrote several novels that have a post-apocalyptic theme. Published in 1895, Wells' short novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Time_Machine" title="The Time Machine">
        The Time Machine
       </a>
      </i>
      has the unnamed protagonist traveling to the year 802,701 A.D. after civilization has collapsed and humanity has split into two distinct species, the elfin Eloi and the brutal Morlocks. Later in the story, the time traveler moves forward to a dying Earth beneath a swollen, red sun. Wells' 1898 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds" title="The War of the Worlds">
        The War of the Worlds
       </a>
      </i>
      depicts an invasion of Earth by inhabitants of the planet
      <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
       Mars
      </a>
      . The aliens systematically destroy Victorian England with advanced weaponry mounted on nearly indestructible vehicles. Due to the famous
      <a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(radio_drama)" title="The War of the Worlds (radio drama)">
       radio adaptation
      </a>
      of the novel by
      <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">
       Orson Welles
      </a>
      on his show,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Mercury_Theatre_on_the_Air" title="The Mercury Theatre on the Air">
        The Mercury Theatre on the Air
       </a>
      </i>
      , the novel has become one of the best known early apocalyptic works. It has subsequently been
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptations_of_The_War_of_the_Worlds" title="Adaptations of The War of the Worlds">
       reproduced or adapted
      </a>
      several times in film,
      <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
       television programming
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Radio_programming" title="Radio programming">
       radio programming
      </a>
      , music, and
      <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
       video games
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="War">
       War
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apocalyptic_and_post-apocalyptic_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: War">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_in_popular_culture" title="Nuclear weapons in popular culture">
       Nuclear holocaust in popular culture
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_nuclear_holocaust_fiction" title="List of nuclear holocaust fiction">

      <a href="/wiki/World_War_III_in_popular_culture" title="World War III in popular culture">

     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Novels and short stories
      </b>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Vincent_Ben%C3%A9t" title="Stephen Vincent Bent">
       Stephen Vincent Bent
      </a>
      's 1937 story
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/By_the_Waters_of_Babylon" title="By the Waters of Babylon">
        By the Waters of Babylon
       </a>
      </i>
      (originally titled
      <i>
       The Place of the Gods
      </i>
      ), a young man explores the ruins of a city in the northeastern United States, possibly
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York
      </a>
      , generations after a war in which future weapons caused "The Great Burning." In 1987, Paul Brians published
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Holocausts:_Atomic_War_in_Fiction" title="Nuclear Holocausts: Atomic War in Fiction">
        Nuclear Holocausts: Atomic War in Fiction
       </a>
      </i>
      , a study that examines atomic war in short stories, novels, and films between 1895 and 1984. Since this measure of destruction was no longer imaginary, some of these new works, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Mordecai_Roshwald" title="Mordecai Roshwald">
       Mordecai Roshwald
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Level_7_(Roshwald_novel)" title="Level 7 (Roshwald novel)">
        Level 7
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nevil_Shute" title="Nevil Shute">
       Nevil Shute
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/On_the_Beach_(novel)" title="On the Beach (novel)">
        On the Beach
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pat_Frank" title="Pat Frank">
       Pat Frank
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alas,_Babylon" title="Alas, Babylon">
        Alas, Babylon
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_McCammon" title="Robert McCammon">
       Robert McCammon
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Swan_Song_(novel)" title="Swan Song (novel)">
        Swan Song
       </a>
      </i>
      , shun the imaginary science and technology that are the identifying traits of general science fiction. Others include more fantastic elements, such as mutants,
      <a href="/wiki/Alien_invasion" title="Alien invasion">
       alien invaders
      </a>
      , or exotic future weapons such as
      <a href="/wiki/James_Axler" title="James Axler">
       James Axler
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Deathlands" title="Deathlands">
        Deathlands
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A seminal work in this subgenre was
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_M._Miller,_Jr." title="Walter M. Miller, Jr.">
       Walter M. Miller, Jr.
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Canticle_for_Leibowitz" title="A Canticle for Leibowitz">
        A Canticle for Leibowitz
       </a>
      </i>
      (1959), in which a
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/recrudescent" title="wiktionary:recrudescent">
       recrudescent
      </a>
      Catholic Church, pseudo-medieval society, and rediscovery of the knowledge of the pre-holocaust world are central themes. Russell Hoban's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Riddley_Walker" title="Riddley Walker">
        Riddley Walker
       </a>
      </i>
      (1980) also has religious or mystical themes, while
      <a href="/wiki/Orson_Scott_Card" title="Orson Scott Card">
       Orson Scott Card
      </a>
      's post-apocalyptic anthology
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Folk_of_the_Fringe" title="The Folk of the Fringe">
        The Folk of the Fringe
       </a>
      </i>
      deals with American Mormons after a nuclear war. According to some theorists, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bombing_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">
       atomic bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki
      </a>
      in 1945 has influenced
      <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Japan#Popular_culture" title="Culture of Japan">
       Japanese popular culture
      </a>
      to include many apocalyptic themes. Much of Japan's
      <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">
       manga
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">
       anime
      </a>
      are filled with apocalyptic imagery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most notably, the 1954 film
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gojira_(1954_film)" title="Gojira (1954 film)">
        Gojira
       </a>
      </i>
      (romanized as
      <a href="/wiki/Godzilla" title="Godzilla">
       Godzilla
      </a>
      ) depicted the title monster as an analogy for nuclear weapons, something Japan experienced first-hand.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Judith_Merril" title="Judith Merril">
       Judith Merril
      </a>
      's first novel
      <i>
       Shadow on the Hearth
      </i>
      , published in 1950, is a key title in the history of the post-nuclear holocaust genre. It is one of the earliest post WWII novels to deal with a post-nuclear-holocaust world. The novel recounts the ordeals of a young suburban housewife and mother of two children as she struggles to survive in a world forever changed by the horrors of a nuclear attack. Several of
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">
       Ray Bradbury
      </a>
      's short stories of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Martian_Chronicles" title="The Martian Chronicles">
        The Martian Chronicles
       </a>
      </i>
      take place before, during, and after a nuclear war on Earth. The people flee Earth and settle on Mars but have constant conflicts with the native Martians. Several of these stories have been adapted to other media. Published in 1952,
      <a href="/wiki/Andre_Norton" title="Andre Norton">
       Andre Norton
      </a>
      s
      <i>
       Star Mans Son
      </i>
      (also known as
      <i>
       Daybreak 2250
      </i>
      ), is an early post-nuclear-war novel that follows a young man, Fors, in search of lost knowledge. Fors begins his Arthurian quest through a radiation-ravaged landscape with the aid of a telepathic mutant cat. He encounters mutated creatures called "the beast things," which are possibly a degenerate form of humans.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">
       Poul Anderson
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Maurai" title="Maurai">
        Maurai
       </a>
      </i>
      series (19591983) takes place after a nuclear war, and his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugo_award" title="Hugo award">
       Hugo
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Award" title="Prometheus Award">
       Prometheus
      </a>
      award-winning story
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_Truce_With_Kings" title="No Truce With Kings">
        No Truce With Kings
       </a>
      </i>
      takes place after a cataclysmic war. Both show the interactions among various kinds of societies that have developed in the centuries of recovery.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Heinlein" title="Robert Heinlein">
       Robert Heinlein
      </a>
      's 1964 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Farnham%27s_Freehold" title="Farnham's Freehold">
        Farnham's Freehold
       </a>
      </i>
      follows the story of a group of people that have survived a nuclear explosion. The group survives the attack in a
      <a href="/wiki/Fallout_shelter" title="Fallout shelter">
       fallout shelter
      </a>
      but are taken to a future in which Africans rule.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Damnation_Alley" title="Damnation Alley">
        Damnation Alley
       </a>
      </i>
      is a 1967 science fiction novella by
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">
       Roger Zelazny
      </a>
      , which he expanded into a novel in 1969. A
      <a href="/wiki/Damnation_Alley_(film)" title="Damnation Alley (film)">
       film adaptation
      </a>
      of the novel was released in 1977.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Harlan_Ellison" title="Harlan Ellison">
       Harlan Ellison
      </a>
      s short story
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Boy_and_His_Dog" title="A Boy and His Dog">
        A Boy and His Dog
       </a>
      </i>
      takes place in a world desolated by the nuclear warfare in World War III. It was adapted into
      <a href="/wiki/A_Boy_and_His_Dog_(1975_film)" title="A Boy and His Dog (1975 film)">
       a film of the same name
      </a>
      as well as a companion graphic novel titled
      <i>
       Vic and Blood
      </i>
      . In 2003, children's novelist
      <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_DuPrau" title="Jeanne DuPrau">
       Jeanne DuPrau
      </a>
      released
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_City_of_Ember" title="The City of Ember">
        The City of Ember
       </a>
      </i>
      , which was the first of four books in a post-apocalyptic series for
      <a href="/wiki/Young-adult_fiction" title="Young-adult fiction">
       young adults
      </a>
      . A film adaption,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/City_of_Ember" title="City of Ember">
        City of Ember
       </a>
      </i>
      , has since been made starring
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">
       Bill Murray
      </a>

       Saoirse Ronan
      </a>
      . In the
      <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">
       anime
      </a>
      film and

      </a>

     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/William_W._Johnstone" title="William W. Johnstone">

      wrote a series of books between 1983 and 2003 (35 books all containing the word "Ashes" in the title) about the aftermath of worldwide nuclear and biological war. Published in 1970,

      .

      <i>

       </a>

The games are usually based around "vaults," safe underground bunkers for long-term survival, and exploring the outside wasteland, in locations such as
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_DC" title="Washington DC">
       Washington DC
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retro" title="Retro">
       retro
      </a>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Metro_2033_(video_game)" title="Metro 2033 (video game)">
        Metro 2033
       </a>
      </i>
      , a nuclear war occurs in late 2013. Russia was targeted with atomic bombs, causing severe radiation across Moscow, forcing the rest of the people to live underground in the metro stations away from the deadly effects of radiation. Many animals and humans left behind mutated into creatures known as the Dark Ones, who were left outside for the next 20 years. The game is played from the perspective of Artyom, a 20-year-old male survivor and one of the first born in the metro. The story takes place in post-apocalyptic Moscow, mostly inside the metro system, but some missions have the player go to the surface which is severely irradiated and a gas mask must be worn at all times due to the toxic air.

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">
       E. M. Forster
      </a>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Machine_Stops" title="The Machine Stops">
        The Machine Stops
       </a>
      </i>
      , humanity has been forced underground due to inhospitable conditions on Earth's surface, and is entirely dependent on "the machine," a god-like mechanical entity which has supplanted almost all
      <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">
       free will
      </a>
      by providing for humankind's every whim. The machine deteriorates and eventually stops, ending the lives of all those dependent upon it, though one of the dying alludes to a group of humans dwelling on the surface who will carry the torch of humanity into the future. In
      <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Barjavel" title="Ren Barjavel">

       German occupation of France
      </a>
      , a future France is devastated by the sudden failure of
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      , causing chaos, disease, and famine, with a small band of survivors desperately struggling for survival.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Boyett" title="Steve Boyett">

      <a href="/wiki/The_Borderland_Series" title="The Borderland Series">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faery" title="Faery">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/S._M._Stirling" title="S. M. Stirling">
       S. M. Stirling
      </a>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dies_the_Fire" title="Dies the Fire">

       </a>
      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>

       gunpowder
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">

      </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cosy_catastrophe">
       Cosy catastrophe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apocalyptic_and_post-apocalyptic_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cosy catastrophe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The "cosy catastrophe" is a name given to a style of post-apocalyptic science fiction that was particularly prevalent after
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      among
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       British
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
       science fiction
      </a>
      writers. A "cosy catastrophe" is typically one in which civilization (as we know it) comes to an end and everyone is killed except for the main characters, who survive relatively unscathed and are then freed from the prior constraints of civilization. The term was coined by
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Aldiss" title="Brian Aldiss">
       Brian Aldiss
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       Billion Year Spree: The History of Science Fiction
      </i>
      (1973). Aldiss was directing his remarks at English author
      <a href="/wiki/John_Wyndham" title="John Wyndham">
       John Wyndham
      </a>
      , especially his novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Day_of_the_Triffids" title="The Day of the Triffids">
        The Day of the Triffids
       </a>
      </i>
      , whose protagonists were able to enjoy a relatively comfortable existence with little associated hardship or danger despite the fall of society.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalan_people" title="Catalan people">
       Spanish Catalan
      </a>
      author
      <a href="/wiki/Manuel_de_Pedrolo" title="Manuel de Pedrolo">
       Manuel de Pedrolo
      </a>
      wrote
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mecanoscrit_del_segon_origen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mecanoscrit del segon origen (page does not exist)">
        Mecanoscrit del segon origen
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Typescript of the Second Origin
      </i>
      ), published in 1974. Two children accidentally survive an alien holocaust that eradicates all life on Earth. They take up the mission of preserving human culture and repopulating the Earth.
     </p>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Ayn_Rand" title="Ayn Rand">
       Ayn Rand
      </a>
      's novella
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Anthem_(novella)" title="Anthem (novella)">
        Anthem
       </a>
      </i>
      (1938) society has entered a near-medieval state after a new government forbids any kind of individual thought, even forbidding the words "I" and "me." In
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
       Arthur C. Clarke
      </a>
      's short story
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Nine_Billion_Names_of_God" title="The Nine Billion Names of God">
        The Nine Billion Names of God
       </a>
      </i>
      (1953), the universe ends when Tibetan monks (making use of a specially-written computer program) finish writing all of the nine billion possible names of God. The story won a retrospective
      <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">
       Hugo Award
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Earth_Caught_Fire" title="The Day the Earth Caught Fire">
        The Day the Earth Caught Fire
       </a>
      </i>
      (1961) is a film by
      <a href="/wiki/Val_Guest" title="Val Guest">
       Val Guest
      </a>
      about an Earth thrown out of its orbit around the sun by excessive nuclear testing. It paints a picture of a society ready to believe that humans could destroy the planet, hoping that science could fix what it has broken but resigned to the possibility of irreversible doom.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/John_Crowley" title="John Crowley">
       John Crowley
      </a>
      's 1979 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Engine_Summer" title="Engine Summer">
        Engine Summer
       </a>
      </i>
      takes place perhaps a thousand years after "the Storm" (not described) destroyed industrial civilization. Surviving cultures seem to be influenced by the 1960s and 1970s
      <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">
       counterculture
      </a>
      . The manga and anime
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/X_(manga)" title="X (manga)">
        X
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Clamp_(manga_artists)" title="Clamp (manga artists)">
       Clamp
      </a>
      features a
      <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">
       supernatural
      </a>
      apocaylpse. In it there is a battle over the end of the world between the "Dragons of Heaven" who wish to save humanity, and the "Dragons of Earth" who wish to wipe out humanity. The central character,
      <a href="/wiki/Kamui_Shir%C5%8D" title="Kamui Shir">
       Kamui Shir
      </a>
      , has to choose which side to fight for. The manga began in 1992 and has been on hiatus since 2003. It has been adapted as an anime film in 1996 and an anime TV series between 20012002.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cormac_McCarthy" title="Cormac McCarthy">
       Cormac McCarthy
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Road" title="The Road">
        The Road
       </a>
      </i>
      (2006) takes place several years after an unspecified cataclysm that forces a father and son to perpetually search for survival. It was
      <a href="/wiki/The_Road_(2009_film)" title="The Road (2009 film)">
       adapted into a film
      </a>
      in 2009. James Wesley Rawles'
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/James_Wesley_Rawles" title="James Wesley Rawles">
        Survivors: A Novel of the Coming Collapse
       </a>
      </i>
      (2011) is a novel about a contemporaneous global economic crash, and focuses on the struggles of a large cast of characters who struggle to survive after what is termed "The Crunch." It covers both the lead up to the economic crash, as well as several years after the crash.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Reed_(author)" title="Robert Reed (author)">
       Robert Reed
      </a>
      's short story
      <i>
       Pallbearer
      </i>
      (2010) deals with most of the developed world's population dying after a mass vaccination program in which the vaccines were purposefully tainted. The survivors are those who were not vaccinated, often for religious reasons, and their descendants. Most of the developing world does not receive the vaccine, and decades later, large numbers of its refugees are arriving to America's shores. The protagonist survives the disaster as a young boy and has a chance encounter with an elderly scientist and her fanatical younger family members. In
      <a href="/wiki/Ubisoft" title="Ubisoft">
       Ubisoft
      </a>
      's 2012 videogame
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/I_Am_Alive" title="I Am Alive">
        I Am Alive
       </a>
      </i>
      , America has gone through a massive earthquake that destroys most cities and areas. Due to the damage of the aftermath, many people are forced to go without resources, causing citizens to become agitated, violent, and bitter, turning them into savage hunters.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many hard rock, heavy metal, and punk bands have post-apocalyptic themes and imagery in their lyrics. Bands with these themes include
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Assault" title="Nuclear Assault">
       Nuclear Assault
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sodom_(band)" title="Sodom (band)">
       Sodom
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Slayer" title="Slayer">
       Slayer
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">
       The Cure
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nine_Inch_Nails" title="Nine Inch Nails">
       Nine Inch Nails
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Misfits_(band)" title="Misfits (band)">
       The Misfits
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Smashing_Pumpkins" title="The Smashing Pumpkins">
       The Smashing Pumpkins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">
       The Doors
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">
       Metallica
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">
       Radiohead
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/R.E.M." title="R.E.M.">
       R.E.M.
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Manson" title="Marilyn Manson">
       Marilyn Manson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/System_of_a_Down" title="System of a Down">
       System of a Down
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">
       The Clash
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arcade_Fire" title="Arcade Fire">
       Arcade Fire
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_apocalyptic_songs" title="List of apocalyptic songs">
       various songs across genres
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">
       Muse
      </a>
      's 2012 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_2nd_Law" title="The 2nd Law">
        The 2nd Law
       </a>
      </i>
      was inspired by post-apocalyptic life in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_Z" title="World War Z">
        World War Z
       </a>
      </i>
      , and the event is referred to specifically in the song "
      <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Please" title="Apocalypse Please">
       Apocalypse Please
      </a>
      " (2003). The music video for the song "
      <a href="/wiki/This_Corrosion" title="This Corrosion">
       This Corrosion
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/The_Sisters_of_Mercy" title="The Sisters of Mercy">
       The Sisters of Mercy
      </a>
      takes place in a post-apocalyptic setting.
     </p>
