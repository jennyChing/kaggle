      <b>
       Hundun
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">

       </span>
      </i>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="WadeGiles">
       WadeGiles
      </a>

       <span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile" xml:lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">

      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">

      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_creation_myth" title="Chinese creation myth">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/World_egg" title="World egg">
       World egg
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">
       Linguistics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
       was semantically extended from a mythic "primordial chaos; nebulous state of the universe before heaven and earth separated" to mean "unintelligible; chaotic; messy; mentally dense; innocent as a child".
     </p>
     <p>
      In modern
      <a href="/wiki/Written_Chinese" title="Written Chinese">
       Written Chinese
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       hndn
      </i>
      "primordial chaos" is , but
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_classic_texts" title="Chinese classic texts">
       Chinese classic texts
      </a>
      wrote it either  ( in the Daoist classic
      <i>
       Zhuangzi
      </i>
      , etc.) or  (
      <i>
       Zuozhuan
      </i>
      ).
      <i>
       Hn
      </i>
      "chaos; muddled; confused" is written either
      <i>
       hn
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B7%B7" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      "abundantly flowing; turbid water; torrent; mix up/in; confuse; muddle through; drift along; thoughtless; senseless" or
      <i>
       hn
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B8%BE" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_character" title="Simplified Chinese character">
       Simplified Chinese character
      </a>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B5%91" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      ) "sound of running water; muddy; muddled; turbid; concealed; confused; dull; stupid; unsophisticated; whole; all over". These two are interchangeable graphic variants readable as
      <i>
       hn
      </i>
       "muddy; dirty; filthy" (e.g.,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandarin_slang" title="Mandarin slang">
       Mandarin slang
      </a>
      <i>
       hndn
      </i>
      / "filthy egg"; bastard; scumbag") and
      <i>
       hn
      </i>
       "nebulous; stupid" (
      <i>
       hndn
      </i>
      ).
      <i>
       Dn
      </i>
      "dull; confused" is written either
      <i>
       dn
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B2%8C" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      "dull; confused; stupid" or
      <i>
       dn
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%95%A6" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      "thick; solid; generous; earnest; honest; sincere".
     </p>
     <p>
      Isabelle Robinet outlines the etymological origins of
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Semantically, the term
       <i>
        hundun
       </i>
       is related to several expressions, hardly translatable in Western languages, that indicate the void or a barren and primal immensity  for instance,
       <i>
        hunlun
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hundong
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        kongdong
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        menghong
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        hongyuan
       </i>
       . It is also akin to the expression "something confused and yet complete" (
       <i>
        huncheng
       </i>
       ) found in the
       <i>
        Daode jing
       </i>
       25, which denotes the state prior to the formation of the world where nothing is perceptible, but which nevertheless contains a cosmic seed. Similarly, the state of
       <i>
        hundun
       </i>
       is likened to an egg; in this usage, the term alludes to a complete world round and closed in itself, which is a receptacle like a cavern (
       <i>
        dong
       </i>
       ) or a gourd (
       <i>
        hu
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        hulu
       </i>
       ). (2007:524)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wonton_1.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Wonton_1.JPG/220px-Wonton_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Wonton_1.JPG/330px-Wonton_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Wonton_1.JPG/440px-Wonton_1.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wonton_1.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A shrimp
        <a href="/wiki/Wonton" title="Wonton">
         wonton
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Most
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">
       Chinese characters
      </a>
      are written using "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese_character)" title="Radical (Chinese character)">
       radicals
      </a>
      " or "semantic elements" and "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic" title="Phonetic">
       phonetic
      </a>
      elements".
      <i>
       Hndn
      </i>
       is written with the "water radical"  or and phonetics of
      <i>
       kn
      </i>
       and
      <i>
       tn
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Hndn
      </i>
      "primordial chaos" is
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognate
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Wonton" title="Wonton">
       Huntun
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       hntun
      </i>
      , , ) "wonton; dumpling soup" written with the "eat radical" . Note that the English
      <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
       loanword
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wonton" title="Wonton">
        wonton
       </a>
      </i>
      is borrowed from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">
       Cantonese
      </a>
      pronunciation
      <i>
       wan
       <sup>
        4
       </sup>
       tan
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
      </i>
      . Mair (1994:16) explains
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       wonton
      </i>
      , "The undifferentiated soup of primordial chaos. As it begins to differentiate, dumpling-blobs of matter coalesce.  With the evolution of human consciousness and reflectiveness, the soup would have been adopted as a suitable metaphor for chaos."
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
       has a graphic variant
      <i>
       hunlun
      </i>
       (using
      <i>
       ln
      </i>
      / "ripples; eddying water; sink down", see the
      <i>
       Liezi
      </i>
      below), which etymologically connects to the mountain name
      <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountain_(mythology)" title="Kunlun Mountain (mythology)">
       Kunlun
      </a>
       (differentiated with the "mountain radical" ). Robinet (2007:525) says, "Kunlun and
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      are the same closed center of the world."
     </p>
     <p>
      Girardot (1983:25) quotes the Chinese philologist Lo Mengci  that reduplicated words like
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      "suggest cyclic movement and transformation", and speculates.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Ritually mumbling the sounds of
       <i>
        hun-tun
       </i>
       might, therefore, be said to have a kind on incantatory significance that both phonetically and morphologically invokes the mythological and ontological idea of the Tao as the
       <i>
        creatio continua
       </i>
       process of infinitely repeated moments of change and new creation.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shuowen_Jiezi" title="Shuowen Jiezi">
        Shuowen Jiezi
       </a>
      </i>
      does not enter
      <i>
       dun
      </i>
       (which apparently lacked a pre-Han
      <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">
       Seal script
      </a>
      ). It defines
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
       as
      <i>
       fengliu
      </i>
       "abundantly flow",
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
       as the sound of
      <i>
       hunliu
      </i>
       "abundantly-flowing flow" or "seemingly impure",
      <i>
       dun
      </i>
       as "anger, rage; scolding" or "who", and
      <i>
       lun
      </i>
       as "ripples; eddies" or "sink into; disappear".
     </p>
     <p>
      English
      <i>
       chaos
      </i>
      is a better translation of
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      in the classical sense of
      <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">
       <i>
        Chaos
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Khaos
       </i>
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
       Greek mythology
      </a>
      meaning "gaping void; formless primordial space preceding creation of the universe" than in the common sense of "disorder; confusion". The latter meaning of
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      is synonymous with Chinese
      <i>
       lun
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BA%82" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      (Simplified
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B9%B1" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      ) "chaos; disorder; upheaval; confusion; turmoil; revolt; indiscriminate; random; arbitrary". Their
      <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">
       linguistic compound
      </a>
      <i>
       hnlun
      </i>
       (lit. "chaos-chaos") "chaos; disorder; confusion" exemplifies the "synonym compound" category in Chinese
      <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
       morphology
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_textual_usages">
       Early textual usages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early textual usages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the Chinese written record,
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      first appears in classics dating from the
      <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">
       Warring States period
      </a>
      . The following summary divides them into Confucianist, Daoist, and other categories, and presents them in roughly chronological order, with the caveat that many early textual dates are uncertain.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Confucian_texts">
       Confucian texts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Confucian texts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
      only occurs in one Confucian classic, the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuozhuan" title="Zuozhuan">
        Zuozhuan
       </a>
      </i>
      commentary to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_Annals" title="Spring and Autumn Annals">
        Chunqiu
       </a>
      </i>
      . Most early Confucianist ancient texts (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunyu" title="Lunyu">
        Lunyu
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shujing" title="Shujing">
        Shujing
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yijing" title="Yijing">
        Yijing
       </a>
      </i>
      , etc.) do not even use
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
      , with four exceptions. One, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mencius_(book)" title="Mencius (book)">
        Mengzi
       </a>
      </i>
      , uses
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
      in its original meaning "sound of flowing water". Mencius explains why Confucius praised water, "There is a spring of water; how it gushes out!" (tr. Legge 1872 2:324). The other three use
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
      as what Girardot (1983:119) calls "a term of opprobrium and condemnation related to the suppression of the "barbarians" or the "legendary rebels"." The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shijing" title="Shijing">
        Shijing
       </a>
      </i>
      (237) mentions Hunyi  "ancient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunni" title="Hunni">
       Hunni
      </a>
      tribe in
      <a href="/wiki/Turan" title="Turan">
       Turan
      </a>
      ". When
      <a href="/wiki/King_Wen_of_Zhou" title="King Wen of Zhou">
       King Wen of Zhou
      </a>
      opened up the roads, "The hordes of the Keun [sic] disappeared, Startled and panting. (tr. Legge 1872 4:441). The
      <i>
       Chunqiu
      </i>
      mentions the Luhun  tribe of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rong_people" title="Rong people">
       Rong
      </a>
       people, "the Jung of Luh-hwn" (tr. Legge 1872 5:293, 667). The
      <i>
       Zuozhuan
      </i>
      commentary to the
      <i>
       Chunqiu
      </i>
      notes they were originally from western
      <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">
       Gansu
      </a>
      and forced into northern
      <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">
       Henan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Another
      <i>
       Zuozhuan
      </i>
      context refers to Hundun  as a worthless son of the
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Emperor" title="Yellow Emperor">
       Yellow Emperor
      </a>
      , one of the mythical
      <i>
       Sixiong
      </i>
       "Four Fiends" banished by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shun_(Chinese_leader)" title="Shun (Chinese leader)">
       Shun
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The ancient emperor Hung [Hwang-te] had a descendant devoid of ability [and virtue]. He hid righteousness from himself, and was a villain at heart; he delighted in the practice of the worst vices; he was shameless and vile, obstinate, stupid, and unfriendly, cultivating only the intimacy of such as himself. All the people under heaven called him Chaos.  When Shun became Yaou's minister, he received the nobles from the four quarters of the empire, and banished these four wicked ones, Chaos, Monster, Block, and Glutton, casting them out into the four distant regions, to meet the spite of the sprites and evil things. (tr. Legge 1872 5:283)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The other "fiends" are Qiongqi , Taowu , and
      <a href="/wiki/Taotie" title="Taotie">
       Taotie
      </a>
      . Legge notes this passage "is worthy of careful study in many respects."
     </p>
     <p>
      Girardot (1983:129) contrasts these rare Confucian usages of
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      pejoratively suggesting the forces thwarting civilization, "the "birds and beasts," barbarian tribes, banished ministers, and legendary rebels)" with the common Daoist usages in a "paradise lost theme".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Daoist_texts">
       Daoist texts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Daoist texts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
      commonly occurs in classics of philosophical Daoism. The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daodejing" title="Daodejing">
        Daodejing
       </a>
      </i>
      does not mention
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      but uses both
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
      graphic variants. One section (49, tr. Mair 1990:17) uses
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
       "bemuddle": "The sage is self-effacing in his dealings with all under heaven, and bemuddles his mind for the sake of all under heaven." Three others (14, 15, 25, tr. Mair 1990:74, 76, 90) use
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
       "bound together," "muddled," and "featureless":
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       "These three cannot be fully fathomed, Therefore, They are bound together to make unity."
      </li>
      <li>
       "plain, as an unhewn log, muddled, as turbid waters, expansive, as a broad valley"
      </li>
      <li>
       "There was something featureless yet complete, born before heaven and earth."
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zhuangzi_(book)" title="Zhuangzi (book)">
        Zhuangzi
       </a>
      </i>
      (ca. 3rd-2nd centuries BCE) has a famous parable involving emperors
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Shu
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%84%B5" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      "a fish name; abrupt; quick", and
      <i>
       Hu
      </i>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BF%BD" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      "ignore; neglect; sudden". Girardot (1983:89) cites
      <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Granet" title="Marcel Granet">
       Marcel Granet
      </a>
      that Shu and Hu synonymously mean "suddenness; quickness" and "etymologically appear to be linked to the images of lightning and thunder, or analogously, flaming arrows." The "
      <a href="/wiki/Heavenly_Questions" title="Heavenly Questions">
       Heavenly Questions
      </a>
      " chapter of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chu_Ci" title="Chu Ci">
        Chu Ci
       </a>
      </i>
      uses Shu and Hu as one name: "Where are the hornless dragons which carry bears on their backs for sport? Where is the great serpent with nine heads and where is the Shu-Hu?" (tr. Hawkes 1985:128)
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The emperor of the South Sea was called Shu [Brief], the emperor of the North Sea was called Hu [Sudden], and the emperor of the central region was called Hun-tun [Chaos]. Shu and Hu from time to time came together for a meeting in the territory of Hun-tun, and Hun-tun treated them very generously. Shu and Hu discussed how they could repay his kindness. "All men," they said, "have seven openings so they can see, hear, eat, and breathe. But Hun-tun alone doesn't have any. Let's trying boring him some!" Every day they bored another hole, and on the seventh day Hun-tun died. (7, tr. Watson 1968:97)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Compare Watson's renderings of the three characters with other
      <i>
       Zhuangzi
      </i>
      translators.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Change, Suddenness, Confusion (or Chaos)  Frederic H. Balfour
      </li>
      <li>
       Sh, H, Chaos 
       <a href="/wiki/James_Legge" title="James Legge">
        James Legge
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Change, Uncertainty, Primitivity 
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yu-Lan_Fung" title="Yu-Lan Fung">
        Yu-Lan Fung
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Shu, Hu, Hun Tun 
       <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Giles" title="Herbert Giles">
        Herbert Giles
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Immediately, Suddenly, Undifferentiation  James R. Ware
      </li>
      <li>
       Light, Darkness, Primal Chaos 
       <a href="/wiki/Gia-Fu_Feng" title="Gia-Fu Feng">
        Gia-Fu Feng
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jane_English" title="Jane English">
        Jane English
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Fast, Furious, Hun-t'un 
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.C._Graham" title="A.C. Graham">
        A.C. Graham
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Lickety, Split, Wonton 
       <a href="/wiki/Victor_H._Mair" title="Victor H. Mair">
        Victor H. Mair
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Change, Dramatic, Chaos 
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Palmer" title="Martin Palmer">
        Martin Palmer
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Helter, Skelter, Chaos  Wang Rongpei
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Two other
      <i>
       Zhuangzi
      </i>
      contexts use
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      . Chapter 11 has an allegory about
      <a href="/wiki/Hong_Meng" title="Hong Meng">
       Hong Meng
      </a>
       "Big Concealment" or "Silly Goose", who "was amusing himself by slapping his thighs and hopping around like a sparrow", which Girardot (1983:110) interprets as shamanic dancing comparable with the
      <i>
       Shanhaijing
      </i>
      below. Hong Meng poetically reduplicates
      <i>
       hunhun-dundun
      </i>
       "dark and undifferentiated chaos" in describing Daoist "mind-nourishment" meditation.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "You have only to rest in inaction and things will transform themselves. Smash your form and body, spit out hearing and eyesight, forget you are a thing among other things, and you may join in great unity with the deep and boundless. Undo the mind, slough off spirit, be blank and soulless, and the ten thousand things one by one will return to the root  return to the root and not know why. Dark and undifferentiated chaos  to the end of life none will depart from it. But if you try to know it, you have already departed from it. Do not ask what its name is, do not try to observe its form. Things will live naturally and of themselves." (tr. Watson 1968:122)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Chapter 12 tells a story about the Confucian disciple
      <a href="/wiki/Disciples_of_Confucius#Zi-gong" title="Disciples of Confucius">
       Zigong
      </a>
      becoming dumbfounded after meeting a Daoist sage. He reported back to Confucius, who denigrated
      <i>
       Hundun Shi zhi shu
      </i>
       "the arts of Mr. Chaos [Hundun]":
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "He is one of those bogus practitioners of the arts of Mr. Chaos. He knows the first thing but doesn't understand the second. He looks after what is on the inside but doesn't look after what is on the outside. A man of true brightness and purity who can enter into simplicity, who can return to the primitive through inaction, give body to his inborn nature, and embrace his spirit, and in this way wander through the everyday world  if you had met one like that, you would have had real cause for astonishment. As for the arts of Mr. Chaos, you and I need not bother to find out about them." (12, tr. Watson 1968:136)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Huainanzi" title="Huainanzi">
        Huainanzi
       </a>
      </i>
      has one occurrence of
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
       in a cosmological description.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Heaven and earth were perfectly joined [
       <i>
        tung-t'ung
       </i>
       ], all was chaotically unformed [
       <i>
        hun-tun wei p'u
       </i>
       ]; and things were complete [
       <i>
        ch'eng
       </i>
       ] yet not created. This is called [the time or condition] of the Great One. [
       <i>
        t'ai-i
       </i>
       ]. All came from this unity which gave to each thing its differences: the birds, fish, and beasts. This is called the lot [or division,
       <i>
        fen
       </i>
       ] of things. (14, tr. Girardot 1983:134)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Three other
      <i>
       Huainanzi
      </i>
      chapters use
      <i>
       hun
      </i>
      , for example, the compound
      <i>
       hunhun cangcang
      </i>
       "pure and unformed, vast and hazy".
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The world was a unity without division into classes nor separation into orders (lit: a disorganised mass): the unaffectedness and homeliness of the natural heart had not, as yet, been corrupted: the spirit of the age was a unity, and all creation was in great affluence. Hence, if a man with the knowledge of I [ A mythical person of great powers] appeared, the world had no use for him. (2, tr. Morgan 1934:46)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Liezi" title="Liezi">
        Liezi
       </a>
      </i>
      uses
      <i>
       hunlun
      </i>
       for
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      , which is described as the confused state in which
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Qi" title="Qi">
        qi
       </a>
      </i>
       "pneuma; breath",
      <i>
       xing
      </i>
       "form; shape", and
      <i>
       zhi
      </i>
       "matter; substance" have begun to exist but are stilled merged as one.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       There was a Primal Simplicity, there was a Primal Commencement, there were Primal Beginnings, there was a Primal Material. The Primal Simplicity preceded the appearance of the breath. The Primal Beginnings were the breath beginning to assume shape. The Primal Material was the breath when it began to assume substance. Breath, shape and substance were complete, but things were not yet separated from each other; hence the name "Confusion." "Confusion" means the myriad things were confounded and not yet separated from each other. (1, tr. Graham 1960:18-19)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_texts">
       Other texts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other texts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shanhaijing" title="Shanhaijing">
        Shanhaijing
       </a>
      </i>
      collection of early myths and legends uses
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
       describing a
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shen_(Chinese_%22spirit;_god%22)" title='Shen (Chinese "spirit; god")'>
        shen
       </a>
      </i>
       "spirit; god" on
      <a href="/wiki/Tian" title="Tian">
       Tian
      </a>
      Shan  "Heaven Mountain".
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       There is a god here who looks like a yellow sack. He is scarlet like cinnabar fire. He has six feet and four wings. He is Muddle Thick. He has no face and no eyes. He knows how to sing and dance. He is in truth the great god Long River. (2, tr. Birrell 2000:226)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      This "great god Long River" translates Di Jiang  "Emperor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">
       Yangtze River
      </a>
      "
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , which is identified with Huang Di  "
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Emperor" title="Yellow Emperor">
       Yellow Emperor
      </a>
      "
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Toshihiko_Izutsu" title="Toshihiko Izutsu">
       Toshihiko Izutsu
      </a>
      (1967 2:19, cited by Girardot 1983:82) suggests that singing and dancing here and in
      <i>
       Zhuangzi
      </i>
      refers to shamanic trance-inducing ceremonies, "the monster is said to be a bird, which is most probably an indication that the shamanistic dancing here in question was some kind of feather dance in which the shaman was ritually ornamented with a feathered headdress."
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Shen yi jing
      </i>
       "Classic of Divine Wonders" records a later variation of Hundun mythology. It describes him as a divine dog who lived on Mt. Kunlun, the mythical mountain at the center of the world.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       It has eyes but can't see, walks without moving; and has two ears but can't hear. It has the knowledge of a man yet its belly is without the five internal organs and, although having a rectum, it doesn't evacuate food. It punches virtuous men and stays with the non-virtuous. It is called. Hun-tun.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Quoting the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuozhuan" title="Zuozhuan">
       Zuozhuan
      </a>
      , Hun-tun was Meng-shih's untalented son. He always gnaws his tail, going round and round. Everyone ridiculed him. (tr. Girardot 1983:188)
     </p>
     <p>
      A poem in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">
       Tang Dynasty
      </a>
      collection
      <a href="/wiki/Hanshan_(poet)" title="Hanshan (poet)">
       Hanshan
      </a>
      refers to the
      <i>
       Zhuangzi
      </i>
      myth and reminisces about
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       How pleasant were our bodies in the days of Chaos, Needing neither to eat or piss! Who came along with his drill And bored us full of these nine holes? Morning after morning we must dress and eat; Year after year, fret over taxes. A thousand of us scrambling for a penny, We knock our heads together and yell for dear life. (78, tr. Watson 1970:77)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Note the addition of two holes (anus and penis) to the original seven (eyes, ears, nostrils, and mouth).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">
       Interpretations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hundun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interpretations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
      myths have a complex history, with many variations on the "primordial chaos" theme and associations with other legends.
     </p>
     <p>
      The sociologist and historian
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Eberhard" title="Wolfram Eberhard">
       Wolfram Eberhard
      </a>
      analyzed the range of various
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      myths in his book (1968:438-446) on local cultures in South and East China. He treated it as a
      <a href="/wiki/World_egg" title="World egg">
       World egg
      </a>
      mythic "chain" from the southern Liao culture, which originated in the
      <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">
       Sichuan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">
       Hubei
      </a>
      region.
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <i>
        Hundun
       </i>
       creation myths involving humanity being born from a "thunder-egg" or lump of flesh, the son of an emperor, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunder_god" title="Thunder god">
        Thunder god
       </a>
       represented as a dog with bat wings, localized with the
       <a href="/wiki/Miao_people" title="Miao people">
        Miao people
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thai_people" title="Thai people">
        Thai people
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The animal Lei
       </i>
       (the rare character combines the "dog radical" with a
       <i>
        lei
       </i>
        "thunder" phonetic) "is a creature like a lump, without head, eyes, hands, or feet. At midnight it produces noises like thunder." (1968:440)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The hundun dumplings
       </i>
       , etymologically connected with "round", "unorganized; chaotic", and perhaps the "round mountain" Kunlun.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The world-system huntian
       </i>
        in ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">
        Chinese astronomy
       </a>
       conceptualized the universe as a round egg and the earth as a yolk swimming within it.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The sack and the shooting of the god
       </i>
       connects sack-like descriptions of
       <i>
        hundun
       </i>
       , perhaps with "sack" denoting "testicles", legends about
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">
        Shang Dynasty
       </a>
       king
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wu_Yi_(ruler)" title="Wu Yi (ruler)">
        Wu Yi
       </a>
       who lost a game of chess with the god Heaven and suspended a sack filled with blood and shot arrows at it, and later traditions of shooting at human dolls.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pangu" title="Pangu">
         Pangu
        </a>
       </i>
        is the mythological creator of the universe, also supposedly shaped like a sack, connected with dog mythologies, and who grew into a giant in order to separate Heaven and Earth.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Heaven and earth as marital partners
       </i>
       within the world-egg refers to the theme of
       <a href="/wiki/Sky_father" title="Sky father">
        Sky father
       </a>
       and Earth
       <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">
        Mother goddess
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Zhongli
       </i>
        or  is identified with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhu_Rong_(god)" title="Zhu Rong (god)">
        Zhu Rong
       </a>
        "god of fire", which is a mythology from the southern state
       <a href="/wiki/Chu_(state)" title="Chu (state)">
        Chu
       </a>
       , with variations appearing as two gods Zhong and Li.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Zhongli
       </i>
        clan, which has variant writings, originated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ba_(state)" title="Ba (state)">
        Ba (state)
       </a>
       , near present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">
        Anhui
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The brother-sister marriage
       </i>
       is a complex of myths explaining the origins or mankind (or certain families), and their first child is usually a lump of flesh, which falls into pieces and populates the world. In later mythology, the brother
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fu_Xi" title="Fu Xi">
        Fu Xi
       </a>
       and sister
       <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCwa" title="Nwa">
        Nwa
       </a>
       , who lived on Mt. Kunlun, exemplify this marriage.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Norman J. Girardot, professor of Chinese religion at
      <a href="/wiki/Lehigh_University" title="Lehigh University">
       Lehigh University
      </a>
      , has written articles and a definitive book on
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      . He summarizes this mythology as follows.
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       The
       <i>
        hun-tun
       </i>
       theme in early Taoism represents an ensemble of mythic elements coming from different cultural and religious situations.
      </li>
      <li>
       The symbolic coherence of the
       <i>
        hun-tun
       </i>
       theme in the Taoist texts basically reflects a creative reworking of a limited set of interrelated mythological typologies: especially the cosmic egg-gourd, the animal ancestor-cosmic giant, and primordial couple mythologies. The last two of these typologies are especially, although not exclusively, linked to what may be called the deluge cycle of mythology found primarily in southern local cultures.
      </li>
      <li>
       While there may also be a cultural connection between the southern deluge cycle and the cosmogonic scenario of the cosmic egg (i.e., via the "thunder-egg," "origin of ancestors [culture hero] from egg or gourd," and "origin of agriculture and mankind from gourd" myths), the fundamental linkage for all these typologies is the early Taoist, innovative perception of a shared symbolic intention that accounts for, and supports, a particular cosmogonic, metaphysical, and mystical vision of creation and life. (1983:209)
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Interpretations of
      <i>
       Hundun
      </i>
      have expanded from "primordial chaos" into other realms. For instance, it is a keyword in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Neidan" title="Neidan">
        Neidan
       </a>
      </i>
      "Chinese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_alchemy" title="Internal alchemy">
       internal alchemy
      </a>
      ". Robinet (2007:525) explains, "Alchemists begin their work by "opening" or "boring"
      <i>
       hundun
      </i>
      ; in other words, they begin from the Origin, infusing its transcendent element of precosmic light into the cosmos in order to reshape it."
     </p>
