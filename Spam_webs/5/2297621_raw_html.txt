&lt;p id="method_toc" class="sp_method_toc"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Eight Methods:&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="#The_Basics_sub" class=""&gt;The Basics&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Meter_and_Time_sub" class=""&gt;Meter and Time&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Rhythm_sub" class=""&gt;Rhythm&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Melody_sub" class=""&gt;Melody&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Sharps.2C_Flats.2C_Naturals.2C_and_Keys_sub" class=""&gt;Sharps, Flats, Naturals, and Keys&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Dynamics_and_Expression_sub" class=""&gt;Dynamics and Expression&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Advancing_sub" class=""&gt;Advancing&lt;/a&gt;&lt;a href="#Table_of_Key_Signatures_sub" class=""&gt;Table of Key Signatures&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Written music is a language that has been developing for thousands of years, and even the music we read today has been around for over 300 years. Music notation is the representation of sound with symbols, from basic notations for pitch, duration, and timing, to more advanced descriptions of expression, timbre, and even special effects. This article will introduce you to the basics of reading music, show you some more advanced methods, and suggest some ways to gain more knowledge about the subject.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;div class="altblock"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="The_Basics"&gt;Method 1 of 8: The Basics&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;div id="steps_1" class="section_text"&gt;

&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;1&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Get a handle on the staff.&lt;/b&gt; Before you are ready to start learning music, you must get a sense for the basic information that virtually everyone who reads music needs to know. The horizontal lines on a piece of music make up &lt;i&gt;the staff&lt;/i&gt;. This is the most basic of all musical symbols and the foundation for everything that is to follow.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The staff is an arrangement of five parallel lines, and the spaces between them. Both lines and spaces are numbered for reference purposes, and are always counted from lowest (bottom of the staff) to highest (top of the staff).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-2.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-2.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 2.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250d850" data-src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/0/0a/2667-2.jpg/670px-2667-2.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250d850');

&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 2.jpg" src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/0/0a/2667-2.jpg/670px-2667-2.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;2&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Start with the Treble Clef.&lt;/b&gt; One of the first things you'll encounter when reading music is the &lt;i&gt;clef&lt;/i&gt;. This sign, which looks like a big, fancy cursive symbol at the left end of the staff, is the legend that tells you approximately what range your instrument will play in. All instruments and voices in the higher ranges use the treble clef, and for this intro to reading music, we'll focus primarily on this clef for our examples.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The Treble Clef, or G Clef, is derived from an ornamental Latin letter G. One good way to remember this is that the line at the center of the clef's "swirl" represents the note G. When notes are added to the staff in the treble clef, they will have the following values:&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The five lines, from the bottom up, represent the following notes: E G B D F.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The four spaces, from the bottom up, represent these notes: F A C E.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;This may seem like a lot to remember, but you can use mnemonicsor word cuesthat may help you remember them. For the lines, "Every Good Boy Does Fine" is one popular mnemonic, and the spaces spell out the word "FACE." Practicing with an &lt;a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sightreadingmastery.com/note-recognition/"&gt;online note recognition tool&lt;/a&gt; is another great way to reinforce these associations.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-3.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-3.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 3.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250d9c8" data-src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/6/6f/2667-3.jpg/670px-2667-3.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250d9c8');

&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;3&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Understand the Bass Clef.&lt;/b&gt; The bass clef, also known as the F clef, is used for instruments in the lower registers, including the left hand of the piano, bass guitar, trombone, and so on.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The name "F clef" derives from its origins as the Gothic letter F. The two dots on the clef lie above and below the "F" line on the staff. The staff of the bass clef represents different notes than that of the treble clef.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The five lines, bottom to top, represent these notes: G B D F A (Good Boys Don't Fool Around).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The four spaces, bottom to top, represent these notes: A C E G (All Cows Eat Grass).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-4.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-4.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 4.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250db37" data-src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/0/0c/2667-4.jpg/670px-2667-4.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250db37');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 4.jpg" src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/0/0c/2667-4.jpg/670px-2667-4.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;4&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Learn the parts of a note.&lt;/b&gt; Individual note symbols are a combination of up to three basic elements: the note head, the stem, and flags.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;b&gt;The note head&lt;/b&gt;. This is an oval shape that is either open (white) or closed (black). At its most basic, it tells the performer what note to play on their instrument.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;b&gt;The stem&lt;/b&gt;. This is the thin vertical line that is attached to the note head. When the stem is pointing up, it joins on the right side of the note head. When the stem is pointing down, it joins the note head on the left. The direction of the stem has no effect on the note, but it makes notation easier to read and less cluttered.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The general rule on stem direction is that at or above the center line of the staff, the stem points down, and when the note is below the middle of the staff, the stem points up.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;b&gt;The flag&lt;/b&gt;. This is the curved stroke that is attached to the end of the stem. No matter if the stem is joined to the right or left of the note head, the flag is &lt;i&gt;always&lt;/i&gt; drawn to the right of the stem, and never to the left.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Taken together, the note, stem, and flag or flags show the musician the time value for any given note, as measured in beats or fractions of beats. When you listen to music, and you're tapping your foot in time to the music, you're recognizing that beat.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ol&gt;

&lt;a name="Meter_and_Time_sub" class="anchor"&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;div class="section steps   sticky "&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;
&lt;div class="altblock"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Meter_and_Time"&gt;Method 2 of 8: Meter and Time&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;div id="steps_2" class="section_text"&gt;
&lt;ol class="steps_list_2"&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;1&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Learn about measure lines.&lt;/b&gt; On a piece of sheet music, you will see thin vertical lines crossing the staff at fairly regular intervals. These lines represent &lt;i&gt;measures&lt;/i&gt;  the space before the first line is the first measure, the space between the first and second lines is the second measure, and so on. Measure lines don't affect how the music sounds, but they help the performer keep her place in the music.
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;As we'll see below, another handy thing about measures is that &lt;i&gt;each one gets the same number of beats.&lt;/i&gt; For example, if you find yourself tapping "1-2-3-4" along to a piece of music on the radio, you've probably subconsciously found the measure lines already.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-5.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-5.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 5.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250dca5" data-src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/a/af/2667-5.jpg/670px-2667-5.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250dca5');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 5.jpg" src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/a/af/2667-5.jpg/670px-2667-5.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;2&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Learn about timing, or meter.&lt;/b&gt; Meter can be generally thought of as the "pulse" or the beat of music. You feel it instinctively when you listen to dance or pop music  the "boom, tiss, boom, tiss" of a stereotypical dance track is a simple example of meter.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;On a piece of sheet music, the beat is expressed by something that looks like a fraction written next to the first clef symbol. Like any fraction, there is a numerator, and a denominator. The numerator, written in the top two spaces of the staff, tells you how many beats there are in one measure. The denominator tells you the note value that receives one beat (the "pulse" that you tap your toe to).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Perhaps the easiest meter to understand is 4/4 time, or "common" time. In 4/4 time, there are four beats in each measure and each quarter note is equal to one beat. This is the time signature you'll hear in most popular music. You can count along to common time music by counting "1-2-3-4 1-2-3-4..." to the beat.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;By changing the numerator, we change the number of beats in a measure. Another very common time signature is 3/4. For example, most waltzes will have a steady "1-2-3 1-2-3" beat, making them in 3/4 time.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ol&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

&lt;a name="Rhythm_sub" class="anchor"&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;div class="section steps   sticky "&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;
&lt;div class="altblock"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Rhythm"&gt;Method 3 of 8: Rhythm&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;div id="steps_3" class="section_text"&gt;
&lt;ol class="steps_list_2"&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;1&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Get in the groove.&lt;/b&gt; Similar to meter and time, "rhythm" is a crucial part of how the music feels. However, whereas meter simply tells you how many beats, rhythm is how those beats are used.
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Try this: tap your finger on your desk, and count 1-2-3-4 1-2-3-4, steadily. Not very interesting, is it? Now try this: on beats 1 and 3, tap louder, and on beats 2 and 4, tap softer. That's got a different feel to it! Now try the reverse: tapping loud on 2 and 4, and soft on beats 1 and 3.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Check out Regina Spektor's &lt;a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BWXWivwhi14"&gt;Don't Leave Me&lt;/a&gt;. You can clearly hear the rhythm: the quieter bass note happens on beat 1 and beat 3, and a loud clap and snare drum happens on beats 2 and 4. You'll start to get a sense of how music is organized. That's what we call rhythm!&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-6.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-6.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 6.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250de18" data-src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/3/34/2667-6.jpg/670px-2667-6.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250de18');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 6.jpg" src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/3/34/2667-6.jpg/670px-2667-6.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;2&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Imagine yourself walking.&lt;/b&gt; Each footstep will equal one beat. Those are represented musically by quarter notes, because in much of Western music (meaning music of the western world, not just the music of Hank Williams!), there are four of these beats for every measure. Musically, the rhythm of your walking will look like this:

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Each step is one quarter note. On a sheet of music, quarter notes are the solid black dots attached to stems without any flags. You can count that off as you walk: "1, 2, 3, 4-1, 2, 3, 4"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;If you were to slow your pace down to half that speed, so that you only took a step every two beats on the 1 and on the 3, that would be notated with half notes (for half a measure). On a sheet of music, half notes look like quarter notes, only they aren't solid black  they are outlined in black with white centers.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;If you slowed your pace down even further, so that you only took a step every four beats, on the 1, you would write that as a whole noteor one note per measure. On a sheet of music, whole notes look like "Os" or donuts  similar to half notes without stems.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-8.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-8.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 8.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250df81" data-src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/7/72/2667-8.jpg/670px-2667-8.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250df81');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 8.jpg" src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/7/72/2667-8.jpg/670px-2667-8.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;3&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Pick up the pace!&lt;/b&gt; Enough of this slowing down. As you noticed, as we slowed the notes down, we started taking away bits of the note. First we took away the solid note, then we took away the stem. Now let's look at speeding things up. To do that, we're going to add things to the note.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Go back to our walking tempo, and picture that in your mind (tapping your foot to the beat can help). Now imagine that your bus has just pulled up to the stop, and you're about a block away. What do you do? You run! And as you run, you try and &lt;i&gt;flag&lt;/i&gt; the bus driver.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To make notes faster in music, we add a flag. Each flag cuts the time value of the note in half. For example, an eighth note (which gets one flag) is 1/2 the value of a quarter note; and a 16th note (which gets two flags) is 1/2 the value of an eighth note. In terms of walking, we go from a walk (quarter note) to a run (8th note)twice as fast as a walk, to a sprint (16th note)twice as fast as a run. Thinking in terms of each quarter note being a step as you walk, tap along with the example above.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-9.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-9.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 9.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250e0e9" data-src="http://pad1.whstatic.com/images/thumb/f/f4/2667-9.jpg/670px-2667-9.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250e0e9');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 9.jpg" src="http://pad1.whstatic.com/images/thumb/f/f4/2667-9.jpg/670px-2667-9.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;4&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Beam me up!&lt;/b&gt; As you can see with that above example, things can start to get a little confusing when there are a bunch of notes on the page like that. Your eyes start to cross, and you lose track of where you were. To group notes into smaller packages that make sense visually, we use &lt;i&gt;beaming&lt;/i&gt;.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Beaming merely replaces individual note flags with thick lines drawn between note stems. These are grouped logically, and while more complex music requires more complex beaming rules, for our purposes, we'll generally beam in groups of quarter notes. Compare the example below with the example above. Try tapping out the rhythm again, and see how much clearer beaming makes the notation.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-10.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-10.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 10.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250e24f" data-src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/2/2f/2667-10.jpg/670px-2667-10.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250e24f');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 10.jpg" src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/2/2f/2667-10.jpg/670px-2667-10.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;5&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Learn the value of ties and dots.&lt;/b&gt; Where a flag will cut the value of a note in half, the dot has a similarbut oppositefunction. With limited exceptions that do not come into play here, the dot is always placed to the right of the note head. When you see a dotted note, that note is increased by one half the length of its original value.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;For example, a dot placed after a half note will be equal to the half note plus a quarter note. A dot placed after a quarter note will be equal to a quarter note plus an eighth note.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Ties are similar to dotsthey extend the value of the original note. A tie is simply two notes linked together with a curved line between the note heads. Unlike dots, which are abstract and based wholly on the value of the original note, ties are explicit: the note is increased in length by exactly as long as the second note value.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;One reason you would use a tie versus a dot is, for example, when a note's duration would not fit musically into the space of a measure. In that case, you simply add the leftover duration into the next measure as a note, and tie the two together.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Note that the tie is drawn from note head to note head, generally in the opposite direction as the stem.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;

Those spaces are called &lt;i&gt;rests&lt;/i&gt;, and even in silence, they can really add motion and life to music. Let's take a look at how they're notated.

&lt;ul&gt;

The C scale, as we learned, comprises the white keys on the piano. The sharps and flats are the black keys. &lt;div class="step_num"&gt;2&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Whole tones and semitones.&lt;/b&gt; In western music, notes are either a whole tone or a semitone apart. If you look at the C note on the piano keyboard, youll see theres a black key between it and the next note up, the D. The musical distance between the C and the D is called a whole tone. The distance between the C and the black key is called a semitone. Now, you may be wondering what that black key is called. The answer is, it depends.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;A good rule of thumb is if you are going up the scale, that note is the sharp version of the beginning note. When moving down the scale, that note would be the flat version of the beginning note. Thus, if you are moving from C to D with the black key, it would be written using a sharp ().&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In this case, the black note is written as C#. When moving down the scale, from D to C and using the black note as a passing tone between them, the black key would be written using a flat ().&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Conventions like that makes music a little easier to read. If you were to write those three notes going up, and used a D instead of a C#, the notation would be written using a natural sign ().&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Notice that there's a new signthe natural. Whenever you see a natural sign () that means that the note cancels any sharps or flats previously written. In this example, the second and third notes are both D's: the first a D, and so the second D, since it goes up a semitone from the first D, has to have the note "corrected" to show the right note. The more sharps and flats scattered around a sheet of music, the more a musician must take in before the score can be played.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Often, composers that previously used accidentals in previous measures may put "unnecessary" natural signs to provide clarity for the player. For example, if a previous measure in a D major piece used an A#, the next measure that uses an A may be notated with an A-natural instead.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;

&lt;li&gt;The starting note, or &lt;i&gt;tonic&lt;/i&gt;, is also the name of the key. You may have heard somebody say "It's in the key of C" or something similar. Take a look at the keyboard above.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Note that between most notes, there is a whole step. But there is only a half step (semitone) between E and F, and between B and C. &lt;li&gt;

&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;1&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Get loudor get soft!&lt;/b&gt; When you listen to music, you have probably noticed that it's not all at the same volume, all the time. Some parts get really loud, and some parts get really soft. These variations are known as "dynamics."

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;If the rhythm and meter are the heart of the music, and notes and keys are the brains, then dynamics are surely the voice of the music. Consider the first version above.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;On your table, tap out: 1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and 5 and 6 and 7 and 8, etc. (the &lt;i&gt;and&lt;/i&gt; is how musicians "say" eighth notes). Make sure every beat is tapped at the same loudness, so that it so it sounds sort of like a helicopter. Now take a look at the second version.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Notice the accent mark (&gt;) above every 4th C note. Tap that out, only this time, accent every beat that you see the accent mark. &lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-20.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-20.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 20.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250f603" data-src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/a/aa/2667-20.jpg/670px-2667-20.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250f603');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 20.jpg" src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/a/aa/2667-20.jpg/670px-2667-20.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;2&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Play it piano, or fortissimo, or somewhere in between.&lt;/b&gt; Just like you don't always talk at the same levelyou modulate your voice louder or softer, depending on the situationmusic modulates in level too. The way the composer tells the musician what is intended is by using dynamic markings.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;There are dozens of dynamic markings you may see on a piece of music, but some of the most common ones you'll find will be the letters &lt;i&gt;f&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;m&lt;/i&gt;, and &lt;i&gt;p&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;p&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt; means "piano," or "softly."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;f&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt; means "forte," or "loud."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;m&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt; means "mezzo," or "medium." This modifies the dynamic after it, as in &lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;mf&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt; or &lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;mp&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, which means "medium loud," or "medium soft."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The more &lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;p&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt;s or &lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;f&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/i&gt;s you have, the softer or louder the music is to be played. Try singing the example above (using solfgethe first note in this example is the tonic, or "do"), and use the dynamic markings to notice the difference.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-21.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-21.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 21.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250f765" data-src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/5/54/2667-21.jpg/670px-2667-21.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250f765');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 21.jpg" src="http://pad3.whstatic.com/images/thumb/5/54/2667-21.jpg/670px-2667-21.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;3&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Get louder and louder and louder, or quieter and quieter and quieter.&lt;/b&gt; Another very common dynamic notation is the &lt;i&gt;crescendo&lt;/i&gt;, and it's corollary, the &lt;i&gt;decrescendo&lt;/i&gt;. They are a visual representations of a gradual change in volume which look like stretched-out "&lt;" and "&gt;" symbols.

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;A crescendo gradually gets louder, and a decrescendo gradually decreases the volume. You'll notice that, with these two symbols, the "open" end of the symbol represents the louder dynamic and the closed end represents the quieter dynamic. For example, if the music directs you to gradually go from forte to piano, you'll see an &lt;i&gt;&lt;b&gt;f'&lt;/b&gt;, then a stretched out "&lt;b&gt;&gt;&lt;/b&gt;", then a '&lt;b&gt;p'&lt;/b&gt;.&lt;/i&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="clearall"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;

&lt;div class="altblock"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Advancing"&gt;Method 7 of 8: Advancing&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;div id="steps_7" class="section_text"&gt;
&lt;ol class="steps_list_2"&gt;
&lt;li&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-22.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-22.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 22.jpg" src="" width="670" height="503" id="55a567250f8c6" data-src="http://pad1.whstatic.com/images/thumb/5/56/2667-22.jpg/670px-2667-22.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250f8c6');

&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 22.jpg" src="http://pad1.whstatic.com/images/thumb/5/56/2667-22.jpg/670px-2667-22.jpg" width="670" height="503"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;1&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Keep learning!&lt;/b&gt; Learning to read music is like learning the alphabet. The basics take a little bit to learn, but are fairly easy, overall. However, there are so many nuances, concepts, and skills that you can learn that it can keep you learning for a lifetime. Some composers even go so far as to write music on staff lines that form spirals or patterns, or the even use no staff lines at all! This article should give you a good foundation to keep growing!

&lt;h3&gt;
&lt;div class="altblock"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Table_of_Key_Signatures"&gt;Method 8 of 8: Table of Key Signatures&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;div id="steps_8" class="section_text"&gt;
&lt;ol class="steps_list_2"&gt;
&lt;li class="final_li"&gt;
&lt;div class="mwimg  largeimage  floatcenter " style="max-width:670px"&gt;
&lt;a href="#/Image:2667-23.jpg" class="image lightbox" data-href="/Image:2667-23.jpg?ajax=true"&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 23.jpg" src="" width="670" height="536" id="55a567250fa28" data-src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/3/3a/2667-23.jpg/670px-2667-23.jpg"&gt;&lt;script&gt;var img = document.getElementById('55a567250fa28');
defer.add(img);
&lt;/script&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;noscript&gt;&lt;img alt="2667 23.jpg" src="http://pad2.whstatic.com/images/thumb/3/3a/2667-23.jpg/670px-2667-23.jpg" width="670" height="536"&gt;&lt;/noscript&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;div class="step_num"&gt;1&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="step"&gt;
&lt;b class="whb"&gt;Learn these key signatures.&lt;/b&gt; There is at least one for every note in the scaleand the savvy student will see that in some cases, there are two keys for the same note. For example, the key of G# sounds exactly the same as the key of A! When playing the pianoand for the purposes of this article, the difference is academic. However, there are some composersespecially those that write for stringswho will suggest that the A is played a little "sharper" than the G#. Here are the key signatures for the major scales:

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The key of C (or atonal)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Keys using sharps: G, D, A, E, B, F, C&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Keys using flats: F, B, E, A, D, G, C&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;As you can see above, as you move through the sharp key signatures, you add sharps one at a time until every note is played sharp in the key of C. As you move through the flat key signatures, you add flats until every note is played flat in the key of C.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;It may be of some comfort to know that composers usually write in key signatures that are comfortable for the player to read. D major is a very common key for string instruments to play because the open strings are closely related to the tonic, D. There are few works out there that have strings play in E minor, or brass playing E major - it's as much a pain for them to write as it is for you to read.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Tips"&gt;Tips&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;div id="tips" class="section_text"&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Practice somewhere quiet or when it's quiet. It's best to try piano first because piano's easy if you practice. If you don't have a piano try using those virtual pianos online. Once you get it, you can start learning how to play other instruments! I hope this helps.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Try these mnemonics: For the right hand Every Good Boy Does Fine for the lines and FACE spells out the spaces. For the left hand Great Big Dreams For America for lines and All Cows Eat Grass for the spaces.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Be patient. Like learning any new language, learning to read music takes time. Like learning anything else, the more you practice at it, the easier it will be, and the better you'll become at it.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Practice this with your main instrument. If you play piano, it's likely you've been exposed to reading music. Many guitar players, however, learn by listening rather than by reading. When you're learning to read music, forget what you already knowlearn to read first, then jam later!&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Try to have fun with your music because if it's just not your thing then it's hard to learn how to play.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Get sheet music to songs you like. A visit to your local library or music store will uncover hundredsif not thousandsof "lead sheets" with the basic notation and chords for your favorite songs. Read the music while you listen, and you'll get a more intuitive understanding of what you're looking at.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Repetition and consistently practicing is key. Create flashcards or use a note-reading workbook to ensure you build a solid note-reading foundation.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;It is very good to know both Western notes, and sheet music. Knowing Western notes eventually help you in the long run, and it's much easier to remember than notes.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Work on the sight singing. You don't need to have a good voice, but it will help you train your ears to "hear" what's on the paper.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;IMSLP hosts a large archive for music performances and scores in the public domain. To improve reading music, it is suggested that you browse for composers' works and reading the music along with listening to it.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Which slides on trombone plays the sharp and flat notes.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;If you have a sheet of music, but can't remember all the notes, start small by writing down the note letter under each note. Don't do it too often, because you want to remember the notes as time goes on.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
