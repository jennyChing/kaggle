        <div class="module-typelist module" id="17_surefire_ways_to_not_suck_at_life">
         <h2 class="module-header">
          17 SURE-FIRE WAYS TO NOT SUCK AT LIFE
         </h2>
         <div class="module-content">
          <ul class="module-list">
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/06/intelligence-a-muscle-to-be-developed.html">
             1. Your smarts aren't born, they're grown.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/05/can-you-fly-below-the-radar-and-be-successful.html">
             2. Learning, like work, is a contact sport.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="">
             3. When you're developing a new skill, do it wrong quickly.  Figure out what you did wrong.  Laugh about it, and then learn from it.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/06/communication-relationship-barriers.html">
             4. In the world of decisions, evidence is paramount.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/07/how-to-get-a-promotion.html">
             5. It's not your boss who promotes you, but your peers and subordinates.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/05/influence-management-three-big-issues.html">
             6. If you have a glass ceiling over your head, learn the politics and then shatter it.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/06/why-a-mentor-is-no-longer-the-way-to-go.html">
             7. Your network is your mentor, not your boss.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="">
             8. Be transparent to some, not to others.  Choose wisely.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/04/geny-competencies-six-relational-skills.html">
             9. In the 21st century communication is king, not your business technology.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/04/geny-skills-managing-your-boss-part-2.html">
             10. To take charge of tomorrow, manage up today.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="">
             11. Ask forgiveness, not permission.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/06/why-small-changes-work-and-big-changes-dont.html">
             12. Keep your work skills fluid and fresh.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/05/how-to-listen-with-your-eyes-part-2.html">
             13. Listen with your eyes as well as your ears.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/02/geny-feedback-evaluative-or-developmental.html">
             14. In order to grow, you must have feedback.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="http://danerwin.typepad.com/my_weblog/2009/05/the-most-important-question-i-know.html">
             15. The most important question for yourself is, "what keeps me from asking that question?"
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="">
             16. Two-and-a-half to three years on a job is ideal.  After that you're risking stagnation.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
           <li class="module-list-item">
            <a href="">
             17. Live fully: an impossible task if all you do is work.
            </a>
            <br/>
           </li>
          </ul>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         July 24, 2010
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-communication_skills entry-category-relationship_skills entry-author-dan_erwin entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00e55187f8f688340133f286d664970b">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           Matching, Clicking and Self-Promotion
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             Thanks to some cool research by Mark Snyder at the University of Minnesota, you may never look at vulnerability the same way again. Snyder, a Minnesota psychologist, has found that the people really good at self-promotion practice the fine art of personal vulnerability.What Snyder found is that the people that really "click" with others are high self-monitors. They are able to adapt their personalities, behavior and attitudes to fit the people around them. In short, they pick up subtle social cues and tailor their responses to the other person. Those that are best at the process do it without even realizing it. Think Bill Clinton or Oprah.
            </p>
            <p>
             The fact of the matter is that the better salespeople understand this process rather well. It shows up in their interactions and presentations. Sure, theyorganize their material to match the client's needs.But rather than keep their cards close to the vest, they open up and and share their feelings.
            </p>
            <p>
             <span style="COLOR: #0060bf">
              <span style="COLOR: #111111">
               Business Week reported the following:
              </span>
              A study conducted by SUNY-Stony Brook social psychologist Arthur Aron and his research team paired individuals who didn't know each other and assigned each a set of cocktail-party-type questions, such as: What did you do over the holidays? The other half were given questions that required more intimate self-revelation, such as: What are your most treasured memories? The pairs who were forced to be more vulnerable in their answers formed incredibly quick, deep connections. One pair even married.
             </span>
            </p>
            <p>
             Most people are exceedingly resistant to being vulnerable in any setting. However, the research is rather clear. Despite what you might think, vulnerability is a strength. The more you're able to open up and share your feelings, the more trust and the more likely a connection. Aside from just working better, vulnerability saves you a lot of time in the workplace.
            </p>
            <p>
             The article on self-monitoring and vulnerability is here:
             <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/magazine/content/10_25/b4183071373230.htm">
              BusinessWeek.
             </a>
            </p>
           </div>
