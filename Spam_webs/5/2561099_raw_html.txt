        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           The tissue boxes are out across Britain as thousands of children embark on
           <strong>
            <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/education/universityeducation/" target="_blank">
             university
            </a>
           </strong>
           life, in most cases moving away from the family home and all its comforts and frustrations for the first time.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           This may be traumatic for both parents and children, but getting a firm handle on the logistical challenges involved in selecting and moving into a student property can ease the suffering.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/universityeducation/student-life/10298536/Freshers-week-what-to-pack-for-university.html" id="10298536">
             &gt;&gt; Freshers' week: what to pack for university
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           Im an old hand at this. Ive taken my three children the length and breadth of the country, from Plymouth to Warwick and Durham. I am also a student-house landlord, so I have a professional perspective (as well as some emotional scars).
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           In both roles I have plenty of hard-won experience; so I hereby share 20 practical tips if youre swapping the cosy family nest for the groves of Academe.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class=" related_links_inline" id="tmg-related-links">
          <div class="headerOne styleOne">
           <h2>
            <span>
             Related Articles
            </span>
           </h2>

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           <strong>
            Finding private accommodation
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Many students find private accommodation cheaper than university halls of residence. But it is still a good idea to go through the university accommodation website if it has one. Then you will have support should you have a gripe against the landlord  websites unconnected with the university are unlikely to offer that.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/universityeducation/10295804/University-rankings-Top-10-world-universities-2013.html" id="10295804">
             &gt;&gt; IN PICTURES: Top 10 world universities 2013
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Dealing with landlords
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Individual private landlords can be more accessible than agencies when there are renting problems. Agencies often charge fees to register; private landlords often dont.
          </p>
          <p>
           When you are looking around a place that you are thinking of renting, try and talk to the outgoing tenants and get the low-down on how they have been treated  how quickly did the landlord respond to requests to get things fixed or replaced? Did they have home and mobile numbers for the landlord?
          </p>
          <p>
           These are absolutely essential. It is no good talking to a voicemail on a Friday night when you have a blocked lavatory.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Take time to inspect the house
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Look at the condition of the place. This is not a matter of aesthetics  though no one wants to live in a dump  but an indicator of the landlords attitude: if the house looks unloved, so will you be.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/universityeducation/student-life/10204200/University-societies-the-12-weirdest-student-clubs.html" id="10204200">
             &gt;&gt; IN PICTURES: The 12 weirdest student clubs
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Make sure bills are fair  and paid
           </strong>
           <br/>
           All-inclusive accommodation bills may seem to be the easiest option, but they often work out as the costliest. Many landlords will estimate bills in their favour, so students can often end up paying 5 or so extra a week each to cover utilities that they didnt use.
          </p>
          <p>
           You will have to learn housekeeping at some point, and university is not a bad place to start. Paying your own bills may also spur your environmental responsibility, as it is an excellent inducement to turn off the lights when you go out.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Inventories
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Insist on an inventory from the landlord before moving in to see whats provided, and to verify the existing condition of the accommodation. This has benefits for both sides: there are many unscrupulous landlords who will claim that damage has been done by tenants when really it was pre-existing.
          </p>
          <p>
           Parents should ensure their children are forensic when signing the inventory and take photos of anything dodgy or dirty. Try the mattress out  if it has springs coming through it, insist that it is changed. Ensure that any facilities that arent working, or unsatisfactory conditions such as damp, are signed off by the landlord with a promise to be fixed within two weeks.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/universityeducation/student-finance/10218590/University-accommodation-12-cheapest-student-rooms.html" id="10218590">
             &gt;&gt; IN PICTURES: The 12 cheapest student rooms
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Contracts
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Make sure that your parents are guarantors of the contract  they will have the right to negotiate any queries that arise over the tenancy.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Negotiate the rent in advance
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Some landlords will be content to offer a discount in return for a years money paid upfront  which will also do wonders for the peace of mind of both student and parents.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Take meter readings the day you move in
           </strong>
           <br/>
           You dont want to pay for someone elses use!
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Tenancy agreements
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Be aware that if you sign a joint tenancy agreement, you might be liable for any default in payment if a housemate drops out. Get details from all other tenants  and their parents if possible  at the beginning of term, so that you are able to track anyone down should you need to get in touch.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/10288835/Student-life-how-to-prepare-for-university.html" id="10288835">
             &gt;&gt; Student life: how to prepare for university
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           <strong>
            Be nice to your landlord
           </strong>
           <br/>
           A good tenant develops and maintains a good relationship with the landlord. Pay rent by standing order at the beginning of each term, and if theres a problem give the landlord advance warning  most landlords have a heart.
          </p>
          <p>
           But dont abuse any goodwill: turning the house into a tip will upset your landlord, who will be justified in retaining a portion of the deposit.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Draw up a cleaning rota at the beginning of the tenancy to save a lot of arguments
           </strong>
           <br/>
           At the end of the tenancy  after exams  no one will feel like pulling their Marigold gloves on to clean up.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Travel
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Many universities make students clear their rooms each holiday. Travelling backwards and forwards can be a real pain. Check if there are other students who come from your area  try to arrange lift-sharing.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Get a car
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Maybe its time your parents forked out on a car? Point out the days of travelling saved, the peace of mind from knowing that offspring will not be cowering on night buses, etc.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/universityeducation/student-life/10286842/Student-accommodation-tips-for-settling-in.html" id="10286842">
             &gt;&gt; Student accommodation: tips for settling in
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Dont take too much stuff
           </strong>
           <br/>
           No, really. There is never enough room to store everything and youll end up bringing half of it home  whether or not you have your own car in which to do so.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Storage
           </strong>
           <br/>
           By the same token, buy storage bags to hang on the back of doors, and boxes to go under the bed  they will be used, and they dont cost much.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Learn some good basic recipes
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Your funds will soon disappear if spent on takeaways, and a diet of pizza is no defence against the cold viruses doing the university rounds throughout the winter.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            The fridge
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Take plastic boxes labelled with your name to keep food in a shared fridge. It wont stop people borrowing stuff but it will at least give them pause for thought.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            <a href="/education/universityeducation/student-finance/10268950/Student-finance-top-5-student-budget-apps.html" id="10268950">
             &gt;&gt; Student finance: top 5 student budget apps
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Utensils
           </strong>
           <br/>
           While establishing what belongs to whom in the fridge, try to work out who has what cooking utensils. A household needs only one wok, even if you all have to take turns washing it up.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Take flip-flops to wear in the communal shower
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Other students are very generous with their verrucae or other foot nasties.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Check your insurance to see if your possessions are covered
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Forking out for a new laptop halfway through term is not a welcome expense.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.thestudentroom.co.uk" target="_blank">
            <i>
             The Student Room
            </i>
           </a>
           <i>
            is a forum run by students for students offering useful tips and information
           </i>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Check out our
            <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/education/universityeducation/student-life/" target="_blank">
             student life
            </a>
            section for more advice.
           </strong>
          </p>

                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
