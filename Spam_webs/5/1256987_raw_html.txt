   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Obfuscation
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>

     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">

      </a>

     </span>
    </div>

     <div class="hatnote">
      For the term as used in computer science, see
      <a href="/wiki/Obfuscation_(software)" title="Obfuscation (software)">
       obfuscation (software)
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Obfuscation
      </b>
      is the
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/obscure#Adjective" title="wikt:obscure">
       obscuring
      </a>
      of intended
      <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(linguistics)" title="Meaning (linguistics)">
       meaning
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">
       communication
      </a>
      , making the message
      <a href="/wiki/Mental_confusion" title="Mental confusion">
       confusing
      </a>
      , willfully
      <a href="/wiki/Ambiguity" title="Ambiguity">
       ambiguous
      </a>
      , or harder to understand. It may be
      <a href="/wiki/Intention" title="Intention">
       intentional
      </a>
      or unintentional (although the former is usually
      <a href="/wiki/Connotation" title="Connotation">
       connoted
      </a>
      ) and may result from
      <a href="/wiki/Circumlocution" title="Circumlocution">
       circumlocution
      </a>
      (yielding wordiness) or from use of
      <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">
       jargon
      </a>
      or even
      <a href="/wiki/Argot" title="Argot">
       argot
      </a>
      (yielding economy of words but excluding
      <a href="/wiki/Ingroups_and_outgroups" title="Ingroups and outgroups">
       outsiders
      </a>
      from the communicative value). Unintended obfuscation in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expository_writing" title="Expository writing">
       expository writing
      </a>
      is usually a natural trait of early
      <a href="/wiki/Draft_document" title="Draft document">
       drafts
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Writing_process" title="Writing process">
       writing process
      </a>
      , when the
      <a href="/wiki/Composition_(language)" title="Composition (language)">
       composition
      </a>
      is not yet advanced, and it can be improved with
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">
       critical thinking
      </a>
      and revising, either by the writer or by another person with sufficient
      <a href="/wiki/Reading_comprehension" title="Reading comprehension">
       reading comprehension
      </a>
      and editing skills.
     </p>
     <p>
      The name comes from Latin
      <i>
       obfuscatio
      </i>
      ,
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/obfuscate#Etymology" title="wikt:obfuscate">
       from
       <i>
        obfuscre
       </i>
       ("to darken")
      </a>
      .
      <b>
       Obfustication
      </b>
      is a common variant of the name, especially in British English.
      <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">
       Synonyms
      </a>
      include
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/beclouding" title="wikt:beclouding">
        beclouding
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abstrusity#Noun" title="wikt:abstrusity">
        abstrusity
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Obscurantism" title="Obscurantism">
       Obscurantism
      </a>
      is intentional obscurity, whether by
      <a href="/wiki/Secrecy" title="Secrecy">
       withholding communication
      </a>
      , obfuscating it, or both.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Obfuscation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Obfuscation may be used for many purposes. Doctors have been accused of using
      <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">
       jargon
      </a>
      to conceal unpleasant facts from a patient; American author
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">
       Michael Crichton
      </a>
      claimed that
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_writing" title="Medical writing">
       medical writing
      </a>
      is a "highly skilled, calculated attempt to confuse the reader".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/B._F._Skinner" title="B. F. Skinner">
       B. F. Skinner
      </a>
      , noted
      <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
       psychologist
      </a>
      , commented on medical notation as a form of multiple audience control, which allows the doctor to communicate to the pharmacist things which might be opposed by the patient if they could understand it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eschew_obfuscation">
       Eschew obfuscation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Obfuscation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eschew obfuscation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "Eschew obfuscation", also stated as "eschew obfuscation, espouse elucidation", is a humorous
      <a href="/wiki/Fumblerules" title="Fumblerules">
       fumblerule
      </a>
      used by English teachers and professors when lecturing about proper writing techniques. Literally, the phrase means "avoid being unclear" or "avoid being unclear, support being clear", but the use of relatively uncommon words causes confusion in much of the audience (those lacking the
      <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
       vocabulary
      </a>
      ), making the statement an example of
      <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">
       irony
      </a>
      , and more precisely a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterological" title="Heterological">
       heterological
      </a>
      phrase. The phrase has appeared in print at least as early as 1959, when it was used as a section heading in a
      <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
       NASA
      </a>
      document.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An earlier similar phrase appears in
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">
       Mark Twain
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Fenimore Cooper's Literary Offenses
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where he lists rule fourteen of good writing as "eschew
      <a href="/wiki/Surplusage" title="Surplusage">
       surplusage
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      The philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Grice" title="Paul Grice">
       Paul Grice
      </a>
      used the phrase in the "Maxim of Manner", one of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gricean_maxims" title="Gricean maxims">
       Gricean maxims
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White-box_cryptography" title="White-box cryptography">
       white-box cryptography
      </a>
      , obfuscation refers to the protection of cryptographic keys from extraction when they are under the control of the adversary, e.g., as part of a
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">
       DRM
      </a>
      scheme.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Network_security">
       Network security
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Obfuscation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Network security">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Network_security" title="Network security">
       network security
      </a>
      , obfuscation refers to methods used to obscure an attack payload from inspection by network protection systems.
     </p>
