        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Fukushima Debris Island The Size Of Texas Near US West Coast ( Oh Yes. Were Fuc$ed )
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="entry-date">
          <a href="http://www.worldwidehippies.com/2013/11/06/fukushima-debris-island-the-size-of-texas-near-us-west-coast/" rel="bookmark">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-11-06T12:28:46+00:00">
            November 6, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="byline">
          <span class="author vcard">
           <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.worldwidehippies.com/author/citizen-journalist-exchange/" rel="author">
            Citizen Journalist Exchange
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="comments-link">
          <a href="http://www.worldwidehippies.com/2013/11/06/fukushima-debris-island-the-size-of-texas-near-us-west-coast/#respond">
           Leave a comment
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <strong>
          by
          <a href="http://www.zerohedge.com/users/tyler-durden">
           Tyler Durden
          </a>
         </strong>
         ,zerohedge.com 
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/www.worldwidehippies.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/f.jpeg">
          <img alt="f" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-90844" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/www.worldwidehippies.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/f.jpeg?resize=474%2C315" style="border-width: 1px; border-color: black; border-style: solid;"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         While it took Japan over two years to admit the Fukushima situation on the ground is 
         <a href="http://www.zerohedge.com/news/2013-09-13/tepco-official-admits-fukushima-out-control">
          out of control
         </a>
         , a development many had predicted for years, a just as important topic is what are the implications of this uncontrolled radioactive disaster on not only the local environment and society but also globally, particularly Japans neighbor across the Pacific  the US.
        </p>
        <p>
         To be sure, there has been much speculation, much of it unjustified, in the past two years debating when, how substantial and how acute any potential debris from Fukushima would be on the US. Which is why it was somewhat surprising to see the NOAA come out with
         <a href="http://marinedebris.noaa.gov/tsunamidebris/debris_model.html">
          its own modeling effort
         </a>
         , which shows that not only some buoyant items first reached the Pacific Northwest coast during winter 2011-2012
         <strong>
          but to openly confirm that a debris field weighing over 1 million tons, and larger than Texas is now on the verge of hitting the American coastline, just west off the state of California.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Obviously, the NOAA in releasing such a stunner could well be hammered by the administration for inciting panic which is why it caveated its disclosure carefully:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <p>
          Many variables affect where the debris will go and when. Items will sink, disperse, and break up along the way, and winds and ocean currents constantly change,
          <strong>
           making it very difficult to predict an exact date and location for the debris arrival on our shores.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          The model gives NOAA an understanding of where debris from the tsunami may be located today, because it incorporates how winds and ocean currents since the event may have moved items through the Pacific Ocean. This model is a snapshot of where debris may be now, but it does not predict when debris will reach U.S. shores in the future.
          <strong>
           Its a hindcast, rather than a forecast
          </strong>
          . The model also takes into account the fact that winds can move different types of debris at different speeds. For example, wind may push an upright boat (large portion above water) faster than a piece of lumber (floating mostly at and below the surface).
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Still despite this indemnity the NOAA does come stunningly close with an estimate of both the location and size of the debris field. One look at the map below shows clearly why, while the Fed may have the economy and markets grasped firmly in its central-planning fist, when it comes to the environment it may be time to panic:
        </p>
        <figure class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 498px;">
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/www.zerohedge.com/sites/default/files/images/user5/imageroot/2013/11/Fuku%20Marine%20Debris.jpg">
          <img alt="" class=" " data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/www.zerohedge.com/sites/default/files/images/user5/imageroot/2013/11/Fuku%20Marine%20Debris_0.jpg?resize=474%2C366" style="border-width: 1px; border-color: black; border-style: solid;"/>
         </a>
         <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
          Click to enlarge
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         <em>
          Source:
          <a href="http://marinedebris.noaa.gov/info/images/gnome_graphic.jpg">
           NOAA
          </a>
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Some of the disclosures surrounding the map:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Japan Ministry of the Environment estimates that 5 million tons of debris washed into the ocean.
         </li>
         <li>
          They further estimated that 70% of that debris sank near the coast of Japan soon after the event.
         </li>
         <li>
          Model Results: High windage items may have reached the Pacific Northwest coast as early as winter 2011-2012.
         </li>
         <li>
          Majority of modeled particles are still dispersed north and east of the Hawaiian Archipelago.
         </li>
         <li>
          NOAA expects widely scattered debris may show up intermittently along shorelines for a long period of time, over the next year, or longer.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         In light of these revelations which come not from some tinfoil website but the Department of Commerces National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, it becomes clear why there has been virtually zero mention of any of these debris traffic patterns on the mainstream media in recent history, or ever.
         <a href="http://www.zerohedge.com/news/2013-11-06/fukushima-debris-island-size-texas-near-us-west-coast" target="_blank">
          Read more
         </a>
        </p>
