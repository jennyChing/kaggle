     <p>
      <b>
       Canadian cuisine
      </b>
      varies widely depending on the regions of the nation. The three earliest cuisines of Canada have
      <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">
       First Nations
      </a>
      , English, Scottish and French roots, with the traditional cuisine of
      <a href="/wiki/English_Canada" title="English Canada">
       English Canada
      </a>
      closely related to
      <a href="/wiki/British_cuisine" title="British cuisine">
       British
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Scottish_cuisine" title="Scottish cuisine">
       Scottish cuisines
      </a>
      , while the traditional cuisine of
      <a href="/wiki/French_Canada" title="French Canada">
       French Canada
      </a>
      has evolved from
      <a href="/wiki/French_cuisine" title="French cuisine">
       French cuisine
      </a>
      and the winter provisions of
      <a href="/wiki/Coureur_des_bois" title="Coureur des bois">
       fur traders
      </a>
      . With subsequent waves of immigration in the 19th and 20th century from
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">
       Central
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">
       Southern
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
       Eastern Europe
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
       South Asia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">
       East Asia
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
       Caribbean
      </a>
      , the regional cuisines were subsequently augmented.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
       Definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_cuisine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although certain dishes may be identified as "Canadian" due to the ingredients used or the origin of its inception, an overarching style of Canadian cuisine is more difficult to define. Some Canadians such as the former Canadian
      <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">
       prime minister
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Clark" title="Joe Clark">
       Joe Clark
      </a>
      believe that Canadian cuisine is a collage of dishes from the cuisines of other cultures. Clark himself has been paraphrased to have noted: "Canada has a cuisine of cuisines. Not a stew pot, but a smorgasbord.".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smorg_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-smorg-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some have sought to define Canadian cuisine along the line of how
      <a href="/wiki/Claus_Meyer" title="Claus Meyer">
       Claus Meyer
      </a>
      defined Nordic cuisine in his
      <i>
       Manifesto for the New Nordic Kitchen
      </i>
      ; namely that dishes in Canadian cuisine should reflect Canadian seasons, that they should use locally sourced ingredients that thrive in the Canadian climate, and that they are combined with good taste and health in mind.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EV_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Others believe that Canadian cuisine is still in the process of being defined from the cuisines of the numerous cultures that have influenced it, and that being a culture of many cultures, Canada and its cuisine is less about a particular dish but rather how the ingredients are combined.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EV_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_contributions">
       Cultural contributions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_cuisine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultural contributions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="825" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg/220px-Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg/330px-Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg/440px-Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ojibwe_woman_tapping_for_sugar_maple_syrup.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A 1908 photo of an
        <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe" title="Ojibwe">
         Ojibwe
        </a>
        woman tapping for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_sap" title="Tree sap">
         tree sap
        </a>
        , which is made into
        <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">
         maple syrup
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Canadian food has been shaped and impacted by continual waves of immigration, with the types of foods and from different regions and periods of Canada reflecting this immigration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hj_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hj-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aboriginal_peoples">
       Aboriginal peoples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_cuisine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aboriginal peoples">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The traditional aboriginal cuisine of Canada was based on a mixture of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_game" title="Wild game">
       wild game
      </a>
      , foraged foods, and farmed agricultural products. Each region of Canada with its own
      <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">
       First Nations
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">
       Inuit
      </a>
      people used their local resources and own food preparation techniques for their cuisines.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">
       Maple syrup
      </a>
      was first collected and used by aboriginal people of Eastern Canada and North Eastern US.
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      is the world's largest producer of
      <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">
       maple syrup
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The origins of maple syrup production are not clear though the first syrups were made by repeatedly freezing the collected maple sap and removing the ice to concentrate the sugar in the remaining sap.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-history-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Maple syrup is one of the most commonly consumed Canadian foods of Aboriginal origins.
     </p>
     <p>
      In most of the Canadian West Coast and Pacific Northwest,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_salmon" title="Pacific salmon">
       Pacific salmon
      </a>
      was an important food resource to the First Nations peoples, along with certain
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">
       marine mammals
      </a>
      . Salmon were consumed fresh when spawning or smoked dry to create a
      <a href="/wiki/Jerky" title="Jerky">
       jerky
      </a>
      -like food that could be stored year-round. The latter food is commonly known and sold as "salmon jerky". Whipped
      <a href="/wiki/Shepherdia_canadensis" title="Shepherdia canadensis">
       Soapberry
      </a>
      , known as
      <i>
       xoosum
      </i>
      (HOO-shum, "Indian ice cream") in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_Salish" title="Interior Salish">
       Interior Salish
      </a>
      languages of British Columbia, is consumed similarly to ice cream or as a cranberry-cocktail-like drink. It is known for being a kidney tonic, which are called
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agutak" title="Agutak">
        agutak
       </a>
      </i>
      in arctic Canada (with animal/fish fat).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mattak.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Mattak.jpg/220px-Mattak.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Mattak.jpg/330px-Mattak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Mattak.jpg/440px-Mattak.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mattak.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sliced and prepared
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Muktuk" title="Muktuk">
          muktuk
         </a>
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the arctic, Inuit traditionally survived on a diet consisting of land and marine mammals, fish, and foraged plant products. Meats were consumed fresh but also often prepared, cached, and allowed to fermented into
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Igunaq" title="Igunaq">
        igunaq
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kiviak" title="Kiviak">
        kiviak
       </a>
      </i>
      . These fermented meats have the consistency and smell of certain soft aged cheeses. Snacks such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Muktuk" title="Muktuk">
        muktuk
       </a>
      </i>
      , which consist of whale skin and blubber is eaten plain, though sometimes dipped in
      <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">
       soy sauce
      </a>
      . Chunks of muktuk are sliced with an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ulu" title="Ulu">
        ulu
       </a>
      </i>
      prior to or during consumption. Fish are eaten boiled, fried, and prior to today's settlements, often in dried forms. The so-called "
      <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_potato" title="Eskimo potato">
       Eskimo potato
      </a>
      " (Inuit:
      <i>
       oatkuk
      </i>
      :
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Claytonia_tuberosa" title="Claytonia tuberosa">
        Claytonia tuberosa
       </a>
      </i>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and other "
      <a href="/wiki/Mousefood" title="Mousefood">
       mousefoods
      </a>
      " are some of the plants consumed in the arctic.
     </p>
     <p>
      Foods such as "
      <a href="/wiki/Bannock_(food)" title="Bannock (food)">
       bannock
      </a>
      ", popular with First Nations and Inuit, reflect the historic exchange of these cultures with Scottish fur traders, who brought with them new ingredients and foods.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gobc_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gobc-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Common contemporary consumption of bannock,
      <a href="/wiki/Powdered_milk" title="Powdered milk">
       powdered milk
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Bologna_sausage" title="Bologna sausage">
       bologna
      </a>
      by aboriginal Canadians reflects the legacy of Canadian colonialism in the prohibition of hunting and fishing, and the institutional food rations provided to
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_reserve" title="Indian reserve">
       Indian reserves
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Due to similarities in treatment under colonialism, many
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
       Native American
      </a>
      communities throughout the continent consume similar food items with some emphasis on local ingredients.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Europeans">
       Europeans
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_cuisine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Europeans">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Settlers and traders from the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
       British Isles
      </a>
      account for the culinary influences of early English Canada in the Maritimes and
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ontario" title="Southern Ontario">
       Southern Ontario
      </a>
      (Upper Canada),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hj_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hj-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while French settlers account for the cuisine of southern
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
       Quebec
      </a>
      (Lower Canada),
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ontario" title="Northern Ontario">
       Northern Ontario
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">
       New Brunswick
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hj_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hj-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Southwestern regions of Ontario have strong Dutch and
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
       Scandinavian
      </a>
      influences.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Canada's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairie" title="Canadian Prairie">
       Prairie
      </a>
      provinces, which saw massive immigration from Eastern and Northern Europe in the pre-WW1 era,
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_cuisine" title="Ukrainian cuisine">
       Ukrainian
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_Germany" title="Cuisine of Germany">
       German
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_cuisine" title="Polish cuisine">
       Polish cuisines
      </a>
      are strong culinary influences. Also noteworthy in some areas of the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_Interior" title="British Columbia Interior">
       British Columbia Interior
      </a>
      and the Prairies is the cuisine of the
      <a href="/wiki/Doukhobor" title="Doukhobor">
       Doukhobors
      </a>
      , Russian-descended vegetarians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hj_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-hj-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterloo_Region" title="Waterloo Region">
       Waterloo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      , region and the southern portion of the Province of
      <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">
       Manitoba
      </a>
      have traditions of
      <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" title="Mennonite">
       Mennonite
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
       Germanic
      </a>
      cookery.
     </p>
     <p>
      The cuisines of
      <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">
       Newfoundland
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritimes" title="Maritimes">
       Maritime provinces
      </a>
      derive mainly from
      <a href="/wiki/British_cuisine" title="British cuisine">
       British
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_cuisine" title="Irish cuisine">
       Irish
      </a>
      cooking, with a preference for salt-cured fish, beef, and pork.
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">
       Manitoba
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">
       British Columbia
      </a>
      also maintain strong British cuisine traditions.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Canadian" title="Jewish Canadian">
       Jewish immigrants
      </a>
      to Canada during the late 1800s played a significant culinary role within Canada, chiefly renowned for
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal-style_bagel" title="Montreal-style bagel">
       Montreal-style bagels
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal-style_smoked_meat" title="Montreal-style smoked meat">
       Montreal-style smoked meat
      </a>
      . A regional variation of both emerged within
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winnipeg,_Manitoba" title="Winnipeg, Manitoba">
       Winnipeg, Manitoba
      </a>
      's Jewish community, which also derived Winnipeg-style Cheesecake from New York recipes. Winnipeg has given birth to numerous other unique dishes, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Schmoo_torte" title="Schmoo torte">
       schmoo torte
      </a>
      and "co-op style" rye bread and cream cheese.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asian">
       East Asian
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

       : Sold bottled as Sortilege, this drink combines Canadian whisky and maple syrup
      </li>

       <a href="/wiki/Caribou_(drink)" title="Caribou (drink)">

       </a>

      <li>

       <a href="/wiki/Caesar_(cocktail)" title="Caesar (cocktail)">
        Caesar
       </a>
       , originally called the Bloody Caesar, is a cocktail made from
       <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">
        vodka
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clamato" title="Clamato">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire_sauce" title="Worcestershire sauce">
        Worcestershire sauce

       <a href="/wiki/Tabasco_sauce" title="Tabasco sauce">
        Tabasco sauce
       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_salt" title="Table salt">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Celery_salt" title="Celery salt">
        celery salt
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Celery" title="Celery">
        celery
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Horseradish" title="Horseradish">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Bouillon_(broth)" title="Bouillon (broth)">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Street_food">
       Street food
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_cuisine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Street food">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      While most major cities in Canada (including
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      , in a pilot project) offer a variety of
      <a href="/wiki/Street_food" title="Street food">
       street food
      </a>
      , regional "specialties" are notable. While
      <a href="/wiki/Poutine" title="Poutine">
       poutine
      </a>
      is available in most of the country, it is far more common in
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
       Quebec
      </a>
      . Similarly,
      <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">
       sausage
      </a>
      stands can be found across Canada, but are far more common in
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      (often sold from mobile canteen trucks, usually referred to as "fry trucks" or "chip trucks" and the sausages "street meat") than in
      <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">
       Vancouver
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_British_Columbia" title="Victoria, British Columbia">
       Victoria
      </a>
      (where the "Mr. Tube Steak" franchise is notable and the term "smokies" or "smokeys" refers to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukrainian_sausage" title="Ukrainian sausage">
       Ukrainian sausage
      </a>
      rather than frankfurters).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      offers a number of specialties including
      <a href="/wiki/Shish_taouk_(Montreal)" title="Shish taouk (Montreal)">
       Shish taouk
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal_hot_dog" title="Montreal hot dog">
       Montreal hot dog
      </a>
      , and dollar
      <a href="/wiki/Falafel" title="Falafel">
       falafels
      </a>
      . Although falafel is widespread in Vancouver,
      <a href="/wiki/Pizza" title="Pizza">
       pizza
      </a>
      slices are much more popular. Vancouver also has many
      <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">
       sushi
      </a>
      establishments.
      <a href="/wiki/Shawarma" title="Shawarma">
       Shawarma
      </a>
      is quite prevalent in
      <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">
       Ottawa
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Ontario" title="Windsor, Ontario">
       Windsor
      </a>
      , while
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halifax_Regional_Municipality" title="Halifax Regional Municipality">
       Halifax
      </a>
      offers its own unique version of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%B6ner_kebab" title="Dner kebab">
       Dner kebab
      </a>
      called the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donair" title="Donair">
       Donair
      </a>
      , which features a distinctive sauce made from
      <a href="/wiki/Condensed_milk" title="Condensed milk">
       condensed milk
      </a>
      , sugar, garlic and vinegar.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_cream_truck" title="Ice cream truck">
       Ice cream trucks
      </a>
      can be seen (and often heard due to a jingle being broadcast on loudspeakers) nationwide during the summer months. Recently, the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">
       Toronto
      </a>
      has encouraged street vendors from around the world to sell their food.
     </p>
