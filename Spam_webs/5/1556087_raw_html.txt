         <h1>
          Climbing the Corporate Ladder: How to Do It in a Retail Setting
         </h1>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="article-container" id="TNArticleBodySection">
        <div id="article-body">
         <p>
          Working in retail provides many career opportunities you might not otherwise think about, even if you start out as a cashier or a stocker. With more than
          <a href="http://www.bls.gov/ooh/sales/retail-sales-workers.htm" target="_blank">
           4.6 million jobs
          </a>
          in the industry, it's a great one to start out in if you want to build a foundation for your future. With that said, here's how to climb the corporate ladder in a retail setting.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          <img alt="woman executive talking tto businessmen" class="media-image" src="http://artstg.icbdr.com/sites/all/files/5765084924_06c236f60d.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Photo via
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/nestle/5765084924/sizes/m/" target="_blank">
           Flickr
          </a>
          by Nestle
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Become a Leader Even if You're Not in a Leadership Role
         </h2>
         <p>
          You don't have to get promoted to a leadership role to prove that you're a natural leader. Take initiative and show managers why they should consider promoting you to a role more suitable to your talents. Some ways in which you can showcase your leadership is by facilitating meetings with other team members to inspire them, leading by example in all the work you do, and stepping up without anyone asking you to do so. Remember, leaders are emotionally strong, well-disciplined, and good listeners. Exemplify these strengths and you'll take a step up.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Bring Fresh Ideas to the Table
         </h2>
         <p>
          The more valuable you are to a company, the better your chances of moving up its corporate ladder. To show just how much you're worth, bring fresh ideas to the table for improving anything to do with store operations. Selling an idea to your boss (or bosses) is an art, though. Make sure you communicate your idea in a way that your boss likes, embrace your boss's agenda before your own, and pitch ideas you know your boss can approve. To build even stronger relationships with higher-ups, give them the credit for your ideas to show them your loyalty.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Work Harder Than Anyone Else in the Store
         </h2>
         <p>
          As clich as it sounds, hard work truly does pay off. Working hard exemplifies that you take pride in your work and that you're passionate about the company you work for. As long as you don't feel taken advantage of at your store, continue to put in the extra hours and effort. Doing so will show other employees and managers that you're committed to the cause and deserving of any steps up the ladder you'll receive once time permits. And once you're manager, employees who worked alongside you will respect you because they know the time you put in.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          <img alt="retail workers sorting clothes" class="media-image" src="http://artstg.icbdr.com/sites/all/files/6904985795_3186f6606b.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Photo via
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/atgeist/6904985795/sizes/m/" target="_blank">
           Flickr
          </a>
          by TownPost Network
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Make Sure That Your Presence is Known
         </h2>
         <p>
          Although working harder than anyone else on the floor is one way to make your presence known within a company, there are other ways to make sure people know who you are and what you can contribute. Speaking up at company meetings, initiating friendly conversations with coworkers in the break room, lending a helping hand when it's needed -- it all helps people remember your name and makes you stand out. Remember not to get too much into anyone else's business and remain mindful of what you say to others. Keep it appropriate at all times.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Put Your Goals on Paper and Plan a Solid Strategy to Achieve Them
         </h2>
         <p>
          Although it helps solidify your current position with the company, climbing up the corporate ladder isn't just about working hard in the store. To get to where you want to be on the ladder, you'll likely need to do a lot of others things to make you the best candidate for the higher-up positions. When putting your goals on paper and planning a solid strategy to achieve them, think about the other variables that'll help propel you to the top. Education, side projects with the company, strong relationships with the bosses -- these will all work in your favor when it's time.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Take Initiative and Diversify Your Position Within the Company
         </h2>
         <p>
          Starting as a cashier and not trying to branch out is one way to show the bosses that you don't care about trying new things and taking on harder challenges. Not only will learning all the floor positions at a store make you more versatile within the company, but it'll come in handy once you start reaching for the higher-up positions. Knowing all aspects of how the store runs will increase your chances of becoming a manager and beyond. After all, there are many stories of people who
          <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2010/10/22/entrylevel-to-ceo-people-_n_772308.html" target="_blank">
           started at the bottom and made their way
          </a>
          to the top -- meaning CEO.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Involve Yourself in Company Events and Activities
         </h2>
         <p>
          Boxing yourself in and avoiding all outside events and activities is another way to show the bosses that you couldn't care less about the company. Attending these events shows that you care about the company and its employees, giving you the perfect opportunity to network and make your presence known. Also, if you notice that your company hasn't done anything in a while, be the one to take initiative and come up with an event or activity that everyone can take part in. No matter whether it's a potluck or a get together at your home, it'll show you care.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          <img alt="girl filling out application" class="media-image" src="http://artstg.icbdr.com/sites/all/files/202136435_d76fcecd5e.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Photo via
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/gtmcknight/202136435/sizes/m/" target="_blank">
           Flickr
          </a>
          by gtmcknight
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Apply for Job Openings That Progress Your Career
         </h2>
         <p>
          Aimlessly staring at the job board at work won't do much for your career, but proactively looking for new positions and applying for them will show that you want to move up. Even though you might not get the first or second job you apply for, keeping your name in the mind's of human resource managers is a good thing. When applying, remember to
          <a href="http://www.careerbuilder.com/article/cb-1978-salaries-promotions-5-secrets-to-climbing-the-ladder-faster/" target="_blank">
           think outside yourself
          </a>
          and examine the company's needs and goals. Ask yourself what your strengths are and how you can contribute to those needs and goals. Align them with your resume and application.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Although climbing the corporate ladder might seem like an Everest-like challenge, it's one that's very much possible if you put your best foot forward. Take the aforementioned points to heart and do your best to live by them.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Are you manager or higher-up that started his or her retail career on the floor? Do you remember the steps you took to get from behind the register or the stockroom? Leave a comment below and share your inspiring story.
         </p>
