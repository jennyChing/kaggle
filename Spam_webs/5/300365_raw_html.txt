         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           Coach Wayne knows ice hockey. And Coach Wayne is telling it like it is.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Developing skills comes first," he says. "Games are the reward for practicing."
          </p>
          <p>
           He pauses, letting me absorb this bit of wisdom on the world's fastest team sport.
          </p>
          <p>
           "But practices need to be fun," Coach Wayne adds. "Players need to hear more positives than negatives."
          </p>
          <p>
           Wayne Cerzullo is a hockey coach with 20 years of experience. But right now, he isn't talking about the big leagues. We're at Twin Ponds ice rink in Mechanicsburg, Pennsylvania, and we're talking about my own eight-year-old Sidney Crosby. Except my big league player can't lace up his own skates yet.
          </p>
          <p>
           I had no idea I'd be a hockey mom.
          </p>
          <p>
           I was a softball and gymnastics mom, two sports I participated in as a child. And I'm a soccer mom, but show me a suburban parent who isn't hauling around kids in cleats and shin guards.
          </p>
          <p>
           These days, I'm also chauffeur to a small child who lugs a hockey equipment bag that's as big as a small child.
          </p>
          <p>
           And that's one of the things I love about this sport: hockey tires kids out.
          </p>
          <p>
           My son was six years old and playing hockey on a "puddle league" with Coach Wayne when I first witnessed this rarest of child-raising miraclesthe miracle of "sitting still." After an hour on the ice, my child sat still without needing a screen. He sat still without being told to sit still.
          </p>
          <p>
           Even now, my son ends every hockey session by collapsing in a stupor of happy exhaustion.
          </p>
          <p>
           During practices, these kids move. They play keep-away on ice. They use big sticks to shoot pucks across the rink in games of tag. Hockey is a rough-and-tumble antidote to the bubble-wrapped childhood. And Coach Wayne looks like he's having as much fun as the kids.
          </p>
          <p>
           "If I'm not having fun, then why coach?" Coach Wayne says. "Why would a parent want a coach who is looking at his watch?"
          </p>
          <p>
           Hockey for younger kids is about good times and developing individual skills. During beginner games, the scoreboard is turned off. If you watch a hockey game played by second graders, you quickly understand why.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Before kids can play well as a team, they need to learn skating skills," says Coach Wayne. "Once you can skate fluently, you can put the puck on the stick."
          </p>
          <p>
           Little kid hockey is more about staying upright, less about slapshots.
          </p>
          <p>
           Coach Craig Horowitz at Klick Lewis Arena in Palmyra, Pennsylvania agrees. Coach Craig started as a competitive figure skater and worked for 17 years with Disney On Ice. Now a figure skating coach himself, Craig also teaches skating skills to up-and-coming hockey players. He says that good skaters are good hockey players.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Hockey skaters learn edge quality, control and agility on ice. If you can't skate, you can't get to the puck and participate."
          </p>
          <p>
           Craig coaches his five-year-old son's team and says that having fun is goal number one.
          </p>
          <p>
           "You make it fun for kids, and they learn skills without realizing it," Coach Craig says. "Younger kids learn coordination and build concentration. During games, I see their excitement and big smiles."
          </p>
          <p>
           Local skating rinks offer learn-to-skate group classes that teach the basic skills girls and boys need for hockey. Additional beginner hockey classes and one-on-one lessons with figure skating and hockey coaches can help a kid nail those quick turns and stops.
          </p>
          <p>
           If your ice princess wants to trade spirals for breakaways, the Hershey Jr. Bears Girls sponsors free
           <a href="http://hersheyjrbearsgirls.com/news.php?news_id=524293">
            Learn To Play Ice Hockey
           </a>
           classes especially for girls. And throughout the year, USA Hockey promotes
           <a href="http://www.tryhockeyforfree.com/">
            Try Hockey For Free
           </a>
           days for boys and girls, ages 4-9 years old.
          </p>
          <p>
           For parents wondering whether that first step onto the ice will lead to college scholarships or an NHL contract, Coach Wayne has a few more words of wisdom:
          </p>
          <p>
           "As they get older, kids figure out their own goals for participating in the sport. The best way to be a good hockey parent is to sit back and let the kids take you on the ride. "
          </p>
          <p>
           As a parent, shouldn't I push my kid to be successful in sports?
          </p>
          <p>
           Coach Wayne says motivation is the coach's job. My job is to get my kid to the rink on time.
          </p>
          <p>
           Then he smiles and adds, "If parents have something to prove, they can always put on skates and play hockey with their kids."
          </p>
          <p>
           Puck drop. Face-off.
          </p>
          <p>
           That's OK, Coach. I'm happy hanging out on the bench.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            To learn more about youth ice hockey and skating classes in your area, you can contact these local rinks:
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <a href="http://twinponds.com/">
             Twin Ponds
            </a>
            in Mechanicsburg and Harrisburg, PA
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>

            </a>
            at the Hersheypark Arena and Giant Center in Hershey, PA
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>

          </p>

          </p>
