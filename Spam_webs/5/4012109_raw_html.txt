         <p>
          Whenever I'm home from college, every Monday, Wednesday, and Friday I have two alarms.
         </p>
         <p>
          The first is on my phone, coaxing me from my warm bed at 4:30 in the morning to put on some shorts and grab my keys.
         </p>
         <p>
          The second comes about twenty minutes later in my high school gym the first time the ball touches the hardwood. Something clicks for me - maybe the sound, maybe the lights, maybe the first swish of the net or the groan of my cold shoes as they warm up too. Something gets my blood pumping faster, snaps me awake.
         </p>
         <p>
          My high school basketball coach, a huge mentor in my short life, has opened the gym at this ungodly hour for several years now. Games are to nine points by one's and two's, call your own fouls. Normally 20 to 30 people show up, enabling us to run multiple 5 v 5 games. I first started playing when I was in 8th grade. The majority of people who played were men in their 30's and 40's, fitting in a game before work, with a handful of the young guys and students like me showing up sporadically.
         </p>
         <p>
          I learned more about the game playing in the mornings with these guys than any coach in grade school or summer teams. These guys were good and in shape, but more than that, they were smart. They were the ones who'd spent the time on the court, played hundreds more games, taken thousands more shots than myself. The best part: they didn't come to the mornings to beat up on an 8th grade kid and his buddies. While they may
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>

         not have come there to teach the game and mentor young athletes either, that's exactly what they accomplished.
         <p>
          They had the mental side of the game down. The smart plays, the patience, and of course: the "old man tricks" as they dubbed them. 8th and 9th grade, those tricks worked. Hell, they worked this morning on a college senior playing against the same guys now in their 50's. Slowly though, those tricks and that wisdom began to rub off on me. Players who showed up frequently and played hard began to gain the trust of the regulars. I became a regular.
         </p>
         <p>
          Each guy offered something different, a new skill to learn or another move to master. I didn't go into the morning thinking like this but it's something I've come to realize looking back. One gentleman taught me how to guard the post. Seriously, he backed me down time after time until I began to learn how to deny the pass, position my body, use my weight, etc. He was quick to answer questions and give me pointers after the game, a task not easily done politely if you're familiar with open gym basketball. Another gentleman would pull up two steps from half court, shoot with the wrong foot forward, bank it in, and talk about it until he hit the next one. There I learned people can shoot at any time so be prepared and the only thing a smack-talker wants to hear is more smack, so don't give him the pleasure. A final gentleman couldn't hit a shot, but he rebounded harder than anybody,

         telling me to find him and "box out" or I would hear about it from my teammates.
         <p>
          I use the word "gentleman" for these players because that is what every last one of these guys was. In all the years, I've never seen a fight or a serious dispute. I've never felt uncomfortable or unwelcome there. It was like having 20 different dads when I'd walk onto the court. Some guys were rough around the edges, some guys talked and others didn't, but every last one of them was a good soul.
         </p>
         <p>
          The morning game got some of these guys through tougher times as well. Two gentleman went through clean but emotionally difficult divorces with young children. One gentleman lost 50 pounds, some of it due to his increased frequency at morning ball. But my coach had created an atmosphere that cultivated an attitude of development, friendliness and respect - a safe place where the only thing that mattered was the community and the game. New players were welcomed the same way a guy who hadn't shown his face in ages was: like the prodigal son returning.
         </p>
         <p>
          As I grew older and began to play high school ball, I'd still play off-seasons in the mornings. Many of the guys went to the high school games, knew other players, all coaching but not overstepping. They were involved. As I left for college but came back for breaks, the questions turned to what I was studying, where I was living, and most importantly: are ya playin' any ball? I come back on breaks and play as a

My game and theirs is a little different due to age and physical shape on both sides, but I'm still learning, still getting tricked, still answering questions for 20 dads. Still involved, still curious. I've never been part of a better pick-up game than with the gentleman from morning ball. And I'll say it: I don't think there's another one out there that could beat it.

	  return new Handlebars.SafeString(str);
	};
	linkify = function(safeContent) {
	  return safeContent.replace(LINK_DETECTION_REGEX, function(url) {
	  	brEnd = false;
	  	if(url.indexOf("\n")) {
	  		brEnd = true;
	  	}
	  	url = url.trim();
	    url = url.replace(/(?:\r\n|\r|\n)/g, '');
	    var address;
	    address = /[a-z]+:\/\//.test(url) ? url : "http://" + url;    
	    url = url.replace(/^https?:\/\//, '');
	    link = "&lt;a href='" + address + "' target='_blank' rel='nofollow'&gt;" + url + "&lt;/a&gt;";
	    if(brEnd) {
	    	return link + '&lt;br/&gt;';
	    } else {
	    	return link;
	    }
	  });
	};

	formatTextForHTML = function(content) {
	  var safeContent;
	  safeContent = ESCAPE_EXPRESSION_FUNCTION(content);
	  safeContent = linkify(safeContent);
	  safeContent = safeContent.replace(/(?:\r\n|\r|\n)/g, '&lt;br /&gt;');
	  //safeContent = emojify(safeContent);
	  return MARKSAFE_FUNCTION(safeContent);
	};

	Handlebars.registerHelper('formatTextForHTML', formatTextForHTML);
  </script>
