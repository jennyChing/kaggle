          <h1 class="h1black" style="margin-bottom:12px;">
           106 Kinds of Japanese Food
          </h1>
          <div id="messagebox">
          </div>
          <span class="h5grey">
           posted by
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/johnSpacey" rel="author">
            John Spacey
           </a>
           , July 08, 2015
          </span>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/new-years-japan-5340-183.jpg"/>
            <br/>
            What is Japan's favorite food?
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Japan has been cooking for thousands of years. As an island nation it tends to go its own way. As a result, Japanese food evolved into a unique culinary tradition.   Beyond tradition, Japanese food is a living part of the culture. It's always growing and as a result there are countless Japanese dishes.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            The following are a few foods that all Japanese know well.
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           1.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/makizushi" id="makizushi">
            Makizushi
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Makizushi is
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/sushi">
             sushi
            </a>
            that's rolled into long cylinders.  It may be cut into shorter pieces before being served.   The word makizushi can be translated "sushi roll."   It's often simply called maki.     It usually has
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/nori">
             nori
            </a>
            on the outside but can also be wrapped with leaves such as
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/shiso">
             shiso
            </a>
            .   Maki is filled with
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/sushi-rice">
             sushi rice
            </a>
            and ingredients such as raw fish, cooked fish and vegetables.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/makizushi">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/maki-rolls-japan-91-197.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>

          <h2>
           2.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/miso-soup" id="miso-soup">
            Miso Soup
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            In Japan,
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/miso-soup">
             miso soup
            </a>
            is as important to breakfast as coffee.   It's a hearty soup of
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/dashi">
             dashi
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/miso">
             miso
            </a>
            and tofu.   It often includes a variety of vegetables, seafood and meat.    A good miso soup balances
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/miso">
             ingredients that float with ingredients that sink
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/miso-soup">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/thumbnails/how-to-drink-miso-soup-66.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           3.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yakitori" id="yakitori">
            Yakitori
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Yakitori can be literally translated "grilled chicken."   It's a category of Japanese cuisine that includes
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yakitori">
             dozens of items
            </a>
            that are grilled on thin bamboo skewers including
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yakitori">
             every imaginable part of the chicken
            </a>
            as well as other meats, tofu and vegetables.
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yakitori">
             Yakitori
            </a>
            restaurants are typically
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/japanese-party-foods">
             lively drinking spots
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yakitori">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/yakitori-at-a-matsuri-25-124.jpg"/>

          <h2>
           4.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/tempura" id="tempura">
            Tempura
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Tempura is deep fried fish and vegetables in a light batter.   Care is taken to cook tempura at a low temperature for a short time to preserve the taste of ingredients.     It's served with a light tentsuyu dip.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/tempura">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/ayu-sweetfish-tempura-middle-97.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           5.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/edamame" id="edamame">
            Edamame
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/edamame">
             Young soybeans
            </a>
            cooked in their pod in salty water.     Most restaurants serve frozen edamame.   They taste infinitely better fresh from the farm.    Some fine
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/izakaya-japanese-pubs">
             izakaya
            </a>
            will only serve edamame in season.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/edamame">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/how-to-eat-edamame-64.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           6.
           <a id="ramen">
           </a>
           Ramen
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Hot Chinese-style wheat noodles in a meat, fish,
            <a href="http://food.japan-talk.com/food/new/miso">
             miso
            </a>
            or soy sauce broth.    It's an inexpensive, filling, easy to find snack.    Despite the fact that ramen is cheap, there's a big difference in quality from one shop to the next.   A shop that earns a reputation amongst
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/12-cheap-gourmet-foods-in-Japan">
             ramen aficionados
            </a>
            will regularly have long lines while a shop just next door may be empty.    It's the type of dish that isn't easy to perfect.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/tonkotsu-ramen-78.jpg"/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           7.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/mochi" id="mochi">
            Mochi
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            A sticky variety of Japanese rice known as
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/japanese-rice">
             mochigome
            </a>
            that has been
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/mochi">
             pounded into a paste
            </a>
            .     Toasted and eaten directly.    Also used in a variety of Japanese dishes and desserts.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/mochi">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/midthumb/kids-with-a-mochi-mallet-39-12.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           8.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/okonomiyaki" id="okonomiyaki">
            Okonomiyaki
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            A type of savory Japanese pancake that was traditionally prepared to use up leftovers.     Okonomiyaki restaurants let you customize your order.   In many cases, you can cook your own at a grill in your table.  There's an intense okonomiyaki
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/okonomiyaki">
             rivalry between Osaka, Tokyo and Hiroshima
            </a>
            .    Each town has its own version of the dish.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/okonomiyaki">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/okonomiyaki-skill-77.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           9.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/sashimi" id="sashimi">
            Sashimi
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Raw sliced fish or meat.    Freshness and aesthetics are important to sashimi dishes.    Otherwise, it's just hunks of fish.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/sashimi">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/sashimi-16-3955.jpg"/>

           10.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/oden" id="oden">
            Oden
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Oden is a Japanese hotpot that is ordered item-by-item.    It's a popular
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/japanese-street-food">
             street food
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/konbini">
             konbini
            </a>
            food, winter food and drinking food.   Oden may be cooked in a
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/oden">
             very old broth
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/oden">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/oden-bowl-10.jpg"/>

          <h2>
           11.
           <a id="soba">
           </a>
           Soba
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Thin Japanese buckwheat noodles served chilled with a dipping sauce or in a hot broth.   Whichever you choose, it's perfectly polite to
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/in-Japan-its-ok-to-slurp">
             loudly slurp your noodles
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/Soba-5473-2219.jpg"/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           12.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/natto" id="natto">
            Natto
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            A type of fermented soybean.    It's stinky and slimy with a challenging taste but
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/natto">
             Japan likes it anyway
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/natto">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/mustard-japan-5582-325.jpg"/>

            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>

          <h2>
           13.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/onigiri" id="onigiri">
            Onigiri
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Onigiri is any rice that's designed to be eaten by hand.    It's the Japanese
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/onigiri">
             equivalent of the sandwich
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/onigiri">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/ume-9.jpg"/>

          <h2>
           14.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/japanese-curry-rice" id="japanese-curry-rice">
            Japanese Curry Rice
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            A Japanese curry that's based on  British Navy Curry.   The Imperial Japanese Navy adopted a similar curry in the 19th century.    Japan's navy still serves it every Friday.  The curry spread from the navy to Japan's civilian population in the
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/meiji-era-japan">
             Meiji-era
            </a>
            .     Over the years, it has adapted to local tastes.   It's incredibly bland for a curry.   Nevertheless, it has become one of Japan's most popular dishes.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/japanese-curry-rice">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/standard-looking-curry-3.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>

          <h2>
           15.
           <a id="udon">
           </a>
           Udon
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Thick wheat flour noodles served hot in a soup or chilled with a dipping sauce of
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/dashi">
             dashi
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/mirin">
             mirin
            </a>
            , and
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/shoyu">
             shoyu
            </a>
            .   Udon is a staple of the Japanese diet and is easy to find in Japan.   It's a somewhat thick noodle that's served with dozens of different toppings including tempura, meat, tofu, seafood or vegetables.  It's perfectly acceptable to slurp noodles in Japan but udon should be slurped with care as they tend to spray broth if slurped quickly.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/midthumb/Udon-5474-1136.jpg"/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           16.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/kaiseki" id="kaiseki">
            Kaiseki
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Kaiseki is a type of multicourse Japanese meal.    It's fine cuisine that can include 5-16 courses.  Each course is small and aesthetically pleasing with seasonal ingredients.    Kaiseki is the creation of a chef.  There are no standard kaiseki dishes.     Instead there are
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/kaiseki">
             categories of dishes are are traditionally served
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/kaiseki">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/ryokan-food-16-179.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           17.
           <a id="yakiniku">
           </a>
           Yakiniku
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Yakiniku, literally: grilled meat, is Japanese style barbecue.    Yakiniku restaurants typically feature a coal grill built into the table.   You order from a selection of bite-sized raw meats and vegetable plates and you cook the food yourself.   If you're not skilled at grilling, you may incidentally cause a great deal of smoke and flame.    The staff may rush over to help you put out your fires.  Yakiniku restaurants are the smokiest places in Japan.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/Yakiniku-5517-840.jpg"/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           18.
           <a id="takoyaki">
           </a>
           Takoyaki
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Ball-shaped savory Japanese pancakes with a piece of octopus in the middle.   Optionally topped with
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/beni-shoga">
             beni shoga
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://food.japan-talk.com/food/new/katsuobushi">
             katsuobushi
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/nori">
             nori flakes
            </a>
            , mayonnaise and takoyaki sauce, a sweet
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/shoyu">
             soy sauce
            </a>
            based condiment.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/Takoyaki-5549-1768.jpg"/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           19.
           <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yuba" id="yuba">
            Yuba
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Yuba is Japanese tofu skin.  It can be enjoyed raw with a light dipping sauce.   Fresh yuba has a texture that's
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yuba">
             similar to mozzarella cheese
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/yuba">
             <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/yuba-aesthetic-for-autum-6.jpg"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </span>
          </div>
          <h2>
           20.
           <a id="sukiyaki">
           </a>
           Sukiyaki
          </h2>
          <div class="readability">
           <span class="blogy">
            Sukiyaki is a Japanese stew that's typically cooked in a hotpot on your table as you dine.    It consists of thin-sliced beef, vegetables and tofu in a fairly thick broth of soy sauce, sugar and
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/mirin">
             mirin
            </a>
            .    Sukiyaki is often served with raw eggs as a dipping sauce.   It's a winter food that's thought to warm you up.     As with any Japanese food cooked at your table, sukiyaki is also considered a
            <a href="http://www.japan-talk.com/jt/new/japanese-party-foods">
             party food
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img alt="" src="http://www.japan-talk.com/images/jt/header/Sukiyaki-5518-1947.jpg"/>
