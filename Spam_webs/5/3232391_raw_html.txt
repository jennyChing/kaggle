     <p>
      In physics, the
      <b>
       world line
      </b>
      of an object is the path of that object in 4-
      <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">
       dimensional
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
       spacetime
      </a>
      , tracing the history of its location in space at each instant in time. The concept of "world line" is distinguished from the concept of "orbit" or "trajectory" (such as an
      <i>
       orbit in space
      </i>
      or a
      <i>
       trajectory
      </i>
      of a truck on a road map) by the
      <i>
       time
      </i>
      dimension, and typically encompasses a large area of spacetime wherein
      <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">
       perceptually
      </a>
      straight paths are recalculated to show their (relatively) more absolute position states  to reveal the nature of
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special relativity
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
       gravitational
      </a>
      interactions. The idea of world lines originates in
      <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
       physics
      </a>
      and was pioneered by
      <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski" title="Hermann Minkowski">
       Hermann Minkowski
      </a>
      . The term is now most often used in relativity theories (i.e.,
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special relativity
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      However, world lines are a general way of representing the course of events. The use of it is not bound to any specific theory. Thus in general usage, a world line is the sequential path of personal human events (with
      <i>
       time
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       place
      </i>
      as dimensions) that marks the history of a person
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
       perhaps starting at the time and place of one's birth until one's death. The log book of a ship is a description of the ship's world line, as long as it contains a time tag attached to every position. The world line allows one to calculate the speed of the ship, given a measure of distance (a so-called metric) appropriate for the curved surface of the
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_physics">
       Usage in physics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage in physics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
       physics
      </a>
      , a world line of an object (approximated as a point in space, e.g., a particle or observer) is the sequence of
      <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
       spacetime
      </a>
      events corresponding to the history of the object. A world line is a special type of curve in spacetime. Below an equivalent definition will be explained: A world line is a time-like curve in spacetime. Each point of a world line is an event that can be labeled with the time and the spatial position of the object at that time.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, the
      <i>
       orbit
      </i>
      of the Earth in space is approximately a circle, a three-dimensional (closed) curve in space: the Earth returns every year to the same point in space. However, it arrives there at a different (later) time. The
      <i>
       world line
      </i>
      of the Earth is
      <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">
       helical
      </a>
      in spacetime (a curve in a four-dimensional space) and does not return to the same point.
     </p>
     <p>
      Spacetime is the collection of points called
      <a href="/wiki/Event_(relativity)" title="Event (relativity)">
       events
      </a>
      , together with a continuous and smooth coordinate system identifying the events. Each event can be labeled by four numbers: a time coordinate and three space coordinates; thus spacetime is a four-dimensional space. The mathematical term for spacetime is a four-dimensional
      <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">
       manifold
      </a>
      . The concept may be applied as well to a higher-dimensional space. For easy visualizations of four dimensions, two space coordinates are often suppressed. The event is then represented by a point in a
      <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_diagram" title="Minkowski diagram">
       Minkowski diagram
      </a>
      , which is a plane usually plotted with the time coordinate, say
      <img alt="t" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png"/>
      , upwards and the space coordinate, say
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      horizontally. As expressed by F.R. Harvey
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       A curve M in [spacetime] is called a
       <i>
        worldline of a particle
       </i>
       if its tangent is future timelike at each point. The arclength parameter is called
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_time" title="Proper time">
        proper time
       </a>
       and usually denoted . The length of M is called the
       <i>
        proper time
       </i>
       of the worldline or particle. If the worldline M is a line segment, then the particle is said to be in
       <a href="/wiki/Free_fall" title="Free fall">
        free fall
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      A world line traces out the path of a single point in spacetime. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_sheet" title="World sheet">
       world sheet
      </a>
      is the analogous two-dimensional surface traced out by a one-dimensional line (like a string) traveling through spacetime. The world sheet of an open string (with loose ends) is a strip; that of a closed string (a loop) is a volume.
     </p>
     <p>
      Once the object is not approximated as a mere point but has extended volume, it traces out not a
      <i>
       world line
      </i>
      but rather a
      <a href="/wiki/World_tube" title="World tube">
       world tube
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_lines_as_a_tool_to_describe_events">
       World lines as a tool to describe events
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World lines as a tool to describe events">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brane-wlwswv.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="735" data-file-width="811" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Brane-wlwswv.png/300px-Brane-wlwswv.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Brane-wlwswv.png/450px-Brane-wlwswv.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Brane-wlwswv.png/600px-Brane-wlwswv.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brane-wlwswv.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        World line, worldsheet, and world volume, as they are derived from
        <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">
         particles
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
         strings
        </a>
        , and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Membrane_(M-theory)" title="Membrane (M-theory)">
         branes
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A one-dimensional
      <i>
       line
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       curve
      </i>
      can be represented by the coordinates as a function of one parameter. Each value of the parameter corresponds to a point in spacetime and varying the parameter traces out a line. So in mathematical terms a curve is defined by four coordinate functions
      <img alt="x^a(\tau),\; a=0,1,2,3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/3/5/a357a02b3bb2a7714dcb1801b8bdfa42.png"/>
      (where
      <img alt="x^{0}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/8/3/a83d7413c3730fe3c7c2a0d1d46c0ee1.png"/>
      usually denotes the time coordinate) depending on one parameter
      <img alt="\tau" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/1/a/81a69207104f00baaabd6f84cafd15a0.png"/>
      . A coordinate grid in spacetime is the set of curves one obtains if three out of four coordinate functions are set to a constant.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometimes, the term
      <b>
       world line
      </b>
      is loosely used for
      <i>
       any
      </i>
      curve in spacetime. This terminology causes confusions. More properly, a
      <b>
       world line
      </b>
      is a curve in spacetime which traces out the
      <i>
       (time) history
      </i>
      of a particle, observer or small object. One usually takes the
      <a href="/wiki/Proper_time" title="Proper time">
       proper time
      </a>
      of an object or an observer as the curve parameter
      <img alt="\tau" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/1/a/81a69207104f00baaabd6f84cafd15a0.png"/>
      along the world line.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trivial_examples_of_spacetime_curves">
       Trivial examples of spacetime curves
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trivial examples of spacetime curves">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Worldlines1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="257" data-file-width="400" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/Worldlines1.jpg" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        Three different world lines representing travel at different constant speeds.
        <i>
         t
        </i>
        is time and
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        distance.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A curve that consists of a horizontal line segment (a line at constant coordinate time), may represent a rod in spacetime and would not be a world line in the proper sense. The parameter traces the length of the rod.
     </p>
     <p>
      A line at constant space coordinate (a vertical line in the convention adopted above) may represent a particle at rest (or a stationary observer). A tilted line represents a particle with a constant coordinate speed (constant change in space coordinate with increasing time coordinate). The more the line is tilted from the vertical, the larger the speed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Two world lines that start out separately and then intersect, signify a
      <i>
       collision
      </i>
      or "encounter." Two world lines starting at the same event in spacetime, each following its own path afterwards, may represent the decay of a particle into two others or the emission of one particle by another.
     </p>
     <p>
      World lines of a particle and an observer may be interconnected with the world line of a photon (the path of light) and form a diagram which depicts the emission of a photon by a particle which is subsequently observed by the observer (or absorbed by another particle).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tangent_vector_to_a_world_line.2C_four-velocity">
       Tangent vector to a world line, four-velocity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tangent vector to a world line, four-velocity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The four coordinate functions
      <img alt="x^a(\tau),\; a=0,1,2,3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/3/5/a357a02b3bb2a7714dcb1801b8bdfa42.png"/>
      defining a world line, are real functions of a real variable
      <img alt="\tau" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/1/a/81a69207104f00baaabd6f84cafd15a0.png"/>
      and can simply be differentiated in the usual calculus. Without the existence of a metric (this is important to realize) one can speak of the difference between a point
      <img alt="p" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/3/8/83878c91171338902e0fe0fb97a8c47a.png"/>
      on the curve at the parameter value
      <img alt="\tau_0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/3/e/53eafda0d7d4671b6f275f4977e8709a.png"/>
      and a point on the curve a little (parameter
      <img alt="\tau_0+\Delta\tau" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/f/7/9f79291f5edc5f5b1e69006082cbfdea.png"/>
      ) farther away. In the limit
      <img alt="\Delta\tau\rightarrow 0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/e/6/ce692f0b4ae3bdde63c9e79dd6f19d46.png"/>
      , this difference divided by
      <img alt="\Delta\tau" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/5/5/355deec8daaddf14b3d7c610cb90e75a.png"/>
      defines a vector, the
      <b>
       tangent vector
      </b>
      of the world line at the point
      <img alt="p" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/3/8/83878c91171338902e0fe0fb97a8c47a.png"/>
      . It is a four-dimensional vector, defined in the point
      <img alt="p" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/3/8/83878c91171338902e0fe0fb97a8c47a.png"/>
      . It is associated with the normal 3-dimensional velocity of the object (but it is not the same) and therefore called
      <a href="/wiki/Four-velocity" title="Four-velocity">
       four-velocity
      </a>
      <img alt="\vec{v}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/d/20d88c8c828fbb1bc9168d9cfd816060.png"/>
      , or in components:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\vec{v} = (v^0,v^1,v^2,v^3) = \left( \frac{dx^0}{d\tau}\;,\frac{dx^1}{d\tau}\;, \frac{dx^2}{d\tau}\;, \frac{dx^3}{d\tau} \right)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/b/1/db1a21a806bbb88163457077db8cb475.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where the derivatives are taken at the point
      <img alt="p" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/3/8/83878c91171338902e0fe0fb97a8c47a.png"/>
      , so at
      <img alt="\tau=\tau_0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/2/de25f3af4726c242b8144de1f9fcb601.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      All curves through point p have a tangent vector, not only world lines. The sum of two vectors is again a tangent vector to some other curve and the same holds for multiplying by a scalar. Therefore all tangent vectors in a point p span a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_space" title="Linear space">
       linear space
      </a>
      , called the
      <a href="/wiki/Tangent_space" title="Tangent space">
       tangent space
      </a>
      at point p. For example, taking a 2-dimensional space, like the (curved) surface of the Earth, its tangent space at a specific point would be the flat approximation of the curved space.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_lines_in_special_relativity">
       World lines in special relativity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World lines in special relativity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      So far a world line (and the concept of tangent vectors) has been described without a means of quantifying the interval between events. The basic mathematics is as follows: The theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special relativity
      </a>
      puts some constraints on possible world lines. In special relativity the description of
      <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
       spacetime
      </a>
      is limited to
      <i>
       special
      </i>
      coordinate systems that do not accelerate (and so do not rotate either), called
      <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">
       inertial coordinate systems
      </a>
      . In such coordinate systems, the
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
       speed of light
      </a>
      is a constant. The structure of spacetime is determined by a
      <a href="/wiki/Bilinear_form" title="Bilinear form">
       bilinear form
      </a>
       which gives a
      <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">
       real number
      </a>
      for each pair of events. The bilinear form is sometimes called a
      <i>
       spacetime metric
      </i>
      , but since distinct events sometimes result in a zero value, unlike metrics in
      <a href="/wiki/Metric_space" title="Metric space">
       metric spaces
      </a>
      of mathematics, the bilinear form is
      <i>
       not
      </i>
      a mathematical metric on spacetime.
     </p>
     <p>
      World lines of particles/objects at constant speed are called
      <a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">
       geodesics
      </a>
      . In special relativity these are straight lines in Minkowski space.
     </p>
     <p>
      Often the time units are chosen such that the speed of light is represented by lines at a fixed angle, usually at 45 degrees, forming a cone with the vertical (time) axis. In general, useful curves in spacetime can be of three types (the other types would be partly one, partly another type}:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        light-like
       </b>
       curves, having at each point the speed of light. They form a cone in spacetime, dividing it into two parts. The cone is three-dimensional in spacetime, appears as a line in drawings with two dimensions suppressed, and as a cone in drawings with one spatial dimension suppressed.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_line2.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="675" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/World_line2.svg/320px-World_line2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/World_line2.svg/480px-World_line2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/World_line2.svg/640px-World_line2.svg.png 2x" width="320"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_line2.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An example of a
        <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">
         light cone
        </a>
        , the three-dimensional surface of all possible light rays arriving at and departing from a point in spacetime. Here, it is depicted with one spatial dimension suppressed.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        time-like
       </b>
       curves, with a speed less than the speed of light. These curves must fall within a cone defined by light-like curves. In our definition above:
       <b>
        world lines are time-like curves in spacetime
       </b>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        space-like
       </b>
       curves falling outside the light cone. Such curves may describe, for example, the length of a physical object. The circumference of a cylinder and the length of a rod are space-like curves.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      At a given event on a world line, spacetime (
      <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">
       Minkowski space
      </a>
      ) is divided into three parts.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        future
       </b>
       of the given event is formed by all events that can be reached through time-like curves lying within the future light cone.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        past
       </b>
       of the given event is formed by all events that can influence the event (that is, which can be connected by world lines within the past
       <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">
        light cone
       </a>
       to the given event).
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        lightcone
       </b>
       at the given event is formed by all events that can be connected through light rays with the event. When we observe the sky at night, we basically see only the past
       <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">
        light cone
       </a>
       within the entire spacetime.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Elsewhere
       </b>
       is the region between the two light cones. Points in an observer's
       <b>
        elsewhere
       </b>
       are inaccessible to her/him; only points in the past can send signals to the observer. In ordinary laboratory experience, using common units and methods of measurement, it may seem that we look at the present, but in fact there is always a delay time for light to propagate. For example, we see the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       as it was about 8 minutes ago, not as it is "right now." Unlike the
       <b>
        present
       </b>
       in Galilean/Newtonian theory, the
       <b>
        elsewhere
       </b>
       is thick; it is not a 3-dimensional volume but is instead a 4-dimensional spacetime region.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Included in "elsewhere" is the
         <b>
          simultaneous hyperplane
         </b>
         , which is defined for a given observer by a
         <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">
          space
         </a>
         which is
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbolic-orthogonal" title="Hyperbolic-orthogonal">
          hyperbolic-orthogonal
         </a>
         to her/his world line. It is really three-dimensional, though it would be a 2-plane in the diagram because we had to throw away one dimension to make an intelligible picture. Although the light cones are the same for all observers at a given spacetime event, different observers, with differing velocities but coincident at the event (point) in the spacetime, have world lines that cross each other at an angle determined by their relative velocities, and thus they have different simultaneous hyperplanes.
        </li>
        <li>
         The
         <b>
          present
         </b>
         often means the single spacetime event being considered.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Simultaneous_hyperplane">
       Simultaneous hyperplane
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Simultaneous hyperplane">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Since a world line
      <img alt="\scriptstyle w(\tau) \isin R^4" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/4/9/349b1f6c0350580d30def8b0cad435a6.png"/>
      determines a velocity 4-vector
      <img alt="\scriptstyle v = \frac {dw}{d\tau}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/a/82ae2fbbdefdb2f2eabfde3df660f319.png"/>
      that is time-like, the Minkowski form
      <img alt="\scriptstyle \eta(v,x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/5/7/d57dbb8767f0ee2edadfcb5cc39ae51b.png"/>
      determines a linear function
      <img alt="\scriptstyle R^4 \rarr R" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/e/b/8ebb46d3c5aa24090797dca92da01702.png"/>
      by
      <img alt="\scriptstyle  x \mapsto \eta( v , x ) ." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/f/6/ff6ef4229ca61ec51a6c7552f3290009.png"/>
      Let
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      be the
      <a href="/wiki/Kernel_(linear_algebra)" title="Kernel (linear algebra)">
       null space
      </a>
      of this linear functional. Then
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      is called the
      <b>
       simultaneous hyperplane
      </b>
      with respect to
      <i>
       v
      </i>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity" title="Relativity of simultaneity">
       relativity of simultaneity
      </a>
      is a statement that
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      depends on
      <i>
       v
      </i>
      . Indeed,
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_complement" title="Orthogonal complement">
       orthogonal complement
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       v
      </i>
      with respect to . When two world lines
      <i>
       u
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      are related by
      <img alt="\scriptstyle \frac {du}{d\tau} = \frac {dw}{d\tau}, " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/3/e/c3ee8ce2637740181a5f76cf38b85e33.png"/>
      then they share the same simultaneous hyperplane. This hyperplane exists mathematically, but physical relations in relativity involve the movement of information by light. For instance, the traditional electro-static force described by
      <a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb's law">
       Coulomb's law
      </a>
      may be pictured in a simultaneous hyperplane, but relativistic relations of charge and force involve
      <a href="/wiki/Retarded_potential" title="Retarded potential">
       retarded potentials
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_lines_in_general_relativity">
       World lines in general relativity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World lines in general relativity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The use of world lines in
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      is basically the same as in special relativity, with the difference that
      <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
       spacetime
      </a>
      can be
      <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">
       curved
      </a>
      . A
      <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor" title="Metric tensor">
       metric
      </a>
      exists and its dynamics are determined by the
      <a href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equations" title="Einstein field equations">
       Einstein field equations
      </a>
      and are dependent on the mass distribution in spacetime. Again the metric defines
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightlike" title="Lightlike">
       lightlike
      </a>
      (null),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacelike" title="Spacelike">
       spacelike
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timelike" title="Timelike">
       timelike
      </a>
      curves. Also, in general relativity, world lines are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timelike" title="Timelike">
       timelike
      </a>
      curves in spacetime, where
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timelike" title="Timelike">
       timelike
      </a>
      curves fall within the lightcone. However, a lightcone is not necessarily inclined at 45 degrees to the time axis. However, this is an artifact of the chosen coordinate system, and reflects the coordinate freedom (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffeomorphism_invariance" title="Diffeomorphism invariance">
       diffeomorphism invariance
      </a>
      ) of general relativity. Any
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timelike" title="Timelike">
       timelike
      </a>
      curve admits a
      <a href="/wiki/Proper_frame" title="Proper frame">
       comoving observer
      </a>
      whose "time axis" corresponds to that curve, and, since no observer is privileged, we can always find a local coordinate system in which lightcones are inclined at 45 degrees to the time axis. See also for example
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddington-Finkelstein_coordinates" title="Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates">
       Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      World lines of free-falling particles or objects (such as planets around the Sun or an astronaut in space) are called
      <a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">
       geodesics
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._C._Fields" title="J. C. Fields">
       J. C. Fields
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">
       University of Toronto
      </a>

     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       I remember [Fields] lecturing at one of the Saturday evening lectures at the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Institute" title="Royal Canadian Institute">
        Royal Canadian Institute
       </a>

The substance of the exercise was as follows: He postulated that, commencing with his birth, every human being had some kind of spiritual aura with a long filament or thread attached, that travelled behind him throughout his life. He then proceeded in imagination to describe the complicated entanglement every individual became involved in his relationship to other individuals, comparing the simple entanglements of youth to those complicated knots that develop in later life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Because they oversimplify world lines, which traverse four-dimensional spacetime, into one-dimensional timelines, almost all purported science-fiction stories about
      <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">
       time travel
      </a>
      are actually wishful fantasy stories. Some device or superpowered person is generally portrayed as departing from one point in time, and with little or no subjective lag, arriving at some other point in time  but at the same literally geographic point in space, typically inside a workshop or near some historic site. However, in reality the planet, its solar system, and its galaxy would all be at vastly different spatial positions on arrival. Thus, the time travel mechanism would also have to provide instantaneous teleportation, with infinitely accurate and simultaneous adjustment of final 3D location, linear momentum, and angular momentum.
     </p>
