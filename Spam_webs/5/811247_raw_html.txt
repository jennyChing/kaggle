     <p>
      <b>
       Emergency medical technician
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       EMT
      </b>
      ) or
      <b>
       ambulance technician
      </b>
      are terms used in some countries to denote a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_provider" title="Health care provider">
       health care provider
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_services" title="Emergency medical services">
       emergency medical services
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BLS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      EMTs are clinicians, trained to respond quickly to emergency situations regarding medical issues, traumatic injuries and accident scenes. Under the British System and those that are influenced by it, are referred to as Ambulance technician (often shortened to tech), whereas in the American system and its influenced countries, it is known as Emergency medical technician (often abbreviated as EMT).
     </p>
     <p>
      EMTs are most commonly found working in
      <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">
       ambulances
      </a>
      , but should not be confused with "ambulance drivers"  ambulance staff who in the past were not trained in emergency care or driving. EMTs are often employed by ambulance services,
      <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">
       governments
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">
       hospitals
      </a>
      , but are also sometimes employed by
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">
       fire departments
      </a>
      (and seen on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" title="Fire apparatus">
       fire apparatus
      </a>
      ), in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_department" title="Police department">
       police departments
      </a>
      (and seen on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_vehicle" title="Police vehicle">
       police vehicles
      </a>
      ), and there are many
      <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">
       firefighter
      </a>
      /EMTs and police officer/EMTs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BLS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      EMTs operate under a limited
      <a href="/wiki/Scope_of_practice" title="Scope of practice">
       scope of practice
      </a>
      . EMTs are typically supervised by a
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_director" title="Medical director">
       medical director
      </a>
      , who is a
      <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">
       physician
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some EMTs are paid employees, while others (particularly in
      <a href="/wiki/Rural_health" title="Rural health">
       rural areas
      </a>
      ) are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volunteer" title="Volunteer">
       volunteers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BLS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">
       Canada
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Canada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Of considerable relevance to understanding the nature of Canadian Paramedic practice, the reader must appreciate the considerable degree of inter-provincial variation. Although a national consensus (by way of the National Occupational Competency Profile) identifies certain knowledge, skills, and abilities as being most synonymous with a given level of Paramedic practice, each province retains ultimate authority in legislating the actual administration and delivery of emergency medical services within its own borders. For this reason, any discussion of
      <i>
       Paramedic Practice
      </i>
      in Canada is necessarily broad, and general. Specific regulatory frameworks and questions related to Paramedic practice can only definitively be answered by consulting relevant provincial legislation, although provincial Paramedic Associations may often offer a simpler overview of this topic when it is restricted to a province-by-province basis.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Canada, the levels of paramedic practice as defined by the National Occupational Competency Profile are: Emergency Medical Responder (EMR), Primary Care Paramedic, Advanced Care Paramedic, and Critical Care Paramedic
     </p>
     <p>
      Regulatory frameworks vary from province to province, and include direct government regulation (such as Ontario's method of credentialing its practitioners with the title of A-EMCA, or Advanced Emergency Medical Care Assistant) to professional self-regulating bodies, such as the Alberta College of Paramedics. Though the title of Paramedic is a generic description of a category of practitioners, provincial variability in regulatory methods accounts for ongoing differences in actual titles that are ascribed to different levels of practitioners. For example, the province of
      <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">
       Alberta
      </a>
      has legally adopted the title "Emergency Medical Technician", or 'EMT', for the Primary Care Paramedic; and 'Paramedic' only for those qualified as Advanced Care Paramedics
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Life_Support" title="Advanced Life Support">
       Advanced Life Support
      </a>
      (ALS) providers. Only someone registered in Alberta can call themselves an EMT or Paramedic in Alberta, the title is legally protected. Almost all other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_provinces" title="Canadian provinces">
       provinces
      </a>
      are gradually moving to adopting the new titles, or have at least recognized the NOCP document as a benchmarking document to permit inter-provincial labour mobility of practitioners, regardless of how titles are specifically regulated within their own provincial systems. In this manner, the confusing myriad of titles and occupational descriptions can at least be discussed using a common language for comparison sake.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_Medical_Responder">
       Emergency Medical Responder
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Emergency Medical Responder">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Most providers that work in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambulances" title="Ambulances">
       ambulances
      </a>
      will be identified as 'Paramedics' by the public. However, in many cases, the most prevalent level of emergency prehospital care is that which is provided by the Emergency Medical Responder (EMR). This is a level of practice recognized under the National Occupational Competency Profile, although unlike the next three successive levels of practice, the EMR is not specifically considered a Paramedic,
      <i>
       per se
      </i>
      . The high number of EMRs across Canada cannot be ignored as contributing a critical role in the chain of survival, although it is a level of practice that is least comprehensive (clinically speaking), and is also generally not consistent with any medical acts beyond advanced first-aid and oxygen therapy, with the exception of automated external defibrillation (which is still considered a regulated medical act in most provinces in Canada).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_care_paramedics">
       Primary care paramedics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Primary care paramedics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Primary care paramedics (PCP) are the entry-level of paramedic practice in Canadian provinces. The
      <a href="/wiki/Scope_of_practice" title="Scope of practice">
       scope of practice
      </a>
      includes performing
      <a href="/wiki/Automated_external_defibrillator" title="Automated external defibrillator">
       semi-automated external defibrillation
      </a>
      , interpretation of 4-lead ECG's, administration of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symptom_Relief_Medications" title="Symptom Relief Medications">
       Symptom Relief Medications
      </a>
      for a variety of emergency medical conditions (these include
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">
       epinephrine
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextrose" title="Dextrose">
       dextrose
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Glucagon" title="Glucagon">
       glucagon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Salbutamol" title="Salbutamol">
       salbutamol
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">
       ASA
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitroglycerine" title="Nitroglycerine">
       nitroglycerine
      </a>
      ), performing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trauma_immobilization" title="Trauma immobilization">
       trauma immobilization
      </a>
      (including
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cervical_immobilization&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cervical immobilization (page does not exist)">
       cervical immobilization
      </a>
      ), and other fundamental basic medical care. Primary Care Paramedics may also receive additional training in order to perform certain skills that are normally in the scope of practice of Advanced Care Paramedics. This is regulated both provincially (by statute) and locally (by the medical director), and ordinarily entails an aspect of medical oversight by a specific body or group of physicians. This is often referred to as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Control&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Medical Control (page does not exist)">
       Medical Control
      </a>
      , or a role played by a base hospital. For example, in the provinces of
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      and Newfoundland and Labrador, many paramedic services allow Primary Care Paramedics to perform 12-lead
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" title="Electrocardiogram">
       ECG
      </a>
      interpretation, or initiate
      <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_therapy" title="Intravenous therapy">
       intravenous therapy
      </a>
      to deliver a few additional medications.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_Care_Paramedics">
       Advanced Care Paramedics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advanced Care Paramedics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Advanced Care Paramedic is a level of practitioner that is in high demand by many services across Canada. However, still not all provinces and jurisdictions have ACPs (Quebec, New Brunswick). The ACP typically carries approximately 20 different medications, although the number and type of medications may vary substantially from region to region. ACPs perform advanced airway management including intubation, surgical airways, intravenous therapy, place external jugular IV lines, perform needle thoracotomy, perform and interpret 12-lead ECGs, perform synchronized and chemical cardioversion, transcutaneous pacing, perform obstetrical assessments, and provide pharmacological pain relief for various conditions. Several sites in Canada have adopted pre-hospital fibrinolytics and rapid sequence induction, and prehospital medical research has permitted a great number of variations in the scope of practice for ACPs. Current programs include providing ACPs with discretionary direct 24-hour access to PCI labs, bypassing the emergency department, and representing a fundamental change in both the way that patients with S-T segment elevation myocardial infarctions (STEMI) are treated, but also profoundly affecting survival rates.[12] as well as bypassing a closer hospitals to get an identified stroke patient to a stroke centre.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_Care_Paramedic">
       Critical Care Paramedic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical Care Paramedic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Critical Care Paramedics (CCPs) are paramedics who generally do not respond to 9-1-1 emergency calls, with the exception of helicopter "scene" calls. Instead they focus on transferring patients from the hospital they are currently in to other hospitals that can provide a higher level of care. CCPs often work in collaboration with registered nurses and respiratory therapists during hospital transfers. This ensures continuity of care. However, when acuity is manageable by a CCP or a registered nurse or respiratory therapist is not available, CCPs will work alone. Providing this care to the patient allows the sending hospital to avoid losing highly trained staff on hospital transfers. CCPs are able to provide all of the care that PCPs and ACPs provide. That being said, CCPs significantly lack practical experience with advanced skills such as IV initiation, peripheral access to cardiovascular system for fluid and drug administration, advanced airway, and many other techniques. Where an PCP and ACP may run 40-50 medical codes per year a CCP may run 1-2 in an entire career. IV/IO starts are nearly non-existent in the field and for this reason CCPs are required to attend nearly double the amount of time in classroom situations or in hospital to keep current. In addition to this they are trained for other skills such as medication infusion pumps, mechanical ventilation and arterial line monitoring.[13] CCPs often work in fixed and rotary wing aircraft when the weather permits and staff are available, but systems such as the Toronto EMS Critical Care Transport Program work in land ambulances. ORNGE Transport operates both land and aircraft in Ontario. In British Columbia, CCP's work primarily in aircraft with a dedicated Critical Care Transport crew in Trail for long-distance transfers and a regular CCP street crew stationed in South Vancouver that often also performs medevacs, when necessary.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Training">
       Training
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

      Paramedic training in Canada varies regionally; for example, the training may be six months (British Columbia) or two to four years (Ontario, Alberta) in length. The nature of training and how it is regulated, like actual paramedic practice, varies from province to province.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      EMT program in the United States began as part of the "Alexandria Plan" in the early 70's, in addition to a growing issue with injuries associated with car accidents. Emergency medicine (EM) as a medical specialty is relatively young. Prior to the 1960s and 70s, hospital emergency departments were generally staffed by physicians on staff at the hospital on a rotating basis, among them general surgeons, internists, psychiatrists, and dermatologists. Physicians in training (interns and residents), foreign medical graduates and sometimes nurses also staffed the Emergency Department (ED). EM was born as a specialty in order to fill the time commitment required by physicians on staff to work in the increasingly chaotic emergency departments (EDs) of the time. During this period, groups of physicians began to emerge who had left their respective practices in order to devote their work completely to the ED. The first of such groups was headed by Dr. James DeWitt Mills who, along with four associate physicians; Dr. Chalmers A. Loughridge, Dr. William Weaver, Dr. John McDade, and Dr. Steven Bednar at Alexandria Hospital, VA established 24/7 year round emergency care which became known as the "Alexandria Plan". It was not until the establishment of American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP), the recognition of emergency medicine training programs by the AMA and the AOA, and in 1979 a historical vote by the American Board of Medical Specialties that EM became a recognized medical specialty. The nation's first EMT's were from the Alexandria plan working as Emergency Care Technicians serving in the Alexandria Hospital Emergency Room. The training for these technicians was modeled after the established "Physician Assistant" training program and later restructured to meet the basic needs for emergency pre-hospital care. On June 24, 2011, The Alexandria Hospital Celebrated the 50th Anniversary of the Alexandria Plan. In attendance were three of the nation's first ECTs/EMTs: David Stover, Larry Jackson, and Kenneth Weaver.
     </p>

        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transition to new levels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In 2009, the NREMT posted information about a transition to a new system of levels for emergency care providers developed by the NHTSA with the National EMS Scope of Practice project.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 2014, these "new" levels will replace the fragmented system found around the United States. The new classification will include Emergency Medical Responder (replacing first responder), Emergency Medical Technician (replacing EMT-Basic), Advanced Emergency Medical Technician (replacing EMT-Intermediate/85), and Paramedic (replacing EMT-Intermediate/99 and EMT-Paramedic). Education requirements in transitioning to the new levels are substantially similar.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="EMR">
       EMR
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: EMR">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      EMR (Emergency Medical Responder) is the most basic, entry level of EMS. EMRs, many of whom are volunteers, provide basic, immediate care including advanced first aid,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardio-pulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardio-pulmonary resuscitation">
       cardio-pulmonary resuscitation
      </a>
      (CPR), and
      <a href="/wiki/Automated_external_defibrillator" title="Automated external defibrillator">
       automated external defibrillator
      </a>
      (AED) usage. The skills allowed at this level are almost identical to that of the EMT level with a few exceptions. An EMR can assume care for a patient while more advanced resources are on the way, and then can assist EMTs and Paramedics when they arrive.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="EMT">
       EMT
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: EMT">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG/220px-DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG/330px-DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG/440px-DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DerbySpiralFracture1221.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        EMTs tend to a woman with a
        <a href="/wiki/Spiral_fracture" title="Spiral fracture">
         spiral fracture
        </a>
        at a
        <a href="/wiki/Roller_derby" title="Roller derby">
         roller derby
        </a>
        bout.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      EMT is the main entry level of EMS.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The procedures and skills allowed at this level are generally non-invasive such as bleeding control,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_pressure_ventilation" title="Positive pressure ventilation">
       positive pressure ventilation
      </a>
      with a
      <a href="/wiki/Bag_valve_mask" title="Bag valve mask">
       bag valve mask
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Oropharyngeal_airway" title="Oropharyngeal airway">
       oropharyngeal airway
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nasopharyngeal_airway" title="Nasopharyngeal airway">
       nasopharyngeal airway
      </a>
      , supplemental oxygen administration,
      <a href="/wiki/Pulse_oximetry" title="Pulse oximetry">
       pulse oximetry
      </a>
      , oral
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suctioning" title="Suctioning">
       suctioning
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardio-pulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardio-pulmonary resuscitation">
       cardio-pulmonary resuscitation
      </a>
      (CPR), use of an
      <a href="/wiki/Automated_external_defibrillator" title="Automated external defibrillator">
       automated external defibrillator
      </a>
      (AED), splinting (including full spinal immobilization), and basic medication administration (such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epinephrine_auto-injectors" title="Epinephrine auto-injectors">
       epinephrine auto-injectors
      </a>
      , oral glucose, aspirin, nitroglycerin, and albuterol). Some areas may add to the scope of practice for EMT's including intransal nalaxone administration, Training requirements and treatment protocols vary from area to area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_EMT">
       Advanced EMT
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Advanced EMT">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Advanced EMT is the levels of training between EMT and Paramedic. They can provide limited
      <a href="/wiki/Advanced_life_support" title="Advanced life support">
       advanced life support
      </a>
      (ALS) care including obtaining
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous" title="Intravenous">
       intravenous
      </a>
      access, use of advanced airway adjuncts (such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combi-tube" title="Combi-tube">
       Combi-tube
      </a>
      or King-LT), medication administration (such as Dextrose, Glucagon, Nitrous Oxide)
     </p>
     <p>
      Advanced EMT in California
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or the levels of Advanced EMT Advanced EMT-Critical Care in New York
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Paramedic">
       Paramedic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Paramedic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Paramedics, represents the highest level of EMT, and in general, the highest level of prehospital medical provider, though some areas utilize physicians as providers on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_ambulance" title="Air ambulance">
       air ambulances
      </a>
      or as a ground provider.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Paramedics perform a variety of medical procedures such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endotracheal_intubation" title="Endotracheal intubation">
       endotracheal intubation
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_resuscitation" title="Fluid resuscitation">
       fluid resuscitation
      </a>
      , drug administration, obtaining
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous" title="Intravenous">
       intravenous
      </a>
      access,
      <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_monitoring" title="Cardiac monitoring">
       cardiac monitoring
      </a>
      (continuous and 12-lead),
      <a href="/wiki/Cardioversion" title="Cardioversion">
       cardioversion
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Transcutaneous_pacing" title="Transcutaneous pacing">
       transcutaneous pacing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cricothyrotomy" title="Cricothyrotomy">
       cricothyrotomy
      </a>
      , manual defibrillation, chest needle decompression, and other advanced procedures and assessments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Staffing_levels">
       Staffing levels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Staffing levels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      An
      <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">
       ambulance
      </a>
      with only EMTs is considered a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_Life_Support" title="Basic Life Support">
       Basic Life Support
      </a>
      (BLS) unit, an ambulance utilizing AEMTs is dubbed an Intermediate Life Support (ILS), or Limited Advanced Life Support (LALS) unit, and an ambulance with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramedics" title="Paramedics">
       Paramedics
      </a>
      is dubbed an Advanced Life Support (ALS) unit. Some states allow ambulance crews to contain a mix of crews levels (e.g. an EMT and a Paramedic or an AEMT and a Paramedic) to staff ambulances and operate at the level of the highest trained provider. There is nothing stopping supplemental crew members to be of a certain certification, though (e.g. if an ALS ambulance is required to have two Paramedics, then it is acceptable to have two Paramedics and an EMT).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_training">
       Education and training
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Education and training">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      EMT training programs for certification vary greatly from course to course, provided that each course at least meets local and national requirements. In the United States, EMR's receive at least 40-50 hours of classroom training, EMTs receive at least 120180 hours of classroom training. AEMTs generally have 200500 hours of training, and Paramedics are trained for 1,0001800 hours or more. In addition, a minimum number of continuing education (CE) hours are required to maintain certification. For example, to maintain NREMT certification, EMTs must obtain at least 48 hours of additional education and either complete a 24 hour refresher course or complete an additional 24 hours of CEs that would cover, on an hour by hour basis, the same topics as the refresher course would.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Recertification for other levels follows a similar pattern.
     </p>
     <p>
      EMT training programs vary greatly in calendar length (number of days or months). For example, fast track programs are available for EMTs that are completed in two weeks by holding class for 8 to 12 hours a day for at least two weeks. Other training programs are months long, or up to 2 years for Paramedics in an associate degree program. In addition to each level's didactic education, clinical rotations may also be required (especially for levels above EMT). Similar in a sense to medical school clinical rotations, EMT students are required to spend a required amount of time in an ambulance and on a variety of hospital services (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Obstetrics" title="Obstetrics">
       obstetrics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Emergency_department" title="Emergency department">
       emergency medicine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">
       surgery
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">
       psychiatry
      </a>
      ) in order to complete a course and become eligible for the certification exam. The number of clinical hours for both time in an ambulance and time in the hour vary depending on local requirements, the level the student is obtaining, and the amount of time it takes the student to show competency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHTSA_NSC_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NHTSA_NSC-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      EMT training programs take place at numerous locations, such as universities, community colleges, technical schools, hospitals or EMS academies. Every state in the United States has an EMS lead agency or state office of emergency medical services that regulates and accredits EMT training programs. Most of these offices have web sites to provide information to the public and individuals who are interested in becoming an EMT.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_direction">
       Medical direction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Medical direction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the United States, an EMT's actions in the field are governed by state regulations, local regulations, and by the policies of their EMS organization. The development of these policies are guided by a
      <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">
       physician
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_director" title="Medical director">
       medical director
      </a>
      , often with the advice of a medical advisory committee.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In California, for example, each county's Local Emergency Medical Service Agency (LEMSA) issues a list of standard operating procedures or protocols, under the supervision of the
      <a href="/wiki/California_Emergency_Medical_Services_Authority" title="California Emergency Medical Services Authority">
       California Emergency Medical Services Authority
      </a>
      . These procedures often vary from county to county based on local needs, levels of training and clinical experiences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      New York State has similar procedures, whereas a regional medical-advisory council ("REMAC") determines protocols for one or more counties in a geographical section of the state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Treatments and procedures administered by Paramedics fall under one of two categories, off-line medical orders (standing orders) or on-line medical orders. On-line medical orders refers to procedures that must be explicitly approved by a base hospital physician or registered nurse through voice communication (generally by phone or
      <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
       radio
      </a>
      ) and are generally rare or high risk procedures (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Rapid_sequence_induction" title="Rapid sequence induction">
       rapid sequence induction
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Cricothyrotomy" title="Cricothyrotomy">
       cricothyrotomy
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, when multiple levels can perform the same procedure (e.g. AEMT-Critical Care and Paramedics in New York), a procedure can be both an on-line and a standing order depending on the level of the provider.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since no set of protocols can cover every patient situation, many systems work with protocols as guidelines and not "cook book" treatment plans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finally, systems also have policies in place to handle medical direction when communication failures happen or in disaster situations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The NHTSA curriculum is the foundation Standard of Care for EMS providers in the US.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Employment">
       Employment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_technician&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Employment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      EMTs and Paramedics are employed in varied settings, mainly the prehospital environment such as in EMS, fire, and police agencies. They can also be found in positions ranging from hospital and health care settings, to
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-BLS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      industrial and entertainment positions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The prehospital environment is loosely divided into non-emergency (e.g. patient transport) and emergency (
      <a href="/wiki/9-1-1" title="9-1-1">
       9-1-1
      </a>
      calls) services, but many ambulance services and EMS agencies operate both non-emergency and emergency care.
     </p>
     <p>
      In many places across the United States, it is not uncommon for the primary employer of EMTs and Paramedics to be the
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">
       fire department
      </a>
      , with the fire department providing the primary emergency medical system response.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In other locations, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">
       Boston
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">
       Massachusetts
      </a>
      , emergency medical services are provided by a separate, or third-party, municipal government emergency agency (e.g.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_EMS" title="Boston EMS">
       Boston EMS
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In still other locations, emergency medical services are provided by volunteer agencies. College and university campuses may provide emergency medical responses on their own campus using students.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In some states of the US, many EMS agencies are run by Independent Non-Profit Volunteer First Aid Squads that are their own corporations set up as separate entities from fire departments. In this environment, volunteers are hired to fill certain blocks of time to cover emergency calls. These volunteers have the same state certification as their paid counterparts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
