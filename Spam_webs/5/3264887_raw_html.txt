          <p>
           Christmas isnt Christmas unless you spend some of it scared shitless. And I dont mean a little bit nervous once you realize the turkey isnt exactly what one would define as cooked-I mean, white-knuckled, terrified into an unmoving mannequin with the pallor of someone whos just realized they might (or might not have) have left the gas on when they left the house this morning and they absolutely have to turn around and drive back home, like,
           <i>
            right now
           </i>
           , or theyll spend the whole weekend picturing the house engulfed with raging flames/sexy firemen (either way, annoying to miss it). Thats the point of all-out terror Im talking about here. So, read on for some solid stories to thoroughly ruin/enhance your Christmas experience
          </p>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #2a3555;">
            The Signal-Man
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           _______________________________________________________________________________________________
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Mugby_Junction.png">
            <img alt="Mugby_Junction_christmas_ghost_stories" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-27326" height="300" src="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Mugby_Junction-186x300.png" width="186"/>
           </a>
           While you might go straight for A Christmas Carol for proper festive terror (the books a whole lot darker than you might imagine, and definitely worth a read), Dickens also produced a superb tale of terror in the form of
           <a href="http://www.pagebypagebooks.com/Charles_Dickens/The_Signal_Man/The_Signal_Man_p1.html" target="_blank">
            The Signal-Man
           </a>
           . What makes it such a good chiller is the sheer amount thats left to the imagination; most of the plot takes place in conversations between the narrator and the titular signalman. Reaching a tense and beautifully drawn climax, its the kind of story thatll lurk with you long after youve cleared away the mince pies.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #2a3555;">
            Oh, Whistle, and Ill Come to You, My Lad
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           ____________________________________________________________________________________
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/mcbryde1.jpg">
            <img alt="whistle_and_i_will_come_my_lad_christmas_ghost_stories" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-27325" height="300" src="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/mcbryde1-358x300.jpg" width="358"/>
           </a>
           Pretty much any M.R. James story would fit comfortably into this list, but I have a particular soft spot for this delicious slow-burner (the title of which comes from a Robert Burns poem). The story is fantastically simple; an academic finds a whistle with a note inscribed with a mysterious epitaph; he whistles, and spends the rest of the story waiting to find out exactly what it is hes summoned. If hunting out the original story is too much hassle, there are some fantastic adaptations floating around out there, and many do justice to the chilling simplicity of
           <a href="http://gaslight.mtroyal.ca/owhistle.htm" target="_blank">
            James ingenious story
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <h2>
          </h2>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #2a3555;">
            Last Breath
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           ________________________________________________________________________________
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/13620074.jpg">
            <img alt="last_breath_christmas_stories" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-27324" height="300" src="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/13620074-274x300.jpg" width="274"/>
           </a>
           While Ive managed to take a step back from Stephen King for this list (for once), I couldnt resist sneaking on his son Joe Hill and a story from his first short-story collection
           <a href="http://www.goodreads.com/book/show/7784152-last-breath" target="_blank">
            20
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            Century Ghosts
           </a>
           . Theres something deliciously gothic about this, the story of a museum that collects the last breaths of the well-known and the ordinary. Creepy, creative and written with a scarcity that lends itself well to a Christmas story, this is a story of ideas rather than outright terror.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #2a3555;">
            A Short Guide to the City
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           ____________________________________________________________________________________
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Peter-Straub-216x300.jpg">
            <img alt="Peter-Straub_short_guide_christmas_stories" class="alignright size-full wp-image-27323" height="300" src="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Peter-Straub-216x300.jpg" width="216"/>
           </a>
           Peter Straub, a ridiculously brilliant horror writer known for his collaborations with Stephen King, is one of those people who can imbue his writing with layer upon layer of sheer menace. This, a richly portentous tale in which a narrator describes the lusciously drawn horror of a city plagued by a mysterious killer, is
           <a href="http://www.nightmare-magazine.com/fiction/a-short-guide-to-the-city/" target="_blank">
            one of his best
           </a>
           ; frightening, visceral and vivid, there are no easy answers in this artistic chiller.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           <span style="color: #2a3555;">
            The Hound
           </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
           _______________________________________________________________________________
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/h-p-lovecraft-the-hound-screenshot-2.jpg">
            <img alt="lovecraft_the_hound_christmas_ghost_stories" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-27322" height="300" src="http://www.modvive.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/h-p-lovecraft-the-hound-screenshot-2-225x300.jpg" width="225"/>
           </a>
           Though not one for the more faint-hearted of readers,
           <a href="http://www.hplovecraft.com/writings/fiction/h.aspx" target="_blank">
            H.P. Lovecrafts oeuvre
           </a>
           offers scores of properly chilling sagas that have taken up legendary status in the world of horror fiction. This remains my favorite; a gruesome, gory, fearful trek through the twisted world of a pair of obsessive grave robbers. Featuring the first appearance of Lovecrafts now-renowned Necronomicon, its rich with his marvelous prose and eerie sense of impending dread. What better way to get into the Christmas spirit?
          </p>
