           Written by
           <strong>

          <p>
           <em>
            <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ztxAEl8ErMY" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ztxAEl8ErMY', '');">
             <img align="right" border="5" height="184" hspace="5" src="http://altercenter.com/images/exercises/neungsiam-lunge-twist.jpg" vspace="5" width="233"/>
            </a>
            A belated Happy Thanksgiving to all the Americans on MMT.  While the rest of the nation kicks off the holiday season by eating everything within reach, I figure nows a good time to give yall a gut-busting antidote to all the holiday cheer and beer
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span id="more-380">
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            CIRCUITS FOR MUAYTHAI &amp; MMA  ROUND 1
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Ive put together a video (page bottom) with a nonstop circuit of 5 functional exercises to condition your entire body for Muaythai, MMA, and other striking-based combat forms.  You might recognize some from my previous postings,
           <a href="http://www.mymuaythai.com/mark-mian/">
            linked on this page
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Each exercise can be done on its own to build your strength &amp; power. However, doing them without rest in a circuit will  develop killer stamina by challenging your ability to maintain your intensity over a longer duration and changing conditions just like fighting.   Circuits like this are especially good for the last month of training before competition  when refining your strength with adaptive endurance is key.  Its hard. It hurts. It works. No crying.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Instructions
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Basic instructions are included in the
           <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ztxAEl8ErMY" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ztxAEl8ErMY', 'video');">
            video
           </a>
           below  but here are some additional notes. Feel free to ask questions.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           [youtube ztxAEl8ErMY]
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Program
           </strong>
           <em>
            : 3-4x/week for 6-8 weeks
           </em>
           <br/>
           It will take a few weeks for your body to change.  As always, we strongly recommend developing control and fluidity before increasing power and intensity.  The reason is to avoid over-stressing your body, which decreases development, performance, and potential.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Preparation:
           </strong>
           <em>
            Nourish &amp; Warmup
           </em>
           <br/>
           This workout is a high-energy investment: you get what you give. Nourish yourself by eating properly beforehand. Warm-up with 10 minutes of rope-jumping/cardio &amp; then some stretching.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Duration:
           </strong>
           <em>
            30-60 seconds per exercise (goal is 60)
           </em>
           <br/>
           Your goal is to (eventually) perform each exercise for 60 seconds without resting between, for a total of 5 minutes of nonstop work.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Volume:
           </strong>
           <em>
            3-5 Circuits
           </em>
           <br/>
           As mentioned previously, do each circuit without stopping between exercises. After completing all 5, rest for 30-60secs before doing 2-4 more, in the same manner.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Resistance:
           </strong>
           <em>
            5-15 lb Medicine Ball
           </em>
           <br/>
           Start with a light med ball to learn the movement properly: fast &amp; controlled . Work your way up slowly in weight to avoid straining your back.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Attitude:
           </strong>
           Go hard but dont get wild, killer.  Keep track of your performance from one workout to the next, counting how many reps you do.
          </p>
          <p>
           Remember that you are training to develop strength, not demonstrate it.
          </p>
          <p>
           *Wai*

           <div class="comment-content">
            <p>
             Gusnark  good question.
            </p>
            <p>
             The short answer is: the best strength exercise for you is the one youre not good at.
            </p>
            <p>
             That would be any one you cant do for more than 8-10 reps, lets say.
            </p>
            <p>
             The long answer involves some explanation.
            </p>
            <p>
             1]
             <br/>
             First, as always, I cant really say theres a best exercise for anything  it really depends on what the athlete has been doing previously. Generally, your body will stop getting much stronger/better at an exercise after about 6-8 weeks, and will need something need to develop it.  So, an exercise that is best for you at one point, will no longer be so after a couple of months.
            </p>
            <p>
             2]
             <br/>
             Burpees: aka Squat Thrusts  the first exercise in the video above is a modified form of it.  I guess I wouldnt really consider Burpees a strength exercise for most athletes who can do more than 10 pushups.  Except for those who find pushups really hard at first, squat thrusts are more about general conditioning and endurance  because they involve two exercises in one.
            </p>
            <p>
             To mostly develop strength on its own, its best to repeat multiple sets of one movement that you can only do for about 3-8 reps (for pure strength is 3-5 reps).
            </p>
            <p>
             Burpees are great for overall conditioning and maintenance of some strength if youre only going to do one exercise.
            </p>
            <p>
             3]
             <br/>
             There are many challenging bodyweight exercises you can do if youre in decent shape and dont have any equipment.  Each exercise will tend to build strength in only one area of your body (eg, upper vs lower, front vs back vs sides, etc), so you should do a at least a few to develop everything.
            </p>
            <p>
             As a basic minimal workout for muaythai, you can do various pushups (eg, on an incline, or one-arms, or clapping), various  lunges (eg, forward, reverse, lateral), various chinups (eg, with different grips), and tons of abs  as explained in my first Kick Your Abs posting.  All of these involve movements and muscles useful to what you do in Muaythai:
             <br/>
             pushups = punching
             <br/>
             lunges = moving &amp; dodging
             <br/>
             chinups = clinching
             <br/>
             various abs = kicking, blocking, balancing
            </p>
            <p>
             To conclude:
             <br/>
             1] build a program of exercises that you suck at, and 2] make sure they make sense for muaythai
             <br/>
             3] make sure they hit your whole body
             <br/>
             4] work hard and then switch them after 6-8 weeks
            </p>

             *wai*

            <p>
             Yes. Dips are essentially vertical pushups and mostly work the same muscles as regular pushups do  though in different ratios.  Dips done on parallel bars tend to work the lower chest fibers, lats, and triceps more.  To fully develop your muscles, its always good to use a variety of angles: from vertical (dips), incline pushups, regular pushups, to upside-down (handstand) pushups.
            </p>
            <p>
             BUT although dips may be good for working &amp; developing those muscles if you think about it, Muaythai doesnt really involve any movements like dips (pushing forcefully downwards).  Dips are not especially functional (relevant) for muaythai performance.
            </p>
