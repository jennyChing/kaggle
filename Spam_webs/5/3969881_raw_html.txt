       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Interactive Game of Life Exhibit
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         By
         <span class="author vcard">
          <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.evilmadscientist.com/author/lenore_edman/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Lenore Edman">
           Lenore Edman
          </a>
         </span>
         on
         <span class="date">
          <a href="http://www.evilmadscientist.com/2010/interactive-game-of-life-exhibit/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Interactive Game of Life Exhibit">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2010-08-18T08:27:13+00:00">
            August 18, 2010
           </time>
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="pf-content">
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4904174150/" title="Interaction by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="Interaction" height="333" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4082/4904174150_527b1bb795.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Recently we put together this interactive
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway's_Game_of_Life">
           Game of Life
          </a>
          display as an educational adjunct for a new exhibit by the
          <a href="http://www.sjmusart.org/">
           San Jose Museum of Art
          </a>
          on the works of
          <a href="http://www.sjmusart.org/content/leo-villareal">
           Leo Villareal
          </a>
          . Leo primarily works with light sculptures, and were very excited to see (and participate in) the exhibit, which opens this Friday.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <br/>
          We put together a video showing off the project, embedded here:
         </p>
         <p>
          <object height="405" width="500">
           <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/9F5oER98xh8?fs=1&amp;hl=en_US&amp;color1=0x3a3a3a&amp;color2=0x999999&amp;border=1">
           </param>
           <param name="allowFullScreen" value="true">
           </param>
           <param name="allowscriptaccess" value="always">
           </param>
           <embed allowfullscreen="true" allowscriptaccess="always" height="405" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/9F5oER98xh8?fs=1&amp;hl=en_US&amp;color1=0x3a3a3a&amp;color2=0x999999&amp;border=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="500">
           </embed>
          </object>
         </p>
         <p>
          (If you cant see the video here,
          <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9F5oER98xh8">
           click here
          </a>
          to visit the youtube page.)
         </p>
         <p>
          <span id="more-609">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4904183938/" title="Sitting on boxes by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="Sitting on boxes" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4134/4904183938_6f7ff94f52.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Prior to its installation on a wall at the museum, we set the exhibit on some office boxes to try it out.
         </p>
         <p>
          As you can see, its pretty big.  The interactive surface is 32 x 44 inches, and has LEDs spaced on a 2-inch grid.  Theres also a control section on the lower right corner.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903592485/" title="One board by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="One board" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4119/4903592485_7755f8d8f9.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Up close, the display is constructed of medium-size printed circuit boards that are tiled together.
         </p>
         <p>
          This printed circuit board was designed for the project.  Its 48 inches and supports eight 10 mm diffused-lens LEDs.
          <br/>
          Each LED site is intended to be an independent cell for the game of life, and each one has its own sensor.   The sensors are active infrared proximity sensors, consisting of an infrared LED (tinted blue) and infrared phototransistor (tinted black).  This pair of components detects reflection off of nearby objects, such as your hand when you reach out to touch the display.
         </p>
         <p>
          An Atmel AVR ATmega164P microcontroller manages the sensors and communicates with neighboring boards.  The communications protocol is a low-grade hack: a bit bang protocol, not so different from the scheme used on the Dropout Design/Adafruit
          <a href="http://www.adafruit.com/index.php?main_page=product_info&amp;cPath=24&amp;products_id=89">
           Game of Life Kit
          </a>
          .    Communications and power are shared between neighboring boards using edge connectors.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <br/>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903578183/" title="Assembly by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="Assembly" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4102/4903578183_668c3abdba.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          The circuit boards are snapped together with their connectors, and then installed on threaded standoffs inside the wooden frame.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4904169590/" title="Array of circuit boards by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="Array of circuit boards" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4141/4904169590_bd85c2a671.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          It takes 44 of the circuit boards to fill up the display.  While each circuit board is only moderately complex, the set of boards adds up quickly to about about 3000 electronic components and 10,000 solder joints.
         </p>
         <p>
          Each board draws up to 200 mA of current at 5 V to run the processor, LEDs, and sensors, so the full set of boards draws up to 8.8 A.  Because the edge connectors are not rated for currents this high, the display is actually separately powered in three sections, tied together by the common ground of the power supplies.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903581765/" title="lattice &amp; controls by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="lattice &amp; controls" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4101/4903581765_ac306f3725.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          The individual cells of the display are isolated in a latticework 1.5 inches tall, made of black Depron foam. (Aside: Depron is amazing material.  Its a fairly rigid polystyrene, much like the core of standard foamboard.  Its remarkably rigid for its weight, relatively inexpensive, and cuts faster than almost anything else either with a hobby knife or laser cutter.)  The latticework is necessary not only to define the cells visually, but also to prevent crosstalk of the infrared sensors.
         </p>
         <p>
          The four control buttons on the lower right (Next Step, Run, Pause, Clear) are constructed on two of the circuit boards, constructed identically to the others except for their LED colors:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903599611/" title="Colored sections by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="Colored sections" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4100/4903599611_9487869705.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          These colored sections illuminate an engraved acrylic overlay that has the human-readable button text; the overlay is transparent to infrared, and only minor software changes are necessary to configure these sections as control buttons rather than cells.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903597167/" title="Programming by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="Programming" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4142/4903597167_ff74402b91.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          The downside of having so many independent boards: Each of the 44 boards had to be programmed individually.  Fortunately each board has an accessible AVR ISP header, even with the lattice installed.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <br/>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903595081/" title="power off by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="power off" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4074/4903595081_74a7a27262.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          The top cover is 3/8 thick clear acrylic, 34 x 46 inches. We had it made for us by conventional routing.  Its held in place by a few 1/4-20 button socket cap screws that connect to nuts in the wooden frame.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <br/>
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/oskay/4903586121/" title="led-life 4 by oskay, on Flickr">
           <img alt="led-life 4" height="333" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4137/4903586121_439032c399.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          And that about covers it.  The Leo Villareal exhibit starts this week at SJMoA.  If you havent seen
          <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9F5oER98xh8">
           our video
          </a>
          of this yet, please check it out.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you havent played with Conways Game of Life, thats definitely worth some time.  You might start with
          <a href="http://www.conwaylife.com/">
           a basic simulator
          </a>
          and check out some of the
          <a href="http://www.math.com/students/wonders/life/life.html">
           more interesting details
          </a>
          when youre ready.
         </p>

            <div class="comment-content">
             <p>
              &gt;Obviously, the first thing I thought of when I saw it was "oh, look, a huge Peggy"
             </p>
             <p>
              Theres actually very little in common with the Peggy/Meggy architecture: no multiplexing, no drive transistors, no LED driver chips.  Also, as theres only "peer to peer" communication, its not easy to program a single large image to appear on the tiled display.
             </p>
             <p>
              &gt; So then I thought it was something more akin to the Interactive LED Panels
             </p>
             <p>
              Yes, these are a low-voltage, digital, relative of our interactive LED panels.
             </p>
             <p>
              &gt; Given the sheer number of parts [] I would imagine that we wont be
              <br/>
              &gt; seeing this in the Evil Mad Science store any time soon, no?
             </p>
             <p>
              The actual parts count is pretty low per board, its only when you build so many that it adds up like this.  We will probably design an open source project and/or kit around some aspects of this project.  We need to spend some time playing with the software to figure out what this circuit is really best at in practice.   We dont intend to release a "game of life kit," of course theres already a good one out there.
             </p>
             <p>
              &gt; []this appears to have been designed with expediency and time constraints
              <br/>
              &gt; in mind over power consumption
             </p>
             <p>
              Power consumption was actually a concern.  The power budget per board (200 mA) essentially allows one IR LED to be on at a time and all eight LEDs at 20 mA.  Lower power means less brightness, ultimately.   200 mA per board allows 20 circuit boards to be connected together with one 4 A, 5 V power supply thats not bad, actually, for such good brightness.
             </p>
             <p>
              &gt; [] Is there a more "elegant", less "bit-banged" way to accomplish such a project
              <br/>
              &gt; with fewer parts, but with the same level of flexibility (like the interactive LED panels)?
             </p>
             <p>
              Bit banging is elegant its way, requiring almost zero parts for bidirectional communication in four directions at once.  However the software required to support it has very high overhead, particularly since we built these without crystal oscillators each processors clock can vary in frequency +/- 10%, which means that we need to do a lot of work in synchronization and error detection.  Were actually using a 1N1 protocol at fairly low baud rate, with a parity bit on each command.
             </p>
             <p>
              Depending how much less oppressive we can make the software requirements, we maybe able to do more interesting things with it.  Depending on application, there are some other interesting tricks that can be used.  For example, the IR LEDs can be used as sensors alone, without phototransistors.   The data rate is typically lower, though, so it wasnt actually a good choice for the game of life project.
             </p>
             <p>
              Towards the future, were probably evolving towards more complexity and elegance at the same time.  The xmega chips that weve been playing with have multiple serial ports that can make communicating with neighbors much easier.  The parts count and BOM cost will be higher, but the process will be more natural.
             </p>
             <p>
              &gt; Any chance that the edge connector idea could be made into its own
              <br/>
              &gt; more detailed blog post some time in the future?
             </p>
             <p>
              Interesting idea; well think about it.
