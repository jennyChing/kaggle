       <h1 class="entry-title">
        Jason Salavon reconfigures data into art
       </h1>
       <div class="entry-meta" style="float:left">
        <a href="http://richardrabel.com/contemporary-artist-jason-salavon" rel="bookmark" title="6:02 am">
         <span class="entry-date">
          January 15, 2014
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style addthis_26x26_style" id="addthis_top">

       <div class="entry-content">
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Salavon-HBO_mmg_web_4.jpg" rel="lightbox[4182]">
          <img alt="Jason Salavon amalgamated images" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4183" height="330" src="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Salavon-HBO_mmg_web_4.jpg" width="263"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Contemporary American artist
         <a href="http://www.salavon.com/" target="_blank">
          Jason Salavon
         </a>
         (b. 1970) has been preaching for years that reconfiguring data can result in amazing art  and I have to agree. Using his proprietary software, he merges masses of data that share a common theme and masterfully extracts a beautiful visual of the shared elements in form, texture, color and pattern.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          One Week Skin (HBO)
         </i>
         of 2012 (above) is the result of sourcing and abstracting one week of HBO television programming at 15 frames per second and rearranging the information into colorful geometric pockets of visual information.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/salavon-Portrait_vanDyck_web.jpg" rel="lightbox[4182]">
          <img alt="salavon-Portrait-vanDyck" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4184" height="400" src="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/salavon-Portrait_vanDyck_web.jpg" width="317"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         The spooky
         <i>
          Portrait (van Dyck)
         </i>
         of 2010 is the result of using high-quality reproductions of the bulk of the Masters paintings, overlaying them and producing and average amalgamation of his portraits in terms of color and forms.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/salavon3.jpg" rel="lightbox[4182]">
          <img alt="Jason Salavon Titanic" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4186" height="326" src="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/salavon3.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         In
         <i>
          The Top Grossing Film of All Time
         </i>
         of 2000, Salavon took all 350,000 frames of the movie Titanic and reduced them to a block of color most represented by each frame. The narrative of the movie was kept, so you can imagine Leonardo DiCaprio playing king of the world in the light blue horizontal strip towards the top and the sinking of the ship represented by the horizontal dark hues towards the bottom.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/salavon.jpg" rel="lightbox[4182]">
          <img alt="Salavon Ode to Rubens" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4185" height="400" src="http://richardrabel.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/salavon.jpg" width="400"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         In his
         <i>
          Baroque Painting
         </i>
         of 2010, he samples and reformats the colors taken from the paintings of Sir Peter Paul Rubens to derive the palette most representational in Rubens paintings. Each line width is proportional to color frequency and its position in the square is based on color saturation. The purpose of ordering it by saturation is to bring depth to a rigid geometric work.
        </p>
        <p>
         Had someone told me about Jasons art without the reference of a visual, I would have chalked it to a juvenile science project. But the art is frankly stunning with serious depth and technical mastery. There is nothing sophomoric about it. This is probably why hes been named one of the 50 most collectible contemporary artists under 50 by Art &amp; Auction Magazine. Glad I have my finger on the pulse and now  you do too!
        </p>
        <h6>
