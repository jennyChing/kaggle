     <p>
      <b>
       Irony punctuation
      </b>
      is any proposed form of notation used to denote
      <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">
       irony
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Sarcasm" title="Sarcasm">
       sarcasm
      </a>
      in text. Written
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      lacks a standard way to mark irony, and several forms of
      <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">
       punctuation
      </a>
      have been proposed. Among the oldest and most frequently attested are the
      <b>
       percontation point
      </b>
      proposed by English printer
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Denham" title="Henry Denham">
       Henry Denham
      </a>
      in the 1580s, and the
      <b>
       irony mark
      </b>
      , used by
      <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Jobard" title="Marcellin Jobard">
       Marcellin Jobard
      </a>
      and French poet
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcanter_de_Brahm" title="Alcanter de Brahm">
       Alcanter de Brahm
      </a>
      during the 19th century. Both marks take the form of a reversed
      <a href="/wiki/Question_mark" title="Question mark">
       question mark
      </a>
      , "
      <span class="Unicode">
       
      </span>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      Irony punctuation is primarily used to indicate that a sentence should be understood at a second level. A bracketed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclamation_point#Sarcasm" title="Exclamation point">
       exclamation point
      </a>
      or question mark as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Scare_quotes" title="Scare quotes">
       scare quotes
      </a>
      are also sometimes used to express irony or sarcasm.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Percontation_point">
       Percontation point
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Percontation point">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The modern question mark (? U+003F) is descended from the "punctus interrogativus" (described as "a lightning flash, striking from right to left"),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but unlike the modern question mark, the punctus interrogativus may be contrasted with the punctus percontativusthe former marking questions that require an answer while the latter marks rhetorical questions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-percontation_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-percontation-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The percontation point (
      <span class="Unicode">
       
      </span>
      ), a reversed question mark later referred to as a rhetorical question mark, was proposed by
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Denham" title="Henry Denham">
       Henry Denham
      </a>
      in the 1580s and was used at the end of a question that does not require an answera
      <a href="/wiki/Rhetorical_question" title="Rhetorical question">
       rhetorical question
      </a>
      . Its use died out in the 17th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This character can be represented using the reversed question mark (
      <span class="Unicode">
       
      </span>
      ) found in Unicode as U+2E2E; another character approximating it is the Arabic question mark (), U+061F.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Irony_mark">
       Irony mark
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Irony mark">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1688,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkins" title="John Wilkins">
       John Wilkins
      </a>
      in his famous
      <i>
       Essay Towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language
      </i>
      proposed using an
      <a href="/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks" title="Inverted question and exclamation marks">
       inverted exclamation mark
      </a>
      to punctuate ironic statements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Jobard" title="Marcellin Jobard">
       Marcellin Jobard
      </a>
      , a Belgian newspaper publisher, introduced an irony mark in the shape of an oversize arrow head with small stem (rather like an
      <a href="/wiki/Ideogram" title="Ideogram">
       ideogram
      </a>
      of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_Tree" title="Christmas Tree">
       Christmas Tree
      </a>
      ) in 1841. The next year he expanded his idea, suggesting the symbol could be used in various orientations (on its side, upside down, etc.) too as "a point of irritation, an indignation point, a point of hesitation."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="712" data-file-width="1252" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png/220px-Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png/330px-Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png/440px-Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ironie-Larousse-1897-p329.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Irony mark as designed by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcanter_de_Brahm" title="Alcanter de Brahm">
         Alcanter de Brahm
        </a>
        in a French encyclopedia from 1905
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          6
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The irony point (
      <span class="Unicode">
       
      </span>
      ) (
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
       <i>
        point dironie
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was proposed by the French poet
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcanter_de_Brahm" title="Alcanter de Brahm">
       Alcanter de Brahm
      </a>
      (alias Marcel Bernhardt) in his 1899 book
      <i>
       L'ostensoir des ironies
      </i>
      to indicate that a sentence should be understood at a second level (irony, sarcasm, etc.). It is illustrated by a glyph resembling but not identical to a small, elevated, backward-facing question mark.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-percontation_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-percontation-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The same mark was used earlier by
      <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Jobard" title="Marcellin Jobard">
       Marcellin Jobard
      </a>
      in an article dated June 11, 1841, and commented in an 1842 report.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Herv%C3%A9_Bazin" title="Herv Bazin">
       Herv Bazin
      </a>
      in his 1966 essay
      <i>
       Plumons lOiseau
      </i>
      ("Let's pluck the bird"), used the Greek letter
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psi_(letter)" title="Psi (letter)">
        psi
       </a>
      </i>
      with a dot below for the same purpose.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the same work, the author proposed five other innovative punctuation marks: the "doubt point" (
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Point_de_doute.svg">
       <img alt="Point de doute.svg" data-file-height="46" data-file-width="28" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Point_de_doute.svg/10px-Point_de_doute.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Point_de_doute.svg/15px-Point_de_doute.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Point_de_doute.svg/20px-Point_de_doute.svg.png 2x" width="10"/>
      </a>
      ), "certitude point" (
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Point_de_certitude.svg">
       <img alt="Point de certitude.svg" data-file-height="23" data-file-width="13" height="18" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Point_de_certitude.svg/10px-Point_de_certitude.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Point_de_certitude.svg/15px-Point_de_certitude.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Point_de_certitude.svg/20px-Point_de_certitude.svg.png 2x" width="10"/>
      </a>
      ), "acclamation point" (
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Point_d%27acclamation.svg">
       <img alt="Point d'acclamation.svg" data-file-height="23" data-file-width="14" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Point_d%27acclamation.svg/10px-Point_d%27acclamation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Point_d%27acclamation.svg/15px-Point_d%27acclamation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Point_d%27acclamation.svg/20px-Point_d%27acclamation.svg.png 2x" width="10"/>
      </a>
      ), "authority point" (
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg">
       <img alt="Point d'autorit.svg" data-file-height="23" data-file-width="13" height="18" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg/10px-Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg/15px-Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg/20px-Point_d%27autorit%C3%A9.svg.png 2x" width="10"/>
      </a>
      ), and "love point" (
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Point_d%27amour.svg">
       <img alt="Point d'amour.svg" data-file-height="23" data-file-width="20" height="17" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Point_d%27amour.svg/15px-Point_d%27amour.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Point_d%27amour.svg/23px-Point_d%27amour.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Point_d%27amour.svg/30px-Point_d%27amour.svg.png 2x" width="15"/>
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2007, the Dutch foundation CPNB
      <i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Collectieve_Propaganda_van_het_Nederlandse_Boek" title="Collectieve Propaganda van het Nederlandse Boek">
        Collectieve Propaganda van het Nederlandse Boek
       </a>
       )
      </i>
      presented another design of an irony mark (
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ironieteken.svg">
       <img alt="Ironieteken.svg" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="341" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Ironieteken.svg/6px-Ironieteken.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Ironieteken.svg/9px-Ironieteken.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Ironieteken.svg/12px-Ironieteken.svg.png 2x" width="6"/>
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [

        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scare_quotes">
       Scare quotes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scare quotes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Scare_quotes" title="Scare quotes">
       Scare quotes
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Scare_quotes" title="Scare quotes">
       Scare quotes
      </a>
      are a particular use of
      <a href="/wiki/Quotation_mark" title="Quotation mark">
       quotation marks
      </a>
      . They are placed around a word or phrase to indicate that it is not used in the fashion that the writer would personally use it. In contrast to the nominal typographic purpose of quotation marks, the enclosed words are not necessarily quoted from another source. When read aloud, various techniques are used to convey the sense, such as prepending the addition of "so-called" or a similar word or phrase of disdain, using a sarcastic or mocking tone, or using
      <a href="/wiki/Air_quotes" title="Air quotes">
       air quotes
      </a>
      , or any combination of the above.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Temherte_slaq.C3.AE">
       <i>
        Temherte slaq
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Temherte slaq">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In certain
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopic_languages" title="Ethiopic languages">
       Ethiopic languages
      </a>
      , sarcasm and unreal phrases are indicated at the end of a sentence with a sarcasm mark called
      <i>
       temherte slaq
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       temherte slaq
      </i>
      (U+00A1) (
      <b>
       
      </b>
      ), a character that looks like the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverted_exclamation_point" title="Inverted exclamation point">
       inverted exclamation point
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_typography">
       Other typography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other typography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Rhetorical questions in some informal situations can use a bracketed question mark, e.g. "Oh, really[?]"  The equivalent for an ironic or sarcastic statement would be a bracketed exclamation mark, e.g. "Oh, really[!]". Subtitles, such as in
      <a href="/wiki/Teletext" title="Teletext">
       Teletext
      </a>
      , sometimes use an exclamation mark within brackets or parentheses to mark sarcasm.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is common in online conversation among computer specialists to use a pseudo-
      <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">
       HTML
      </a>
      element:
      <code>
       &lt;/sarcasm&gt;
      </code>
      . The tag is often written only after the sarcasm so as to momentarily trick the reader before admitting the joke. Another example is bracketing text with the symbol for the element iron (
      <code>
       &lt;Fe&gt;
      </code>
      and
      <code>
       &lt;/Fe&gt;
      </code>
      ) to denote irony. Commonly used on social news-based sites is a single
      <code>
       /s
      </code>
      , placed at the end of a comment to indicate a sarcastic tone for the preceding text. "Rolling eyes," ":&gt;" and ":P"
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emoticons" title="Emoticons">
       emoticons
      </a>
      are often used as well, particularly in
      <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">
       instant messaging
      </a>
      , while a
      <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">
       Twitter
      </a>
      -style
      <a href="/wiki/Hashtag" title="Hashtag">
       hashtag
      </a>
      , #sarcasm, is also gaining currency
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      On
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twitch_(website)" title="Twitch (website)">
       Twitch.tv
      </a>
      , users often use emotes to denote sarcastic messages, typically
      <i>
       Kappa
      </i>
      , depicting the face of former employee Josh "Kappa" DeSeno
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with a smug look. This usage has spread to sites where the
      <i>
       Kappa
      </i>
      emote is not implemented.
     </p>
     <p>
      A "SarcMark" symbol requiring custom computer font software was proposed in 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The sports blog
      <i>
       Card Chronicle
      </i>
      has a standardized means to show the preceding text is intended sarcastically: inserting a tilde (~) after the period at the end of the sentence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
