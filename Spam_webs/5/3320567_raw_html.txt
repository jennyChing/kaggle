     <p>
      <b>
       Military ranks
      </b>
      are a system of
      <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">
       hierarchical
      </a>
      relationships in
      <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" title="Armed forces">
       armed forces
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">
       police
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligence_agencies" title="Intelligence agencies">
       intelligence agencies
      </a>
      or other institutions organized along military lines. Usually, uniforms denote the bearer's rank by particular insignia affixed to the uniforms. Ranking systems have been known for most of military history to be advantageous for military operations, in particular with regards to logistics, command, and coordination; as time continued and military operations became larger and more complex, military ranks increased and ranking systems themselves became more complex.
     </p>
     <p>
      Within modern armed forces, the use of ranks is almost universal.
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       Communist
      </a>
      states have sometimes abolished rank (e.g., the Soviet
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">
       Red Army
      </a>
      19181935,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">
       Chinese
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="People's Liberation Army">
       People's Liberation Army
      </a>
      19651988,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Albania" title="Military of Albania">
       Albanian Army
      </a>
      19661991
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), only to re-establish them after encountering operational difficulties of
      <a href="/wiki/Command_and_control" title="Command and control">
       command and control
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term "rank" comes from Persian 
      <i>
       rang
      </i>
      meaning "colour", as the Sassanid army was ranked and dressed by colour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval_ranks">
       Ancient and medieval ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_ranks">
       Greek ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greek ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From 501 BC the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athenians" title="Athenians">
       Athenians
      </a>
      annually elected ten individuals to the rank of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Strategos" title="Strategos">
        strategos
       </a>
      </i>
      , one for each of the ten "tribes" that had been created with the founding of the
      <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
       democracy
      </a>
      . Strategos literally means "strategist" which was considered the army leader and so it is usually translated as "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">
       general
      </a>
      ". Originally these generals worked together with the old
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Polemarch" title="Polemarch">
        polemarchos
       </a>
      </i>
      ("warlord") but over time the latter figure was absorbed into the generalship: each of the ten generals would rotate as polemarch for one day, and during this day his vote would serve as tie-breaker if necessary.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ten generals were equal to one another; there was no hierarchy among them, however a basic form of democracy was in effect: For example, at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marathon" title="Battle of Marathon">
       Battle of Marathon
      </a>
      in 490 BC, the generals determined the battle plan by majority vote. Particular assignments, however, might have been given to individual generals; inevitably there was a regular division of responsibilities.
     </p>
     <p>
      The rank that was subordinate to a top general was a
      <i>
       taxiarchos
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxiarhos" title="Taxiarhos">
        taxiarhos
       </a>
      </i>
      , something akin to the modern
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier" title="Brigadier">
       brigadier
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
       Sparta
      </a>
      , however, the title was polemarchos. Below this was the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Syntagmatarchis" title="Syntagmatarchis">
        syntagmatarchis
       </a>
      </i>
      , which can be translated as "leader of a
      <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">
       regiment
      </a>
      " (
      <i>
       syntagma
      </i>
      ) and was therefore like a modern
      <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">
       colonel
      </a>
      . Below him was the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tagmatarches" title="Tagmatarches">
        tagmatarches
       </a>
      </i>
      , a commanding officer of a
      <i>
       tagma
      </i>
      (near to the modern
      <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">
       battalion
      </a>
      ). The rank was roughly equivalent to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Legatus" title="Legatus">
        legatus
       </a>
      </i>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">
       Roman legion
      </a>
      . Next was the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lokhagos" title="Lokhagos">
        lokhagos
       </a>
      </i>
      , an officer who led an infantry unit called a
      <i>
       lokhos
      </i>
      that consisted of roughly a hundred men, much the same as in a modern
      <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">
       company
      </a>
      led by a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(OF-2)" title="Captain (OF-2)">
       captain
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A Greek cavalry (
      <i>
       hippikon
      </i>
      ) regiment was called a
      <i>
       hipparchia
      </i>
      and was commanded by an
      <a href="/wiki/Epihipparch" title="Epihipparch">
       epihipparch
      </a>
      . The unit was split into two and led by two
      <i>
       hipparchos
      </i>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hipparchos_(cavalry_officer)" title="Hipparchos (cavalry officer)">
       hipparch
      </a>
      , but Spartan cavalry was led by a
      <i>
       hipparmostes
      </i>
      . A
      <i>
       hippotoxots
      </i>
      was a mounted archer. A Greek cavalry company was led by a
      <i>
       tetrarchs
      </i>
      or
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tetrarch" title="wikt:tetrarch">
       tetrarch
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The rank and file of the military in most of the Greek city states was composed of ordinary citizens. Heavily armed foot soldiers were called
      <i>
       hoplits
      </i>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Hoplite" title="Hoplite">
       hoplites
      </a>
      and a
      <i>
       hoplomachos
      </i>
      was a drill or weapons instructor.
     </p>
     <p>
      Once
      <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
       Athens
      </a>
      became a naval power, the top generals of the land armies had authority over the naval fleets as well. Under them, each
      <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">
       warship
      </a>
      was commanded by a
      <i>
       trirarchos
      </i>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Trierarch" title="Trierarch">
       trierarch
      </a>
      , a word which originally meant "
      <a href="/wiki/Trireme" title="Trireme">
       trireme
      </a>
      officer" but persisted when other types of vessels came into use. Moreover, as in modern navies, the different tasks associated with running a ship were delegated to different subordinates. Specifically, the
      <i>
       kybernts
      </i>
      was the helmsman, the
      <i>
       keleusts
      </i>
      managed the rowing speed, and the
      <i>
       trirauls
      </i>
      was the flute player who maintained the strike rate for the oarsmen. Following further specialization, the naval strategos was replaced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Navarch" title="Navarch">
       <i>
        nauarchos
       </i>
      </a>
      , a sea officer equating to an
      <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">
       admiral
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      With the rise of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedon" title="Macedon">
       Macedonia
      </a>
      under
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Macedon" title="Philip II of Macedon">
       Philip II of Macedon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
       Alexander the Great
      </a>
      , the Greek military became professional, tactics became more sophisticated and additional levels of ranking developed. Foot soldiers were organized into heavy infantry
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phalanx_formation" title="Phalanx formation">
       phalanxes
      </a>
      called
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phalangites" title="Phalangites">
        phalangites
       </a>
      </i>
      . These were among the first troops ever to be drilled, and they fought packed in a close rectangular formation, typically eight men deep, with a leader at the head of each column (or file) and a secondary leader in the middle so that the back rows could move off to the sides if more frontage was needed.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <i>
       tetrarchia
      </i>
      was a unit of four files and a
      <i>
       tetrarchs
      </i>
      or tetrarch was a commander of four files; a
      <i>
       dilochia
      </i>
      was a double file and a
      <i>
       dilochits
      </i>
      was a double-file leader; a lochos was a single file and a lochagos was a file leader; a
      <i>
       dimoiria
      </i>
      was a half file and a
      <i>
       dimoirites
      </i>
      was a half-file leader. Another name for the half file was a
      <i>
       hmilochion
      </i>
      with a
      <i>
       hmilochits
      </i>
      being a half-file leader.
     </p>
     <p>
      Different types of units, however, were divided differently and therefore their leaders had different titles. For example, under a numbering system by tens, a
      <i>
       dekas
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       dekania
      </i>
      was a unit of ten led by a
      <i>
       dekarchos
      </i>
      , a
      <i>
       hekatontarchia
      </i>
      was a unit of hundred led by a
      <i>
       hekatontarchos
      </i>
      and a
      <i>
       khiliostys
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       khiliarchia
      </i>
      was a unit of a thousand led by a
      <i>
       khiliarchos
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The cavalry, for which Alexander became most famous (in a military sense), grew more varied. There were heavy cavalry and wing cavalry (
      <i>
       il
      </i>
      ) units, the latter commanded by an
      <i>
       ilarchos
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_ranks">
       Roman ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The use of formalized ranks came into widespread use with the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">
       Roman legions
      </a>
      after the reforms by
      <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Marius" title="Gaius Marius">
       Marius
      </a>
      . Comparisons to modern ranks, however, can only be loose because the Roman army's command structure was very different from the organizational structure of its modern counterparts, which arose from the medieval mercenary companies, rather than from the writings of Fourth Century Roman writer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetius" title="Vegetius">
       Vegetius
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
       Caesar
      </a>
      's commentaries on his conquest of
      <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">
       Gaul
      </a>
      and the civil war.
     </p>
     <p>
      Military command properly so-called was a political office in Rome. A commander needed to be equipped with
      <a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">
       imperium
      </a>
      , a politico-religious concept. The
      <a href="/wiki/King_of_Rome" title="King of Rome">
       king
      </a>
      of course possessed it (the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rex_sacrorum" title="Rex sacrorum">
        rex sacrorum
       </a>
      </i>
      was strictly forbidden to have it to avoid a return to the monarchy). In the Republic, commanding was confined to
      <a href="/wiki/Consul" title="Consul">
       consuls
      </a>
      or (seldom) to
      <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">
       praetor
      </a>
      , or in cases of necessity a
      <a href="/wiki/Dictator" title="Dictator">
       dictator
      </a>
      .
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proconsuls" title="Proconsuls">
       Proconsuls
      </a>
      , after the establishment of the office, would also be used. In Imperial times, each legion was commanded by the emperor, who was technically either consul or proconsul.
     </p>
     <p>
      The commander could appoint a deputy, a so-called
      <a href="/wiki/Legatus" title="Legatus">
       legate
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       legatus
      </i>
      ). The association of "legatus" with "legion" is
      <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">
       folk etymology
      </a>
      , as the meaning of
      <i>
       legatus
      </i>
      is "proxy" or "envoy". Legates were typically drawn from the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">
       Roman Senate
      </a>
      for a three-year term. The political nature of high military command was even reflected here, in that legions were always subordinate to the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">
       governor
      </a>
      , and only the second and further legions stationed in a province had their own
      <i>
       legatus legionis
      </i>
      . The real commanders and the legates together were, in modern terms, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_officers" title="General officers">
       general officers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Immediately beneath the commander (or his legate) were six
      <a href="/wiki/Military_tribune" title="Military tribune">
       military tribunes
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       tribuni militum
      </i>
      ), five of whom were young men of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equestrian_(Roman)" title="Equestrian (Roman)">
       equestrian
      </a>
      rank and one of whom was a nobleman who was headed for the Senate. The latter is called laticlavian tribune (
      <i>
       tribunus laticlavius
      </i>
      ) and was second in command. If in modern
      <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">
       divisions
      </a>
      the deputy commander is a
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general" title="Brigadier general">
       brigadier general
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Tribunus_laticlavius" title="Tribunus laticlavius">
       laticlavian tribune
      </a>
      can perhaps be translated with this rank, though he commanded no formation of his own. The other tribunes are called
      <i>
       tribuni angusticlavii
      </i>
      and are equivalent to staff officers in both senses of the term: of ranks
      <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">
       major
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel" title="Lieutenant colonel">
       lieutenant colonel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">
       colonel
      </a>
      , and with administrative duties. They did not command a formation of their own. The term
      <i>
       military tribune
      </i>
      is even sometimes translated into English as "colonel"  most notably by the late classicist
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">
       Robert Graves
      </a>
      in his "
      <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">
       Claudius
      </a>
      " novels and his translation of
      <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">
       Suetonius
      </a>
      '
      <i>
       Twelve Caesars
      </i>
       to avoid confusion with the political "
      <a href="/wiki/Tribune" title="Tribune">
       tribunes
      </a>
      of the people"; in addition, they must not either be confused with the "military tribunes with consular authority", who in early Republican times could replace the consuls.
     </p>
     <p>
      The third highest officer of a legion, above the angusticlavian tribunes, was the
      <a href="/wiki/Praefectus_Castrorum" title="Praefectus Castrorum">
       praefectus castrorum
      </a>
      . He too would have colonel rank in modern armies, yet he differed much from the tribunes in that his office was not part of the rather administrative
      <i>
       cursus
      </i>
      , but normally filled by former centurions. (Modern armies have a
      <a href="/wiki/Warrant_officer" title="Warrant officer">
       similar distinction
      </a>
      on a lower scale, i.e. between commissioned and non-commissioned officers.)
     </p>
     <p>
      The fighting men in the legion were formed into
      <i>
       ranks
      </i>
      , rows of men who fought as a unit. Under Marius's new system, legions were divided into ten
      <a href="/wiki/Cohort_(military_unit)" title="Cohort (military unit)">
       cohorts
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       cohortes
      </i>
      ) (roughly equivalent to
      <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">
       battalion
      </a>
      and immediately subject to the legion), each consisting of three manipula, each of them of two
      <i>
       centuries
      </i>
      (a rather small
      <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">
       company
      </a>
      in modern terms), each consisting of between 60 and 160 men. Each century was led by a
      <a href="/wiki/Centurion" title="Centurion">
       centurion
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       centurio
      </i>
      , traditionally translated as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(OF-2)" title="Captain (OF-2)">
       captain
      </a>
      ), who was assisted by a number of junior officers, such as an Optio. Centuries were further broken into ten
      <i>
       contubernia
      </i>
      of eight soldiers each. The manipula were commanded by one of their two centurions, the cohorts by one of their three manipulum's centurions; the most senior cohort-commanding centurions was called
      <i>
       primus pilus
      </i>
      . The ranks of centurions in the individual cohorts were, in descending order,
      <i>
       pilus prior
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       pilus posterior
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       princeps prior
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       princeps posterior
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       hastatus prior
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       hastatus posterior
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Individual soldiers were referred to as soldiers (
      <i>
       milites
      </i>
      ) or legionaries (
      <i>
       legionarii
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Roman discipline was severe, with all ranks subject to corporal and capital punishment at the commander's discretion. For example, if a cohort broke in battle, the typical punishment was
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Decimation_(Roman_army)" title="Decimation (Roman army)">
        decimation
       </a>
      </i>
      , in which every tenth soldier, selected by lot, was killed. Decimation was not commonplace as lack of men would reduce combat effectiveness, which would eventually overcome the psychological "benefit" of keeping the troops in line.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Turko-Mongol_ranks_.28Ancestral_Central_Asian_Tribes.29">
       Turko-Mongol ranks (Ancestral Central Asian Tribes)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Turko-Mongol ranks (Ancestral Central Asian Tribes)">

       </a>

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">

       Mongol military tactics and organization
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      There were no ranks in the modern sense of a hierarchy of titles, although the army was organized into a hierarchical command. The organization of the army was based on the decimal system, employed by
      <a href="/wiki/Oghuz_Khagan" title="Oghuz Khagan">
       Oghuz Khagan
      </a>
      . The army was built upon a squad of ten (
      <i>
       aravt
      </i>
      ) led by an appointed chief. Ten of these would then compose a company of a hundred (
      <i>
       zuut
      </i>
      ), also led by an appointed chief. The next unit was a regiment of a thousand (
      <i>
       myangat
      </i>
      ) led by an appointed
      <i>
       noyan
      </i>
      . The largest organic unit was a ten thousand man unit (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tumen_(unit)" title="Tumen (unit)">
        tumen
       </a>
      </i>
      ) also led by an appointed
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Noyan" title="Noyan">
        noyan
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_ranks">
       Persian ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Persian ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The army of ancient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">
       Persia
      </a>
      consisted of manageable military groupings under the individual commands. Starting at the bottom, a unit of 10 was called a
      <i>
       dathabam
      </i>
      and was led by a
      <i>
       dathapatis
      </i>
      . A unit of 100 men was a 'satabam' lead by a 'satapatis'. A unit of 1,000 was a
      <i>
       hazarabam
      </i>
      and was commanded by a
      <i>
       hazarapatis
      </i>
      . A unit of 10,000 was a
      <i>
       baivarabam
      </i>
      and was commanded by a
      <i>
       baivarapatis
      </i>
      . The Greeks called such masses of troops a
      <i>
       myrias
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       myriad
      </i>
      . Among mounted troops, an
      <i>
       asabam
      </i>
      was a
      <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">
       cavalry
      </a>
      unit led by an
      <i>
       asapatis
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Historians have discovered the existence of the following ranks in
      <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">
       Parthian
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sassanid_army" title="Sassanid army">
       Sassanian
      </a>
      armies:
     </p>
     <ul>

       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spahbod" title="Spahbod">

        </a>
       </i>
       (to be replaced with four
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spahbod" title="Spahbod">
         spahbods
        </a>
       </i>
       , one for each frontier of the Empire during the reign of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khosrau_I" title="Khosrau I">
        Khosrau I
       </a>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       Commander of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">
        Cavalry
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        aspwargan salar
       </i>
       (Parthian) or
       <i>
        aswaran salar
       </i>
       (Sassanian).
      </li>
      <li>
       Commander of the Archers:

       </i>
      </li>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_ranks">
       Medieval ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Medieval ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Medieval militaries did not have a unified rank structure; while the
      <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">
       feudal
      </a>
      lords were in some ways equivalent to modern officers, they didn't have a strict hierarchya king was conceived of as
      <a href="/wiki/Primus_inter_pares" title="Primus inter pares">
       first among equals
      </a>
      , not a
      <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">
       monarch
      </a>
      as later or ancient societies understood the concept, and all nobles were theoretically equals (hence "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">
       peers
      </a>
      "). A nobleman was obligated to bring a set number of troops when asked by his liege-lord, a king or merely a higher-ranked noble who had obtained his service by
      <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">
       the gift of land
      </a>
      . The troops' lord retained at least nominal control over themmany medieval military planning sessions involved negotiating each lord's role in the coming battleand each lord was allowed to leave after a predetermined amount of time had passed.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="High_command_in_medieval_armies">
       High command in medieval armies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High command in medieval armies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">
       king
      </a>
      s army was placed under the command of the High
      <a href="/wiki/Constable" title="Constable">
       Constable
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">
       commander-in-chief
      </a>
      . The High Constable had authority over the local constables, commanders of the garrisons of major castles. The High Constable had the help of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Marshal" title="Field Marshal">
       Field Marshal
      </a>
      , an officer that set up the armys camp. (Marshals acted as chiefs of logistics and were also employed by royal and noble courts.) The High Constable derived his authority over the army from his role of head of the Cavalry.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_modern_ranks">
       Origins of modern ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Origins of modern ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      As the Middle Ages came to an end, the rank structure of medieval armies became more formalized. The top officers were known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commissioned_officers" title="Commissioned officers">
       commissioned officers
      </a>
      because their rank came from a royal commission. Army commissions were reserved for the elite  the aristocracy of mainland Europe and the aristocracy and gentry of Great Britain.
     </p>
     <p>
      The basic unit of the medieval army was the
      <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">
       company
      </a>
      , a band of soldiers assigned (or raised) by a vassal
      <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">
       lord
      </a>
      on behalf of
      <i>
       his lord
      </i>
      (in later times the king himself). The vassal lord in command of the company was a commissioned officer with the rank of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(OF-2)" title="Captain (OF-2)">
       captain
      </a>
      . Captain was derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
       Late Latin
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       capitaneus
      </i>
      (meaning "head man" or
      <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">
       chief
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      The commissioned officer assisting the captain with command of the company was the
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">
       lieutenant
      </a>
      . Lieutenant was derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French language
      </a>
      ; the
      <i>
       lieu
      </i>
      meaning place as in a position; and
      <i>
       tenant
      </i>
      meaning holding as in holding a position; thus a lieutenant is somebody who holds a position in the absence of his superior. When he was not assisting the captain, the lieutenant commanded a unit called a
      <a href="/wiki/Platoon" title="Platoon">
       platoon
      </a>
      , particularly a more specialized platoon. The word is derived from the 17th-century French peloton, meaning a small ball or small detachment of men, which came from pelote, a ball.
     </p>
     <p>
      The commissioned officer carrying the (infantry) companys flag was the
      <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">
       ensign
      </a>
      . The word ensign was in fact derived from the Latin word
      <a href="/wiki/Insignia" title="Insignia">
       insignia
      </a>
      . In cavalry companies the equivalent rank was cornet. In English usage, these ranks were merged into the single rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Second_lieutenant" title="Second lieutenant">
       second lieutenant
      </a>
      in the 19th Century.
     </p>
     <p>
      Not all officers received a commission from the king. Certain specialists were granted a warrant, certifying their expertise as craftsmen. These
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warrant_officers" title="Warrant officers">
       warrant officers
      </a>
      assisted the commissioned officers but ranked above the non-commissioned officers. They received their authority from superior officers rather than the king. The first NCOs were the armed servants (men-at-arms) of the aristocracy, assigned to command, organize and train the militia units raised for battle. After years of commanding a squad, an NCO could be promoted to
      <a href="/wiki/Sergeant" title="Sergeant">
       sergeant
      </a>
      , the highest NCO rank. While a sergeant might have commanded a squad upon promotion, he usually became a staff officer. While commissioned staff officers assisted their commander with personnel, intelligence, operations and logistics, the sergeant was a jack of all trades, concerning himself with all aspects of administration to maintain the enlisted men serving under his commander. Over time, sergeants were differentiated into many ranks as various levels of sergeants were used by the commanders of various levels of units.
     </p>
     <p>
      A corporal commanded a
      <a href="/wiki/Squad" title="Squad">
       squad
      </a>
      . Squad derived from the Italian word for a square or block of soldiers. In fact, corporal was derived from the Italian caporal de squadra (head of the squad). Corporals were assisted by lancepesades. Lancepesades were veteran soldiers;
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lancepesade" title="Lancepesade">
       lancepesade
      </a>
      was derived from the Italian "lancia spezzata" meaning broken spear - the broken spear being a metaphor for combat experience, where such an occurrence was likely. The first lancepesades were simply experienced privates; who either assisted their corporal or performed the duties of a corporal themselves. It was this second function that made armies increasingly regard their lancepesades as a grade of corporal rather than a grade of private. As a result, the rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Lance_corporal" title="Lance corporal">
       lance corporal
      </a>
      was derived from combining lancepesade and corporal.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the Middle Ages came to an end, kings increasingly relied on professional soldiers to fill the bottom ranks of their armies instead of militiamen. Each of these professionals began their careers as a
      <a href="/wiki/Private_(rank)" title="Private (rank)">
       private
      </a>
      . The private was a man who signed a private contract with the company commander, offering his services in return for pay. The money was raised through taxation; those yeomen (smallholding peasants) who did not fulfill their annual 40-day militia service paid a tax that funded professional soldiers recruited from the yeomanry. This money was handed to the company commanders from the royal treasury, the company commanders using the money to recruit the troops.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_higher_ranks">
       Origins of higher ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Origins of higher ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      As armies grew larger, composed of multiple companies, one captain was granted general (overall) authority over the field armies by the king. (National armies were the armies of the kings. Field armies were armies raised by the king to enter the battle field in preparation for major battles.) In French history,
      <i>
       lieutenant du roi
      </i>
      was a title born by the officer sent with military powers to represent the king in certain provinces. A
      <i>
       lieutenant du roi
      </i>
      was sometimes known as a
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_g%C3%A9n%C3%A9ral" title="Lieutenant gnral">
        lieutenant gnral
       </a>
      </i>
      to distinguish him from lieutenants subordinate to mere captains. The sergeant acting as staff officer to the captain general was known as the sergeant-major general. This was eventually shortened to
      <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">
       major general
      </a>
      , while captain general was shortened to simply
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">
       general
      </a>
      . This is the reason a
      <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">
       major
      </a>
      outranks a lieutenant, but a lieutenant general outranks a major general.
     </p>
     <p>
      As armies grew bigger, heraldry and unit identification remained primarily a matter of the regiment.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigades" title="Brigades">
       Brigades
      </a>
      headed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigadier_generals" title="Brigadier generals">
       brigadier generals
      </a>
      were the units invented as a tactical unit, by the Swedish king
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus" title="Gustavus Adolphus">
       Gustavus Adolphus
      </a>
      the second ("Gustav II Adolf," who was killed at the battle of Ltzen 1632). It was introduced to overcome the normal army structure, consisting of regiments. The so-called brigada was a mixed unit, comprising infantry, cavalry and normally
      <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">
       artillery
      </a>
      too, designated for a special task. The size of such brigada was a reinforced company up to two regiments. The brigada was a 17th-century form of the modern task force. In some armies "brigadier general" has been shortened to "
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier" title="Brigadier">
       brigadier
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      Around the end of the 16th century, companies were grouped into regiments. The officers commissioned to lead these regiments were in fact called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonels" title="Colonels">
       colonels
      </a>
      (column officers). They were first appointed in Spain by King
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
       Ferdinand II of Aragon
      </a>
      where they were also known as coronellos (crown officers) since they were appointed by the Crown. Thus the English pronunciation of the word colonel.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first colonels were captains granted command of their regiments by commission of the king. The lieutenants of the colonel were the
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel" title="Lieutenant colonel">
       lieutenant colonels
      </a>
      . In the 17th century, the sergeant of the colonel was the
      <a href="/wiki/Sergeant_major" title="Sergeant major">
       sergeant major
      </a>
      . These were field officers, third in command of their regiments (after their colonels and lieutenant colonels), with a role similar to the older, army-level sergeants major (although obviously on a smaller scale). The older position became known as
      <a href="/wiki/Sergeant_major_general" title="Sergeant major general">
       sergeant major general
      </a>
      to distinguish it. Over time, the sergeant was dropped from both titles since both ranks were used for commissioned officers. This gave rise to the modern ranks of
      <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">
       major
      </a>
      and major general.
     </p>
     <p>
      The full title of sergeant major fell out of use until the latter part of the 18th century, when it began to be applied to the senior non-commissioned officer of an infantry battalion or cavalry regiment.
     </p>
     <p>
      Regiments were later split into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battalions" title="Battalions">
       battalions
      </a>
      with a lieutenant colonel as a
      <a href="/wiki/Commanding_officer" title="Commanding officer">
       commanding officer
      </a>
      and a major as an
      <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">
       executive officer
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_ranks">
       Modern ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Modern military services recognize three broad categories of personnel. These are codified in the
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">
       Geneva Conventions
      </a>
      , which distinguish
      <i>
       officers
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       non-commissioned officers
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       enlisted men
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Apart from
      <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">
       conscripted
      </a>
      personnel one can distinguish:
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commissioned_officers">
       Commissioned officers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Commissioned officers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">
       Officers
      </a>
      are distinguished from other military members (or an
      <i>
       officer in training
      </i>
      ) by holding a
      <a href="/wiki/Commission_(document)" title="Commission (document)">
       commission
      </a>
      ; they are trained or training as leaders and hold command positions.
     </p>
     <p>
      Officers are further generally separated into four levels:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       General, flag, or air officers
      </li>
      <li>
       Field or senior officers
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company-grade_officer" title="Company-grade officer">
        Company grade
       </a>
       or junior officers
      </li>
      <li>
       Subordinate officer (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_cadet" title="Naval cadet">
        naval cadet
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Officer_cadet" title="Officer cadet">
        officer cadet
       </a>
       in the Canadian Forces, for example)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="General.2C_flag.2C_and_air_officers">
       General, flag, and air officers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: General, flag, and air officers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Officers who typically command units or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_(military)" title="Formation (military)">
       formations
      </a>
      that are expected to operate independently for extended periods of time (i.e.,
      <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">
       brigades
      </a>
      and larger, or
      <a href="/wiki/Flotilla" title="Flotilla">
       flotillas
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(naval)" title="Squadron (naval)">
       squadrons
      </a>
      of ships), are referred to variously as
      <a href="/wiki/General_officer" title="General officer">
       general officers
      </a>
      (in armies, marines, and some air forces),
      <a href="/wiki/Flag_officer" title="Flag officer">
       flag officers
      </a>
      (in navies and coast guards), or
      <a href="/wiki/Air_officer" title="Air officer">
       air officers
      </a>
      (in some
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth
      </a>
      air forces).
     </p>
     <p>
      General-officer ranks typically include (from the most senior)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General" title="General">
       general
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general" title="Lieutenant general">
       lieutenant general
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">
       major general
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general" title="Brigadier general">
       brigadier general
      </a>
      , although there are many variations like division general or (air-, ground-) force general.
     </p>
     <p>
      Flag-officer ranks, named after the traditional practice of showing the presence of such an officer with a flag on a ship and often land, typically include (from the most senior)
      <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">
       admiral
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vice_admiral" title="Vice admiral">
       vice admiral
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral" title="Rear admiral">
       rear admiral
      </a>
      . In some navies, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces_Maritime_Command" title="Canadian Forces Maritime Command">
       Canada's
      </a>
      , the rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Commodore_(rank)" title="Commodore (rank)">
       commodore
      </a>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_rank" title="Flag rank">
       flag rank
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      and most other
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth
      </a>
      air forces,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_officer" title="Air officer">
       air-officer
      </a>
      ranks usually include
      <a href="/wiki/Air_chief_marshal" title="Air chief marshal">
       air chief marshal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_marshal" title="Air marshal">
       air marshal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_vice-marshal" title="Air vice-marshal">
       air vice-marshal
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Air_commodore" title="Air commodore">
       air commodore
      </a>
      . For some air forces, however, such as those of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCAF" title="RCAF">
       Canada
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">
       United States
      </a>
      and many other air forces, general officer rank titles are used. Brazil and Argentina use a
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier#Officer_rank_in_Latin_America" title="Brigadier">
       system of general officer ranks based on the term brigadier
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In some forces, there may be one or more superior ranks to the common examples, above, that are given distinguishing titles, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Marshal" title="Field Marshal">
       Field Marshal
      </a>
      (most armies of the world, notably excluding the United States) or General of the Army (mainly the United States because "Marshal" is used as a peace officer's designation),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Fleet" title="Admiral of the Fleet">
       Admiral of the Fleet
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">
       U.S. Navy
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_the_Royal_Air_Force" title="Marshal of the Royal Air Force">
       Marshal of the Royal Air Force
      </a>
      , or other national air force. These ranks have often been discontinued, such as in
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      , or limited to wartime and/or honorific promotion, such as in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In various countries, particularly the United States, these may be referred to as "star ranks" for the number of stars worn on some rank insignia: typically one-star for brigadier general or equivalent with the addition of a star for each subsequent rank. In the United States, five stars has been the highest rank regularly attainable (excluding the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps_rank_insignia" title="United States Marine Corps rank insignia">
       Marines
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_Guard" title="U.S. Coast Guard">
       Coast Guard
      </a>
      , which have traditionally served as branches of the Navy in times of war and thus under the command of an Admiral of the Fleet). There also exists the specialty ranks of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_of_the_Armies_of_the_United_States" title="General of the Armies of the United States">
       General of the Armies of the United States
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Navy" title="Admiral of the Navy">
       Admiral of the Navy
      </a>
      which at their inception were considered senior four star officers but came to be considered
      <a href="/wiki/Six-star_rank" title="Six-star rank">
       six-star rank
      </a>
      after the creation of five star officers. To date only one other officer has held a six star rank in their lifetime,
      <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">
       John J. Pershing
      </a>
      while
      <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">
       George Washington
      </a>
      was posthumously promoted to the post in 1976. Additionally,
      <a href="/wiki/George_Dewey" title="George Dewey">
       Admiral George Dewey
      </a>
      was promoted to Admiral of the Navy but died well before statute made it senior to an Admiral of the Fleet upon the latter's inception.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some titles are not genuine ranks, but either functions assumed by generals or honorific titles. For instance, in the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">
       French Army
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9n%C3%A9ral_de_corps_d%27arm%C3%A9e" title="Gnral de corps d'arme">
        gnral de corps d'arme
       </a>
      </i>
      is a function assumed by some
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General" title="Major General">
        gnraux de division
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" title="Marshal of France">
        marchal de France
       </a>
      </i>
      , which is a distinction denoting the most superior military office, but one that has often neutered the practical command powers of those on whom it is conferred. In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commodore_(USN)" title="Commodore (USN)">
       commodore
      </a>
      currently is a senior
      <a href="/wiki/Captain_(naval)" title="Captain (naval)">
       captain
      </a>
      commanding a
      <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(naval)" title="Squadron (naval)">
       squadron
      </a>
      that is too small for a
      <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral" title="Rear admiral">
       rear admiral
      </a>
      to command, although that name has historically been used as a rank. The title (not rank) of commodore can also indicate an officer who is senior to a ship's captain (since only the ship's commander is addressed as captain while underway). Marine captains are referred to as major to distinguish themselves while shipboard.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Field_or_senior_officers">
       Field or senior officers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Field or senior officers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Field_officer" title="Field officer">
       Field officers
      </a>
      , also called "field-grade officers" or "senior officers", are officers who typically command units that can be expected to operate independently for short periods of time (i.e., infantry battalions, cavalry or artillery regiments, warships,
      <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(aviation)" title="Squadron (aviation)">
       air squadrons
      </a>
      ). Field officers also commonly fill staff positions of superior commands.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term "field(-grade) officer" is primarily used by armies and marines; air forces, navies and coast guards generally prefer the term "senior officer." The two terms are not necessarily synonymous because the former is frequently used to describe any officer who holds a command position from a platoon to a
      <a href="/wiki/Theater_(warfare)" title="Theater (warfare)">
       Theater (warfare)
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Typical army and marine field officer ranks include
      <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">
       colonel
      </a>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'ir' in 'bird'">
           r
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="/l/ 'le' in 'bottle'">
           l
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel" title="Lieutenant colonel">
       lieutenant colonel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">
       major
      </a>
      and, in the British army, captains holding an
      <a href="/wiki/Adjutant" title="Adjutant">
       adjutant
      </a>
      's appointment. In many Commonwealth countries the field rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier" title="Brigadier">
       brigadier
      </a>
      is used, although it fills the position held by brigadier general in other countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      Naval and coast guard senior officer ranks include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(Navy)" title="Captain (Navy)">
       captain
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Commander" title="Commander">
       commander
      </a>
      . In some countries, the more senior rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Commodore_(rank)" title="Commodore (rank)">
       commodore
      </a>
      is also included. In others
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant-commander" title="Lieutenant-commander">
       lieutenant-commanders
      </a>
      , as equivalents to army and marine majors, are considered senior officers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Commonwealth air force senior officer ranks include
      <a href="/wiki/Group_captain" title="Group captain">
       group captain
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wing_Commander_(rank)" title="Wing Commander (rank)">
       wing commander
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Squadron_leader" title="Squadron leader">
       squadron leader
      </a>
      , where such ranks are still used.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Company_grade_or_junior_officers">
       Company grade or junior officers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Company grade or junior officers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The ranks of junior officers are the three or four lowest ranks of officers. Units under their command are generally not expected to operate independently for any significant length of time. Company grade officers also fill staff roles in some units. In some militaries, however, a captain may act as the permanent commanding officer of an independent company-sized army unit, for example a signal or field engineer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squadron_(cavalry)" title="Squadron (cavalry)">
       squadron
      </a>
      , or a field artillery battery.
     </p>
     <p>
      Typical army company officer ranks include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(land)" title="Captain (land)">
       captain
      </a>
      and various grades of
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">
       lieutenant
      </a>
      . Typical naval and coast guard junior officer ranks include grades of
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_commander" title="Lieutenant commander">
       lieutenant commander
      </a>
      , lieutenant,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_junior_grade" title="Lieutenant junior grade">
       lieutenant junior grade
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sub-lieutenant" title="Sub-lieutenant">
       sub-lieutenant
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">
       ensign
      </a>
      . Commonwealth (excluding Canada) air force junior officer ranks usually include
      <a href="/wiki/Flight_lieutenant" title="Flight lieutenant">
       flight lieutenant
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Flying_officer" title="Flying officer">
       flying officer
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Pilot_officer" title="Pilot officer">
       pilot officer
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      "The [U.S.] commissioned officer corps is divided into 10 pay grades (O-1 through O-10). Officers in pay grades O-1 through O-3 are considered company grade officers. In the Army, Marine Corps, and Air Force, these pay grades correspond to the ranks of second lieutenant (O-1), first lieutenant (O-2), and captain (O-3), and in the Navy, ensign, lieutenant junior grade, and lieutenant. Officers in the next three pay grades (O-4 through O-6) are considered field grade officers. In the Army, Marine Corps, and Air Force, these pay grades correspond to the ranks of major (O-4), lieutenant colonel (O-5), and colonel (O-6), and in the Navy, lieutenant commander, commander, and captain. The highest four pay grades are reserved for general officers in the Army, Marine Corps, and Air Force, and flag officers in the Navy. The ranks associated with each pay grade are as follows: in the Army, Marine Corps, and Air Force, brigadier general (O-7), major general (O-8), lieutenant general (O-9), and general (O-10); in the Navy, rear admiral-lower half, rear admiral-upper half, vice admiral, and admiral."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Subordinate.2Fstudent_officer">
       Subordinate/student officer
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Subordinate/student officer">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Officers in training in the Canadian Armed Forces are either
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_cadet" title="Naval cadet">
       naval cadet
      </a>
      for naval training or
      <a href="/wiki/Officer_cadet" title="Officer cadet">
       officer cadet
      </a>
      for army or air force training.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the US and several other western forces, officers in training are referred to as student officers, and carry the rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Cadet" title="Cadet">
       cadet
      </a>
      (army and air force) or
      <a href="/wiki/Midshipman" title="Midshipman">
       midshipman
      </a>
      (navy, and in some countries, marines). These officers may be serving at a
      <a href="/wiki/Military_academy" title="Military academy">
       military academy
      </a>
      , or, as common in the United States, as members of a military training unit attached to a civilian college or university, such as an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROTC" title="ROTC">
       ROTC
      </a>
      unit. This is due to a requirement that commissioned officers have at least a four-year degree.
     </p>
     <p>
      The British Army refers to its trainee officers as officer cadets, who rank as private soldiers at the start of their training, with no authority over other ranks (except when appointed to carry out a role as part of training). Officer cadets are addressed to as "Mister" or "Miss" until the completion of the early stages of their training at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College_Sandhurst" title="Royal Military College Sandhurst">
       Royal Military College Sandhurst
      </a>
      (at which point cadets "pass out" and formally gain their commissions), thereafter other ranks (non-officers) will address them as "Sir" or "Ma'am".
     </p>
     <p>
      While cadet has always been a rank of limited authority and prestige (cadets and US Navy midshipmen have no authority over commissioned personnel, warrants, or officers, only subordinate cadets), midshipman has historically been a rank with limited leadership responsibility, particularly in the Royal Navy (where cadets are commissioned at the start of their training, unlike their Army counterparts). This tradition was continued by the US Navy after its original adoption of the rank, but now US Navy midshipmen are limited in the same manner as cadets in the other US services. Additionally, US Marine officers in training are also midshipmen, trained and educated alongside their naval counterparts, and wear distinctive insignia to indicate their branch of service.
     </p>
     <p>
      Note:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Coast_Guard_Academy" title="US Coast Guard Academy">
       US Coast Guard Academy
      </a>
      students are referred to as cadets, while those attending the military branch's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_candidate_school" title="Officer candidate school">
       officer candidate school
      </a>
      are officer candidates.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the US an alternative to spending four years as a cadet or midshipmen is for college graduates with a four-year degree to attend officer candidate school, an intensive twelve week training course designed to convert college graduates into military officers. Each service has at least one, and usually several, officer candidate school facilities. Students at these programs are called
      <a href="/wiki/Officer_candidate" title="Officer candidate">
       officer candidates
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Warrant_officers">
       Warrant officers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Warrant officers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Warrant_officer" title="Warrant officer">
       Warrant officers
      </a>
      (as receiving authority by virtue of a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Warrant_(law)" title="Warrant (law)">
        warrant
       </a>
      </i>
      ) are a hybrid rank treated slightly differently in each country and/or service. Warrant officers may either be effectively senior
      <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">
       non-commissioned officers
      </a>
      or an entirely separate grade between commissioned and non-commissioned officers, usually held by specialist personnel.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States,
      <a href="/wiki/Warrant_officer_(United_States)" title="Warrant officer (United States)">
       warrant officers
      </a>
      are appointed by warrant then commissioned by the President of the United States at the rank of chief warrant officer. Warrant officers range from W01-CW5. A warrant officer is not a chief warrant officer until they reach W2.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Enlisted_personnel">
       Enlisted personnel
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Enlisted personnel">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Enlisted personnel are personnel below commissioned rank and make up the vast majority of military personnel. They are known by different names in different countries, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Other_ranks_(UK)" title="Other ranks (UK)">
       other ranks
      </a>
      (ORs) in the United Kingdom and some Commonwealth countries, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_members" title="Non-commissioned members">
       non-commissioned members
      </a>
      (NCMs) in Canada.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-commissioned_officers">
       Non-commissioned officers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Non-commissioned officers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">
       Non-commissioned officers
      </a>
      (NCOs) are enlisted personnel, under the command of an officer, granted delegated authority to supervise other military members or assigned significant administrative responsibilities. They are responsible for the care and direct control of junior military members, often functioning in the smaller field units as
      <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">
       executive officers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Even the most senior NCO officially ranks beneath the most junior commissioned officer or warrant officer. However, most senior NCOs have more experience, possibly including combat, than junior officers. In many armies, because junior officers have a great amount of responsibility and authority but little operational experience, they are paired with senior NCO advisers. In some organizations, senior NCOs may have formal responsibility and informal respect beyond that of junior officers, but less than that of warrant officers. Many warrant officers come from the ranks of mid-career NCOs. In some countries warrant ranks replace senior enlisted ranks.
     </p>
     <p>
      NCO ranks typically include a varying number of grades of
      <a href="/wiki/Sergeant" title="Sergeant">
       sergeant
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">
       corporal
      </a>
      (air force, army and marines), or
      <a href="/wiki/Chief_petty_officer" title="Chief petty officer">
       chief petty officer
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Petty_officer" title="Petty officer">
       petty officer
      </a>
      (navy and coast guard). In many navies the term
      <i>
       rate
      </i>
      is used to designate specialty, while
      <i>
       rank
      </i>
      denotes paygrade.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some countries warrant officers come under the non-commissioned officer branch (senior non-commissioned officiers).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_enlisted_ranks">
       Other enlisted ranks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other enlisted ranks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Personnel with no command authority usually bear titles such as
      <a href="/wiki/Private_(rank)" title="Private (rank)">
       private
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Airman" title="Airman">
       airman
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraftman" title="Aircraftman">
       aircraftman
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Seaman" title="Seaman">
       seaman
      </a>
      (starting with
      <a href="/wiki/Seaman_recruit" title="Seaman recruit">
       seaman recruit
      </a>
      in the United States Navy and Coast Guard). In the United States Marine Corps individuals of all ranks regardless of command status may be referred to as "Marine". In the United States Air Force individuals of all ranks regardless of command status may be referred to as "airman". Shortly after the Sailor's Creed was formally instituted, Secretary of the Navy John Dalton directed that the word "Sailor" should be capitalized when referring to any uniformed member of the Navy. In some countries and services, personnel in different branches have different titles. These may have a variety of grades, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Private_first_class" title="Private first class">
       private first class
      </a>
      , but these usually only reflect variations in pay, not increased authority. These may or may not technically be ranks, depending on the country and/or service. Each rank gives the individual an indication of how long and how well they have served in combat and training.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment">
       Appointment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Appointment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Appointment refers to the instrument by virtue of which the person exercises his or her authority. Officers are appointed by a royal
      <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">
       commission
      </a>
      in most monarchies or a presidential commission in many other countries. In the Commonwealth, warrant officers hold a royal or presidential warrant. In the United States, officers are appointed by the president, with the advice and consent of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">
       United States Senate
      </a>
      . Most officers are approved
      <i>
       en bloc
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Voice_vote" title="Voice vote">
       voice vote
      </a>
      , but flag officers are usually required to appear before the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Armed_Services" title="United States Senate Committee on Armed Services">
       Armed Services Committee
      </a>
      and answer questions to the satisfaction of its members, prior to a vote on their commission.
     </p>
     <p>
      NCOs are appointed by an instrument of appointment, a written document, often a certificate, usually from the service head. Entry into service is often referred to as enlistment throughout the English-speaking world, even in countries where soldiers do not technically enlist.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometimes personnel serve in an appointment which is higher than their actual rank. For instance,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commodore_(RN)" title="Commodore (RN)">
       commodore
      </a>
      used to be an appointment of
      <a href="/wiki/Captain_(Royal_Navy)" title="Captain (Royal Navy)">
       captain
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
       Royal Navy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lance_corporal" title="Lance corporal">
       lance corporal
      </a>
      used to be an appointment of private in the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_rank">
       Types of rank
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Types of rank">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are a number of different forms of rank; from highest to lowest degree, they are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Substantive
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        permanent
       </i>
       : the fully paid and confirmed rank.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Retired
       </i>
       : usually granted to those officers of the rank of
       <i>
        lieutenant
       </i>
       in the Navy, or
       <i>
        captain
       </i>
       in the Army, or above, who have reached the end of their service obligation and have not been
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dishonorably_discharged" title="Dishonorably discharged">
        dishonorably discharged
       </a>
       . A retired rank is usually kept for life, if the officer so concerned wishes. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
        Commonwealth of Nations
       </a>
       , such an officer will also hold the style of
       <a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">
        Esquire
       </a>
       , if they do not hold a higher title.
       <i>
        Veterans
       </i>
       rank is different in each country. Members of the United States military maintain their highest rank after discharge. 10 U.S. Code  772(e) states: A person not on active duty who served honorably in time of war in the Army, Navy, Air Force, or Marine Corps may bear the title and wear the uniform of the highest grade held by him during that war. After a war, regular serving members of the military holding war substantive or temporary rank often revert to their former, substantive rank and all others often end their service. However, the holder may be granted permission to permanently
       <i>
        retain
       </i>
       the rank they held when the conflict ended.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Temporary
       </i>
       : usually granted for a specific task or mission. The holder holds the rank while occupying that position. Despite the name, temporary rank may be held for a considerable period of time, perhaps even years. In wartime, temporary ranks are often common. In the United Kingdom, the rank of brigadier was long considered a temporary rank; while its holder was addressed as "brigadier," he would retain the substantive rank of colonel or lieutenant-colonel if not selected for promotion to general officer rank. Sub-classes of temporary rank include:
       <ul>
        <li>
         <i>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brevet_rank" title="Brevet rank">
           Brevet
          </a>
         </i>
         : an honorary promoted rank, without the full official authority or pay appropriate to the rank.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          War substantive
         </i>
         or
         <i>
          theater
         </i>
         : a temporarily confirmed rank only held for the duration of that war.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Acting_(rank)" title="Acting (rank)">
         Acting
        </a>
       </i>
       is where the holder assumes the pay and allowances appropriate to the acting rank, but a higher commanding officer may revert the holder to previous rank held. This is normally for a short period of time while the permanent occupant of the office is absent.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Local
       </i>
       : a form of temporary rank restricted to a specific location instead of a specific duty.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Honorary
       </i>
       : Often granted on retirement, or in certain special cases to honour a deserving civilian. Generally, honorary rank is treated as if it were substantive, but usually does not grant a corresponding wage or pension (increase).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Size_of_command">
       Size of command
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Size of command">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rank_and_unit_size">
       Rank and unit size
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Rank and unit size">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Military_organization" title="Military organization">
       Military organization
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      To get a sense of the practical meaning of these ranksand thus to be able to compare them across the different armed services, different nations, and the variations of titles and insigniaan understanding of the relative levels and sizes of each command is helpful. The ranking and command system used by
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">
       U.S. Marine
      </a>
      ground forces or
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
       U.S. Army
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">
       infantry
      </a>
      units can serve as a template for this purpose. It should be remembered that different countries will often use their own systems that do not match the presentation here.
     </p>
     <p>
      Under this system, starting from the bottom and working up, a corporal leads a
      <a href="/wiki/Fireteam" title="Fireteam">
       fireteam
      </a>
      consisting of three other individuals. A sergeant leads a
      <a href="/wiki/Squad" title="Squad">
       squad
      </a>
      consisting of three fireteams. As a result, a full squad numbers 13 individuals. Squads usually have numbered designations (e.g., 1st Squad).
     </p>
     <p>
      Generally, in most armies and marine units, a lieutenant or equivalent rank leads a
      <a href="/wiki/Platoon" title="Platoon">
       platoon
      </a>
      , which can consist of three or four squads. For example, in U.S. Marine
      <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">
       infantry
      </a>
      units, rifle platoons usually consist of three rifle squads of 13 men each, with a Navy
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corpsman" title="Corpsman">
       corpsman
      </a>
      , the platoon leader, and a platoon sergeant (i.e., a
      <a href="/wiki/Staff_sergeant" title="Staff sergeant">
       staff sergeant
      </a>
      who serves as second-in-command). An infantry platoon can number from 42 to 55 individuals, depending on the service. Platoons are usually numbered (e.g., 1st Platoon) or named after their primary function (e.g., service platoon).
     </p>
     <p>
      A captain or equivalent rank commands a
      <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">
       company
      </a>
      , usually consisting of four platoons (three line platoons and one heavy weapons platoon). His headquarters can include a
      <a href="/wiki/First_sergeant" title="First sergeant">
       first sergeant
      </a>
      and as many as seven others. As such, a company can comprise from roughly 175 to 225 individuals. Equivalent units also commanded by captains are batteries (for field artillery units) and detachments. In English speaking countries, a company (or troop in the cavalry, and battery in the artillery) is usually designated by a letter (e.g., "A Company"). In non-English speaking countries, they are usually numbered. In most Commonwealth armies a company is commanded by a major, assisted by a captain.
     </p>
     <p>
      A lieutenant colonel or equivalent rank commands a
      <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">
       battalion
      </a>
      or a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squadron_(cavalry)" title="Squadron (cavalry)">
       squadron
      </a>
      , often consisting of four companies, plus the various members of his headquarters. A battalion is around 5001,500 men and usually consists of between two and six companies.
     </p>
     <p>
      A colonel or equivalent commands a
      <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">
       regiment
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_(air_force)" title="Group (air force)">
       group
      </a>
      , often consisting of four battalions (for an Infantry unit) or five to six air groups (for a wing). Battalions and regiments are usually numbered, either as a separate battalion or as part of a regimental structure (e.g., 1-501st Infantry in the US Army).
     </p>
     <p>
      In these latter, abstractions cease to be helpful and it becomes necessary to turn to an actual unit. The
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion_1st_Marines" title="1st Battalion 1st Marines">
       1st Battalion
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="1st Marine Regiment (United States)">
       1st Marine Regiment
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Marine Division (United States)">
       1st Marine Division
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/I_Marine_Expeditionary_Force" title="I Marine Expeditionary Force">
       I Marine Expeditionary Force
      </a>
      consists of three infantry companies, one weapons company, and one headquarters and service company. Above that, the 1st Marine Regiment (also known as "First Marines") consists of four such battalions and one headquarters company.
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_Air_Control_Group_18" title="Marine Air Control Group 18">
       Marine Air Control Group 18
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Air_Wing" title="1st Marine Air Wing">
       1st Marine Air Wing
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/III_Marine_Expeditionary_Force" title="III Marine Expeditionary Force">
       III Marine Expeditionary Force
      </a>
      consists of four squadrons, one battery, and one detachment, a mix of different-sized units under a regimental equivalent-sized unit.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next level has traditionally been a
      <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">
       brigade
      </a>
      , commanded by a brigadier general, and containing two or more regiments. But this structure is considered obsolete today. At the present time, in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
       U.S. Army
      </a>
      , a brigade is roughly equal to or a little larger than a regiment, consisting of three to seven battalions. Strength typically ranges from 1,500 to 3,500 personnel. In the U.S. Marines, brigades are only formed for certain missions. In size and nature they are larger and more varied collections of battalions than is common for a regiment, fitting them for their traditional role as the smallest formation able to operate independently on a battlefield without external logistical tactical support. Brigades are usually numbered (e.g., 2nd Brigade).
     </p>
     <p>
      The level above regiment and brigade is the
      <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">
       division
      </a>
      , commanded by a major general and consisting of from 10,000 to 20,000 persons. The 1st Marine Division, for example, is made up of four marine regiments (of the type described above), one assault amphibian battalion, one reconnaissance battalion, two light armored reconnaissance battalions, one combat engineer battalion, one tank battalion, and one headquarters battaliontotalling more than 19,000 marines. (Within the headquarters battalion are one headquarters company, one service company, one
      <a href="/wiki/Military_police" title="Military police">
       military police
      </a>
      company, one communications company, and one truck company.) An equivalent elsewhere within the same marine expeditionary force (MEF) might be a MEF logistics group (MLG) - which is not a regimental-sized unit (as the word "group" implies), but rather a large support unit consisting of several battalions of support personnel. Divisions are normally numbered, but can be named after a function or personage.
     </p>
     <p>
      Considering such a variety of units, the command sizes for any given rank will vary widely. Not all units are as troop intensive as infantry forces need to be.
      <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">
       Tank
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">
       artillery
      </a>
      crews, for example, involve far fewer personnel. Numbers also differ for non-combat units such as
      <a href="/wiki/Quartermaster" title="Quartermaster">
       quartermasters
      </a>
      , cooks, and hospital staff. Beyond this, in any real situation, not all units will be at full strength and there will be various attachments and detachments of assorted specialists woven throughout the system.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 1st Marine Division is part of the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.globalsecurity.org/military/agency/usmc/i-mef.htm" rel="nofollow">
       I Marine Expeditionary Force
      </a>
      , which also includes the 3rd Marine Air Wing, 1st Marine Logistics Group, 1st Marine Expeditionary Brigade (as required), three marine expeditionary units (featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">
       helicopter
      </a>
      groups), and a battalion-sized marine air ground task force. In the U.S. Marine Corps there are three marine expeditionary forces.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the U.S. Army, the level above division is called a
      <a href="/wiki/Corps" title="Corps">
       corps
      </a>
      instead of an expeditionary force. It is commanded by a lieutenant general. In many armies, a corps numbers around 60,000, usually divided into three divisions. Corps (and similar organizations) are normally designated with Roman numerals and their nationality when operating in a combined (international) force, e.g. V (US) Corps, VIII (ROK) Corps, II MEF, I Canadian Corps.
     </p>
     <p>
      During World War II, due to the large scale of combat, multiple corps were combined into
      <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">
       armies
      </a>
      commanded in theory by a general (four stars), but often by a lieutenant general (three stars), and comprising as many as 240,000 troops. Armies are numbered by spelled-out numerals or functional titles, using their nationality in "combined" forces (e.g., Eighth US Army, Third ROK Army, British Army of the Rhine). These were in their turn formed into
      <a href="/wiki/Army_group" title="Army group">
       army groups
      </a>
      , these being the largest field organization handled by a single commander in modern warfare. Army groups included between 400,000 and 1,500,000 troops. Army groups received Arabic numeral designations and national designations when combined.
     </p>
     <p>
      These examples illustrate a standard that holds true all over the world and throughout military history, namely that higher rank generally implies command of larger units in a nested system of ranks and commands. The specific size of a command for any given rank will, however, depend on the task the unit performs, the nature of weapons used, and the strategies of warfare.
     </p>
