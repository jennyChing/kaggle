        <h1 class="txt-detail-header">
         Op-ed: Marvels Big Bad is becoming a big problem
        </h1>
        <div class="author">
         by Matt Singer
        </div>
        <div class="pic scroll-track" id="header-images">
         <img alt="Op-ed: Marvels Big Bad is becoming a big problem" src="//cdn3.thedissolve.com/articles/2895/detail.178696aa.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="scroll-track" id="copy">
         <div class="article-content">
          <p class="p1">
           <em>
            The following post contains SPOILERS for
           </em>
           The Avengers
           <em>
            and
           </em>
           Guardians Of The Galaxy
           <em>
            .
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            Finally, after years of build-up, here he is: Thanos. The Mad Titan. The Avatar Of Death. The baddest dude in the universe. Introduced in a post-credits scene in
            <i>
             The Avengers
            </i>
            , his arrival has been teased, directly or indirectly, ever since. In
            <i>
             Guardians Of The Galaxy
            </i>
            he appears at last in the purple flesh. And he does absolutely nothing. He sits on a throne in space in a vaguely ridiculous outfit. He gives his henchman a few orders. And then he vanishes from the movie completely. I guess he had more important things to do.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            Hell be back, though. The entire Marvel Cinematic Universe is building to a confrontation with Thanos. He is searching for these mythic objects of immense power, the Infinity Stones, and eventually (probably around the time of
            <i>
             Avengers 3
            </i>
            ) hell become too powerful to ignore. But thats several movies and years away. In the meantime, we are stuck with these teases, which are becoming so tiresome theyre beginningto undermine Thanos credibility as a legitimate threat before he even gets going. The guy hasnt done anything yet, and Im already kind of sick of him.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            One big reason why was highlighted in a piece yesterday at
            <a href="http://screencrush.com/thanos-infinty-stone/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             <span class="s2">
              ScreenCrush
             </span>
            </a>
            by Mike Ryan, who observed the same thing I did while watching
            <em>
             Guardians Of The Galaxy
            </em>
            : Thanos is not very good at his job. He writes:
           </span>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p class="p2">
            <i style="font-size: 16px; line-height: 24px;">
             So far, we have been introduced to three (maybe four, depending on who you talk to) Infinity Stones and one is safely on Asgard, one is safely on Xandar, one is with the Collector, and another (maybe) is with Baron von Strucker. After ten movies, Thanos only has his space chair and a voice that sounds a lot like Brandon from
            </i>
            <span style="font-size: 16px; line-height: 24px;">
             The Goonies
            </span>
            <i style="font-size: 16px; line-height: 24px;">
             .
            </i>
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            Ryan is exaggerating slightlyThanos didnt appear onscreen until Marvel movie No. 6,
            <i>
             The Avengers
            </i>
            , and the first Infinity Stone didnt show up until movie #No. 5,
            <i>
             Captain America
            </i>
            but his point is still a good one. Thanos is the supposedly king of the supervillains. He has immensely powerful lackies like Loki,an actual god, and Ronan The Accuser, who canwipe out entire planets. He commands armies of warring aliens and giant flying space slugs. But so far, hes been completely ineffectual. Isnt the title of supervillain supposed to denote someone is
            <em>
             super
            </em>
            -effective at being a villain? Ive met gassy toddlers who wreaked havoc more efficientlythan Thanos.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            The characters defenders say that his success rate is not what matters, because hes supposed to fillthe same role as the Emperor in the original
            <i>
             Star Wars
            </i>
            trilogy: a vaguely defined presence who gives marching orders to mid-level baddies like Darth Vader. But think of how much the Emperor accomplished (through Vader) in just his first film,
            <i>
             The Empire Strikes Back
            </i>
            . He destroyed the Rebel base on the ice planet Hoth, lopped off Lukes hand, and encased Han Solo in carbonite. Thats a damn good days work for a bad guy. The heroes arent entirely defeated, but theyre certainly left reeling.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            What, in comparison, has Thanos done so far? True, the Battle Of Manhattan in
            <i>
             The Avengers
            </i>
            cost some human lives. But it brought the Avengers together as a team, and the organization suffered zero significant casualties. (Agent Coulson, one of their support staff, died, but only temporarily.)He did the same for the Guardians Of The Galaxy, uniting five selfish misfits into a band of altruistic do-gooders. (And, again, one character died, but is already on the road to recovery before the end credits roll.)
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            In a sense, Thanos has accomplished more good deedshelping heroes overcome their differences and learning to work together as a teamthan bad ones. His master plan better explain why he wanted to create all these groups to stand against him, otherwise hes officially a dope. At this point, a better comparison for Thanos than Emperor Palpatine is Dr. Claw, the shadowy puppet master from the
            <i>
             Inspector Gadget
            </i>
            cartoon, who spent the entire series ordering people around while he sat in a chair, and was repeatedly outsmarted by a little girl and her dog. Thanos even kind of sounds like Dr. Claw.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            Thanos incompetence wouldnt be so problematic if every Marvel villain besidesLoki wasnt a complete and total dud. From Iron Monger to Abomination to Whiplash to Ronan The Accuser to the guy from
            <i>
             Thor: The Dark World
            </i>
            whose name I couldnt even remember until I looked it up on Wikipedia just now (its Malekith), theyre all interchangeably generic evildoers with interchangeably generic evil plans. The glimpses of Thanos are used to paper over their blandness with the promise of excitement down the road. These bad guys might be bad, they insist, but just hang in there;this
            <em>
            </em>
            purple guy will be
            <em>
             really
            </em>
            nasty.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            Much of Marvels remarkable onscreen success can be credited to the ingenious way the companys applied its business model from the world of comicsendless serialized storytelling yoked to densely structured continuity that hooks readers and never lets them goto the world of films. But its underwhelming rollout of Thanos has exposed one of the first serious flaws in that plan. In comics, a guy like Thanos might be teased in the background for three, or four,or even six issuesbut thats only a six-month wait for the big reveal, long enough to whetreadersappetite, but not so long that they get tired of waiting. Comic-book movies come out so infrequently, even in todays marketplace, that while the build-up to Thanos scheme might only take place across six individual films, those six films might take six
            <i>
             years
            </i>
            to show up in theaters. (Or more; Marvel hasnt announced a release date for
            <i>
             Avengers 3
            </i>
            yet, but Id predict it slides into the May 3, 2019 slot the company has claimed, which would make it almost seven years to the day between Thanos set-up and payoff.)
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s1">
            Thats an enormous investment in timemore than half the number of years chronicled in Richard Linklaters
            <i>
             Boyhood
            </i>
            just for a purple dude on a throne. The extreme wait diffuses the sense of enthuisasm around the character, and it only serves to make Thanos look even sillier than he already does. (Thanos is coming
           </span>
           
           <span class="s1">
            in approximately three to four years! So get ready
           </span>
           eventually!
           <span class="s1">
            )It might not be so bad if Thanos
            <i>
            </i>
            was the main antagonist in
            <em>
             Avengers 2
            </em>
            , but hes not;thats Ultron. You can be sure, though, that Thanos will pop up somewhere in
            <em>
             Age Of Ultron
            </em>
            , hinting at the heinous acts hell try (and fail) to complete. And then maybe hell fly off into space, howlingIll get you next timeAvengers! Next tiiiiime...
           </span>
          </p>
         </div>
