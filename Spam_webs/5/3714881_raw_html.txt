      <div class="a-module" itemid="http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.2118410!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_635/mlb-drug-investigation.jpg" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
       <figure class="a-image h">
        <img alt="Anthony Bosch (r.) turns himself in with his attorney Susy Ribero-Ayala, Tuesday at federal court in Miami." src="http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.2118410.1424192961!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_635/mlb-drug-investigation.jpg"/>

         Alan Diaz/AP
        </span>
        <h2 class="a-caption" itemprop="description">
         Anthony Bosch (r.) turns himself in with his attorney Susy Ribero-Ayala, Tuesday at federal court in Miami.
        </h2>
        <a class="a-enlarge" href="#">
        </a>
       </figure>
      </div>
      <p>
       Minutes before Alex Rodriguezs steroid dealer was sentenced to four years in federal prison, Anthony Bosch huddled with more than a dozen family members and friends in a courthouse alcove, tearfully reciting the Lords Prayer.
      </p>
      <p>
       Then Bosch, the man behind the notorious Biogenesis doping ring, walked into the courtroom of U.S. District Court Judge Darrin Gayles and stood to receive the terms of his sentence  48 months, followed by three years of supervised release.
      </p>
      <p>
       My addiction took the best of me, Bosch told Judge Gayles, while family members sobbed in the courtroom gallery. I cant put into words how sorry I am.
      </p>
      <p>
       Judge Gayles gave Bosch until 11:15 a.m. to surrender to the U.S. Marshals at the courthouse.
      </p>
      <div class="a-module">
       <div class="a-video">
        <script async="" id="_nw2e-js" src="http://launch.newsinc.com/js/embed.js" type="text/javascript">
        </script>
        <div class="ndn_embed" data-config-site-section="nydailynews-sports" data-config-tracking-group="90051" data-config-type="VideoPlayer/Single" data-config-video-id="28583162" data-config-widget-id="2">
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite pleas for leniency from family members, Gayles gave Bosch three months less than the maximum sentence of 51 months allowed in the federal guidelines for Boschs record of steroid distribution. The sentence could potentially be reduced later this year if Bosch testifies against Bosch co-conspirators Yuri Sucart and ex-Miami pitching coach Lazer Collazo, who both pleaded not guilty and are scheduled to face trial in April.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sucart is Rodriguezs cousin, and was implicated previously by Rodriguez as having provided him steroids a decade ago. Rodriguez could also be called to testify in those cases.
      </p>
      <p>
       The gallery was crowded with spectators, supporters of Bosch and members of the media. Among the faces was Porter Fisher, the former Biogenesis employee whose decision to swipe several boxes of medical records from the facility helped turn the case into a national story (the records were allegedly stolen from Fishers car outside a tanning salon, and later appeared on the black market). Fisher declined to be interviewed by the Daily News.
      </p>
      <div class="a-module" itemid="http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.2118256!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_635/yankees-white-sox.jpg" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
       <figure class="a-image h">
        <img alt="NYC PAPERS OUT. Social media use restricted to low res file max 184 x 128 pixels and 72 dpi" src="http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.2118256.1424192962!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_635/yankees-white-sox.jpg"/>
        <a class="a-credit" href="/photos/dn-photographers/photographer?author=Corey-Sipkin" itemprop="copyrightHolder">
         Corey Sipkin/New York Daily News
        </a>
        <h2 class="a-caption" itemprop="description">
         Alex Rodriguez was one of a dozen baseball players Anthony Bosch supplied performance-enhancing drugs to.
        </h2>
        <a class="a-enlarge" href="#">
        </a>
       </figure>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Collazos lawyer, Frank Quintero, sent his partner to the sentencing, which Quintero described as within the guidelines. Judge Gayles made a decision and he knows the case better than anybody.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also present was DLA Piper attorney Charlie Scheeler, who helped former Senator George Mitchell compile the ground-breaking 2007 report on doping in baseball.
      </p>
      <p>
       The sentence was longer than that suggested by even the prosecutor, Pat Sullivan, who said 41 months was appropriate given Boschs cooperation with the governments probe, which began nearly three years ago.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bosch supplied A-Rod and more than a dozen other pro ballplayers with steroids, human growth hormone and other performance-enhancing drugs. Even before Boschs arrest by federal agents in August of 2014, his testimony about A-Rod and other clients put him at the center of one of the most explosive doping cases in Major League Baseball history.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bosch cooperated in the MLB investigation that resulted in the suspension of 14 players, including Rodriguez. He has also agreed to cooperate with the United States Anti-Doping Agency.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bosch pleaded guilty in October to conspiracy to distribute testosterone to athletes from the now-closed clinic in Coral Gables. His best customer was Rodriguez who, in exchange for immunity from prosecution, confessed to DEA agents in January of last year that he had paid thousands of dollars a month to Bosch for PEDs.
      </p>
      <p>
       The sentencing of Bosch comes as the investigation out of the Southern District of Miami nears an end. Two other defendants in the case, including Rodriguez operative Ugi Velazquez, have pleaded guilty and accepted jail sentences, while one received probation. Two additional defendants await sentencing.
      </p>
      <p>
       Major League Baseball, meanwhile, claims to have moved on: Rodriguez served his historic season-long suspension last year and is scheduled to report to the Yankees spring training complex in the next few days.
      </p>
      <div class="a-module" itemid="http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.2118257!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_635/mlb-drug-investigation.jpg" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
       <figure class="a-image h">
        <img alt="PHOTO TAKEN THROUGH A GLASS WINDOW" src="http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.2118257.1424192962!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_635/mlb-drug-investigation.jpg"/>
        <span class="a-credit" itemprop="copyrightHolder">
         Alan Diaz/AP
        </span>
        <h2 class="a-caption" itemprop="description">
         Anthony Bosch, former owner of a South Florida anti-aging clinic at the center of Major League Baseball's most recent steroid scandal, arrives at Federal Court in Miami Tuesday.
        </h2>
        <a class="a-enlarge" href="#">
        </a>
       </figure>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       The other players have also served their suspensions, including Milwaukee Brewers outfielder Ryan Braun, who agreed to a 65-game suspension in July of 2013. Braun struck a deal with MLB after being presented with evidence that he had obtained testosterone through Biogenesis. Braun had previously tested positive for testosterone in December of 2011, but maintained his innocence and ultimately avoided suspension for that violation on a technicality that his test sample had been improperly handled.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rodriguezs suspension followed on Aug. 5, 2013, when then-commissioner Bud Selig hit the Yankee third baseman with an historic 211-game suspension. Rodriguez immediately appealed the ban and began a scorched-earth battle against MLB that resulted in lawsuits against Selig and MLB, the Players Association and Yankee team doctor Christopher Ahmad.
      </p>
      <p>
       Amid public denials that he had procured PEDs through Bosch, or that he even knew Bosch, Rodriguez attacked MLB and Yankee officials through almost all of 2013. It wasnt until arbitrator Fredric Horowitz upheld the suspension in January of last year, and the DEAs confrontation of Rodriguez later that month with evidence of his dealings with Bosch, that A-Rod admitted his involvement to federal agents. He has not publicly admitted his involvement with Bosch.
      </p>
      <p>
       Twelve other players agreed to 50-game suspensions without the right to appeal: Antonio Bastardo, Everth Cabrera, Francisco Cervelli, Nelson Cruz, Fautino de los Santos, Sergio Escalona, Fernando Martnez, Jess Montero, Jordan Norberto, Jhonny Peralta, Csar Puello and Jordany Valdespin.
      </p>
