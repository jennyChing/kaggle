         <h1>
          6 Best Back-to-School Jobs for College Kids
         </h1>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="article-container" id="TNArticleBodySection">
        <div id="article-body">
         <p>
          For many students, college means more than lectures and long hours in the library. It also means preparing for a future career by working part-time while they study. Whether you're working to rev up your resume or cover your bills, a part-time job is a valuable part of your college experience. Take a look at these part-time positions that build real-world skills and reward you with decent pay.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Customer Service Representative
         </h2>
         <br/>
         <p>
          <img alt="girl on phone at call center" class="media-image" src="http://artstg.icbdr.com/sites/all/files/2486090836_ca7cd152d2.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Image via
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/alanclarkdesign/2486090836/in/photolist-4MFSc7-8tfLEL-4GhjqZ-4HXqQp-4HXqQg-6Xqup8-6XutNN-4HXD2z-4PFKok-4HXqQc-a3VA8f-a3VAmG-a3VzUu-a3SJzt-a3VTxm-a3SKeV-a3VAqb-a3VA49-a3VzBC-a3VzEJ-a3VTtW-a3SJSB-a3VzX5-a3SJwD-a3SKt8-a3VAbq-a3SK2i-a3VAhQ-a3SJKX-9WxULK-aCmRmw-8nqDab-4GmsSj-9yq3S-8nqGcJ-8nnuWi-2oWf7C-2mYsjp-9uVvCJ-o8XZJg-2n3KTj-2n3Pv5-2n3Jvw-2mYoRF-2mYrwV-2mYozz-2mYuLg-2n3JW5-2n3Gyq-2n3KqU" target="_blank">
           Flickr
          </a>
          by Alan Clark
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Customer service positions have a few great advantages over some of the other jobs on this list. First of all, most companies staff their customer service call center 24 hours a day, so it's easy to find shifts that work around your class schedule. Another advantage is that many companies use technology that allows customer service reps (CSR) to work from home, which opens up opportunities to work for companies outside your geographical location. It also cuts down on job-related expenses like commuting and meals on the go.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          You'll need a lot of self-control to work as a CSR, though, because more often than not you'll be dealing with disgruntled customers. However, customer service experience is a plus in just about any future career you're considering; after all, having good interpersonal skills is just as important for dealing with coworkers as it is with customers. Most CSR positions pay well above minimum wage; expect hourly pay in the $14 to $16 range.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Bartender
         </h2>
         <p>
          If you have an outgoing personality, the ability to stay calm, and careful attention to detail, you can make big bucks tending bar. Bartending jobs are great for college students because they offer night and weekend shifts that don't interfere with classesand those shifts are usually the most lucrative, besides.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Contrary to urban myths, you don't need a license to be a bartender, although bartending school is a good investment if you're serious about the position. In many states,
          <a href="http://www2.potsdam.edu/alcohol/StateAndLocalLaws/1133835008.html#.U9g_fcb7qag" target="_blank">
           you don't even need to be 21
          </a>
          to mix drinks. You'll learn essential life skills
          <a href="http://www.jobs.net/article/cb-131-talent-network-restaurant-5-reasons-everyone-should-work-in-a-restaurant-at-least-once/" target="_blank">
           working in a restaurant
          </a>
          or bar, like time management and the value of working as a team. Best of all, depending on your skill and the bar's clientele, you can take home a couple of hundred a night in tips to complement your hourly wages.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Personal Trainer/Fitness Instructor
         </h2>
         <br/>
         <p>
          <img alt="pilates floor barre" class="media-image" src="http://artstg.icbdr.com/sites/all/files/5014012821_cb54cb28c7.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Image via
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/heraldpost/5014012821/sizes/m/">
           Flickr
          </a>
          by HeraldPost
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Working at your local gym is a fine way to combat countless hours spent sitting in lecture halls and library cubbies. Teaching a class looks great on a resume; it shows you have energy, initiative, and impressive people skills. It takes a lot of coaching and encouragement to keep people motivated to keep their gym commitments.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          In addition to an average salary of $15 an hour, most fitness instructors get additional perks like free membership at the gyms where they teach and discounts on fitness equipment, workout clothes, and any nutritional products the gym offers its members. Another bonus is the flexibility most gyms give their instructors in scheduling their classes.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          If you're serious about your workouts and have an extensive background in weightlifting and exercise physiology from your own researchand an impressive body to matchyou can really rake in cash as a
          <a href="http://www.livestrong.com/article/301751-how-much-does-a-fitness-trainer-make/" target="_blank">
           personal trainer
          </a>
          . Some trainers are entrepreneurs while others contract with local gyms to provide services. Either way, if you've invested in your own fitness and have the skills to teach others how to get healthy, working as a personal trainer is a flexible and lucrative part-time position.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Tutor
         </h2>
         <p>
          Tutors are always in demand, especially in difficult subjects like physics, organic chemistry, advanced math, and hard sciences. If your college has a student resource center, they may offer formal tutoring positions paid directly by your college. Check with the HR department to see how to apply.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          However, if you're more entrepreneurial, consider passing out leaflets to students in classes you've aced and negotiate your own fees and your own schedule. You can also market yourself to area high school students who are struggling with their coursework. Depending on your location and the subjects you teach, you can charge between $15 and $25 per hour for tutoring services. You can also apply to work with an online tutoring company.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Pet Sitter/House Sitter/Dog Walker
         </h2>
         <p>
          You'd be surprised at how much people are willing to pay for someone they trust to care for their home and/or pets while they're away. In some cities, you can make $100 or more per day. And house and
          <a href="http://www.entrepreneur.com/article/172396" target="_blank">
           pet sitting
          </a>
          come with the added bonus of getting you out of your cramped dorm into more spacious digs with more comfortable furniture.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Starting out, let faculty and college staff know you're available and ask for referrals. Create eye-catching flyers with a trustworthy-looking photo of yourself and listing your availability and the services you offer and post them at grocery stores and coffee shops in upscale residential areas. After one or two satisfied customers, you'll find word of mouth referrals will keep you as busy as you'd like to be.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          Tech Support/Computer Repair
         </h2>
         <p>
          If you're the type of person who handles laptop disasters with ease and knows what to do with the blue screen of death, there's a market for your services. For a guaranteed income, apply to work at your campus' tech support center; you can make $15 to $20 an hour at some schools.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          If you've got an entrepreneurial streak, offer your services directly to your fellow students. Nearly all of them have a laptop, Mac, or other device that will misbehave at some point during the school year. Get a reputation for solving computer crises, and you'll pocket a surprising amount of cash. Tech support work looks great on your resume if you're planning for an IT career.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          While you can't make a career out of most of these jobs, they do provide valuable experience, much-needed cash, and a useful stepping stone to a high-paying job after graduation.
         </p>
