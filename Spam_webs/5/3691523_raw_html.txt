      <p>
       <a href="http://www.bksteam.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Announcement.png">
        <img alt="PSA IT Scam" class="alignnone wp-image-808" height="448" src="http://www.bksteam.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Announcement.png" width="448"/>
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are IT tech support scams out there and they will call anyone. Although they usually prey on less tech-savy individuals and older people, Ive been getting calls from someone claiming to be MS Support about a virus on my computer. I decided to hear out this scammer and listen to his pitch and well, mess with him a little bit. I have no respect for someone that can cheat and lie his way onto an innocent persons computer and steal their data.
      </p>
      <h3>
       He asked me to go to my computer and view the event viewer where there is a log with red and yellow warnings. I know these are normal and did not panic, but he tried to instill fear in me with these warnings. For only $99, I could give him my credit card and he would remote into my computer and fix the problem. Uh, no.
      </h3>
      <p>
       After much antagonizing and question asking, he did not like what I had to say and called me bad names. He might have just been a worker instructed on how to scam individuals for their credit card information, but we must have ethics in the work we do. In the IT industry, we are dealing with a lot of sensitive information and data that cannot be shared and must be protected. For individuals to prey on the less educated or more gullible people with scams like this makes me pretty angry and creates a distrust for the legitimate IT professionals out there.
      </p>
      <p>
       I had to share this because his next call may have been to my grandmother with minimal computer skills at best. And it just isnt right.
      </p>
      <p>
       So a few notes for those of you out there wanting to know how to educate your parents/grandparents on avoiding telephone scams. Remind them that if someone calls them, they should not:
      </p>
      <p>
       1. Give their credit card information to someone that calls them.
      </p>
      <p>
       2. Go to a website that someone tells them to go to and download anything or input credit card information.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Noone from Microsoft will cold call you and ask you for your password or your credit card information.
      </p>

        Avoiding Tech Support Phone Scams
