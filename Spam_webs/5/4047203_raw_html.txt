        <h1>
         5 Insane Theories That Change How You See Great Works Of Art
        </h1>
       </header>
       <footer>
        <div class="meta">
         <span class="by">
          By
         </span>
         <a class="byline" href="/members/Hatchback" rel="author">
          Nathan Williams
         </a>
         <time class="date" datetime="2015-04-29T05:00:00-07:00">
          April 29, 2015
         </time>
         <span class="views" id="viewCounts">
          817,352 Views
         </span>
         <span class="imgFlame">

        </div>
        <div class="socialShareModule">

       <figure>

       <div id="persistent-share">
       </div>
       <section>
        <p>
         We wouldn't dream of diminishing the work of any of the great masters on this list, or insist you start enjoying it less. But even great artists are just fallible human beings, which is why we can't dismiss these theories about their famous work being the result of accidents, trickery, or outright fraud.
        </p>
        <p>
         We want to emphasize the word "theories" here -- none of these have been proven, and probably never will be. Still, it's interesting to think that ...
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #5. Vincent Van Gogh Might Have Been Color-Blind
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/6/0/398460_v1.jpg" height="203" width="610"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 610px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh#/media/File:Vincent_van_Gogh_-_Self-Portrait_-_Google_Art_Project_(454045).jpg" target="c">
           Vincent Van Gogh
          </a>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Even if you know nothing about art, you can probably spot a Vincent Van Gogh painting due to his extremely distinctive style of visible brush strokes, swirls, and unusual coloration. You've surely seen this one, for example:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/2/2/398422_v1.jpg" height="277" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Starry_Night#/media/File:Van_Gogh_-_Starry_Night_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" target="c">
           Vincent Van Gogh
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Or
          <a href="http://blogs.phoenixnewtimes.com/bella/2011/05/van_goghs_the_starry_night_rec.php" target="c">
           eaten it
          </a>
          .
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Now, when we say "unusual coloration," we mean that sometimes shit just isn't the right color. For instance, here's another famous painting of his, of a vase of sunflowers:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/2/4/398424_v1.jpg" height="313" width="250"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 250px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Vincent_Willem_van_Gogh_128.jpg" target="c">
           Vincent Van Gogh
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "I originally wanted to include some ears of corn, but decided to cut it."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         The center of his flowers are a very bright red -- real sunflowers tend to be a dark reddish-brown -- and the yellows are washed out by comparison. But hey, that's why he's a genius artist and we're just a bunch of dickheads. Those oddball choices are what separate true artists from the rest of us (and sometimes from their own ears). But here's the thing: Show that painting to someone who has a specific type of color-blindness -- the kind that keeps them from seeing red very well -- and they'll say, "Unusual coloration? Looks pretty realistic to me." That's because they're seeing this:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/2/6/398426_v1.jpg" height="313" width="250"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 250px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://asada0.tumblr.com/post/11517603099/the-day-i-saw-van-goghs-genius-in-a-new-light" target="c">
           Kazunori Asada
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          So, basically reverse Zack Snyder.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         See, it has been long rumored that Van Gogh was color-blind, for this very reason. So designer Kazunori Asada
         <a href="http://asada0.tumblr.com/post/11517603099/the-day-i-saw-van-goghs-genius-in-a-new-light" target="c">
          ran all of the artist's paintings through a lighting filter
         </a>
         to see how they would have looked to the artist if he was as color-blind as the theory goes. And what you find is that the odd color choices may have been accidents due to the poor bastard being unable to perceive them. He just didn't know the pigment he'd mixed up was that
         <em>
          red
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Look, he's not saying the paintings are better this way, but they're definitely more muted and realistic. Here's another one -- first the original, as Van Gogh painted it:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/2/8/398428_v1.jpg" height="274" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Vincent_van_Gogh_-_The_Road_Menders_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" target="c">
           Vincent Van Gogh
          </a>
         </font>
        </p>
        <br/>
        <p>
         Sharp black lines like a comic book, surrounding inexplicable green in the tree trunks. And now, here's what a color-blind Van Gogh would have seen:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/3/0/398430_v1.jpg" height="277" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://asada0.tumblr.com/post/11517603099/the-day-i-saw-van-goghs-genius-in-a-new-light" target="c">
           Kazunori Asada
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Follow him on Instagram, @VinnieG.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Likewise, look at the unnatural greens in the middle of this one ...
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/3/2/398432_v1.jpg" height="284" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Vincent_van_Gogh_-_Garden_at_Arles_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" target="c">
           Vincent Van Gogh
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          The grass is always greener when you're color-blind.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         ... versus the one adjusted for color-blindness:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/3/4/398434_v1.jpg" height="277" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://asada0.tumblr.com/post/11517603099/the-day-i-saw-van-goghs-genius-in-a-new-light" target="c">
           Kazunori Asada
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          This is like cheap,
          <i>
           off-brand
          </i>
          LSD.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Again, we're not going to say that messing with the color scale in a Van Gogh "improves" it -- we're not a bunch of assholes. But it definitely looks more like the real world, meaning Van Gogh's surreal use of color may have been a result of him simply not being able to see his paintings the way everyone else did. He may literally have not known he was painting a wheat field bright orange ...
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/3/6/398436_v1.jpg" height="270" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Van_Gogh_-_Weizenfeld_hinter_dem_Hospital_Saint-Paul_(Die_Ernte).jpeg" target="c">
           Vincent Van Gogh
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          ... Kraft microwaveable wheat right there ...
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         ... instead of, you know, wheat color:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/3/8/398438_v2.jpg" height="270" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://asada0.tumblr.com/post/11517603099/the-day-i-saw-van-goghs-genius-in-a-new-light" target="c">
           Kazunori Asada
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Note: If
          <i>
           you
          </i>
          are color-blind, this article will make no sense to you.
         </font>
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #4. Andy Warhol's Success May Be Credited To Asperger's Syndrome
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/6/2/398462_v1.jpg" height="203" width="610"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 610px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Warhol#/media/File:Andy_Warhol_by_Jack_Mitchell.jpg" target="c">
           Jack Mitchell
          </a>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         In the 1960s, eccentric artist Andy Warhol changed the landscape of visual art forever when he hung up 32 identical paintings of a soup can in a gallery, dropped his paintbrush like a comedian at the end of his set, and called it his masterpiece. Thus the genre of pop art was born. The phenomenon of using advertising imagery, celebrities, and comic book panels in gallery artwork became one of the most iconic and widespread art movements of the 20th century.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now, if you were strolling through the gallery with an insensitive asshole, he might smirk at the rows of identical soup cans and say, "That shit looks like something some Asperger's kid made while he was locked in his room for the weekend. A kid who really likes soup." Well, asshole or not, some experts would agree.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/4/0/398440_v1.jpg" height="198" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campbell's_Soup_Cans#/media/File:Campbells_Soup_Cans_MOMA.jpg" target="c">
           Andy Warhol
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          On the back of each is a painting of a grilled cheese sandwich.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Decades after Warhol's death, several mental health professionals and scientists including
         <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/uk/1999/mar/14/vanessathorpe.theobserver" target="c">
          Dr. Judith Gould
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/health/3380569.stm" target="c">
          Prof. Michael Fitzgerald
         </a>
         diagnosed the artist with being somewhere on the autism spectrum. Autism spectrum disorders
         <a href="https://books.google.com.au/books?id=HrzIJvNzq1YC&amp;pg=PA4&amp;lpg=PA4&amp;dq=&amp;redir_esc=y" target="c">
          are characterized
         </a>
         by behaviors such as limited displays of emotion, concise speech mannerisms, and obsessions with fixed routines and hobbies -- all of which, according to those who knew him, described Warhol to a T.
        </p>
        <p>
         His eccentricity wasn't just limited to drawing the same picture of a soup tin over and over again -- his friends have reported that he
         <a href="http://www.warholstars.org/andy_warhol_soup_can.html" target="c">
          ate tomato soup every single day
         </a>
         , and in his autobiography he admitted that he obsessively purchased the same brand of green cotton underpants over and over again. On top of all that, Warhol was notorious for his unusual way of relating to people -- once responding to a friend's suicide by saying only that he wished he'd been there in time to film it -- which is also the kind of social awkwardness that's common with autism spectrum disorders. At least, we hope that's the explanation, because otherwise it implies that Warhol was just kind of a dick.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/5/2/398452_v1.jpg" height="242" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://www.thomlafferty.net/portraits.html" target="c">
           Thom Lafferty
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "If you're holding a camera, it's just being an artist."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         After his death, it was also discovered that Warhol had a
         <a href="https://books.google.com.au/books?id=Ce0PBQAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PA193&amp;lpg=PA193&amp;dq=&amp;redir_esc=y" target="c">
          massive hoarding problem
         </a>
         (not just green underwear but other pointless trinkets, like cookie jars that he obsessively collected but never unpacked) and had
         <a href="http://pastexhibitions.guggenheim.org/warhol/" target="c">
          spent his final years
         </a>
         endlessly re-creating Da Vinci's painting
         <i>
          The Last Supper
         </i>
         over and over again, more than 60 times.
        </p>
        <p>
         Given that Warhol always notoriously insisted that his artwork
         <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/apps/news?pid=newsarchive&amp;sid=ajZFckoavebY&amp;refer=muse" target="c">
          didn't mean a damn thing
         </a>
         , it's likely that he was completely sincere -- he wasn't trying to make some complicated statement about art or the state of society; he was just an obsessive dude who really, really liked soup. A lot. But here's the ironic part: It might be that in the process of trying to create something meaningless, he actually created a striking portrait of a mind getting locked into the same pointless task, over and over again. So it did have meaning after all! Suck it,
         <em>
          Warhol
         </em>
         !
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #3. Goya's Black Paintings Might Have Been Forged
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/6/4/398464_v1.jpg" height="203" width="610"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 610px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Paintings#/media/File:El_Aquelarre.jpg" target="c">
           Francisco Goya
          </a>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Francisco Goya was a Spanish painter of the 18th-century romantic era, who mostly painted portraits of pudgy rich folk and friends enjoying a merry picnic in the sun. Then, after suffering an illness in his old age, he
         <a href="http://www.wga.hu/html_m/g/goya/9/index.html" target="c">
          went batshit insane
         </a>
         , locked himself in his house, and started covering the walls with things like this:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/4/2/398442_v1.jpg" height="295" width="250"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 250px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Paintings#/media/File:Francisco_de_Goya,_Saturno_devorando_a_su_hijo_(1819-1823)_crop.jpg" target="c">
           Francisco Goya
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "Look, it's this or feces; choice is yours."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Saturn Devouring His Son
         </i>
         is the most famous of the so-called Black Paintings, a series of 14 murals that Goya decided to decorate his house with in his dying years, and the rest aren't much less disturbing.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/4/4/398444_v1.jpg" height="207" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Paintings#/media/File:Viejos_comiendo_sopa.jpg" target="c">
           Francisco Goya
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          It's also unclear how Andy Serkis was alive back then.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Nobody knew what Goya had been up to until after he died and his grandson stumbled upon the carnival of horrors that he'd turned the inside of his house into.
        </p>
        <p>
         At least, that's always been the story.
        </p>
        <p>
         In recent years, art historian Juan Jose Junquera began intensive research into the Black Paintings and
         <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2003/07/27/magazine/the-secret-of-the-black-paintings.html?src=pm&amp;pagewanted=1" target="c">
          began to notice some peculiarities
         </a>
         with the story. For one, the mentally unstable Goya did still have a few friends and visitors during the time that the paintings were apparently made, but there's no evidence that any of them ever observed, "Gee, Frank, that sure is an interesting picture of a man whose eyes reflect a horror and insanity hitherto not experienced by any mortal as he tearfully devours a raw human baby! Pass the beans?" In fact, inventories of the contents of the house taken after Goya's death make no mention whatsoever of the Black Paintings, which are the kind of thing that most estate appraisers would probably notice during a property evaluation.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/4/5/4/398454_v1.jpg" height="362" width="250"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 250px; text-align: right;">
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Paintings#/media/File:Mujeres_riendo.jpg" target="c">
           Francisco Goya
          </a>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Presuming they weren't immediately transported to the void.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Junquera thinks the Black Paintings may have been forgeries, part of a conspiracy by Goya's descendants to milk more money out of the sale of the estate. Juliet Wilson-Bareau, an expert on Goya's work who has previously called out other suspected Goya forgeries, was quick to add that if Junquera's theory is true, the likeliest creator of the murals is Goya's son, Javier, who knew how to paint but was never able to make any money off his own work. It's easy to imagine him taking a bunch of the unmarketable paintings he'd created (presumably for the cover of his metal album) and throwing them in with his famous father's work. "Oh, look, everybody! We found a bunch more super valuable Goya masterpieces just lying around! Who wants to start the bidding?"
        </p>
        <p>
         Despite all the clues, the Prado Museum's curator has insisted the paintings will not be sold or relocated even if they are proven to be fakes, because of their place in Spanish cultural heritage. The most dignified way yet anyone has ever plugged their ears with their fingers and said, "La, la, la, la, la, I can't hear you!"
        </p>

         <a href="http://society6.com/product/binary-art_t-shirt?utm_campaign=1301#11=49&amp;4=104&amp;utm_source=cracked.com&amp;utm_medium=crackedendofarticle&amp;utm_campaign=crackedhouseads#11=49&amp;4=75" target="_blank">
          <img src="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/9/2/2/377922.jpg?v=1"/>
         </a>
        </p>
