      <b>
       Explanation:
      </b>
      Why would the shadow of a
      <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/shuttle/main/index.html">
       space shuttle
      </a>
      launch plume point toward the Moon?

In early 2001 during a launch of
      <a href="http://science.ksc.nasa.gov/shuttle/resources/orbiters/atlantis.html">
       Atlantis
      </a>
      , 
the
      <a href="sun.html">
       Sun
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="http://antwrp.gsfc.nasa.gov/apod/image/0011/earthlights2_dmsp_big.jpg">
       Earth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="http://www.nineplanets.org/luna.html">
       Moon
      </a>
      , 
and rocket were all properly aligned for
      <a href="http://visibleearth.nasa.gov/view_rec.php?vev1id=7574">
       this 
photogenic coincidence
      </a>
      .  

First, for the
      <a href="http://www.shuttlepresskit.com/STS-98/index.htm">
       space shuttle
      </a>
      's plume to cast a long shadow, 
the time of day must be either near
      <a href="ap990817.html">
       sunrise
      </a>
      or
      <a href="ap980526.html">
       sunset
      </a>
      .  

Next, just at sunset, the
      <a href="ap001225.html">
       shadow
      </a>
      is the longest and extends all the way to the
      <a href="ap990619.html">
       horizon
      </a>
      .  

Finally, during a
      <a href="ap000113.html">
       Full Moon
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="ap010129.html">
       Sun
      </a>
      and
      <a href="moon.html">
       Moon
      </a>
      are on 
opposite sides of the sky.  

Just after
      <a href="ap060723.html">
       sunset
      </a>
      , for example, the Sun is slightly below the
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon">
       horizon
      </a>
      , and, 
in the other direction, the Moon is slightly above the
      <a href="ap000320.html">
       horizon
      </a>
      .  

Therefore, as
      <a href="ap950812.html">
       Atlantis
      </a>
      blasted off, just after
      <a href="ap990726.html">
       sunset
      </a>
      , 
its shadow projected away from the Sun 
toward the opposite horizon, where the
      <a href="ap010104.html">
       Full Moon
      </a>
      just happened to be.
     </p>
