       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         What is Old World Wine and New World Wine?
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         Posted on
         <a href="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/old-vs-new-world-wine/" rel="bookmark" title="">
          <time class="entry-date" datetime="2015-04-07T20:49:17+00:00">
           April 7, 2015
          </time>
         </a>
         <span class="byline">
          by
          <span class="author vcard">
           <a class="url fn n" href="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/author/casey/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Casey">
            Casey
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="What is 'Old World Wine' and 'New World Wine'? - TC Wine Blog" class="lazy aligncenter size-full wp-image-6854" data-original="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/What-is-Old-World-Wine-and-New-World-Wine-TC-Wine-Blog.png" height="507" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/plugins/wp-images-lazy-loading/images/grey.gif" width="800"/>
        </p>
        <noscript>
         <img alt="What is 'Old World Wine' and 'New World Wine'? - TC Wine Blog" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6854" height="507" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/What-is-Old-World-Wine-and-New-World-Wine-TC-Wine-Blog.png" width="800"/>
        </noscript>
        As you may have seen on my
        <a href="https://www.facebook.com/TravelCork" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.facebook.com/TravelCork', 'Facebook page');" target="_blank">
         Facebook page
        </a>
        , last week I started a free online wine course through the University of Adelaide calledWine101x The World of Wine. Now Im not much of a reader as I tend to get sidetracked by everything else bar what I am supposed to read (bar being the exact thing that pulls me off course!). So therefore instead of just reading through my weekly lessons for the next 6 weeks I thought I would blog about it and share the knowledge/strategically drill the info into my brain.
        <span id="more-6848">
        </span>
        <p>
         From week 1, I thought it would be interesting to look at the terms Old World Wine and New World Wine and what the heck they mean to everyday wine drinkers like ourselves. Ive been meaning to blog about the topic for sometime, but yet again I have been sidetracked :) To make it easy, I have put together some bullet points below to help you understand what the heck the old and new world are when it comes to wine.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Old World Wine vs. New World Wine
        </h2>
        <ul>
         <li>
          These terms refer to wine regions around the world
         </li>
         <li>
          The terms traditionally reflect the differences in winemaking and viticulture as well as encompassing the soil, climate and terrain of a region, otherwise known in the wine world as the
          <em>
           terrior
          </em>
          of a region
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <img alt="Old World Wine - Europe - TC Wine Blog" class="lazy aligncenter size-full wp-image-6852" data-original="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/Old-World-Wine-Europe-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg" height="532" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/plugins/wp-images-lazy-loading/images/grey.gif" width="800"/>
        </p>
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Old World Wine - Europe - TC Wine Blog" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6852" height="532" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/Old-World-Wine-Europe-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg" width="800"/>
        </noscript>
        <h2>
         The Old World Wines = Tradition
        </h2>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Generally
          <strong>
           Old World Wine
          </strong>
          refers to wines made in Europe
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Old World Wine
          </strong>
          is called exactly that because itscultural roots date rightback to the Roman Empire. Most of these regions werewhere thetechniques to make, storeand distribute wine all began and have been passed down via generation after generation to today
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Old World Wine
          </strong>
          regions typically include:Italy, France, Spain, Portugal, Austria, Germany, Greece Hungary, Israel, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia, Croatia, Cyprus, Switzerland, England and Macedonia
         </li>
         <li>
          The wine coming out of the
          <strong>
           Old World
          </strong>
          typically have various rules and laws they must stick by for wine production for instanceDenominazione di Origine Controllata in Italy &amp;Appellation dOrigine Controlee in France which limits how creative wine makers can get
         </li>
         <li>
          Youll find some winemakers in the New World Wine regions like to make their wines in an Old World Wine style
         </li>
         <li>
          In terms of flavour profile,an Old World Winecan typically be explained as
          <br/>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            <strong>
             earthy
            </strong>
            ,
            <strong>
             elegant
            </strong>
            ,
            <strong>
             tannic
            </strong>
            and full of that
            <strong>
             terrior-ness
            </strong>
            !
           </p>
          </blockquote>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <img alt="Dom Perignon Statue in Champagne - France" class="lazy aligncenter size-full wp-image-6286" data-original="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Dom-Perignon-Statue-in-Champagne-France.jpg" height="800" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/plugins/wp-images-lazy-loading/images/grey.gif" width="600"/>
        </p>
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Dom Perignon Statue in Champagne - France" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6286" height="800" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Dom-Perignon-Statue-in-Champagne-France.jpg" width="600"/>
        </noscript>
        <h2>
         The New World Wines
        </h2>
        <div class="tiled-gallery type-rectangular tiled-gallery-unresized" data-carousel-extra="null" data-original-width="630">
         <div class="gallery-row" data-original-height="211" data-original-width="630" style="width: 630px; height: 211px;">
          <div class="gallery-group images-1" data-original-height="211" data-original-width="315" style="width: 315px; height: 211px;">
           <div class="tiled-gallery-item tiled-gallery-item-large">
            <a border="0" href="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/old-vs-new-world-wine/new-world-wine-asia-oceania-tc-wine-blog/#main">
             <img alt="New World Wine - Asia &amp; Oceania - TC Wine Blog" data-attachment-id="6851" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="&lt;p&gt;New World Wine  Asia &amp; Oceania  TC Wine Blog&lt;/p&gt; " data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"1428438970","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="New World Wine  Asia &amp; Oceania  TC Wine Blog" data-large-file="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-Asia-Oceania-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg" data-medium-file="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-Asia-Oceania-TC-Wine-Blog-300x200.jpg" data-orig-file="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-Asia-Oceania-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg" data-orig-size="800,532" data-original-height="207" data-original-width="311" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-Asia-Oceania-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg?resize=311%2C207" style="width: 311px; height: 207px;" title="New World Wine - Asia &amp; Oceania - TC Wine Blog"/>
            </a>
           </div>
          </div>
          <!-- close group -->
          <div class="gallery-group images-1" data-original-height="211" data-original-width="315" style="width: 315px; height: 211px;">
           <div class="tiled-gallery-item tiled-gallery-item-large">
            <a border="0" href="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/old-vs-new-world-wine/new-world-wine-america-tc-wine-blog/#main">
             <img alt="New World Wine - America - TC Wine Blog" data-attachment-id="6850" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="&lt;p&gt;New World Wine  America  TC Wine Blog&lt;/p&gt; " data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"1428438976","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="New World Wine  America  TC Wine Blog" data-large-file="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-America-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg" data-medium-file="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-America-TC-Wine-Blog-300x200.jpg" data-orig-file="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-America-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg" data-orig-size="800,532" data-original-height="207" data-original-width="311" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/New-World-Wine-America-TC-Wine-Blog.jpg?resize=311%2C207" style="width: 311px; height: 207px;" title="New World Wine - America - TC Wine Blog"/>
            </a>
           </div>
          </div>
          <!-- close group -->
         </div>
         <!-- close row -->
        </div>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Therefore
          <strong>
           New World Wine
          </strong>
          is the other side of the bottle, these regions are fairly new to wine production (maybe not in our lifetime, lets say in the last 100 years, but theyre new as in they dont have history dating back to the Romans!)
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           New World Wines
          </strong>
          regions haven taken the traditional techniques and ways from the Old World and made them their own  made it work for them in their environments
         </li>
         <li>
          Therefore youll find winemakers in the new world like to push boundaries and experiment  the mad scientists of the wine world perhaps
         </li>
         <li>
          Alongside the above, winemakers in the new world are using new and improved technology to make simple tasks more efficient and less prone to disaster for instance moving to screw cap rather than cork
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           New World Wine
          </strong>
          regions typically include: Australia, New Zealand, USA, Argentina, Chile, South Africa, China, India, Uruguay, Brazil, Mexico and Canada
         </li>
         <li>
          In terms of flavour profile a New World Wine can typically be explained as
          <br/>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            <strong>
             fruit-forward
            </strong>
            ,
            <strong>
             oaky
            </strong>
            ,
            <strong>
             lush
            </strong>
            and
            <strong>
             full of booze
            </strong>
            !
           </p>
          </blockquote>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <img alt="Olive Farm Wines Cellar Door in the Swan Valley" class="lazy aligncenter size-full wp-image-5557" data-original="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/Olive-Farm-Wines-Cellar-Door-in-the-Swan-Valley.jpg" height="903" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/plugins/wp-images-lazy-loading/images/grey.gif" width="600"/>
        </p>
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Olive Farm Wines Cellar Door in the Swan Valley" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5557" height="903" src="http://travellingcorkscrew.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/Olive-Farm-Wines-Cellar-Door-in-the-Swan-Valley.jpg" width="600"/>
        </noscript>
        <p>
         At the end of the day, the best thing to do is grab a few Chardonnays or Cabernets or whatever takes your fancy from the old and new world and sit back and compare them! Of course in todays world the lines are being blurred between the old and the new worlds with many super innovative winemakers out there and I say bring it on! If you make something delicious then that makes me one happy lady.Cheers!
        </p>
