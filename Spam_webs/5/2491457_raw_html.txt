             <h1>
              Is Pay What You Want an Effective Sales Strategy?
             </h1>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
       </header>
       <div class="row">
        <div class="large-14 push-5 columns">
         <div class="padded-box popped-box plain-white pvs authored-content looser">
          <div class="at-above-post addthis-toolbox at-wordpress-hide" data-title="Is Pay What You Want an Effective Sales Strategy?" data-url="http://www.realbusiness.com/2014/11/uncategorized/is-pay-what-you-want-an-effective-sales-strategy/">
          </div>
          <div class="at-above-post-recommended addthis-toolbox at-wordpress-hide">
          </div>
          <p class="sub-header prominent h5">
           <time class="updated" datetime="2014-11-12" pubdate="">
            November 12, 2014
           </time>
           By Sachin Shenolikar
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Pay what you want.
           </em>
           For many customers, the term sounds like a dream scenario. It could mean anything from contributing more than face value to absolute zilch. They have the freedom to choose.
          </p>
          <p>
           On first instinct, many people may lean toward the freebie but sense of duty also kicks in. When you say pay what you want, almost everybody is going to pay
           <em>
            something
           </em>
           , says customer service expert
           <a href="http://www.hyken.com/">
            Shep Hyken
           </a>
           . Thats the beautiful thing.
          </p>
          <p>
           Despite the risk involved, Pay What You Want (PWYW) is a sales strategy that a number of businesses have tried. In 2010, the Panera Bread restaurant chain offered customers a PWYW option at select locations around the country. That lasted until last year, when Panera stopped the campaign
           <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/wp/2013/07/14/down-with-price-tags-heres-how-to-profit-when-people-pay-what-they-want/">
            to retool
           </a>
           it, citing waning interest from customers.
          </p>
          <p>
           This year, a diner owner in Dallas, N.C., asked patrons to pay what they felt
           <a href="http://finance.yahoo.com/news/north-carolina-diner-takes-prices-130000696.html">
            God would want
           </a>
           them to pay for their meal. She said her profits tripled in the first week. Perhaps the most famous example was when the rock band Radiohead used the PWYW tactic for its 2007 album
           <em>
            In Rainbows
           </em>
           . The move received a lot of media attention, but singer Thom Yorke
           <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2013/3/4/4054634/musics-pay-what-you-want-pioneers-sour-on-giving-away-songs">
            later said
           </a>
           he regretted the decision because of its impact on the music industry, where many customers have now become accustomed to not paying a dime for songs and albums.
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/ifqE3QNqqHg?feature=oembed" width="500">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           Since it is still rare, the PWYW strategy has one clear benefit: publicity. The decision to possibly give away products for no charge means a good chance of media coverage and Web chatter, especially for large brands.
          </p>
          <p>
           Think about the money [companies] spend on publicity or public relations, says Hyken. The right product, delivered on a pay-what-you-want basis, gives you a better chance of getting bigger exposure and having more impressions, getting your information into more peoples hands.
          </p>
          <p>
           If done with honesty and integrity, PWYW can also help deepen the connection between businesses and customers. The business is implicitly saying: We trust you to do whats right. It also shows confidence: Youll love our product so much that youll pay even if you dont have to. There are also tie-ins to corporate social responsibility: Some companies get more engagement by donating a percentage of PWYW proceeds to charity.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.wired.co.uk/news/archive/2012-04/25/pay-what-you-want-study">
            Studies
           </a>
           have shown that customers often feel compelled to pay, which brings us to the wording of the campaign. This year, the investments startup Aspiration is trying a
           <a href="http://www.wired.com/2014/11/andrei-cherny-aspiration/">
            Pay What is Fair
           </a>
           model. Its a subtle change in messaging from Pay What You Want: People
           <em>
            want
           </em>
           to pay nothing, or at least less than face value. But what do they think is
           <em>
            fair
           </em>
           ? That is a different question, and one that could subliminally convince them to give more.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_5050" style="width: 200px">
           <img alt="Shep Hyken" class="wp-image-5050 size-half colorbox-5049" height="267" src="http://www.realbusiness.com/content/uploads/2014/11/shep-hyken2-lg-190x267.jpg" width="190"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Shep Hyken
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           The rewards are feasible, but there are reasons that PWYW hasnt been adopted on a wide scale. First, as Panera found out, it can confuse customers, causing them to avoid PWYW items altogether. Also, it asks customers to work  some may just want to pay a set price and get on with their day. Finally, anytime something is offered at a reduced cost, defense mechanisms prop up: Whats the catch?
          </p>
          <p>
           Whether PWYW can work long-term as the main income generator for a business is still unclear, but it has proven to be valuable for short-term campaigns. If you set it up properly, I believe its a win-win situation, says Hyken.
          </p>
