     <p>
      <b>
       Rallying
      </b>
      , also known as
      <b>
       rally racing
      </b>
      , is a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">
       auto racing
      </a>
      that takes place on public or private roads with modified production or specially built road-legal cars. This motorsport is distinguished by running not on a
      <a href="/wiki/Race_track" title="Race track">
       circuit
      </a>
      , but instead in a point-to-point format in which participants and their co-drivers drive between set control points (
      <a href="/wiki/Special_stage_(rallying)" title="Special stage (rallying)">
       special stages
      </a>
      ), leaving at regular intervals from one or more start points. Rallies may be won by pure speed within the stages or alternatively by driving to a predetermined ideal journey time within the stages.
     </p>

        <div class="magnify">

        entrant

      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The term "rally", as a branch of
      <a href="/wiki/Motorsport" title="Motorsport">
       motorsport
      </a>
      , probably dates from the first
      <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_Rally" title="Monte Carlo Rally">
       Monte Carlo Rally
      </a>
      of January 1911. Until the late 1920s, few if any other events used the term.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rallying itself can be traced back to the 1894
      <a href="/wiki/Paris%E2%80%93Rouen_(motor_race)" title="ParisRouen (motor race)">
       ParisRouen Horseless Carriage Competition
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Concours des Voitures sans Chevaux
      </i>
      ), sponsored by a Paris newspaper,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Le_Petit_Journal" title="Le Petit Journal">
        Le Petit Journal
       </a>
      </i>
      , which attracted considerable public interest and entries from leading manufacturers. Prizes were awarded to the vehicles by a jury based on the reports of the observers who rode in each car; the official winner was
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Albert Lematre">
       Albert Lematre
      </a>
      driving a 3hp
      <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">
       Peugeot
      </a>
      , although the
      <a href="/wiki/Jules-Albert_de_Dion" title="Jules-Albert de Dion">
       <i>
        Comte
       </i>
       de Dion
      </a>
      had finished first but his steam powered vehicle was ineligible for the official competition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This event led directly to a period of city-to-city road races in France and other European countries, which introduced many of the features found in later rallies: individual start times with cars running against the clock rather than head to head; time controls at the entry and exit points of towns along the way; road books and route notes; and driving over long distances on ordinary, mainly gravel, roads, facing hazards such as dust, traffic, pedestrians and farm animals.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first of these great races was the
      <a href="/wiki/Paris%E2%80%93Bordeaux%E2%80%93Paris" title="ParisBordeauxParis">
       ParisBordeauxParis
      </a>
      race of June 1895, won by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Koechlin" title="Paul Koechlin">
       Paul Koechlin
      </a>
      in a Peugeot, despiting arriving 11 hours after
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Levassor" title="mile Levassor">
       mile Levassor
      </a>
      in a Panhard et Levassor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Levassor's time for the 1,178km (732mi) course, running virtually without a break, was 48 hours and 48 minutes, an average speed of 24km/h (15mph).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Just eight years later, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Paris%E2%80%93Madrid_race" title="ParisMadrid race">
       ParisMadrid race
      </a>
      of May 1903, the
      <a href="/wiki/Mors_(automobile)" title="Mors (automobile)">
       Mors
      </a>
      of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fernand_Gabriel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fernand Gabriel (page does not exist)">
       Fernand Gabriel
      </a>
      , running over the same roads, took just under five and a quarter hours for the 550km (340mi) to Bordeaux, an average of 105km/h (65.3mph). Speeds had now far outstripped the safe limits of dusty highways thronged with spectators and open to other traffic, people and animals; there were numerous crashes, many injuries and eight deaths. The French government stopped the race and banned this style of event.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From then on, racing in Europe (apart from Italy) would be on closed circuits, initially on long loops of public highway and then, in 1907, on the first purpose-built track, England's
      <a href="/wiki/Brooklands" title="Brooklands">
       Brooklands
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Racing was going its own separate way.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the earliest of road races, the
      <i>
       Tour de France
      </i>
      of 1899, was to have a long history, running 18 times as a reliability trial between 1906 and 1937, before being revived in 1951 by the
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile-Club_de_Nice&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Automobile-Club de Nice (page does not exist)">
        Automobile-Club de Nice
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Italy had been running road competitions since 1895, when a reliability trial was run from
      <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">
       Turin
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Asti" title="Asti">
       Asti
      </a>
      and back. The country's first true motor race was held in 1897 along the shore of Lake Maggiore, from Arona to Stresa and back.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This led to a long tradition of road racing, including events like Sicily's
      <a href="/wiki/Targa_Florio" title="Targa Florio">
       Targa Florio
      </a>
      (from 1906
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) and
      <i>
       Giro di Sicilia
      </i>
      (Tour of Sicily, 1914), which went right round the island,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      both of which continued on and off until after World War II. The first Alpine event was held in 1898, the Austrian Touring Club's three-day Automobile Run through South Tyrol, which included the infamous
      <a href="/wiki/Stelvio_Pass" title="Stelvio Pass">
       Stelvio Pass
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Britain, the legal maximum speed of 12mph (19km/h) precluded road racing, but in April and May 1900, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile_Club_of_Great_Britain" title="Automobile Club of Great Britain">
       Automobile Club of Great Britain
      </a>
      (the forerunner of the Royal Automobile Club) organised the Thousand Mile Trial, a 15-day event linking Britain's major cities, in order to promote this novel form of transport. Seventy vehicles took part, the majority of them trade entries. They had to complete thirteen stages of route varying in length from 43 to 123 miles (69 to 198km) at average speeds of up to the legal limit of 12mph (19km/h), and tackle six hillclimb or speed tests. On rest days and at lunch halts, the cars were shown to the public in exhibition halls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (October 2010)">
          unreliable source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This was followed in 1901 by a five-day trial based in Glasgow
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Scottish Automobile Club organised an annual GlasgowLondon non-stop trial from 1902 to 1904, then the Scottish Reliability Trial from 1905.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowbourne_2005_p_279_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cowbourne_2005_p_279-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Motor Cycling Club allowed cars to enter its trials and runs from 1904 (London
      <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">
       Edinburgh
      </a>
      , London
      <a href="/wiki/Land%27s_End" title="Land's End">
       Land's End
      </a>
      , London
      <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">
       Exeter
      </a>
      all still in being as mud-plugging classic trials).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowbourne_2005_p_279_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cowbourne_2005_p_279-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1908 the Royal Automobile Club held its 2,000mi (3,200km) International Touring Car Trial,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 1914 the important Light Car Trial for manufacturers of cars up to 1400 cc, to test comparative performances and improve the breed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1924, the exercise was repeated as the Small Car Trials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Germany, the Herkomer Trophy was first held in 1905, and again in 1906. This challenging five-day event attracted over 100 entrants to tackle its 1,000km (620mi) road section, a
      <a href="/wiki/Hillclimbing" title="Hillclimbing">
       hillclimb
      </a>
      and a speed trial, but sadly it was marred by poor organisation and confusing regulations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One participant had been Prince Henry of Austria, who was inspired to do better, so he enlisted the aid of the Imperial Automobile Club of Germany to create the first
      <i>
       Prinz Heinrich Fahrt
      </i>
      (Prince Henry Trial) in 1908. Another trial was held in 1910. These were very successful, attracting top drivers and works cars from major teams  several manufacturers added "Prince Henry" models to their ranges.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first Alpine Trial was held in 1909, in Austria; by 1914, this was the toughest event of its kind, producing a star performance from Britain's James Radley in his
      <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Limited" title="Rolls-Royce Limited">
       Rolls-Royce Alpine Eagle
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Then in 1911 came the first
      <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_Rally" title="Monte Carlo Rally">
       Monte Carlo Rally
      </a>
      (later known colloquially as "the Monte"), organised by a group of wealthy locals who formed the "Sport Automobile Vlocipdique Mongasque" and bankrolled by the "Socit des Bains de Mer" (the "sea bathing company"), the operators of the famous casino who were keen to attract wealthy sporting motorists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The competitive elements were slight, but getting to Monaco in winter was a challenge in itself. A second event was held in 1912.
     </p>
     <p>
      Two ultra long distance challenges took place at this time. The
      <a href="/wiki/Peking_to_Paris" title="Peking to Paris">
       Peking-Paris
      </a>
      of 1907 was not officially a competition, but a "raid", the French term for an expedition or collective endeavour whose promoters, the newspaper "Le Matin", rather optimistically expected participants to help each other; it was 'won' by Prince Scipione Borghese, Luigi Barzini, and Ettore Guizzardi in an
      <a href="/wiki/Itala" title="Itala">
       Itala
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/1908_New_York_to_Paris_Race" title="1908 New York to Paris Race">
       New YorkParis
      </a>
      of the following year, which went
      <i>
       via
      </i>
      Japan and
      <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">
       Siberia
      </a>
      , was won by George Schuster and others in a
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Motor_Company" title="Thomas Motor Company">
       Thomas Flyer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each event attracted only a handful of adventurous souls, but in both cases the successful drivers exhibited characteristics modern rally drivers would recognise: meticulous preparation, mechanical skill, resourcefulness, perseverance and a certain single-minded ruthlessness. The
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_York%E2%80%93Seattle_Rally&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New YorkSeattle Rally (page does not exist)">
       New YorkSeattle
      </a>
      race of 1909, if shorter, was no easier. Rather gentler (and more akin to modern rallying) was the
      <a href="/wiki/Glidden_Tour" title="Glidden Tour">
       Glidden Tour
      </a>
      , run by the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Automobile_Association" title="American Automobile Association">
       American Automobile Association
      </a>
      between 1902 and 1913, which had timed legs between control points and a marking system to determine the winners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interwar_years">
       Interwar years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interwar years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG/220px-RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG/330px-RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG/440px-RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RenaultNervaSport_typeZC4.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Renault_Nervasport" title="Renault Nervasport">
         Renault Nervasport
        </a>
        won the
        <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_Rally" title="Monte Carlo Rally">
         Monte Carlo Rally
        </a>
        in 1935.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The First World War brought a lull to rallying. The Monte Carlo Rally was not resuscitated until 1924, but since then, apart from World War II and its aftermath, it has been an annual event and remains a regular round of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">
       World Rally Championship
      </a>
      . In the 1930s, helped by the tough winters, it became the premier European rally, attracting 300 or more participants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1920s, numerous variations on the Alpine theme sprang up in Austria, Italy, France, Switzerland and Germany. The most important of these were Austria's
      <i>
       Alpenfahrt
      </i>
      , which continued into its 44th edition in 1973, Italy's
      <i>
       Coppa delle Alpi
      </i>
      , and the
      <i>
       Coupe Internationale des Alpes
      </i>
      (International Alpine Trial), organised jointly by the automobile clubs of Italy, Germany, Austria, Switzerland and, latterly, France. This last event, run from 1928 to 1936, attracted strong international fields vying for an individual Glacier Cup or a team Alpine Cup, including successful
      <a href="/wiki/Talbot" title="Talbot">
       Talbot
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riley_(automobile)" title="Riley (automobile)">
       Riley
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MG_(car)" title="MG (car)">
       MG
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Motor_Company" title="Triumph Motor Company">
       Triumph
      </a>
      teams from Britain and increasingly strong and well funded works representation from
      <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">
       Adolf Hitler
      </a>
      's Germany, keen to prove its engineering and sporting prowess with successful marques like
      <a href="/wiki/Adler_(automobile)" title="Adler (automobile)">
       Adler
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wanderer_(car)" title="Wanderer (car)">
       Wanderer
      </a>
      and Trumpf.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The French started their own
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Rally" title="Alpine Rally">
        Rallye des Alpes Franaises
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1932, which continued after World War II as the
      <i>
       Rallye International des Alpes
      </i>
      , the name often shortened to
      <i>
       Coupe des Alpes
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other important rallies started between the wars included Britain's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAC_Rally" title="RAC Rally">
       RAC Rally
      </a>
      (1932)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Belgium's
      <i>
       Lige-Rome-Lige
      </i>
      , officially called "Le Marathon de la Route" (1931),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      two events of radically different character; the former a gentle tour between cities from various start points, "rallying" at a seaside resort with a series of manoeuvrability and car control tests; the latter a thinly disguised road race over some of Europe's toughest mountain roads.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Ireland, the first
      <i>
       Ulster Motor Rally
      </i>
      (1931) was run from multiple starting points. After several years in this format, it transitioned into the 1,000-mile (1,600km)
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_of_Ireland_Rally" title="Circuit of Ireland Rally">
       Circuit of Ireland Rally
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Italy,
      <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">
       Benito Mussolini
      </a>
      's government encouraged motorsport of all kinds and facilitated road racing, so the sport quickly restarted after World War I. In 1927 the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mille_Miglia" title="Mille Miglia">
        Mille Miglia
       </a>
      </i>
      (Thousand Mile) was founded, run over a 1,000-mile (1,600km) loop of highways from
      <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">
       Brescia
      </a>
      to Rome and back. It continued in this form until 1938.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Lige of August 1939 was the last major event before World War II. Belgium's
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ginet_Trasenster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ginet Trasenster (page does not exist)">
       Ginet Trasenster
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Bugatti" title="Bugatti">
       Bugatti
      </a>
      ) and France's
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Trevoux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Trevoux (page does not exist)">
       Jean Trevoux
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Hotchkiss_(car)" title="Hotchkiss (car)">
       Hotchkiss
      </a>
      ) tied for first place, denying the German
      <a href="/wiki/Works_team" title="Works team">
       works teams
      </a>
      shortly before their countries were overrun.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This was one of five Lige wins for Trasenster; Trevoux won four Montes between 1934 and 1951.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Post_war_years">
       Post war years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post war years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="450" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg/220px-Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg/330px-Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg/440px-Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Osmo_Kalpala_-_1956_Rally_Finland.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Osmo Kalpala servicing his car during the 1956
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jyv%C3%A4skyl%C3%A4n_Suurajot" title="Jyvskyln Suurajot">
         Jyvskyln Suurajot
        </a>
        , now known as
        <i>
         Rally Finland
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Rallying was again slow to get under way after a major war, but the 1950s were the Golden Age of the long-distance road rally. In Europe, the Monte Carlo Rally, the French and Austrian Alpines, and the Lige were joined by a host of new events that quickly established themselves as classics: the Lisbon Rally (Portugal, 1947), the Tulip Rally (the Netherlands, 1949), the Rally to the Midnight Sun (Sweden, 1951, now the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Rally" title="Swedish Rally">
       Swedish Rally
      </a>
      ), the Rally of the 1000 Lakes (Finland, 1951 - now the
      <a href="/wiki/Rally_Finland" title="Rally Finland">
       Rally Finland
      </a>
      ), and the
      <a href="/wiki/Acropolis_Rally" title="Acropolis Rally">
       Acropolis Rally
      </a>
      (Greece, 1956).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The RAC Rally gained International status on its return in 1951, but for 10 years its emphasis on map-reading navigation and short manoevrability tests made it unpopular with foreign crews.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fdration Internationale de l'Automobile">
        FIA
       </a>
      </i>
      created in 1953 a
      <a href="/wiki/European_Rally_Championship" title="European Rally Championship">
       European Rally Championship
      </a>
      (at first called the "Touring Championship") of eleven events; it was first won by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Helmut_Polensky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Helmut Polensky (page does not exist)">
       Helmut Polensky
      </a>
      of Germany. This was the premier international championship until 1973, when the FIA created the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_World_Rally_Championship_Constructors%27_Champions" title="List of World Rally Championship Constructors' Champions">
       World Rally Championship for Manufacturers
      </a>
      , won that first year by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine-Renault" title="Alpine-Renault">
       Alpine-Renault
      </a>
      . Not until 1979 was there a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_World_Rally_Championship_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of World Rally Championship Drivers' Champions">
       World Rally Championship for Drivers
      </a>
      , won that year by
      <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rn_Waldeg%C3%A5rd" title="Bjrn Waldegrd">
       Bjrn Waldegrd
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Initially, most of the major postwar rallies were fairly gentlemanly, but the organisers of the French Alpine and the Lige (which moved its turning point from Rome into Yugoslavia in 1956) straight away set difficult time schedules: the
      <i>
       Automobile Club de Marseille et Provence
      </i>
      laid on a long tough route over a succession of rugged passes, stated that cars would have to be driven flat out from start to finish, and gave a coveted
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coupe_des_Alpes" title="Coupe des Alpes">
        Coupe des Alpes
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Alpine Cup") to anyone achieving an unpenalised run;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while Belgium's Royal Motor Union made clear no car was expected to finish the Lige unpenalised - when one did (1951 winner
      <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Claes" title="Johnny Claes">
       Johnny Claes
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_XK120" title="Jaguar XK120">
       Jaguar XK120
      </a>
      ) they tightened the timing to make sure it never happened again.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These two events became the ones for "the men" to do. The Monte, because of its glamour, got the media coverage and the biggest entries (and in snowy years was also a genuine challenge); while the Acropolis took advantage of Greece's appalling roads to become a truly tough event.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1956 came Corsica's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_Corse" title="Tour de Corse">
        Tour de Corse
       </a>
      </i>
      , 24 hours of virtually non-stop flat out driving on some of the narrowest and twistiest mountain roads on the planet  the first major rally to be won by a woman, Belgium's Gilberte Thirion, in a
      <a href="/wiki/Renault_Dauphine" title="Renault Dauphine">
       Renault Dauphine
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (October 2010)">
          unreliable source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Lige continued as uncompromisingly an open road event run to an impossible time schedule, and remained Europe's toughest rally until 1964, by which time it had turned to the wilds of Yugoslavia and Bulgaria to find traffic-free roads; but in the end the pressures were irresistible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Coupe des Alpes struggled on until 1973 until it too succumbed, its demise no doubt hastened by the decision of the French motor sporting authorities to select the Tour de Corse as its representative event in international rally championships.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      These events were road races in all but name, but in Italy such races were still allowed, and the
      <i>
       Mille Miglia
      </i>
      continued until a serious accident in 1957 caused it to be banned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, in 1981, the
      <i>
       Tour de France
      </i>
      was revived by the Automobile-Club de Nice as a different kind of rally, based primarily on a series of races at circuits and hillclimbs around the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was successful for a while and continued until 1986. It spawned similar events in a few other countries, but none survive.
     </p>
     <p>
      Rallying became very popular in Sweden and Finland in the 1950s, thanks in part to the invention there of the
      <i>
       specialstrcka
      </i>
      (Swedish) or
      <i>
       erikoiskoe
      </i>
      (Finnish), or special stage: shorter sections of route, usually on minor or private roadspredominantly gravel in these countriesaway from habitation and traffic, which were separately timed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These at long last provided the solution to the conflict inherent in the notion of driving as fast as possible on ordinary roads. The idea spread to other countries, albeit more slowly to the most demanding events.
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007_Wales_Rally_GB" title="2007 Wales Rally GB">

       </div>

     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAC_Rally" title="RAC Rally">
       RAC Rally
      </a>
      had formally become an International event in 1951, but Britain's laws precluded the closure of public highways for special stages. This meant it had to rely on short manoeuvrability tests, regularity sections and night map-reading navigation to find a winner, which made it unattractive to foreign crews. In 1961,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Kemsley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jack Kemsley (page does not exist)">
       Jack Kemsley
      </a>
      was able to persuade the
      <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">
       Forestry Commission
      </a>
      to open their many hundreds of miles of well surfaced and sinuous gravel roads, and the event was transformed into one of the most demanding and popular in the calendar, by 1983 having over 600 miles (970km) of stage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is now called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rally_GB" title="Rally GB">
       Rally GB
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Rallying also took off in Spain and Portugal and by the 1960s had spread to their colonial territories in the mid-Atlantic. By the end of the 1960s events had not only begun in
      <a href="/wiki/Rali_Vinho_da_Madeira" title="Rali Vinho da Madeira">
       Madeira
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Rally_Islas_Canarias" title="Rally Islas Canarias">
       Canary Islands
      </a>
      , but also on the far-flung
      <a href="/wiki/Rallye_A%C3%A7ores" title="Rallye Aores">
       Azores
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      In countries where there was no shortage of demanding roads across remote terrain, other events sprang up. In South America, the biggest of these took the form of long distance city to city races, each of around 5,000 to 6,000 miles (8,0009,500km), divided into daily legs. The first was the
      <i>
       Gran Premio del Norte
      </i>
      of 1940, run from
      <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
       Buenos Aires
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">
       Lima
      </a>
      and back; it was won by
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Fangio" title="Juan Manuel Fangio">
       Juan Manuel Fangio
      </a>
      in a much modified
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">
       Chevrolet
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coup">
       coup
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This event was repeated in 1947, and in 1948 an even more ambitious one was held, the
      <i>
       Gran Premio de la Amrica del Sur
      </i>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
       Buenos Aires
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">
       Caracas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
       Venezuela
      </a>
      Fangio had an accident in which his co-driver was killed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Then in 1950 came the fast and dangerous
      <a href="/wiki/Carrera_Panamericana" title="Carrera Panamericana">
       Carrera Panamericana
      </a>
      , a 1,911-mile (3,075km) road race in stages across Mexico to celebrate the opening of the asphalt highway between the
      <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">
       Guatemala
      </a>
      and United States borders, which ran until 1954.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      All these events fell victim to the cost - financial, social and environmental - of putting them on in an increasingly complex and developed world, although smaller road races continued long after, and a few still do in countries like
      <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
       Bolivia
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Africa, 1950 saw the first French-run
      <i>
       Mditerrane-le Cap
      </i>
      , a 10,000-mile (16,000km) rally from the Mediterranean to
      <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
       South Africa
      </a>
      ; it was run on and off until 1961, when the new political situation hastened its demise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1953 East Africa saw the demanding Coronation Safari, which went on to become the
      <a href="/wiki/Safari_Rally" title="Safari Rally">
       Safari Rally
      </a>
      and a World Championship round,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to be followed in due course by the
      <a href="/wiki/Rallye_du_Maroc" title="Rallye du Maroc">
       Rallye du Maroc
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Rallye_C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire" title="Rallye Cte d'Ivoire">
       Rallye Cte d'Ivoire
      </a>
      . Australia's Redex Round Australia Trial also dates from 1953, although this remained isolated from the rest of the rallying world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Canada hosted one of the world's longest and most gruelling rallies in the 1960s, the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.shell-4000-rally.org" rel="nofollow">
       Shell 4000 Rally
      </a>
      . It was also the only one sanctioned by
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fdration Internationale de l'Automobile">
       FIA
      </a>
      in North America.
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://shell-4000-rally.org/index.htm" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intercontinental_rallying">
       Intercontinental rallying
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Intercontinental rallying">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The quest for longer and tougher events saw the re-establishment of the intercontinental rallies beginning with the
      <a href="/wiki/London%E2%80%93Sydney_Marathon" title="LondonSydney Marathon">
       LondonSydney Marathon
      </a>
      held in 1968. The rally trekked across Europe, the Middle-East and the sub-continent before boarding a ship in Bombay to arrive in Fremantle eight days later before the final push across Australia to Sydney. The huge success of this event saw the creation of the World Cup Rallies, linked to Association Football's FIFA World Cup. The first was the
      <a href="/wiki/1970_London_to_Mexico_World_Cup_Rally" title="1970 London to Mexico World Cup Rally">
       1970 London to Mexico World Cup Rally
      </a>
      which saw competitors travel from London eastwards across to Bulgaria before turning westwards on a more southerly route before boarding a ship in Lisbon. Disembarking in Rio de Janeiro the route travelled southward into Argentina before turning northwards along the western coast of South America before arriving in Mexico City.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/1974_London-Sahara-Munich_World_Cup_Rally" title="1974 London-Sahara-Munich World Cup Rally">
       1974 London-Sahara-Munich World Cup Rally
      </a>
      followed four years later. The rally travelled southwards into Africa but a navigational error saw most of the rally become lost in Algerian desert. Eventually only seven teams reached the southernmost point of the rally in Nigeria with five teams making it back to West Germany having driven all legs and only the winning team completing the full distance. This, coupled with the economic climate of the 1970s the heat went out of intercontinental rallying after a second LondonSydney Marathon in 1977. The concept though was revived in 1979 for the original
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris-Dakar_Rally" title="Paris-Dakar Rally">
       Paris-Dakar Rally
      </a>
      . The success of the
      <i>
       Dakar
      </i>
      would eventually see intercontinental rallying recognised as its own discipline; the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rally_Raid" title="Rally Raid">
       Rally Raid
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">
       Modern times
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern times">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg/220px-Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg/330px-Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg/440px-Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peugeot_206_WRC.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Juuso_Pyk%C3%A4list%C3%B6" title="Juuso Pyklist">
         Juuso Pyklist
        </a>
        in his
        <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_206_WRC" title="Peugeot 206 WRC">
         Peugeot 206 WRC
        </a>
        at the 2003
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Rally" title="Swedish Rally">
         Swedish Rally
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The introduction of the special stage brought rallying effectively into the modern era. It placed a premium on fast driving, and enabled healthy programmes of smaller events to spring up in Britain, France, Scandinavia, Belgium and elsewhere.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since then, the nature of the events themselves has evolved relatively slowly. The increasing costs, both of organization and of competing, as well as safety concerns, have, over the last twenty years, brought progressively shorter rallies, shorter stages and the elimination of nighttime running, scornfully referred to as "office hours rallying" by older hands. Some of the older international events have gone, replaced by others from a much wider spread of countries around the world, until today rallying is truly a worldwide sport. At the same time, fields have shrunk dramatically, as the amateur in his near-standard car is squeezed out.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gruelling long distance events continued to be run. In 1967, a group of American offroaders created the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Baja_1000" title="Baja 1000">
        Mexican 1000
       </a>
      </i>
      Rally, a tough 1,000 mile race for cars and motorcycles which ran the length of the Baja California peninsula, much of it initially over roadless desert, which quickly gained fame as the Baja 1000, today run by the
      <a href="/wiki/SCORE_International" title="SCORE International">
       SCORE
      </a>
      organization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Baja" events now take place in a number of other countries worldwide.
     </p>
     <p>
      1968 brought the first of a series of British-organised intercontinental rallies, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">
        Daily Express
       </a>
      </i>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London-Sydney_Marathon" title="London-Sydney Marathon">
       London-Sydney Marathon
      </a>
      , which attracted over 100 crews including a number of works teams and top drivers; it was won by the
      <a href="/wiki/Rootes_Arrow" title="Rootes Arrow">
       Hillman Hunter
      </a>
      of Andrew Cowan/Brian Coyle/Colin Malkin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Not to be outdone, the rival
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">
        Daily Mirror
       </a>
      </i>
      sponsored in 1970 the London-Mexico World Cup Rally, linking the stadia of two successive football World Cups, on a route that crossed Europe to Bulgaria and back before shipping out from Lisbon to Rio de Janeiro, after looping around South America, and a run through some of the most frightening sections of Peru's road race, the Caminos del Inca, they wrap it up being shipped to Panama and a final run up Central America. The
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(Europe)" title="Ford Escort (Europe)">
       Ford Escort
      </a>
      of Hannu Mikkola and Gunnar Palm won.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These were followed in 1974 by the London-Sahara-Munich World Cup Rally,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in 1977 by the Singapore Airlines London-Sydney Rally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1979, a young Frenchman, Thierry Sabine, founded an institution when he organised the first "rallye-raid" from Paris to Dakar, in Senegal, the event now called the Dakar Rally. From amateur beginnings it quickly became a massive commercial circus catering for cars, motorcycles and trucks, and spawned other similar events.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since 2008, it has been held in South America.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rally_car_evolution">
       Rally car evolution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rally car evolution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>

     <p>
      The main change over that period has been in the cars, and in the professionalisation and commercialisation of the sport. Manufacturers had entered works cars in rallies, and in their forerunner and cousin events, from the very beginning: the 1894 Paris-Rouen was mainly a competition between them, while the Thousand Mile Trial of 1900 had more trade than private entries.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although there had been exceptions like the outlandish Ford V8 specials created by the
      <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">
       Romanians
      </a>
      for the 1936 Monte Carlo Rally, rallies before World War II had tended to be for standard or near-standard production cars, a rule supported by manufacturers because it created a relatively even playing field. After the war, most competing cars were production
      <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">
       saloons
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Sports_car" title="Sports car">
       sports cars
      </a>
      , with only minor modifications to improve performance, handling, braking and suspension. This kept costs down and allowed many more people to afford the sport using ordinary family cars, so entry lists grew into the hundreds.
     </p>

     <p>
      As public interest grew,
      <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">
       car companies
      </a>
      started to introduce special models or variants for rallying, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Motor_Corporation" title="British Motor Corporation">
       British Motor Corporation
      </a>
      's highly successful
      <a href="/wiki/Mini_Cooper" title="Mini Cooper">
       Mini Cooper
      </a>
      , introduced in 1962, and its successor the Mini Cooper S (1963), developed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Car_Company" title="Cooper Car Company">
       Cooper Car Company
      </a>
      . Shortly after,
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_of_Britain" title="Ford of Britain">
       Ford of Britain
      </a>
      first hired
      <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Cars" title="Lotus Cars">
       Lotus
      </a>
      to create a high-performance version of their
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_Cortina" title="Ford Cortina">
       Cortina
      </a>
      family car, then in 1968 launched the
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(Europe)" title="Ford Escort (Europe)">
       Escort Twin Cam
      </a>
      , one of the most successful rally cars of its era.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly,
      <a href="/wiki/Abarth" title="Abarth">
       Abarth
      </a>
      developed high performance versions of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">
       Fiats
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fiat_124" title="Fiat 124">
       124
      </a>
      roadster and
      <a href="/wiki/Fiat_131" title="Fiat 131">
       131
      </a>
      saloon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other manufacturers were not content with modifying their 'bread-and-butter' cars.
      <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">
       Renault
      </a>
      bankrolled the small volume sports-car maker
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_(car)" title="Alpine (car)">
       Alpine
      </a>
      to transform their little
      <a href="/wiki/Alpine_A110" title="Alpine A110">
       A110 Berlinette coup
      </a>
      into a world-beating rally car, and hired a skilled team of drivers too; then in 1974 came the
      <a href="/wiki/Lancia_Stratos" title="Lancia Stratos">
       Lancia Stratos
      </a>
      , the first car designed from scratch to win rallies, and the dominant asphalt rally car of its time. These makers overcame the rules of FISA (as the FIA was called at the time) by building the requisite number of these models for the road.
     </p>

     <p>
      In 1980, a German car maker,
      <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">
       Audi
      </a>
      , at that time not noted for their interest in rallying, introduced a rather large and heavy coup version of their family saloon, installed a
      <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">
       turbocharged
      </a>
      2.1
      <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">
       litre
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Straight-five_engine" title="Straight-five engine">
       five-cylinder
      </a>
      engine, and fitted it with
      <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">
       four-wheel drive
      </a>
      . Thus the
      <a href="/wiki/Audi_Quattro" title="Audi Quattro">
       Audi Quattro
      </a>
      was born. International regulations had prohibited four-wheel drive; but FISA accepted that this was a genuine production car, and changed the rules. The Quattro quickly became the car to beat on snow, ice or gravel; and in 1983 took
      <a href="/wiki/Hannu_Mikkola" title="Hannu Mikkola">
       Hannu Mikkola
      </a>
      to the World Rally Championship title. Other manufacturers had no production four-wheel drive car on which to base their response, so FISA was persuaded to change the rules, and open the Championship to cars in
      <a href="/wiki/Group_B" title="Group B">
       Group B
      </a>
      . This allowed cars to be much further removed from production models, and so was created a generation of rallying supercars, of which the most radical and impressive were the
      <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_205" title="Peugeot 205">
       Peugeot 205 T16
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Renault_5_Turbo" title="Renault 5 Turbo">
       Renault 5 Turbo
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Lancia_Delta_S4" title="Lancia Delta S4">
       Lancia Delta S4
      </a>
      , with flimsy fibreglass bodies roughly the shape of the standard car tacked onto lightweight spaceframe chassis, four-wheel drive, and power outputs reportedly as high as 600hp (450kW). Further Group B cars were developed by Ford (the
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_RS200" title="Ford RS200">
       RS200
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/British_Leyland" title="British Leyland">
       British Leyland
      </a>
      (the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rover_Metro" title="Rover Metro">
       Metro 6R4
      </a>
      ) and many others, but these were less successful.
     </p>
     <p>
      This particular era was not to last. On the 1986
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rallye_de_Portugal" title="Rallye de Portugal">
       Rallye de Portugal
      </a>
      , four spectators were killed; then in May, on the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_Corse" title="Tour de Corse">
        Tour de Corse
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Henri_Toivonen" title="Henri Toivonen">
       Henri Toivonen
      </a>
      went over the edge of a mountain road and was incinerated in the fireball that followed. FISA immediately changed the rules again: rallying after 1987 would be in
      <a href="/wiki/Group_A" title="Group A">
       Group A
      </a>
      cars, closer to the production model. One notably successful car during this period was the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lancia_Delta_Integrale" title="Lancia Delta Integrale">
       Lancia Delta Integrale
      </a>
      , dominating world rallying during 1987, 1988, 1989, 1990, 1991 and 1992  winning six consecutive world rally championships, a feat yet unbeaten.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Drivers">
       Drivers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sebastien_Loeb.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Sebastien_Loeb.jpg/220px-Sebastien_Loeb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Sebastien_Loeb.jpg/330px-Sebastien_Loeb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Sebastien_Loeb.jpg/440px-Sebastien_Loeb.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sebastien_Loeb.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Loeb" title="Sbastien Loeb">
         Sbastien Loeb
        </a>
        , the world's most successful rally driver in terms of
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Rally_Championship_records" title="List of World Rally Championship records">
         WRC wins
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Most of the works drivers of the 1950s were amateurs, paid little or nothing, reimbursed their expenses and given bonuses for winning (although there were certainly exceptions, such as the Grand Prix drivers who were brought in for some events). Then in 1960 came arguably the first rallying superstar (and one of the first to be paid to rally full-time), Sweden's
      <a href="/wiki/Erik_Carlsson" title="Erik Carlsson">
       Erik Carlsson
      </a>
      , driving for
      <a href="/wiki/Saab_Automobile" title="Saab Automobile">
       Saab
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1960s, the competitions manager of BMC, Stuart Turner, hired a series of brave and gifted young
      <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
       Finns
      </a>
      , skills honed on their country's highly competitive gravel or snow rallies, and the modern professional driver was born. As special stage rallying spread around the world Scandinavian drivers were challenged by drivers from Italy, Germany, Britain, Spain and elsewhere. Today, a World Champion may be of any nationality.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">
       World Rally Championship
      </a>
      now visits nearly all continents, taking its stylish sideways driving style and specialized cars to a vast global market, estimated by some to be second only to the
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">
       Formula One
      </a>
      juggernaut. This has produced unprecedented levels of visibility in recent years, but in many ways removed the motorsport from its grassroots past. For better or worse, rally has become a lucrative business.
     </p>

      </span>

     <div class="thumb tleft">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

        <a href="/wiki/Chris_Atkinson" title="Chris Atkinson">

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subaru_Impreza_WRC" title="Subaru Impreza WRC">

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are two main forms: stage rallies and road rallies. Since the 1960s, stage rallies have been the professional branch of the sport. They are based on straightforward speed over stretches of road closed to other traffic. These may vary from asphalt mountain passes to rough forest tracks, from ice and snow to desert sand, each chosen to provide an enjoyable challenge for the crew and a test of the car's performance and reliability.
     </p>
     <p>
      The entertaining and unpredictable nature of the stages, and the fact that the vehicles are in some cases closely related to road cars, means that the bigger events draw massive spectator interest, especially in Europe, Asia and Oceania.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wilson_Escort.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="762" data-file-width="1065" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Wilson_Escort.jpg/220px-Wilson_Escort.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Wilson_Escort.jpg/330px-Wilson_Escort.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Wilson_Escort.jpg/440px-Wilson_Escort.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wilson_Escort.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An
        <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_RS_Cosworth" title="Ford Escort RS Cosworth">
         Escort RS Cosworth
        </a>
        on a stage rally, driven by British driver
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malcolm_Wilson_(Rally)" title="Malcolm Wilson (Rally)">
         Malcolm Wilson
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Road_rally" title="Road rally">
       Road rallies
      </a>
      are the original form, held on highways open to normal traffic, where the emphasis is not on outright speed but on accurate timekeeping and navigation and on vehicle reliability, often on difficult roads and over long distances. They are now primarily amateur events. There are several types of road rallies testing accuracy, navigation or problem solving. Some common types are:
      <a href="/wiki/Regularity_rally" title="Regularity rally">
       Regularity rally
      </a>
      or a Time-Speed-Distance rally (also TSD rally, testing ability to stay on track and on time),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      others are Monte-Carlo styles (Monte Carlo, Pan Am, Pan Carlo, Continental) rally (testing navigation and timing), and various Gimmick rally types (testing logic and observation).
     </p>
     <p>
      Many early rallies were called trials, and a few still are, although this term is now mainly applied to the specialist form of motor sport of climbing as far as you can up steep and slippery hills. And many meets or assemblies of car enthusiasts and their vehicles are still called rallies, even if they involve merely the task of getting there (often on a trailer).
     </p>
     <p>
      Rallying is a very popular sport at the "grass roots" of motorsportthat is, motor clubs. Individuals interested in becoming involved in rallying are encouraged to join their local automotive clubs. Club rallies (e.g. road rallies or
      <a href="/wiki/Regularity_rally" title="Regularity rally">
       regularity rallies
      </a>
      ) are usually run on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_roads" title="Public roads">
       public roads
      </a>
      with an emphasis on navigation and teamwork. These skills are important fundamentals required for anyone who wishes to progress to higher-level events. (See
      <a href="/wiki/Categories_of_rallies" title="Categories of rallies">
       Categories of rallies
      </a>
      .) Short special stage practice events on public roads are in some countries organized by the local clubs, with a permission of the local police, the community normally using the road, and the road authority. The public road is closed during these by the organisers or the police.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rally_courses">
       Rally courses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rally courses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fran%C3%A7ois_Duval_-_2007_Rallye_Deutschland.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fran%C3%A7ois_Duval_-_2007_Rallye_Deutschland.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Duval" title="Franois Duval">
         Franois Duval
        </a>
        takes a
        <a href="/wiki/Hairpin_turn" title="Hairpin turn">
         hairpin turn
        </a>
        on an asphalt-based special stage in
        <a href="/wiki/Rallye_Deutschland" title="Rallye Deutschland">
         Germany
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Rallying is also unique in its choice of where and when to race. Rallies take place on all surfaces and in all conditions:
      <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">
       asphalt
      </a>
      (tarmac),
      <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">
       gravel
      </a>
      , or snow and ice, sometimes more than one in a single rally, depending on the course and event. Rallies are also run every month of the year, in every climate from bitter cold to monsoonal rain. As a result of the drivers not knowing exactly what lies ahead, the lower traction available on dirt roads, and the driving characteristics of small cars, the drivers are much less visibly smooth than circuit racers, regularly sending the car literally flying over bumps, and sliding the cars out of corners.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg/220px-Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg/330px-Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg/440px-Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gr%C3%B6nholm_vs._Loeb.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Gr%C3%B6nholm" title="Marcus Grnholm">
         Marcus Grnholm
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Loeb" title="Sbastien Loeb">
         Sbastien Loeb
        </a>
        compete on a gravel-based super special stage in
        <a href="/wiki/Rally_Argentina" title="Rally Argentina">
         Argentina
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A typical rally course consists of a sequence of relatively short (up to about 50km (31mi)), timed "
      <a href="/wiki/Special_stage_(rallying)" title="Special stage (rallying)">
       special stages
      </a>
      " where the actual competition takes place, and untimed "transport stages" where the rally cars must be driven under their own power to the next competitive stage within a generous time limit. Rally cars are thus unlike virtually any other top-line racing cars in that they retain the ability to run at normal driving speeds, and indeed are registered for street travel. Some events contain "super special stages" where two competing cars set off on two parallel tracks (often small enough to fit in a football stadium), giving the illusion they are circuit racing head to head. Run over a day, a weekend, or more, the winner of the event has the lowest combined special and super special stage times. Given the short distances of super special stages compared to the regular special stages and consequent near-identical times for the frontrunning cars, it is very rare for these spectator-oriented stages to decide rally results, though it is a well-known axiom that a team cannot win the rally at the super special, but they can certainly lose it.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pacenotes_and_reconnaissance">
       Pacenotes and reconnaissance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rallying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pacenotes and reconnaissance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pacenotes" title="Pacenotes">
       Pacenotes
      </a>
      are a unique and major tool in modern rallying. Television spectators will occasionally notice the voice of a co-driver in mid-race reading the pacenotes over the car's internal intercom. These pacenotes provide a detailed description of the course and allow the driver to predict conditions ahead and prepare for various course conditions such as turns and jumps.
     </p>
     <p>
      In many rallies, including those of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">
       World Rally Championship
      </a>
      (WRC), drivers are allowed to run on the stages of the course before competition and create their own pacenotes. This process is called reconnaissance or recce. During reconnaissance, the co-driver writes down shorthand notes (the pacenotes) on how to best drive the stage. Usually the drivers call out the turns and road conditions for the co-drivers to write down. These pacenotes are read aloud through an internal intercom system during the actual race, allowing the driver to anticipate the upcoming terrain and thus take the course as fast as possible.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other rallies provide organizer-created "route notes" also referred to as "stage notes" and disallow reconnaissance and use of other pacenotes. These notes are usually created using a predetermined pacenote format, from which a co-driver can optionally add comments or transpose into other pacenote notations. Many North American rallies do not conduct reconnaissance but provide stage notes through the use of the
      <a href="/wiki/Jemba_Inertia_Notes_System" title="Jemba Inertia Notes System">
       Jemba Inertia Notes System
      </a>
      , due to time and budget constraints.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the past, most rally courses were not allowed to be scanned prior to the race, and the co-drivers used only maps supplied by the organization. The exact route of the rally often remained secret until race day. Modern rallies have mostly converted to using organizer-supplied notes or allowing full reconnaissance, as opposed to racing the stages blindly. This change has been brought on in large part due to competitor demand.
     </p>

     <p>
      In the wake of the ever more advanced rally cars of the 21st century is a trend towards historic rallying (also known as
      <a href="/wiki/Classic_rally" title="Classic rally">
       classic rallying
      </a>
      ), in which older cars compete under older rules.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is a popular sport and even attracts some previous drivers back into the sport. Many who enter, however, have started their competition careers in historic rallying.
     </p>
