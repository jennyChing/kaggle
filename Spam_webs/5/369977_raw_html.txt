               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 Last winter, I persuaded my husband, Paul, to leave our home of 31 years near Portland, Oregon, to live in Argentina for three months. I needed a break from Oregon winters and a shakeup from sitting at the same desk, taking the same runs, and shopping at the same grocery store. Plus, our youngest child was off to college. Wed bankrolled our kids to have adventures in far corners of the world and, frankly, I was jealous. It was time for mom and dad to leave the nest.
                </p>
               </div>

               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 Were both self-employed and can work anywhere theres an Internet connection, so we decided to give the idea a three-month trial. We learned a lot about setting up shop in another country. But mostly, we learned that technology makes it possible to live and work just about anywhere.
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>

                <p>
                 We turned to the Internet to get started on this scary, exciting adventure. Our selection criteria: to be in a Spanish-speaking country, in relatively the same time zone as our kids and clients, and in a city big enough to have reliable, high-speed Internet. We researched South American countries, climates, and outdoor opportunities and ended up selecting Bariloche, Argentina (in northern Patagonia), as our home away from home.
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 Next: lodging. After searching rental houses in Bariloche, we found and rented a small house spitting distance from Lago Nahuel Huapi. Then we swallowed hard and bought our plane tickets. You can change your life with just a few mouse-clicks. It was exhilarating.
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 Days after storing the Christmas decorations, we stepped on a plane, suitcases crammed with office paraphernalia and hiking gear, and flew 8,000 miles to the other side of the world. We were actually doing this. More than 30 hours later, we stepped into our little Patagonian hobbit house. It was even tinier and cuter than it had looked on the Internet, with a big stone fireplace, a little wooden door, and winding wisteria along the roof. And it was 80 degrees outside.
                </p>

However, the main challenge was that I had planned to use a service to make phone calls over the Internet, but the quality of the Internet connection in our rented house was nowhere near fast enough for hour-long phone interviews, which are critical in my work. After a few bullet-sweating calls riddled with distortions, gaps, and drops (with me envisioning my business going up in flames in Argentina), I realized that the cell phone was my best fallback option.

The delicious medialuna (croissants) from the little bakery around the corner. Riding public buses. Learning to cook with cuts of meat youd never see in the States. Getting an 8,000-mile perspective on our lives. We came home grateful, refreshed, and aware of and in love with a whole new swath of the world.

                <p>
                 Its really not difficult. You already use technology to bring the wide world into your home. Now use it to take you into the wide worldto live abroad or take a trip youve always dreamed of. My tips and advice for sojourning far from home:
                </p>

              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <ol>
                 <li>
                  Do your research about Internet quality and other technology needs, but also have backup plans.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Research your cellular carriers
                  <a href="http://www.att.com/shop/wireless/international/roaming.html#fbid=veClW0PHSnc" target="_blank">
                   international packages
                  </a>
                  and find one that works for your needs and budget. Check your average domestic data usage on your previous bills. Then, consider how long youll be away, and how much data you might use. This handy
                  <a href="http://www.att.com/att/datacalculator/#fbid=6SycH9LzCzc" target="_blank">
                   data calculator
                  </a>
                  might help you decide.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Your carriers
                  <a href="http://www.att.com/shop/wireless/international/roaming.html#fbid=veClW0PHSnc" target="_blank">
                   international packages
                  </a>
                  may also include messaging, and this can also offer considerable savings when youre traveling abroad.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  If you have a computer and Internet connection, youll never feel that far from home. We did video calls with our kids at least once a week and kept up with friends and family on Facebook, posting our own
                  <a href="http://janeandpaulinbariloche.wordpress.com/" target="_blank">
                   blogs
                  </a>
                  there.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Worried about the home you leave behind? A wonderful young woman housesat our home and pets while we were gone. Through websites such as
                  <a href="http://www.housecarers.com/" target="_blank">
                   housecarers.com
                  </a>
                  , you can find someone to sit your house or even swap houses.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  If youre going to work during your time away, carve out time for fun. Although having Internet-based jobs made this trip possible, having to work once there was frankly a drag. We took Fridays off, but next time well work two months and play one month.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Just do it! If your heart is telling you to go for it but your head is asking, What will the neighbors think? then you need to go.
                 </li>
