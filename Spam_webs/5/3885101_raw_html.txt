       <h1>
        One step closer to controlling nuclear fusion
       </h1>
       <h5 class="data">
        January 13, 2012 
                      By Nicolas Gurin
       </h5>
      </header>
      <section class="content-holder content-table content-story">
       <!--begin:content-->
       <section class="news-content">
        <!--begin:article-block-->
        <section class="content article-block">
         <article>
          <div class="first-block">
           <figure class="image-block">
            <div class="image-block-ins">
             <a href="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/gfx/news/hires/2012/onestepclose.jpg" rel="lightbox" title="">
              <img alt="One step closer to controlling nuclear fusion" src="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/csz/news/800/2012/onestepclose.jpg"/>
             </a>
            </div>
            <figcaption class="image-block-caption">
             Confined chamber for the plasma. Credit: EPFL
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            Using a heating system, physicists have succeeded for the first time in preventing the development of instabilities in an efficient alternative way relevant to a future nuclear fusion reactor. Its an important step forward in the effort to build the future ITER reactor.
           </p>
          </div>
          <!-- 1st block -->
          <div class="article-banner first-banner gtext-ad">
           <script language="JavaScript">
            &lt;!-- 
            var google_adnum = 0;
            var ad_channel_cmn = '+0559369967', google_hints="";
            
            OA_show(61);
        --&gt;
           </script>
          </div>
          <p>
           Scientists have achieved a milestone: they have managed to stop the growth of instabilities inside a nuclear fusion reactor. How? Heres a look at this energy source, which despite being challenging to control, is nevertheless extremely promising.
          </p>
          <figure class="image-block">
           <div class="image-block-ins">
            <a href="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/gfx/news/hires/2012/1-onestepclose.jpg" rel="lightbox" title="">
             <img alt="One step closer to controlling nuclear fusion" src="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/csz/news/800/2012/1-onestepclose.jpg"/>
            </a>
           </div>
          </figure>
          <p>
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/nuclear+fusion/" rel="tag">
            Nuclear fusion
           </a>
           is an attempt to reproduce the energy of the Sun in an Earth-based reactor system. When gas is heated to several million degrees, it becomes
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/plasma/" rel="tag">
            plasma
           </a>
           . Sometimes in the plasma, an instability will appear and grow large enough to perturb the plasma, making it vibrate despite the presence of the magnetic field in which it is contained. If the plasma touches the walls of the reactor, it will cool rapidly and create large electromagnetic forces within the structure of the machine.
          </p>
          <p>
           The challenge is to reduce the instabilities deep within in the interior of the plasma so that they dont amplify, while at the same time allowing the reactor to continue to function normally. Thus it is necessary to work within the specific configuration of these fusion reactors, where the plasma is strongly confined by a magnetic field. By adjusting an antenna that emits electromagnetic radiation, physicists from EPFLs Center for Research in Plasma Physics were able to quench the instabilities when they appear, in the precise region where they are forming, and without perturbing the rest of the installation.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            From theory to practice
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           The physicists first conducted simulations to verify the extent to which specific radiation frequencies and locations of application would suppress the growth of instabilities. Then they carried out tests to confirm their calculations. The beauty of their approach is that they were able to use antennas that are used as part of the system to heat the plasma, and that are already present in the Joint European Torus (JET), the largest reactor currently in use. Surprisingly, the simulations and the tests showed that heating and instability suppression can be combined, by aiming the radiation slightly off-center in the plasma.
          </p>
          <p>
           The next step will be to add a detector system that will make it possible to neutralize instabilities in real time over longer time periods. These improvements can then be implemented in the ITER
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/fusion+reactor/" rel="tag">
            fusion reactor
           </a>
           , currently in development in Southern France.
          </p>

            Taming thermonuclear plasma with a snowflake

          </p>
