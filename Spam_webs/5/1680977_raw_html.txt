     <p>
      With regard to
      <a href="/wiki/Warez" title="Warez">
       warez
      </a>
      groups or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement_of_audio-visual_works" title="Copyright infringement of audio-visual works">
       organized piracy groups
      </a>
      , a movie is usually released in several formats and different versions because the primary sources used by a group for a particular movie may vary. Pirated movies are primarily released by these organized groups, commonly referred to as
      <i>
       scene
      </i>
      groups or
      <i>
       warez groups
      </i>
      . The first release of a movie is usually of a lower quality (due to a lack of sources), and is eventually replaced with higher-quality releases as better sources become available.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pirated_movie_release_types&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cam_(movie_piracy)" title="Cam (movie piracy)">
       Cam
      </a>
      releases were the early attempts at movie piracy which were implemented by recording the on-screen projection of a movie in a cinema. This enabled groups to pirate movies which were in their theatrical period (not released for personal entertainment). But because these releases often suffered distinctly low quality and required undetected videotaping in movie theaters, alternative methods were sought.
     </p>
     <p>
      Beginning in 1998, feature films began to be released on the internet by warez groups prior to their theatrical release. These pirated versions usually came in the form of VCD or SVCD. A prime example was the release of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/American_Pie_(film)" title="American Pie (film)">
        American Pie
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is notable for three reasons:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       It was released in an uncensored workprint format. The later theatrical release was cut down by several minutes and had scenes reworked to avoid nudity to pass
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">
        MPAA
       </a>
       guidelines.
      </li>
      <li>
       It was released nearly two months prior to its release in theaters (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNN_Headline_News" title="CNN Headline News">
        CNN Headline News
       </a>
       reported on its early release).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       It was listed by the movie company as one of the reasons it released an
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_picture_rating_system" title="Motion picture rating system">
        Unrated
       </a>
       DVD edition.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      In October 1999,
      <a href="/wiki/DeCSS" title="DeCSS">
       DeCSS
      </a>
      was released. This program allowed anyone to remove the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Content-scrambling_system" title="Content-scrambling system">
       CSS
      </a>
      encryption on a DVD. Although its authors only intended the software to be used for playback purposes,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      it also meant that one could decode the content perfectly for
      <a href="/wiki/Ripping" title="Ripping">
       ripping
      </a>
      ; combined with the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/DivX" title="DivX">
        DivX
       </a>
       3.11 Alpha
      </i>
      codec released shortly after, the new codec increased video quality from near
      <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">
       VHS
      </a>
      to almost DVD quality when encoding from a DVD source.
     </p>
     <p>
      The early DivX releases were mostly internal for group use, but once the codec spread, it became accepted as a standard and quickly became the most widely used format for the scene. With help from associates who either worked for a
      <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">
       movie theater
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_production" title="Movie production">
       movie production
      </a>
      company, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_rental" title="Video rental">
       video rental
      </a>
      company, groups were supplied with massive amounts of material, and new releases began appearing at a very fast pace. When version 4.0 of DivX was released, the codec went commercial and the need for a free codec,
      <a href="/wiki/Xvid" title="Xvid">
       Xvid
      </a>
      (then called "XviD", "DivX" backwards), was created. Today, Xvid has replaced DivX almost entirely. Although the DivX codec has evolved from version 4 to 7.0 during this time, it is banned
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the warez scene due to its commercial nature.
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 2012, a consortium of popular piracy groups officially announced
      <a href="/wiki/X264" title="X264">
       x264
      </a>
      , the free
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.264" title="H.264">
       H.264
      </a>
      codec, as the new standard for releases,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      replacing the previous format, which was Xvid wrapped in an
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_Video_Interleave" title="Audio Video Interleave">
       AVI
      </a>
      container. The move to H.264 also obsoletes AVI in favor of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MP4" title="MP4">
       MP4
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Matroska" title="Matroska">
       Matroska
      </a>
      , although AVI video files are still common.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Release_formats">
       Release formats
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       <td colspan="3">
        A copy made in a cinema using a
        <a href="/wiki/Camcorder" title="Camcorder">
         camcorder
        </a>
        or mobile phone. The sound source is the camera microphone. Cam rips can quickly appear online after the first preview or premiere of the film. The quality ranges from terrible to very good, depending on the group of persons performing the recording and the resolution of the camera used. The main disadvantage of this is the sound quality. The microphone does not only record the sound from the movie, but also the background sound in the cinema. The camera can also record movements and audio of the audience in the theater, for instance, when someone stands up in front of the screen, or when the audience laughs at a funny moment in the movie.
       </td>
      </tr>

        A
        <a href="/wiki/Telesync" title="Telesync">

        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">
         movie theater
        </a>
        , sometimes filmed using a professional camera on a tripod in the projection booth. The main difference between a CAM and TS copy is that the audio of a TS is captured with a direct connection to the sound source (often an
        <a href="/wiki/Microbroadcasting" title="Microbroadcasting">
         FM microbroadcast
        </a>
        provided for the hearing-impaired, or from a drive-in theater). Often, a
        <i>
         cam
        </i>
        is mislabeled as a telesync.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>

        <a href="/wiki/Workprint" title="Workprint">

          </span>

         WORKPRINT
        </i>

        Very rare
       </td>

      <tr>
       <td colspan="3">
        A copy made from an unfinished version of a film produced by the studio. Typically a workprint has missing effects and overlays, and often differs from its theatrical release. Some workprints have a time index marker running in a corner or on the top edge; some may also include a watermark. A workprint might be an uncut version, and missing some material that would appear in the final movie (or including scenes later cut).

      <tr>
       <td colspan="3">
        Similar to DVD-Rip, only the source is a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">
         Blu-ray Disc
        </a>
        . A BD/BRRip in DVD-Rip size often looks better than a same-size DVD rip because encoders have better source material. A common misconception among downloaders is that BDRip and BRRip are the same thing. They differ in that a BDRip comes directly from the Blu-ray source, while a BRRip is encoded from a pre-release, usually from a 1080p BDRip from another group. BDRips are available in DVD-Rip sized releases (commonly 700 MB and 1.4 GB) encoded in Xvid or x264, as well as larger DVD5 or DVD9 (often 4.5 GB or larger, depending on length and quality) sized releases encoded in x264.
        <p>
         BD5 or BD9 are also available, which are slightly smaller than their counterpart DVD5/DVD9 releases. They are AVCHD compatible using the BD folder structure, and are intended to be burnt onto DVDs to play in AVCHD compatible Blu-ray players. More recent types, probably associated with the use of newsgroups and cheaper storage at home, are complete Blu-ray copies (images). They are commonly referred to as BD25 or BD50 and may or may not be remuxed (but not transcoded). (Remuxing is keeping the original video, but eliminating audio tracks, and/or adding audio tracks in other languages.)
        </p>
        <p>
         BD/BRRips come in various versions: the m-720p (or mini 720p), which is a compressed version of a 720p and usually weighs around 23 GB; the 720p, which usually weighs around 47 GB and is the most downloaded form of BDRip; the m-1080p (or mini 1080p), which usually weighs a little bit more than 720p; and the 1080p, which can weigh from 8 GB to sizes as big as 4060 GB. There are also mHD (or mini HD) versions available, which are encoded in lower resolution and are smaller in size.
        </p>
