     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Bloody Chamber
       </b>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       <b>
        The Bloody Chamber and Other Stories
       </b>
      </i>
      ) is a collection of
      <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">
       short fiction
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Angela_Carter" title="Angela Carter">
       Angela Carter
      </a>
      . It was first published in the United Kingdom in 1979 by
      <a href="/wiki/Victor_Gollancz_Ltd" title="Victor Gollancz Ltd">
       Gollancz
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and won the
      <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Prize_for_Literature" title="Cheltenham Prize for Literature">
       Cheltenham Festival Literary Prize
      </a>
      . All of the stories share a common theme of being closely based upon
      <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">
       fairytales
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
       folk tales
      </a>
      . However, Angela Carter has stated:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       My intention was not to do 'versions' or, as the American edition of the book said, horribly, 'adult' fairy tales, but to extract the latent content from the traditional stories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The anthology contains ten stories: "The Bloody Chamber", "The Courtship of Mr Lyon", "The Tiger's Bride", "Puss-in-Boots", "The Erl-King", "The Snow Child", "The Lady of the House of Love", "The Werewolf", "The Company of Wolves" and "Wolf-Alice".
     </p>
     <p>
      The tales vary greatly in length, with the
      <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">
       novelette
      </a>
      "The Bloody Chamber" being "more than twice the length of any of the other stories, and more than thirty times the length of the shortest [the
      <a href="/wiki/Vignette_(literature)" title="Vignette (literature)">
       vignette
      </a>
      "The Snow Child"]."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simpson_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Simpson-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :viii
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The anthology's contents are also reprinted in Carter's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Burning_Your_Boats" title="Burning Your Boats">
        Burning Your Boats
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Story_summaries">
       Story summaries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Story summaries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The stories within "The Bloody Chamber" are explicitly based on fairy tales. Carter was no doubt inspired by the works of author and fairytale collector
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Perrault" title="Charles Perrault">
       Charles Perrault
      </a>
      , whose fairy tales she had translated shortly beforehand.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bloody_Chamber">
       The Bloody Chamber
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Bloody Chamber">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      (based on
      <a href="/wiki/Bluebeard" title="Bluebeard">
       Bluebeard
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      A teenage girl marries an older, wealthy French Marquis, whom she does not love. When he takes her to his castle, she learns that he enjoys sadistic pornography and takes pleasure in her embarrassment. She is a talented pianist, and a young man, a blind piano tuner, hears her music and falls in love with her. The woman's husband tells her that he must leave on a business trip and forbids her to enter one particular room while he is away. She enters the room in his absence and realises the full extent of his perverse and murderous tendencies when she discovers the bodies of his previous wives. Marquis returns home prematurely, discovers that she has entered the room and proceeds to try to add her to his collection of corpses through beheading. The brave piano tuner is willing to stay with her even though he knows he will not be able to save her. She is saved at the last moment at the end of the story by her mother, who shoots the Marquis just as he is about to murder the girl. The girl, her mother and the piano tuner go on to live together, and the girl uses her now considerable fortune to convert the castle into a school for blind children.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Courtship_of_Mr_Lyon">
       The Courtship of Mr Lyon
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Courtship of Mr Lyon">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      (based on
      <a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast" title="Beauty and the Beast">
       Beauty and the Beast
      </a>
       the concept of the Beast as a lion-like figure is a popular one, most notably in the
      <a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast_(1946_film)" title="Beauty and the Beast (1946 film)">
       French film version of 1946
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      Beauty's father, after experiencing car trouble, takes advantage of a stranger's hospitality. However, his benefactor  the Beast  takes umbrage when he steals a miraculous white rose for his beloved daughter. Beauty becomes the guest of the
      <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">
       leonine
      </a>
      Beast, and the Beast aids her father in getting his fortune back. Beauty later joins her father in London, where she almost forgets the Beast, causing him to wither away from heartache. When Beauty learns that he is dying, she returns, saving him. Beauty and the Beast disclose their love for one another and the Beast's humanity is revealed. They live happily ever after.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Tiger.27s_Bride">
       The Tiger's Bride
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Tiger's Bride">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      (also based on
      <a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast" title="Beauty and the Beast">
       Beauty and the Beast
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      A woman moves in with a mysterious, masked "Milord," the Beast, after her father loses her to him in a game of cards. Milord is eventually revealed to be a tiger. In a reversal of the ending of "The Courtship of Mr Lyon", the heroine transforms at the end into a glorious tiger who is the proper mate to the Beast, who will from now on be true to his own nature and not disguise himself as a human.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Puss-in-Boots">
       Puss-in-Boots
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_themes">
       Style and themes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Style and themes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Angela Carter's short stories challenge the way women are represented in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" title="Fairy tales">
       fairy tales
      </a>
      , yet retain an air of tradition and convention through her voluptuously descriptive prose. For example, in the opening tale "The Bloody Chamber" which is a retelling of
      <a href="/wiki/Bluebeard" title="Bluebeard">
       Bluebeard
      </a>
      , Carter plays with the conventions of canonical
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" title="Fairy tales">
       fairy tales
      </a>
      ; instead of the heroine being rescued by the stereotypical male hero, she is rescued by her mother.
     </p>
     <p>
      Carter effectively draws out the theme of feminism by contrasting traditional elements of
      <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">
       Gothic fiction
      </a>
       which usually depicted female characters as weak and helpless  with strong female protagonists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By contrasting the barren and horrific atmosphere found typically within the Gothic to the strong heroines of her story, Carter is able to create sexually liberated female characters that are set against the more traditional backdrop of the fairy tale. In doing so, Carter reinvents the outdated fairy tales and offers insight on the archetypes and stereotypes of women in these well-known and celebrated stories.
     </p>
     <p>
      The stories deal with themes of women's roles in relationships and marriage, their sexuality, coming of age and corruption. Stories such as "The Bloody Chamber" and "The Company of Wolves" explicitly deal with the horrific or corrupting aspects of marriage and/or sex and the balance of power within such relationships. Themes of female identity are explored in the "Beauty and the Beast" stories such as "The Tiger's Bride". In one instance, Beauty: the story's heroine, is described as removing the petals from a white rose as her father gambles her away, a seeming representation of the stripping away of the false layers of her personality to find her true identity; an image that finds a mirror in the story's fantastical conclusion.
     </p>
     <p>
      As a whole,
      <i>
       The Bloody Chamber
      </i>
      can be treated as a collection of short stories that speak to a bigger narrative that deals with issues of feminism and metamorphosis instead a set of individual tales. Although each particular narrative deals with a different set of characters, the 'oppressed female seeking liberation' is a common theme and concept that is explored throughout the collection. The characters seem to blend into each other and become indistinguishable from one another when recognising this theme in the text.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The stories are updated to more modern settings. The exact time periods remains vague, but they are clearly anchored intentionally. For example, in "The Bloody Chamber" the
      <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_telephone" title="Timeline of the telephone">
       existence of transatlantic telephone
      </a>
      implies a date 1930 or later. On the other hand, the mention of painters such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Moreau" title="Gustave Moreau">
       Gustave Moreau
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Odilon_Redon" title="Odilon Redon">
       Odilon Redon
      </a>
      , and of fashion designer
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Poiret" title="Paul Poiret">
       Paul Poiret
      </a>
      (who designs one of the heroine's gowns) all suggest a date before 1945. "The Lady of the House of Love" is clearly set on the eve of the First World War, and the young man's bicycle on which he arrives at the tradition-bound vampire's house is a symbol of the encroaching modernity which fundamentally altered European society after 1914.
     </p>

         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Several critical works have been published that focus on Carter's use of fairy tales in
      <i>
       The Bloody Chamber
      </i>
      and her other works
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the anthology is also frequently taught and studied in University literature courses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been used as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Assessment_and_Qualifications_Alliance" title="Assessment and Qualifications Alliance">
       AQA
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Literature" title="English Literature">
       English Literature
      </a>
      and Edexcel English Language/Literature syllabus for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-Levels" title="A-Levels">
       A-Levels
      </a>
      in some schools in the United Kingdom.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">
       Adaptations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Adaptations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">
       Radio
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Radio">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Carter later adapted "The Company of Wolves" and "Puss-in-Boots" into radio plays which were broadcast on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_3" title="BBC Radio 3">
       BBC Radio 3
      </a>
      in 1980 and 1982 respectively. The 1982 adaptation of "Puss in Boots" (as it was retitled) starred
      <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sachs" title="Andrew Sachs">
       Andrew Sachs
      </a>
      in the title role.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_Bell_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-a_Bell-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The scripts for both of these plays were published in Carter's
      <i>
       Come Unto These Yellow Sands
      </i>
      and later the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_works_published_posthumously" title="List of works published posthumously">
       posthumous
      </a>
      collection
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Curious_Room" title="The Curious Room">
        The Curious Room
       </a>
      </i>
      , which also included production notes.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Film">
       Film
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Film">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The 1984 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Company_of_Wolves" title="The Company of Wolves">
        The Company of Wolves
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Neil_Jordan" title="Neil Jordan">
       Neil Jordan
      </a>
      was based upon the werewolf stories in this collection, in particular the Little Red Riding Hood analogue "The Company of Wolves". Carter also directly contributed to the screenplay of this film, which bears close resemblance to her 1980 radio play adaptation of "The Company of Wolves." Carter's original screenplay for this film is published in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Curious_Room" title="The Curious Room">
        The Curious Room
       </a>
      </i>
      . Jordan and Carter also discussed producing a film adaptation of "Vampirella", the radio drama that became "The Lady of the House of Love", but this project was never released.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">
       Music video
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Music video">

       Punk
      </a>
      band
      <a href="/wiki/Daisy_Chainsaw" title="Daisy Chainsaw">
       Daisy Chainsaw
      </a>
      adapted the story of "The Lady of the House of Love" for their 1992 music video for "Hope Your Dreams Come True" (from the
      <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">
       EP
      </a>
      of the same name and also later the album
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleventeen_(Daisy_Chainsaw_album)" title="Eleventeen (Daisy Chainsaw album)">
        Eleventeen
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">

        <span>
         [

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">
       Theatre
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Bloody_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Theatre">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The stories within
      <i>
       The Bloody Chamber
      </i>
      are a popular subject for theatrical adaptation. The story "The Bloody Chamber" has been adapted for the theatre more than once, including a performance by the "Zoo District" which was accompanied by an
      <a href="/wiki/Amateur_film" title="Amateur film">
       amateur film
      </a>
      adaptation of "Wolf-Alice".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "The Company of Wolves" is also a popular subject for adaptation by amateur/student theatre groups (e.g. by a Welsh drama college
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2013, Melbourne's Malthouse Theatre presented a stage adaptation of The Bloody Chamber by writer Van Badham, directed by Matthew Lutton, with composers David Chisholm (scoring for three live harps) and Jethro Woodward (live and replayed electronic soundscore). Set and costumedesign were by Anna Cordingley with lighting designer Paul Jackson. Save for a relatively brief appearance by Shelly Lauman, the piece was in essence performed by Alison Whyte. The three harpists were Jacinta Dennett, Jess Fotinos, Yinuo Mu.
     </p>
