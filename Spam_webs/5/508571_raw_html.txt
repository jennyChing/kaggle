   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Wildcat formation
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search

    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Wildcat formation
      </b>
      describes a
      <a href="/wiki/Formation_(American_football)" title="Formation (American football)">
       formation
      </a>
      for the offense in
      <a href="/wiki/Gridiron_football" title="Gridiron football">
       gridiron football
      </a>
      in which the ball is snapped not to the quarterback but directly to a player of another position lined up at the quarterback position (in most systems, this is a
      <a href="/wiki/Running_back" title="Running back">
       running back
      </a>
      , but some playbooks have the
      <a href="/wiki/Wide_receiver" title="Wide receiver">
       wide receiver
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fullback_(American_football)" title="Fullback (American football)">
       fullback
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Tight_end" title="Tight end">
       tight end
      </a>
      taking the snap). The Wildcat features an unbalanced offensive line and looks to the defense like a
      <a href="/wiki/Sweep_(American_football)" title="Sweep (American football)">
       sweep
      </a>
      behind
      <a href="/wiki/Zone_blocking" title="Zone blocking">
       zone blocking
      </a>
      . A player moves across the formation prior to the snap. However, once this player crosses the position of the running back who will receive the snap, the play develops unlike the sweep.
     </p>
     <p>
      Defending against the Wildcat is straightforward, but the defense does not always identify that a play will use the Wildcat, and preparing to defend against it may take practice time away from other preparation. If a defense interprets the Wildcat as a regular sweep play to one side, it may reduce its defense of the weak side and make it easier for the running back to advance the ball by running to that side.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Wildcat is a gambit rather than an overall offensive philosophy. It can be a part of many offenses. For example, a
      <a href="/wiki/Option_offense#Types" title="Option offense">
       spread-option
      </a>
      offense might use the Wildcat formation to keep the defense guessing, or a
      <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_offense" title="West Coast offense">
       West Coast offense
      </a>
      may use the power-
      <a href="/wiki/I_formation" title="I formation">
       I formation
      </a>
      to threaten a powerful run attack.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Wildcat scheme was invented by Billy Ford and Ryan Wilson, and was originally called the "Dual" formation. The offensive coaching staff of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kansas_State_Wildcats" title="Kansas State Wildcats">
       Kansas State Wildcats
      </a>
      , namely including Bill Snyder and Del Miller, made significant contributions to the formation's development throughout the 1990s and 2000s and is often cited as being the formation's namesake. It has been used since the late 1990s at every level of the game, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">
       CFL
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NFL" title="NFL">
       NFL
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA">
       NCAA
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Intercollegiate_Athletics" title="National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics">
       NAIA
      </a>
      , and many high schools across North America. Specific coaching staffs have used it with various innovations and have given their versions a variety of names.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wildcat_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      One possible precursor to the wildcat formation was named the "
      <a href="/wiki/T_formation" title="T formation">
       wing-T
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is widely credited to being first implemented by Coach
      <a href="/wiki/Tubby_Raymond" title="Tubby Raymond">
       Tubby Raymond
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Fightin%27_Blue_Hens_football" title="Delaware Fightin' Blue Hens football">
       Delaware Fightin' Blue Hens football
      </a>
      team.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tubby Raymond later wrote a book on the innovative formation. The wildcat's similarity to the wing-T is the focus on series football, where the initial movements of every play look similar. For example, the wing-T makes use of motion across the formation as well in order to draw a reaction from the defense, but runs several different plays from the same look.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another possible precursor
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      to the wildcat is the offense of
      <a href="/wiki/Six-man_football" title="Six-man football">
       Six-Man Football
      </a>
      , a form of high school football, played mostly in rural West Texas and Montana, that was developed in 1934. In six-man, the person who receives the snap may not run the ball past the line of scrimmage. To bypass this limitation, teams often snap the ball to a receiver, who then tosses the ball to the potential passer. The passer may then throw the ball to a receiver or run with the ball himself.
     </p>
     <p>
      The virtue of having a
      <a href="/wiki/Running_back" title="Running back">
       running back
      </a>
      take the snap in the wildcat formation is that the rushing play is 11-on-11, although different variations have the running back hand off or throw the football.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In a standard football formation, when the quarterback stands watching, the offense operates 10-on-11 basis. The motion also presents the defense with an immediate threat to the outside that it must respect no matter what the offense decides to do with the
      <a href="/wiki/Football_(ball)" title="Football (ball)">
       football
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="High_school">
       High school
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wildcat_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: High school">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">
        The Wall Street Journal
       </a>
      </i>
      credited Hugh Wyatt, a longtime coach in the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">
       Pacific Northwest
      </a>
      , with naming the offense. Wyatt, coaching the
      <a href="/wiki/La_Center,_Washington" title="La Center, Washington">
       La Center
      </a>
      High School Wildcats, published an article in
      <i>
       Scholastic Coach and Athletic Director
      </i>
      magazine in 1998, where he explained his version of the offense, which relied on two wing backs and two backfield players directly behind the center alternating who would receive the snap. Many other high school football programs across the United States adopted Wyatt's Wildcat offense.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="College">
       College
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wildcat_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: College">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The wildcat was popularized on the college level by
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Snyder" title="Bill Snyder">
       Bill Snyder
      </a>
      , head coach of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kansas_State_Wildcats_football" title="Kansas State Wildcats football">
       Kansas State University Wildcats
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bishop_(gridiron_football)" title="Michael Bishop (gridiron football)">
       Michael Bishop
      </a>
      as quarterback in
      <a href="/wiki/1997_Kansas_State_Wildcats_football_team" title="1997 Kansas State Wildcats football team">
       1997
      </a>
      and 1998 when they made a run at the top of the national rankings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Bishop rushed for 1304 career yards in two seasons, including 748 yards on 177 carries during the '98 season. As stated in the previous article, this type of offense was the catalyst for Urban Meyer's offense during the start of his career. It was Meyer's success with quarterback
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Tebow" title="Tim Tebow">
       Tim Tebow
      </a>
      that helped the formation come to the forefront.
     </p>
     <p>
      The wildcat has been continued by current
      <a href="/wiki/Auburn_Tigers_football" title="Auburn Tigers football">
       Auburn
      </a>
      head coach
      <a href="/wiki/Gus_Malzahn" title="Gus Malzahn">
       Gus Malzahn
      </a>
      , and former
      <a href="/wiki/Ole_Miss_Rebels" title="Ole Miss Rebels">
       Ole Miss Rebels
      </a>
      offensive coordinator
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Lee_(quarterback)" title="David Lee (quarterback)">
       David Lee
      </a>
      when they were offensive coordinators for the
      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Razorbacks_football" title="Arkansas Razorbacks football">
       Arkansas Razorbacks
      </a>
      after seeing the success of Bill Snyder and Urban Meyer. In
      <a href="/wiki/2006_NCAA_Division_I_FBS_football_season" title="2006 NCAA Division I FBS football season">
       2006
      </a>
      , Malzahn was the offensive coordinator for the
      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Razorbacks_football" title="Arkansas Razorbacks football">
       Razorbacks
      </a>
      . Malzahn introduced the wildcat into the
      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Razorbacks_football" title="Arkansas Razorbacks football">
       Arkansas
      </a>
      offense. When Malzahn left for
      <a href="/wiki/Tulsa_Golden_Hurricane_football" title="Tulsa Golden Hurricane football">
       Tulsa
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/2007_NCAA_Division_I_FBS_football_season" title="2007 NCAA Division I FBS football season">
       2007
      </a>
      , Lee became the offensive coordinator for the
      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Razorbacks_football" title="Arkansas Razorbacks football">
       Razorbacks
      </a>
      . Both Malzahn and Lee ran a variation of the wildcat formation which prominently featured running backs
      <a href="/wiki/Darren_McFadden" title="Darren McFadden">
       Darren McFadden
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_Jones" title="Felix Jones">
       Felix Jones
      </a>
      . The wildcat formation was sometimes called the "wildhog" (in honor of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Razorback" title="Razorback">
       Razorback
      </a>
      mascot at the University of Arkansas) and subsequently rebranded as the "Wild Rebel"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      when Arkansas head coach
      <a href="/wiki/Houston_Nutt" title="Houston Nutt">
       Houston Nutt
      </a>
      went to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ole_Miss_Rebel_football" title="Ole Miss Rebel football">
       Ole Miss
      </a>
      as head coach (Ole Miss' mascot being the Rebels), and a variation involving a direct snap to a tight end has also been called the "Wild Turkey" popularized by the
      <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_Hokies" title="Virginia Tech Hokies">
       Virginia Tech Hokies
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Several other college teams have used the wildcat formation regularly, including the wildcats of
      <a href="/wiki/Kansas_State_Wildcats_football" title="Kansas State Wildcats football">
       Kansas State
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Wildcats_football" title="Kentucky Wildcats football">
       Kentucky
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Villanova_Wildcats_football" title="Villanova Wildcats football">
       Villanova
      </a>
      , as well as the
      <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Panthers_football" title="Pittsburgh Panthers football">
       Pitt Panthers
      </a>
      . Pitt had great success with the formation having star running back
      <a href="/wiki/LeSean_McCoy" title="LeSean McCoy">
       LeSean McCoy
      </a>
      or running back
      <a href="/wiki/LaRod_Stephens-Howling" title="LaRod Stephens-Howling">
       LaRod Stephens-Howling
      </a>
      take the snap. The Panthers scored numerous times from this formation during those years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Kentucky has successfully run the formation with wide receiver
      <a href="/wiki/Randall_Cobb_(American_football)" title="Randall Cobb (American football)">
       Randall Cobb
      </a>
      taking the snaps. This formation is frequently called the "WildCobb" and is often very successful given the fact that Cobb also started numerous games at quarterback for Kentucky during his first year. Villanova won the
      <a href="/wiki/2009_NCAA_Division_I_FCS_football_season" title="2009 NCAA Division I FCS football season">
       2009 FCS championship
      </a>
      with a multiple offense that included the wildcat, with wide receiver
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Szczur" title="Matt Szczur">
       Matt Szczur
      </a>
      taking the snap. Szczur scored a key touchdown in the Wildcats' semifinal against
      <a href="/wiki/2009_William_%26_Mary_Tribe_football_team" title="2009 William &amp; Mary Tribe football team">
       William &amp; Mary
      </a>
      out of the formation,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and made a number of big plays out of the wildcat against
      <a href="/wiki/Montana_Grizzlies_football" title="Montana Grizzlies football">
       Montana
      </a>
      in the final.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/UCF_Knights_football" title="UCF Knights football">
       UCF
      </a>
      also uses a wildcat formation they call the "Wild Knight".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knight_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-knight-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was originally intended to be run by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rob_Calabrese&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rob Calabrese (page does not exist)">
       Rob Calabrese
      </a>
      , even after he lost the starting job in 2010 to
      <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Godfrey" title="Jeff Godfrey">
       Jeff Godfrey
      </a>
      , but he tore his
      <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cruciate_ligament" title="Anterior cruciate ligament">
       ACL
      </a>
      using the play to score a rushing touchdown against
      <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Thundering_Herd_football" title="Marshall Thundering Herd football">
       Marshall
      </a>
      on October 13, 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the time, most agreed that Calabrese was effective at running the Wild Knight formation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knight_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-knight-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="National_Football_League">
       National Football League
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wildcat_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National Football League">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1716" data-file-width="2576" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg/250px-081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg/375px-081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg/500px-081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:081116Saints-Chiefs02.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Former Chiefs running back
        <a href="/wiki/Larry_Johnson_(American_football)" title="Larry Johnson (American football)">
         Larry Johnson
        </a>
        lines up at the quarterback position in the wildcat formation, 2008.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The wildcat formation made an appearance in 1998, when
      <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Vikings" title="Minnesota Vikings">
       Minnesota Vikings
      </a>
      ' offensive coordinator
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Billick" title="Brian Billick">
       Brian Billick
      </a>
      began employing formations where QB
      <a href="/wiki/Randall_Cunningham" title="Randall Cunningham">
       Randall Cunningham
      </a>
      lined-up as a wide receiver and third-down specialist
      <a href="/wiki/David_Palmer_(American_football)" title="David Palmer (American football)">
       David Palmer
      </a>
      took the direct snap from the center with the option to pass or run.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_NFL_playoffs" title="199899 NFL playoffs">
       1998 NFC Championship
      </a>
      , with 7:58 to go in the third quarter, on a second and 5 play, the
      <a href="/wiki/1998_Atlanta_Falcons_season" title="1998 Atlanta Falcons season">
       Atlanta Falcons
      </a>
      deployed quarterback
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Chandler" title="Chris Chandler">
       Chris Chandler
      </a>
      wide left as a receiver while receiver
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Dwight" title="Tim Dwight">
       Tim Dwight
      </a>
      took a direct snap and ran 20 yards for a first down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a December 24, 2006 game between the
      <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Panthers" title="Carolina Panthers">
       Carolina Panthers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Falcons" title="Atlanta Falcons">
       Atlanta Falcons
      </a>
      , the Panthers deployed a formation without a quarterback and snapped the ball directly to running back
      <a href="/wiki/DeAngelo_Williams" title="DeAngelo Williams">
       DeAngelo Williams
      </a>
      for much of the game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Panthers ran the ballmostly in this formationfor the first twelve plays of the opening drive, and ran the ball 52 times, with only 7 passing plays. The
      <a href="/wiki/Offensive_coordinator" title="Offensive coordinator">
       offensive coordinator
      </a>
      of the Carolina Panthers at the time,
      <a href="/wiki/Dan_Henning" title="Dan Henning">
       Dan Henning
      </a>
      , later developed this concept into the wildcat as the offensive coordinator for the
      <a href="/wiki/Miami_Dolphins" title="Miami Dolphins">
       Miami Dolphins
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Relying on the experience of quarterbacks coach
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Lee_(quarterback)" title="David Lee (quarterback)">
       David Lee
      </a>
      who had run the scheme at Arkansas, the
      <a href="/wiki/2008_Miami_Dolphins_season" title="2008 Miami Dolphins season">
       2008 Miami Dolphins
      </a>
      under Henning implemented the wildcat offense beginning in the third game of the 2008 season with great success, instigating a wider trend throughout the NFL.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Dolphins started the wildcat trend in the NFL lining up either running back
      <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Brown" title="Ronnie Brown">
       Ronnie Brown
      </a>
      (in most cases) or
      <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Williams" title="Ricky Williams">
       Ricky Williams
      </a>
      to take a shotgun snap with the option of handing off, running, or throwing. Through eleven games, the wildcat averaged over seven yards per play for the Dolphins. "It could be the single wing, it could be the Delaware split buck business that they used to do," Dolphins offensive coordinator Dan Henning said. "It comes from all of that."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On September 21, 2008, the Miami Dolphins used the wildcat offense against the
      <a href="/wiki/New_England_Patriots" title="New England Patriots">
       New England Patriots
      </a>
      on six plays, which produced 5 touchdowns (four rushing and one passingfrom Ronnie Brown himself) in a 3813 upset victory.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game,_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1970" data-file-width="3143" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game%2C_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg/220px-Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game%2C_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game%2C_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg/330px-Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game%2C_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game%2C_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg/440px-Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game%2C_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ronnie_Brown_throwing_out_of_Wildcat_Jets-Dolphin_game,_Nov_2009_-_039.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Brown" title="Ronnie Brown">
         Ronnie Brown
        </a>
        (far right) of the
        <a href="/wiki/Miami_Dolphins" title="Miami Dolphins">
         Miami Dolphins
        </a>
        passing out of the wildcat in November 2009.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      As the popularity of the wildcat spread during the
      <a href="/wiki/2008_NFL_season" title="2008 NFL season">
       2008 NFL season
      </a>
      , several teams began instituting it as a part of their playbook.
     </p>
     <p>
      Defending plays from the wildcat requires linemen and linebackers to know and execute their own assignments without over-pursuing what may turn into a fake or a reverse. The formation's initial success in 2008 can be attributed in part to surprisedefenses had not practiced their countermeasures against such an unusual offensive strategy.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Since then, most teams are well prepared to stop the wildcat; an example came in November 2008 when the Patriots traveled to Miami nine weeks after the Dolphins win in
      <a href="/wiki/Gillette_Stadium" title="Gillette Stadium">
       Foxboro
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Belichick" title="Bill Belichick">
       Bill Belichick
      </a>
      's defense limited the wildcat to just 27 yards and forced the Dolphins to try a conventional passing attack;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the game lead changed six times but the Patriots wore out the Dolphins with a 4828 win.
     </p>
     <p>
      Though defenses now understand how to stop the wildcat, it does not mean the formation is no longer useful. A defense's practice time is finite. Opponents who prepare to stop the wildcat have less time available to prepare for other offensive approaches. Many teams admit to spending an inordinate amount of time having to prepare for this scheme.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other teams that use the wildcat formation in the NFL may use different names for their versions. For example, the Carolina Panthers call their version the 'Mountaineer formation', named after the
      <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_State_Mountaineers_football" title="Appalachian State Mountaineers football">
       Appalachian State Mountaineers
      </a>
      , the alma mater of their wildcat quarterback
      <a href="/wiki/Armanti_Edwards" title="Armanti Edwards">
       Armanti Edwards
      </a>
      , who played quarterback for the Mountaineers. The
      <a href="/wiki/Denver_Broncos" title="Denver Broncos">
       Denver Broncos
      </a>
      utilize 'Wild Horses', developed in 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The New York Jets referred to their version as the Tigercat formation in reference to
      <a href="/wiki/Brad_Smith_(American_football)" title="Brad Smith (American football)">
       Brad Smith
      </a>
      having attended the University of Missouri when Smith played for New York from 20092010. The 2011 Minnesota Vikings referred to their formation as the "Blazer package" which employed former
      <a href="/wiki/UAB_Blazers_football" title="UAB Blazers football">
       UAB Blazers
      </a>
      quarterback
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Webb" title="Joe Webb">
       Joe Webb
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_Football_League">
       Canadian Football League
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wildcat_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canadian Football League">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Until the
      <a href="/wiki/2009_CFL_season" title="2009 CFL season">
       2009 season
      </a>
      , a technicality in the league rules made the wildcat offense illegal; essentially, the rule stated that a designated quarterback must be in position to take all snaps. This has since been changed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>

      </sup>
     </p>
