     <p>
      <b>
       George Ivanovich Gurdjieff
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'g' in 'guy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'ir' in 'bird'">
           r
          </span>
          <span title="/d/ 'j' in 'jam'">
           d
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">
           i
          </span>
          <span title="// secondary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'e' in 'bed'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'f' in 'find'">
           f
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      (January 13, 18661877? - October 29, 1949
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), also commonly referred to as
      <b>
       Georges Ivanovich Gurdjieff
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       G. I. Gurdjieff
      </b>
      , was an influential
      <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">
       spiritual
      </a>
      teacher of the early to mid-20th century who taught that most humans live their lives in a state of hypnotic "waking sleep", but that it is possible to transcend to a higher state of consciousness and achieve full human potential. Gurdjieff developed a method for doing so, calling his discipline "The Work"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (connoting "work on oneself") or "the Method".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to his principles and instructions,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gurdjieff's method for awakening one's consciousness is different from that of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fakir" title="Fakir">
       fakir
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">
       monk
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Yogi" title="Yogi">
       yogi
      </a>
      , so his discipline is also called (originally) the "
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Way" title="Fourth Way">
       Fourth Way
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At one point, he described his teaching as being "
      <a href="/wiki/Esoteric_Christianity" title="Esoteric Christianity">
       esoteric Christianity
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At different times in his life, Gurdjieff formed and closed various schools around the world to teach The Work. He claimed that the teachings he brought to the West from his own experiences and early travels expressed the truth found in ancient religions and wisdom teachings relating to
      <a href="/wiki/Self-awareness" title="Self-awareness">
       self-awareness
      </a>
      in people's daily lives and humanity's place in the universe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The title of his third series of writings,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Is_Real_Only_Then,_When_%27I_Am%27" title="Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'">
        Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'
       </a>
      </i>
      , expresses the essence
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      of his teachings. His complete series of books is entitled
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All_and_Everything" title="All and Everything">
        All and Everything
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">
       Early years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      George Ivanovich Gurdjieff
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shirley_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Shirley-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
       Armenian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="hy" xml:lang="hy">
        
      </span>
      ) was born to a
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Greeks" title="Caucasus Greeks">
       Caucasus Greek
      </a>
      father called   (Yiannis Georgiades)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and an
      <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">
       Armenian
      </a>
      mother Tavrizovy-Bagratouni (  ) in Alexandropol (now
      <a href="/wiki/Gyumri" title="Gyumri">
       Gyumri
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">
       Armenia
      </a>
      ), then part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">
       Russian Empire
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcaucasus" title="Transcaucasus">
       Transcaucasus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Challenger_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Challenger-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The name Gurdjieff represents a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russified" title="Russified">
       Russified
      </a>
      form of the
      <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Greek" title="Pontic Greek">
       Pontic Greek
      </a>
      surname "Georgiades" (
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
       
      </span>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shirley_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Shirley-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The exact date of his birth remains unknown; conjectures range from 1866 to 1877. Some authors (such as Moore) argue for 1866. Both Olga de Hartmannthe woman Gurdjieff called "the first friend of my inner life"and Louise Goepfert March, Gurdjieff's secretary in the early thirties, believed that Gurdjieff was born in 1872. A passport gave a birthdate of November 28, 1877, but he once stated that he was born at the stroke of midnight at the beginning of New Year's Day (Julian calendar). Although the dates of his birth vary, the year of 1872 is inscribed in a plate on the grave-marker at the Cimetire d'Avon, in the Prefecture of Paris, France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff spent his childhood in
      <a href="/wiki/Kars" title="Kars">
       Kars
      </a>
      , which between 1878 and 1918 was the administrative capital of the Russian ruled
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcaucasus" title="Transcaucasus">
       Transcaucasus
      </a>
      province of
      <a href="/wiki/Kars_Oblast" title="Kars Oblast">
       Kars Oblast
      </a>
      , a border region recently captured from the
      <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
       Ottoman Empire
      </a>
      consisting of extensive grassy plateau-steppe and high mountains with a multi-ethnic and multi-confessional population that had a history of respect for travelling mystics and holy men and for religious syncretism and conversion. Both the city of Kars and the surrounding territory was home to an extremely diverse population:
      <a href="/wiki/Kars_Oblast" title="Kars Oblast">
       Kars Oblast
      </a>
      was home to
      <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">
       Armenians
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">
       Russians
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Greeks" title="Caucasus Greeks">
       Caucasus Greeks
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Georgians" title="Georgians">
       Georgians
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">
       Turks
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kurds" title="Kurds">
       Kurds
      </a>
      , and smaller numbers of Christian communities from eastern and central Europe such as
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Germans" title="Caucasus Germans">
       Caucasus Germans
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Estonians" title="Estonians">
       Estonians
      </a>
      , and Russian sectarian communities like the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molokans" title="Molokans">
       Molokans
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doukhobors" title="Doukhobors">
       Doukhobors
      </a>
      . Gurdjieff makes particular mention of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yazidi" title="Yazidi">
       Yazidi
      </a>
      community. Growing up in a multi-ethnic society, Gurdjieff became fluent in Armenian, Pontic Greek, Russian and Turkish, speaking the last in a mixture of elegant Osmanli and some dialect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He later acquired "a working facility with several European languages."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Challenger_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Challenger-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Early influences on him included his father, a carpenter and amateur
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ashik" title="Ashik">
        ashik
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">
       bardic
      </a>
      poet,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the priest of the town's
      <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Kars" title="Cathedral of Kars">
       Russian church
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dean_(Christianity)" title="Dean (Christianity)">
       Dean
      </a>
      Borsh, a family friend. The young Gurdjieff avidly read Russian-language scientific literature.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Influenced by these writings, and having witnessed a number of phenomena he could not explain, he formed the conviction that there existed a hidden truth not to be found in science or in mainstream religion.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Seeker_after_truth">
       Seeker after truth
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seeker after truth">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In early adulthood, according to his own account, Gurdjieff's curiosity led him to travel to
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">
       Central Asia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
       Egypt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
       Iran
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">
       Tibet
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
       Rome
      </a>
      , before returning to
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      for a few years in 1912. He was always unforthcoming about the source of his teachings. The only account of his wanderings appears in his book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Meetings_with_Remarkable_Men" title="Meetings with Remarkable Men">
        Meetings with Remarkable Men
       </a>
      </i>
      . Most commentators
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      leave his background unexplained and it is not generally considered to be a reliable or straightforward autobiography.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each chapter is named after an individual "remarkable man", many of them putatively members of a society of "Seekers after truth".
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._G._Bennett" title="J. G. Bennett">
       J.G.Bennet
      </a>
      , who researched Gurdjieff's sources extensively after his death, suggested these characters were symbolic of the three types of men Gurdjieff used to refer to: men #1 centered in their physical body; men #2 centered in their emotions, and men #3 centered in their minds. He asserts that he has encounters with
      <a href="/wiki/Dervish" title="Dervish">
       dervishes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fakir" title="Fakir">
       fakirs
      </a>
      and with descendents of the extinct
      <a href="/wiki/Essenes" title="Essenes">
       Essenes
      </a>
      , whose teaching had been, he claimed, conserved in a monastery at Sarmoung. The book also has an overarching
      <a href="/wiki/Quest" title="Quest">
       quest
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">
       narrative
      </a>
      , involving a map of "pre-sand Egypt," and culminating in an encounter with the "
      <a href="/wiki/Sarmoung_Brotherhood" title="Sarmoung Brotherhood">
       Sarmoung Brotherhood
      </a>
      ", an organisation which has never been definitively identified and which historian
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Sedgwick" title="Mark Sedgwick">
       Mark Sedgwick
      </a>
      has described as "overtly fictional" and "entirely imaginary."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sedgwick_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sedgwick-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Businessman">
       Businessman
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Businessman">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff claimed to have been supporting himself during his travels with odd jobs and trading schemes (some of them dishonest, such as dyeing hedgerow birds yellow and selling them as canaries
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ). On his re-appearance, as far as the historical record is concerned, the ragged wanderer had transformed into a well-heeled businessman. His only autobiographical writing concerning this period is
      <i>
       Herald of Coming Good
      </i>
      , a work, if anything, even less reliable than
      <i>
       Meetings
      </i>
      . In it, he mentions acting as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypnotherapist" title="Hypnotherapist">
       hypnotherapist
      </a>
      specialising in the cure of addictions, and using people as
      <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pig" title="Guinea pig">
       guinea pigs
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for his methods. It is also speculated that during his travels he was engaged in a certain amount of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_activity" title="Political activity">
       political activity
      </a>
      , as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Game" title="The Great Game">
       great game
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Russia">
       In Russia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Russia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From 1913 to 1949 the chronology appears to be based on material that can be confirmed by primary documents, independent witnesses, cross-references and reasonable inference.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On New Year's Day in 1912, Gurdjieff arrived in Moscow and attracted his first students, including his cousin, the sculptor
      <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Merkurov" title="Sergey Merkurov">
       Sergey Merkurov
      </a>
      , and the eccentric Rachmilievitch. In the same year he married the
      <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">
       Polish
      </a>
      Julia Ostrowska in
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">
       Saint Petersburg
      </a>
      . In 1914, Gurdjieff advertised his ballet,
      <i>
       The Struggle of the Magicians,
      </i>
      and supervised his pupils' writing of the sketch "Glimpses of Truth." In 1915, Gurdjieff accepted
      <a href="/wiki/P._D._Ouspensky" title="P. D. Ouspensky">
       P. D. Ouspensky
      </a>
      as a pupil, while in 1916 he accepted the composer
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Hartmann" title="Thomas de Hartmann">
       Thomas de Hartmann
      </a>
      and his wife Olga as students. At this time he had about 30 pupils. Ouspensky already had a reputation as a writer on mystical subjects and had conducted his own, ultimately disappointing, search for wisdom in the East. The Fourth Way "system" taught during this period was complex and metaphysical, partly expressed in scientific terminology.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the midst of revolutionary upheaval in Russia, Gurdjieff left
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrograd" title="Petrograd">
       Petrograd
      </a>
      in 1917 to return to his family home in Alexandropol. During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik_Revolution" title="Bolshevik Revolution">
       Bolshevik Revolution
      </a>
      , he set up temporary study communities in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essentuki" title="Essentuki">
       Essentuki
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">
       Caucasus
      </a>
      , then in
      <a href="/wiki/Tuapse" title="Tuapse">
       Tuapse
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maikop" title="Maikop">
       Maikop
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">
       Sochi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Poti" title="Poti">
       Poti
      </a>
      , all on the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
       Black Sea
      </a>
      coast of southern Russia, where he worked intensively with many of his Russian pupils. Gurdjieff said, "Begin in Russia, End in Russia".
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 1918, Ouspensky separated from Gurdjieff, settling in England and teaching the Fourth Way in his own right. The two men were to have a very ambivalent relationship for decades to come.
     </p>
     <p>
      Four months later, Gurdjieff's eldest sister and her family reached him in Essentuki as refugees, informing him that Turks had shot his father in Alexandropol on 15 May. As Essentuki became more and more threatened by civil war, Gurdjieff fabricated a newspaper story announcing his forthcoming "scientific expedition" to "Mount Induc". Posing as a scientist, Gurdjieff left Essentuki with fourteen companions (excluding Gurdjieff's family and Ouspensky). They traveled by train to Maikop, where hostilities delayed them for three weeks. In spring 1919, Gurdjieff met the artist Alexandre de Salzmann and his wife Jeanne and accepted them as pupils. Assisted by Jeanne de Salzmann, Gurdjieff gave the first public demonstration of his Sacred Dances (Movements at the Tbilisi Opera House, 22 June).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Georgia_and_Turkey">
       In Georgia and Turkey
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Georgia and Turkey">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1919, Gurdjieff and his closest pupils moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">
       Tbilisi
      </a>
      , formerly known as Tiflis. There, Gurdjieff's wife, Julia Ostrowska; the Stjoernvals, the de Hartmann and the de Salzmann, gathered the fundamentals of his teaching. Gurdjieff concentrated on his still unstaged ballet,
      <i>
       The Struggle of the Magicians
      </i>
      . Thomas de Hartmann (who had made his debut years ago, before Czar
      <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">
       Nicholas II of Russia
      </a>
      ) worked on the music for the ballet, and Olga Ivanovna Hinzenberg (who years later wed the American architect
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">
       Frank Lloyd Wright
      </a>
      ) practiced the ballet dances.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1919, Gurdjieff established his first
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_for_the_Harmonious_Development_of_Man" title="Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man">
       Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In late May 1920, when political conditions in
      <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">
       Georgia
      </a>
      changed and the old order was crumbling, his party travelled to
      <a href="/wiki/Batumi" title="Batumi">
       Batumi
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
       Black Sea
      </a>
      coast and then took ship to
      <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">
       Istanbul
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gurdjieff rented an apartment on Koumbaradji Street in Pra and later at 13 Abdullatif Yemeneci Sokak near the
      <a href="/wiki/Galata_Tower" title="Galata Tower">
       Galata Tower
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The apartment is near the
      <a href="/wiki/Khanqah" title="Khanqah">
       khaneqah
      </a>
      (monastery) of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molavieh" title="Molavieh">
       Molavieh
      </a>
      Order of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sufi" title="Sufi">
       Sufis
      </a>
      (founded by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jalal_al-Din_Muhammad_Rumi" title="Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi">
       Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi
      </a>
      ), where Gurdjieff, Ouspensky and Thomas de Hartmann witnessed the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sema" title="Sema">
        sema
       </a>
      </i>
      ceremony of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Whirling_Dervishes" title="The Whirling Dervishes">
       the Whirling Dervishes
      </a>
      . In Istanbul, Gurdjieff also met his future pupil Capt.
      <a href="/wiki/John_G._Bennett" title="John G. Bennett">
       John G. Bennett
      </a>
      , then head of
      <a href="/wiki/Directorate_of_Military_Intelligence" title="Directorate of Military Intelligence">
       British Military Intelligence
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
       Constantinople
      </a>
      , who describes his impression of Gurdjieff as follows:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       It was there that I first met Gurdjieff in the autumn of 1920, and no surroundings could have been more appropriate. In Gurdjieff, East and West do not just meet. Their difference is annihilated in a world outlook which knows no distinctions of race or creed. This was my first, and has remained one of my strongest impressions. A Greek from the Caucasus, he spoke
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       with an accent of unexpected purity, the accent that one associates with those born and bred in the narrow circle of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_court" title="Ottoman court">
        Imperial Court
       </a>
       . His appearance was striking enough even in Turkey, where one saw many unusual types. His head was shaven, immense black moustache, eyes which at one moment seemed very pale and at another almost black. Below average height, he gave nevertheless an impression of great physical strength
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prieur.C3.A9_at_Fontainebleau">
       <i>
        Prieur
       </i>
       at Fontainebleau
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prieur at Fontainebleau">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In August 1921 and 1922, Gurdjieff travelled around western Europe, lecturing and giving demonstrations of his work in various cities, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
       Berlin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      . He attracted the allegiance of Ouspensky's many prominent pupils (notably the editor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._R._Orage" title="A. R. Orage">
       A. R. Orage
      </a>
      ). After an unsuccessful attempt to gain British citizenship, Gurdjieff established the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_for_the_Harmonious_Development_of_Man" title="Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man">
       Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man
      </a>
      south of
      <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
       Paris
      </a>
      at the
      <i>
       Prieur des Basses Loges
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Fontainebleau" title="Fontainebleau">
       Fontainebleau-Avon
      </a>
      near the famous
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chateau_Fontainebleau" title="Chateau Fontainebleau">
        Chteau de Fontainebleau
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      This once-impressive but somewhat crumbling mansion, set in extensive grounds, housed an entourage of several dozen, including some of Gurdjieff's remaining relatives and some
      <a href="/wiki/White_%C3%A9migr%C3%A9" title="White migr">
       White Russian
      </a>
      refugees.
     </p>
     <p>
      New pupils included
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._S._Nott" title="C. S. Nott">
       C. S. Nott
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Zuber" title="fr:Ren Zuber">
       Ren Zuber
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_Anderson" title="Margaret Anderson">
       Margaret Anderson
      </a>
      and her ward Fritz Peters. The generally intellectual and middle-class types who were attracted to Gurdjieff's teaching often found the Prieur's spartan accommodation and emphasis on hard labour in the grounds disconcerting. Gurdjieff was putting into practice his teaching that man needs to develop physically, emotionally and intellectually, hence the mixture of lectures, music, dance, and manual work. Older pupils noticed how the Prieur teaching differed from the complex metaphysical "system" that had been taught in Russia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition to the physical hardships, his personal behaviour towards pupils could be ferocious:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Gurdjieff was standing by his bed in a state of what seemed to me to be completely uncontrolled fury. He was raging at Orage, who stood impassively, and very pale, framed in one of the windows . . . Suddenly, in the space of an instant, Gurdjieff's voice stopped, his whole personality changed, he gave me a broad smilelooking incredibly peaceful and inwardly quiet motioned me to leave, and then resumed his tirade with undiminished force. This happened so quickly that I do not believe that Mr. Orage even noticed the break in the rhythm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         26
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      During this period, Gurdjieff acquired notoriety as "the man who killed Katherine Mansfield" after
      <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Mansfield" title="Katherine Mansfield">
       Katherine Mansfield
      </a>
      died there of
      <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">
       tuberculosis
      </a>
      under his care on 9 January 1923.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, James Moore and Ouspensky
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      convincingly show that Mansfield knew she would soon die and that Gurdjieff made her last days happy and fulfilling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_car_accident.2C_writing_and_visits_to_America">
       First car accident, writing and visits to America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: First car accident, writing and visits to America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Starting in 1924, Gurdjieff made visits to North America, where he eventually received the pupils taught previously by A.R. Orage. In 1924, while driving alone from Paris to Fontainebleau, he had a near-fatal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_accident" title="Car accident">
       car accident
      </a>
      . Nursed by his wife and mother, he made a slow and painful recovery against medical expectation. Still convalescent, he formally "disbanded" his institute on 26 August (in fact he dispersed only his "less dedicated" pupils), which he explained as an undertaking "in the future, under the pretext of different worthy reasons, to remove from my eyesight all those who by this or that make my life too comfortable."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After recovering, he began writing
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales
      </i>
      , the first part of
      <i>
       All and Everything
      </i>
      in a mixture of Russian and Armenian. The book was deliberately convoluted and obscure, forcing the reader to "work" to find its meaning. He also composed it according his own principles, writing in noisy cafes to force a greater effort of concentration.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1925 Gurdjieff's mother died, and his wife developed
      <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">
       cancer
      </a>
      ; she was to die in June 1926. Ouspensky attended her funeral. According to
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fritz_Peters_(writer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fritz Peters (writer) (page does not exist)">
       Fritz Peters
      </a>
      , Gurdjieff was in New York from November 1925 to the spring of 1926, when he succeeded in raising over $100,000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In all he was to make six or seven trips to the U.S. During them he alienated a number of people with his brash and undisguised demands for money. Some
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2013)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      have interpreted this in terms of his following the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Malamatiyya" title="Malamatiyya">
        Malamatiyya
       </a>
      </i>
      technique of the Sufis, deliberately attracting disapproval.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite his fund-raising efforts in America, the Prieur operation ran into debt and was shut down in 1932. Gurdjieff constituted a new teaching group in Paris. Known as The Rope, it comprised only women, many of them writers, and many
      <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">
       lesbians
      </a>
      . Members included
      <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Hulme" title="Kathryn Hulme">
       Kathryn Hulme
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Heap" title="Jane Heap">
       Jane Heap
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_Anderson" title="Margaret Anderson">
       Margaret Anderson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Caruso" title="Enrico Caruso">
       Enrico Caruso
      </a>
      's widow, Dorothy. Gurdjieff became acquainted with
      <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_Stein" title="Gertrude Stein">
       Gertrude Stein
      </a>
      through Rope members, although she was never a follower of his.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1935 Gurdjieff stopped work on
      <i>
       All and Everything.
      </i>
      He had completed the first two parts of the planned trilogy but only started on the
      <i>
       Third Series.
      </i>
      (It was later published under the title
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Is_Real_Only_Then,_When_%27I_Am%27" title="Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'">
        Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      ) In 1936, he settled in a flat at 6,
      <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rue_des_Colonels-Renard" title="fr:Rue des Colonels-Renard">
       Rue des Colonels-Renard
      </a>
      in Paris, where he was to stay for the rest of his life. In 1937, his brother Dmitry died, and The Rope disbanded.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">
       World War II
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: World War II">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although the flat at 6 Rue des Colonels-Renard was very small for the purpose, he continued to teach groups of pupils throughout
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . Visitors recalled the pantry, stocked with an extraordinary collection of eastern delicacies, that served as his inner sanctum, and the suppers he held with elaborate toasts to "idiots"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in vodka and cognac. Having cut a physically impressive figure for many years, he was now distinctly paunchy. His teaching was now far removed from the original "system", being based on proverbs, jokes and personal interaction, although pupils were required to read, three times if possible, copies of his magnum opus
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      His personal business enterprises (he had intermittently been a dealer in oriental rugs and carpets for much of his life, among other activities) enabled him to offer charitable relief to neighbours who had been affected by the difficult circumstances of the war, and also brought him to the attention of the authorities, leading to a night in the cells.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">
       Final years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Final years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After the war, Gurdjieff tried to reconnect with his former pupils. Ouspensky was reluctant, but after his death (October 1947), his widow advised his remaining pupils to see Gurdjieff in Paris. J. G. Bennett also visited from England, the first meeting for 25 years. Ouspensky's pupils in England had all thought that Gurdjieff was dead. They discovered he was alive only after Ouspensky's death. The latter had not told them that Gurdjieff still was living. They were overjoyed to hear this, and numbers of Ouspensky's pupils including Rina Hands, Basil Tilley and Catherine Murphy visited Gurdjieff in Paris. Hands and Murphy worked on the typing and retyping of the forthcoming book
      <i>
       All and Everything
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff suffered a second car accident in 1948, but again made an unexpected recovery.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "[I] was looking at a dying man. Even this is not enough to express it. It was a dead man, a corpse, that came out of the car; and yet it walked. I was shivering like someone who sees a ghost.
      </p>
      <p>
       With iron-like tenacity Gurdjieff managed to gain his room, where he sat down and said: Now all organs are destroyed. Must make new. Then he turned to Bennett, smiling: Tonight you come dinner. I must make body work. As he spoke a great spasm of pain shook his body and blood gushed from an ear. Bennett thought: He has a cerebral haemorrhage. He will kill himself if he continues to force his body to move. But then he reflected: He has to do all this. If he allows his body to stop moving, he will die. He has power over his body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         35
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After recovering, Gurdjieff finalised plans for the official publication of
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales
      </i>
      and made two trips to New York. He also visited the famous prehistoric cave paintings at
      <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">
       Lascaux
      </a>
      , giving his interpretation of their significance to his pupils.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff died on October 29, 1949, at the American Hospital in
      <a href="/wiki/Neuilly-sur-Seine" title="Neuilly-sur-Seine">
       Neuilly-sur-Seine
      </a>
      , France. His funeral took place at the St. Alexandre Nevsky Russian Orthodox Cathedral at 12 Rue Daru, Paris. He is buried in the cemetery at Fontainebleau-Avon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ideas">
       Ideas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ideas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff claimed that people cannot perceive reality in their current states because they do not possess a unified consciousness but rather live in a state of a hypnotic "waking sleep."
     </p>
     <p>
      "Man lives his life in sleep, and in sleep he dies."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result of this condition, each person perceives things from a completely subjective perspective. He asserted that people in their typical state function as unconscious
      <a href="/wiki/Automaton" title="Automaton">
       automatons
      </a>
      , but that one can "wake up" and become a different sort of human being altogether.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Self-development_teachings">
       Self-development teachings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Self-development teachings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Way" title="Fourth Way">
       Fourth Way
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff argued that many of the existing forms of religious and spiritual tradition on Earth had lost connection with their original meaning and vitality and so could no longer serve humanity in the way that had been intended at their inception. As a result humans were failing to realize the truths of ancient teachings and were instead becoming more and more like automatons, susceptible to control from outside and increasingly capable of otherwise unthinkable acts of
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_hysteria" title="Mass hysteria">
       mass psychosis
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      . At best, the various surviving sects and schools could provide only a one-sided development, which did not result in a fully integrated human being.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Gurdjieff, only one dimension of the three dimensions of the personnamely, either the emotions, or the physical body or the mindtends to develop in such schools and sects, and generally at the expense of the other faculties or
      <i>
       centers,
      </i>
      as Gurdjieff called them. As a result these paths fail to produce a properly balanced human being. Furthermore, anyone wishing to undertake any of the traditional paths to spiritual knowledge (which Gurdjieff reduced to threenamely the path of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fakir" title="Fakir">
       fakir
      </a>
      , the path of the
      <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">
       monk
      </a>
      , and the path of the
      <a href="/wiki/Yogi" title="Yogi">
       yogi
      </a>
      ) were required to renounce life in the world. Gurdjieff thus developed a "Fourth Way"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which would be amenable to the requirements of modern people living modern lives in Europe and America. Instead of developing body, mind, or emotions separately, Gurdjieff's discipline worked on all three to promote comprehensive and balanced inner development.
     </p>
     <p>
      In parallel with other spiritual traditions, Gurdjieff taught that one must expend considerable effort to effect the
      <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_transformation" title="Spiritual transformation">
       transformation
      </a>
      that leads to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mystical_experience" title="Mystical experience">
       awakening
      </a>
      . The effort that one puts into practice Gurdjieff referred to as
      <i>
       The Work
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Work on oneself.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Gurdjieff, "...Working on oneself is not so difficult as wishing to work, taking the decision."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Though Gurdjieff never put major significance on the term "Fourth Way" and never used the term in his writings, his pupil
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P.D._Ouspensky" title="P.D. Ouspensky">
       P.D. Ouspensky
      </a>
      from 1924 to 1947 made the term and its use central to his own teaching of Gurdjieff's ideas. After Ouspensky's death, his students published a book titled
      <i>
       The Fourth Way
      </i>
      based on his lectures.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff's teaching addressed the question of humanity's place in the universe and the importance of developing latent potentialitiesregarded as our natural endowment as human beings but rarely brought to fruition. He taught that higher levels of consciousness, higher bodies,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      inner growth and development are real possibilities that nonetheless require conscious work to achieve.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In his teaching Gurdjieff gave a distinct meaning to various ancient texts such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      and many religious
      <a href="/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">
       prayers
      </a>
      . He claimed that those texts possess a very different meaning than what is commonly attributed to them. "Sleep not"; "Awake, for you know not the hour"; and "The Kingdom of Heaven is Within" are examples of biblical statements which point to a psychological teaching whose essence has been forgotten.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff taught people how to increase and focus their attention and energy in various ways and to minimize daydreaming and absentmindedness. According to his teaching, this inner development in oneself is the beginning of a possible further process of change, the aim of which is to transform people into what Gurdjieff believed they ought to be.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Distrusting "morality," which he describes as varying from culture to culture, often contradictory and hypocritical, Gurdjieff greatly stressed the importance of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Conscience" title="Conscience">
        conscience
       </a>
       .
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      To provide conditions in which inner attention could be exercised more intensively, Gurdjieff also taught his pupils "sacred dances" or "movements," later known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Gurdjieff_movements" title="Gurdjieff movements">
       Gurdjieff movements
      </a>
      , which they performed together as a group. He also left a body of music, inspired by what he heard in visits to remote monasteries and other places, written for piano in collaboration with one of his pupils,
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Hartmann" title="Thomas de Hartmann">
       Thomas de Hartmann
      </a>
      . Gurdjieff also used various exercises, such as the "Stop" exercise, to prompt self-observation in his students. Other shocks to help awaken his pupils from constant daydreaming were always possible at any moment.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">
       Methods
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Methods">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Work
      </i>
      is in essence a training in the development of consciousness. During his lifetime Gurdjieff used a number of different methods and materials, including meetings, music, movements (sacred dance), writings, lectures, and innovative forms of group and individual work. Part of the function of these various methods was to undermine and undo the ingrained habit patterns of the mind and bring about moments of insight. Since each individual has different requirements, Gurdjieff did not have a one-size-fits-all approach, and he adapted and innovated as circumstance required.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Russia he was described as keeping his teaching confined to a small circle,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      whereas in Paris and North America he gave numerous public demonstrations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff felt that the traditional methods of self-knowledgethose of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fakir" title="Fakir">
       fakir
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">
       monk
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Yogi" title="Yogi">
       yogi
      </a>
      (acquired, respectively, through pain, devotion, and study)were inadequate on their own and often led to various forms of stagnation and one-sidedness. His methods were designed to augment the traditional paths with the purpose of hastening the developmental process. He sometimes called these methods
      <i>
       The Way of the Sly Man
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      because they constituted a sort of short-cut through a process of development that might otherwise carry on for years without substantive results. The teacher, possessing consciousness, sees the individual requirements of the disciple and sets tasks that he knows will result in a transformation of consciousness in that individual. Instructive historical parallels can be found in the annals of
      <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">
       Zen
      </a>
      Buddhism, where teachers employed a variety of methods (sometimes highly unorthodox) to bring about the arising of
      <a href="/wiki/Satori" title="Satori">
       insight
      </a>
      in the student.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
       Music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Gurdjieff music divides into three distinct periods. The "first period" is the early music, including music from the ballet
      <i>
       Struggle of the Magicians
      </i>
      and music for early Movements, dating to the years around 1918.
     </p>
     <p>
      The "second period" music, for which Gurdjieff arguably became best known, written in collaboration with Russian composer
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Hartmann" title="Thomas de Hartmann">
       Thomas de Hartmann
      </a>
      , is described as the Gurdjieff-de Hartmann music.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inc.1999_60-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Inc.1999-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dating to the mid-1920s, it offers a rich repertory with roots in Caucasian and Central Asian folk and religious music, Russian Orthodox liturgical music, and other sources. This music was often first heard in the salon at the Prieur, where much was composed. Since the publication of four volumes of this piano repertory by Schott, recently completed, there has been a wealth of new recordings, including orchestral versions of music prepared by Gurdjieff and de Hartmann for the Movements demonstrations of 192324. Solo piano versions of these works have been recorded by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Lytle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cecil Lytle (page does not exist)">
       Cecil Lytle
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Keith_Jarrett" title="Keith Jarrett">
       Keith Jarrett
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The "last musical period" is the improvised
      <a href="/wiki/Pump_organ" title="Pump organ">
       harmonium
      </a>
      music which often followed the dinners Gurdjieff held in his Paris apartment during the Occupation and immediate post-war years to his death in 1949. In all, Gurdjieff in collaboration with de Hartmann composed some 200 pieces.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In May 2010, 38 minutes of unreleased solo piano music on acetate was purchased by Neil Kempfer Stocker from the estate of his late step-daughter, Dushka Howarth.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Movements">
       Movements
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Movements">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Gurdjieff_movements" title="Gurdjieff movements">
       Gurdjieff movements
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Movements, or sacred dances, constitute an integral part of the Gurdjieff Work. Gurdjieff sometimes referred to himself as a "teacher of dancing" and gained initial public notice for his attempts to put on a ballet in Moscow called
      <i>
       Struggle of the Magicians.
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      Films of movements demonstrations are occasionally shown for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_viewing" title="Private viewing">
       private viewing
      </a>
      by the Gurdjieff Foundations and one is shown in a scene in the Peter Brook movie
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Meetings_with_Remarkable_Men_(film)" title="Meetings with Remarkable Men (film)">
        Meetings with Remarkable Men
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">
       Writings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Writings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff wrote a unique trilogy with the Series title
      <i>
       All and Everything
      </i>
      . The first volume, finalized by Gurdjieff shortly before his death and first published in 1950, is the First Series and titled
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An_Objectively_Impartial_Criticism_of_the_Life_of_Man" title="An Objectively Impartial Criticism of the Life of Man">
        An Objectively Impartial Criticism of the Life of Man
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Beelzebub%27s_Tales_to_His_Grandson" title="Beelzebub's Tales to His Grandson">
        Beelzebub's Tales to His Grandson
       </a>
      </i>
      . At 1238 pages it is a lengthy allegorical work that recounts the explanations of Beelzebub to his grandson concerning the beings of the planet Earth. I provides a vast platform for Gurdjieff's deeply considered philosophy. A controversial redaction of
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales
      </i>
      was published by some of Gurdjieff's followers as an alternative "edition," in 1992. [See Paul Beekman Taylor's'
      <i>
       Gurdjieff's Worlds of Words
      </i>
      (2014) for an informed account.) The posthumous second series, edited by
      <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_de_Salzmann" title="Jeanne de Salzmann">
       Jeanne de Salzmann
      </a>
      is titled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Meetings_with_Remarkable_Men" title="Meetings with Remarkable Men">
        Meetings with Remarkable Men
       </a>
      </i>
      (1963) is written in a seemingly accessible manner as a memoir of his early years, but also contains many 'Arabian Nights' embellishments and allegorical statements. His posthumous Third Series, written as if unfinished and also edited by Jeanne de Salzmann (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Is_Real_Only_Then,_When_%27I_Am%27" title="Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'">
        Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'
       </a>
      </i>
      ) contains an intimate account of Gurdjieff's inner struggles during his later years, as well as transcripts of some of his lectures. There is an enormous and growing amount written about Gurdjieff's ideas and methods but his own challenging writings remain the primary sources. On his page of
      <i>
       Friendly Advice
      </i>
      facing the first Contents page of
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales
      </i>
      Gurdjieff lays out his own program of three obligatory initial readings of each of the three series in sequence and concludes, "Only then will you be able to count upon forming your own impartial judgement, proper to yourself alone, on my writings. And only then can my hope be actualized that according to you understanding you will obtain the specific benefit for your self which I anticipate."
     </p>

     <p>
      Gurdjieff gave new life and practical form to ancient teachings of both East and West. For example, the Socratic and Platonic emphasis on "the examined life" recurs in Gurdjieff's teaching as the practice of self-observation. His teachings about self-discipline and restraint reflect Stoic teachings. The Hindu and Buddhist notion of attachment recurs in Gurdjieff's teaching as the concept of identification. His descriptions of the "three being-foods" matches that of Ayurveda, and his statement that "time is breath" echoes jyotish, the Vedic system of astrology. Similarly, his cosmology can be "read" against ancient and esoteric sources, respectively Neoplatonic and in such sources as Robert Fludd's treatment of macrocosmic musical structures.
     </p>
     <p>
      An aspect of Gurdjieff's teachings which has come into prominence in recent decades is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_Way_Enneagram" title="Fourth Way Enneagram">
       enneagram
      </a>
      geometric figure. For many students of the Gurdjieff tradition, the enneagram remains a
      <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dan" title="Kan">
       koan
      </a>
      , challenging and never fully explained. There have been many attempts to trace the origins of this version of the enneagram; some similarities to other figures have been found, but it seems that Gurdjieff was the first person to make the enneagram figure publicly known and that only he knew its true source.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Others have used the enneagram figure in connection with personality analysis, principally with the
      <a href="/wiki/Enneagram_of_Personality" title="Enneagram of Personality">
       Enneagram of Personality
      </a>
      as developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Ichazo" title="Oscar Ichazo">
       Oscar Ichazo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Naranjo" title="Claudio Naranjo">
       Claudio Naranjo
      </a>
      and others. Most aspects of this application are not directly connected to Gurdjieff's teaching or to his explanations of the enneagram.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff inspired the formation of many groups after his death, all of which still function today and follow his ideas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Gurdjieff_Foundation" title="Gurdjieff Foundation">
       Gurdjieff Foundation
      </a>
      , the largest establishment organization influenced by the ideas of Gurdjieff, was organized by
      <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_de_Salzmann" title="Jeanne de Salzmann">
       Jeanne de Salzmann
      </a>
      during the early 1950s, and led by her in cooperation with other pupils of his. Other pupils of Gurdjieff formed independent groups. Willem Nyland, one of Gurdjieff's closest students and an original founder and trustee of The Gurdjieff Foundation of New York, left to form his own groups in the early 1960s.
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Heap" title="Jane Heap">
       Jane Heap
      </a>
      was sent to London by Gurdjieff, where she led groups until her death in 1964. Louise Goepfert March, who became a pupil of Gurdjieff's in 1929, started her own groups in 1957 and founded the Rochester Folk Art Guild in the
      <a href="/wiki/Finger_Lakes" title="Finger Lakes">
       Finger Lakes
      </a>
      region of New York State. Independent thriving groups were also formed and initially led by
      <a href="/wiki/John_G._Bennett" title="John G. Bennett">
       John G. Bennett
      </a>
      and A. L. Staveley near Portland, Oregon.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gurdjieff.27s_pupils">
       Gurdjieff's pupils
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Gurdjieff's pupils">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Gurdjieff's notable pupils include:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_D._Ouspensky" title="Peter D. Ouspensky">
       Peter D. Ouspensky
      </a>
      (1878-1947) was a Russian journalist, author and philosopher. He met Gurdjieff in 1915 and spent the next five years studying with him, then formed his own independent groups at London in 1921. Ouspensky became the first "career" Gurdjieffian and led independent Fourth Way groups in London and New York for his remaining years. He wrote
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_the_Miraculous" title="In Search of the Miraculous">
        In Search of the Miraculous
       </a>
      </i>
      about his encounters with Gurdjieff and it remains the best known and most widely read account of Gurdjeff's early experiments with groups.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Hartmann" title="Thomas de Hartmann">
       Thomas de Hartmann
      </a>
      (1885-1956) was a Russian composer. He and his wife Olga first met Gurdjieff in 1916 at Saint Petersburg. They remained Gurdjieff's close students until 1929. During that time they lived at Gurdjieff's Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man near Paris. Between July 1925 and May 1927 Thomas de Hartmann transcribed and co-wrote some of the music that Gurdjieff collected and used for his Movements exercises. They collaborated on hundreds of pieces of concert music arranged for the piano. This concert music was first recorded and published privately from the 1950s to 1980s; then first issued publicly as the
      <i>
       Music of Gurdjieff / de Hartmann
      </i>
      , Thomas de Hartmann, piano by Triangle Records, with 49 tracks on 4 vinyl disks in 1998, then reissued as a 3 CD set in containing 56 tracks in 1989. A more extensive compilation was later issued as the
      <i>
       Gurdjieff / de Hartmann Music for the Piano
      </i>
      in 4 printed volumes by Schott between 1996 and 2005, and as audio CDs under the same title in four volumes with nine discs recorded with three concert pianists, by Schott/Wergo between 1997 and 2001. Olga de Hartmann (ne Arkadievna, 1885-1987) was Gurdjieff's personal secretary during their Prieur years and took most of the original dictations of his writings during that period. She also authenticated Gurdjieff's early talks in the book
      <i>
       Views from the Real World
      </i>
      (1973). The de Hartmann's memoir,
      <i>
       Our Life with Mr Gurdjieff
      </i>
      (1st ed, 1964, 2nd ed, 1983, 3rd ed 1992), records their Gurdjieff years in great detail. Their Montreal Gurdjieff group, literary and musical estate is represented by retired Canadian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Film_Board" title="National Film Board">
       National Film Board
      </a>
      producer Tom Daly.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_de_Salzmann" title="Jeanne de Salzmann">
       Jeanne de Salzmann
      </a>
      (1899-1990). Alexander and Jeanne de Salzmann met Gurdjieff in Tiflis in 1919. She was originally a dancer, Dalcroze Eurythmics teacher. She was, along with Jessmin Howarth and Rose Mary Nott, responsible for transmitting Gurdjieff's choreographed movements exercises and institutionalizing Gurdjieff's teachings through the
      <a href="/wiki/Gurdjieff_Foundation" title="Gurdjieff Foundation">
       Gurdjieff Foundation
      </a>
      of New York, the Gurdjieff Institute of Paris, London's Gurdjieff Society Inc and other groups, she established in 1953. She also established Triangle Editions in the US, which imprint holds copyright on Gurdjieff's posthumous writings.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/John_G._Bennett" title="John G. Bennett">
       John G. Bennett
      </a>
      , a British intelligence officer, polyglot (fluent in Turkish and Russian), technologist, industrial research director and author best known for his many books on psychology and spirituality, particularly the teachings of Gurdjieff. Bennett met both Ouspensky and then Gurdjeiff at Istanbul in 1921, spent August 1922 at Gurdjieff's institute, became Ouspensky's pupil between 1922 and 1941 and, after learning that Gurdjieff was still alive, was one of Gurdjieff's frequent visitors in Paris during 1949. See
      <i>
       Witness: the Autobiography of John Bennett
      </i>
      (1974),
      <i>
       Gurdjieff: the Making of a New World
      </i>
      (1974),
      <i>
       Idiots in Paris: diaries of J. G. Bennett and Elizabeth Bennett, 1949
      </i>
      (1991).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Richard_Orage" title="Alfred Richard Orage">
       Alfred Richard Orage
      </a>
      (1873-1934) was an influential British editor best known for the magazine
      <i>
       New Age
      </i>
      . He began attending Ouspensky's London talks in 1921 then met Gurdjieff when the latter first visited London early in 1922. Shortly thereafter, Orage sold
      <i>
       New Age
      </i>
      and relocated to Gurdjieff's institute at the Prier and in 1924 was appointed by Gurdjieff to lead the institute's branch in New York. After Gurdjieffs nearly fatal automobile accident in July 1924 and because of his prolonged recuperation during 1924 and intense writing period for several years, Orage continued in New York until 1931. During this period, Orage was responsible for editing the English typescript of
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales
      </i>
      (1931) and
      <i>
       Meetings with Remarkable Men
      </i>
      (1963) as Gurdjieff' assistant. This period is described in some detail by Paul Beekman Tayloy in his
      <i>
       Gurdjieff and Orage: Brothers in Elysium
      </i>
      (2001).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Nicoll" title="Maurice Nicoll">
       Maurice Nicoll
      </a>
      (1884-1953) was a Harley Street psychiatrist and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_Jung%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carl Jung's (page does not exist)">
       Carl Jung's
      </a>
      delegate in London. Along with Orage he attended Ouspensky's 1921 London talks where he met Gurdjieff. With his wife Catherine and their new-born daughter, he spent almost a year at Gurdjieff's Prieur institute. A year later, when they returned to London, Nicoll rejoined Ouspensky's group. In 1931, on Ouspensky's advice he started his own Fourth Way groups in England. He is best known for the encyclopedic six volume series of articles in
      <i>
       Psychological Commentaries on the Teaching of Gurdjieff and Ouspensky
      </i>
      (Boston: Shambhala, 1996, and Samuel Weiser Inc., 1996).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Willem_Nyland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Willem Nyland (page does not exist)">
       Willem Nyland
      </a>
      (1890-1975) was a Dutch-American chemist who first met Gurdjieff early in 1924 during the latter's first visit to the US. He was a charter member of the NY branch of Gurdjieff's Institute, participated in Orage's meetings between 1924 and 1931 and was a charter member of the Gurdjieff Foundation from 1953 and through its formative years. In the early 1960s he established an independent group in Warwick NY, where he began making reel-to-reel audio recordings of his meetings which became archived in a private library of some 2600, 90 minute audio tapes. Many of these tapes have also been transcribed and indexed, but remain unpublished.
      <i>
       Gurdjieff Group Work with Wilhem (sic-Willem) Nyland
      </i>
      (1983) by Irmis B. Popoff, sketches Nyland's group work.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Heap" title="Jane Heap">
       Jane Heap
      </a>
      (1883-1964) was an American writer, editor, artist, and publisher. She met Gurdjieff during his 1924 visit to New York, and set up a Gurdjieff study group at her apartment in Greenwich Village. In 1925, she moved to Paris to study at Gurdjieffs Institute, re-established her group in Paris until 1935 when Gurdjieff sent her to London to lead the group C. S. Nott had established and which she continued to lead until her death. Jane Heap's Paris group became Gurdjieff's 'Rope' group after her departure and contained several notable writers including,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_Anderson" title="Margaret Anderson">
       Margaret Anderson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solita_Solano" title="Solita Solano">
       Solita Solano
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Katheryn_Hulme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Katheryn Hulme (page does not exist)">
       Katheryn Hulme
      </a>
      and others who proved helpful to Gurdjieff while he was editing his first two books.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kenneth Macfarlane Walker (18821966) was a prominent British surgeon and prolific author. He was a member of Ouspensky's London group for decades and after the latter's death in 1947 visited Gurdjieff in Paris many times. As well as many accessible medical books for lay readers, he wrote some of the earliest informed accounts of Gurdjieff's ideas,
      <i>
       Venture with Ideas
      </i>
      (1951) and
      <i>
       A Study of Gurdjieff's Teaching
      </i>
      (1957).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_John_Sinclair,_2nd_Baron_Pentland" title="Henry John Sinclair, 2nd Baron Pentland">
       Henry John Sinclair, 2nd Baron Pentland
      </a>
      (1907-1984) was a pupil of Ouspensky's during the 1930s and 1940s. He visited Gurdjieff regularly in Paris in 1949, then was appointed as President of the Gurdjieff Foundation of America by Jeanne de Salzmann when she founded that institution at New York in 1953. He established the Gurdjieff Foundation of California in the mid 1950s and remained President of the US Foundation branches until his death. Pentland also became President of Triangle Editions when it was established in 1974.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Responses">
       Responses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Gurdjieff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Responses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Louis Pauwels, among others,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      criticizes Gurdjieff for his insistence on considering people as "asleep" in a state closely resembling "hypnotic sleep." Gurdjieff said, even specifically at times, that a pious, good, and moral man was no more "spiritually developed" than any other person; they are all equally "asleep."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Miller" title="Henry Miller">
       Henry Miller
      </a>
      approved of Gurdjieff's not considering himself holy but, after writing a brief introduction to Fritz Peters' book
      <i>
       Boyhood with Gurdjieff
      </i>
      , Miller wrote that man is not meant to lead a "harmonious life," as Gurdjieff claimed in naming his institute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Critics note that Gurdjieff gives no value to most of the elements that comprise the life of an average man. According to Gurdjieff, everything an "average man" possesses, accomplishes, does, and feels is completely accidental and without any initiative. A common everyday ordinary man is born a machine and dies a machine without any chance of being anything else.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This belief seems to run counter to the Judeo-Christian tradition that man is a living soul. Gurdjieff believed that the possession of a soul (a state of psychological unity which he equated with being "awake") was a "luxury" that a disciple could attain only by the most painstaking work of over a long period of time. The majorityin whom the true meaning of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">
       gospel
      </a>
      failed to take root
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      went the "broad way" that "led to destruction."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       Beelzebub's Tales to His Grandson
      </i>
      (see bibliography), Gurdjieff expresses his reverence for the founders of the mainstream religions of East and West and his contempt (by and large) for what successive generations of believers have made of those religious teachings. His discussions of "orthodoxhydooraki" and "heterodoxhydooraki"orthodox fools and heterodox fools, from the Russian word
      <i>
       durak
      </i>
      (fool)position him as a critic of religious distortion and, in turn, as a target for criticism from some within those traditions. Gurdjieff has been interpreted by some, Ouspensky among others, to have had a total disregard for the value of mainstream religion, philanthropic work and the value of doing right or wrong in general.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>

     <p>
      Gurdjieff's former students who have criticized him argue that, despite his seeming total lack of pretension to any kind of "guru holiness," in many anecdotes his behavior displays the unsavory and impure character of a man who was a cynical manipulator of his followers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gurdjieff's own pupils wrestled to understand him. For example, in a written exchange between Luc Dietrich and Henri Tracol dating to 1943: "L.D.: How do you know that Gurdjieff wishes you well? H.T.: I feel sometimes how little I interest himand how strongly he takes an interest in me. By that I measure the strength of an intentional feeling."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Louis Pauwels wrote
      <i>
       Monsieur Gurdjieff
      </i>
      (first edition published in Paris, France in 1954 by Editions du Seuil).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an interview, Pauwels said of the Gurdjieff work: "... After two years of exercises which both enlightened and burned me, I found myself in a hospital bed with a thrombosed central vein in my left eye and weighing ninety-nine pounds... Horrible anguish and abysses opened up for me. But it was my fault."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [

      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Haushofer" title="Karl Haushofer">
       Karl Haushofer

      <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">
       geopolitics
      </a>
      whose protegee was Deputy Reich Fhrer
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Hess" title="Rudolf Hess">
       Rudolf Hess

      <a href="/wiki/Achmed_Abdullah" title="Achmed Abdullah">
       Achmed Abdullah
      </a>
      told him at the beginning of the 20th century that Gurdjieff was a Russian secret agent in Tibet who went by the name of "Hambro Akuan Dorzhieff" (i.e.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agvan_Dorjiev" title="Agvan Dorjiev">
       Agvan Dorjiev
      </a>
      ), a tutor to the
      <a href="/wiki/Dalai_Lama" title="Dalai Lama">

      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ovshe_Norzunov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ovshe Norzunov (page does not exist)">

King wrote that Gurdjieff did not state it as clearly and specifically as this, but was quick to add that to him, nothing Gurdjieff said was specific or clear.

     <p>
      Gurdjieff's views have arguably become best known through the published works of his pupils. His one-time student
      <a href="/wiki/P._D._Ouspensky" title="P. D. Ouspensky">
       P. D. Ouspensky
      </a>
      wrote
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_the_Miraculous" title="In Search of the Miraculous">
        In Search of the Miraculous: Fragments of an Unknown Teaching
       </a>
      </i>
      , which some, Rodney Collins among others, regard as a crucial introduction to the teaching. Others refer to Gurdjieff's own books (detailed below) as the primary texts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Published accounts of time spent with Gurdjieff have appeared written by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._R._Orage" title="A. R. Orage">
       A. R. Orage
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stanley_Nott" title="Charles Stanley Nott">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Daumal" title="Ren Daumal">
       Ren Daumal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_G._Bennett" title="John G. Bennett">
       John G. Bennett
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Nicoll" title="Maurice Nicoll">

      </a>

       Margaret Anderson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pauwels" title="Louis Pauwels">
       Louis Pauwels
      </a>
      , among others. Many others found themselves drawn to his "ideas table":
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">
       Frank Lloyd Wright
      </a>
      ,

     <p>
      Three books by Gurdjieff were published in the English language in the United States after his death:
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Beelzebub%27s_Tales_to_His_Grandson" title="Beelzebub's Tales to His Grandson">
        Beelzebub's Tales to His Grandson
       </a>
      </i>
      published in 1950 by E. P. Dutton &amp; Co. Inc.,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Meetings_with_Remarkable_Men" title="Meetings with Remarkable Men">
        Meetings with Remarkable Men
       </a>
      </i>
      , published in 1963 by E. P. Dutton &amp; Co. Inc., and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Is_Real_Only_Then,_When_%27I_Am%27" title="Life Is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'">
        Life is Real Only Then, When 'I Am'
       </a>
      </i>
      , printed privately by E. P. Dutton &amp; Co. and published in 1978 by Triangle Editions Inc. for private distribution only. This
      <a href="/wiki/Trilogy" title="Trilogy">
       trilogy
      </a>
      is Gurdjieff's legominism, known collectively as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All_and_Everything" title="All and Everything">
        All and Everything
       </a>
      </i>
      . A
      <i>
       legominism
      </i>
      is, according to Gurdjieff, "one of the means of transmitting information about certain events of long-past ages through initiates". A book of his early talks was also collected by his student and personal secretary,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olga_de_Hartmann" title="Olga de Hartmann">
       Olga de Hartmann
      </a>
      , and published in 1973 as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Views_from_the_Real_World" title="Views from the Real World">
        Views from the Real World: Early Talks in Moscow, Essentuki, Tiflis, Berlin, London, Paris, New York, and Chicago, as recollected by his pupils
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Terence_Stamp" title="Terence Stamp">
       Terence Stamp
      </a>
      star, as does South African playwright and actor,
      <a href="/wiki/Athol_Fugard" title="Athol Fugard">
