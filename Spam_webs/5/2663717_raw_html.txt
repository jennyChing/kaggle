     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Marib
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Ma'rib
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Ma'arib Dam
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
       Arabic
      </a>
      :
      <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        
      </span>
      ) blocks the Wadi Adhanah (also Dhana or Adhana) in the valley of Dhana in the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Balaq_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Balaq Hills (page does not exist)">
       Balaq Hills
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">
       Yemen
      </a>
      . The current dam is close to the
      <a href="/wiki/Ruins" title="Ruins">
       ruins
      </a>
      of the Great Dam of Ma'rib, dating from around the 8th century BCE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">
       engineering
      </a>
      wonders of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_world" title="Ancient world">
       ancient world
      </a>
      and a central part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">
       south Arabian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">
       civilization
      </a>
      around
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marib" title="Marib">
       Marib
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      There are also other important ancient dams in Yemen such as the Dam of Jufaynah, the Dam of Khrid, the Dam of Araah, the Dam of Miqrn and the Dam of Yathn. Historically, Yemen has been recognized for the magnificence of its ancient water engineering. From the Red Sea coast to the limits of the
      <a href="/wiki/Rub%27_al_Khali" title="Rub' al Khali">
       Rub' al Khali
      </a>
      desert are numerous ruins of small and large dams made of earth and stone.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Great_Dam_of_Ma.27rib">
       The Great Dam of Ma'rib
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marib_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Great Dam of Ma'rib">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">

     <p>
      The site of the great
      <b>
       Dam of Ma'rib
      </b>
      (Arabic  : sadd Ma'rib, or sudd Ma'rib), also called the Dam of 'Arim (Arabic   ): sadd al-Arim) is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Source_(river_or_stream)" title="Source (river or stream)">
       upstream
      </a>
      (south-west) of the ancient city of Ma'rib, once the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_(political)" title="Capital (political)">
       capital
      </a>
      of the ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Sheba" title="Sheba">
       Kingdom of Saba'
      </a>
      . The Kingdom of Saba' was a prosperous trading nation, with control of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incense_route" title="Incense route">
       frankincense
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spice_route" title="Spice route">
       spice routes
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia">
       Arabia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">
       Abyssinia
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Sabaeans" title="Sabaeans">
       Sabaeans
      </a>
      built the dam to capture the periodic
      <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">
       monsoon
      </a>
      rains which fall on the nearby mountains and so irrigate the land around the city.
     </p>
     <p>
      Recent
      <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">
       archaeological
      </a>
      findings suggest that simple
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_dam" title="Earth dam">
       earth dams
      </a>
      and a
      <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">
       canal
      </a>
      network were constructed as far back as 2000 BCE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Great Dam of Ma'rib certainly dates back to about the 8th century BCE and is considered the oldest known dam in the world, being counted as one of the most wonderful feats of engineering in the ancient world.
     </p>
     <p>
      The medieval Arab geographer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y%C4%81q%C5%ABt_al-%E1%B8%A4amaw%C4%AB" title="Yqt al-amaw">
       Yqt al-amaw
      </a>
      describes it thus:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       It is between three mountains, and the flood waters all flow to the one location, and because of that the water only discharges in one direction; and the ancients blocked that place with hard rocks and lead. The water from springs gathers there as well as floodwater, collecting behind the dam like a sea. Whenever they wanted to they could irrigate their crops from it, by just letting out however much water they needed from sluice gates; once they had used enough they would close the gates again as they pleased.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The historical dam was severely damaged by a Saudi airstrike in the night of May 31 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">
       Construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marib_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The date of the first construction of the Dam at Marib goes back to somewhere between 1750 and 1700 BCE. The earliest inscription on the dam is one placed there at the time of its construction or repair of parts of the dam undertaken by Yithamar bin Samal Yanf, and the period of his reign is uncertain. The following repair was in the time of Yada 'Il Bayyin who reigned in 780-750 BCE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Renovations were then carried out by Dham Al Dhar, who reigned in 730-720 BCE. Then Karabil Bayyin, son of Yathimar, who reigned from 720-710 BCE. All of these repairs were slight ones, essential maintenance, such as removing dirt, or opening water courses, or stopping up gaps. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rulers_of_Sheba_and_Himyar" title="Rulers of Sheba and Himyar">
       Makrib
      </a>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Yanouf_Bin_Dhamar_Ali&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ali Yanouf Bin Dhamar Ali (page does not exist)">
       Ali Yanouf Bin Dhamar Ali
      </a>
      (c. 790 BCE) had his name carved into parts of the dam to mark the completion and repair of the dam.
     </p>
     <p>
      The dam was of packed earth,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangular" title="Triangular">
       triangular
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">
       cross section
      </a>
      , 580 m in length and 4 meters high. It ran between two groups of rocks on either side of the river and was linked to the rock with substantial stonework. The dam's position allowed for a
      <a href="/wiki/Spillway" title="Spillway">
       spillway
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sluice" title="Sluice">
       sluices
      </a>
      between the northern end of the dam and the
      <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">
       cliffs
      </a>
      to the west. Around 500 BCE the dam height was increased to 7 meters, the upstream
      <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">
       slope
      </a>
      (the water face) was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reinforce" title="Reinforce">
       reinforced
      </a>
      with a cover of stones, and irrigation was extended to include the southern side as well as the northern side.
     </p>
     <p>
      After the end of the Kingdom of Saba', the dam fell under the control of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himyarites" title="Himyarites">
       imyarites
      </a>
      in around 115 BCE. They undertook a further reconstruction, creating a structure 14 meters high with extensive water works at both the northern and southern ends - with five spillway channels, two
      <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">
       masonry
      </a>
      -reinforced sluices, a settling
      <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">
       pond
      </a>
      , and a 1000 meter canal to a distribution
      <a href="/wiki/Water_tank" title="Water tank">
       tank
      </a>
      . These extensive works were not actually finalized until 325 AD and allowed the
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      of 25,000 acres (100km).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">
       Maintenance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marib_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Maintenance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Muslim historian al-Isfahn (whose
      <i>
       Annals
      </i>
      were completed in 961 CE) maintains
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that the famous breach of the dam occurred some four hundred years before Islam, but Yqt al-amaw
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      assigns it to the time of Abyssinian rule.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ancient South Arabian sources report that in about 145 BCE, the dam suffered a major breach during the war between the people of Raydn and the Kingdom of Saba, and that is the very breach that many scholars consider to have caused the great flood of Arim mentioned in the Quran; it is also mentioned in Arab proverbs which speak about the
      <i>
       hands of Saba'
      </i>
      having separated at that time. The fighting between the Raydnites and the Sabaeans delayed the repair of the dam, and this caused devastating losses of crops and fruit, leading large numbers of people to disperse in search of new land capable of supporting life, so huge migrations ensued. It is still uncertain though whether it was that particular breach that caused the flood of Arim or not, since some migrations certainly took place in the 2nd or 3rd centuries CE, and they are also ascribed to the breaking of the Dam of Marib. Generally speaking the dam was repaired twice shortly before the coming of Islam, once by Sharabl Yafar bin Ab Karab Yasad in 450 CE, and by
      <a href="/wiki/Abraha" title="Abraha">
       Abrahah
      </a>
      in 543 CE,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the inscriptions on the dam explain the costs of repair and the large number of workers involved.
     </p>
     <p>
      The archaeology of the Ma'rib Dam shows the effects of
      <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">
       siltation
      </a>
      behind dams in antiquity, and measures to extend the operational life of the reservoir. The Ma'rib dam impounded floodwater to enable irrigation of downstream oases. Over several centuries, silts entrained in the irrigation water accumulated on the irrigated fields up to 15 m high. This caused loss of slope and conveyance, and the dam was raised to compensate. In addition to small operational openings for irrigation water withdrawals, large openings on the upstream side of the dam were probably used during construction to pass the water of the river, but may also have been used for periodical flushing of accumulated sediments. These examples show that the engineers of the time were keenly aware of reservoir siltation problems and were able to successfully extend the operational lifetime of the reservoirs by structural enhancements and probable sediment flushing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The same problems of siltation encountered in the ancient dam was also faced in the construction of the new dam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite the increases in height, the dam suffered numerous breaches (recorded major incidents occurred in 449, 450, 542 and 548) and the maintenance work became increasingly onerous; the last recorded repairs took place in 557.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Final_breach">
       Final breach
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marib_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Final breach">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Local tales report that the final breach of the dam had been predicted by a king called Imrn who was also a soothsayer, and later by the wife of the king of the area. In legend the breach was caused by large rats gnawing at it with their teeth and scratching it with their nails. In 570 or 575, the dam was again overtopped, and this time left unrepaired. The breaching and destruction of the Dam of Marib was a historical event, and was alluded to in the Quran:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
                                             
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       " There was for [the tribe of] Saba' in their dwelling place a sign: two [fields of] gardens on the right and on the left. [They were told], "Eat from the provisions of your Lord and be grateful to Him. A good land [have you], and a forgiving Lord. But they turned away [refusing], so We sent upon them the flood of the dam, and We replaced their two [fields of] gardens with gardens of bitter fruit,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamarisk" title="Tamarisk">
        tamarisks
       </a>
       and something of sparse
       <a href="/wiki/Sidrat_al-Muntaha" title="Sidrat al-Muntaha">
        lote trees
       </a>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The consequent failure of the irrigation system provoked the migration of up to 50,000 people from Yemen to other areas of the Arabian Peninsula, and even to Syria and Iraq.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_dam_and_the_location_of_the_lost_city_of_Ubar">
       The dam and the location of the lost city of Ubar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marib_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The dam and the location of the lost city of Ubar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Soil surveys show that the location of the dam may have been near the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Iram_of_the_Pillars" title="Iram of the Pillars">
       Ubar
      </a>
      . During mapping for the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wadi_al_Jubal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wadi al Jubal (page does not exist)">
       Wadi al Jubal
      </a>
      Archaeological Project in Yemen, USGS Geologists Overstreet and Grolier mapped
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthrosols" title="Anthrosols">
       anthrosols
      </a>
      of pre-Islamic age east of the Ma'rib dam site (15 degrees 24N, 45 degrees 18E). These soils were the result of agriculture supported by
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      enabled by water impounded by the dam, areas which were abandoned after dam failure. During analysis of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landsat" title="Landsat">
       Landsat
      </a>
      Thematic Mapper satellite images of Yemen and
      <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">
       Oman
      </a>
      for the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mahra_Archaeological_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mahra Archaeological Project (page does not exist)">
       Mahra Archaeological Project
      </a>
      , we noted that these
      <a href="/wiki/Anthrosol" title="Anthrosol">
       anthrosols
      </a>
      had a distinctive image expression. Many anthrosol sites were noted, most, but not all, of them previously documented. Undocumented possible anthrosol sites include an area east of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shisr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shisr (page does not exist)">
       Shisr
      </a>
      in Oman, the archaeological site discovered by us to be responsible for some features of the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lost_City_of_Ubar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lost City of Ubar (page does not exist)">
       Lost City of Ubar
      </a>
      legends. Included in legendary accounts of the Ubar region are reports of fertile oases, and areas that have known the plow. Based on demonstrated reliability of aspects of carefully interpreted legendary accounts, we postulate that we may have located the area of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Desert_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Desert agriculture (page does not exist)">
       desert agriculture
      </a>
      that may have existed to support the
      <a href="/wiki/Frankincense" title="Frankincense">
       frankincense
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caravansary" title="Caravansary">
       caravansary
      </a>
      of Ubar. The possible anthrosol area is located at approximately 18 degrees 10N, 53 degrees 54E, and will be the subject of study in a future expedition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_current_dam">
       The current dam
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marib_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The current dam">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A new dam was then built more recently, close to the location of the old one, at the expense of
      <a href="/wiki/Zayed_bin_Sultan_Al_Nahyan" title="Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan">
       Shaykh Zayed Bin Suln l Nayhn
      </a>
      , the late ruler of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">
       United Arab Emirates
      </a>
      , whose tribe resettled from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marib" title="Marib">
       Ma'rib
      </a>
      to the present
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UAE" title="UAE">
       UAE
      </a>
      some time in the 17th century. The opening ceremony took place in 1986 in the presence of His Highness Shaykh Zayed.
     </p>
     <p>
      The new dam is 38 m high, 763 m long, built of earth across the Wadi Dhana at
      <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
       <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Marib_Dam&amp;params=15_23_47.21_N_45_14_36.18_E_&amp;title=New+Ma%27rib+Dam">
        <span class="geo-default">
         <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
          <span class="latitude">
           152347.21N
          </span>
          <span class="longitude">
           451436.18E
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          / 
        </span>
        <span class="geo-nondefault">
         <span class="vcard">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           15.3964472N 45.2433833E
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
            /
           <span class="geo">
            15.3964472; 45.2433833
           </span>
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
            (
           <span class="fn org">
            New Ma'rib Dam
           </span>
           )
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
       </a>
      </span>
      , creating a storage capacity of 398 million cubic meters. The dam site is located 3km upstream of the ruins of the old Ma'rib dam. The new dam, like the old, was designed to store water for irrigating the Ma'rib plains. However, the
      <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">
       wadi
      </a>
      bed at the new dam site consists of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alluvial_sand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alluvial sand (page does not exist)">
       alluvial sand
      </a>
      and gravel material 3050 m thick.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seepage" title="Seepage">
       Seepage
      </a>
      emanates from this dam that does not threaten its structure,but does lose water. As a way of capturing the seepage, consideration is being given to rebuilding the ancient Ma'rib dam, both as a functioning structure, and also as a historic monument and tourist attraction. The complexity and volume of work involved in this project make it necessary that several organizations work together under the aegis of
      <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
       UNESCO
      </a>
      , using financial contributions from international organizations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Newly constructed dams in Yemen, such as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Batis_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Batis Dam (page does not exist)">
       Batis Dam
      </a>
      and Ma'rib, are used to collect water for the population. A newspaper wrote in July 2010: "The water level in the Ma'rib Dam has risen to 45 million cubic meters as a result of the heavy rainfall in various areas of Yemen in the past two days. Under-Secretary of Ma'rib province,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ali_Muhammad_al-Fatimid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ali Muhammad al-Fatimid (page does not exist)">
       Ali Muhammad al-Fatimid
      </a>
      , was briefed Tuesday on the amount of water flowing into the dam and the maintenance of drainage channels and sub systems. The Deputy Director of the Ma'rib Dam Project, Engineer
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ahmed_al-Arifi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ahmed al-Arifi (page does not exist)">
       Ahmed al-Arifi
      </a>
      , said the water level in the dam on Tuesday rose to 45 million cubic meters, while the water level last April rose to 100 million cubic meters. The gate of the main channel for groundwater was opened in the valley of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ubaida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ubaida (page does not exist)">
       Ubaida
      </a>
      for three months, where the quantity of water flowing amounted to 80 million cubic meters."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ma'rib Dam was built without an environmental impact assessment study, and some of the effects of the dam were not anticipated. In 1995 and 1996 its impact on water quality, agriculture and groundwater recharge and socioeconomics was studied. Lake water could become overly laden with plant nutrients when floods are weak and algae growth is not controlled. Introducing
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tilapia_nilotica" title="Tilapia nilotica">
        Tilapia nilotica
       </a>
      </i>
      provided biological control of algae growth. The dam positively affected agriculture and groundwater within the designed irrigation scheme, but negatively affected them beyond it. The dam also negatively affected health conditions in the lands downstream, while increasing conflicts over water distribution. It positively affected women by allowing them to work in agriculture and participate in decision-making
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . The dam raised income levels of farmers and encouraged tourism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
