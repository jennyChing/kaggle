             <h2>
              <b>
               Healthcare Analytics Summit: Day 1
              </b>
             </h2>
             <p>
              Data can transform the healthcare industry. Thats what 88 percent of the attendees of the first-ever Healthcare Analytics Summit said going into day one. But it was just a start. After the welcome from Dan Burton, Health Catalyst CEO, and an introduction to the days events and the summit app, it was clear that data would be front and center. Youll gain more insight into the data collected at HAS14 through our
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/HAS14-Day1-Infographic.pdf" target="_blank">
               infographic
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              Thats when the festivities began.
             </p>
             <h3>
              Keynote: How Geisinger Uses Analytics to Transform Healthcare
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               Glenn D. Steele, Jr., MD,
              </b>
              <b>
               President and Chief Executive Officer, Geisinger Health System
              </b>
              <b>
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              First up? Dr. Glenn Steele. The packed house listened  and interacted (something theyd do in each session throughout the day)  as Dr. Steele gave detailed insights into Geisingers history and experience with data, including the objections and obstacles the organization faced. A favorite complaint was Well be robots without variation. ProvenCare  what Geisinger calls its data-driven care delivery  proved this wrong. Today the system continues to rely on data to transform healthcare through affordable coverage, payment for value and coordinated care. The real good comes when healthcare organizations start to change behaviors  but none of this good happens unless data comes first.
             </p>
             <h3>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/glenn-steele-HAS.png">
               <img alt="glenn steele HAS" class="aligncenter wp-image-12937" height="478" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/glenn-steele-HAS.png" width="720"/>
              </a>
             </h3>
             <h3>
              Keynote: How Cleveland Clinic Uses Data to Transform the Patient Experience
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               James Merlino, MD,
              </b>
              <b>
               Chief Experience Officer, Cleveland Clinic
              </b>
              <b>
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Next, Dr. James Merlino took the stage. A colorectal surgeon,  and a funny one at that  Dr. Merlino now heads the patient experience efforts at the Cleveland Clinic. Interestingly, Dr. Merlino made it known you cant rely solely on feedback. To really know what the patient wants, you have to look at the data. For example, data coupled with feedback has indicated that patients are concerned about the experience, being kept informed and being treated as an individual. Data is also put to use to make improvements in the patient experience. Workflow, for example, changed once the organization started studying the data from their own patients and other industries and companies, like FedEx.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Jim-Merlino-HAS.png">
               <img alt="Jim Merlino HAS" class="aligncenter wp-image-12940" height="478" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Jim-Merlino-HAS.png" width="720"/>
              </a>
              <b>
              </b>
             </p>
             <h3>
              <b>
               Building an Analytics Strategy Based on the Healthcare Analytics Adoption Model
              </b>
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               Dale Sanders, SVP Health Catalyst
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Dale Sanders presented the
              <a href="/healthcare-analytics-adoption-model/">
               Healthcare Analytics Adoption
              </a>
              model, a way to frame and build an analytics strategy for a healthcare organization, based on the HIMSS EMR Adoption Model. He went step-by-step for how to evaluate an organization in the model. After participants finished the exercise, they were able to email or print themselves a copy of the evaluation.
             </p>
             <p>
              According to a survey taken before the presentation 2.6 percent operate at Level 7-8 of the model, 10.8 percent at Level 5-6, 27.8 percent at Level 3-4, 38.7 percent at Level 1-2, and 20.0 percent were at the bottom, Level 0.
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/HAAM.png">
               <img alt="HAAM" class="aligncenter wp-image-12939" height="540" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/HAAM.png" width="720"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <h3 style="text-align: left;">
              Making Healthcare Waste Reduction Actionable
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               Dr. David A. Burton, Health Catalyst Sr. Vice President, and Greg Stock, Thibodaux Regional Medical Center CEO
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Dr. David A. Burton and Mr. Greg Stock, CEO of Thibodaux Regional Medical Center, shared the timely topic of healthcare waste reduction with a packed house in this post-lunch session. One of the most liked moments by the audience was a quote Stock shared, In the kingdom of the blind, the one-eyed man is king, by Desiderius Erasmus. What does the saying mean? Even if somebody is not perfect in something, in comparison to others who are worse than him or her, he or she is considered to be the best. At one time Thibodaux just compared itself to other hospitals. If they were slightly better, that was good enough. But, good enough doesnt work anymore, says Stock. Thibodaux has and continues to be in a cultural transformation to achieve the IHI Triple AIM: improving the patient experience of care (including quality and satisfaction), improving the health of populations, and reducing the per capita cost of health care. Waste reduction is critical to achieving these goals. To help better explain the concept, Dr. Burton shared the
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/anatomy-of-healthcare-delivery.pdf">
               Anatomy of Healthcare Delivery Model
              </a>
              , a framework hes developed over 20 years, which includes information about the three primary categories of waste: ordering, workflow and defect. Workflow, incidentally, has the largest opportunity.
             </p>
             <h3>
              Organizing for Analytics Success
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               Health Catalyst Co-Founder Steve Barlow and Holly Rimmasch, Health Catalyst Chief Clinical Officer
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              What happens if you dont have interaction? We learned in session # 7 (Organizing for Analytics Success) from Health Catalyst chief clinical officer Holly Rimmasch, that what you ask for and what you get are often two different things. See for yourself:
             </p>
             <p>
              What the intended image should look like:
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Organizing-Analytics-Success.png">
               <img alt="Organizing Analytics Success" class="aligncenter wp-image-12943" height="720" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Organizing-Analytics-Success.png" width="720"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              But when you dont have interaction during the development process, you end up with something more like this:
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Organizing-analytics-success-2.png">

             <h3>
              Overview of the Healthcare Analytics Market
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               Jim Adams, Executive Director, The Advisory Board
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Until a few years ago, the healthcare market was fairly stable and the business model was straightforward: as costs went up, providers raised prices and insurers raised premiums. But now the business model has changed, explained by The Advisory Boards
              <a href="/jim-adams-intuition-based-healthcare-data-informed-healthcare">
               Jim Adams
              </a>
              as he spoke at day 1 of the Healthcare Analytics Summit.
             </p>
             <p>
              In todays healthcare market, the two new major themes are health management and the retail revolution (retail clinics). The retail environment means that individuals are making more decisions about their own care. It also means individuals are more price sensitive, so they are more inclined to vote for high-deductible health plans. This translates into a whole new set of requirements in the retail market because theres more competition and individual choice  just like in a free market. It also means that prices should go down and quality should go up  a great benefit. But a free market brings with it more competition for healthcare providers, along with new prices and quality measures that have to be met. As a result, healthcare today needs to collect data from many different sources, bringing about the top three BI challenges in healthcare: culture,
              <a href="/demystifying-healthcare-data-governance">
               data governance
              </a>
              and organization.
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Top-3-challenges.png">
               <img alt="Top 3 challenges" class="aligncenter wp-image-12945" height="528" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Top-3-challenges.png" width="702"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <h3 style="text-align: left;">
              Getting the Most out of Your Data Analyst
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               John Wadsworth, Health Catalyst Vice President
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Truth be told, minions are adorable. But they dont make for the best analysts. You may get what you want to hear when your analyst acts as little more than a minion, but to really get, grasp, accept and act upon your data, you need to empower your analysts to give you the straight scoop  nothing but the facts.
             </p>
             <p>
              This was the message shared by Health Catalysts John Wadsworth, who did everything in his power to
              <b>
               not
              </b>
              equip his attendees for success. For example, he made some attendees sort coins wearing oven mitts while others were expected to quickly analyze the contents of coin-filled jars sealed with duct tape. But there was a method to the madness  to prove a point. If you really want to use your data, let your analysts perform the jobs they were hired to do: analyze data and tell you the truth about whats really there. You can read more of what Wadsworth has to say on
              <a href="/3-frequent-mistakes-healthcare-data-analytics">
               common mistakes in healthcare data analytics
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/John-Wadsworth-analyst-and-minions.png">

             <h3>
              How To Make Analytics A Strategic, C-Level Imperative
             </h3>
             <p>
              <b>
               Gene Thomas, CIO Memorial Gulfport Hospital, and Jon Brown,
              </b>
              <b>
               Vice President and Chief Information Officer, Memorial Hospital Gulfport
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              As the CIO at
              <a href="http://www.gulfportmemorial.com/default.cfm" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.gulfportmemorial.com/default.cfm', 'Memorial Gulfport Hospital');">
               Memorial Gulfport Hospital
              </a>
              , Gene Thomas stated that CIOs can bend the cost curve but simply having the data isnt enough. What you DO with the data is what will improve that curve and quality will be a byproduct. In turn, that means who an organization selects as its analytics partner is more important than who they select as an EMR partner.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/two-key-decisions.png">
               <img alt="two key decisions" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-12946" height="249" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/two-key-decisions.png" width="337"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              Thomas also believes that the challenge of people coming into the hospital with unmanaged manageable conditions can be addressed with analytics.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.mission-health.org/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.mission-health.org/', 'Mission Healthcare');">
               Mission Healthcare
              </a>
              is based in Asheville, NC, which is predominantly a retirement community. Therefore, it is no surprise that 75% of Missions volume is Medicare/Medicaid. This fact makes the drive to attain higher quality at lower cost front and center.
             </p>
             <p>
              Mission knew that it needed change. So, says Jon Brown, Vice President, Associate CIO Information Technology Mission Health, they launched a campaign called Mission re:DESIGN where the aim is to improve internal operations while remaining flexible to the external demographics and the aging community. At the start, they had no access to data that would help them trend what was happening in in various care areas to inform any change. But, over time, Mission gained valuable access to the data  and the trends (specifically workflow and technology) were eye opening. Change resulted until they had analysis paralysis (Data became cool at Mission), a state that included plenty of fancy pivot tables and Excel analyses, all the while nothing was changing to improve quality and lower costs. That meant more work had to be done, including highlighting whats in it for the patient, as seen in this video.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2ckk-d5bPHY" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2ckk-d5bPHY', 'https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2ckk-d5bPHY');">
               https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2ckk-d5bPHY
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              Teaching people how to USE that data was key in terms of organizational readiness for Mission. The fundamental question everyone should ask is: How does this information change how I am going to provide care? Says Brown, If you cant answer question, then its probably not worth sharing the analysis.
             </p>

              Creating Physician Engagement
             </h3>
             <p>

              <a href="/proven-physician-engagement-strategies">
               physician engagement
              </a>
              . But, say Dr. Bryan Oshiro and Dr. Christopher D. Spahr, building physician engagement means developing a partnership between the organizations leadership and physicians. And this isnt always so easy.
             </p>
             <p>
              Since the goals of physicians differ from hospital administrators, getting them on the same page as key. This starts with a visionary leader, something that Drs. Oshiro and Spahr illustrated through Tinker Toys (Tinker Toys? you say. Yes, read on.). The toys were used in a building exercise that showed the importance of a well-organized team with a solid set of goals. Its the same approach required in healthcare. The pair also shared a list of best practices for getting physician engagement. Included were training and supporting the leader, putting physicians at the helm of teams building clinical quality programs, engaging intellect, and making data transparent, among others. You can read more about Dr. Oshiros own experience getting buy in

But that was the point: to obtain user feedback on future products and a user group.              <h3>
              Healthcare Reform 2.0: Anticipating Whats Next
             </h3>

              <b>
              </b>

Says Leavitt, the healthcare reform debate today is our generations greatest challenge, akin to the Great Wars and the Great Depression of the past. The debate, interesting enough, is as much about who will run the system as it is anything else.

             <h3>
              The Acceleration of Technology in the 21
              <sup>
               st
              </sup>
              Century: Impacts on Healthcare and the Environment
             </h3>
             <p>
              <a href="https://healthcatalyst.app.box.com/files/0/f/2444808335/1/f_21001420507" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://healthcatalyst.app.box.com/files/0/f/2444808335/1/f_21001420507', 'Ray Kurzweil');">
               <b>
                Ray Kurzweil
               </b>
              </a>
              <b>
               ,
              </b>
              <b>
               Chairman, Kurzweil Technologies and Director of Engineering, Google
              </b>
              <b>
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              To say Ray Kurzweil is interesting, diverse, or brilliant would be an understatement. Kurzweil is all three. And during the days final presentation, he was on hand to discuss  the future  and the merger of healthcare and technology. Our bodies, says Kurzweil, are remarkable and very much like technology. He relates the body to being akin to having 23,000 software programs called genes.
             </p>
             <p>
              Looking to the future, Kurzweils message becomes clear: theres a difference between linear and exponential growth and how exponential growth impacts technology and healthcare. Technological gains, for example, are exponential. With the law of accelerating trends, advancements in technology will continue at an exponential rate. And the price performance capacity of information technology will make a lot of it more cost-effect. Social media, for example, wasnt cost-effective until about 10 years ago. Before, says Kurzweil, it was just too expensive to download all those cat pictures.
             </p>

              Healthcare will benefit from the same exponential growth rate in the future.

You can read more of what Wadsworth has to say on &lt;a href=\"\/3-frequent-mistakes-healthcare-data-analytics\"&gt;common mistakes in healthcare data analytics&lt;\/a&gt;.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/John-Wadsworth-analyst-and-minions.png\"&gt;&lt;img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-12941\" alt=\"John Wadsworth analyst and minions\" src=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/John-Wadsworth-analyst-and-minions.png\" width=\"673\" height=\"900\" \/&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n&lt;h3&gt;How To Make Analytics A Strategic, C-Level Imperative&lt;\/h3&gt;\r\n&lt;b&gt;Gene Thomas, CIO Memorial Gulfport Hospital, and Jon Brown, &lt;\/b&gt;&lt;b&gt;Vice President and Chief Information Officer, Memorial Hospital Gulfport&lt;\/b&gt;\r\n\r\nAs the CIO at &lt;a href=\"http:\/\/www.gulfportmemorial.com\/default.cfm\"&gt;Memorial Gulfport Hospital&lt;\/a&gt;, Gene Thomas stated that CIOs can bend the cost curve but simply having the data isn\u2019t enough. What you DO with the data is what will improve that curve and quality will be a byproduct. In turn, that means who an organization selects as its analytics partner is more important than who they select as an EMR partner.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/two-key-decisions.png\"&gt;&lt;img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-12946\" alt=\"two key decisions\" src=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/two-key-decisions.png\" width=\"337\" height=\"249\" \/&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n\r\nThomas also believes that the challenge of people coming into the hospital with \u201cunmanaged manageable conditions\u201d can be addressed with analytics.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"http:\/\/www.mission-health.org\/\"&gt;Mission Healthcare&lt;\/a&gt; is based in Asheville, NC, which is predominantly a retirement community. Therefore, it is no surprise that 75% of Mission\u2019s volume is Medicare\/Medicaid. This fact makes the drive to attain higher quality at lower cost front and center.\r\n\r\nMission knew that it needed change. So, says Jon Brown, Vice President, Associate CIO Information Technology Mission Health, they launched a campaign called \u201cMission re:DESIGN\u201d where the aim is to improve internal operations while remaining flexible to the external demographics and the aging community. \u00a0At the start, they had no access to data that would help them trend what was happening in in various care areas to inform any change. But, over time, Mission gained valuable access to the data \u2013 and the trends (specifically workflow and technology) were eye opening. Change resulted\u2026 until they had \u201canalysis paralysis\u201d (\u201cData became cool at Mission\u201d), a state that included plenty of fancy pivot tables and Excel analyses, all the while nothing was changing to improve quality and lower costs. That meant more work had to be done, including highlighting what\u2019s in it for the patient, as seen in this video.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=2ckk-d5bPHY\"&gt;https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=2ckk-d5bPHY&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n\r\nTeaching people how to USE that data was key in terms of organizational readiness for Mission. The fundamental question everyone should ask is: \u201cHow does this information change how I am going to provide care?\u201d Says Brown, \u201cIf you can\u2019t answer question, then it\u2019s probably not worth sharing the analysis.\u201d\r\n&lt;h3&gt;Creating Physician Engagement&lt;\/h3&gt;\r\n&lt;b&gt;Dr. Bryan Oshiro, Health Catalyst Chief Medical Officer, and Dr. Christopher D. Spahr, E&lt;\/b&gt;&lt;b&gt;nterprise Quality Program Development Executive, Children\u2019s Hospital of Wisconsin&lt;\/b&gt;&lt;b&gt;&lt;\/b&gt;\r\n\r\nTwo percent margins can change how a health system views things \u2013 including &lt;a href=\"\/proven-physician-engagement-strategies\"&gt;physician engagement&lt;\/a&gt;. But, say Dr. Bryan Oshiro and Dr. Christopher D. Spahr, building physician engagement means developing a partnership between the organization\u2019s leadership and physicians. And this isn\u2019t always so easy.\r\n\r\nSince the goals of physicians differ from hospital administrators, getting them on the same page as key. This starts with a visionary leader, something that Drs. Oshiro and Spahr illustrated through Tinker Toys (\u201cTinker Toys?\u201d you say. Yes, read on.). The toys were used in a building exercise that showed the importance of a well-organized team with a solid set of goals. It\u2019s the same approach required in healthcare. The pair also shared a list of best practices for getting physician engagement. Included were training and supporting the leader, putting physicians at the helm of teams building clinical quality programs, engaging intellect, and making data transparent, among others. You can read more about Dr. Oshiro\u2019s own experience getting buy in &lt;a href=\"\/wake-up-call-how-data-saves-lives-has-2014\"&gt;here&lt;\/a&gt;.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/health-catalyst-twitter.jpg\"&gt;&lt;img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-12969\" alt=\"health catalyst twitter\" src=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/health-catalyst-twitter.jpg\" width=\"600\" height=\"450\" \/&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n&lt;h3&gt;User Group Kickoff and Product Roadmap&lt;\/h3&gt;\r\n&lt;b&gt;Holly Rimmasch, Health Catalyst Chief Clinical Officer; Tom Burton, Co-Founder; Steve Barlow, Co-Founder (collectively, \u201cthe Three Amigos\u201d); and Eric Just, Vice President &lt;\/b&gt;\r\n\r\nThe user group kickoff and product roadmap session won the prize for soliciting the most audience feedback in a single session. But that was the point: to obtain user feedback on future products and a user group. Tom Burton, Holly Rimmasch and Steve Barlow, or as they will forever after be known, the Three Amigos, introduced the session by discussing three systems -- content, deployment and analytics.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/the-three-amigos-HAS.png\"&gt;&lt;img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-12944\" alt=\"the three amigos HAS\" src=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/the-three-amigos-HAS.png\" width=\"669\" height=\"669\" \/&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n\r\nThe Amigos also introduced the concept of three categories of metrics -- outcomes, shared risk\/revenue and technology \u2014 which can each lead to the same destination, &lt;a href=\"\/implementing-healthcare-performance-improvement-initiatives\"&gt;performance improvement&lt;\/a&gt;. Audience feedback indicated that the top two content category interests were population health and workflows. The top two financial categories of interest were improved care management and advanced shared risk solutions.\r\n&lt;h3&gt;Healthcare Reform 2.0: Anticipating What\u2019s Next&lt;\/h3&gt;\r\n&lt;b&gt;Mike Leavitt, former Utah governor and U.S. HHS administrator&lt;\/b&gt;&lt;b&gt;&lt;\/b&gt;\r\n\r\nWill the ACA be substantially amended? That\u2019s one of the topics discussed by former U.S. HHS administrator and Utah governor Mike Leavitt. Says Leavitt, the healthcare reform debate today is our generation\u2019s greatest challenge, akin to the Great Wars and the Great Depression of the past. The debate, interesting enough, is as much about who will run the system as it is anything else.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/Gov-Leavitt-HAS.png\"&gt;&lt;img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-12938\" alt=\"Gov Leavitt HAS\" src=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/Gov-Leavitt-HAS.png\" width=\"654\" height=\"471\" \/&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n\r\nBut the key to reforming healthcare, says Leavitt, is reforming Medicare, which permeates the entire delivery system. And that puts the federal government in a sticky situation \u2013 what is their role in the system they\u2019ve created?\r\n&lt;h3&gt;The Acceleration of Technology in the 21&lt;sup&gt;st&lt;\/sup&gt; Century: Impacts on Healthcare and the Environment&lt;\/h3&gt;\r\n&lt;a href=\"https:\/\/healthcatalyst.app.box.com\/files\/0\/f\/2444808335\/1\/f_21001420507\"&gt;&lt;b&gt;Ray Kurzweil&lt;\/b&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;&lt;b&gt;, &lt;\/b&gt;&lt;b&gt;Chairman, Kurzweil Technologies and Director of Engineering, Google&lt;\/b&gt;&lt;b&gt;&lt;\/b&gt;\r\n\r\nTo say Ray Kurzweil is interesting, diverse, or brilliant would be an understatement. Kurzweil is all three. And during the day\u2019s final presentation, he was on hand to discuss \u2026 the future \u2014 and the merger of healthcare and technology. Our bodies, says Kurzweil, are remarkable and very much like technology. He relates the body to being akin to having 23,000 software programs called genes.\r\n\r\nLooking to the future, Kurzweil\u2019s message becomes clear: there\u2019s a difference between linear and exponential growth and how exponential growth impacts technology and healthcare. Technological gains, for example, are exponential. With the law of accelerating trends, advancements in technology will continue at an exponential rate. And the price performance capacity of information technology will make a lot of it more cost-effect. Social media, for example, wasn\u2019t cost-effective until about 10 years ago. Before, says Kurzweil, it was just too expensive to download all those cat pictures.\r\n\r\nHealthcare will benefit from the same exponential growth rate in the future.\r\n\r\n&lt;a href=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/ray-kurzweil-HAS.jpg\"&gt;&lt;img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-12970\" alt=\"ray kurzweil HAS\" src=\"\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/09\/ray-kurzweil-HAS.jpg\" width=\"600\" height=\"337\" \/&gt;&lt;\/a&gt;\r\n\r\nThere\u2019s plenty more to come for Day 2.
