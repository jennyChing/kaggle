     <p>
      A
      <b>
       shieldmaiden
      </b>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="non" xml:lang="non">
       <i>
        skjaldmr
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
       Danish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="da" xml:lang="da">
       <i>
        skjoldm
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
       Norwegian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="no" xml:lang="no">
       <i>
        skjoldmy
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
       Swedish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv">
       <i>
        skldm
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
       <i>
        Schildmaid
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was a woman who had chosen to fight as a warrior in
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_folklore" title="Scandinavian folklore">
       Scandinavian folklore
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
       mythology
      </a>
      . They are often mentioned in
      <a href="/wiki/Saga" title="Saga">
       sagas
      </a>
      such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hervarar_saga_ok_Hei%C3%B0reks" title="Hervarar saga ok Heireks">
        Hervarar saga ok Heireks
       </a>
      </i>
      and in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gesta_Danorum" title="Gesta Danorum">
        Gesta Danorum
       </a>
      </i>
      . Shieldmaidens also appear in stories of other
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
       Germanic peoples
      </a>
      :
      <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">
       Goths
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cimbri" title="Cimbri">
       Cimbri
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Marcomanni" title="Marcomanni">
       Marcomanni
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordisk_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nordisk-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The mythical
      <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">
       Valkyries
      </a>
      may have been based on the shieldmaidens.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nordisk_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nordisk-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The historical existence of shieldmaidens is heavily debated, but scholars including
      <a href="/wiki/Lars_Magnar_Enoksen" title="Lars Magnar Enoksen">
       Lars Magnar Enoksen
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Neil_Price_(archaeologist)" title="Neil Price (archaeologist)">
       Neil Price
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history.com_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-history.com-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and scholar Britt-Mari Nsstrm
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      argue evidence for their existence, while scholar Judith Jesch disagrees, citing lack of hard evidence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_accounts">
       Historical accounts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shieldmaiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical accounts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are few historical attestations that
      <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">
       Viking Age
      </a>
      women took part in warfare,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harrison-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but the
      <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
       Byzantine
      </a>
      historian
      <a href="/wiki/John_Skylitzes" title="John Skylitzes">
       John Skylitzes
      </a>
      records that women fought in battle when
      <a href="/wiki/Sviatoslav_I_of_Kiev" title="Sviatoslav I of Kiev">
       Sviatoslav I of Kiev
      </a>
      attacked the Byzantines in
      <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
       Bulgaria
      </a>
      in 971.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harrison-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the
      <a href="/wiki/Varangians" title="Varangians">
       Varangians
      </a>
      had
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Dorostolon" title="Siege of Dorostolon">
       suffered a devastating defeat
      </a>
      , the victors were stunned at discovering armed women among the fallen warriors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harrison-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When
      <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">
       Leif Erikson
      </a>
      's pregnant half-sister
      <a href="/wiki/Freyd%C3%ADs_Eir%C3%ADksd%C3%B3ttir" title="Freyds Eirksdttir">
       Freyds Eirksdttir
      </a>
      was in
      <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">
       Vinland
      </a>
      , she is reported to have taken up a sword, and, bare-breasted, scared away the attacking
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
       Native Americans
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harrison-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fight is recounted in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland_saga" title="Greenland saga">
        Greenland saga
       </a>
      </i>
      , though Freyds is not explicitly referred to as a shieldmaiden in the text.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thorsson_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Thorsson-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Saxo Grammaticus reported that shieldmaidens fought on the side of the
      <a href="/wiki/Danes_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Danes (Germanic tribe)">
       Danes
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Br%C3%A1vellir" title="Battle of Brvellir">
       Battle of Brvellir
      </a>
      in the year 750:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Now out of the town of Sle, under the captains
       <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0r" title="Heir">
        Hetha
       </a>
       (Heid) and Wisna, with Hakon Cut-cheek came Tummi the Sailmaker. On these captains, who had the bodies of women, nature bestowed the souls of men. Webiorg was also inspired with the same spirit, and was attended by Bo (Bui) Bramason and Brat the Jute, thirsting for war.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       
       <a href="/wiki/Saxo_Grammaticus" title="Saxo Grammaticus">
        Saxo Grammaticus
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saxo_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Saxo-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legendary_accounts">
       Legendary accounts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shieldmaiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legendary accounts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Examples of shieldmaidens mentioned by name in the Norse sagas include
      <a href="/wiki/Brynhildr" title="Brynhildr">
       Brynhildr
      </a>
      in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C7%ABlsunga_saga" title="Vlsunga saga">
        Vlsunga saga
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hervor" title="Hervor">
       Hervor
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hervarar_saga_ok_Hei%C3%B0reks" title="Hervarar saga ok Heireks">
        Hervarar saga ok Heireks
       </a>
      </i>
      , the Brynhildr of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B3sa_saga_ok_Herrau%C3%B0s" title="Bsa saga ok Herraus">
        Bsa saga ok Herraus
       </a>
      </i>
      , the Swedish princess Thornbjrg in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hr%C3%B3lfs_saga_Gautrekssonar" title="Hrlfs saga Gautrekssonar">
        Hrlfs saga Gautrekssonar
       </a>
      </i>
      and Princess Hed, Visna and
      <a href="/wiki/Veborg" title="Veborg">
       Veborg
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gesta_Danorum" title="Gesta Danorum">
        Gesta Danorum
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Two shieldmaidens appear in certain translations of the
      <i>
       Hervarar saga
      </i>
      . The first of these Hervors was known to have taken up typically masculine roles early in her childhood and often raided travelers in the woods dressed as a man. Later in her life, she claimed the cursed sword
      <a href="/wiki/Tyrfing" title="Tyrfing">
       Tyrfing
      </a>
      from her father's burial site and became a seafaring raider. She eventually settled and married. Her granddaughter was also named Hervor and commanded forces against attacking
      <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">
       Huns
      </a>
      . Although the saga remarks on her bravery she is mortally wounded by enemy forces and dies on the battlefield.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tolkien_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tolkien-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scholars Judith Jesch and Jenny Jochens theorize that shieldmaidens' often grim fates or their sudden return to typically female roles is a testament to their role as figures of both male and female fantasy as well as emblematic of the danger of abandoning gender roles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tolkien_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tolkien-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       Brynhildr Buladttir and Gurn Gjkadtti

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Brynhildr" title="Brynhildr">
       Brynhildr
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Brynhildr of the
      <i>
       Vlsunga saga
      </i>
      , along with her rival in love,
      <a href="/wiki/Gudrun" title="Gudrun">
       Gurn Gjkadttir
      </a>
      , provides an example of how a shieldmaiden compares to more conventional aristocratic womanhood in the sagas. Brynhildr is chiefly concerned with honor, much like a male warrior. When she ends up married to Gudrun's brother
      <a href="/wiki/Gunther" title="Gunther">
       Gunnarr
      </a>
      instead of
      <a href="/wiki/Sigurd" title="Sigurd">
       Sigurr
      </a>
      , the man she intended to marry, Brynhildr speaks a verse comparing the courage of the two men:
     </p>
     <div class="poem">
      <p>
       Sigurd fought the dragon
       <br/>
       And that afterward will be
       <br/>
       Forgotten by no one
       <br/>
       While men still live.
       <br/>
       Yet your brother
       <br/>
       Neither dared
       <br/>
       To ride into the fire
       <br/>
       Nor to leap across it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byock_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Byock-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         10
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </div>
     <p>
      <br/>
      Brynhildr is married to Gunnarr and not Sigurr because of deceit and trickery, including a potion of forgetfulness given to Sigurr so he forgets his previous relationship with her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byock_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Byock-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brynhildr is upset not only for the loss of Sigurr but also for the dishonesty involved. Similar to her male counterparts, the shieldmaiden prefers to do things straightforwardly, without the deception considered stereotypically feminine in much of medieval literature. She enacts her vengeance directly, resulting in the deaths of herself, Sigurr, and Sigur's son by Gurn. By killing the child, she demonstrates an understanding of feud and filial responsibility; if he lived, the boy would grow up to take vengeance on Brynhildr's family.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gurn has a similar concern with family ties, but at first does not usually act directly. She is more inclined to incite her male relatives to action than take up arms herself. Gurn is no shieldmaiden, and Brynhildr mocks her for this, saying, "Only ask what is best for you to know. That is suitable for noble women. And it is easy to be satisfied while everything happens according to your desires.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byock_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Byock-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In her later marriages, however, she is willing to kill her children, burn down a hall, and send her other sons to avenge the murder of her daughter, Svanhildr. In the world of the sagas, women can be both honorable and remorseless, much like the male heroes. While a shieldmaiden does not fill a woman's typical role, her strength of character is found in even the more domestic women in these stories.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Archeology">
       Archeology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shieldmaiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Archeology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Graves of women containing weapons have been uncovered, but scholars do not agree how these should be interpreted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Norse immigrant graves in
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      and chemical analysis of the remains suggested a somewhat equal distribution of men and women, suggesting husbands and wives, while some of the women were buried with weapons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a tie-in special to the TV series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vikings_(TV_series)" title="Vikings (TV series)">
        Vikings
       </a>
      </i>
      Neil Price showed that a
      <a href="/wiki/Birka" title="Birka">
       Birka
      </a>
      -burial excavated in the 1970s containing a large number of weapons and the bones of two horses turned out to be the grave of a woman upon bone analysis by Anna Kjellstrm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history.com_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-history.com-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">
       In popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shieldmaiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While women warriors are a staple of
      <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">
       fantasy
      </a>
      , they are not often referred to as shieldmaidens. Some who are include
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89owyn" title="owyn">
       owyn
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">
       J. R. R. Tolkien
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">
        The Lord of the Rings
       </a>
      </i>
      and Thorgil in
      <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Farmer" title="Nancy Farmer">
       Nancy Farmer
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sea_of_Trolls" title="The Sea of Trolls">
        The Sea of Trolls
       </a>
      </i>
      trilogy.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 2013 TV series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vikings_(TV_series)" title="Vikings (TV series)">
        Vikings
       </a>
      </i>
      , the legendary Viking shieldmaiden
      <a href="/wiki/Lagertha" title="Lagertha">
       Lagertha
      </a>
      , played by
      <a href="/wiki/Katheryn_Winnick" title="Katheryn Winnick">
       Katheryn Winnick
      </a>
      , is a principal character.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the fantasy book series
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Forever_Avalon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Forever Avalon (page does not exist)">
        Forever Avalon
       </a>
      </i>
      by Mark Piggott, Shield Maidens are a cast of female warriors who serve side-by-side with the Knights of the Round Table. The Shield Maiden Sarafina is the adopted daughter of Lord Bryan MoonDrake,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gil-Gamesh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gil-Gamesh (page does not exist)">
       Gil-Gamesh
      </a>
      of Avalon, and serves as his Shield Maiden. The Shield Maidens of Avalon train at the Convent at Glennish Hills. Their patron is a variation of the Holy Trinity ... the
      <a href="/wiki/Lady_of_the_Lake" title="Lady of the Lake">
       Lady of the Lake
      </a>
      , Queen
      <a href="/wiki/Guinevere" title="Guinevere">
       Guinevere
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">
       Virgin Mary
      </a>
      .
     </p>
