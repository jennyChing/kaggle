          <p>
           <em>
            <a href="http://blog.chron.com/bakerblog/files/2014/02/BIV-Nullification.jpg">
             <img alt="BIV-Nullification" class="size-full wp-image-6094 alignright" height="298" src="http://blog.chron.com/bakerblog/files/2014/02/BIV-Nullification.jpg" width="300"/>
            </a>
            Jury nullification  when a jury disagrees with the law and acquits a defendant they believe to be guilty  has a long history in the United States of serving as a bellwether for social change on unpopular laws. There are numerous historical examples of juries nullifying laws that would later change due to vast public opposition. More recently, legal professionals have identified jury nullification verdicts in marijuana cases. This should not be surprising given societys rapidly changing view of marijuana  support for legalization now polls at 58 percent nationally, and in Texas it polls equally well. In this installment of
            <a href="http://bakerinstitute.org/drug-policy-program/baker-institute-viewpoints/" target="_blank">
             <em>
              Baker Institute Viewpoints
             </em>
            </a>
            , experts discuss the question, What does jury nullification of marijuana cases in Texas indicate about the possibility of marijuana legalization?
           </em>
          </p>

              <em>
               Can jurors hasten the legalization of cannabis?
              </em>
             </a>
             by
            </em>
            <em>
             <i>
              Clay S. Conrad, an attorney and
             </i>
             <i>
              author of
              <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Jury-Nullification-The-Evolution-Doctrine/dp/1939709008" target="_blank">
               
              </a>

             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Jury-Nullification-The-Evolution-Doctrine/dp/1939709008" target="_blank">

           <li>
            <em>
             <a href="http://blog.chron.com/bakerblog/2014/02/jury-nullification-local-option-2/" target="_blank">
              <em>
               Jury nullification: Local option
              </em>
             </a>
            </em>
            <em>
             by
            </em>

             <a href="http://bakerinstitute.org/experts/william-martin/" target="_blank">
              <em>
               William Martin
              </em>
             </a>
             , Ph.D., the Harry and Hazel Chavanne Senior Fellow in Religion and Public Policy at the Baker Institute, the Chavanne Emeritus Professor of Sociology at Rice University and the director of the institutes Drug Policy Program
            </i>
           </li>
           <li>
            <em>
             <a href="http://blog.chron.com/bakerblog/2014/02/6134/" target="_blank">
              <em>
               Ceasefire in the war on marijuana in Texas? Trial by jury and jury nullification
              </em>
             </a>
             by Gilbert G. Garcia, a board certified criminal lawyer and a member of The National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws
            </em>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           The desire for public safety is a force influencing far-reaching legal decisions; the highest court in the country often uses safety as a rationale to reduce restrictions on law enforcement officers to
           <a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/467/649">
            question
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/395/752/case.html">
            search
           </a>
           and
           <a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/html/95-1268.ZO.html">
            seize
           </a>
           individuals. Public safety is also a concern in the minds of average people serving in jury trials  people who deliberate on the cases that affect their peers. In fact, the concern for public safety may be a reason why some juries choose not to support marijuana prohibition penalties.
          </p>
          <p>
           One of the ways marijuana criminalization detracts from public safety is by shifting limited law enforcement resources away from violent crime and toward drug crime.
           <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/neill-franklin/war-on-drugs_b_3461739.html">
            As Neill Franklin, a 33-year police veteran, pointed out,
           </a>
           when the police shifted their focus from combating violent crimes to dealing with drug crimes in the past four decades,
           <a href="http://articles.washingtonpost.com/2008-12-28/news/36894099_1_homicide-victims-cold-case-gang-and-drug-related-killings">
            the rate of murders that were solved rapidly decreased across the nation
           </a>
           . In 1963, before police intensified their fight against illegal drugs, police were able to solve 91 percent of murders. By 2007, when the war on drugs was in full swing, that rate had dropped to 61 percent, despite dramatic advances in forensic sciences that greatly improve murder investigations. The war on drugs is largely a war on marijuana. According to an
           <a href="https://www.aclu.org/billions-dollars-wasted-racially-biased-arrests#mjanalysis">
            original analysis by the American Civil Liberties Union
           </a>
           , marijuana arrests account for the majority of all drug arrests in this country. Most marijuana arrests, as the ACLUs analysis shows, are for simple possession.
          </p>
          <p>
           Some juries may also recognize marijuana criminalization hurts public safety by taking away an individuals option to substitute alcohol with marijuana, which scientists are finding to be a safer alternative. Though researchers have long been trying to figure out whether marijuana legalization would lead to more alcohol consumption  since the substances are possibly complementary  or if it would actually reduce alcohol use  since marijuana could substitute for alcohol  new research points to an answer. In findings published in the
           <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pam.21727/abstract">
            Journal of Policy Analysis and Management
           </a>
           ,
           <i>
           </i>
           researchers discovered marijuana use generally serves as a safer substitute for alcohol consumption. They concluded that marijuana legalization in Colorado and Washington state will lead to less overall alcohol consumption and consequently reduce the social harms of alcohol use. These findings are visible in many states that have modernized their marijuana laws. For example, the researchers discovered that states that legalized medical marijuana saw a decrease in beer sales and a drop in heavy drinking. This discovery is consistent with a prior study showing that states that legalized medical marijuana had a
           <a href="http://pages.uoregon.edu/bchansen/MML_Alcohol_Consumption.pdf">
            13 percent drop
           </a>
           in alcohol-related traffic fatalities. As
           <a href="http://www.politifact.com/florida/statements/2013/aug/19/marijuana-policy-project/pro-pot-group-claims-marijuana-less-toxic-alcohol/">
            objective fact checkers
           </a>
           have shown, marijuana is safer than alcohol. On average, marijuana is 100 times less toxic than alcohol. Scientists have not found a single instance of death caused by marijuana use.
          </p>
          <p>
           Juries on marijuana possession cases may also realize marijuana prohibition threatens public safety by preventing patients in need of medical marijuana from legally acquiring such medicine. If these patients do not turn to illegal sources for marijuana, they may resort to pharmaceuticals, which physicians reveal may be more addictive and deadly as well as less effective than marijuana. Even over-the-counter pain-relieving drugs may be more dangerous than the plant in question.
           <a href="http://www.boston.com/lifestyle/health/blogs/daily-dose/2013/08/09/sanjay-gupta-new-pro-medical-marijuana-stance-irresponsible/rRk8DqzWy3XmTqJFjdFR6K/blog.html">
            Dr. Sanjay Guptas investigation
           </a>
           into medical marijuana use found that for some patients the plant is safer and more effective than common prescription painkillers like oxycodone and dilaudid.
           <a href="http://illinoistimes.com/article-11388-physician-claims-prescription-drugs-worse-than-marijuana.html">
            Dr. David Walters testimony
           </a>
           to the Illinois Senate also noted that many prescription drugs are less efficient, more prone to abuse and overdose, and more mentally impairing than marijuana.
           <a href="http://www.independent.co.uk/life-style/health-and-families/health-news/cannabis-less-harmful-than-aspirin-says-scientist-634183.html">
            Leslie Iversen, a professor of pharmacology at the University of Oxford, has found
           </a>
           even aspirin and related steroidal anti-inflammatory compounds to be more destructive than marijuana, due to their tendency to cause catastrophic gastric bleeding. Scientists
           <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/homeandrecreationalsafety/overdose/facts.html">
            relate pharmaceuticals
           </a>
           to the highest numbers of drug overdose deaths.
          </p>
          <p>
           Whether persuading law enforcement to focus on violent crime rather than drug crime, challenging a law that prohibits less destructive substitutes for alcohol, or shunning a policy preventing patients from acquiring a substance safer than pharmaceuticals, through jury nullification jurors may be prioritizing public safety over applying outdated drug laws. Texas lawmakers should acknowledge that because of their refusal to modernize marijuana laws, jurors charged with representing local communities have to correct the laws that unjustly jeopardize public safety.
          </p>

         <p>
          The mission of the nonpartisan
          <a href="http://www.bakerinstitute.org/">
           James A. Baker III Institute for Public
Policy
          </a>
          is to help bridge the gap between theory and practice of public
policy by drawing together experts from academic, government, media, business
and nongovernmental organizations. By involving policymakers, scholars
and students, the institute seeks to improve the debate on selected public
policy issues and to make a difference in the formulation, implementation and
evaluation of public policy. The institutes more than 20 programs, which include research, speaking series, events and special projects, have helped attract a host of prominent leaders who provide their views and insights on key issues.
         </p>
