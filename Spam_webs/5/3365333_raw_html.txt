        <h1 class="single-post-title">
         Alpheratz, in Andromeda, part of Great Square
        </h1>
        <div class="post_intro clearfix">
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td class="featured_image">
             <img alt="Star Alpheratz, by Princess Andromeda's left eye" class="attachment-post_header_image wp-post-image" height="214" src="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/princess_Andromeda_cropped-300x214.jpg" width="300"/>
             <div class="thumbnail_credit">
              <span>
               Star Alpheratz, by Princess Andromeda's left eye
              </span>
             </div>
            </td>
            <td>
             <table>
              <tr>
               <td class="excerpt_border">

              <tr>
               <td class="excerpt">
                <p>
                 Come to know this star and be one step further along the path of finding the Andromeda galaxy.
                </p>
               </td>
              </tr>
              <tr>
               <td class="excerpt_border">

       <!-- entry header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <!-- PostLinkUnit -->
        <div id="post_link_unit" style="height:15px;padding-top:10px;text-align:left;color:red;margin-left:-5px;">
         <script type="text/javascript">
          googletag.cmd.push(function() { googletag.display('post_link_unit'); });
         </script>
        </div>
        <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_150159" style="width: 410px">
         <a href="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/Alpheratz_Great_Square_Andromeda.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-150159" src="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/Alpheratz_Great_Square_Andromeda.jpg" title="Alpheratz_Great_Square_Andromeda" width="400"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Alpheratz is part of the famous Great Square of Pegasus.  It connects two constellations: Pegasus and Andromeda.  People often find the Andromeda galaxy by star-hopping from Alpheratz to Mirach and Mu Andromedae.  Mirach and Mu point to the galaxy.
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         If you can find the
         <a href="http://earthsky.org/favorite-star-patterns/great-square-of-pegasus-wings-in-sept-equinox" target="_blank">
          Great Square of Pegasus
         </a>
         , you can find the star Alpheratz, one of four medium-bright stars that form this noticeable pattern on our skys dome.  Unlike the other stars in the Great Square, Alpheratz does not belong to the constellation Pegasus.  It belongs, officially, to the constellation Andromeda and in fact is designated as Alpha Andromedae. The star marks the point where Pegasus and
         <a href="http://earthsky.org/tonight/star-hop-in-constellation-andromeda-to-andromeda-galaxy" target="_blank">
          Andromeda
         </a>
         meet.  Alpheratz is a star you might want to come to know, because it can help you spot the famed
         <a href="http://earthsky.org/clusters-nebulae-galaxies/andromeda-galaxy-closest-spiral-to-milky-way" target="_blank">
          Andromeda Galaxy
         </a>
         , also known as M31, the nearest large spiral galaxy to Earth.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When can you see the star Alpheratz?
         </strong>
         This star reaches its
         <em>
          midnight culmination
         </em>
          its highest point in the sky at midnight  on October 9.  So its generally considered an autumn star for Northern Hemisphere observers.  You can see it in summertime, too, of course; it rises late at night then.  And by late winter, Alpheratz is setting with the sun.
        </p>
        <p>
         At magnitude 2.07, Alpheratz isnt as bright as the skys brightest stars.  You can spot it easily, except in very light-polluted areas.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          How can you use Alpheratz to help find the Andromeda Galaxy?
         </strong>
         The Andromeda Galaxy, also known as M31, is the nearest large spiral galaxy to Earth.  Its the most distant thing we can see with the unaided eye.  You need a dark sky to see it.  Autumn is a good time to look.  People often find the Andromeda galaxy by star-hopping from Alpheratz to Mirach and Mu Andromedae.  Mirach and Mu point to the galaxy.  See the chart at the top right of this post.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_144345" style="width: 560px">
         <a href="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2012/08/Andromeda_galaxy_Ted_Van_August_2012.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-144345" src="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2012/08/Andromeda_galaxy_Ted_Van_August_2012.jpeg" title="Andromeda_galaxy_Ted_Van_August_2012" width="550"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          The Andromeda galaxy (upper right of photo) as seen by
          <a href="http://facebook.com/earthsky" target="_blank">
           EarthSky Facebook
          </a>
          friend Ted Van at a Montana campsite in mid-August 2012.  Thank you, Ted!
          <a href="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2012/08/Andromeda_galaxy_Ted_Van_August_2012.jpeg" target="_blank">
           Click here to expand image.
          </a>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_150163" style="width: 210px">
         <a href="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/Alpheratz.gif">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-150163" height="200" src="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/Alpheratz.gif" title="Alpheratz" width="200"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Alpheratz looks like one star to the eye, but spectroscopic analysis reveals that it is two stars.  Image via Wikimedia Commons.
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Science of Alpheratz.
         </strong>
         By the best current estimates, Alpheratz is about 97 light-years away, making it a relatively nearby star.  Its temperature is high  about 13,000 kelvins, more than twice that of our sun.  As a result, its color is primarily blue. The bluer color also represents a relatively high energy output, probably about 200 times that of our sun.
        </p>
        <p>
         Alpheratz appears single, but it is really two stars orbiting a common center of gravity every 97 days.  We know of the binary nature of Alpheratz because its
         <a href="http://earthsky.org/astronomy-essentials/what-is-the-electromagnetic-spectrum" target="_blank">
          light spectrum
         </a>
         shows two distinct sets of
         <em>
          spectroscopic lines
         </em>
         .  The spectral class of this star is
         <em>
          B9p
         </em>
         .  The
         <em>
          B9
         </em>
         represents a star that is much hotter and more massive than our sun.  The
         <em>
          p
         </em>
         indicating that this is a peculiar star.  It is peculiar in the sense that its spectrum reveals an unexpected amount of manganese.  It may also be peculiar due to effects of the nearby companion star.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Alpheratz in star history and mythology
         </strong>
         The most interesting part of this stars history, from our modern perspective, is its assignment to the constellation Andromeda in the 1930s by the International Astronomical Union.  Prior to that, Alpheratz was often referred to as Delta Pegasi, indicating that it was the fourth-brightest star in Pegasus.  But some classical star charts showed it as part of Andromeda.  It was variously considered part of the constellation Pegasus, or part of the constellation Andromeda, or, by some people, part of both constellations.  Today, Alpheratz is officially Alpha Andromedae, the brightest star in Andromeda.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_150170" style="width: 560px">
         <a href="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/princess_Andromeda.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-150170" src="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/princess_Andromeda.jpeg" title="princess_Andromeda" width="550"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          In the mythology of the sky, Princess Andromeda was chained to a rock by the sea.  Perseus, the Hero, saved her and swept her away on Pegasus, the Flying Horse.  Notice the star Alpheratz near the left eye of the Princess.
          <a href="http://en.es-static.us/upl/2010/12/princess_Andromeda.jpeg" target="_blank">
           Click here to expand image.
          </a>
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         The early Arabian stargazers certainly saw Alpheratz as part of Pegasus, not Andromeda.  We know this because the name Alpheratz derives from an Arabic phrase meaning the horses navel, an obvious reference to Pegasus the Flying Horse. According to Richard Hinckley Allen in his classic book
         <a href="http://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Gazetteer/Topics/astronomy/_Texts/secondary/ALLSTA/home.html" target="_blank">
          Star Names, Their Lore and Meaning,
         </a>
         Alpheratz was called
         <em>
          Al Surrat al Faras
         </em>
         (the horses navel).  That name emcompasses another name by which the star still is known today: Sirrah.
        </p>
        <p>
         Alpheratzs position is: RA: 00h 08m 23s, dec: +29 05 27
        </p>
