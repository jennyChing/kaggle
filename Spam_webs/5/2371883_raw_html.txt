     <p>
      <b>
       Weregild
      </b>
      (also spelled
      <i>
       wergild
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       wergeld
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       weregeld
      </i>
      , etc.), also known as "man price", was a value placed on every being and piece of property in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salic_Law" title="Salic Law">
       Salic Code
      </a>
      . If property was stolen, or someone was injured or killed, the guilty person would have to pay weregild as
      <a href="/wiki/Restitution" title="Restitution">
       restitution
      </a>
      to the victim's family or to the owner of the property.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Weregild payment was an important legal mechanism in early
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_society" title="Germanic society">
       Germanic society
      </a>
      ; the other common form of legal reparation at this time was
      <a href="/wiki/Feud#Blood_feuds.2Fvendetta" title="Feud">
       blood revenge
      </a>
      . The payment was typically made to the
      <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">
       family
      </a>
      or to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibb" title="Sibb">
       clan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      No distinction was made between
      <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">
       murder
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">
       manslaughter
      </a>
      until these distinctions were instituted by the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman
      </a>
      imperial law in the 12th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Payment of the weregild was gradually replaced with capital punishment, starting around the 9th century, and almost entirely by the 12th century when weregild began to cease as a practice throughout the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_related_concepts">
       Etymology and related concepts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Weregild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and related concepts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="border:1px solid #aaa;background-color:#f9f9f9">
      <tr>
       <td class="mbox-image">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="1089" data-file-width="1000" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Wiktionary-logo-en.svg/37px-Wiktionary-logo-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Wiktionary-logo-en.svg/55px-Wiktionary-logo-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Wiktionary-logo-en.svg/73px-Wiktionary-logo-en.svg.png 2x" width="37"/>
       </td>
       <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
        Look up
        <i>
         <b>
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/were-" title="wiktionary:were-">
           were-
          </a>
         </b>
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/geld" title="wiktionary:geld">
           geld
          </a>
         </b>
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         <b>
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gild" title="wiktionary:gild">
           gild
          </a>
         </b>
        </i>
        in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       weregild
      </i>
      is composed of
      <i>
       were
      </i>
      , meaning "man", and
      <i>
       geld
      </i>
      , meaning "payment or fee", as in
      <a href="/wiki/Danegeld" title="Danegeld">
       Danegeld
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Geld
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Jeld
      </i>
      was the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
       Old English
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Frisian" title="Old Frisian">
       Old Frisian
      </a>
      word for money, and still is in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_(language)" title="Dutch (language)">
       Dutch
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_(language)" title="German (language)">
       German
      </a>
      . The Danish word
      <i>
       gld
      </i>
      and Norwegian
      <i>
       gjeld
      </i>
      both mean "debt". "-Gld" is also a constituent of some Swedish words, having the same meaning: e.g.
      <i>
       terglda
      </i>
      (retribute, return favor),
      <i>
       gengld
      </i>
      (in return/exchange),
      <i>
       vederglda
      </i>
      (revenge), and the formal/legal term
      <i>
       gldenr
      </i>
      (geldeneer, referring to someone who is indebted).
     </p>
     <p>
      The same concept outside Germanic culture is known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_money_(term)" title="Blood money (term)">
       blood money
      </a>
      . Words include
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ericfine" title="Ericfine">
        ericfine
       </a>
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
       Ireland
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Galanas" title="Galanas">
        galanas
       </a>
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
       Wales
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       veriraha
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
       Finnish
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       vira
      </i>
      ("
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ") in
      <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">
       Russia
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/G%C5%82%C3%B3wszczyzna" title="Gwszczyzna">
        gwszczyzna
       </a>
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">
       Arab world
      </a>
      , the very similar institution of
      <a href="/wiki/Diyya" title="Diyya">
       diyya
      </a>
      is maintained into the present day.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amount">
       Amount
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Weregild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amount">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The size of the weregild was largely conditional upon the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_rank" title="Social rank">
       social rank
      </a>
      of the victim. There used to be somewhat of a "basis" fee for a standard "free man" that could then be multiplied according to the social rank of the victim and the circumstances of the crime. The weregild for women relative to that of men of equal rank varied: among the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">
       Alamanni
      </a>
      it was double the weregild of men, among the Saxons half that of men.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migration_period" title="Migration period">
       Migration period
      </a>
      the standard weregeld for a freeman appears to have been 200
      <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">
       solidi
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">
       shillings
      </a>
      ), an amount reflected as the basic fee due for the death of a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Churl" title="Churl">
        churl
       </a>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       ceorl
      </i>
      ) both in later Anglo-Saxon and continental law codes.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 8th century the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Lex_Alamannorum" title="Lex Alamannorum">
        Lex Alamannorum
       </a>
      </i>
      sets the weregeld for a duke or archbishop at three times the basic value (600 shillings), while the killing of a low ranking cleric was fined with 300, raised to 400 if the cleric was attacked while he was reading mass.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">
       Charlemagne
      </a>
      his
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missi_dominici" title="Missi dominici">
        missi dominici
       </a>
      </i>
      required three times the regular weregild should they be killed whilst on a mission from the king.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 9th century
      <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">
       Mercian
      </a>
      law a regular freeman (
      <a href="/wiki/Churl" title="Churl">
       churl
      </a>
      ) was worth 200 shillings
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (
      <i>
       twyhyndeman
      </i>
      ), and a nobleman was worth 1,200 (
      <i>
       twelfhyndeman
      </i>
      ), a division established enough that two centuries later a
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English#Charter_of_Cnut" title="Old English">
       charter
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Cnut" title="King Cnut">
       King Cnut
      </a>
      's would simply refer to "all his people - the twelve-hundreders and the two-hundreders". The law code even mentions the weregeld for a king, at 30,000, composed of 15,000 for the man, paid to the royal family, and 15,000 for the kingship, paid to the people. An archbishop is likewise valued at 15,000. The weregild for a Welshman was 110 if he owned at least one
      <a href="/wiki/Hide_(unit)" title="Hide (unit)">
       hide
      </a>
      of land, and 80 if he was landless.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Thrall" title="Thrall">
       Thralls
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaves" title="Slaves">
       slaves
      </a>
      legally commanded no weregild, but it was commonplace to make a nominal payment in the case of a thrall and the value of the slave in such a case. Technically this amount cannot be called a weregild, because it was more akin to a reimbursement to the owner for lost or damaged property.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">
       In literature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Weregild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In literature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A classic example of a dispute over the weregild of a slave is contained in Iceland's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Egil%27s_Saga" title="Egil's Saga">
        Egil's Saga
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lsungasaga" title="Vlsungasaga">
       Vlsungasaga
      </a>
      or Saga of the Volsungs, the sir (Odin, Loki and Hnir) are asked to pay weregild for killing Otr, son of Hreidmar. Otr was a 'great fisherman' and resembled an otter. He was 'eating a salmon and half dozing' on the river banks of Andvari's Falls when Loki kills him by throwing a stone at him. Later that evening, the sir visit Hreidmar's house where they are seized and imposed with a fine. Their fine consists of filling the [Otr] skin with gold and cover the outside with red gold. Loki is sent to get the gold and he manages to trick the dwarf Andvari into giving him the gold as well as a curse ring- The dwarf went into the rock and said that the gold ring would be the death of whoever owned it, and the same applied to all the gold.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grettis_saga" title="Grettis saga">
        Story of Grettir the Strong
       </a>
      </i>
      , chapter 27, "The Suit for the Slaying of Thorgils Makson", Thorgeir conveys to court Thorgils Arison's offer of weregild as atonement for killing Thorgils Makson.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">
       epic poem
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">
        Beowulf
       </a>
      </i>
      , lines 156-158
      <a href="/wiki/Grendel" title="Grendel">
       Grendel
      </a>
      refuses to settle his killings with payment or recompense, and at lines 456-472,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hro%C3%B0gar" title="Hrogar">
       Hrogar
      </a>
      recalls the story of how
      <a href="/wiki/Ecg%C3%BEeow" title="Ecgeow">
       Ecgeow
      </a>
      (Beowulf's father) once came to him for help, for he had slain Heaolaf, a man from another tribe called the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wulfings" title="Wulfings">
       Wulfings
      </a>
      , and either could not pay the wergild or they refused to accept it. Hrogar had married
      <a href="/wiki/Wealh%C3%BEeow" title="Wealheow">
       Wealheow
      </a>
      , who probably belonged to the Wulfing tribe, and was able to use his
      <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">
       kinship
      </a>
      ties to persuade the Wulfings to accept the wergild and end the feud. Hrogar sees Beowulf's offer as a son's gratitude for what Hrogar had done for Beowulf's father.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">
        The Lord of the Rings
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">

      </a>
      , the journal of
      <a href="/wiki/Isildur" title="Isildur">
       Isildur
      </a>
      reveals that he justified taking the
      <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">
       One Ring
      </a>
      as a weregild for the deaths of his father (
      <a href="/wiki/Elendil" title="Elendil">
       Elendil
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/An%C3%A1rion" title="Anrion">

       <a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Return of the King">

      </i>
      also mentions a rich weregild of
      <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
       gold
      </a>
      sent by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%BArin_II" title="Trin II">

      <a href="/wiki/Stewards_of_Gondor" title="Stewards of Gondor">
