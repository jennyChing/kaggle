     <p>
      <b>
       Australia's involvement in the
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
        Vietnam War
       </a>
      </b>
      , beginning with a small commitment of 30 military advisors in 1962, increased over the following decade to a peak of 7,672 Australian personnel following the Menzies Government's April 1965 decision to upgrade its military commitment to South Vietnam's security.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the time the last Australian personnel were withdrawn in 1972, the Vietnam War had become Australia's longest war, and was only recently surpassed by Australia's long term commitment of combat forces to the
      <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001present)">
       War in Afghanistan
      </a>
      . It currently remains Australia's largest force contribution to a foreign conflict since the Second World War and was also the most controversial in Australian society since the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_plebiscite,_1916" title="Australian plebiscite, 1916">
       conscription controversy
      </a>
      during the First World War. Although initially enjoying broad support due to concerns about the spread of
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       communism
      </a>
      in Southeast Asia, a vocal anti-war movement developed in response to Australia's programme of conscription.
     </p>
     <p>
      The withdrawal of Australia's forces from South Vietnam began in November 1970 when
      <a href="/wiki/8th_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="8th Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       8 RAR
      </a>
      completed its tour of duty and was not replaced. A phased withdrawal followed, and by 11 January 1973 Australian involvement in hostilities in Vietnam had ceased. Nevertheless, Australian troops from the Australian Embassy Platoon remained deployed in the country until 1 July 1973,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Australian forces were deployed briefly in April 1975, during the
      <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">
       Fall of Saigon
      </a>
      , to evacuate personnel from the Australian embassy. Approximately 60,000 Australians served in the war; 521 were killed and more than 3,000 were wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWMVW_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWMVW-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem_presidential_visit_to_Australia" title="Ngo Dinh Diem presidential visit to Australia">
       Ngo Dinh Diem presidential visit to Australia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem_presidential_visit_to_the_United_States" title="Ngo Dinh Diem presidential visit to the United States">
       Ngo Dinh Diem presidential visit to the United States
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Australia's involvement in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      was driven largely by the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       communism
      </a>
      in Southeast Asia after the Second World War, and the fear of its spread which developed in Australia during the 1950s and early 1960s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following the end of the Second World War the French had sought to reassert control over
      <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">
       French Indochina
      </a>
      . In 1950 as the communist-backed
      <a href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">
       Viet Minh
      </a>
      , led by
      <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">
       Ho Chi Minh
      </a>
      , began to gain the ascendency in the
      <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">
       First Indochina War
      </a>
      , the Vietnamese nation had two parallel administrations; the Democratic Republic of Vietnam (DRV) (recognised by the Soviet Union and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">
       People's Republic of China
      </a>
      ) and the
      <a href="/wiki/State_of_Vietnam" title="State of Vietnam">
       State of Vietnam
      </a>
      (SoV), an associated state in the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Union" title="French Union">
       French Union
      </a>
      (recognised by the non-communist world). In 1954, after the defeat of the French at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu" title="Battle of Dien Bien Phu">
       Battle of Dien Bien Phu
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">
       Geneva Accords
      </a>
      of 1954 split the country geographically, with the DRV to the north of the 17th parallel and the SoV in the south.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Geneva Accords imposed a deadline of July 1956 for the governments of the two Vietnams to hold elections, with a view to uniting the country under one government.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham59_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham59-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1955,
      <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Ngo Dinh Diem">
       Ngo Dinh Diem
      </a>
      , the prime minister of the State of Vietnam, deposed the head of state
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bao_Dai" title="Bao Dai">
       Bao Dai
      </a>
      in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1955_State_of_Vietnam_referendum" title="1955 State of Vietnam referendum">
       fraudulent referendum
      </a>
      and declared himself President of the newly proclaimed Republic of Vietnam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He then refused to take part in the elections, claiming that the communist north would engage in election fraud and that as a result they would win because they had more people. After this deadline passed, the military commanders in the North began preparing an invasion of the South.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham59_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham59-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over the course of the late 1950s and early 1960s this invasion took root in a campaign of insurgency, subversion and sabotage in the South employing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guerilla_warfare" title="Guerilla warfare">
       guerilla warfare
      </a>
      tactics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In September 1957, Diem visited Australia and was given strong support by both the ruling
      <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">
       Liberal Party of Australia
      </a>
      of Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Menzies" title="Robert Menzies">
       Robert Menzies
      </a>
      and the opposition
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">
       Australian Labor Party
      </a>
      . Diem was particularly feted by the Catholic community, as he pursued policies that discriminated in favour of the Catholic minority in his country and gave special powers to the Catholic Church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 1962 the situation in South Vietnam had become bad enough that Diem submitted a request for assistance to the United States and its allies in order to counter the growing insurgency and the threat that it posed to South Vietnam's security. Following this the US began to send advisors to provide tactical and logistical advice to the South Vietnamese. At the same time, the US sought to increase the legitimacy of the South Vietnamese government by instituting the
      <a href="/wiki/Many_Flags" title="Many Flags">
       Many Flags
      </a>
      program, hoping to counter the communist propaganda that South Vietnam was merely a US puppet state
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and to involve as many nations as possible. Thus Australia, as an ally of the United States with obligations under the
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia_Treaty_Organization" title="Southeast Asia Treaty Organization">
       Southeast Asia Treaty Organization
      </a>
      (SEATO) and
      <a href="/wiki/ANZUS" title="ANZUS">
       ANZUS
      </a>
      Pacts, and in the hope of shoring up its alliance with the US, became involved in the Vietnam War.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Between 1962 and 1972 it would send almost 60,000 personnel to Vietnam, including ground troops, naval forces and air assets, and would contribute large amounts of material to the war effort.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWMVW_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWMVW-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia.27s_military_involvement">
       Australia's military involvement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Australia's military involvement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_advisors.2C_1962.E2.80.9365">
       Australian advisors, 196265
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Australian advisors, 196265">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RAAF_TFV_(HD-SN-99-02052).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="598" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/RAAF_TFV_%28HD-SN-99-02052%29.jpg/220px-RAAF_TFV_%28HD-SN-99-02052%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/RAAF_TFV_%28HD-SN-99-02052%29.jpg/330px-RAAF_TFV_%28HD-SN-99-02052%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/RAAF_TFV_%28HD-SN-99-02052%29.jpg/440px-RAAF_TFV_%28HD-SN-99-02052%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RAAF_TFV_(HD-SN-99-02052).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Personnel and aircraft of RAAF Transport Flight Vietnam arrive in South Vietnam in August 1964
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      While assisting the British during the
      <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">
       Malayan Emergency
      </a>
      , Australian and New Zealand military forces had gained valuable experience in jungle warfare and counter-insurgency. According to historian
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ham" title="Paul Ham">
       Paul Ham
      </a>
      , the US Secretary of State, Dean Rusk, "freely admitted to the ANZUS meeting in Canberra in May 1962, that the US armed forces knew little about jungle warfare".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Given the experience that Australian forces had gained in Malaya it was felt that initially Australia could contribute to the situation by providing advisors who were experts in the tactics of jungle warfare. In this regard the Australian government's initial response was to send 30 military advisers, dispatched as the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Army_Training_Team_Vietnam" title="Australian Army Training Team Vietnam">
       Australian Army Training Team Vietnam
      </a>
      (AATTV), also known as "the Team". The Australian military assistance was to be in jungle warfare training, and the Team comprised highly qualified and experienced officers and
      <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">
       NCOs
      </a>
      , led by Colonel
      <a href="/wiki/Ted_Serong" title="Ted Serong">
       Ted Serong
      </a>
      , many with previous experience from the Malayan Emergency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their arrival in South Vietnam during July and August 1962 was the beginning of Australia's involvement in the war in Vietnam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Relationships between the AATTV and US advisors were generally very cordial, but there were sometimes significant differences of opinion on the training and tactics that should be employed. For example, when Serong expressed doubt about the value of the
      <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Hamlet_Program" title="Strategic Hamlet Program">
       Strategic Hamlet Program
      </a>
      at a US counter Insurgency Group meeting in Washington on 23 May 1963, he drew a "violent challenge" from US Marine General
      <a href="/wiki/Victor_H._Krulak" title="Victor H. Krulak">
       Victor "Brute" Krulak
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Captain Barry Petersen's work with raising an anti-communist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montagnard_(Vietnam)" title="Montagnard (Vietnam)">
       Montagnard
      </a>
      force in the central highlands between 1963 and 1965 highlighted another problemSouth Vietnamese officials sometimes found sustained success by a foreigner difficult to accept.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Warrant Officer Class Two Kevin Conway of the AATTV, was killed on 6 July 1964, side by side with Master Sergeant Gabriel Alamo of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Special_Operations_Forces" title="United States Special Operations Forces">
       USSF
      </a>
      , during a sustained Viet Cong attack on
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nam_Dong" title="Battle of Nam Dong">
       Nam Dong Special Forces Camp
      </a>
      , becoming Australia's first battle
      <a href="/wiki/Casualty_(person)" title="Casualty (person)">
       casualty
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vvaaCalendar_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vvaaCalendar-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_Australian_commitment.2C_1965.E2.80.9370">
       Increased Australian commitment, 196570
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Increased Australian commitment, 196570">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="798" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg/220px-AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg/330px-AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg/440px-AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AO-32_HMAS_Hobart_1967.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        HMAS
        <i>
         Hobart
        </i>
        refueling from a United States Navy tanker while operating off Vietnam in 1967
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In August 1964 the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">
       Royal Australian Air Force
      </a>
      (RAAF) sent a flight of
      <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada_DHC-4_Caribou" title="De Havilland Canada DHC-4 Caribou">
       Caribou transports
      </a>
      to the port town of
      <a href="/wiki/V%C5%A9ng_T%C3%A0u" title="Vng Tu">
       Vung Tau
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWMVW_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWMVW-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the end of 1964, there were almost 200 Australian military personnel in the
      <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">
       Republic of Vietnam
      </a>
      , including an engineer and surgical team as well as a larger AATTV team.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In order to boost the size of the Army by providing a greater pool for infantrymen, the Australian Government had introduced
      <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_Australia#Vietnam_War" title="Conscription in Australia">
       conscription for compulsory military service
      </a>
      for 20-year-olds, in November 1964, despite opposition from within the Army and many sections of the broader community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thereafter, battalions serving with 1 ATF all contained National Servicemen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With the war escalating the AATTV increased to approximately 100 men by December.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis59_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dennis59-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 29 April 1965, Australian Prime Minister Robert Menzies announced that the government had received a request for further military assistance from South Vietnam. "We have decided...in close consultation with the Government of the United Statesto provide an infantry battalion for service in Vietnam." He argued that a communist victory in South Vietnam would be a direct military threat to Australia. "It must be seen as part of a thrust by Communist China between the Indian and Pacific Oceans" he added.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The issue of whether a formal request was made by the South Vietnamese government at this time has been disputed, however. Although the South Vietnamese Prime Minister,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tran_Van_Huong" title="Tran Van Huong">
       Tran Van Huong
      </a>
      , made a request in December 1964,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham121_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham121-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Huong's replacement,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phan_Huy_Quat" title="Phan Huy Quat">
       Phan Huy Quat
      </a>
      , had to be "coerced into accepting an Australian battalion"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham121_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham121-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and stopped short of formally requesting the commitment in writing, simply sending an acceptance of the offer to Canberra the day before Menzies announced it to the Australian parliament.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this regard it has been argued that the decision was made by Australian politicians against advice of the Department of Defence,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to coincide with the commitment of US combat troops earlier in the year, and that the decision would have been made regardless of the wishes of the South Vietnamese government.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham121_25-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham121-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As a result of the announcement, the
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="1st Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       1st Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment
      </a>
      (1 RAR) was deployed. Advanced elements of the battalion departed Australia on 27 May 1965.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham128_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham128-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Accompanied by a
      <a href="/wiki/Troop" title="Troop">
       troop
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/4th/19th_Prince_of_Wales%27s_Light_Horse" title="4th/19th Prince of Wales's Light Horse">
       4th/19th Prince of Wales's Light Horse
      </a>
      as well as logistics personnel, they embarked upon
      <a href="/wiki/HMAS_Sydney_(R17)" title="HMAS Sydney (R17)">
       <i>
        Sydney
       </i>
      </a>
      and following their arrival in Vietnam in June,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham128_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham128-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      they were attached to the US
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/173rd_Airborne_Brigade_Combat_Team_(United_States)" title="173rd Airborne Brigade Combat Team (United States)">
       173rd Airborne Brigade
      </a>
      along with a New Zealand artillery battery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Throughout 1965 they undertook several operations in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bien_Hoa" title="Bien Hoa">
       Bien Hoa
      </a>
      province and subsequently fought a number of significant actions, including
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gang_Toi" title="Battle of Gang Toi">
       Gang Toi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crimp" title="Operation Crimp">
       Operation Crimp
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Suoi_Bong_Trang" title="Battle of Suoi Bong Trang">
       Suoi Bong Trang
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, 1 RAR's attachment to US forces had highlighted the differences between Australian and American operational methods
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Australian and US military leaders subsequently agreed to the future deployment of Australian combat forces in a discrete province. This would allow the Australian Army to "fight their own tactical war", independently of the US.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In April 1966
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Australian_Task_Force" title="1st Australian Task Force">
       1st Australian Task Force
      </a>
      (1 ATF) was established in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phuoc_Tuy_Province" title="Phuoc Tuy Province">
       Phuoc Tuy Province
      </a>
      , based at
      <a href="/wiki/Nui_Dat" title="Nui Dat">
       Nui Dat
      </a>
      . 1 ATF consisted of two (and after 1967 three) infantry
      <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">
       battalions
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Troop" title="Troop">
       troop
      </a>
      and later a
      <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(army)" title="Squadron (army)">
       squadron
      </a>
      of armoured personnel carriers from the
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Armoured_Personnel_Carrier_Squadron_(Australia)" title="1st Armoured Personnel Carrier Squadron (Australia)">
       1st Armoured Personnel Carrier Squadron
      </a>
      and a detachment of the
      <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service_Regiment" title="Special Air Service Regiment">
       Special Air Service Regiment
      </a>
      (SASR) as well as various support services under the command of the
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Australian_Logistic_Support_Group" title="1st Australian Logistic Support Group">
       1st Australian Logistic Support Group
      </a>
      (1 ALSG) based in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vung_Tau" title="Vung Tau">
       Vung Tau
      </a>
      . A squadron of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centurion_tanks" title="Centurion tanks">
       Centurion tanks
      </a>
      was added in December 1967. 1 ATF's responsibility was the security of Phuoc Tuy Province, excluding larger towns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>
      The RAAF contingent was also expanded, growing to include three squadrons
      <a href="/wiki/No._35_Squadron_RAAF" title="No. 35 Squadron RAAF">
       No. 35 Squadron
      </a>
      , flying Caribou
      <a href="/wiki/STOL" title="STOL">
       STOL
      </a>
      transports,
      <a href="/wiki/No._9_Squadron_RAAF" title="No. 9 Squadron RAAF">
       No. 9 Squadron
      </a>
      flying
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UH-1_Iroquois" title="UH-1 Iroquois">
       UH-1 Iroquois
      </a>
      battlefield helicopters and
      <a href="/wiki/No._2_Squadron_RAAF" title="No. 2 Squadron RAAF">
       No. 2 Squadron
      </a>
      flying
      <a href="/wiki/English_Electric_Canberra" title="English Electric Canberra">
       Canberra
      </a>
      bombers. Based at
      <a href="/wiki/Phan_Rang_Air_Base" title="Phan Rang Air Base">
       Phan Rang Air Base
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninh_Thuan_province" title="Ninh Thuan province">
       Ninh Thuan province
      </a>
      the Canberras flew many bombing sorties, and two were lost, while the Caribou transport aircraft supported anti-communist ground forces and the Iroquois helicopters were used in troop-lift, medical evacuation and as gunships from their base at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vung_Tau" title="Vung Tau">
       Vung Tau
      </a>
      in support of 1 ATF. At its peak it included over 750 personnel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the war RAAF
      <a href="/wiki/CAC_Sabre" title="CAC Sabre">
       CAC-27 Sabre
      </a>
      fighters from
      <a href="/wiki/No._79_Squadron_RAAF" title="No. 79 Squadron RAAF">
       No. 79 Squadron
      </a>
      were also deployed to
      <a href="/wiki/Ubon_Royal_Thai_Air_Force_Base" title="Ubon Royal Thai Air Force Base">
       Ubon Air Base
      </a>
      in Thailand as part of Australia's SEATO commitments. However, the Sabres took no part in direct hostilities against North Vietnam, and were withdrawn in 1968.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Navy" title="Royal Australian Navy">
       Royal Australian Navy
      </a>
      (RAN) also made a significant contribution, which consisted of a
      <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">
       destroyer
      </a>
      on six-month rotations deployed on the gun-line in a shore bombardment role, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAN_Helicopter_Flight_Vietnam" title="RAN Helicopter Flight Vietnam">
       RAN Helicopter Flight Vietnam
      </a>
      , and a RAN
      <a href="/wiki/Clearance_Diving_Team_(RAN)" title="Clearance Diving Team (RAN)">
       Clearance Diving Team
      </a>
      . The ageing aircraft carrier HMAS
      <i>
       Sydney
      </i>
      , after being converted to a troop-ship, was used to convey the bulk of Australian ground forces to South Vietnam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Female members of the Army and RAAF nursing services also served in Vietnam from the outset, and as the force grew the medical capability was also expanded with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_Australian_Field_Hospital" title="1st Australian Field Hospital">
       1st Australian Field Hospital
      </a>
      established at Vung Tau on 1 April 1968.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From an Australian perspective, one of the most famous engagements in the war was the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Long_Tan" title="Battle of Long Tan">
       Battle of Long Tan
      </a>
      which took place on 18 and 19 August 1966. During the battle a company from
      <a href="/wiki/6th_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="6th Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       6 RAR
      </a>
      , despite being heavily outnumbered, fought off a large enemy assault of regimental strength. 18 Australians were killed and 24 wounded, while at least 245 Viet Cong were killed. It was a decisive Australian victory and is often cited as an example of the importance of combining and coordinating infantry, artillery, armour and military aviation. The battle had considerable tactical implications as well, being significant in allowing the Australians to gain dominance over Phuoc Tuy Province, and although there were other large-scale encounters in later years, 1 ATF was not fundamentally challenged again.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis619_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dennis619-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regardless, during February 1967 1 ATF sustained its heaviest casualties in the war to that point, losing 16 men killed and 55 wounded in a single week, the bulk of them during
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bribie" title="Operation Bribie">
       Operation Bribie
      </a>
      . 1 ATF appeared to have lost the initiative and for the first time in nine months of operations the number of Australians killed in battle, or from friendly fire, mines or booby traps, had reversed the task force's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kill_ratio" title="Kill ratio">
       kill ratio
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Such losses underscored the need for a third battalion and the requirement for tanks to support the infantry; a realisation which challenged the conventional wisdom of Australian counter-revolutionary warfare doctrine which had previously allotted only a minor role to armour. Yet, it would be nearly a year before additional Australian forces would finally arrive in Vietnam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To Brigadier
      <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Clarence_Graham" title="Stuart Clarence Graham">
       Stuart Graham
      </a>
      , the 1 ATF commander, Operation Bribie confirmed the need to establish a physical barrier to deny the Viet Cong freedom of movement and thereby regain the initiative, and the subsequent decision to establish an 11-kilometre (6.8mi)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minefield" title="Minefield">
       barrier minefield
      </a>
      from Dat Do to the coast increasingly came to dominate task force planning. Yet ultimately this would prove both controversial and costly for the Australians, and despite initial success, the minefield would become a source of munitions for the Viet Cong to use against 1 ATF and later the decision would be made to remove it in 1969.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, with the war continuing to escalate following further American troop increases, 1 ATF was heavily reinforced in late-1967. A third infantry battalion arrived in December 1967, while a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squadron_(cavalry)" title="Squadron (cavalry)">
       squadron
      </a>
      of Centurion tanks and additional Iroquois helicopters would also be added in early 1968. In all a further 1,200 men were deployed, taking the total Australian troop strength to over 8,000 men, its highest level during the war. This increase effectively doubled the combat power available to the task force commander.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although primarily operating out of Phuoc Tuy, the 1 ATF was also available for deployment elsewhere in the
      <a href="/wiki/III_Corps_(South_Vietnam)" title="III Corps (South Vietnam)">
       III Corps Tactical Zone
      </a>
      . Indeed with the province progressively coming under control, 1968 saw the Australians spending a significant period of time conducting operations further afield. The communist
      <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">
       Tet offensive
      </a>
      began on 30 January 1968 with the aim of inciting a general uprising, simultaneously engulfing population centres across South Vietnam. In response, 1 ATF was deployed along likely infiltration routes in order to defend the vital
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bien_Hoa" title="Bien Hoa">
       Bien Hoa
      </a>
      -
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Binh" title="Long Binh">
       Long Binh
      </a>
      complex near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">
       Saigon
      </a>
      , as part of
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Coburg" title="Operation Coburg">
       Operation Coburg
      </a>
      between January and March. Heavy fighting resulted in 17 Australians killed and 61 wounded, while communist casualties included at least 145 killed, 110 wounded and 5 captured, with many more removed from the battlefield.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, Tet also affected Phuoc Tuy Province, and although stretched thin the remaining Australian forces there successfully repelled an attack on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba_Ria" title="Ba Ria">
       Ba Ria
      </a>
      , as well as spoiling a harassing attack on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Dien" title="Long Dien">
       Long Dien
      </a>
      and conducting a sweep of Hoa Long, killing 50 Viet Cong and wounding 25 for the loss of five Australians killed and 24 wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In late February the communist offensive collapsed, suffering more than 45,000 killedagainst South Vietnamese and allied losses of only 6,000 men.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regardless, Tet proved to be a turning point in the war, and although it had been a tactical disaster for the communists it proved a strategic victory for Hanoi as confidence in the American military and political leadership collapsed, as did public support for the war in the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Tet had a similar effect on Australian public opinion, and caused growing uncertainty in the government about the determination of the United States to remain militarily involved in Southeast Asia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Amid the initial shock, Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/John_Gorton" title="John Gorton">
       John Gorton
      </a>
      unexpectedly declared for the first time that Australia would not increase its military commitment in Vietnam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The war continued without respite however, and between May and June 1968 1 ATF was again deployed away from Phuoc Tuy in response to intelligence reports of another impending offensive. In May 1968
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="1st Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       1 RAR
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/3rd_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="3rd Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       3 RAR
      </a>
      with armour and artillery support fought off large-scale attacks during the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Coral%E2%80%93Balmoral" title="Battle of CoralBalmoral">
       Battle of CoralBalmoral
      </a>
      . Twenty-five Australians were killed and nearly 100 wounded, while the North Vietnamese lost in excess of 300 killed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis619_39-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dennis619-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="618" data-file-width="1295" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg/220px-Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg/330px-Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg/440px-Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canberra_B20_2_Sqn_RAAF_over_Vietnam_1970.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A No. 2 Squadron Canberra bomber operating over South Vietnam in 1970
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Later, from December 1968 to February 1969 two battalions from 1 ATF again deployed away from their base in Phuoc Tuy province, operating against suspected communist bases in the Hat Dich area, in western Phuoc Tuy, south-eastern Bien Hoa and south-western Long Khan provinces during
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hat_Dich" title="Battle of Hat Dich">
       Operation Goodwood
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fighting lasted 78 days and was one of the longest out of province operations mounted by the Australians during the war.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From May 1969 the main effort of the task force returned to Phuoc Tuy Province.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later in June 1969,
      <a href="/wiki/5th_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="5th Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       5 RAR
      </a>
      fought one of the last large-scale actions of the Australian war, during the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Binh_Ba" title="Battle of Binh Ba">
       Battle of Binh Ba
      </a>
      , 5 kilometres (3.1mi) north of Nui Dat in Phuoc Tuy Province. The battle was unusual in the Australian experience, involving infantry and armour in close-quarter house-to-house fighting through the village of Binh Ba against a combined force of Viet Cong and North Vietnamese Army. For the loss of one Australian killed at the communists lost 107 killed, six wounded and eight captured in a hard fought but one-sided engagement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the losses suffered at Binh Ba forced the NVA to move out of Phuoc Tuy into adjoining provinces and although the Australians did encounter main force units in the years to come, the Battle of Binh Ba marked the end of such clashes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Yet while the Viet Cong had largely been forced to withdraw to the borders of the province by 196869, the situation in Phuoc Tuy was challenged on several occasions in the following years, including during the 1968
      <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">
       Tet Offensive
      </a>
      , as well as in mid-1969 following the incursion of the North Vietnamese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/33rd_NVA_Regiment" title="33rd NVA Regiment">
       33rd Regiment
      </a>
      , again in mid-1971 with further incursions by the 33rd Regiment and several Viet Cong main force units, and finally during the
      <a href="/wiki/Easter_Offensive" title="Easter Offensive">
       Easter Offensive
      </a>
      in 1972. Attacks on RF outposts and incursions into the villages had also continued.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Such large-scale battles were not the norm in Phuoc Tuy Province. More typical of the Australian war was company-level patrolling and
      <a href="/wiki/Cordon_and_search" title="Cordon and search">
       cordon and search
      </a>
      operations which were designed to put pressure on enemy units and disrupt their access to the local population. To the end of Australian operations in Phuoc Tuy this remained the focus of Australian efforts and was this approach arguably allowed the restoration of government control in the province.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis620_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dennis620-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Australia's peak commitment at any one time was 7,672 combat troops and New Zealand's, 552, in 1969. New Zealand first committed a detachment of engineers and an artillery battery. New Zealand infantry units were also deployed in 1967 and were integrated into Australian battalions serving with 1 ATF after March 1968. These combined battalions being designated "ANZAC Battalions".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Special forces from the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Special_Air_Service" title="New Zealand Special Air Service">
       New Zealand Special Air Service
      </a>
      were also attached to each Australian SASR squadron from late 1968.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During this time the AATTV had continued to operate in support of the South Vietnamese forces, with an area of operations stretching from the far south to the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Vietnamese Demilitarized Zone">
       Demilitarized Zone
      </a>
      (DMZ) forming the border between
      <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">
       North Vietnam
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">
       South Vietnam
      </a>
      . Members of the team were involved in many combat operations, often commanding formations of Vietnamese soldiers. Some advisors worked with regular ARVN units and formations, while others worked with the Montagnard hill tribes in conjunction with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Special_Forces" title="US Special Forces">
       US Special Forces
      </a>
      . A few were involved in the controversial
      <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Program" title="Phoenix Program">
       Phoenix Program
      </a>
      run by the US
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">
       Central Intelligence Agency
      </a>
      (CIA), which was designed to target the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietcong" title="Vietcong">
       Vietcong
      </a>
      infrastructure through infiltration, arrest and assassination. The AATTV became Australia's most decorated unit of the war, including all four
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">
       Victoria Crosses
      </a>
      awarded during the conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis59_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dennis59-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_counter-insurgency_tactics_and_civic_action">
       Australian counter-insurgency tactics and civic action
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australian counter-insurgency tactics and civic action">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Historian Albert Palazzo comments that when the Australians entered the Vietnam War, it was with their own "well considered ...concept of war", and this was often contradictory or in conflict with US concepts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 1 ATF light infantry tactics such as patrolling, searching villages without destroying them (with a view to eventually converting them), and ambush and counter ambush drew criticism from some US commanders.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_William_Westmoreland" title="General William Westmoreland">
       General William Westmoreland
      </a>
      is reported to have complained to Major General
      <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Vincent_(general)" title="Douglas Vincent (general)">
       Tim Vincent
      </a>
      that 1 ATF was "not being aggressive enough".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By comparison, US forces sought to flush out the enemy and achieve rapid and decisive victory through "brazen scrub bashing" and the use of "massive firepower."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Australians acknowledged they had much to learn from the US forces about heliborne assault and joint armour and infantry assaults. Yet the US measure of successthe
      <a href="/wiki/Body_count" title="Body count">
       body count
      </a>
      was apparently held in contempt by many 1 ATF battalion commanders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1966 journalist Gerald Stone described tactics then being used by Australian soldiers newly arrived in Vietnam:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        The Australian battalion has been described ...as the safest combat force in Vietnam... It is widely felt that the Australians have shown themselves able to give chase to the guerillas without exposing themselves to the lethal ambushes that have claimed so many American dead...
        <p>
         Australian patrols shun jungle tracks and clearings... picking their way carefully and quietly through bamboo thickets and tangled foliage... .It is a frustrating experience to trek through the jungle with Australians. Patrols have taken as much as nine hours to sweep a mile of terrain. They move forward a few steps at a time, stop, listen, then proceed again.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
          <a href="#cite_note-65">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           65
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </p>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>

     <p>
      Looking back on ten years of reporting the war in Vietnam and Cambodia, journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Neil_Davis_(cameraman)" title="Neil Davis (cameraman)">
       Neil Davis
      </a>
      said in 1983; "I was very proud of the Australian troops. They were very professional, very well trained and they fought the people they were sent to fightthe Viet Cong. They tried not to involve civilians and generally there were fewer casualties inflicted by the Australians."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another perspective on Australian operations was provided by
      <a href="/wiki/David_Hackworth" title="David Hackworth">
       David Hackworth
      </a>
      , Vietnam's most decorated US soldier. "The Aussies used squads to make contact... and brought in reinforcements to do the killing; they planned in the belief that a platoon on the battlefield could do anything."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For some Viet Cong leaders there was no doubt the Australian jungle warfare approach was effective. One former Viet Cong leader is quoted as saying; "Worse than the Americans were the Australians. The Americans style was to hit us, then call for planes and artillery. Our response was to break contact and disappear if we could...The Australians were more patient than the Americans, better guerilla fighters, better at ambushes. They liked to stay with us instead of calling in the planes. We were more afraid of their style."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a junior partner, Australians had little opportunity to influence US strategy in the war. "The American concept [of how the war should be fought] remained unchallenged and it prevailed almost by default."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Overall, the tactics used by the Australian Army in Vietnam were not successful. Like the Americans, Australian tactics were focused on seeking to engage the Communist forces in battle and ultimately failed as the Communists were generally able to evade Australian forces when conditions were not favourable. Moreover, the Australians did not devote sufficient resources to disrupting the logistical infrastructure which supported the Communist forces in Phuoc Tuy Province and popular support for the Communists remained strong. After 1 ATF was withdrawn in 1971 the insurgency in Phuoc Tuy rapidly expanded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile, although the bulk of Australian military resources in Vietnam were devoted to operations against the Viet Cong and North Vietnamese forces, a civic action program was also undertaken to assist the local population and government authorities in Phuoc Tuy. This included various projects aimed at winning the support of the people and was seen as an essential element of Australian counter-revolutionary doctrine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Australian forces had first undertaken some civic action projects in 1965 while 1 RAR was operating in Bien Hoa, and similar work was started in Phuoc Tuy following the deployment of 1 ATF in 1966.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost166_72-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Frost166-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, in June 1967 the 40-man
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Australian_Civil_Affairs_Unit" title="1st Australian Civil Affairs Unit">
       1st Australian Civil Affairs Unit
      </a>
      (1 ACAU) was established to undertake the program.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palazzo116_73-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Palazzo116-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 1970 this unit had grown to 55 men, with detachments specialising in engineering, medical, education and agriculture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost166_72-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Frost166-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the first three years of the Australian presence civic action was mainly an adjunct to military operations, with the unit involved in the cordon and search of villages and resettlement programs, as well as occasionally in directly aiding and reconstructing villages that had been damaged in major actions. In the final years of the Australian presence it became more involved in assistance to villages and to the provincial administration. While 1 ACAU was the main agency involved in such tasks, at times other task force units were also involved in civic action programs. Activities included construction and public works, medical and dental treatment, education, agriculture development and youth and sports programs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        74
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although extensive, these programs were often undertaken without reference to the local population and it was not until 1969 that villagers were involved in determining what projects would be undertaken and in their construction. Equally, ongoing staff and material support was usually not provided, while maintenance and sustainment was the responsibility of the provincial government which often lacked the capacity or the will to provide it, limiting the benefit provided to the local population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palazzo116_73-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Palazzo116-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The program continued until 1 ATF's withdrawal in 1971, and although it may have succeeded in generating goodwill towards Australian forces, it largely failed to increase support for the South Vietnamese government in the province. Equally, while the program made some useful contributions to the civil facilities and infrastructure in Phuoc Tuy which remained following the Australian departure, it had little impact on the course of the conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Withdrawal_of_Australian_forces.2C_1970.E2.80.9373">
       Withdrawal of Australian forces, 197073
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Withdrawal of Australian forces, 197073">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Australian withdrawal effectively commenced in November 1970. As a consequence of the overall US strategy of
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnamization" title="Vietnamization">
       Vietnamization
      </a>
      and with the Australian government keen to reduce its own commitment to the war,
      <a href="/wiki/8th_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="8th Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       8 RAR
      </a>
      was not replaced at the end of its tour of duty. 1 ATF was again reduced to just two infantry battalions, albeit with significant armour, artillery and aviation support remaining.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horner231_76-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Horner231-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Australian
      <a href="/wiki/Area_of_operations" title="Area of operations">
       area of operations
      </a>
      remained the same however, with the reduction in forces only adding further to the burden on the remaining battalions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horner231_76-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Horner231-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regardless, following a sustained effort by 1 ATF in Phuoc Tuy Province between September 1969 and April 1970, the bulk of communist forces had become inactive and had left the province to recuperate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 1971 the province had been largely cleared of local VC forces, who were now increasingly reliant on reinforcements from North Vietnam. As a measure of some success, Highway 15, the main route running through Phuoc Tuy between Saigon and Vung Tau, was open to unescorted traffic. Regardless, the Viet Cong maintained the ability to conduct local operations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis620_59-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dennis620-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, the AATTV had been further expanded and a jungle training centre was established in Phuoc Tuy province.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In November 1970, the unit's strength peaked at 227 advisors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        79
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
       <a href="#cite_note-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        80
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="600" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg/220px-Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg/330px-Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg/440px-Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Australian_civic_action_team_in_Vietnam_2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Members of an Australian civic action team confer with Vietnamese village officials on plans for local improvements
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Australian combat forces were further reduced during 1971.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Long_Khanh" title="Battle of Long Khanh">
       Battle of Long Khanh
      </a>
      on 67 June 1971 took place during one of the last major joint US-Australian operations, and resulted in three Australians killed and six wounded during heavy fighting in which an RAAF UH-1H Iroqouis was shot down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">
       <a href="#cite_note-81">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        81
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 18 August 1971, Australia and New Zealand decided to withdraw their troops from Vietnam, with the Australian prime minister,
      <a href="/wiki/William_McMahon" title="William McMahon">
       William McMahon
      </a>
      , announcing that 1 ATF would cease operations in October, commencing a phased withdrawal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horner_2008.2C_p._233_82-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Horner_2008.2C_p._233-82">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        82
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83">
       <a href="#cite_note-83">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        83
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nui_Le" title="Battle of Nui Le">
       Battle of Nui Le
      </a>
      on 21 September proved to be the last major battle fought by Australian forces in the war, and resulted in five Australians killed and 30 wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odgers246_84-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odgers246-84">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        84
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finally, on 16 October Australian forces handed over control of the base at Nui Dat to South Vietnamese forces, while the main body from
      <a href="/wiki/4th_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="4th Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">
       4 RAR
      </a>
      the last Australian infantry battalion in South Vietnamsailed for Australia on board HMAS
      <i>
       Sydney
      </i>
      on 9 December 1971.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odgers247_85-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odgers247-85">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        85
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile D Company, 4 RAR with an assault pioneer and mortar section and a detachment of APCs remained in Vung Tau protect the task force headquarters and 1 ALSG until the final withdrawal of stores and equipment could be completed, finally returning to Australia on 12 March 1972.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">
       <a href="#cite_note-86">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        86
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Australian advisors continued to train Vietnamese troops however, until the announcement by the newly elected Australian Labor government of
      <a href="/wiki/Gough_Whitlam" title="Gough Whitlam">
       Gough Whitlam
      </a>
      that the remaining advisors would be withdrawn by 18 December 1972. It was only on 11 January 1973 that the
      <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Australia" title="Governor-General of Australia">
       Governor-General of Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hasluck" title="Paul Hasluck">
       Paul Hasluck
      </a>
      , announced the cessation of combat operations against the communists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Whitlam recognised
      <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">
       North Vietnam
      </a>
      , which welcomed his electoral success.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87">
       <a href="#cite_note-87">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        87
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Australian troops remained in Saigon guarding the Australian embassy until 1 July 1973.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The withdrawal from Vietnam meant that 1973 was the first time since the beginning of World War II in 1939 that Australia's armed forces were not involved in a conflict somewhere in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-GOV-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In total approximately 60,000 Australiansground troops, air-force and naval personnelserved in Vietnam between 1962 and 1972. 521 died as a result of the war and over 3,000 were wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWMVW_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWMVW-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      15,381 conscripted national servicemen served from 1965 to 1972, sustaining 202 killed and 1,279 wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWMNSS_88-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWMNSS-88">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        88
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition there were six Australians listed as
      <a href="/wiki/Australians_Missing_in_Action_in_the_Vietnam_War" title="Australians Missing in Action in the Vietnam War">
       missing in action
      </a>
      , although these men are included in the list of Australians killed in action and the last of their remains were finally located and returned to Australia in 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89">
       <a href="#cite_note-89">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        89
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90">
       <a href="#cite_note-90">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        90
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Between 1962 and March 1972 the estimated cost of Australia's involvement to the war in Vietnam was $218.4 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91">
       <a href="#cite_note-91">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        91
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 1975 the Australian Government dispatched RAAF transport aircraft to South Vietnam to provide humanitarian assistance to refugees fleeing the North Vietnamese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_Campaign" title="Ho Chi Minh Campaign">
       Ho Chi Minh Campaign
      </a>
      . The first Australian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" title="C-130 Hercules">
       C-130 Hercules
      </a>
      arrived at
      <a href="/wiki/Tan_Son_Nhat_International_Airport" title="Tan Son Nhat International Airport">
       Tan Son Nhat Airport
      </a>
      on 30 March and the force, which was designated 'Detachment S', reached a strength of eight Hercules by the second week of April. The aircraft of detachment S transported refugees from cities near the front line and evacuated Australians and several hundred Vietnamese orphans from Saigon to
      <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
       Malaysia
      </a>
      . In addition, they regularly flew supplies to a large refugee camp at An Thoi on the island of
      <a href="/wiki/Ph%C3%BA_Qu%E1%BB%91c" title="Ph Quc">
       Phu Quoc
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92">
       <a href="#cite_note-92">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        92
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The deteriorating security situation forced the Australian aircraft to be withdrawn to Bangkok in mid-April, from where they flew into South Vietnam each day. The last three RAAF flights into Saigon took place on 25 April, when the Australian embassy was evacuated. While all Australians were evacuated, 130 Vietnamese who had worked at the embassy and had been promised evacuation were left behind.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93">
       <a href="#cite_note-93">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        93
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Whitlam later refused to accept South Vietnamese refugees following the
      <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">
       fall of Saigon
      </a>
      to the communists in April 1975, including Australian embassy staff who were later sent to
      <a href="/wiki/Reeducation_camp" title="Reeducation camp">
       reeducation camps
      </a>
      by the communists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94">
       <a href="#cite_note-94">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        94
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Liberalsled by
      <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Fraser" title="Malcolm Fraser">
       Malcolm Fraser
      </a>
      condemned Whitlam,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95">
       <a href="#cite_note-95">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        95
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and after defeating Labor in the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_federal_election,_1975" title="Australian federal election, 1975">
       1975 federal election
      </a>
      , allowed South Vietnamese refugees to
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_Australian" title="Vietnamese Australian">
       settle in Australia in large numbers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96">
       <a href="#cite_note-96">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        96
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Protests_against_the_war">
       Protests against the war
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Protests against the war">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In Australia, resistance to the war was at first very limited. Initially public opinion was strongly in support of government policy in Vietnam and when the leader of the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">
       Australian Labor Party
      </a>
      (ALP) (in opposition for most of the period),
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Calwell" title="Arthur Calwell">
       Arthur Calwell
      </a>
      announced that the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_federal_election,_1966" title="Australian federal election, 1966">
       1966 federal election
      </a>
      would be fought specifically on the issue of Vietnam the party suffered their biggest political defeat in decades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97">
       <a href="#cite_note-97">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        97
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, anti-war sentiment escalated rapidly from 1967,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98">
       <a href="#cite_note-98">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        98
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although it never gained support from the majority of the Australian community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99">
       <a href="#cite_note-99">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        99
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The centre-left ALP became more sympathetic to the communists and Calwell stridently denounced South Vietnamese Prime Minister
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nguyen_Cao_Ky" title="Nguyen Cao Ky">
       Nguyen Cao Ky
      </a>
      as a "fascist dictator" and a "butcher" ahead of his 1967 visit
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100">
       <a href="#cite_note-100">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        100
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      at the time Ky was the chief of the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_Air_Force" title="Vietnam Air Force">
       Vietnam Air Force
      </a>
      and headed a military junta. Despite the controversy leading up to the visit, Ky's trip was a success. He dealt with the media effectively, despite hostile sentiment from some sections of the press and public.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101">
       <a href="#cite_note-101">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        101
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The introduction of
      <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_Australia#Vietnam_War" title="Conscription in Australia">
       conscription
      </a>
      by the Australian government in response to a worsening regional strategic outlook during the war was consistently opposed by the Australian Labor Party and by many sections of society, and some groups resisted the call to military service by burning the letters notifying them of their conscription (which was punishable by imprisonment). Growing public uneasiness about the death toll was fuelled by a series of highly publicised arrests of
      <a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">
       conscientious objectors
      </a>
      , and exacerbated by revelations of atrocities committed against Vietnamese civilians, leading to a rapid increase in domestic opposition to the war between 1967 and 1970.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102">
       <a href="#cite_note-102">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        102
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_federal_election,_1969" title="Australian federal election, 1969">
       1969 federal election
      </a>
      , which Labor lost again but with a much reduced margin, public debate about Vietnam was increasingly dominated by those opposed to government policy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103">
       <a href="#cite_note-103">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        103
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 8 May 1970,
      <a href="/wiki/Moratorium_to_End_the_War_in_Vietnam" title="Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam">
       moratorium
      </a>
      marches were held in major Australian cities to coincide with the marches in the US. The demonstration in Melbourne, led by future deputy prime minister
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Cairns" title="Jim Cairns">
       Jim Cairns
      </a>
      , was supported by an estimated 100,000 people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104">
       <a href="#cite_note-104">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        104
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Across Australia, it was estimated that 200,000 people were involved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWMVW_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWMVW-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nevertheless, opinion polls taken at the time demonstrated that the moratorium failed to achieve its goals and had only a very limited impact upon public opinion, with over half respondents saying that they still supported national service and slightly less stating that they did not want Australia to pull out of the war.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105">
       <a href="#cite_note-105">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        105
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, the numbers that resisted the draft remained low. Indeed, by 1970 it was estimated that 99.8 per cent of those issued with call up papers complied with them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106">
       <a href="#cite_note-106">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        106
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Further moratoria were undertaken on 18 September 1970 and again on 30 June 1971. Arguably, however, the peace movement had lost its original spirit, as the political debate degenerated, according to author Paul Ham, towards "menace and violence".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107">
       <a href="#cite_note-107">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        107
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dominated by elements Ham identifies as "left-wing extremists", the organisers of the events extended invitations to members of the North Vietnamese government to attend, although this was prevented due to a refusal by the Australian government to grant them visas. Attendance at the subsequent marches was lower than that of May 1970, and as a result of several factors including confusion over the rules regarding what the protesters were allowed to do, aggressive police tactics, and agitation from protesters, the second march became violent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham529_108-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham529-108">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        108
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Sydney, 173 people were arrested, while in Melbourne the police attempted to control the crowd with a baton-charge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham529_108-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham529-108">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        108
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_attitudes_and_treatment_of_veterans">
       Social attitudes and treatment of veterans
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Social attitudes and treatment of veterans">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AS_Vietnam_1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/AS_Vietnam_1.jpg/200px-AS_Vietnam_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/AS_Vietnam_1.jpg/300px-AS_Vietnam_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/AS_Vietnam_1.jpg/400px-AS_Vietnam_1.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AS_Vietnam_1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnam_Forces_National_Memorial,_Canberra" title="Vietnam Forces National Memorial, Canberra">
         Vietnam Forces National Memorial, Canberra
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Initially there was considerable support for Australia's involvement in Vietnam, and all Australian battalions returning from Vietnam participated in well attended welcome home parades through either Sydney, Adelaide, Brisbane or Townsville, even during the early 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109">
       <a href="#cite_note-109">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        109
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regardless, as opposition to the war increased service in Vietnam came to be seen by sections of the Australian community in less than sympathetic terms and opposition to it generated negative views of veterans in some quarters. In the years following the war, some
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_veteran" title="Vietnam veteran">
       Vietnam veterans
      </a>
      experienced social exclusion and problems readjusting to society. Nevertheless, as the tour of duty of each soldier during the Vietnam War was limited to one year (although some soldiers chose to sign up for a second or even a third tour of duty), the number of soldiers suffering from
      <a href="/wiki/Combat_stress_reaction" title="Combat stress reaction">
       combat stress
      </a>
      was probably more limited than it might otherwise have been.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110">
       <a href="#cite_note-110">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        110
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the negative sentiments towards returned soldiers from some sections of the anti-war movement, some Second World War veterans also held negative views and attitudes toward the Vietnam War veterans. As a result many Australian Vietnam veterans were excluded from joining the
      <a href="/wiki/Returned_and_Services_League_of_Australia" title="Returned and Services League of Australia">
       Returned Servicemen's League
      </a>
      (RSL) during the 1960s and 1970s on the grounds that the Vietnam War veterans did not fight a "real war".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham565_111-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham565-111">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        111
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The response of the RSL varied across the country, and while some rejected Vietnam veterans, other branches, particularly those in rural areas, were said to be very supportive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham565_111-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham565-111">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        111
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nevertheless, many Vietnam veterans were excluded from marching in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANZAC_Day" title="ANZAC Day">
       ANZAC Day
      </a>
      parades during the 1970s because some soldiers of earlier wars saw the Vietnam veterans as unworthy heirs to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANZAC_spirit" title="ANZAC spirit">
       ANZAC
      </a>
      title and tradition, a view which hurt many Vietnam veterans and resulted in continued resentment towards the RSL.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ham565_111-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ham565-111">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        111
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regardless, in 1972 the RSL decided that Vietnam veterans should lead the march, which attracted large crowds throughout the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112">
       <a href="#cite_note-112">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        112
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Australian Vietnam veterans were honoured at a "Welcome Home" parade in Sydney on 3 October 1987, and it was then that a campaign for the construction of the Vietnam War Memorial began.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113">
       <a href="#cite_note-113">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        113
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This memorial, known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnam_Forces_National_Memorial,_Canberra" title="Vietnam Forces National Memorial, Canberra">
       Vietnam Forces National Memorial
      </a>
      , was established on
      <a href="/wiki/ANZAC_Parade,_Canberra" title="ANZAC Parade, Canberra">
       ANZAC Parade
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">
       Canberra
      </a>
      , and was dedicated on 3 October 1992.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1992dedication_114-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-1992dedication-114">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        114
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_Australian_foreign_and_defence_policy">
       Effect on Australian foreign and defence policy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_history_of_Australia_during_the_Vietnam_War&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect on Australian foreign and defence policy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the aftermath of the Vietnam War the withdrawal of the US from South-East Asia forced Australia to adopt a more independent foreign policy, moving away from forward defence and reliance on powerful allies to a greater emphasis on the
      <a href="/wiki/Defence_of_Australia_policy" title="Defence of Australia policy">
       defence of continental Australia
      </a>
      and military self-reliance, albeit in the context of a continued alliance with the United States. This later had important implications for the military's force structure in the 1980s and 1990s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115">
       <a href="#cite_note-115">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        115
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The experience in Vietnam also caused an intolerance for casualties which resulted in successive Australian governments becoming more cautious towards the deployment of military forces overseas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116">
       <a href="#cite_note-116">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        116
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regardless, the "imperative to deploy forces overseas" remained a feature of Australian strategic behavior in the post-Vietnam era,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117">
       <a href="#cite_note-117">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        117
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while the US alliance has continued to be a fundamental aspect of its foreign policy into the early 21st century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118">
       <a href="#cite_note-118">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        118
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

          <a href="/wiki/6th_Battalion,_Royal_Australian_Regiment" title="6th Battalion, Royal Australian Regiment">

          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidential_Unit_Citation_(Vietnam)" title="Presidential Unit Citation (Vietnam)">
           US Presidential Unit Citation (Vietnam)
          </a>
          .
         </li>
         <li>
          OctoberUS President Johnson visits Australia. Large crowds welcome him in Sydney and Melbourne, although some demonstrations take place; images of protesters throwing eggs at Johnson's car are later sent worldwide.

       <td>
        <ul>
         <li>
          7 AprilMajor
          <a href="/wiki/Peter_Badcoe" title="Peter Badcoe">
           Peter Badcoe
          </a>
          dies leading his company against more powerful opposition. He is posthumously awarded the Victoria Cross for bravery, conspicuous gallantry and leadership on more than one occasion.
         </li>
         <li>
          6 AugustA Company, 7 RAR was involved in heavy fighting in the eastern
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hat_Dich" title="Hat Dich">
           Hat Dich
          </a>
          area during the
          <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Suoi_Chau_Pha" title="Battle of Suoi Chau Pha">
           Battle of Suoi Chau Pha
          </a>
          . Australian casualties were heavy with five killed, one died of wounds and 19 wounded. A sweep of the area resulted in the recovery of only five dead Viet Cong, however drag marks and extensive blood trails indicated that they had suffered heavily, with perhaps another 33 killed or wounded in the contact, while a further 200 casualties were estimated from artillery and mortar fire, as well as airstrikes.

        <ul>
         <li>
          30 January
          <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">
           Tet Offensive
          </a>
          is launched by the
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Front_for_the_Liberation_of_Vietnam" title="National Front for the Liberation of Vietnam">

         <li>
          12 FebruaryPrime Minister,
          <a href="/wiki/John_Gorton" title="John Gorton">
           John Gorton
          </a>
          , announces that Australia will not increase its commitment to Vietnam.
         </li>
         <li>
          MayThe National Service Act is amended to impose a two-year civil gaol term for draft resisters.
         </li>
         <li>
          13 May
          <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Coral%E2%80%93Balmoral" title="Battle of CoralBalmoral">
           Battle of CoralBalmoral
          </a>
          takes place and becomes the bloodiest engagement for Australians in Vietnam when 25 Australians are killed and nearly 100 wounded during 26 days of fighting in AO Surfers, north-east of Saigon. The operation lasts till 6 June 1968.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130">
           <a href="#cite_note-130">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            130
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>

         <li>
          14 October
          <a href="/wiki/John_Zarb" title="John Zarb">
           John Zarb
          </a>
          is the first person to be found guilty of having failed to comply with his call up notice during the Vietnam War. He is convicted in Melbourne and sentenced to two years gaol. He loses his appeal to the full High Court on 25 November 1968. He is released on compassionate grounds in August 1969 after serving 10 months and seven days in
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentridge_Prison" title="Pentridge Prison">
           Pentridge Prison
          </a>
          .
         </li>

          <a href="/wiki/Ben_Het_Camp" title="Ben Het Camp">

          <a href="/wiki/Keith_Payne" title="Keith Payne">
           Keith Payne
          </a>
          shows outstanding courage and leadership in saving the lives of many of the soldiers under his command, leading his men to safety under most difficult circumstances after an attack by the enemy in superior strength. He is awarded the Victoria Cross.
         </li>

          <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Binh_Ba" title="Battle of Binh Ba">

          <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Long_Khanh" title="Battle of Long Khanh">
