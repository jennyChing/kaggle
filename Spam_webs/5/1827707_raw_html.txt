           Kitchen : The Big Reveal
          </a>
         </h2>
         <div class="title_byline">
          <span class="post_author_intro">
           by
          </span>
          <span class="post_author" itemprop="author">

         <p>
          <img height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_1.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          Okay guys! I am so happy to share with you the finished kitchen!
         </p>
         <p>
          I hope you dont hate me for taking so long with this post, and maybe you happened to see it yesterday on
          <a href="http://www.designsponge.com/2014/01/before-after-a-minneapolis-kitchen-gets-a-fresh-bright-new-look.html" target="_blank" title="Before &amp; After - Design Sponge">
           Design*Sponge
          </a>
          . It was all a matter of timing, the
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/12/kitchen-16-week-progress-report.html" title="Light Fixture">
           light fixture
          </a>
          took forever to arrive, and then those crazy holidays happened. So here we are, months after the project started, at the finish line.
         </p>
         <p>
          I cannot describe to you how great it feels to have invested the time and work to make this kitchen feel like it was a part of our home. This is especially true due to the sheer amount of time I spend in the kitchen as a stay-at-home mom. This space feels like a reflection of me and my family, and functions so much more efficiently for our day-to-day uses. Im not sure if I have even been more proud of one project than I am of this.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its been awhile, I know, but you guys may remember me starting this project back at the end of the summer when I started to
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/07/saving-up-projects.html" title="Home To-Do's">
           get the itch for an indoor interior project
          </a>
          , I was so over gardening. Renovating the kitchen had been on my list for a while, but over the last month of summer I was able to really solidify
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/08/kitchen-plans.html" title="Kitchen Planning">
           a plan
          </a>
          for how I was going to tackle it. As with most of the projects around this place, I tried to throw it all together on a tight (not tyte) budget, and ended up doing pretty decent job of staying close to what I had anticipated. I had planned for $1,500, but then we had a few additions as the project progressed  the kitchen cabinets, microwave and light fixture  that knocked it up to a grand total of $2,358. Im not mad at that total.
         </p>
         <p>
          All right, enough of me jabbering, check out some of these Before-and-Afters!
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Before" height="400" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_before_1.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          Without a doubt, the part of the kitchen I am most proud of is the transformation (below) of the weird corner space. This whole area was an almost completely-non-functional, junk-holding eyesore (above). Someone before our time had attempted to make it a bit more functional by adding one floating shelf and an outlet. I give them credit for the outlet, it has allowed us use the space as a place to make coffee in the mornings, but it just wasnt pretty. From left to right (above), we have a huge ginormous fridge that just floated out in the middle of the space, then the brick chimney which had nice texture, but didnt connect with any of the rest of the space, and then the cove which at some point had been the utility staircase. The stairs have been removed long ago and the 
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2011/04/cleaning-up-coffee-zone.html" title="Coffee Zone">
           coffee nook
          </a>
           was all that was left. The entire wall of space had troubled me ever since we moved in, and I had no idea what was the best way to attack it. I went through a few different
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/09/unifying-spaces-in-the-kitchen.html" title="Unifying Kitchen Space">
           sketches
          </a>
          for the space before coming up with a
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/kitchen_reveal/sketch.jpg" target="_blank" title="Kitchen Nook Sketch">
           solution
          </a>
          of
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/11/kitchen-10-week-progress-report.html" title="Kitchen : Week 10">
           custom fitting some Ikea cabinets
          </a>
          in the cove and chimney space and finishing it off with some inexpensive butcher-block counters and shelves.
         </p>
         <p>
          Not only does this look awesome now, but it also anchors, and minimizes the size of the fridge, which looks actually built into the space without overwhelming the nearby kitchen table. I love it. The cabinets house all our baking stuff, coffee accessories, and a very critical rack of paper towels inside the cupboard door  perfect for getting messy 2-year olds clean after their afternoon meals. Guess what else? Inside the top cabinet drawer is all of our silverware which used to make its home in the dining room buffet because there were not enough drawers in the kitchen previously  life changing, you guys. It has been so great to have a space dedicated to all these kitchen activities. Making pancakes is super easy, and Christmas cookies were not a chaotic mess. I am in love with the beauty and usefulness of this space.
         </p>
         <p>
          Oh and also guys, part of the reason it looks so pretty is because my good friend Nicole Ripplinger (aka Colz) helped me style it all. Shes got a highly cool website called
          <a href="http://middlenorth.net" target="_blank" title="Middle North Creative">
           Middle North Creative
          </a>
          if youre interested in her styling skillz.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal Nook - Detail" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_2.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Butcher Block Counter" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_3.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Cabinets, Shelves and Table" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_4.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Butcher Block Shelves Detail " height="400" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_5.1.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal" height="465" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_6.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Corner Detail" height="898" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_7.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - From the Kitchen to the Dining Room" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_8.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Subway Tile Backsplash" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_9.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Before" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_before_2.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          When we bought the house we inherited this metal copper backsplash (above). It wasnt the worst thing in the world, but it was totally not my taste. I decided to live with it and
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2010/04/house-update-my-kitschy-kitchen.html" title="Aqua and Copper Kitchen">
           tried to embrace it
          </a>
          . In the end, I just couldnt. It needed to go, and one warm September Sunday I just
          <a href="http://instagram.com/p/eC14LJy7a3/" target="_blank" title="Bye Bye Backsplash">
           started ripping it out
          </a>
          . There was no way it was staying any longer. One of the best things Ive done to this house, for sure, dawgs.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - White Subway Tile Backsplash, White Cabinets" height="897" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_10.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          Can we talk about the backsplash yet? Can I tell you how much I love the new subway tile? Can I tell you how it makes such an amazing and dramatic difference in the space? Can I tell you how much I love it? I can? Thank you! Using subway tile as a back splash is a classic move, and yet so inexpensive. I love how clean and tidy everything is. Nothing seems messy and splattered anymore and I am so thankful for that. It also brings together the newer, more modern cabinets in the main part of the kitchen with the original pantry cabinets  tile is their common language.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Black and White Kitchen, Subway Tile Backsplash" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_11.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          I wanted to take a minute and let you guys know about a few of things I would do differently if I could do it all over again. Yes, most of them are barely noticeable and very small, but still, I am a GD perfectionist.
         </p>
         <p>
          First of all, I wouldve waited to paint the cabinets until after the temperature started to drop and the air became drier. Like all things wood, they expand and contract. The cabinets contracted and minimally shrunk after I painted them in the warm fall weather, and now there are cracks in the paint at many of the seams. I can go back and fix this, yes, its just finding the motivation to do it now that the project is done.
         </p>
         <p>
          Secondly, I wouldve trusted my gut on the color of the grout. I originally wanted black grout because it looks awesome. But I wimped out, thinking that it might not be the best option for a first time tiler like myself, as I thought a darker color might highlight imperfections. I had to play a little game of What would piss you off more? Having black grout and seeing all the screw ups, or having a shade lighter and not seeing as many of the screw ups. I determined that seeing the screw ups would piss me off more. I decided to go a shade lighter with the grout, and I was totally cool with this choice. But then when confronted face-to-face with grout options at the hardware store, I went even one more shade lighter. Oops  Stick with your gut, Scoops (cool new rap). This isnt a major crisis or anything, but I do regret it. I wish the grout was just a shade darker. I shouldnt have second guessed myself.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thirdly, when measuring the butcher block counters I allowed for an overhang of 1/2, but I measured just the cabinet bases without the drawers or door front being considered, which were 1/2 deep. I now have a counter that is totally flush with the front of my cabinets. This could be much worse  I couldve forgotten to add any depth to the counters at all. So I am thankful for that. However, if I had to do it all over again, I would add an additional 1/2 overhang (1 deeper than the base cabinets).
         </p>
         <p>
          Other than that, I am over the moon with how the kitchen turned out. Please, if you have any questions, or need tips on a project, leave a comment below and I will try my best to give you advice based on what I have learned.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Black and White Kitchen and Mudroom" height="400" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_12.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Before" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_before_5.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Black and White Kitchen, Subway Tile Backsplash" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_13.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          When I started this renovation the biggest motivation was to get these original cabinets and hardware whipped into shape. Our house was originally built in 1910 (thats more than 100 years ago, dawgs) and Ive done my best to honor and highlight all of the unique eccentricities of the house and draw out that original character while still ensuring that it speaks to our style.
         </p>
         <p>
          Our kitchen is oddly set up (
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/08/kitchen-plans.html" title="Kitchen Layout">
           seen in the post
          </a>
          ). Im fairly certain all the walls and the layout are original to the house, but I dont understand why it was set up this way. This house was built with this pantry back in 1910. Was it used as a pantry then? I dunno, but its still here and tucked back in this little area of the kitchen. I have no idea how the original kitchen was laid out, but it has to be similar to how it functions now  just with an older stove, an ice box and definitely no dishwasher. I wanted to make sure I honored that history in some way, so I made
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/09/kitchen-2-week-progress-report.html" title="Restoring the Cabinets">
           restoring the cabinets
          </a>
          a major focus.
         </p>
         <p>
          I started by stripping all the hardware, which had at least of 5 layers of paint on them. Then I spent weeks
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/09/tips-on-how-to-paint-vintage-paneled-cabinets.html" title="Video: How to Paint Cabinets">
           sanding and painting and sanding and painting and sanding the cabinets
          </a>
          , making them look as perfect and pristine as I could manage without losing my mind. After 100 years, the cabinets had adjusted to the natural settling of the house; in other words, not one of them closed properly. With a lot of sanding, I was able to get each cabinet door to hang properly in its cabinet box and I now get to enjoy the click of closed cabinet latch.
         </p>
         <p>
          It doesnt look like much, but I really put a lot of heart and soul into this old pantry, and Im really proud and happy with how it turned out. It functions as a traditional pantry  keeping all our canned and boxed goods  and as a place to also store pots and pans, some bakeware, and I have a few cabinets that still have room for more stuff. Im good on storage now! I also built the big butcher block cutting board, added under-cabinet lighting and now use this space for all my meal prep. Its great for chopping and I love that this space now has more purpose then just storing all my kitchen junk!
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Before" height="899" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_before_3.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal" height="837" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_14.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          So I told you guys in my renovation updates that
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/11/tiny-table-transformations.html" title="Table Troubles">
           I was having trouble with this table
          </a>
          . It was really pulling at my heart strings as well as my creative problem-solving strings. The table has been with me since I was 18. Its lived with me and Jeff at all our old rentals, our loft, and now here at our house. Our kids eat all their meals here, and both Jeff and I are way sentimental about it. I also am a big believer in having your home being a museum of objects you love. So the table was staying. And although we loved the crap out of it, it was hard to make it look it good in our new vintage industrial kitchen with turquoise accents. This table has a bright yellow formica top and curvy chrome legs. Although its vintage, its not the vintage I had in mind.
         </p>
         <p>
          After trying different ways to disguise its existence  none of which were really that successful  it came down to an extra piece of curtain paneling made into a table cloth. Although Jeff isnt entirely convinced that this tablecloth is cool, I kind of love it. I think the table looks great, I love the kids stools painted to match the grey wall in the pantry and mudroom, and I think the Eames chairs with their dowel bases look great with all the cutting boards and butcher block. I like that it just sinks into this space, right between the new cabinets and the radiator (that I painted black), and I love how it gets bathed in winter light pouring through our kitchen windows. Its good, yah?
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Before" height="899" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_before_4.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Black, White &amp; Turquoise" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_15.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          This photo is here to show you how totally amazing
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/10/pulling-the-trigger-on-a-light-fixture.html" title="Aqua Light Fixture">
           this aqua light fixture
          </a>
          from
          <a href="http://www.schoolhouseelectric.com/" target="_blank" title="School House Electric">
           Schoolhouse Electric
          </a>
          is. I had to close my eyes when I clicked the order button on the net. Nearly $400 bucks on an seriously-aqua light fixture is not something that I normally go for, but fer realz guys, in this space, it is 100% amazing. Im so glad Jeff talked me into it after I suggested this crazy scheme to him once in passing.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Subway Tiled Mudroom, Chalk Message board" height="900" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_16.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          You guys! Guess how much money I spent on the tile in this mudroom? Would you be surprised if I told you that all this wonderfulness cost less than $100 and 2 days worth of work?
         </p>
         <p>
          I was just sitting in the living room one day, staring at my handy tile job in the kitchen when it occurred to me that
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/10/kitchen-8-week-progress-report.html" title="Tiling the Mudroom">
           tiling the mudroom
          </a>
          would be the best idea ever. Id tile it so that it would be an identical height to the height of the tile in the pantry. It would go around the entire room and it would be totally awesome. Jeff was not convinced at all that this would be a good idea. We were already weeks into the kitchen project and he was definitely over having me spend all my waking time focusing on it. I had to convince him that this wouldnt add on that much time or money and that it would be so awesome. 48 hours later, and blamo, it was done. This is probably the best $100 bucks Ive ever spent.
         </p>
         <p>
          I added the
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2011/05/mud-room-makeover.html" title="Mudroom Makeover">
           magnetic chalk message boards
          </a>
          a few years ago. I painted and wallpapered them way back then, to add a bit of function and a little bit of pretty to this space. When I tiled the mudroom I added built-in hooks for the kids to hang jackets and backpacks. It has become an essential part of our house, so much so that I dont think I could design a new more modern kitchen without including a space like this. We had problems with a landing space forever. Keys, coats, shoes, hats and mittens were taking over our house.  It probably took us 3+ years of living in the home before we really figured it out and started utilizing the mudroom. This space is organized; everything has a place. Towels for summer backyard pools, car keys, gardening shears, gardening gloves, canning gear, bubbles and bubble accessories, scarves, hats, mittens, snowpants and all that good wintery stuff. Its basically all the stuff we need for the outdoors  all in one place, no matter what time of year. Plus we have the
          <a href="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/2013/03/stylish-shoe-storage.html" title="Stylish Shoe Storage">
           shoe storage
          </a>
          right off the kitchen, which by the way, has entirely changed our clutter and our lives  couldnt recommend it enough.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Detail Mudroom, Subway Tile" height="918" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_17.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Deuce Cities Henhouse Kitchen Reveal - Subway Tile in the Mudroom, Cabinets and Storage" src="http://www.deucecitieshenhouse.com/images/2014/kitchen_reveal/henhouse_kitchen_18.jpg" width="600"/>
          <br/>
          I mentioned it in my 2013 post from last week, but this has seriously been such a great project for me. Ive learned that I am capable of doing most home repairs! I know I cant do everything, but I can do a lot. I have the skillz and every time I tackle a new unknown project I get an education on what I need to know to accomplish the next home renovation waiting around the corner. The satisfaction I feel from this project is pretty great, and it doesnt hurt that the end result turned out so amazing (I must humbly say).
         </p>
         <p>
          I honestly think we could live with this kitchen for a long time. We had originally scheduled a kitchen gut job and renovation around the 10-year mark of owning the house. But as of this moment, I dont envision that happening because it really doesnt need it. This old kitchen is so pretty and functional for us now with these tiny renovations, and has maintained the original character of the house. Owning an old home is a special thing. Although all of them need renovating and updating, its important to respect the age and history of these places whenever possible.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thanks for all the support and comments throughout the project, guys. It really helped motivate me to see it through. Youre the best!

             <p>
              Hey Caroline!  That is the worst, when things
              <i>
               seem
              </i>
              to have been updated recently but dont really show it.  That is how I felt about our kitchen too. Its great for someone elses taste, but not your own.
             </p>
             <p>
              The tile went a long way for our money.  I was really scared to tile at first, but I just watched a bunch of you tube videos,
              <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v6Ng9f2ucU4" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               this one was especially helpful
              </a>
              .  Once I started, I realized there wasnt that much to be scared of.
             </p>
             <p>
              I did back-butter all of my tile instead of putting the mastic (I used mastic because there wasnt a lot of water being splashed around  otherwise you would use thinset) directly on the wall.  I think this was a good method for me, because trying to get mastic on the wall and then tile it quickly was to daunting.  I did get pretty fast at back-buttering and things moved quickly.
             </p>
             <p>
              Grouting was also a big unknown.  I think the biggest surprise for me was really having to remove the grout out of the tile spaces with a damp sponge after the grout began to set.  I thought it would be a quick wipe down but the grout gets really built up around the spaces between the tiles, and if you dont get in there and wipe it down you will end up with fat old grout lines.
             </p>
             <p>
              I think anyone can do it though, its not that scary, even though I know it seems like it is.
             </p>
             <p>
              Oh also, I borrowed my parents neighbors tile saw.  It was awesome because I was able to cut out all the hard outlet spots and weird tricky corners, but it totally sucked because it was 40 outside and I spent an entire day out there with a wet saw.  I was frozen.
             </p>

Now that everything is wireless, though it isnt as important.
