       <div>
        <h3>
         Short Summary
        </h3>
        <p>
         My name is Shell Underwood. I am a former teacher, a board member of the Conasauga River Alliance and a volunteer for Whitfield County Schools. As a teacher I took my students each year to the snorkeling hole and saw what an impact it had on students. Students who are in college now tell me how they are now going to study freshwater biology because of their experience at the snorkeling hole.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Few places in the world equal the biodiversity of streams in the southeastern United States. The streams of the Southeast are rich in freshwater mussels, snails, aquatic insects, crayfish, salamanders, and freshwater fish. The Conasauga River Watershed is tiny compared to large Western watersheds such as the Columbia or the Colorado. The Conasauga River Watershed encompasses only 1 percent of the land base of these two major watersheds combined but supports more native fish species.
         <br/>
        </p>
        <div>
         <div>
          <p>
           Some think of gold as a mountain treasure, but around the Conasauga River, the real gem is clean, free-flowing water. The River begins as several small cold-water springs in the Blue Ridge Mountains at an elevation of 4,000 feet. Fish diversity is low in the headwaters, with only 6 to 8 different species. As the river courses through the Chattahoochee and Cherokee National Forests, the slope decreases, the river widens, and the water becomes warm enough to support a greater diversity of fish.  In the Alaculsy Valley of Georgia and Tennessee, the biodiversity blossoms and fish diversity grows to 40 native species.
          </p>
          <br/>
          <p>
           There is an intimate relation between our streams and the development and conservation of all the other great and permanent sources of wealth.
          </p>
          <p>
            Theodore Roosevelt, 1907 speech
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div>
        <h3>
         What We Need &amp; What You Get
        </h3>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Funding will pay for the cost of students who are unable to pay for the lifeguards, wetsuits, snorkel gear, bus transportation, and will go to the development of new curriculum to be distributed to teachers once the students return to their classrooms.
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          Anyone who funds this cause with a donation of $100 or more will get a free trip to the snorkeling hole with the US Forest Service. Donations of $1000 or more will get a group of 20 people to attend their own snorkeling adventure. Ages are 12 and up. Put on a face mask and get a closer look at fish species common to the 
Conasauga River like the freckled-belly madtom, blue shiner, 
green-breasted darter, and Conasauga logperch. Downstream of the 
Snorkeling Hole, the River becomes larger, supporting a total of 26 
species of mussels and 90 species of fish.
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          If the goal is not reached all funding will still go to the program. Each dollar will be matched by the local school systems.
          <br/>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </div>
       <div>
        <h3>
         The Impact
        </h3>
        <p>
         Our campaign will make a difference in the lives of students who would not get the opportunity to experience this hidden jewel in the Cherokee National Forest and your contribution will allow:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Students will have an authentic and engaging opportunity to learn about how the local watershed affects the environment and community.
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          Teachers will have a unique lesson plan that will help students to continue their learning with state standards.
         </li>
         <li>
          Students will develop a passion for helping others impact their local community that will continue for a life time.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </div>
       <div>
        <h3>
         Other Ways You Can Help
        </h3>
        <p>
         Some people just can't contribute, but that doesn't mean you can't help:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <p>
           Volunteer with conservation organizations and partnerships, such as the Conasauga River Alliance. Which have been established to help maintain a healthy watershed to stabilize river banks and filter out non-point source pollutants in runoff, businesses and landowners maintain a 50-foot wide corridor of native trees, shrubs, and grasses along the river.
           <br/>
          </p>
         </li>
         <li>
          <p>
           Engage in river-friendly conservation practices to include fencing livestock out of streams, constructing chicken litter composting sheds away from water sources, redesigning forest roads and parking lots to slow and filter stormwater runoff, repairing septic systems, and designing interpretive walks along the river.
          </p>
         </li>
         <li>
          <p>
           Volunteer in the Annual Conasauga River Clean Up in October. For additional information, please contact John Lugthart at jlugthart@daltonstate.edu, 706-272-2485.
          </p>
         </li>
         <li>
          Ask folks to get the word out and make some noise about the Conasauga River Snorkeling Program campaign.
         </li>
         <li>
          Use the Indiegogo share tools on your Facebook and other Social Networking Sites!
         </li>
        </ul>
       </div>
