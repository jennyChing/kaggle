     <div class="post-title">
      <h1>
       Details:
       <em>
        Alleyne v. United States
       </em>
      </h1>
      <div class="subhead">
       <div class="time">
        Posted Mon, June 17th, 2013 11:33 am by Mike Gottlieb
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="post-content">
      <p>
       <i>
        Alleyne v. United States
       </i>
       raised a longstanding controversy in criminal law regarding the method of proof that the Sixth Amendment requires to impose a mandatory minimum sentence. It has been settled since the Courts 2000 decision in
       <i>
        Apprendi v. New Jersey
       </i>
       that any facts which increase a criminal defendants maximum possible sentence are considered elements of the criminal offense that must be proved to a jury beyond a reasonable doubt. In other words, if a statute makes it illegal to sell a drug and authorizes a ten-year maximum sentence for such an offense, but provides for a twenty-year maximum sentence for a sale of a larger quantity of the same drug, the jury rather than the judge must make a finding about the quantity before the twenty-year maximum may be imposed.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="more-165169">
       </span>
       In 2002, the Court decided in
       <i>
        Harris v. United States
       </i>
       that
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       did not apply to facts that would increase a defendants mandatory
       <i>
        minimum
       </i>
       sentence, and therefore that a judge could constitutionally decide to apply a mandatory minimum sentence on the basis of facts not proven to a jury. Justice Breyer concurred in part in
       <i>
        Harris
       </i>
       not because he agreed that
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       could be distinguished, but rather because he continued to disagree with
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       as a whole and could not yet accept its rule. For years, commentators have wondered whether
       <i>
        Harris
       </i>
       could survive as
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       became increasingly settled, and Justice Breyer himself has in the past called into question his vote in
       <i>
        Harris.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Alleyne
       </i>
       answers this question by overruling
       <i>
        Harris
       </i>
       . At issue in
       <i>
        Alleyne
       </i>
       was a seven-year sentence imposed on a defendant for having brandished a firearm while using or carrying [it] during and in relation to a crime of violence. At trial, the jury found only that the defendant used or carried the firearm, which carries a five-year mandatory minimum sentence. The judge, relying on
       <i>
        Harris,
       </i>
       found that the defendant had brandished the firearm, and thereby increased the defendants mandatory minimum sentence to seven years. In a five-to-four decision by Justice Thomas (joined by Justices Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor, and Kagan), the Court today held that the defendants seven-year mandatory minimum sentence violated his Sixth Amendment right to trial by jury because the question of brandishing was never submitted to the jury. The Courts opinion explains that the logic of
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       requires a jury to find all facts that fix the penalty range of a crime. According to the Court, the mandatory minimum is just as important to the statutory range as is the statutory maximum. The Court made clear that its holding was not designed to limit the discretion of the trial judge in imposing sentences within the range defined by the statutory maximum and mandatory minimum. The Court therefore vacated Alleynes sentence and remanded the case for resentencing in line with the jurys verdict.
      </p>
      <p>
       Justice Breyer, who provided the pivotal fifth vote, concurred in part and in the judgment. His concurrence reiterates his belief that
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       was wrongly decided, but explains that because 
       <i>
        Apprendi
       </i>
       has now defined the relevant legal regime for an additional decade the law can no longer tolerate the anomaly that the
       <i>
        Apprendi/Harris
       </i>
       distinction creates. Justice Sotomayors concurrence (joined by Justices Ginsburg and Kagan), argues that overruling
       <i>
        Harris
       </i>
       is consistent with the principles of
       <i>
        stare decisis.
       </i>
       The Chief Justice (joined by Justices Scalia and Kennedy) and Justice Alito each wrote dissents.
      </p>
