        <a href="http://www.asianart.org/blog/index.php/2009/04/23/welcome/" rel="bookmark" title="Welcome to the blog of the Asian Art Museum">
         Welcome to the blog of the Asian Art Museum
        </a>
       </h2>
       <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 440px">
        <a href="http://67.52.109.59/code/emuseum.asp?style=browse&amp;currentrecord=1&amp;page=search&amp;profile=objects&amp;searchdesc=B66S8&amp;quicksearch=B66S8&amp;newvalues=1&amp;newstyle=single&amp;newcurrentrecord=1" title="dancing ganesha">
         <img alt="dancing ganesha, asian art museum" src="http://www.rightreading.com/asian_art_museum/_AAM_photos/images/ganesha.jpg" title="ganesha, asian art museum" width="430"/>
        </a>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Dancing Ganesha, 900-1000, sandstone. India, Madhya Pradesh state. Asian Art Museum, the Avery Brundage Collection, B66S8.
        </p>
       </div>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Below Ganeshas right foot is the rodent that serves as his animal mount. Ganesha once battled a powerful demon who had been terrorizing the gods. Using one of his tusks, which had broken during the struggle, he speared the demon, who was then transformed into a rodent and brought under Ganeshas control. Because it can find a way through the most daunting of barriers, the rodent is an appropriate vehicle for Ganesha, who has the power to create and remove obstacles. Some of Ganeshas hand gestures in this sculpture, in addition to his swaying posture and his entourage of musicians, indicate that he is dancing.
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Welcome to the blog of the
        <a href="http://www.asianart.org/i" title="asian art museum web home">
         Asian Art Museum  Chong-Moon Lee Center for Asian Art and Culture
        </a>
        . Its always good to begin with Ganesha, lord of auspicious beginnings. May he favor this blog.
       </p>
