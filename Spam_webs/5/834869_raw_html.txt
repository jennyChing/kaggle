       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         How to Make Maple Syrup
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2015-01-12T08:13:31+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          January 12, 2015
         </time>
         by
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://commonsensehome.com/author/laurieneverman/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Laurie Neverman
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="entry-comments-link">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/#comments">
           51 Comments
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <div class="pf-content">
         <p>
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/how-to-make-maple-syrup.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - From tapping the trees and gathering the sweet sap to bottling your syrup, using it in recipes and making maple cream." class="aligncenter wp-image-22014 size-full pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/how-to-make-maple-syrup.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="400" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/how-to-make-maple-syrup.jpg" width="600"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Maple syrup is a delicious, healthy alternative to refined sugar, and is enjoyed on the table as well as in many recipes. It is simple to make maple syrup in the backyard, though once bitten by the sugaring bug, many go on to expand their operations!
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Note: If youre here from the 
           <strong>
            50 Things You Should Make Instead of Buy
           </strong>
            article, we do have a quick and easy homemade maple syrup substitute at the bottom of the page.
          </em>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #1: Identify Maple Trees and Wait for the Right Temperature Range
         </h2>
         <p>
          There are many species of maple trees. The sap gathered from all of them can be boiled down into syrup.
          <a href="http://www.massmaple.org/treeid.php" target="_blank" title="Maple Tree Identification">
           <em>
            Read more about Maple Tree Identification
           </em>
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Most syrup is produced in the northern states, but can be made as far south as Tennessee.
          <strong>
           The temperature must drop below freezing at night and rise above during the day for the sap to run.
          </strong>
          <strong>
           Trees should have at least a twelve inch diameter before being tapped.
          </strong>
          When the maple trees start budding, sugaring is over, as the sap produces bitter syrup.
          <span id="more-7404">
          </span>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #2: Gather Your Syrup Making Equipment
         </h2>
         <p>
          Traditional
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B002O7LUWO?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creativeASIN=B002O7LUWO&amp;linkCode=xm2&amp;tag=commosensehom-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="Maple syrup starter kit">
           maple syrup making equipment
          </a>
          includes sap spouts, buckets, and covers; which are available in hardware stores, from maple sugaring equipment suppliers, or used from other sugarmakers.
          <strong>
           To tap the trees
          </strong>
          y
          <strong>
           ou will need a drill and bit, and a hammer.
          </strong>
          The minimum that a backyard sugarmaker must do is to drill the taphole and tap in the spout.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           To collect the sap:
          </strong>
          Milk jugs or even plastic bags can be hung on the spout; or plastic, food-grade tubing can be connected to the spout and the sap run into a bucket or collecting tank on the ground.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           To boil the sap into syrup:
          </strong>
          Plan how you will boil the sap  a simple pan on the stove, a
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0009JXYQ4?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creativeASIN=B0009JXYQ4&amp;linkCode=xm2&amp;tag=commosensehom-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="Outdoor propane burner">
           turkey fryer
          </a>
          , an outside set-up over a fire-place, or a larger, purchased arch and evaporator.
          <em>
           Editors note: a friend of mine cooked her maple syrup down in her kitchen  once. It left a sticky film on every surface. I highly recommend processing outside or in a sugar shack.
          </em>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7424" style="width: 410px">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/tapping-maple-tree.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - Tapping a maple tree" class="size-full wp-image-7424 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/tapping-maple-tree.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="600" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/tapping-maple-tree.jpg" title="How to Make Maple Syrup - Tapping a Maple Tree" width="400"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Using a cordless drill to tap maple trees.
          </p>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #3  Tap the Trees
         </h2>
         <p>
          Tap the trees in the early spring, as daytime temperatures rise above freezing. Drill a hole the size of your spout, at a slight incline. (Note angle above.) Tap the spout into the hole firmly, hang the bucket, and put the cover on to keep out rain and bugs. This is the magic moment, as the first drop of clear, sweet sap runs off the spout  often onto an eagerly waiting tongue!
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7425" style="width: 610px">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-syrup-making.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - Gathering the sap" class="size-full wp-image-7425 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-syrup-making.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="437" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-syrup-making.jpg" title="How to Make Maple Syrup - Gathering the Sap" width="600"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Dad and Great-Uncle Gathering maple sap the old-fashioned way.
          </p>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #4  Gather the Sap
         </h2>
         <p>
          Sap is perishable, and should be gathered and boiled daily, or kept cold in a storage container until boiling. Filter the sap to remove any impurities.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #5  Boil the Sap
         </h2>
         <p>
          Boil the sap until it reaches seven degrees above the boiling point of water on that day, or until it aprons, or runs off a spoon in a sheet. Whenever maple sap is boiled, it tends to foam up and boil over the side of the pan. Rubbing a little butter around the rim of the pan, or dripping in a few drops of milk or dairy cream during boiling will keep the sap or syrup from boiling over. My Dad always said, Never turn your back on boiling sap! The amount of milk is just a few drops to any size kettle, though dripping in a few more drops is more frequently needed with a larger quantity.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7431" style="width: 615px">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Testing-maple-syrup.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - Aproning the Maple Syrup" class="wp-image-7431 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Testing-maple-syrup.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="454" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Testing-maple-syrup.jpg" title="How to Make Maple Syrup - Aproning the Maple Syrup" width="605"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Testing, or aproning, the maple syrup to see if it is ready
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Sugar on snow is made by boiling pure maple syrup to 233 degrees, on a candy thermometer. Do not stir. We drizzle the hot syrup over pans of packed snow-or directly onto fresh snowbanks! Each person is given a fork to twirl the taffy-like candy up on. In warmer climates, those wishing to try this treat can make it with purchased maple syrup, and pour it over crushed ice.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7430" style="width: 610px">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-sugar-snow.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - Maple sugar on snow" class="size-full wp-image-7430 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-sugar-snow.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="450" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-sugar-snow.jpg" title="How to Make Maple Syrup - Maple sugar on snow" width="600"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Drizzling the maple sugar on snow on a fresh, clean snowbank.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7427" style="width: 610px">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/homemade-maple-syrup.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - Enjoying maple sugar on snow" class="size-full wp-image-7427 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/homemade-maple-syrup.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="450" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/homemade-maple-syrup.jpg" title="How to Make Maple Syrup - Enjoying maple sugar on snow" width="600"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Enjoying some homemade maple sugar on snow.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          We produce maple cream by boiling it to the soft ball stage, cooling it without moving to room temperature, and whipping into a smooth, creamy spread. This is delicious served on toast, doughnuts, crackers, fruit.Candies, traditionally shaped in molds as maple leaves or little maple men and women, are made by boiling the syrup to 243 degrees, and then stirring the syrup while it is hot and pouring into mods before it sets up. My children used to boil the syrup, and then roll the candy into little balls, pressing in nutmeats. A wonderful treat, made from their big sugar maple in the yard.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #6  Filter and Bottle the Syrup
         </h2>
         <p>
          Filter the hot syrup through clean wool or synthetic syrup filters to remove
          <a href="http://umaine.edu/publications/7038e/" target="_blank" title="Maple Syrup Quality Control Manual">
           niter
          </a>
          , or sugar sand, which occurs naturally and gives the syrup a cloudy appearance and is gritty. Syrup can be canned in Mason jars, commercial syrup containers, or kept in either the refrigerator or freezer. At Silloway Maple, we can our syrup in glass and in plastic jugs by heating it up to 180 degrees. We leave no head space and do not process it in a canner. The syrup keeps indefinitely, though some say the flavor is best preserved for long periods by freezing. It does not expand, when frozen, like cider. Once the container has been opened, it should be refrigerated. If any mold does form on your syrup, it is harmless and can be removed by gently heating and skimming off.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7429" style="width: 296px">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/bottling-maple-syrup.jpg">
           <img alt="How to Make Maple Syrup - Canning the Maple Syrup" class="size-full wp-image-7429 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/bottling-maple-syrup.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="400" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/bottling-maple-syrup.jpg" title="How to Make Maple Syrup - Canning the Maple Syrup" width="286"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Louise Silloway, canning syrup.
          </p>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Maple Syrup Step #7  Enjoying Your Maple Syrup
         </h2>
         <p>
          Many sweet treats can be made from maple syrup. Pure maple cream, hard sugar, fudge, and maple sugar on snow are all enjoyed.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           How to substitute maple syrup for sugar (works for most recipes):
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Use -1 cup of maple syrup for each cup of sugar.
          </li>
          <li>
           Decrease liquid by 2-4 tablespoons for each cup of syrup used.
          </li>
          <li>
           Add  teaspoon of baking soda.
          </li>
          <li>
           Decrease oven temperature by 25 degrees.
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
          Maple granola is one of our family favorites, and is very easy to make.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <a href="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-granola.jpg">
           <img alt="Homemade maple granola" class="aligncenter wp-image-7432 pib-hover-img" data-pin-media="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-granola.jpg" data-pin-url="http://commonsensehome.com/make-maple-syrup/" height="337" src="http://commonsensehome.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/maple-granola.jpg" title="Homemade maple granola" width="400"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Maple Granola Recipe
         </h2>
         <p>
          <em>
           Courtesy of Silloway Maple
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Ingredients
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           7 c. rolled oats
          </li>
          <li>
           1 c. coconut
          </li>
          <li>
           1 c. almonds
          </li>
          <li>
           1 c. sunflower seeds
          </li>
          <li>
           1 c. oil
          </li>
          <li>
           1 c. maple syrup
          </li>
          <li>
           1 T. vanilla
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Directions
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Combine the dry ingredients in a large bowl. Mix the oil, syrup and vanilla in a separate bowl, and pour over the oat mixture. Mix well, and spread on baking sheets. Bake in 300 degree oven for two hours, stirring occasionally. Remove from oven, and add raisins or any additional dried fruit. Store in a tightly closed container.
         </p>
         <p>
          I hope this post will encourage you to give maple syrup making a try and that it will become a tradition for your family like it is for mine. If you dont have access to maple trees, we do sell Pure Vermont Maple Syrup, Maple Cream and Maple Sugar Coated Nuts in our online store, or you can visit a local producer near you to enjoy some real maple syrup.
         </p>
         <p>

           <a href="http://www.sillowaymaple.com/index.php/a-farm-wife-s-journal" target="_blank" title="A Farm Wife's Journal">
            A Farm Wifes Journal
           </a>
           , and is a maple sugarmaker and homemaker. Silloway Maple is family owned and operated. They recently built a new solar-powered sugarhouse, which will also produce part of the energy used on the family dairy farm. Daughter Laura manages the business Facebook page,

           <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
            <strong>
             For those of you visiting from the 50 Things You Should Stop Buying and Start Making post who dont happen to have maple trees available and were looking for a quick and easy homemade maple syrup substitute.
            </strong>
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Homemade Maple Syrup Substitute
         </h2>
         <p>
          <em>
           Adapted from
           <a href="http://allrecipes.com/recipe/homemade-maple-syrup/" target="_blank" title="Homemade Maple Syrup">
            All Recipes.com
           </a>
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Ingredients
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           1 cup water
          </li>
          <li>
           1 cup white sugar
          </li>
          <li>
           1 cup brown sugar or
           <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B005P0JP56?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creativeASIN=B005P0JP56&amp;linkCode=xm2&amp;tag=commosensehom-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="organic sucanat">
            sucanat
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           1 tablespoon maple flavored extract (
           <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B007C8DVZY?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creativeASIN=B007C8DVZY&amp;linkCode=xm2&amp;tag=commosensehom-20" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            Buy pure maple extract
           </a>
           )
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Directions
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Bring the water, white sugar, and brown sugar to a boil in a saucepan over medium-high heat. Reduce heat to medium-low, and stir in the maple extract; simmer 3 minutes longer. Use immediately, or store in a mason jar in the refrigerator for up to two weeks. For a thicker syrup, use two cups brown sugar. You may also substitute 2 cups sucanat (less refined cane sugar) for the sugars. Makes 2 cups of syrup.
         </p>
         <p>
          I still highly recommend the real deal, but I understand that not everyone has maple trees or local maple syrup producers available.

            Just a couple of notes-
            <br/>
            A 7/16 drill bit is the size to drill the hole. Spiles are tapered and will fit into this size hole nicely. The hole will normally be about two inches deep.
           </p>
           <p>
            Approximately 30 litres or quarts of sap are required to make one litre or quart of syrup. This will vary depending upon the sugar content of the sap.
           </p>
           <p>
            I make around 25 to 30 litres of syrup each year which I give to family, friends and neighbors. Its a great opportunity to get outside as winter ends.

            <br/>
            Milk can be substituted for water and your fat of choice can be substituted for oil
            <br/>
            I use honey or maple syrup instead of sugar
            <br/>
            I also use the bread machine to take care of the bread, but put the dough in a pan and bake it at the end.  The times are listed below  different temperatures are listed.  If I am not baking anything else, I usually use the 400 degree listing.
            <br/>
            Our absolute favorite is the oatmeal bread.  It only lasts about 3 days without going moldy or being too dry  freeze it if you dont intend to use it up quick.
            <br/>
            The wheat bread is heavy.  I dont add anything to make it rise a bit better.
            <br/>
            With any bread machine, always Put into container in order It is listed  Hot water first ..
           </p>
           <p>
            Options
            <br/>
            Can mix milk and 1/3 c water to make 1 1/8 c
            <br/>
            2T. Butter melted can replace oil
            <br/>
            Can sprinkle  c sugar mixed with 2 tsp.
            <br/>
            cinnamon and roll up dough and bake
           </p>
           <p>
            Can bake in bread pan:
            <br/>
            350 for 40 min. or
            <br/>
            Metal 400 for 35
            <br/>
            Or 425 for 30 min.
           </p>
           <p>
            1  # loaf
           </p>
           <p>
            Put into container in order
            <br/>
            It is listed.
           </p>
           <p>
            White bread
           </p>

            1 1/8 C. warm water
            <br/>
            1  Tbsp. vegetable oil
            <br/>
            1 1/2 Tbsp honey
            <br/>
            3 c. white flour
            <br/>
            1  tsp. salt
            <br/>
            2 tsp. dry yeast
           </p>
           <p>
            OATMEAL BREAD      the best one!
            <br/>
            1 1/8 C. warm water
            <br/>
            1  Tbsp. vegetable oil
            <br/>
            2 Tbsp honey
            <br/>
             c. ground quick oatmeal
            <br/>
            2  c. flour
            <br/>
            1  tsp. salt
            <br/>
            2 tsp. dry yeast
           </p>

            WHEAT BREAD
            <br/>
            1 1/8 C. warm water
            <br/>
            1  Tbsp. vegetable oil
            <br/>
            1 Tbsp honey
            <br/>
            Scant 2 c. white flour
            <br/>
            1 c. wheat flour
            <br/>
            1  tsp. salt
            <br/>
            2 tsp. dry yeast
