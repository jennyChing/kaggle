     <p>
      <b>
       Roxy Music
      </b>
      were an English
      <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">
       glam rock
      </a>
      group formed in 1971 by
      <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Ferry" title="Bryan Ferry">
       Bryan Ferry
      </a>
      , who became the group's lead vocalist and chief songwriter, and bassist
      <a href="/wiki/Graham_Simpson_(musician)" title="Graham Simpson (musician)">
       Graham Simpson
      </a>
      . Alongside Ferry, the other longtime members were
      <a href="/wiki/Phil_Manzanera" title="Phil Manzanera">
       Phil Manzanera
      </a>
      (guitar),
      <a href="/wiki/Andy_Mackay" title="Andy Mackay">
       Andy Mackay
      </a>
      (saxophone and
      <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">
       oboe
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Thompson_(musician)" title="Paul Thompson (musician)">
       Paul Thompson
      </a>
      (drums and percussion). Other former members include
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">
       Brian Eno
      </a>
      (synthesiser and "treatments"),
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Jobson" title="Eddie Jobson">
       Eddie Jobson
      </a>
      (synthesiser and violin), and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Gustafson_(musician)" title="John Gustafson (musician)">
       John Gustafson
      </a>
      (bass). Although the band took a break from group activities in 1976 and again in 1983, they reunited for a concert tour in 2001, and toured together intermittently between that time and their break-up in 2011. Ferry frequently enlisted members of Roxy Music as session musicians for his solo releases.
     </p>
     <p>
      Roxy Music attained popular and critical success in Europe and Australia during the 1970s and early 1980s, beginning with their debut album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music_(album)" title="Roxy Music (album)">
        Roxy Music
       </a>
      </i>
      (1972).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prendergast_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prendergast-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The band was highly influential as leading proponents of the more experimental, musically sophisticated element of
      <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">
       glam
      </a>
      , as well as a significant influence on early English
      <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">
       punk
      </a>
      music.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also provided a model for many
      <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">
       new wave
      </a>
      acts and the experimental
      <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">
       electronic
      </a>
      groups of the early 1980s. The group is distinguished by their visual and musical sophistication and their preoccupation with style and glamour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ferry and co-founding member Eno have also had influential solo careers, the latter becoming one of the most significant record producers and collaborators of the late 20th century.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine ranked Roxy Music No.98 on its "The Immortals  100 The Greatest Artists of All Time" list.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The band's last studio album was 1982's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Avalon_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Avalon (Roxy Music album)">
        Avalon
       </a>
      </i>
      . In 2005 they began recording a new studio album, which would have been their ninth, and would have been their first record since 1973 with Brian Eno, who wrote two songs for it and also played keyboards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Bryan Ferry eventually confirmed that material from these sessions would be released as a Ferry solo album, with Eno playing on "a couple of tracks,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanghera_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sanghera-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and that he doesn't think they'll ever record as Roxy Music again.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The album ultimately became Ferry's 2010 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Olympia_(Bryan_Ferry_album)" title="Olympia (Bryan Ferry album)">
        Olympia
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Roxy Music played a series of 40th anniversary shows in 2011, but has since become inactive as a performing entity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_early_years_.281970.E2.80.9371.29">
       Formation and early years (197071)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roxy_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and early years (197071)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In November 1970, Bryan Ferry, who had recently lost his job teaching ceramics at a girls' school for holding impromptu record listening sessions,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      advertised for a keyboard player to collaborate with him and Graham Simpson, a bass player he knew from his Newcastle art college band, the Gas Board, and with whom he collaborated on his first songs. In early 1970 Ferry had auditioned as lead singer for
      <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">
       King Crimson
      </a>
      , who were seeking a replacement for departed vocalist
      <a href="/wiki/Greg_Lake" title="Greg Lake">
       Greg Lake
      </a>
      . Although
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fripp" title="Robert Fripp">
       Robert Fripp
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pete_Sinfield" title="Pete Sinfield">
       Pete Sinfield
      </a>
      decided that Ferry's voice was unsuitable for King Crimson's material, they were impressed with his talent and helped the fledgling Roxy Music to obtain a contract with
      <a href="/wiki/E.G._Records" title="E.G. Records">
       E.G. Records
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Andy_Mackay" title="Andy Mackay">
       Andy Mackay
      </a>
      replied to Ferry's advertisement, not as a keyboard player but a saxophonist and oboist, though he did have a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VCS3" title="VCS3">
       VCS3
      </a>
      synthesizer. Mackay had already met
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">
       Brian Eno
      </a>
      during university days, as both were interested in avant-garde and electronic music. Although Eno was a non-musician, he could operate a synthesizer and owned a
      <a href="/wiki/Revox" title="Revox">
       Revox
      </a>
      reel-to-reel tape machine, so Mackay convinced him to join the band as a technical adviser. Before long Eno was an official member of the group. When founding drummer Dexter Lloyd, a classically trained
      <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">
       timpanist
      </a>
      , left the band, an advertisement was placed in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">
        Melody Maker
       </a>
      </i>
      saying "wonder drummer wanted for an avant rock group".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Thompson_(musician)" title="Paul Thompson (musician)">
       Paul Thompson
      </a>
      responded to the advertisement and joined the band in June 1971. The group's name was partly an homage to the titles of old cinemas and
      <a href="/wiki/Dance_hall" title="Dance hall">
       dance halls
      </a>
      , and partly a
      <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">
       pun
      </a>
      on the word
      <i>
       rock
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Ferry had named the band Roxy originally, but after learning of an American band with the same name he changed the name to Roxy Music.
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 1971 Roxy advertised in
      <i>
       Melody Maker
      </i>
      seeking the "Perfect Guitarist" and
      <a href="/wiki/Phil_Manzanera" title="Phil Manzanera">
       Phil Manzanera
      </a>
      was one of about twenty players who auditioned. Manzanera, the son of an English father and a Colombian mother, had spent a considerable amount of time in South America and Cuba as a child and although he did not have the same art school background as Ferry, Mackay and Eno, he was perhaps the most proficient member of the band, with an interest in a wide variety of music. Manzanera also knew other well-known musicians, such as
      <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">
       David Gilmour
      </a>
      , who was a friend of his older brother, and
      <a href="/wiki/Soft_Machine" title="Soft Machine">
       Soft Machine
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wyatt" title="Robert Wyatt">
       Robert Wyatt
      </a>
      . However, Manzanera did not initially make the band as a guitarist; the successful applicant was
      <a href="/wiki/David_O%27List" title="David O'List">
       David O'List
      </a>
      , former guitarist with
      <a href="/wiki/The_Nice" title="The Nice">
       The Nice
      </a>
      . The group was impressed enough with Manzanera that he was invited to become Roxy Music's roadie, an offer which he accepted.
     </p>
     <p>
      The band's fortunes were greatly increased by the support of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">
        Melody Maker
       </a>
      </i>
      journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Williams_(journalist)" title="Richard Williams (journalist)">
       Richard Williams
      </a>
      and broadcaster
      <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">
       John Peel
      </a>
      . Williams became an enthusiastic fan after meeting Ferry and being given a demonstration tape during mid-1971, and wrote the first major article on the band, featured on
      <i>
       Melody Maker'
      </i>
      s "Horizons" page in edition of 7 August 1971. This line-up of Roxy Music (Ferry/Mackay/Eno/Simpson/Thompson/O'List) recorded a BBC session shortly thereafter.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_two_albums_.281972.E2.80.931973.29">
       First two albums (19721973)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roxy_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First two albums (19721973)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In early February 1972, guitarist O'List quit the group abruptly after an altercation with Paul Thompson which took place at their audition for
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Enthoven&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Enthoven (page does not exist)">
       David Enthoven
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/E.G._Records" title="E.G. Records">
       EG Management
      </a>
      . When O'List didn't show up for the next rehearsal, Manzanera was asked to come along, on the pretext of becoming the band's sound mixer. When he arrived he was invited to play guitar and quickly realised that it was an informal audition. Unbeknownst to the rest of the group, Manzanera had learned their entire repertoire and as a result, he was immediately hired as O'List's permanent replacement, joining on 14 February 1972. Two weeks later Roxy Music signed with EG Management.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      With this team, EG Management financed the recording of the tracks for their first album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music_(album)" title="Roxy Music (album)">
        Roxy Music
       </a>
      </i>
      , recorded in MarchApril 1972 and produced by King Crimson lyricist
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sinfield" title="Peter Sinfield">
       Peter Sinfield
      </a>
      . Both the album and its famous cover artwork were apparently completed before the group signed with Island Records. A&amp;R staffer Tim Clark records that although he argued strongly that Island should contract them, company boss
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Blackwell" title="Chris Blackwell">
       Chris Blackwell
      </a>
      at first seemed unimpressed and Clark assumed he was not interested. A few days later however, Clark and Enthoven were standing in the hallway of the Island offices examining cover images for the album when Blackwell walked past, glanced at the artwork and said "Looks great! Have we got them signed yet?"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The band signed with
      <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">
       Island Records
      </a>
      a few days later. The LP was released in June to good reviews and became a major success, reaching No.10 on the UK album chart in September 1972.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prendergast_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prendergast-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the first half of 1972 bassist Graham Simpson became increasingly withdrawn and uncommunicative, which led to his leaving the band almost immediately after the recording of the debut album. He was replaced by Rik Kenton.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To bring more attention to their album, Roxy Music decided to record and release a single. Their debut single was "
      <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Plain" title="Virginia Plain">
       Virginia Plain
      </a>
      ", which scored No.4 in the British charts. The band's eclectic visual image, captured in their debut performance on the BBC's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">
        Top of the Pops
       </a>
      </i>
      , became a cornerstone for the glam trend in the UK; the
      <i>
       TOTP
      </i>
      video of "Virginia Plain" was later parodied by the British comedy series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Big_Train" title="Big Train">
        Big Train
       </a>
      </i>
      . The single caused a renewed interest in the album. Soon after "Virginia Plain", Rik Kenton departed the band, which would never again have a permanent bass player.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/For_Your_Pleasure" title="For Your Pleasure">
        For Your Pleasure
       </a>
      </i>
      , was released in March 1973. It marked the beginning of the band's long, successful collaboration with producer
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Thomas_(record_producer)" title="Chris Thomas (record producer)">
       Chris Thomas
      </a>
      , who worked on all of the group's classic albums and singles in the 1970s. The album was promoted with the non-album single "Pyjamarama"; no album track was released as a single. At the time, Ferry was dating French model
      <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Lear" title="Amanda Lear">
       Amanda Lear
      </a>
      , who was photographed with a black jaguar for the front cover of the album (Ferry appears on the back cover as a dapper chauffeur standing in front of a
      <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">
       limousine
      </a>
      ). John Porter (credited as a guest) played bass on the record, while Sal Maida played bass for subsequent live shows.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stranded.2C_Country_Life.2C_Siren.2C_and_solo_projects_.281973.E2.80.9377.29">
       <i>
        Stranded
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Country Life
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Siren
       </i>
       , and solo projects (197377)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roxy_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stranded, Country Life, Siren, and solo projects (197377)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roxy_Music_band.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="348" data-file-width="512" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Roxy_Music_band.jpg/220px-Roxy_Music_band.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Roxy_Music_band.jpg/330px-Roxy_Music_band.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Roxy_Music_band.jpg/440px-Roxy_Music_band.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roxy_Music_band.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Roxy Music, 1974
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Soon after recording
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/For_Your_Pleasure" title="For Your Pleasure">
        For Your Pleasure
       </a>
      </i>
      , Brian Eno left Roxy Music amidst increasing differences with Ferry about the management of the group.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The other members of the band are reported to have shared some of Eno's concerns about Ferry's dominance, but they elected to remain. Also, Johnny Gustafson became the band's permanent bass player for the next three studio albums, but not always for live shows; though he toured with Roxy on certain dates in 1973 and 1975, other live Roxy bassists of this period (19731976) included Sal Maida, John Wetton and Rick Wills.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Eno, meanwhile, was replaced by a 19-year-old multi-instrumentalist
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Jobson" title="Eddie Jobson">
       Eddie Jobson
      </a>
      , formerly of
      <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">
       progressive rockers
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Curved_Air" title="Curved Air">
       Curved Air
      </a>
      , who played keyboards and electric violin. Although some fans lamented the loss of the experimental attitude and camp aesthetic that Eno had brought to the band, the classically trained Jobson was an accomplished musician. Eno himself later acknowledged the quality of the two albums that followed his departure,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Stranded_(album)" title="Stranded (album)">
        Stranded
       </a>
      </i>
      (1973) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Country_Life_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Country Life (Roxy Music album)">
        Country Life
       </a>
      </i>
      (1974), and they are widely regarded as being among the most original and consistent British rock music albums of the period.
      <i>
       Rolling Stone
      </i>
      referred to the albums as marking "the zenith of contemporary British art rock".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The songs on these albums also cemented Ferry's persona as the epitome of the suave, jaded Euro-sophisticate. Although this persona undoubtedly began as a deliberately ironic device, during the mid-1970s it seemed to merge with Ferry's real life, as the working-class miner's son from the north of England became an international rock star, an icon of male style who had love affairs with many beautiful women, among them
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">
        Playboy
       </a>
      </i>
      playmate
      <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Cole" title="Marilyn Cole">
       Marilyn Cole
      </a>
      (who appeared on the cover of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Stranded_(album)" title="Stranded (album)">
        Stranded
       </a>
      </i>
      album) and fashion models
      <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Lear" title="Amanda Lear">
       Amanda Lear
      </a>
      (who would later date David Bowie) and
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Hall" title="Jerry Hall">
       Jerry Hall
      </a>
      (who later became
      <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">
       Mick Jagger
      </a>
      's partner).
     </p>
     <p>
      On the first two Roxy albums, all songs were written solely by Bryan Ferry. Beginning with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Stranded_(album)" title="Stranded (album)">
        Stranded
       </a>
      </i>
      , Mackay and Manzanera began to co-write some material. Gradually, their songwriting and musicianship became more integrated into the band's sound, although Ferry remained the dominant songwriter; throughout their career, all but one of Roxy's singles were written either wholly or jointly by Ferry. (However, Manzanera, Mackay and Thompson did individually write a few of the band's B-sides).
      <i>
       Stranded
      </i>
      was released in November 1973, and produced the top-10 single "
      <a href="/wiki/Street_Life_(Roxy_Music_song)" title="Street Life (Roxy Music song)">
       Street Life
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      The fourth album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Country_Life_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Country Life (Roxy Music album)">
        Country Life
       </a>
      </i>
      , was released in 1974, and was the first Roxy Music album to enter the US Top 40, albeit at No.37.
      <i>
       Country Life
      </i>
      was met with widespread critical acclaim, with
      <i>
       Rolling Stone
      </i>
      referring to it "as if Ferry ran a cabaret for psychotics, featuring chanteurs in a state of shock".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their fifth album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Siren_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Siren (Roxy Music album)">
        Siren
       </a>
      </i>
      , contained their only US hit, "
      <a href="/wiki/Love_Is_the_Drug" title="Love Is the Drug">
       Love Is the Drug
      </a>
      ". (Ferry said the song came to him while kicking the leaves during a walk through
      <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">
       Hyde Park
      </a>
      .) At this time Ferry was involved in a relationship with Texas-born supermodel
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Hall" title="Jerry Hall">
       Jerry Hall
      </a>
      . Ferry's paean to Hall, "Prairie Rose", directly inspired the
      <a href="/wiki/Talking_Heads" title="Talking Heads">
       Talking Heads
      </a>
      song "The Big Country" and was later covered by the Scottish rock group
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Country" title="Big Country">
       Big Country
      </a>
      as a B-side to their single "East of Eden" in 1984. Hall is also featured on the cover of the
      <i>
       Siren
      </i>
      LP and in the video for Ferry's 1976 international solo success, a cover of
      <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Harrison" title="Wilbert Harrison">
       Wilbert Harrison
      </a>
      's "Let's Stick Together".
     </p>
     <p>
      After the concert tours in support of
      <i>
       Siren
      </i>
      in 1976, Roxy Music disbanded. Their live album
      <i>
       Viva!
      </i>
      was released in August 1976. During this time Ferry released two solo records on which Manzanera and Thompson performed, and Manzanera reunited with Eno on the critically acclaimed one-off
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/801_Live" title="801 Live">
        801 Live
       </a>
      </i>
      album.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Final_albums_and_hiatus_.281978.E2.80.9383.29">
       Final albums and hiatus (197883)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roxy_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Final albums and hiatus (197883)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Roxy Music reunited during 1978 to record a new album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Manifesto_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Manifesto (Roxy Music album)">
        Manifesto
       </a>
      </i>
      , but with a reshuffled cast. Jobson was not present, and was reportedly not contacted for the reunion. (At that time, Jobson was touring and recording with his own band,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_(band)" title="UK (band)">
       UK
      </a>
      .) The sleeve of Manifesto explicitly identifies the revived Roxy Music line-up as a septet of Ferry, Manzanera, Mackay, Thompson, and new members Gary Tibbs (bass), Alan Spenner (bass) and
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Carrack" title="Paul Carrack">
       Paul Carrack
      </a>
      (keyboards).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, these newest three members were downgraded to session musician status (as opposed to full band members) on all subsequent releases.
     </p>
     <p>
      Three singles were spun off from
      <i>
       Manifesto
      </i>
      , including the major UK hits "Angel Eyes" (UK No.4), and "Dance Away" (UK No.2). Both these tracks are significantly different from the album versions, as "Dance Away" was remixed for single release, and "Angel Eyes" was entirely re-recorded.
     </p>
     <p>
      After the tour and before the recording of the next album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Flesh_and_Blood_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Flesh and Blood (Roxy Music album)">
        Flesh + Blood
       </a>
      </i>
      (1980), Thompson broke his thumb in a motorcycle mishap and took a leave from the band. Soon after, he left permanently.
     </p>
     <p>
      At this point, Ferry, Mackay and Manzanera became the only permanent members of Roxy Music, and were supplemented by a variety of session players over the next few years (including Tibbs, Spenner, Carrack,
      <a href="/wiki/Andy_Newmark" title="Andy Newmark">
       Andy Newmark
      </a>
      and Neil Hubbard.) The trio's 1980 album
      <i>
       Flesh + Blood
      </i>
      became a huge commercial success in their homeland, as the album went to No.1 on the UK charts, and spun off three UK hits: "Oh Yeah" (UK No.5), "Over You" (UK No.5), and "Same Old Scene" (UK No.12).
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the changed cast reflected a distinct change in Roxy's musical style. Gone were the unpredictable elements of the group's sound, giving way to smoother musical arrangements.
      <i>
       Rolling Stone
      </i>
      panned
      <i>
       Manifesto
      </i>
      ("Roxy Music has not gone disco. Roxy Music has not particularly gone anywhere else either"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) as well as
      <i>
       Flesh + Blood
      </i>
      ("such a shockingly bad Roxy Music record that it provokes a certain fascination"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), while other sources praised the reunion.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">
        Melody Maker
       </a>
      </i>
      said, of
      <i>
       Manifesto,
      </i>
      "...reservations aside, this may be the first such return bout ever attempted with any degree of genuine success: a technical knockout against the odds."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1981, Roxy Music recorded the non-album single "
      <a href="/wiki/Jealous_Guy" title="Jealous Guy">
       Jealous Guy
      </a>
      ". A cover of a song written and originally recorded by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">
       John Lennon
      </a>
      , Roxy Music recorded "Jealous Guy" as a tribute to Lennon after his 1980 death. The song topped the UK charts for two weeks in March 1981, becoming the band's only No.1 single.
     </p>
     <p>
      Later, with more sombre and carefully sculpted soundscapes, the band's eighthand finalstudio album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Avalon_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Avalon (Roxy Music album)">
        Avalon
       </a>
      </i>
      (1982), recorded at
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Blackwell" title="Chris Blackwell">
       Chris Blackwell
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Compass_Point_Studios" title="Compass Point Studios">
       Compass Point Studios
      </a>
      , was a major commercial success and restored the group's critical reputation
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and contained the successful single "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/More_Than_This_(Roxy_Music_song)" title="More Than This (Roxy Music song)">
       More Than This
      </a>
      ". The album also included several Roxy Music classics, such as "Avalon," "The Main Thing," "The Space Between," and "True to Life." The trio (augmented by session players) toured extensively until 1983, when Bryan Ferry dissolved the band and band members devoted themselves full-time to solo careers (see
      <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music#Solo_work" title="Roxy Music">
       below
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reunions_and_split_.282001.E2.80.932011.29">
       Reunions and split (20012011)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roxy_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reunions and split (20012011)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Ferry, Manzanera, Mackay, and Thompson re-formed in 2001 to celebrate the 30th anniversary of the band and toured extensively. A festival performance in Portugal and a short tour of the United States followed in 2003. Absent was Brian Eno, who criticised the motives of the band's reunion, saying, "I just don't like the idea. It leaves a bad taste".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later Eno remarked that his comment had been taken out of context. Manzanera and Thompson recorded and toured with Ferry on his 2002 album
      <i>
       Frantic
      </i>
      . Eno also contributed to
      <i>
       Frantic
      </i>
      on the track "I Thought".
     </p>
     <p>
      During 2002, Image Entertainment, Inc., released the concert DVD
      <i>
       Roxy Music Live at the Apollo
      </i>
      featuring performances of 20 songs plus interviews and rehearsal footage.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004,
      <i>
       Rolling Stone
      </i>
      magazine ranked the group No.98 on its list of the 100 Greatest Artists of All Time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Roxy Music gave a live performance at the 2005
      <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Festival" title="Isle of Wight Festival">
       Isle of Wight Festival
      </a>
      on 11 June 2005, their first UK concert since the 20012002 world tour. On 2 July 2005, the band played "
      <a href="/wiki/Jealous_Guy" title="Jealous Guy">
       Jealous Guy
      </a>
      ", "Do the Strand", and "Love is the Drug" at the Berlin contribution to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live8" title="Live8">
       Live8
      </a>
      ; "Do the Strand" is available on the 4-disc DVD collection, whereas "Love Is the Drug" can be found on the Live 8 Berlin DVD.
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2005, it was announced on Phil Manzanera's official site
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that the band, including Brian Eno, had decided to record an album of new material. The project would mark the first time Eno worked with Roxy Music since 1973's
      <i>
       For Your Pleasure
      </i>
      . After a number of denials that he would be involved with any Roxy Music reunion, on 19 May 2006 Eno revealed that he had contributed two songs to the new album as well as playing keyboards on other tracks. He did, however, rule out touring with the band.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Had the record been released as a Roxy Music album, it would have been the first album since
      <i>
       Manifesto
      </i>
      on which original drummer Paul Thompson performed. The album has, however, been released as a Bryan Ferry solo album entitled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Olympia_(Bryan_Ferry_album)" title="Olympia (Bryan Ferry album)">
        Olympia
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">

      <i>

      </i>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dylanesque_(album)" title="Dylanesque (album)">

     <p>
      In June 2007, the band hired a Liverpool-based design agency to develop their new website supporting their new album. Early in the year, Phil Manzanera revealed that the band were planning to sign a record contract. During an October 2007 interview, Ferry said that the album would include a collaboration with
      <a href="/wiki/Scissor_Sisters" title="Scissor Sisters">
       Scissor Sisters
      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_legacy">
       Style and legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roxy_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Style and legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The early style and presentation of Roxy Music was influenced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">
       art school
      </a>
      backgrounds of its principal members. Ferry, Mackay and Eno all had studied at prominent UK art colleges during the mid-to-late 1960s, when these institutions were introducing courses that avoided traditional art teaching practice, with its emphasis on painting, and instead focused on more recent developments, most notably
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">
       pop art
      </a>
      , and explored new concepts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">
       cybernetics
      </a>
      . As writer Michael Bracewell notes in his book
      <i>
       Roxy: the band that invented an era
      </i>
      , Roxy Music was created expressly by Ferry, Mackay and Eno as a means of combining their mutual interests in music, modern art and fashion.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ferry studied at the
      <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">
       Newcastle University
      </a>
      in the Sixties under renowned
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">
       pop artist
      </a>
      and educator
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hamilton_(artist)" title="Richard Hamilton (artist)">
       Richard Hamilton
      </a>
      , and many of Ferry's university friends, classmates and tutors  e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Rita_Donagh" title="Rita Donagh">
       Rita Donagh
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Head" title="Tim Head">
       Tim Head
      </a>
       became well-known artists in their own right. Eno studied at
      <a href="/wiki/Winchester_School_of_Art" title="Winchester School of Art">
       Winchester School of Art
      </a>
      and although his iconoclastic style became apparent early and caused some conflict with the college establishment, it also resulted in him meeting important artists and musicians including
      <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Cardew" title="Cornelius Cardew">
       Cornelius Cardew
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Bryars" title="Gavin Bryars">
       Gavin Bryars
      </a>
      . His interest in electronic music also resulted in his first meetings with Andy Mackay, who was studying at Reading University and who had likewise developed a strong interest in avant garde and electronic music.
     </p>
     <p>
      The three eventually joined forces in London during 197071 after meeting through mutual friends and decided to form a rock band.
     </p>
     <p>
      Roxy Music was one of the first rock music groups to create and maintain a carefully crafted look and style that included their stage presentation, music videos, album and single cover designs, and promotional materials such as posters, handbills, cards and badges. They were assisted in this by a group of friends and associates who helped to sculpt the classic Roxy Music 'look', notably fashion designer
      <a href="/wiki/Antony_Price" title="Antony Price">
       Antony Price
      </a>
      , hair stylist Keith Mainwaring, photographer Karl Stoecker, the group's "PR consultant" Simon Puxley (a former university friend of Mackay's) and Ferry's art school classmate Nicholas De Ville. Well-known critic
      <a href="/wiki/Lester_Bangs" title="Lester Bangs">
       Lester Bangs
      </a>
      went so far as to say that Roxy represented "the triumph of artifice".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The band's debut album, produced by
      <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">
       King Crimson
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pete_Sinfield" title="Pete Sinfield">
       Pete Sinfield
      </a>
      , was the first in a series of increasingly sophisticated album covers, art-directed by Ferry in collaboration with his friend Nick De Ville. The album artwork imitated the visual style of classic "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girlie_magazine" title="Girlie magazine">
       girlie
      </a>
      " and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_magazine" title="Fashion magazine">
       fashion magazines
      </a>
      , featuring high-fashion shots of scantily clad models
      <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Lear" title="Amanda Lear">
       Amanda Lear
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Cole" title="Marilyn Cole">
       Marilyn Cole
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Hall" title="Jerry Hall">
       Jerry Hall
      </a>
      , each of whom had romances with Ferry during the time of their contributions, as well as model Kari-Ann Muller who appears on the cover of the first Roxy album but who was not otherwise involved with anyone in the band, and who later married
      <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">
       Mick Jagger
      </a>
      's brother Chris.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The title of the fourth Roxy album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Country_Life_(Roxy_Music_album)" title="Country Life (Roxy Music album)">
        Country Life
       </a>
      </i>
      , was intended as a parody of the well-known British rural magazine of the same name, and the visually punning front cover photo featured two models (two German fans, Constanze Karolisister of
      <a href="/wiki/Can_(band)" title="Can (band)">
       Can
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Karoli" title="Michael Karoli">
       Michael Karoli
      </a>
      and Eveline Grunwald)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      clad only in semi-transparent lingerie standing in a forest. As a result, in many areas of the United States the album was sold in an opaque plastic wrapper because retailers refused to display the cover. Later, an alternative cover, featuring just a picture of the forest, was used.
     </p>
