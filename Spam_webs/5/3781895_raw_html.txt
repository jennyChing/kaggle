     <p>
      <b>
       Snooker
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
         UK
        </a>
       </small>
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n

          <span title="/u/ long 'oo' in 'food'">
           u
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'er' in 'finger'">
           r
          </span>
         </span>
         /

       </span>
      </span>
      ,
      <span class="nowrap">
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
         US
        </a>
       </small>
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'oo' in 'foot'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'er' in 'finger'">
           r
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macmillan_British_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Macmillan_British-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macmillan_American_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Macmillan_American-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Cue_sports" title="Cue sports">
       cue sport
      </a>
      played on a table covered with a green cloth or
      <a href="/wiki/Baize" title="Baize">
       baize
      </a>
      , with
      <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Pockets_2" title="Billiard table">
       pockets
      </a>
      at each of the four corners and in the middle of each of the long side cushions. A full-size table measures 11ft 8
      <span class="frac nowrap">
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </span>
      in  5ft 10in (3569mm x 1778mm), commonly referred to as 12  6ft.
     </p>
     <p>
      The game is played using a
      <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">
       cue
      </a>
      and 22
      <a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball#Snooker" title="Billiard ball">
       snooker balls
      </a>
      : one white cue ball, 15 red balls worth one point each, and six balls of different colours: yellow (2 points), green (3), brown (4), blue (5), pink (6) and black (7).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsrulessnooker_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsrulessnooker-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The red balls are initially placed in a triangular formation, and the other coloured balls on marked positions on the table known as "spots". Players execute shots by striking the cue ball with the cue, causing the cue ball to hit a red or coloured ball. Points are scored by sinking the red and coloured balls (knocking them into the pockets, called "potting") in the correct sequence. A player receives additional points if the opponent commits a foul. A player (or team) wins a frame (individual game) of snooker by scoring more points than the opponent(s). A player wins a match when a predetermined number of frames have been won.
     </p>
     <p>
      Snooker, generally regarded as having been invented in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      officers, is popular in many of the English-speaking and
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth countries
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commonwealth_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-commonwealth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with top professional players attaining multi-million-
      <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">
       pound
      </a>
      career earnings from the game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earnings_and_era_dominance_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-earnings_and_era_dominance-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The sport is now increasingly popular in
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeebDing2_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BeebDing2-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Touring professional players compete regularly around the world, the premier tournament being the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">
       World Championship
      </a>
      , held annually in
      <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">
       Sheffield
      </a>
      , England.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_snooker" title="History of snooker">
       History of snooker
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Studenten_Billard.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1799" data-file-width="2590" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Studenten_Billard.JPG/220px-Studenten_Billard.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Studenten_Billard.JPG/330px-Studenten_Billard.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Studenten_Billard.JPG/440px-Studenten_Billard.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Studenten_Billard.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Illustration of a game of three ball
        <a href="/wiki/Pool_(cue_sports)" title="Pool (cue sports)">
         pocket billiards
        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbingen" title="Tbingen">
         Tbingen
        </a>
        , Germany
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Snooker in its modern form originated in the latter half of the 19th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-origins-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Cue_sports" title="Cue sports">
       Billiards
      </a>
      had been a popular activity amongst British Army officers stationed in India, and variations on the more traditional billiard games were devised. One variation, devised in the officers' mess in
      <a href="/wiki/Jabalpur" title="Jabalpur">
       Jabalpur
      </a>
      during 1874 or 1875,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-origins-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was to add coloured balls in addition to the reds and black which were used for
      <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_pool" title="Pyramid pool">
       pyramid pool
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Life_pool" title="Life pool">
       life pool
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_of_billiards_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-book_of_billiards-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The rules were formally finalised in 1884 by Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain" title="Neville Francis Fitzgerald Chamberlain">
       Neville Chamberlain
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Ooty" title="Ooty">
       Ooty
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="No source cited yet (November 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The word
      <i>
       snooker
      </i>
      also has military origins, being a
      <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">
       slang
      </a>
      term for first-year cadets or inexperienced personnel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-origins-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One version of events states that Colonel
      <a href="/wiki/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain" title="Neville Francis Fitzgerald Chamberlain">
       Sir Neville Chamberlain
      </a>
      of the Devonshire regiment was playing this new game when his opponent failed to pot a ball and Chamberlain called him a
      <i>
       snooker
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_of_billiards_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-book_of_billiards-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It thus became attached to the billiards game now bearing its name as inexperienced players were labelled as snookers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titan_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-titan-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Snooker grew in popularity in England but generally it was still a game for the gentry, and many well established gentleman clubs which had a billiards table would not allow nonmembers inside to play. To accommodate the popularity of the game, smaller and more open snooker-specific clubs started to be formed.
     </p>
     <p>
      The game of snooker grew in the later half of the 19th century and the early 20th century, and by
      <a href="/wiki/1927_World_Snooker_Championship" title="1927 World Snooker Championship">
       1927
      </a>
      the first
      <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">
       World Snooker Championship
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-origins-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      had been organised by
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis" title="Joe Davis">
       Joe Davis
      </a>
      who, as a professional
      <a href="/wiki/English_billiards" title="English billiards">
       English billiards
      </a>
      and snooker player, moved the game from a
      <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">
       pastime
      </a>
      activity into a more professional sphere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CnVTimeline_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CnVTimeline-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Joe Davis won every world championship until 1946 when he retired. The game went into a decline through the 1950s and 1960s with little interest generated outside of those who played. In 1959, Davis introduced a variation of the game, known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snooker_plus" title="Snooker plus">
       snooker plus
      </a>
      , to try to improve the game's popularity by adding two extra colours. However, it never caught on.
     </p>
     <p>
      A major advance occurred in 1969, when
      <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">
       David Attenborough
      </a>
      commissioned the snooker tournament
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pot_Black" title="Pot Black">
        Pot Black
       </a>
      </i>
      to demonstrate the potential of
      <a href="/wiki/Color_television#Europe" title="Color television">
       colour television
      </a>
      , with the green table and multi-coloured balls being ideal for showing off the advantages of colour broadcasting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cue_China_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cue_China-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The TV series became a ratings success and was for a time the second most popular show on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">
       BBC Two
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FCS-PB_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FCS-PB-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Interest in the game increased and the
      <a href="/wiki/1978_World_Snooker_Championship" title="1978 World Snooker Championship">
       1978 World Championship
      </a>
      was the first to be fully televised.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commonwealth_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-commonwealth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OTT-40_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OTT-40-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The game quickly became a mainstream game
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianThatch_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GuardianThatch-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the UK, Ireland and much of the
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth
      </a>
      and has enjoyed much success in the last 30 years, with most of the ranking tournaments being televised. In 1985 a total of 18.5 million viewers watched the
      <a href="/wiki/1985_World_Snooker_Championship_final" title="1985 World Snooker Championship final">
       concluding frame
      </a>
      of the world championship final between
      <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Taylor" title="Dennis Taylor">
       Dennis Taylor
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Davis" title="Steve Davis">
       Steve Davis
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The loss of
      <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_advertising" title="Tobacco advertising">
       tobacco sponsorship
      </a>
      during the 2000s led to a decrease in the number of professional tournaments, although some new sponsors were sourced;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianSponsor_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GuardianSponsor-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the popularity of the game in the
      <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">
       Far East
      </a>
      and China, with emerging talents such as
      <a href="/wiki/Liang_Wenbo" title="Liang Wenbo">
       Liang Wenbo
      </a>
      and more established players such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ding_Junhui" title="Ding Junhui">
       Ding Junhui
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marco_Fu" title="Marco Fu">
       Marco Fu
      </a>
      , boosted the sport in that part of the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeebDing2_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BeebDing2-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeebDing1_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BeebDing1-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2010, promoter
      <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hearn" title="Barry Hearn">
       Barry Hearn
      </a>
      gained a controlling interest in World Snooker Ltd, the professional sport's commercial arm, pledging to revitalise the "moribund" professional game. Under his direction, the number of professional tournaments has increased, certain tournament formats have been changed in an attempt to increase their appeal, and, as of 2013, total prize money had more than doubled from 3m to more than 7m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_game">
       The game
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The game">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_snooker" title="Rules of snooker">
       Rules of snooker
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="758" height="418" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg/220px-Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg/330px-Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg/440px-Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Snooker_tables" title="Billiard table">
         Snooker table
        </a>
        with balls placed in their starting positions. At the start of the game, the cue ball (white) may be placed anywhere in the semicircle, known as the "D".
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:To_pot_the_red.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1088" data-file-width="1850" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/To_pot_the_red.jpg/220px-To_pot_the_red.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/To_pot_the_red.jpg/330px-To_pot_the_red.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/To_pot_the_red.jpg/440px-To_pot_the_red.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:To_pot_the_red.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Game in progress on a half-size table. A red ball about to be potted.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The object of the game is to score more
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#point" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Point">
         points
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      than one's opponent by potting
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#object_ball" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Object ball">
         object balls
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      in the correct order. At the start of a frame, the balls are positioned as shown, and the players then take turns to hit shots by striking the cue ball with the
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#cue_tip" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Cue tip">
         tip
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">
       cue
      </a>
      , their aim being to pot one of the red balls into a pocket and thereby score a point, or, if this is not possible, to at least hit a red ball so as to avoid making a foul shot. After the striker pots a red ball, he or she must then pot one of the six "colours" (in snooker, the term
      <i>
       colour
      </i>
      is understood to exclude the red balls). If the player successfully pots a colour, the value of that ball is added to the player's score, and the ball is returned to its correct position on the table. After that, the player must pot another red ball, then another colour, and so on. This process continues until the striker fails to pot the desired ball, at which point the opponent comes back to the table to play the next shot.
     </p>
     <p>
      The game continues in this manner until all the reds are potted and only the six colours are left on the table. At this point the colours must be potted in the order from least to most valuable ball that is, yellow first (2 points), then green (3 points), brown (4 points), blue (5 points), pink (6 points) and finally black (7 points), the balls not being returned to play. When the final ball is potted, the player with more points wins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsrulessnooker_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsrulessnooker-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If the scores are equal when all the balls have been potted, the black is placed back on its spot as a
      <a href="/wiki/Tiebreaker" title="Tiebreaker">
       tiebreaker
      </a>
      . A player may also concede a frame while on strike if he or she thinks there are not enough points available on the table to beat the opponent's score. In professional snooker this is a common occurrence.
     </p>
     <p>
      Points may also be scored in a game when a player's opponent
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#foul" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Foul">
         fouls
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      . A foul can occur for various reasons, most commonly for failing to hit the correct ball (e.g. hitting a colour first when the player was attempting to hit a red), or for sending the cue ball into a pocket. The former may occur when the player fails to escape from "
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#snooker" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Snooker">
         a snooker
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      " a situation in which the previous player leaves the cue ball positioned such that no legal ball can be struck directly without obstruction by an illegal ball. Points gained from a foul vary from a minimum of 4 to a maximum of 7 if the black ball is involved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsrulessnooker_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsrulessnooker-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The total number of consecutive points (excluding fouls) that a player amasses during one
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#visit" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Visit">
         visit
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      to the table is known as a "
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#break" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Break">
         break
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      ". A player attaining a break of 15, for example, could have reached it by potting a red then a black, then a red then a pink, before failing to pot the next red. The traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Maximum_break" title="Maximum break">
       maximum break
      </a>
      in snooker is achieved by potting all reds with blacks then all colours, yielding 147 points; this is often known as a "147" or a "maximum".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC147_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC147-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The highest possible break is a
      <a href="/wiki/Maximum_break#Highest_break_following_a_foul" title="Maximum break">
       155 break
      </a>
      , also known as a
      <i>
       super maximum
      </i>
      . This is achieved via the opponent leaving a
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#free_ball" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: free ball">
         free ball
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      , with the black being potted as the additional colour, and then potting 15 reds and blacks with the colours.
      <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Cope" title="Jamie Cope">
       Jamie Cope
      </a>
      has the distinction of being the first player in snooker history to post a verified 155 break, achieved in a practice frame in 2005.
     </p>
     <p>
      One
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#game" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Game">
         game
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      , from the balls in their starting position until the last ball is potted, is called a
      <i>
       <dfn id="">
        <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#frame" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
         <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Frame">
          frame
         </span>
        </a>
       </dfn>
      </i>
      . A
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#match" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Match">
         match
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      generally consists of a predetermined number of frames and the player who wins the most frames wins the match. Most professional matches require a player to win five frames, and are called "Best of Nine" as that is the maximum possible number of frames. Tournament finals are usually best of 17 or best of 19, while the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">
       World Championship
      </a>
      uses longer matches ranging from best of 19 in the qualifiers and the first round proper, up to 35 frames in length (first to 18), and is played over two days, extended if necessary until a winner is determined.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCWSN1_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCWSN1-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Professional and competitive amateur matches are officiated by a referee who is the sole judge of fair play. The referee also replaces the colours on the table when necessary and calls out how many points the player has scored during a break. Professional players usually play the game in a sporting manner, declaring fouls the referee has missed, acknowledging good shots from their opponent, or holding up a hand to apologise for fortunate shots, also known as "flukes".
     </p>

     <p>
      <a href="#Equipment">
       Accessories
      </a>
      used for snooker include
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#chalk" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Chalk">
         chalk
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      for the tip of the cue,
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#rest" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Rest">
         rests
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      of various sorts (needed often, due to the length of a full-size table), a
      <a href="/wiki/Rack_(billiards)" title="Rack (billiards)">
       triangle
      </a>
      to
      <dfn id="">
       <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#rack" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
        <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Rack">
         rack
        </span>
       </a>
      </dfn>
      the reds, and a scoreboard. One drawback of snooker on a full-size table is the size of the room (22 by 16 feet (6.7m 4.9m)), which is the minimum required for comfortable cueing room on all sides.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roomsize_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-roomsize-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This limits the number of locations in which the game can easily be played. While
      <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Pool_.28pocket_billiards.29_tables" title="Billiard table">
       pool tables
      </a>
      are common to many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">
       pubs
      </a>
      , snooker tends to be played either in private surroundings or in public
      <a href="/wiki/Billiard_hall" title="Billiard hall">
       snooker halls
      </a>
      . The game can also be played on smaller tables using fewer red balls. The variants in table size are: 10ft  5ft, 9ft  4.5ft, 8ft  4ft, 6ft  3ft (the smallest for realistic play) and 4ft  2ft. Smaller tables can come in a variety of styles, such as fold away or dining-table convertible.

     <p>
      Professional snooker players can play on the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Billiards_and_Snooker_Association" title="World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association">
       World Snooker
      </a>
      main tour ranking circuit.
      <a href="/wiki/Snooker_world_rankings" title="Snooker world rankings">
       Ranking points
      </a>
      , earned by players through their performances over the previous two seasons, determine the current world ranking.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSrankingchart_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSrankingchart-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A player's ranking determines what level of qualification he or she requires for ranking tournaments. The elite of professional snooker is generally regarded as the "Top 16" ranking players,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top16-1_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-top16-1-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who are not required to pre-qualify for three of the tournaments, namely the
      <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Masters_(snooker)" title="Shanghai Masters (snooker)">
       Shanghai Masters
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Goldfields_Open" title="Australian Goldfields Open">
       Australian Open
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">
       World Snooker Championship
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top16-2_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-top16-2-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Players_Tour_Championship" title="Players Tour Championship">
       Players Tour Championship
      </a>
      Order of Merit who are not in the top 64, 12 players from the Q School, and various regional, junior and amateur champions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">

      <a href="/wiki/Crucible_Theatre" title="Crucible Theatre">
       Crucible Theatre
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">

      <a href="/wiki/Snooker_world_rankings" title="Snooker world rankings">
       world ranking points
      </a>
      . A high ranking ensures qualification for next year's tournaments, invitations to invitational tournaments and an advantageous draw in tournaments.

      <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">

      <a href="/wiki/UK_Championship_(snooker)" title="UK Championship (snooker)">
       UK Championship
      </a>
      . Third in line are the invitational tournaments, to which most of the highest ranked players are invited. The most important tournament in this category is
      <a href="/wiki/Masters_(snooker)" title="Masters (snooker)">
       The Masters
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masters1_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-masters1-42">

         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which to most players is the second or third most sought-after prize.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dottmasters_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dottmasters-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Champion_of_Champions_(snooker)" title="Champion of Champions (snooker)">

      </a>

     <dl>
      <dt>
       Table
      </dt>
      <dd>
       The playing surface, 11 feet 8.5 inches by 5 ft 10 inches for a standard full-size table, with six pockets, one at each corner and one at the center of each of the longer side cushions. For further information see
       <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table" title="Billiard table">
        Billiard table
       </a>
       , specifically the section
       <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Snooker_and_English_billiards_tables" title="Billiard table">
        Snooker and English billiards tables
       </a>
       .
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Cloth
      </dt>
      <dd>
       The cloth is usually green with a
       <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Cloth" title="Billiard table">
        directional nap
       </a>
       running from the baulk end of the table towards the end with the black ball spot (often called baize, however baize is a much inferior type of cloth sometimes used on pool tables). The nap will affect the direction of the cue ball depending on which direction the cue ball is shot and also on whether left or right side (spin) is placed on the ball. Even if the cue ball is hit in exactly the same way, the nap will cause a different effect depending on whether the ball is hit down table (towards the black ball spot) or up table towards the baulk line. The cloth on a snooker table should not be vacuumed especially if the vacuum head has beater brushes as this can destroy the nap. The best method is to brush the cloth in a straight line from the baulk end to the far end. Multiple brush strokes are fine as long as they are straight in direction (i.e. not across the table). Some table men will also then drag a dampened cloth wrapped around a short piece of board (like a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensional_lumber#Dimensional_lumber" title="Dimensional lumber">
        two by four
       </a>
       ) to collect any remaining fine dust and help lay the nap down. Strachan cloth as used in official snooker tournaments is made up of 100% wool. Some other cloths include a small percentage of nylon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         47
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cues_Cues_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cues_Cues-48">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         48
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Balls
      </dt>
      <dd>
       22 balls (15 red, six colour balls and a white cue ball), 52.5 mm or 2
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        
        <sub>
         16
        </sub>
       </span>
       inches in diameter. For further information see
       <a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball" title="Billiard ball">
        Billiard ball
       </a>
       , particularly the section
       <a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball#Snooker" title="Billiard ball">
        Snooker
       </a>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">
        Cue
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A stick, made of wood or fibreglass, tapering to a tip, usually ending in leather, which is used to strike the cue-ball.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       <a href="/wiki/Cue_sports#Chalk" title="Cue sports">
        Cue-tip chalk
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       The tip of the cue is "chalked" to ensure good contact between the cue and the cue-ball. This "chalk" is generally a silica based compound rather than actual chalk of the type used used on blackboards.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Extension
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A shorter baton that fits over, or screws into, the back end of the cue, effectively lengthening it. Is used for shots where the cue ball is a long distance from the player.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Rest
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A stick with an X-shaped head that is used to support the cue when the cue ball is out of reach at normal extension.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Rest head adaptor
      </dt>
      <dd>
       An attachment that slips onto a conventional rest head to make a spider or just to give a slightly different bridge.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Hook rest
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Identical to the normal rest, yet with a hooked metal end. It is used to set the rest around another ball. The hook rest is the most recent invention in snooker.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Spider
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Similar to the rest but with an arch-shaped head; it is used to elevate and support the tip of the cue above the height of the cue-ball.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Swan (or swan-neck spider or giraffe)
      </dt>
      <dd>
       This equipment, consisting of a rest with a single extended neck and a fork-like prong at the end, is used to give extra cueing distance over a group of balls. If not available, a regular X rest can be placed on a spider so it in turn hangs the required distance beyond to provide similar support.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Triangle/Rack
      </dt>
      <dd>
       The piece of equipment is used for gathering the red balls into the formation required for the break to start a frame.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Extended rest
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Similar to the regular rest, but with a mechanism at the butt end which makes it possible to extend the rest by up to three feet.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Extended spider
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A hybrid of the swan and the spider. Its purpose is to bridge over large packs of reds. Is less common these days in professional snooker but can be used in situations where the position of one or more balls prevents the spider being placed where the striker desires.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Half butt
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Usually housed underneath the side of the table, the half butt is a combination of a table length rest and cue which is rarely used unless the cue ball needs to be struck in such a way that the entire length of the table is the actual obstacle.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Ball marker
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A multi-purpose instrument with a "D" shaped notch, which a referee can (1) place next to a ball, in order to mark the position of it. They can then remove the ball to clean it; (2) use to judge if a ball is preventing a colour from being placed on its spot; (3) use to judge if the cue ball can hit the extreme edge of a "ball on" when awarding a free ball (by placing it alongside the potentially intervening ball).
      </dd>
     </dl>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_players">
       Notable players
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan" title="Ronnie OSullivan">
         Ronnie OSullivan
        </a>
        has won the most world titles in the 21st century (in 2001, 2004, 2008, 2012 and 2013).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_snooker_players_by_number_of_ranking_titles" title="List of snooker players by number of ranking titles">
       List of snooker players by number of ranking titles
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Century_break#List_of_players_with_over_100_century_breaks" title="Century break">
       List of snooker players with over 100 century breaks
      </a>

     <p>
      In the professional era that began with
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis" title="Joe Davis">
       Joe Davis
      </a>
      in the 1930s and continues up until the present day, a relatively small number of players have succeeded at the top level.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-number1_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-number1-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reaching and maintaining a place amongst the snooker elite is a tough task, with the standards of the game being such that it requires many years of dedication and effort as well as natural ability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardwork_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hardwork-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Certain players have tended to dominate the sport through the decades.
      <a href="/wiki/John_Pulman" title="John Pulman">
       John Pulman
      </a>
      is generally regarded as the principal player through the 1960s,
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Reardon" title="Ray Reardon">
       Ray Reardon
      </a>
      through the 1970s,
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Davis" title="Steve Davis">
       Steve Davis
      </a>
      through the 1980s and
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hendry" title="Stephen Hendry">
       Stephen Hendry
      </a>
      through the 1990s. Reardon won 6 (1970, 19731976 and 1978), Davis also 6 (1981, 1983, 1984 and 19871989) and Hendry 7 (1990, 19921996 and 1999)
      <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">
       World Championships
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_O%27Sullivan" title="Ronnie O'Sullivan">
       Ronnie O'Sullivan
      </a>
      dominates in the modern era, having won the title on five occasions during the current period (2001, 2004, 2008, 2012 and 2013).
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Williams_(snooker_player)" title="Mark Williams (snooker player)">
       Mark Williams
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Higgins_(snooker_player)" title="John Higgins (snooker player)">
       John Higgins
      </a>
      won twice (2000, 2003 and 2007, 2009 respectively). Higgins has four world titles in total, having also won in 1998 and 2011, while O'Sullivan has five having added further titles in 2012 and 2013. By retaining his title in 2013, O'Sullivan became the first player to successfully defend the World Championship since 1996 when Hendry won the sixth of his seven titles, his fifth in a row.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Champions_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-World_Champions-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">
       Variants
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variants">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Category:Snooker_variants" title="Category:Snooker variants">
       Snooker variants
      </a>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        American snooker
       </b>
       , a variant dating to 1925, usually played on a 10by5foot table with
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        2
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        
        <sub>
         8
        </sub>
       </span>
       inch balls, and a simpler rule set influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/Pool_(cue_sports)" title="Pool (cue sports)">
        pool
       </a>
       (despite its name, American snooker is not governed or recognised by the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Snooker_Association" title="United States Snooker Association">
        United States Snooker Association
       </a>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Power_Snooker" title="Power Snooker">
         Power Snooker
        </a>
       </b>
       , a variant with only nine reds, in a diamond-shaped pack, instead of 15 in a triangle, and matches limited to 30 minutes.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Sinuca_brasileira" title="Sinuca brasileira">
         Sinuca brasileira
        </a>
       </b>
       , a Brazilian version with only one red ball, and divergent rules.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Six-red_snooker" title="Six-red snooker">
         Six-red snooker
        </a>
       </b>
       , a variant played with only six reds in a triangular pack.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Snooker plus
       </b>
       , a variant with two additional
       <dfn id="">
        <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#colour_ball" title="Glossary of cue sports terms">
         <span class="" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms: Colour ball">
          colour balls
         </span>
        </a>
       </dfn>
       (8pt orange and 10pt purple), allowing a
       <a href="/wiki/Maximum_break" title="Maximum break">
        maximum break
       </a>
       of 210.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SGame_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SGame-52">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         52
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         53
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The variation was created by
       <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis" title="Joe Davis">
        Joe Davis
       </a>
       in 1959, but failed to gain popularity.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Snookball
       </b>
       , a combination of snooker and
       <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">
        association football
       </a>
       played on an oversized snooker table. Regular snooker rules apply except footballs are used rather than snooker balls (which resemble regular snooker balls, including the cue ball) and the cue ball is kicked rather than hit with a cue stick. The game is based on "poolball", a combination of
       <a href="/wiki/Eight-ball" title="Eight-ball">
        eight-ball
       </a>
       and association football invented by Cameroonian footballers
       <a href="/wiki/Aur%C3%A9lien_Chedjou" title="Aurlien Chedjou">
        Aurlien Chedjou
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Eto%27o" title="Samuel Eto'o">
        Samuel Eto'o
       </a>
       , where the footballs resemble regular pool balls.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Snooker_Shoot-Out" title="Snooker Shoot-Out">
         Snooker Shoot-Out
        </a>
       </b>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Ten-red snooker
       </b>
       , a variant played with only ten reds in a triangle.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Volunteer snooker
       </b>
       , a variant from the early 1900s.
      </li>
     </ul>
