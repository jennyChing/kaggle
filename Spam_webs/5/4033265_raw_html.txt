     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Pet Sematary
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1983
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horror_novel" title="Horror novel">
       horror novel
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">
       Stephen King
      </a>
      , nominated for a
      <a href="/wiki/World_Fantasy_Award_for_Best_Novel" title="World Fantasy Award for Best Novel">
       World Fantasy Award for Best Novel
      </a>
      in 1984,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWE-1984_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WWE-1984-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and adapted into a
      <a href="/wiki/Pet_Sematary_(film)" title="Pet Sematary (film)">
       1989 film of the same name
      </a>
      . In November 2013,
      <a href="/wiki/PS_Publishing" title="PS Publishing">
       PS Publishing
      </a>
      released
      <i>
       Pet Sematary
      </i>
      in a limited 30th Anniversary Edition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pet_Sematary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Louis Creed, a
      <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">
       doctor
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">
       Chicago
      </a>
      , is appointed director of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maine" title="University of Maine">
       University of Maine
      </a>
      's campus health service. He moves to a large house near the small town of Ludlow with his wife Rachel, their two young children, Ellie and Gage, and Ellie's cat, Church. From the moment they arrive, the family runs into trouble: Ellie hurts her knee after falling off a swing, and Gage is stung by a bee. Luckily, their new neighbor, an elderly man named Jud Crandall, comes to help. He warns Louis and Rachel about the highway that runs past their house; it is constantly used by big trucks.
     </p>
     <p>
      Jud and Louis quickly become close friends. Since Louis's father died when he was three, he sees Jud as a surrogate father. A few weeks after the Creeds move in, Jud puts the friendship on the line when he takes the family on a walk in the woods behind their home. A well-tended path leads to a
      <a href="/wiki/Pet_cemetery" title="Pet cemetery">
       pet cemetery
      </a>
      (misspelled "sematary") where the children of the town
      <a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">
       bury
      </a>
      their deceased animals. This provokes a heated argument between Louis and Rachel the next day. Rachel disapproves of discussing death, and she worries about how Ellie may be affected by what she saw at the "sematary". (It is explained later that Rachel was
      <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">
       traumatized
      </a>
      by the early death of her sister, Zelda, from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spinal_meningitis" title="Spinal meningitis">
       spinal meningitis
      </a>
      an issue that is brought up several times in
      <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">
       flashbacks
      </a>
      .)
     </p>
     <p>
      Louis himself has a traumatic experience during the first week of classes. Victor Pascow, a student who has been fatally injured in an automobile accident, addresses his dying words to Louis personally, even though the two men are strangers. On the night following Pascow's death, Louis experiences what he believes is a very vivid dream in which he meets Pascow, who leads him to the "sematary" and warns Louis to not "go beyond, no matter how much you feel you need to." Louis wakes up in bed the next morning convinced it was, in fact, a dream  until he finds his feet and the bedsheets covered with dirt and pine needles. Nevertheless, Louis dismisses the dream as the product of the stress he experienced during Pascow's death, coupled with his wife's lingering anxieties about the subject of death.
     </p>
     <p>
      Louis is forced to confront the subject of death at Halloween, when Jud's wife, Norma, suffers a near-fatal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">
       heart attack
      </a>
      . Thanks to Louis's prompt attention, Norma makes a quick recovery. Jud is grateful for Louis's help and decides to repay him after Church is run over outside his home at Thanksgiving. Rachel and the kids are visiting Rachel's parents in Chicago, but Louis frets over breaking the bad news to Ellie. Sympathizing with Louis, Jud takes him to the pet cemetery, supposedly to bury Church. But instead of stopping there, Jud leads Louis farther on a frightening journey to "the real cemetery": an ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">
       burial ground
      </a>
      that was once used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Mi%27kmaq" title="Mi'kmaq">
       Micmac
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_North_America" title="Indigenous peoples of North America">
       Indians
      </a>
      . There Louis buries the cat on Jud's instruction, with Jud saying that animals buried there have come back to life.
     </p>
     <p>
      Not really believing, Louis thinks that the subject is finished  until the next afternoon when the cat returns home. But it is obvious that Church is not the same as before. While he used to be vibrant and lively, he now acts ornery and "a little dead", in Louis's words. Church hunts for mice and birds much more often, but he rips them apart without eating them. The cat also smells so bad that Ellie no longer wants him in her room at night. Jud confirms that this condition is the rule, rather than the exception, for animals who have been resurrected in this fashion.
     </p>
     <p>
      Louis is deeply disturbed by Church's
      <a href="/wiki/Resurrection" title="Resurrection">
       resurrection
      </a>
      and begins to wish that he had never done it. Tragically, two-year-old Gage is run over by a speeding truck several months later, even though Louis very nearly manages to prevent the accident. Overcome with despair, Louis considers bringing his son back to life with the help of the burial ground. Jud, guessing what Louis is planning, attempts to dissuade him by telling him the gruesome story of the last person who was resurrected by the burial ground. Jud concludes that "the place has a power... its own evil purpose," and may have caused Gage's death because Jud introduced Louis to it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite this, and his own reservations about the idea, Louis's
      <a href="/wiki/Grief" title="Grief">
       grief
      </a>
      and guilt spur him to carry out his plan  with horrifying consequences for him and his loved ones. Gage returns from the dead as a monstrous, demonic shadow of his former self and first kills Jud with one of Louis's surgical scalpels, then his mother as well. Louis confronts his son and sends him back to the grave with a lethal injection of chemicals from his medical supply stock. We learn, however, that he still has not learned from his mistakes, for after burning the Crandall house down, he returns to the burial ground with his wife's corpse. That very night, Louis is playing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solitaire" title="Solitaire">
       solitaire
      </a>
      when he feels a cold hand fall upon his shoulder and hears the voice of Rachel cooing, "Darling..."
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_inspiration">
       Origin and inspiration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pet_Sematary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin and inspiration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1978, King returned to his
      <a href="/wiki/Alma_mater" title="Alma mater">
       alma mater
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Maine_at_Orono" title="University of Maine at Orono">
       University of Maine at Orono
      </a>
      , to teach for a year as a gesture of gratitude for the education he had received there. During this time his family rented a house on a busy road in Orrington. The road claimed the lives of a number of pets, and the neighborhood children had created a pet cemetery in a field near the Kings' home. King's daughter Naomi buried her cat "Smucky" there after it was hit, and shortly thereafter their son Owen had a close call running toward the road. King wrote the novel based on their experiences, but feeling he had gone too far with the subject matter of the book, he discarded the idea of having it published. However, needing a final book for his contract King reluctantly submitted it to
      <a href="/wiki/Doubleday_(publisher)" title="Doubleday (publisher)">
       Doubleday
      </a>
      on the advice of his wife Tabitha
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and friend
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Straub" title="Peter Straub">
       Peter Straub
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      <i>
       Pet Sematary
      </i>
      was made into a
      <a href="/wiki/Pet_Sematary_(film)" title="Pet Sematary (film)">
       film
      </a>
      in the autumn of 1988 (released April 1989) and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lambert" title="Mary Lambert">
       Mary Lambert
      </a>
      , starring
      <a href="/wiki/Dale_Midkiff" title="Dale Midkiff">
       Dale Midkiff
      </a>
      as Louis,
      <a href="/wiki/Fred_Gwynne" title="Fred Gwynne">
       Fred Gwynne
      </a>
      as Jud,
      <a href="/wiki/Denise_Crosby" title="Denise Crosby">
       Denise Crosby
      </a>
      as Rachel and
      <a href="/wiki/Miko_Hughes" title="Miko Hughes">
       Miko Hughes
      </a>
      as Gage. A man,
      <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Hubatsek" title="Andrew Hubatsek">
       Andrew Hubatsek
      </a>
      , was chosen for Zelda's role because the filmmakers could not find a woman bony enough to portray the terminally ill girl.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There was also a sequel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pet_Sematary_Two" title="Pet Sematary Two">
        Pet Sematary Two
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1997
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">
       BBC Radio 4
      </a>
      broadcast a dramatisation of the story in six half-hour episodes. It was adapted by
      <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Evans_(dramatist)" title="Gregory Evans (dramatist)">
       Gregory Evans
      </a>
      and starred
      <a href="/wiki/John_Sharian" title="John Sharian">
       John Sharian
      </a>
      , Briony Glassco and
      <a href="/wiki/Lee_Montague" title="Lee Montague">
       Lee Montague
      </a>
      . The director was Gordon House.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      In March 2010, it was announced that a remake was in the works, with
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Greenberg" title="Matt Greenberg">
       Matt Greenberg
      </a>
      (writer of another King adaptation,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/1408_(film)" title="1408 (film)">
        1408
       </a>
      </i>
      ) currently working on the screenplay.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 21 September 2010,
      <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_del_Toro" title="Guillermo del Toro">
       Guillermo del Toro
      </a>
      announced that he would like to direct the
      <i>
       Pet Sematary
      </i>
      remake, along with a new adaptation of another Stephen King novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/It_(novel)" title="It (novel)">
        It
       </a>
      </i>
      , but stated that his busy schedule made it unlikely that he would be able to work on either film any time soon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On October 31, 2013,
      <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_di_Bonaventura" title="Lorenzo di Bonaventura">
       Lorenzo di Bonaventura
      </a>
      and Steven Schneider are producing the remake and both di Binaventura and the studio are in talks with
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_Fresnadillo" title="Juan Carlos Fresnadillo">
       Juan Carlos Fresnadillo
      </a>
      in talks to direct the remake.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
