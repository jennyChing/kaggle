        The Art of App Store Icons
       </h1>

         <a id="dd_start">
         </a>
         <h4>
          Your icon is the face of your app. All your hard work and all your GitHub hours boil down to a little 1024 x 1024 square. Creating a great icon is pretty well recognized as one of the best things you can do for app store optimization (ASO). Translation? Your icon is the last thing between developers and above-the-poverty-line riches. So were going to go through some best practices that you can apply to make your icon design (metaphorically) shine.
         </h4>
         <h1>
          Why its important
         </h1>
         <p>
          <img alt="this is very important bench" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4094" height="960" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/290711738_2ae51d677c_o.jpg" width="1280"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Image via
           <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/valeriebb/290711738/" target="_blank">
            Flickr
           </a>
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Before we get into what Apple says about icons (not much) and what other people say about icons (lots and lots), why is icon design so important? Its fair question.
         </p>
         <p>
          First, a picture tells a thousand words, and if a user scrolling through dozens or hundreds of apps at a time, then your icon needs to stand out. That much is pretty obvious.
          <a href="https://www.mobiledevhq.com/howtogetnoticedinappstoresearch-200/articles" target="_blank">
           This challenge has become even more pertinent since Apple released their iOS 8 update back in 2014
          </a>
          . The change to continuous scrolling means that users can move through apps an awful lot quicker than they used to.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The result? Less time per app, so you really need to nail your pictures and screenshots.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <h2>
          What Apples got to say
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class=" alignleft" height="220" src="http://icons.iconarchive.com/icons/igh0zt/ios7-style-metro-ui/512/MetroUI-Apps-Mac-App-Store-icon.png" width="220"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          So whats Apple got to say on the subject? A quick look at the iOS Dev Library and theyve
          <a href="https://developer.apple.com/library/ios/documentation/UserExperience/Conceptual/MobileHIG/AppIcons.html#//apple_ref/doc/uid/TP40006556-CH19-SW1" target="_blank">
           got a whole section on app icons
          </a>
          . Most of it is about making the icon look good once its on the device, rather than in the App Store, but they do offer some very high level advice like:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Create an abstract interpretation of your apps main idea
          </li>
          <li>
           If you want to portray real substances, do it accurately
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          So Apples not exactly doling out actionable advice, but its a good place to start  essentially, dont use a picture, and make your icon tangibly related to what you want users to see your app as.
         </p>
         <p>
          For example,
          <a href="http://desk.pm/" target="_blank">
           Desk
          </a>
          (
          <a href="http://blog.placeit.net/10-apps-made-money-sales-tactics-learn/" target="_blank">
           which I talked about last week
          </a>
          ) has a great icon. Its a tiny typewriter, and without knowing anything about the product, Id say most people would guess it has something to do with typing or word processing. See? Tangibly related.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           At the end of the day, Apples bit tight lipped on what makes good icon.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <h1>
          App icon best practices (with examples!)
         </h1>
         <p>
          So what should you be doing to help convey all of your apps awesomeness to the outside world?
         </p>
         <h2>
          General design
         </h2>
         <p>
          While there are lots of tricks to really stand out, like adding a free tag or using a main character (more on those later) there are also some basic design guidelines that you should follow. Naturally, these can all be broken with superb results, but for the most part:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Consider having a border, so once your app is downloaded itll look nice no matter what the background is
          </li>
          <li>
           Dont make your logo transparent. Itll reduce legibility
          </li>
          <li>
           Dont use words  theyll jut get lost at smaller sizes
          </li>
          <li>
           Dont make your icon glossy  itll make a lot of it invisible
          </li>
          <li>
           <strong>
            Keep it simple  app icons are small and details get lost
           </strong>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <h2>
          Stand out
         </h2>
         <p>
          Easier said than done, I know. Regardless, it remains one of the core functions of your icon  to differentiate yourself and get the user to tap your app over all the others.
         </p>
         <p>
          Think about it this way. There are 1.2 million(ish) apps in the app store  its unlikely that youre going to be the only one offering your product. So how do people know that you have the best calculator app compared to others?
          <strong>
           Your icon, thats how.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          For example, heres a screenshot of the calculator apps search results for iPhone.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="calculator apps" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4089" height="618" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/calculator.png" width="1049"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          You know what stands out to me? The free tag on the calculator app.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="calculator screenshot edited" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4088" height="245" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/calculator-Copy.png" width="1049"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Its more than a little ridiculous, since the vast majority of apps in the app store are free, but still  by putting that little tag on it, it automatically makes me think that if theyre tagged as free,
          <strong>
           then the rest must not be
          </strong>
          . And that split-second different is really all you the edge you need in the app store.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Change it up
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Splashy Fish" class="alignleft wp-image-4093 " height="151" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/splashyfish-e1427384886960.jpg" width="150"/>
          Not too much, but changing your app icon just a little bit is a great way to reinvigorate downloads. Seasonal icons are the best example of this.
         </p>
         <p>
          Last Halloween, for example,
          <a href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/app/splashy-fish-adventure-flappy/id811035387?mt=8" target="_blank">
           Splashy Fish
          </a>
          changed their icon to be more Halloween-y. Its a small change but if youre the owner of a well-known app, then it might be just the thing to boost your downloads.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Branding
         </h2>
         <p>
          If you have a strong brand value then simple branding is a great way to go. But remember: keep it really simple. Nike, for example, goes a really good job of this  this is what happens when you search them:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Nike Apps" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4092" height="240" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/nike.png" width="1043"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          It makes it pretty much instantly recognizable which apps are theirs, so if Im looking for something by Nike, then its nice and fast. Also, since theyre an
          <a href="http://www.rankingthebrands.com/The-Brand-Rankings.aspx?rankingID=6" target="_blank">
           incredibly well-trusted brand
          </a>
          branding for them makes a lot of sense.
         </p>
         <p>
          Another point about branding is that if thats the path youre going to go down (hopefully) in the future, pick something that can be adapted to lots of different settings. The Nike swoosh, for example, neatly fits into lots of different app icons.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="clash of clans icon" class=" wp-image-4090 alignleft" height="99" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/clash-of-clans.png" width="99"/>
          The Clash of Clans icon, for contrast, isnt nearly as flexible, and
          <a href="https://blog.sensortower.com/blog/2013/07/11/how-to-make-a-great-first-impression-with-an-amazing-app-icon/" target="_blank">
           while there might be an exception for game icons that feature main characters
          </a>
          , Id say err on the side of caution when it comes to highly stylized character icons.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Brand piggy-backing
         </h2>
         <p>
          This is a fun way to get better recognition with your audience.
          <strong>
           (NB: I am not advocating that you break the law, mislead your users, so do this with caution).
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          So were clear on what I mean, lets look at an example.
          <a href="https://itunes.apple.com/ca/app/hipstamatic/id342115564?mt=8" target="_blank">
           Hipstamatic
          </a>
          is a digital photo app that increases the functionality of your camera phone exponentially. Basically, its a really nice version of Instagram. Knowing that, icon designer
          <a href="https://dribbble.com/ak47" target="_blank">
           Aravind Kaimal
          </a>
          came up with this:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="hipstamatic icon" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4091" height="300" src="http://blog.placeit.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/hipstamatic_icon.png" width="400"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Image via
           <a href="https://dribbble.com/shots/568632-Hipstamatic-Icon?list=users&amp;offset=19" target="_blank">
            Dribbble
           </a>
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Its pretty clear where his insta-ration came from, if you know what I mean. But thats not a bad thing! Instagram has built this amazing brand and their iconography is synonymous with filtered pictures 
          <em>
           which is exactly what Hipstamatic does
          </em>
          . So why not capitalize on other brands recognition?
         </p>
         <p>
          If brand piggy-backing is done well, as if similar enough to get recognition and forge the link, but different enough there are not accidental oh I thought this was this clicks, then its a really excellent strategy.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Google Android
         </h2>
         <p>
          Lastly, I wanted to give a nod towards the Play store, who is so often neglected in app-chat, and I think unfairly so.
         </p>
         <p>
          <del>
           Google actually has an
          </del>
          Roman Nurik made a nifty
          <a href="http://romannurik.github.io/AndroidAssetStudio/icons-launcher.html#foreground.space.trim=1&amp;foreground.space.pad=0&amp;foreColor=607d8b%2C0&amp;crop=0&amp;backgroundShape=square&amp;backColor=ffffff%2C100&amp;effects=none" target="_blank">
           icon generator
          </a>
          , to presumably help you make your icon better and more focused for the Google Play Store. It seems to me that while its not that useful a tool for app creation, its be a great tool to test and iterate your app icon really fast (especially if you dont have a designer on staff/design skills). So thats one thing.
         </p>
         <p>
          Second, most of what applies to the App Store will apply to the Play Store as well.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           Were not really dealing in different software, were dealing with the psychology of what makes people see something and think yes,
           <em>
            that
           </em>
           is the app for me.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          In a hyper-saturated market, that is absolutely golden. (that said there are of course some technical differences  theres a nice breakdown
          <a href="http://forumone.com/insights/how-publish-your-mobile-app-google-play-and-apple-app-stores/#store_graphics" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          .)
         </p>
         <h1>
          Wrap up
         </h1>
         <p>
          Your App Store/Play Store icon is rally important. Its a tiny window of opportunity you have to snag the attention of App Store browsers and convince them that your screenshots, your promo video, your description and eventually downloading your app are all worth it to them. Unfortunately, theres not willing formula for what makes a good or a bad App Store icon. Its more about what is going to separate you enough to stand out, but still be recognizable enough that your audience knows instantaneously what your product does.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats a lot do do, and not a lot of space to do it in, so youd best get cracking.
         </p>
