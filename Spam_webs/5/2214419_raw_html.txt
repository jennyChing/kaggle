   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Military payment certificate
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1_Dollar_Series_692_MPC.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="435" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/1_Dollar_Series_692_MPC.jpg/220px-1_Dollar_Series_692_MPC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/1_Dollar_Series_692_MPC.jpg/330px-1_Dollar_Series_692_MPC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/1_Dollar_Series_692_MPC.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Military payment certificates
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       MPC
      </b>
      , was a form of currency used to pay U.S. military personnel in certain foreign countries. It was used in one area or another from a few months after the end of
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      until a few months after the end of U.S. participation in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
       from 1946 until 1973. MPC utilized layers of line
      <a href="/wiki/Lithography" title="Lithography">
       lithography
      </a>
      to create colorful
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banknotes" title="Banknotes">
       banknotes
      </a>
      that could be produced cheaply. Fifteen series of MPCs were created. However, only 13 series were issued. The remaining two were largely destroyed, although some examples remain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among the 13 released series a total of 94 notes are recognized.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_MPCs">
       History of MPCs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_payment_certificate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of MPCs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      MPCs evolved from
      <a href="/wiki/Allied_Military_Currency" title="Allied Military Currency">
       Allied Military Currency
      </a>
      as a response to the large amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">
       US Dollars
      </a>
      circulated by American servicemen in post-
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      . The local citizens might not trust local currencies, as the future of their governments was unclear. Preferring a stable currency like U.S. dollars, local civilians often accepted payment in dollars for less than the accepted conversion rates. Dollars became more favorable to hold,
      <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">
       inflating
      </a>
      the local currencies and thwarting plans to stabilize local economies. Contributing to this problem was the fact that troops were being paid in dollars, which they could convert in unlimited amounts to the local currency with merchants at the floating (black market) conversion rate, which was much more favorable to the
      <a href="/wiki/G.I._(military)" title="G.I. (military)">
       GIs
      </a>
      than the government fixed conversion rate. From this conversion rate imbalance, a black market developed where the servicemen could profit from the more favorable exchange rate.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To reduce profiteering from currency
      <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">
       arbitrage
      </a>
      , the U.S. military devised the MPC program. MPCs were paper money denominated in amounts of 5 cents, 10 cents, 25 cents, 50 cents, 1 dollar, 5 dollars, 10 dollars, and starting in 1968 20 dollars. MPCs were fully convertible to U.S. dollars upon leaving a designated MPC zone, and convertible to local currencies when going on leave (but not vice versa). It was illegal for unauthorized personnel to possess MPC, and that policy, in theory, eliminated U.S. dollars from local economies. Although actual
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_currency" title="United States currency">
       greenbacks
      </a>
      were not circulating, many local merchants accepted MPC on par with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_dollars" title="US dollars">
       US dollars
      </a>
      , since they could use them on the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">
       black market
      </a>
      . This was especially evident during the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      when the MPC program was at its zenith. To prevent MPC from being used as a primary currency in the host country and destroying the local currency value and economy, MPC banknote styles were frequently changed to deter black marketers and reduce hoarding, as the old style would become worthless. Many veterans can recount a conversion day or C-Day.
     </p>
     <p>
      C-days in Vietnam were always classified, never pre-announced. On C-day, soldiers would be restricted to base, preventing GIs from helping Vietnamese civiliansespecially local bars, brothels, bar girls and other
      <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">
       black market
      </a>
      peoplefrom converting old MPC to the newer version. Since Vietnamese were not allowed to convert the currency, they frequently lost savings by holding old, worthless MPC. People angry over their MPC loss would sometimes attack the nearest U.S. base the next night in retaliation.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To illustrate the Vietnam War MPC cycle, in mid-1970, a GI could have a friend in the United States mail a $100 bill in standard U.S. currency, take it "downtown" and convert it to $180 MPC, then change the MPC to
      <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnamese_%C4%91%E1%BB%93ng" title="South Vietnamese ng">
       South Vietnamese piastres
      </a>
      at double the legal rate. The soldier could then have a day shopping, bar hopping, or otherwise spending freely, paying in low-cost local currency, and finishing the day with a hefty profit.
     </p>
     <p>
      To continue the black market cycle, that $100 greenback would find its way to high-level Vietnamese government officials, especially the corrupt ones, who could travel out of country, where the U.S. currency could be deposited safely (Bangkok, Taipei, or Hong Kong). Rumors also suggested that this hard currency (US dollars), would find its way to North Vietnamese European exchange accounts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Thirteen series of MPC were issued between 1946 and 1973, with varied designs often compared to
      <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">
       Monopoly
      </a>
      money due to their colors. After the official end of U.S. participation in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      in early 1973, the only place where MPC remained in use was South Korea. In autumn of 1973, a surprise conversion day was held there, retiring MPC and substituting greenbacks. MPC was never again issued, and the concept lay dormant until the late 1990s, when it was revived somewhat in the form of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stored_Value_Card" title="Stored Value Card">
       Stored Value Card
      </a>
      system, used by U.S. armed forces in Iraq.
     </p>
