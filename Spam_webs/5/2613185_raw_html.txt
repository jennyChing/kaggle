          <div class="entry_widget_small entry_widget_right" id="asset-13711765">
           <span class="adv-photo-small">
            <img alt="lane.jpeg" class="lazy" data-original="http://media.cleveland.com/plain_dealer_metro/photo/13711765-small.jpeg" src="https://blog.advance.net/static/common/img/blank.gif" style="display: block;"/>

          <p>
           COLUMBUS, Ohio  Amid his legal appeal, convicted Chardon High School killer T.J. Lane appears to be struggling as he gets used to life in prison.
          </p>
          <p>
           Lane, 19, is serving
           <a href="http://www.cleveland.com/chardon-shooting/index.ssf/2013/03/tj_lane_sentenced_in_chardon_h.html">
            three consecutive life sentences
           </a>
           at the Allen Correctional Institution in Lima for killing three students and wounding three others at the school on Feb. 27, 2012. He pleaded guilty to three counts of aggravated murder, two counts of attempted aggravated murder and one count of felonious assault.
          </p>
          <p>
           The plea came a day before the one-year mark of the shootings. Because he was a juvenile at the time of the shooting, he could not face the death penalty.
          </p>
          <p>
           On Oct. 21, his attorney, Michael Partlow, filed his appeal brief in
           <a href="http://www.11thcourt.co.trumbull.oh.us/">
            the 11th Ohio District Court of Appeals in Warren
           </a>
           He said the appeal focused on two key issues: a mandatory bind-over of juvenile offenders -- a move that would treat them as adults in the legal system -- is unconstitutional, and a juvenile convicted as an adult should not be sentenced to life in prison. Lane was 17 at the time of the shooting.
          </p>
          <p>
           Geauga County prosecutors are expected to file an opposition to the appeal in about a month.
          </p>
          <p>
           The appeal comes as Lane's time in prison is just beginning. And so are his troubles.
          </p>
          <p>
           In July, he was ordered to spend 10 days in segregation on a prison charge of "self-mutilating, including tattoo," or giving himself a tattoo. In August, he refused to carry out a work-related or institutional assignment and was given a verbal reprimand. Last month, he was charged with doing the same thing, losing 10 days of recreation time, said JoEllen Smith, a  spokeswoman for
           <a href="http://www.drc.ohio.gov/">
            the Ohio Department of Rehabilitation and Correction
           </a>
           , said Monday.
          </p>
          <p>
           Smith said he has not been involved in any type of assaults. Lane is listed as a student in prison records and enrolled in an educational program, which Smith said is not a matter of public record.
          </p>
          <p>
           He originally was taken to the Lorain Correctional Institution within days of his sentencing in Geauga County Common Pleas Court in March. He was later transferred to the Corrections Reception Center near Columbus and later to the Lebanon Correctional Institution. He is in protective custody in the Lima prison.
          </p>
          <p>
           He is one of about 430 inmates serving life in prison without parole, according to state prison records. And as one of the youngest, Lane could cost state taxpayers about $1.4 million in costs if he lives to the age of 80, according to estimates based on the current annual cost of $22,836 per inmate.
          </p>
          <p>
           Lane admitted that he killed students Demetrius Hewlin, 16; Russell King Jr. 17; and Daniel Parmertor, 16 on Feb. 27 in the school's cafeteria.  Injured in the shooting were students Joy Rickers, Nick Walczak and Nate Mueller.
          </p>
          <p>
           At his sentencing in March, Lane took off a blue dress shirt and revealed a T-shirt with the word "killer'' on it. Lane wore a similar shirt when he opened fire in the school cafeteria.
          </p>
