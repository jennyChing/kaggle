          <h1 class="pos-title">
           Creating Professional Food Photos without Expensive Gear
          </h1>
          <p class="pos-meta">
           <span class="element element-relateditems first">
            Written by:
            <a href="/blog/featured-photographers/cheryl-woods" title="Cheryl Woods">
             Cheryl Woods
            </a>
            ,
           </span>
           <span class="element element-itemcreated last">
            Published on:  26 JUNE 2014
           </span>
          </p>
          <div class="floatbox">
           <div class="pos-media media-left">
            <div class="element element-image first last">
             <img alt="Creating Professional Food Photos without Expensive Gear" height="444" src="http://savageuniversal.com/cache/com_zoo/images/food photography 3_fe51fb66899d9edb91ac002428048bd1.jpg" width="666"/>
            </div>
           </div>
           <div class="pos-content">
            <div class="element element-textarea first last">
             <div>
              <p>
               The food industry has exploded in the past few years and along with it a new breed of foodies has sprouted. You've seen them at restaurants documenting every bite and morsel or their meal with their smart phones. With a quick stroll through Yelp restaurant reviews you can see that most of the images are poor at best. You can take great looking food images without investing a lot in equipment by learning just a few simple techniques.
              </p>
              <h2>
               Use Natural Lighting
              </h2>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Use a room with lots of windows. If youre in a restaurant, ask to be seated near a large window. Natural light will give you a beautiful, diffuse light with soft shadows and make the color and the texture of the food pop. If its midday, hang a white sheer in front of the window to diffuse the light. If youre at home take multiple shots at various times during the day to see what effects you get.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                <img alt="Creating Professional Food Photos Without Expensive Gear" border="0" class="size-auto" height="340" src="/images/blogs/cherylwoods/food-photography/food photography 2.jpg" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Creating Professional Food Photos Without Expensive Gear" width="650"/>
               </span>
              </p>
              <h2>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Reflectors
               </span>
              </h2>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Reflectors help tremendously with contrast as well as to fill in the shadows. They will help to evenly distribute the light and pick up more of the details in the food, making the overall image more appealing. All you need is an inexpensive piece of white cardboard (or white foam core) and place it on the opposite side of the food facing the window.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h2>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Use a Tripod
               </span>
              </h2>
              <p>
               Tripods are great investments and can be purchased fairly inexpensively. If you are shooting frequently, you will want to get one. And, tripods are a must when it comes to food photography. It allows you to set up the shot and tweak the compositional elements. Additionally, youll be able to use the timer to avoid any shake from pressing the shutter.
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                If you do not have a tripod, set your camera up on a table, prop it up with some books or buy a Gorilla pod that lets you wrap the legs around any type of surface.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h2>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Interesting Surfaces
               </span>
              </h2>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Be sure to factor in the type of surface you shooting on. For elegant meals, youll want an upscale looking black marble and for a homemade, farm to table supper on piece of wood will work perfect to enhance the atmosphere of the image.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h3>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Fabric
               </span>
              </h3>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Keep a cache of various fabric colors and textures on hand to swap out.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h3>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Marble
               </span>
              </h3>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Find a local stone or marble store that caters to the kitchen refurnishing industry. Theyll often have inexpensive cast off pieces you can purchase in a variety of colors and textures.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h3>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Plastic
               </span>
              </h3>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Local plastic manufacturers are also a good source for obtaining low-cast surfaces. If you want to spend a little extra money, have the edges rounded off. Youll be able to choose from hundreds of different colors as well.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h3>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Paper
               </span>
              </h3>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                If you are looking to convey a more bold, retro or exotic tone, consider a
                <a href="/products/seamless-paper">
                 paper backdrop
                </a>
                . You'll have over 65 different colors to choose from, all offering a seamless, clean look.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                <img alt="Creating Professional Food Photos Without Expensive Gear" border="0" class="size-auto" height="340" src="/images/blogs/cherylwoods/food-photography/food photography 1.jpg" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Creating Professional Food Photos Without Expensive Gear" width="650"/>
               </span>
              </p>
              <h2>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Play Around with the Composition
               </span>
              </h2>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                If you really want to get serious about food photography, youll want to learn how to use stand in food. Even over a brief period of a few minutes, some foods can wither or become lifeless  especially if youre using hot lights. Have your final plating of food in the fridge or on a low oven so that its ready to go once you have the composition and lighting nailed down.
               </span>
              </p>
              <h2>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Photoshop
               </span>
              </h2>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                There is absolutely no harm in using Photoshop as a tool to help you adjust your settings after they are taken. White balance, contrast issues, and sharpening are all tricky to master in camera, so if you need to adjust these settings on a bigger scale in post-production, go for it! Be sure to shoot in RAW so that you can get the most detail out of your image.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Food photography can be extremely creative - from the textures and colors of the food to the plating and composition of the photo. Equipment is expensive, and for most it is not necessarily practical. Many photographers (pros or not) have been able to capture images that emphasize the creative nature of the food with out having to spend hundreds of dollars on equipment. These tips will help you to take the best image you can and will look like they where taken by a pro.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
