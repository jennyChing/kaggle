       <h2 class="entry-title">
        10 Self-Survival Tips and Info Bits for Urban Homesteading
       </h2>
       <div class="title-wrapper">
       </div>
      </header>
      <div class="author-single">
       <div class="author-image">
        <img alt="Christina Sarich" class="photo" height="75" src="http://naturalsociety.com/wp-content/uploads/userphoto/24.thumbnail.jpg" width="57"/>

        <div class="printfriendly pf-alignright">
         <a class="noslimstat" href="#" onclick="window.print(); return false;" rel="nofollow">

        <div style="clear:both;">
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="pf-content">
        <p>
         <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
          <img alt="garden" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-43511" height="164" src="http://naturalsociety.com/wp-content/uploads/garden_food_survival_homestead-263x164.jpg" style="margin: 2px 8px 4px 0px;" width="263"/>
          You dont have to live in a rural area to enjoy the fruits of your own labor. Many people in the suburbs and cities are learning
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homesteading">
           how to homestead
          </a>
          (live a lifestyle of self-sufficiency) without ever leaving their cities.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
          If growing 3 tons of organic produce annually on your 4000 square foot lawn sounds good to you, or learning how to plant a bee garden sounds good to you, then read on.Urban homesteadingoffers a more sovereign life, even if you dont live on a ranch or farm.
         </span>
        </p>
        <!-- Quick Adsense WordPress Plugin: http://quicksense.net/ -->
        <div style="float:left;margin:5px 5px 5px 0;">
         <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
         </script>
         <!-- 336x280 Rectangle NS -->
         <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-4569510391698920" data-ad-slot="0555374131" style="display:inline-block;width:336px;height:280px">
         </ins>
         <script>
          (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
         </script>
        </div>
        <p>
         Here are 10 great tips for homesteading, paving way for
         <a href="http://naturalsociety.com/urban-survivalism-radical-self-sufficiency-food-crisis/" target="_blank">
          a more self-sustaining, live-free lifestyle
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong>
           1.
          </strong>
          There are so many sites providing excellenttips for people who want to grow their own food, learn how to can peaches for the first time, save the Monarch butterflies by planting herbs and flowers they love, or plant their own miniature fruit trees.
          <a href="http://www.motherearthnews.com/">
           Mother Earth News
          </a>
          , for example, promotes organic gardening and homestead living for people of all walks of life. You can learn how to grow pole beans in coffee cans, churn your own butter or even
          <b>
           turn a patch of dry dirt by your mailbox into a
           <a href="http://organicgardening.about.com/od/vegetablesherbs/a/growmicrogreens.htm" target="_blank">
            microgreens garden
           </a>
           ,
          </b>
          out of which you harvest your dinner salad as you fetch your electric bill from the box. I love this site. Its an absolute library on homestead living.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           2.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           You can
          </span>
          start a garden in your kitchen
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           .
           <a href="http://kgi.org/" target="_blank">
            This site
           </a>
           tells you how to start seedlings, transplant them, put them in pots or a small garden, and more.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           3.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           If you are going to grow your own food, you need to know how to save it. After all, a bumper crop of apples, or a whole lot of onions cant all be eaten at once. This is why people learned to
          </span>
          <b style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           can, freeze-dry, and dehydrate foods so they could be eaten over longer periods of time
          </b>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           . Pickyourown.org tells you all about ways to save your food  and lower your grocery bills tremendously. Who wouldnt want some tasty canned peach pie or strawberry-rhubarb jam, organic and GMO-free, made in your own kitchen? Delicious. Its easier to do these things than you think, and there areseveral different techniques you can try.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Read:
          <a href="http://naturalsociety.com/5-plants-grow-organic-store-bought-produce/" target="_blank">
           5 Foods You can Grow from Organic, Store-Bought Produce
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           4.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           UrbanHomestead.org and thelazyhomesteader.comshow people how a
          </span>
          <a href="http://urbanhomestead.org/" style="line-height: 1.5em;" target="_blank">
           working micro-farm in the middle of a busy city
          </a>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           works, literally a mile from the freeway, or how to
          </span>
          <b style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           build water catchment systems that are still within city codes
          </b>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           .
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           5.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           Good homesteaders have gotten so good at urban homestead living, that
          </span>
          <b style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           they sell theirsurplus produceto local farmers markets or give it to food pantries
          </b>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           .
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           6.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           Seasoned urban homesteaders teach you
          </span>
          <b style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           how to compost kitchen scraps
          </b>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           .Backyard food compostingis as easy as getting some good bins with some composting worms, or burying your scraps in a pre-dug hole.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           7.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           Would you like to
          </span>
          <b style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           be a beekeeper in the middle of a growing suburb
          </b>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           ? No problem. There are even homesteaders who bee-keep in the
           <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/home/urban-beekeeping-47093003#slide-1" target="_blank">
            middle of Brooklyn on rooftops
           </a>
           . These satellite bee-keeping individuals help plants get pollinated.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           8.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           Homesteaders can also teach you
          </span>
          about
          <a href="http://lazyhomesteader.com/2013/08/22/in-the-shares-this-week/" target="_blank">
           joining an organic food co-op
          </a>
          to get more good food for less
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           . Its a great way to start to dip your toes into theself-sovereign
          </span>
          <a href="http://eartheasy.com/blog/2011/10/free-land-are-you-ready-to-try-homesteading/" style="line-height: 1.5em;" target="_blank">
           movement that is sweeping the US
          </a>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           .
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           9.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           There are
          </span>
          <a href="http://extension.oregonstate.edu/sorec/sites/default/files/Dan__Medford_soil.pdf" target="_blank">
           organic soil amendments
          </a>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           that can help fix old, tired, pesticide-laden lawns and help prepare urban and suburban land for growing. This site tells you more than youd ever need to know.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           10.
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           If you really have no space to start a growing project, you can also find
          </span>
          <a class="broken_link" href="http://www.organicgardening-101.com/urban-gardening.html" target="_blank">
           community gardens
          </a>
          <b style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           .
          </b>
          <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
           Community gardens encourage an urban communitys food security, allowing people to grow their own food. They bring urban gardeners closer in touch with the source of their food, and break down social isolation by encouraging community interaction.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
