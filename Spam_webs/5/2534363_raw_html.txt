     <div class="entry-hdr">
      <h1 class="entry-title">
       Facing Reality
      </h1>
     </div>
     <div class="entry-meta">
      <div class="meta-info-wrap">
       <span class="sep posted-on">
        Posted on
       </span>
       <a href="http://mydishwasherspossessed.com/2014/01/facing-reality/" rel="bookmark" title="8:27 pm">
        <time class="entry-date" datetime="2014-01-19T20:27:00+00:00">
         January 19, 2014
        </time>
       </a>
       <span class="by-author">
        <span class="sep">
         by
        </span>
        <span class="author vcard">
         <a class="url fn n" href="http://mydishwasherspossessed.com/author/kathyradigan/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Kathy Radigan">
          Kathy Radigan
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="updated">
        January 6, 2015
       </span>
      </div>
      <!-- .entry-meta-icons -->
     </div>
     <!-- .entry-meta -->
    </header>
    <!-- .entry-header -->
    <div class="entry-content cf">
     <div>
     </div>
     <div dir="ltr">
      <p dir="ltr">
       <a href="http://mydishwasherspossessed.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Facing-Reality-.jpg" rel="attachment wp-att-1123">
        <img alt="Facing Reality" class="aligncenter wp-image-1123" height="720" src="http://mydishwasherspossessed.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Facing-Reality-.jpg" width="481"/>
       </a>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       The other day I was in the middle of my morning routine of packing snacks, reminding people to wear underwear, and ordering them to brush their teeth, when I happened to see it, my special needs daughters red take home folder.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Inside the folder, along with the words she worked on that day and drawings of the weather for her calendar, was the all-too-familiar Parent Intake Form along with a note:
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Kathy, Im sending home another form for you to fill out. We know this can be difficult, but since Lizzy is now 12, we are required by law to collect this information. If you have any questions or need help please contact me or the school psychologist. Thank you.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I felt guilty that I put it off. Ive always done my best to face my childrens issues head on, and I pride myself on being acknowledged as a parent who is always on my game.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Truth be told, I hate these forms.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Ive never been great at defining my children in a sentence or two or stating the goals I wanted them to reach. What I really wanted was to have kids who didnt have to work so hard to reach milestones that others did with ease.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I looked down at the form and started to read the questions asking me to list my daughters strengths and weaknesses and my hopes for her future.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       My mind went back five years ago, when I had to fill out another form.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       This time it was for my then three-year-old son. His learning issues are much less severe than Lizzys, but he was attending the same special needs preschool that she did. We loved that the school had a variety of programs for kids with issues ranging from speech delays, like Peter had, to helping children with much more severe developmental delays and disorders.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       The school had become a second home in some ways, and I was friendly with most of the teachers, therapists, and administrators.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       At the time Lizzy happened to be going through an extremely difficult period. My days had been spent taking her back and forth to different specialists in an attempt to finally get a name for the disorder that was wreaking so much havoc on her.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I was pushed to my limit and stressed out to the max.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       As I dropped Peter off at his class, the head of his program cornered me in the hallway and told me that I had yet to fill out another form for Peter.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I sort of lost it.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I didnt yell, or get angry at the woman. I knew she was only doing her job. But in my stressed-out state of mind, I quickly filled out the familiar form in a way that I often joked I would to the different teachers, therapists, and social workers that I had known in my many years of being a mom to special needs kids.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       If memory serves me it went something like this:
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <strong>
        What are the speech goals you are hoping your child obtains in the next three months?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Well, I would like Peter to be able to recite the Gettysburg Address, but if hes only able to recite one of Shakespeares sonnets, I will be happy.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <strong>
        What are the fine motor skills you are hoping your child will reach in the next three months?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       It would be great if Peter could rewire the house so we could install central air.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <strong>
        What are the educational goals you are hoping your child will reach in the next three months?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       We would be thrilled if Peter could start on physics this year, but we will be just as happy if he starts calculous.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I continued in this vein for the remainder of the form and submitted it. I later heard that the head of the preschool program was not amused. Fortunately my school district, the ones that the form had to be submitted to, knew me and my family well. They were familiar with all we were going through and found my answers funny.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I have to admit that the thought crossed my mind to do a repeat performance with this new form.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       After all what did they want to learn from me? Did they want to know that I realized that my daughters issues are severe? Do they want me to admit that I know she will not have the same bright future that her brothers will have?
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I know.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Lizzy may not have a concrete diagnosis, but the brain damage that has easily been detected in five MRIs does more than just affect her development and speech. Lizzy also suffers from mental illness that makes staying in reality a real struggle.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       There are days shes able to follow directions and even read a bit. Twenty-four hours later, she is virtually unresponsive.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I honestly have no idea what type of program will be best for her when she is an adult. From the way things stand now, I know that she will probably need a lot of help and that she will never live on her own.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I start filling out the form. Giving the answers I know are expected of me.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Then I get to the last question:
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <strong>
        What type of job has your child said they would like, or has shown an interest in?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       I write down the most truthful answer I can:
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       Lizzy would like to be a princess, but we are aware that there are limited positions available at this time. We do believe though that if anyone can pull it off, she can.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
     </div>
