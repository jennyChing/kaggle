     <div class="hatnote">

     </div>
     <p>
      People with the surname
      <b>
       Zaidi
      </b>
      trace their origins to the Arabian Peninsula particularly the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">
       Mecca
      </a>
      located in present day Saudi Arabia. The Zaidi family are a part of the Arab Tribe of
      <a href="/wiki/Banu_Hashim" title="Banu Hashim">
       Banu Hashim
      </a>
      .The Zaidi surname is derived from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaid_ibn_Ali" title="Zaid ibn Ali">
       Zaid ibn Ali
      </a>
      , the son of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_al-Husayn_Zayn_al-%27Abidin" title="Ali ibn al-Husayn Zayn al-'Abidin">
       Ali ibn al-Husayn Zayn al-'Abidin
      </a>
      , who was the great grandson of the Islamic prophet,
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
       Muhammad
      </a>
      . People with the surname Zaidi trace their lineage to Zaid ibn Ali. Descendants of Zaid ibn Ali who chose to move away from the Arabian Peninsula and have the surname Zaidi are commonly located in Iraq, Iran, Afghanistan and Pakistan.
     </p>
     <p>
      Descendants of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaid_ibn_Ali" title="Zaid ibn Ali">
       Zaid ibn Ali
      </a>
      are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">
       Sayyid
      </a>
      as they are descendants of
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
       Muhammad
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The surname
      <b>
       Al-Zaidi
      </b>
      (Az-Zaidi) can denote one or both of the following:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">
        Sayyid
       </a>
       Arab descendents of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zayd_bin_Ali" title="Zayd bin Ali">
        Zayd bin Ali
       </a>
       that either stayed in Kufa, Iraq or returned to Al-Hijaz.
      </li>
      <li>
       The use of the surname Al-Zaidi to designate one's
       <a href="/wiki/Madhhab" title="Madhhab">
        madhhab
       </a>
       , which is akin to the use of the surnames Al-Hanafi, Al-Maliki, Al-Shafi'i, etc.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Also note people of the Indian subcontinent with the surname Zaidi are not associated with the
      <a href="/wiki/Zaidiyyah" title="Zaidiyyah">
       Zaidiyyah
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Madhhab" title="Madhhab">
       madhhab
      </a>
      but are generally
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ithna_Ashari" title="Ithna Ashari">
       Ithna Ashari
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Wasitis.2FZaidis_in_South_Asia">
       The Wasitis/Zaidis in South Asia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zaidi_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Wasitis/Zaidis in South Asia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Zaidis of the Indian subcontinent are known as
      <i>
       Wasitis
      </i>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Zayd_ibn_Ali" title="Zayd ibn Ali">
       Zayd ibn Ali
      </a>
      was martyred in Kufa, Iraq; many of his descendants either returned to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Hijaz" title="Al-Hijaz">
       al-Hijaz
      </a>
      or remained in Iraq. Some of those who stayed in Iraq settled in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasit" title="Wasit">
       Wasit
      </a>
      . Some descendants from Wasit then moved to the Indian subcontinent. Most of the Zaidis migrated after the Mongol
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Baghdad_(1258)" title="Siege of Baghdad (1258)">
       Siege of Baghdad
      </a>
      in 1258. Majority Zaidis are part of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelver_Shiism" title="Twelver Shiism">
       Twelver Shiism
      </a>
      . Most of them are settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">
       Iraq
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
       Iran
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
       Pakistan
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sayyid_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sayyid-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The largest group of Zaidis is known as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Saadat-e-Bara" title="Saadat-e-Bara">
        Saadat-e-Bara
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Saadat
      </i>
      means descendant of
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
       Muhammad
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       Bara
      </i>
      means twelve in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urdu_language" title="Urdu language">
       Urdu
      </a>
      . There are many interpretations of word bara and many spellings are current: Bara, Bahera, Barha (as spelled in
      <i>
       Tuzuk-e-Jahangiri
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Akbarnama" title="Akbarnama">
        Akbarnama
       </a>
      </i>
      and other Moghul sources) and Bahira meaning "bright" in Arabic language. One explanation of the word is as mentioned above; another is that there are twelve villages in Muzaffarnagar District and their residents were called Sadat Barha. This explanation is mentioned by the Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Jahangir" title="Jahangir">
       Jahangir
      </a>
      in his autobiography
      <i>
       Tuzuk-e-Jahangiri
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Memoirs of Jahangir
      </i>
      . Living outside of imperial camps and not indulging in hedonism of court life is another explanation of the term, as these families avoided the wrath of the noble families of Moghul court, most popular belief about Barha epithet is that they live in twelve villages in Muzaffarnagar district.
     </p>
     <p>
      These Sayyeds are descendants of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abul_Farah_Wasti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abul Farah Wasti (page does not exist)">
       Abul Farah Wasti
      </a>
      who came to India from Wasit (Iraq) in the late 13th century along with his four sons who settled in four villages of Punjab, Kundli, Chhatbanur, Tihanpur and Jajner giving names to all four clans of Sadat Barha. Their numbers are highest in
      <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">
       Karachi
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
       Pakistan
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Muzaffarnagar" title="Muzaffarnagar">
       Muzaffarnagar
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      ). The Kundliwal clan mainly live in
      <a href="/wiki/Mujhera" title="Mujhera">
       Mujhera
      </a>
      , Hashimpur, Valipura, Saifpur,
      <a href="/wiki/Sikrehra_Khola" title="Sikrehra Khola">
       Sikrehra Khola
      </a>
      , Tandhera, Khujhera, Khedhi Pachenda and Sarai Rasulpur. The Chhatraudi clan live in
      <a href="/wiki/Sambalhera" title="Sambalhera">
       Sambalhera
      </a>
      , Luckhmapur (Jaunpur), Peeropur (Bhadohi), Kakrauli, Saidpura Kalan, Morna, Senthal and Kaithora(Gothada)Gujarat. Zaidi Sayyed also migrated from Jansath to the villages of eastern part of Uttar Pradesh namely Sikanderpur,Kandipur in Ambedkar Nagar district.
     </p>
