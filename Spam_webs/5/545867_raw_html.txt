           <h1 class="article-title" id="content-title">
            <span class="" id="parent-fieldname-title-baebe305d1bed3df6d62daeaceeb0fcf">
             Why Wont the Wall Street Journal Cover the Cooperative Economy?
            </span>
           </h1>
           <div class="article-description" id="content-description">
            <span class="" id="parent-fieldname-subheadline-baebe305d1bed3df6d62daeaceeb0fcf">
             Cooperative businesses are proliferating quickly, but you wouldnt know it from reading the Wall Street Journal.
            </span>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="row" id="article-lead-image-row">
          <figure class="article-lead-image">

         <div class="row byline">
          <div class="col-sm-offset-1 col-sm-10">
           <span class="author" rel="author">
            <a class="articleAuthor" href="http://www.yesmagazine.org/@@also-by?author=Gar+Alperovitz%2C+Keane+Bhatt">
             Gar Alperovitz, Keane Bhatt
            </a>
           </span>
           <time class="published updated" datetime="2014-02-16" pubdate="" title="February 16, 2014">
            posted Dec 20, 2012
           </time>
          </div>
         </div>
        </header>
        <div class="row">
         <div class="col-sm-offset-1 col-sm-10" id="story-content">
          <div class="" id="parent-fieldname-text-baebe305d1bed3df6d62daeaceeb0fcf">
           <div>
            <div>
             <div>
              <div style="text-align: center;">
              </div>
              <p>
               Social pain, anger at ecological degradation and the inability of traditional politics to address deep economic failings has fueled an extraordinary amount
    of practical on-the-ground institutional experimentation and innovation by activists, economists and socially minded business leaders in communities around
    the country.
              </p>
              <p>
               A vast democratized "new economy" is slowly emerging throughout the United States. The general public, however, knows almost nothing about it because the
    American press simply does not cover the developing institutions and strategies.
              </p>
              <p>
               For instance, a sample assessment of coverage between January and November of 2012 by the most widely circulated newspaper in the United States, the Wall
    Street Journal, found ten times more references to caviar than to employee-owned firms, a growing sector of the economy that involves more than $800
    billion in assets and 10 million employee-ownersaround three million more individuals than are members of unions in the private sector.
              </p>
              <dl class="image-inline captioned">
               <dt>
                <img alt="WSJ-Employee-Ownership-555.jpg" class=" img-responsive no-min" height="349" src="http://www.yesmagazine.org/new-economy/why-wont-the-wall-street-journal-cover-cooperative-economy/wsj-employee-ownership-555.jpg/image_large" title="WSJ-Employee-Ownership-555.jpg" width="555"/>
               </dt>
               <dd class="image-caption" style="max-width:555px;">
                <div>
                </div>
                <div class="image-credit">
                 <p>
                  Graph by the
                  <a class="external-link" href="http://www.democracycollaborative.org/">
                   Democracy Collaborative
                  </a>
                  .
                 </p>
                </div>
               </dd>
              </dl>
              <p>
               Worker ownershipthe most common form of which involves ESOPs, or Employee Stock Ownership Planswas mentioned in a mere five articles. By contrast, over
    60 articles referred to equestrian activities like horse racing, and golf clubs appeared in 132 pieces over the same period.
              </p>
              <p>
               Although 2012 was designated by the United Nations as the International Year of the Cooperativean institution that now has more than one billion members
    worldwidethe Journals coverage was similarly thin. More than 120 million Americans are members of co-operatives and cooperative credit unions, 30 million
    more people than are owners of mutual funds. The Journal, however, devoted some 700 articles to mutual funds between January and October and only 183 to
    cooperatives. Of these the majority were concerned with high-end New York real estate, with headlines like Pricey Co-ops Find Buyers
              </p>
              <p>
               The vast number of cooperative businesses on Main Streets across the country were discussed in just 70 articles and a mere 14 gave co-op businesses more
    than passing mention. Together, the articles only narrowly outnumbered the 13 Journal pieces that mentioned the Dom Perignon brand of champagne over the
    same time frame, and were eclipsed by the 40 Journal entries that refer to the French delicacy foie gras.
              </p>
              <dl class="image-inline captioned">
               <dt>
                <img alt="WSJ-foie-gras-555.jpg" class=" img-responsive no-min" height="288" src="http://www.yesmagazine.org/new-economy/why-wont-the-wall-street-journal-cover-cooperative-economy/wsj-foie-gras-555.jpg/image_large" title="WSJ-foie-gras-555.jpg" width="555"/>
               </dt>
               <dd class="image-caption" style="max-width:555px;">
                <div>
                </div>
                <div class="image-credit">
                 <p>
                  Graph by the
                  <a class="external-link" href="http://www.democracycollaborative.org/">
                   Democracy Collaborative
                  </a>
                  .
                 </p>
                </div>
               </dd>
              </dl>
              <p>
               Another democratized economic institution is the not-for-profit Community Development Corporation (CDC), roughly 4,500 of which operate in all 50 states
    and the District of Columbia. Such neighborhood corporations create tens of thousands of units of affordable housing and millions of square feet of
    commercial and industrial space a year. The Journal ran no articles mentioning CDCs in 2012 and only 43 over the past 28 yearsless than two a year.
    Meanwhile, the wordchteauappeared in 30 times as many articles, and luxury apartments received 300 times as much coverage over the same period.
              </p>
              <dl class="image-inline captioned">
               <dt>
                <img alt="WSJ-chateaux-555.jpg" class=" img-responsive no-min" height="288" src="http://www.yesmagazine.org/new-economy/why-wont-the-wall-street-journal-cover-cooperative-economy/wsj-chateaux-555.jpg/image_large" title="WSJ-chateaux-555.jpg" width="555"/>
               </dt>
               <dd class="image-caption" style="max-width:555px;">
                <div>
                </div>
                <div class="image-credit">
                 <p class="discreet">
                  Graph by the
                  <a class="external-link" href="http://www.democracycollaborative.org">
                   Democracy Collaborative
                  </a>
                  .
                 </p>
                </div>
               </dd>
              </dl>
              <p>
               Not surprisingly, the growing new economy movement championing democratization of the economy has itself received even less coverage, despite growing
    citizen involvement on many levels. Over the past year, major national, state and other conferences focusing on worker-owned companies, cooperatives,
    public banking, nonprofit and public land trusts, and neighborhood corporations were oversubscribed, reflecting the growing interest in these forms. The
    Journal, however, gave scant coverage to the movement.
              </p>
              <p>
               Thousands of other creative projectsfrom green businesses to new forms of combined community-worker effortsare also underway across the country but
    receive little coverage. A number are self-consciously understood as attempts to develop working prototypes in state and local laboratories of democracy
    that may be applied at regional and national scale when the right political moment occurs. In Cleveland, Ohio, for instance, a complex of sophisticated
    worker-owned firms has been developing in desperately poor, predominantly black neighborhoods. The model is partially structured along lines of the
    Mondragn Corporation, a vibrant network of worker-owned cooperatives in northern Spain with more than 80,000 members and billions of dollars in annual
    revenue.
              </p>
              <p>
               Since 2010 legislation to set up public banks along the lines of the long-established Bank of North Dakota has been proposed in twenty states. Several
    citiesincluding Los Angeles and Kansas Cityhave passed responsible banking ordinances that require banks to reveal their impact on the community and/or
    require city officials to do business only with banks that are responsive to community needs. But municipally led responsible banking initiatives appear to
    have received no attention in the Journal, whereas the newspaper published seven articles this year discussing President Obamas birth certificate.
              </p>
              <p>
               The limited nature of the coverage can also be seen in particular cases. Recreational Equipment, Inc. (REI) is a highly successful consumer co-op with $1.8
    billion in sales for 2011, allowing it to share $165 million of its profits with its 4.7 million active members and 11,000 employees. Organic Valley , a
    Wisconsin-based cooperative dairy, generated more than $700 million in revenue for nearly 1,700 farmer-owners. From January through October 2012, the
    Journal referred (briefly) to REI in just three articles; Organic Valley rated just one mention. In combination, REI and Organic Valley appear in the
    Journal only as often as the Cavalier King Charles spaniel, a breed of dog that turned up in four entries in the Journals pages this year.
              </p>
              <dl class="image-inline captioned">
               <dt>
                <img alt="WSJ-King-Charles-555.jpg" class=" img-responsive no-min" height="288" src="http://www.yesmagazine.org/new-economy/why-wont-the-wall-street-journal-cover-cooperative-economy/wsj-king-charles-555.jpg/image_large" title="WSJ-King-Charles-555.jpg" width="555"/>
               </dt>
               <dd class="image-caption" style="max-width:555px;">
                <div>
                </div>
                <div class="image-credit">
                 <p>
                  Graph by the
                  <a class="external-link" href="http://www.democracycollaborative.org/">
                   Democracy Collaborative
                  </a>
                  .
                 </p>
                </div>
               </dd>
              </dl>
              <p>
               Further perspective on the coverage is offered in the way in which hot topics are presented, and others of greater economic significance played down.
    Co-ops in the U.S. generate over $500 billion in annual revenues. The global market for smartphones is estimated by Bloomberg Industries at $219
    billionless than half as large. Furthermore, there are 20 million more co-op members than smartphone users in the United States. The Journal, however,
    published over 1,000 print articles that included the terms smartphone or smartphones from January through October this yearmore than five articles
    for each piece mentioning co-ops (many of which, as noted, were about upscale Manhattan apartments.)
              </p>
              <p>
               The print coverage of the Journal was analyzed by the Democracy Collaborative of the University of Maryland through the online database ProQuest. Although
    the assessment focused on the Journal, the nations preeminent source of news for economic and business affairs, a preliminary review suggests that other
    national media outlets devote a similarly minuscule proportion of space to the exploding new economy sector. This highlights the need for greater media
    exposure regarding important developments toward a more democratic, sustainable and community-based economy.
              </p>
              <hr width="50%"/>
              <p>
               This article was originally published on .
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Interested?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                <br/>
                When companies are owned by workers and the communityinstead of Wall Street financierseverything changes.
               </li>
               <li>
                <br/>
                We can't go back, and we really don't want to. We're witnessing the birth of something better: a new economy where livelihoods that are just, sustainable, and meaningful will be available to all.
               </li>
               <li>
                <br/>
                Its time we the people declare our independence from the money-favoring Wall Street economy.
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
