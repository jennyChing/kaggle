        <h2 style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://goodmenproject.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/Pencil.jpg">
          <img alt="Pencil" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-124432" height="360" src="//goodmenproject.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/Pencil.jpg" width="588"/>
         </a>
        </h2>
        <h2 style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          <strong>
           Tere
          </strong>
          <strong>
           sa Mei Chuc sheds light on the violence of war, reflecting on the pencilsand the missilesability to both write history and erase it.
          </strong>
         </em>
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Pencil
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          In spite of everything I shall rise again:
          <br/>
          I will take up my pencil, which I have
          <br/>
          forsaken in my great discouragement,
          <br/>
          and I will go on with my drawing.
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
           Vincent Van Gogh
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         A missile is shaped like a pencil 
         <br/>
         its long, slender body and pointed
         <br/>
         end creates history.
        </p>
        <p>
         A girl walking down the street
         <br/>
         a few steps ahead of her sister and friend,
         <br/>
         two medics who were trying to help
         <br/>
         injured people, the parked ambulance 
         <br/>
         all were annihilated by the same weapon.
        </p>
        <p>
         Above, drones  silent, unmanned planes.
         <br/>
         A metal, predatory bird that shoots a missile
         <br/>
         with precision, identifying the colors of a shirt,
         <br/>
         the features on a face  the shape of a nose,
         <br/>
         the color and length of a mustache.
        </p>
        <p>
         In a room far away, in another country, a man
         <br/>
         sits at a desk and looks at a screen; he strokes
         <br/>
         his thick, dark mustache as he carefully
         <br/>
         contemplates, then pushes a button.
        </p>
        <p>
         There is a charred hole in the ground
         <br/>
         where the girl once stood.
        </p>
        <p>
         There are pencils that write and erase,
         <br/>
         write and erase, so that there is nothing
         <br/>
         to be read on the page. The page blank
         <br/>
         as the desert sky, blank as the smooth shell of a drone.
        </p>
        <p>
         There is a family drinking mint tea
         <br/>
         in a living room.
         <br/>
         The man holds a cup to his lips,
         <br/>
         the glass touches his mustache.
         <br/>
         A silent bird hovers above.
         <br/>
         In a split second, everyone is dead,
         <br/>
         the house is in rubbles  arms, legs,
         <br/>
         splattered organs among broken concrete.
        </p>
        <p>
         Soon, there will be no trace.
        </p>

        <strong>
         About Teresa Mei Chuc
        </strong>
        <br/>
        <p>
         Teresa Mei Chuc was born in Saigon, Vietnam and immigrated to the U.S. under political asylum with her mother and brother shortly after the Vietnam War while her father remained in a Vietcong "reeducation" camp for nine years. Her poetry appears in journals such as
         <i>
          EarthSpeak Magazine, Hawaii Pacific Review, Hypothetical Review, Kyoto Journal, The Prose-Poem Project, The National Poetry Review, Rattle, Verse Daily
         </i>
         and in anthologies such as
         <i>
          New Poets of the American West
         </i>
         (Many Voices Press, 2010),
         <i>
          With Our Eyes Open: Poems of the New American Century
         </i>
         (West End Press, 2014), and
         <i>
          Mo Joe
         </i>
         (Beatlick Press, 2014).
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Red-Thread-Teresa-Mei-Chuc/dp/1564745287">
          <i>
           Red Thread
          </i>
         </a>
         is her first full-length collection of poetry. Teresas second collection of poetry is forthcoming from FootHills Publishing in 2015.
        </p>
