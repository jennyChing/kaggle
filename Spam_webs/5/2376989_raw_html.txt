              <div id="op-ed-banner">
               <a href="/category/analysis">
                <h2>
                 Analysis
                </h2>
               </a>
              </div>
             </header>
             <section class="article-content">
              <p>
               Overshadowed by the
               <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/06/duke-wisconsin-ncaa-championship-recap/">
                epic game
               </a>
               and
               <a href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/07/march-madness-duke-national-championship-refs/">
                refereeing controversy
               </a>
               as March Madness came to a close with Duke's victory over Wisconsin on Monday night, was perhaps the most beautiful image of the entire tournament.
              </p>
              <p>
               As seen, above, it shows Duke guard Quinn Cook in tears as he embraces coach Mike Krzyzewski while "
               <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/07/one-shining-moment-2015/">
                One Shining Moment
               </a>
               " plays on the stadium video board after their national championship win.
              </p>
              <p>
               It's a striking photo, to be sure  but even more special because it signifies the perfect final chapter in a college basketball career. Cook's is a journey that casual fans may not be aware of, but as our collective March Madness hangover clears, it's one worth celebrating nonetheless.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                See also:
                <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/07/march-madness-duke-national-championship-refs/">
                 March Madness: Did bad refs hand Duke the national championship?
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               Wunderkind freshmen scored 60 of Duke's 68 points in its national title win. Cook, a senior guard from Washington, D.C., accounted for six of the remaining eight. The comeback victory for all the marbles was a fittingly awesome end for Cook, who arrived at Duke as a hyped high school All-American, struggled to earn playing time initially, did little to distinguish himself for three years then entered this, his final season, with the potential of being overshadowed by Coach K's all-star freshman class.
              </p>
              <p>
               But instead of sulking about the hot-shots threatening to overshadow his final season, Cook adjusted. He moved from his natural point guard position over to shooting guard to make room in the starting lineup for Tyus Jones, the nation's top point guard recruit. He became a leader on and off the court. He became the team's second-most prolific scorer, after center Jahlil Okafor, another hyped freshman.
              </p>
              <p>
               It wasn't always so easy for Cook, though. His dad died in 2008 when he was just starting high school. After becoming a local legend in D.C., he arrived at Duke a cocky kid who had to be humbled. After three seasons as a Blue Devil, that was still the case. Coach K, according to a recent
               <em>
                <a href="http://www.si.com/college-basketball/2015/04/06/quinn-cook-duke-blue-devils-wisconsin-badgers-ncaa-tournament-national-final" target="_blank">
                 Sports Illustrated
                </a>
               </em>
               profile, had a simple message for Cook after Duke's upset loss in last year's tournament: "You need to grow up."
              </p>
              <p>
               That he did.
              </p>
              <p>
               <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="360" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/vnCZrFr9RCs?enablejsapi=1&amp;" width="640">
               </iframe>
              </p>
              <p>
               Cook only made three baskets on Monday night, but Duke wouldn't have been there in the national title game without him. The Raleigh
               <em>
                <a href="http://www.newsobserver.com/sports/college/acc/duke/article13137869.html" target="_blank">
                 News &amp; Observer
                </a>
               </em>
               dubbed him "Duke's indispensable man" before the tournament, and he proved those words true. He scored 17 in the Blue Devils' Final Four win over Michigan State, and averaged 15 points per game through the tourney's first five rounds.
              </p>
              <p>
               After the big win on Monday night to conclude Cook's long journey from hyped freshman, to mediocre Blue Devil, to senior leader, to national champion, the weight of it all hit appeared to hit home as he stood there on the podium in tears embracing the coach who told him to grow up.
              </p>
              <figure class="image">
               <p>
                <img alt="QCook" class="" data-caption="Cook, center, exhorts teammates during Monday's national title game. " data-credit-link="" data-credit-name="Robin Alam" data-credit-provider="AP" data-fragment="m!3739" data-image="http://rack.3.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzA4L2Q3L1FDb29rLjVlMjZlLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/e40e5bbc/297/QCook.jpg" data-micro="1" data-width="1920" src="http://rack.3.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzA4L2Q3L1FDb29rLjVlMjZlLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/e40e5bbc/297/QCook.jpg" title="QCook"/>
               </p>
               <figcaption class="image-caption">
                <p>
                 Cook, center, exhorts teammates during Monday's national title game.
                </p>
               </figcaption>
               <div class="image-credit">
                <p>
                 Image: Robin Alam/Associated Press
                </p>
               </div>
              </figure>
              <p>
               "To be next to Coach, he's been like a father to me over these last four years, to have his arm around me and hugging me while we're watching 'One Shining Moment' was probably the best feeling in my life," Cook
               <a href="http://www.news-record.com/sports/accxtra/a-shining-moment-for-duke-s-quinn-cook/article_deb6f75c-dd4d-11e4-bd68-8bc9cc37f3c6.html" target="_blank">
                said
               </a>
               after. "I'm just blessed. I'm just blessed that Coach thought I was good enough to come to Duke."
              </p>
              <p>
               So what does the future hold for Quinn Cook? That's unclear. For his superstar freshmen teammates, though, the answer is simple: Three of them will likely be first-round selections in June's
               <a href="http://mashable.com/category/nba/">
                NBA
               </a>
               Draft. Cook's pro potential is less certain; he's likely not big enough or fast enough to make the NBA. Perhaps he'll go overseas.
              </p>
              <p>
               But those are questions for another day. For now, Cook is a champion and a Duke hero  two things worth celebrating on their own.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               And that's the beauty of college basketball: No matter what the future does or doesn't bring for Cook, his emotional moment there on that podium will last from Monday night until the end of time.
              </p>
              <p>

               </strong>
              </p>
