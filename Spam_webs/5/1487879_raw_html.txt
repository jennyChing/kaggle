         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Expats living abroad
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="sep">
           Posted on
          </span>
          <a href="http://www.my-qrops.com/expats-living-abroad/" rel="bookmark" title="9:21 am">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2014-10-17T09:21:28+00:00">
            October 17, 2014
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="by-author">
           <span class="sep">
            by
           </span>
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.my-qrops.com/author/myqrops-admin/" rel="author" title="View all posts by My QROPS Administrator">
             My QROPS Administrator
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <img alt="thumbnail" class="postFeatured" height="207" src="http://www.my-qrops.com/wp-content/themes/myqrops/images/thumb-default.png" width="170"/>
         <p>
          According to statistics, more Brits live abroad than any other nationality, and in the last twenty years at its peak in 2006 the number leaving the UK was as many as 207,000 in one year.
         </p>
         <p>
          The
          <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/home-office" title="the home office">
           Home Office
          </a>
          estimates that 4.7 million UK citizens now live abroad. Although there are over one hundred countries where more than 1,000 British expats live, most of those leaving the UK settle in one of the top ten countries on the list of most popular destinations.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span id="more-1692">
          </span>
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Australia
          </li>
          <li>
           USA
          </li>
          <li>
           Spain
          </li>
          <li>
           Canada
          </li>
          <li>
           Ireland
          </li>
          <li>
           France
          </li>
          <li>
           New Zealand
          </li>
          <li>
           South Africa
          </li>
          <li>
           Germany
          </li>
          <li>
           UAE
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
          This list has been consistently topped by
          <strong>
           Australia
          </strong>
          as the number one preference.Whilst Spain and the United States have vied for second place.
         </p>
         <p>
          Australia and the US, along with Canada and Ireland in 4th and 5th place, have the benefit of being English speaking which is one of the top considerations when looking to move abroad.
         </p>
         <p>
          Spain has the advantage of its proximity to the UK (especially since the availability of cheap flights), its good weather and familiarity as a holiday destination.
         </p>
         <p>
          Weather is obviously an important factor for many Brits considering moving overseas when you bear in mind that the weather is the most popular topic of conversation in the UK, with the majority of people complaining that the weather is too unpredictable.
         </p>
         <p>
          Although there are beautiful areas of Britain and some stunning beaches, even in the main summer months leisure plans can be ruined by bad weather.
         </p>
         <p>
          The lure of guaranteed good weather and al fresco living is an understandable enticement.
         </p>
         <p>
          Some of the countries on the top ten list are predominantly popular for reasons of employment rather than as a retirement destination because of strict visa regulations. This is especially the case with the USA and UAE.
         </p>
         <p>
          In terms of retirement destinations the number one spot for those who are considering leaving the UK once they retire is held by Spain, partly because many already enjoy it as a holiday destination and the idea of feeling as if they are on holiday on a permanent basis appeals, even if this isnt necessarily the reality.
         </p>
         <p>
          Spain is also still relatively cheap in terms of the cost of living compared to many other countries and the large expat communities are a draw for many people who still yearn for their home comforts.
         </p>
         <p>
          For those looking to retire elsewhere, upcoming popular retirement destinations include Malta, Barbados, Thailand, Morocco and Panama, which was voted the best retirement location in the world in 2014 by International Living.
         </p>
         <p>
          Whichever destination appeals, it is important to consider all the factors involved in moving to another country to live. Doing your homework before you leave is crucial but it is also important to visit the area before making the move to ensure that it is the right place for you. Language, cost of living and distance from family and friends should be addressed.
         </p>
         <p>
          Most important are the financial implications of a move abroad and therefore it is always wise to take financial advice from a regulated adviser who has knowledge and experience of the rules and regulations in your intended country of residence. With regard to pensions, your options should be considered with regard to keeping your pension scheme in the UK and therefore within the UK tax net or transferring it to an overseas pension scheme where you may be able to benefit from significant tax savings.
         </p>
         <p>
          The advantages of transferring a UK pension scheme to a QROPS (Qualifying Recognised Overseas Pension Scheme) if you are non-UK resident include:
         </p>
         <p>
           The pension income falls outside the UK tax net
         </p>
         <p>
           The lifetime allowance (LTA), currently 1.25 million, no longer applies
         </p>
         <p>
           The scheme is protected from creditors and pension sharing in divorce
         </p>
         <p>
           Currency options
         </p>
         <p>
           Consolidation of pension schemes
         </p>
         <p>
           Flexibility of investment
         </p>
         <p>
           30% lump sum as opposed to 25% in the UK
         </p>
         <p>
           Removal of 55% tax charge on death that applies in the UK
         </p>
         <p>
           Potential retirement age may fall from 55 in the UK to 50
         </p>
        </div>
