                  Pittsburgh Penguins and Referees are at Odds
                 </span>
                </div>
                <!--hide-title-->
                <div id="left-content-mobi">
                 <div class="author-img left">
                  <img alt="James A Conley" class="avatar avatar-120 wp-user-avatar wp-user-avatar-120 alignnone photo" height="120" src="http://www.todaysslapshot.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/dfcgh-200x200.png" width="120"/>

                <p>
                 <!-- BEGIN THEIA POST SLIDER -->
                </p>
                <p>
                 Youll never hear an NHL referee say they are tired of dealing with a certain player or team.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Thats mostly because the NHL doesnt let its officials speak to the press following games (and reprimandsthe ones who would talk to them
                 <a href="http://www.sportingnews.com/nhl/story/2015-02-02/tim-peel-suspended-puck-daddy-interview-nhl-official-greg-wyshynski" target="_blank">
                  in, say, a bar
                 </a>
                 ).
                </p>
                <p>
                 So to hear former NHL referee and host of TSNs CMon Ref blog Kerry Fraser identify the Pittsburgh Penguins as a team that has specifically
                 <a href="http://www.tsn.ca/c-mon-ref-third-man-in-not-in-this-case-1.232409" target="_blank">
                  drawn the ire
                 </a>
                 of a group of officials, if not totally surprising, is still as rare as it is candid.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Following the Penguins 5-1 loss to Detroit last Sunday (in which the Penguins were assessed 64 penalty minutes and five misconduct penalties), Pittsburghs disgust with league officiating was just about as evident as its ever going to be, short of a police affidavit. Chris Kunitz was tagged with a misconduct for flipping a puck towards a referee. Kris Letang was given the rest of the night off after a series of on-ice discussions, and visibly mocked one officials explanation on his way out of the building.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Given those five misconduct calls and the general problems of verbosity that have followed the Penguins all year, its safe to assume that the feeling of disgust was mutual.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <a href="http://www.tsn.ca/c-mon-ref-third-man-in-not-in-this-case-1.232409" target="_blank">
                  From Fraser at TSN
                 </a>
                 ,
                </p>
                <p>
                 <em>
                  I can guarantee that there was way too much being said from the penalty box, the players bench and on the ice by the Penguin players that resulted in the unsportsmanlike conduct and misconduct penalty that was assessed to Kunitz at the end of the period. There was absolutely no need for Kunitz to go out of his way to slide the puck in the direction of referee McCauley who was standing at the Zamboni entrance waiting to exit the ice.When players engage in excessive whining and complaining, as it appeared the Penguins did in the first period, the referee will stop the drip and shut off the tap by imposing a penalty. Teams can develop a reputation for whining no differently than players do for diving and embellishment.
                 </em>
                </p>
                <p>
                 <em>
                  <strong>
                   The Penguins have earned the undesirable reputation with several of the referees for having too much to say.
                  </strong>
                  As a team, they need to turn off the tap on their own as they move toward the playoffs. The referees patience has already worn thin.
                 </em>
                </p>
                <p>
                 Yikes.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Theories have abound this year on whether the officials have a beef with the Penguins. This may be as close as it comes to verifying a potential bias, although claims that the Penguins bring any undue treatment on themselves are really hard to disagree with.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Notice the many instances of the Penguins making life more difficult forthose charged with managing the game.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <a href="http://sportsforecaster.com/nhl/player/4093?aff=TodaysSlapshot" target="_blank" title="Sidney Crosby">
                  Sidney Crosby
                 </a>
                 s
                 <a href="http://www.russianmachineneverbreaks.com/2014/12/27/sidney-crosby-has-hissy-fit-after-shanking-one-timer-gif/" target="_blank">
                  stick-durability test
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p>
                 Letangs yapping in
                 <a href="http://thepensblog.com/2015-archives/kris-letang-mocks-an-official-when-learning-he-has-been-kicked-out-of-the-game-vs-detroit.html" target="_blank">
                  the Detroit meltdown
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p>
                 Steve Downie.
                </p>
                <p>
                 (The list ends there, but only because we cant .GIF a thousand lip-reading moments between plays.)
                </p>
                <p>
                 Theres no question that the Penguins have one hand on the shovel digging the foundation for that doghouse. Their reduced power play opportunities are immense (an astounding drop from the
                 <a href="https://twitter.com/JoshYohe_Trib/status/578007233713209344" target="_blank">
                  first 15 games this year to now
                 </a>
                 ) and they are distancing themselves for the league lead in penalty minutes.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Referees are only human. Continued headaches from guys in the same jerseys are going to lead to an inescapable and perfectly reasonable negative bias.
                </p>
                <p>
                 But if the Penguins way out of the doghouse is to turn off the tap on their own, as Fraser puts it, then a similar onus must fall on those worn-thin referees to continue doing their jobs as blind to the jerseys as possible, regardless of whicheverpain in the ass is skating around in black and gold.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Note that in their first game after the Detroit debacle, the Penguinsdid not record a power play opportunity in Tuesdays loss to New Jersey (the third time theyve gone without a man-advantage this year). The only Devils minor was negated by an embellishment call on Pens forward Daniel Winnik (he of a whole19 PIM this season), while an apparent power play opportunity late in the game was missedwhen Letang was very much tripped but no call was assessed.
                </p>
                <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="550">
                 <p>
                  Ghislain Hebert and Dan O'Rourke did not feel this was a trip:
                  <a href="https://t.co/KtItlCDOeJ">
                   https://t.co/KtItlCDOeJ
                  </a>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                   Seth Rorabaugh (@emptynetters)
                  <a href="https://twitter.com/emptynetters/status/578015154325041152">
                   March 18, 2015
                  </a>
                 </p>
                </blockquote>
                <p>
                 <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
                 </script>
                </p>
                <p>
                 NHL referees, again, are not held to meeting with reporters following games. Blogs like Frasers and off-ice interviews like the instance with Peel are about as close as anyone gets to getting in their minds.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The Penguins, as a team and as an organization, have been at odds with officiating all year long. With obstruction up and offense marginalized, its easy to see why they would be. Its on them not to allow bad or missed calls, real or perceived, to change their behavior.
                </p>
                <p>
                 But to hear things from officials past and present such as patience has worn thin, develop a reputation and making calls to fit a
                 <a href="http://sports.yahoo.com/blogs/nhl-puck-daddy/my-night-at-the-bar-with-tim-peel--nhl-ref-and-object-of-scorn-171058557.html" target="_blank">
                  mandate from the league
                 </a>
                 , one has to wonder how much those officialsare allowing the Penguins and their feelings towards the team to change their behavior, too.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The Penguins will rightfully have to answer for themselves, but will do so in a very public manner. It would be great to see more of the same from the other side.
                </p>

                </p>
