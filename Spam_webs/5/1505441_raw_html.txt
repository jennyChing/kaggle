          <h1>
           Aquaponic Gardening Rules of Thumb
          </h1>
         </div>

        <div class="xg_module">
         <div class="xg_module_body wpage">
          <div class="description" id="page-content">
           <div class="xg_user_generated">
            <h2>
             By Sylvia Bernstein and Dr. Wilson Lennard
            </h2>
            <p>
             <br/>
             <img src="http://api.ning.com:80/files/-Eo5gBPVCd5MMYB8SL7Qnlf-YjyE3o4bDcCcrPU6AYgxNtDNSKMpnczNvU7FYUgl2ICAmrCLQFAFAtc11DEz1aVGlo8HG86G/greenthumb.jpg" style="float: right;"/>
             Many of our community site members have pointed out that we need some basic rules-of-thumb for aquaponic gardening. Why? Because they will help the beginners among us get started without needing to spend weeks researching what to do.
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             Ive had the distinct honor of collaborating with
             <a href="http://www.aquaponic.com.au/" target="_blank">
              Dr. Wilson Lennard
             </a>
             from Australia on the guidelines that follow below.Dr. Lennard has earned one of the few PhDs in aquaponics in the world.
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             Nothing we say below is set in stone and there are exceptions to almost every one of the listed rules-of-thumb given certain conditions. However, they do offer a set of generally accepted principles that, if adhered to, will put you on the path towards successful aquaponic gardening.
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             If you decide that you would like to go to a deeper understanding of these guidelines and their application then I recommend purchasing my book and online course,
             <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponic-Gardening-Course-s/147.htm" target="_blank">
              Aquaponic Gardening
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             One final caveat. These rules are intended for media-based home or community systems only. If you are intending to get into aquaponics commercially, first please read 
             <a href="http://theaquaponicsource.com/2010/10/14/commercial-aquaponics/" target="_self">
              Commercial Aquaponics: 10 Things to Consider Before Taking the Plunge
             </a>
             . Then please seek a professional training course that will help you design an optimal system that has a chance to generate a profit.
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             Lets get started
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             <strong>
              System type
             </strong>
              Media bed is recommended for new, hobby growers. Why not NFT or Deep Water Culture (AKA raft orDWC)?
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               A media bed performs three (3) filtering functions;
              </li>
              <li style="list-style: none; display: inline;">
               <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
                <ol start="1" type="1">
                 <li>
                  mechanical (solids removal)
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  mineralization (solids breakdown and return to the water)
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  bio-filtration
                 </li>
                </ol>
               </div>
              </li>
              <li>
               Because the media bed also acts as the place for plant growth,it basically does everything all in one component  making it allsimple.
              </li>
              <li>
               Media also provides better plant support and is more closely related to traditional soil gardening because there is a media to plant into.
              </li>
              <li>
               The cost of building the
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/AquaBundance-Aquaponics-Systems-s/142.htm" target="_blank">
                aquaponics system
               </a>
               is lower because there are fewer components.
              </li>
              <li>
               It is easier to understand and learn.
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Grow Bed
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               The industry standard is to be at least 12 (30 cm) deep to allow for growing the widest variety of plants and to provide complete filtration.
              </li>
              <li>
               Must be made of food safe materials and should not alter the pH of your system (beware of concrete).
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Fish tank
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               If you have flexibility here, 250 gallon (1000 liters) or larger seems to create the most stable
               <a href="http://theaquaponicsource.com/" target="_blank">
               </a>
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/AquaBundance-Aquaponics-Systems-s/142.htm" target="_blank">
                aquaponics systems
               </a>
               .Larger volumes are better for beginners because they allow more room for error; things happen more slowly at larger volumes.
              </li>
              <li>
               Must be made of food safe materials and should not alter the pH of your system (again,beware of concrete).
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Stocking Density
             </strong>
             
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               1 pound of fish per 5  7 gallons of tank water (.5 kg per 20-26 liters)
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Steps for Planning your System
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ol start="1" type="1">
              <li>
               Determine the total grow bed area in sq ft (or sq m)
              </li>
              <li>
               From grow bed area, determine the fish weight required (pounds or kg) using the ratio rule 1 lb (.5 kg) of fish for every 1 sq ft(.1 sq m) of grow bed surface area, assuming the beds are at least12 (30 cm) deep.
              </li>
              <li>
               Determine fish tank volume from the stocking density rule above (1 pound fish per 5  7 gallons of fish tank volume or .5 kg per 20-26 liters). When your fish are young and small, reduce the number of plants in proportion to the size of the fish and their corresponding feed rate / waste production.
              </li>
             </ol>
             <p>
              For example, if you plan to have 2 2x4 grow beds, then you will have 16 sq ft of growing area. Plan to stock so you have a mature weight of 16 pounds of fish which require an 80  112 gallon fish tank.
             </p>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Media
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Must be inert  i.e wont decompose or alter the pH of the system.
              </li>
              <li>
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponics-Grow-Media-s/85.htm" target="_blank">
                LECA
               </a>
               (Lightweight Expanded Clay Aggregate)and Gravel are the most widely used media types. If you choose gravel, understand its source and avoid limestone and marble as they could affect your pH.
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Water Flow
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               You should flood, then drain your grow beds. The draining action pulls oxygen through the grow beds. The least complicated way to achieve a reliable
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponic-Flood-and-Drain-Fittings-Kit-p/cwhas005.htm" target="_blank">
                flood drain system
               </a>
               is using a timer.While more complex,
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponics-Bell-Siphon-with-Media-Guard-p/cmfas105.htm" target="_blank">
                siphons
               </a>
               are also excellent options for aquaponics.
              </li>
              <li>
               If you are operating your system with a timer you should run itfor 15 minutes on, and 45 minutes off.
              </li>
              <li>
               You want to flow the entire volume of your fish tank through your
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/AquaBundance-Aquaponics-Grow-Bed-p/cmfgr001.htm" target="_blank">
                aquaponic grow beds
               </a>
               every hour if possible. Therefore, if you are running your pump for 15 minutes every hour (see above), and you have a 100 gallon tank, you need at least a 400 gallon per hour (gph) pump. Now consider the lift or how far against gravity you need to move that water and use the sliding scale that is on the pump packaging to see how much more power you need beyond the 400 gph.
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Starting you System or Cycling
             </strong>
             
             <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponics-Cycling-Kit-Medium-100-300-gal-p/abaas002.htm" target="_blank">
              Fishless Cycling
             </a>
             is recommended because it will develop a robust bacteria base and allow you to fully stock your fish tank in a couple of weeks vs. the traditional method of using fish which can be very stressful for the fish. For instructions see
             <a href="http://theaquaponicsource.com/2010/11/01/starting-your-aquaponics-system-using-fishless-cycling/" target="_self">
              Fishless Cycling
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <br/>
             <strong>
              Ammonia, Nitrites, Nitrates
             </strong>
              after cycling,
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Ammonia and Nitrite levels should be less than .5 ppm
              </li>
              <li>
               If you see Ammonia levels rise suddenly, you may have a dead fish in your tank.
              </li>
              <li>
               If you see Nitrite levels rise you may have damaged the bacterial environment in your system.
              </li>
              <li>
               If either of the above circumstances occur, stop feeding your fish until the levels stabilize, and, in extreme cases, do a 1/3 water exchange to dilute the existing solution.
              </li>
              <li>
               Nitrates can rise as high as 150 ppm without causing a problem, but much above that, you should consider adding another grow bed to your system.
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Oxygen
             </strong>
             Be sure there is plenty of oxygen inyour fish tank. You can do this through the use of a separate
             <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponics-Fish-Tank-Aeration-s/42.htm" target="_blank">
              aeration
             </a>
             device and by diverting part of the water from flooding and draining your grow beds directly into your
             <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponics-Fish-Tanks-s/115.htm" target="_blank">
              aquaponics fish tank
             </a>
             . The only way you can have too much oxygen in a fish tank is if you are literally blowing your fish out of the tank. If you dont have enough oxygen being infused into your tank your fish will be gasping for air at the water surface, but if you reach this stage you may have done permanent damage to your fishs respiratory system.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              When to add plants
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Add plants as soon as you start cycling your system, but accept that they may not grow well for the few weeks required for cycling to occur.
              </li>
              <li>
               If you add
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/MaxiCrop-Liquid-Seaweed-quart-p/abahf002.htm" target="_blank">
                Maxicrop Liquid Seaweed
               </a>
               to your tank when planting (Australians call this
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/MaxiCrop-Liquid-Seaweed-quart-p/abahf002.htm" target="_blank">
                Seasol
               </a>
               ) at the rate of 1 quart bottle per 250 gallons (1000 liters), your plants will establish themselves much more quickly.
              </li>
              <li>
               You can place your plants roughly twice as closely together as you would in soil.
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              When to add fish if you are using a Fishless Cycling technique
             </strong>
              add
             <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/Aquaponics-Tilapia-Fingerlings-s/89.htm" target="_blank">
              fish
             </a>
             once nitrates are present and the ammonia and nitrite levels have peaked and declined below 1.0ppm.
             <br/>
             <strong>
              Feeding Rate
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Feed as much as your fish will eat in 5 minutes, 1  3 times per day. An adult fish will eat approximately 1% of its body weight per day. Fish fry (babies) will eat as much as 7%. Be careful not to over feed your fish.
              </li>
              <li>
               If your fish arent eating they are probably stressed, outside of their optimal temperature range, or they dont have enough oxygen.
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Worms
             </strong>
             - Add a handful of
             <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/1-LB-1000-Live-Red-Wiggler-Compost-Worms-p/lwruj001.htm" target="_blank">
              composting red worms
             </a>
             to each grow bed once your system is fully cycled and fish have been added. They will help process the solid waste from the fish in your grow beds.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             <strong>
              pH
             </strong>
            </p>
            <div style="margin-left: 2em;" type="disc">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Target a pH of neutral, or 7.0, in your
               <a href="http://theaquaponicsource.com/" target="_blank">
                aquaponic
               </a>
               system.This is a compromise between the optimal ranges of the fish, the plants, and the bacteria. For fish, this is a pH of around 6.5 to 8.0. For plants, this is a pH of around 5.0 to 7.0 and for bacteria it is a pH of 6.0 to 8.0.
              </li>
              <li>
               Test pH at least weekly, and as frequently as 3  4 times per week.
              </li>
              <li>
               During cycling pH will tend to rise.
              </li>
              <li>
               After cycling your
               <a href="http://www.theaquaponicstore.com/AquaBundance-Aquaponics-Systems-s/142.htm" target="_blank">
                aquaponic systems
               </a>
               , pH will probably drop below 7.0 on a regular basis and require being buffered up. If you need tolower pH it is generally because of the water source (such as hard ground water) or because you have a base buffer in your system (eggshells, oyster shell, shell grit, incorrect media).
              </li>
              <li>
               Best method for raising (buffering) pH if it drops below6.6
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div style="margin-left: 4em;" type="disc">
              <ul type="square">
               <li>
                Calcium hydroxide  hydrated lime or builders lime.
               </li>
               <li>
                Potassium carbonate (or bicarbonate) or potassium hydroxide (pearlash or potash).
               </li>
               <li>
                If possible, alternate between these two each time your system needs the pH raised. These also add calcium and potassium,which your plants will appreciate.
               </li>
               <li>
                While they work, be cautious about using natural Calcium Carbonate products (egg shells, snail shells, sea shells). They dont do any harm, but they take a long time to dissolve and affect the pH. So, you add it, check pH two hours later and nothing has changed, so you add more. Then suddenly, the pH spikes because you have added so much.
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Best methods for lowering pH, in order of preference, if it goes above 7.6
               <div style="margin-left: 4em;" type="disc">
                <ul type="square">
                 <li>
                  Acids like nitric orphosphoric as the plants can use the nitrate orphosphate produced.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Other acids, such as vinegar (weak), hydrochloric (strong), and sulphuric (strong)  last resort as directly adding these acids to your system could be stressful for your fish.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </div>
              </li>
              <li>
               Use caution when adding anything to your system containing sodium as it could build-up over time and cause harm to your plants.
              </li>
              <li>
               Do not use citric acid as this is anti-bacterial and will kill the bacteria in your bio-filter.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              <strong>
               Plant Nutrient Deficiencies
              </strong>
              -
              <a href="http://community.theaquaponicsource.com/page/nutrient-deficiency-chart-for" target="_self">
               click here
              </a>
              for a guide to identifying nutrient deficiencies inplants
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
