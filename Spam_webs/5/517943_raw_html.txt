      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Microsoft Scraps Windows 8 Major Updates. Bets The Farm On Windows 10
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">
        Comment Now
       </a>
       <div class="comments_follow followable_block">
        <div class="followable_block initializing" data-object-json='{"title":"Microsoft Scraps Windows 8 Major Updates. Bets The Farm On Windows 10","avatar_src":"","follow_count":""}' data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/2907-4346" data-target-name="" data-target-type="article" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow" data-type-advoice="false">
         <div class="follow_unireg">
          <a class="follow_unireg_link" href="javascript://follow">

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         Its official,
         <span class="tweet_quote">
          <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1ycUBUu&amp;text=Windows%208%20is%20a%20write-off">
           Windows 8 is a write-off
           <span>
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         . Sales for the operating system have been poor and now it is even starting to
         <a href="http://www.theinquirer.net/inquirer/news/2358461/windows-8-market-share-down-slightly-as-windows-7-climbs-again">
          lose market share
         </a>
         to Windows 7. To
         <a href="/companies/microsoft/">
          Microsoft
         </a>
         <span class="quotecard_hook" data-exchange="NASDAQ" data-naturalid="fred/company/2854" data-quotes-closing="45.53" data-quotes-now="45.62" data-ticker="MSFT" data-type="organization">
          <span class="wrapper">
           <a href="/companies/microsoft/">
            Microsoft
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         s credit it has bravely persisted addressing issue after issue. Most notable was the major Windows 8.1 Update 1 patch released in April which makes the OS a genuinely credible platform. Still it remains far from perfect and now Microsoft is prematurely pulling the plug.
        </p>
        <p>
         In a
         <a href="http://blogs.windows.com/bloggingwindows/2014/08/05/august-updates-for-windows-8-1-and-windows-server-2012-r2/">
          blog post
         </a>
         by Microsoft Senior Marketing Communications Manager Brandon LeBlanc, he explains that there will be no more major update releases for Windows 8: despite rumours and speculation, we are not planning to deliver a Windows 8.1 Update 2.
        </p>
        <p>
         LeBlanc reassured Windows 8 users that they can expect that well use our already existing monthly update process to deliver more frequent improvements along with the security updates, but in my opinion this doesnt wash. A roadmap with no major updates on the horizon is the deathknell for any operating system. Android, iOS and Microsofts Windows Phone along with previous Windows versions all deliver regular improvements, but they still make a point of issuing major updates to introduce the biggest changes and generate interest.
        </p>
        <p>
         Instead what is now clear is forMicrosoftthe next big update will be
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/gordonkelly/2014/01/17/what-windows-9-must-do-to-avoid-flopping-like-windows-8/">
          Windows 10
         </a>
         and in taking the decision to sideline an OS that wont even be two years old until the end of October the company runs a huge risk of undermining the confidence of users who expect Windows versions to last 12 years like Windows XP.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/gordonkelly/files/2014/08/12.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where Is Microsofts Backbone?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The announcement also risks spreading the feeling that this new Microsoft under Satya Nadella has no backbone. Yes the company looks more progressive embracing Cloud services and delivering products like Microsoft Office on rival platforms, but it also appears to take the scorched earth approach to anything that looks tough:
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/kellyclay/2014/07/20/microsoft-layoffs-also-impact-thousands-of-contractors/">
          Nokia employee integration
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/erikkain/2014/07/15/xbox-not-a-core-part-of-microsofts-business-ceo-says/">
          Xbox divisional integration
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/microsoft-surface-tablet-2014-8">
          Surface Mini
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/erikkain/2014/07/17/microsoft-is-shutting-down-xbox-entertainment-studios/">
          Microsoft Entertainment Studios
         </a>
         and now
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/gordonkelly/2014/01/17/what-windows-9-must-do-to-avoid-flopping-like-windows-8/">
          turning around Windows 8
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Furthermore the speed with which these sweeping changes are occurring is cause for concern:
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <a href="/companies/nokia/">
          Nokia
         </a>
         <span class="quotecard_hook" data-exchange="null" data-naturalid="fred/company/3134" data-quotes-closing="0.0" data-quotes-now="0.0" data-ticker="null" data-type="organization">
          <span class="wrapper">
           <a href="/companies/nokia/">
            Nokia
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         acquisition was only completed in April, the Xbox One is less than one year old, the Surface Mini was canned only days before it was due to be unveiled and Xbox Entertainment Studios was shut only two months after announcing plans for extensive original programming. Meanwhile at BUILD in April Microsoft
         <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2014/4/2/5574830/windows-9-start-menu-new-desktop-experience">
          demoed
         </a>
         the return of the much requested start menu in Windows 8  something planned for the oh it never existed Update 2. This new Microsoft moves on fast.
        </p>
        <p>
         As such it is no surprise to see convenient Windows 9 leaks popping up all over the place and sweet talking us with goodies galore. This week alone we are
         <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2014/8/7/5977989/windows-9-virtual-desktops-no-more-charms-menu">
          told
         </a>
         the next gen OS will now be the one to
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/gordonkelly/2014/07/22/windows-9-new-start-menu/">
          bring back the start menu
         </a>
         , that it will ditch the reviled Charms Bar, add the long requested virtual desktops seen in Mac OS (Spaces  below) and Ubuntu and even tempt users to forget about dusty old Windows 8 because Windows 9  for some users  might
         <a href="http://www.zdnet.com/windows-threshold-more-on-microsofts-plan-to-win-over-windows-7-users-7000031070/">
          be free
         </a>
         !
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="spaces-mavericks.large" class=" size-full wp-image-4362" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/gordonkelly/files/2014/08/spaces-mavericks.large_.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Dont Question, Just Keep Upgrading
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Forget the fact the company has a major responsibility to users and businesses who have widely deployed Windows 8. The message is clear:
         <em>
          Dont question, just keep upgrading!
         </em>
         This is over simplistic and repeats a mistake the company
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/gordonkelly/2014/04/15/microsoft-abandons-windows-8-1-take-immediate-action-or-be-cut-off-like-windows-xp/">
          has made before
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         And yet the sad thing in all of this is Windows 8 is close to being the outrageously ambitious and creative OS Microsoft originally intended.Desktop users can now live almost entirely in the desktop interface if they so wish, the merger of desktop and Windows 8 apps is well underway, it is increasingly friendly to touch, mouse and keyboard interaction and is arguably the fastest and most stable version of Windows to date.
        </p>
        <p>
         Windows 8 will never be loved like Windows 7 or Windows XP, but there was still hope for a better legacy. How might this be done? Here are my suggestions:
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <span style="line-height: 13px">
           Return the Start Menu to Windows 8. Having gone completely silent since its initial reveal it is an easy PR win and shows the company follows through
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          Be transparent about future of Windows 8. Is it now feature locked? If so, explain why.
         </li>
         <li>
          With Windows 9 previews expected before the end of 2014 give free upgrades to Windows 8 owners who have had the OS less than 12 months. The last thing Microsoft wants is customers who feel shortchanged.
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/gordonkelly/2014/01/17/what-windows-9-must-do-to-avoid-flopping-like-windows-8/">
          Read more:What Windows 9 Must Do To Avoid Flopping Like Windows 8
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         None of this is rocket science. In fact it can be summed up simply: show backbone. As it is Microsoft is fast burning good will in all directions and
         <span class="tweet_quote">
          <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1ycUBUu&amp;text=The%20pressure%20for%20Windows%209%20to%20now%20amaze%20us%20is%20greater%20than%20on%20any%20previous%20Microsoft%20OS">
           the pressure for Windows 9 to now amaze us is greater than on any previous Microsoft OS
           <span>
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         You see you can keep running from your mistakes as long as you are convinced it is towards a glorious future, but should that falter everything you previously cast aside will suddenly catch up with you. With a bang.
        </p>
