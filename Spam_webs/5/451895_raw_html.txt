     <p>
      <b>
       Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder predominantly inattentive
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       ADHD-PI
      </b>
      ), formerly
      <b>
       attention deficit disorder
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       ADD
      </b>
      ), is one of the two types of
      <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">
       attention deficit hyperactivity disorder
      </a>
      (ADHD). The term was formally changed in 1994 in the new
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">
        Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders
       </a>
      </i>
      , fourth edition (
      <i>
       DSM-IV
      </i>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to "ADHD predominantly inattentive" (ADHD-PI or ADHD-I)  though the term 'attention deficit disorder' is still widely used. 'Predominantly Inattentive' is similar to the other subtypes of ADHD except that it is characterized primarily by inattentive concentration or a deficit of sustained attention, such as procrastination, hesitation, and forgetfulness; it differs in having fewer or no typical symptoms of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">
       hyperactivity
      </a>
      or impulsiveness.
      <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">
       Lethargy/fatigue
      </a>
      is sometimes reported, but ADHD-PI is a separate condition from the proposed cluster of symptoms known as
      <a href="/wiki/Sluggish_cognitive_tempo" title="Sluggish cognitive tempo">
       sluggish cognitive tempo
      </a>
      (SCT).
     </p>
     <p>
      The three most popular ADHD medications in order are:
      <a href="/wiki/Adderall" title="Adderall">
       Adderall
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">
       dopamine
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">
       norepinephrine
      </a>
      basically balanced,
      <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">
       methylphenidate
      </a>
      for a
      <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">
       dopamine
      </a>
      emphasis, and
      <a href="/wiki/Atomoxetine" title="Atomoxetine">
       atomoxetine
      </a>
      for a
      <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">
       norepinephrine
      </a>
      emphasis.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
       Classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ADHD-PI is an attention-concentration deficit that has everything in common with ADHD except that it has fewer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">
       hyperactivity
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Impulsivity" title="Impulsivity">
       impulsivity
      </a>
      symptoms and has more
      <a href="/wiki/Directed_attention_fatigue" title="Directed attention fatigue">
       directed attention fatigue
      </a>
      symptoms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-add_college_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-add_college-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Different countries have used different ways of diagnosing ADHD-PI. In the United Kingdom, diagnosis is based on quite a narrow set of symptoms, and about 0.51% of children are thought to have attention or hyperactivity problems.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)">
         <span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (April 2015)">
          medical citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">
       Signs and symptoms
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="DSM-5_criteria">
       DSM-5 criteria
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: DSM-5 criteria">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">
       DSM-
      </a>
      5 allows for diagnosis of the
      <i>
       predominantly inattentive
      </i>
      subtype of ADHD (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSM-5_codes" title="DSM-5 codes">
       code 314.00
      </a>
      ) if the individual presents six or more (five for adults) of the following symptoms of inattention for at least six months to a point that is disruptive and inappropriate for developmental level:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Often does not give close attention to details or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, work, or other activities.
      </li>
      <li>
       Often has trouble keeping attention on tasks or play activities.
      </li>
      <li>
       Often does not seem to listen when spoken to directly.
      </li>
      <li>
       Often does not follow instructions and fails to finish schoolwork, chores, or duties in the workplace (not due to oppositional behavior or failure to understand instructions).
      </li>
      <li>
       Often has trouble organizing activities.
      </li>
      <li>
       Often avoids, dislikes, or doesn't want to do things that take a lot of mental effort for a long period (such as schoolwork or homework).
      </li>
      <li>
       Often loses things needed for tasks and activities (e.g. toys, school assignments, pencils, books, or tools).
      </li>
      <li>
       Is often easily distracted.
      </li>
      <li>
       Is often forgetful in daily activities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      An ADHD diagnosis is contingent upon the symptoms of impairment presenting themselves in two or more settings (e.g., at school or work and at home). There must also be clear evidence of clinically significant impairment in social, academic, or occupational functioning. Lastly, the symptoms must not occur exclusively during the course of a
      <a href="/wiki/Pervasive_developmental_disorder" title="Pervasive developmental disorder">
       pervasive developmental disorder
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">
       schizophrenia
      </a>
      , or other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychotic_disorder" title="Psychotic disorder">
       psychotic disorder
      </a>
      , and are not better accounted for by another mental disorder (e.g.,
      <a href="/wiki/Mood_disorder" title="Mood disorder">
       mood disorder
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Anxiety_disorder" title="Anxiety disorder">
       anxiety disorder
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dissociative_disorder" title="Dissociative disorder">
       dissociative disorder
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Personality_disorder" title="Personality disorder">
       personality disorder
      </a>
      ).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
       Treatment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treatment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_management" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder management">
       Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder management
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although ADHD has most often been treated with medication, medications do not cure ADHD. They are used solely to treat the symptoms associated with this disorder and the symptoms will come back once the medication stops.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmacological">
       Pharmacological
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pharmacological">

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png">
        <img alt="NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="506" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png/220px-NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png/330px-NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png/440px-NorepinephrineDopamineSerotonin.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

     <p>
      Stimulants are typically formulated in fast and slow-acting as well as short and long-acting formulations. The fast-acting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphetamine_mixed_salts_(medication)" title="Amphetamine mixed salts (medication)">
       amphetamine mixed salts
      </a>
      (Adderall) and its derivatives, with short and long-acting formulations produce reuptake inhibition of
      <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine-dopamine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor">
       dopamine and norepinephrine
      </a>
      and also increase the release of these neurotransmitters into the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synaptic_cleft" title="Synaptic cleft">
       synaptic cleft
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-junginger_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-junginger-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They may have a better
      <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">
       cardiovascular disease
      </a>
      profile than methylphenidate and potentially better tolerated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kolar_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kolar-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The fast-acting
      <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">
       methylphenidate
      </a>
      (Ritalin), with short and long-acting formulations produce
      <a href="/wiki/Dopamine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Dopamine reuptake inhibitor">
       dopamine
      </a>
      , and to a lesser extent,
      <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine-dopamine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor">
       norepinephrine reuptake inhibition
      </a>
      . In the short term,
      <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">
       methylphenidate
      </a>
      is well tolerated. However, long term studies have not been conducted in adults and concerns about increases in blood pressure have not been established.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Safety_of_therapeutic_methylphenidate_in_adults:_a_systematic_review_of_the_evidence_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Safety_of_therapeutic_methylphenidate_in_adults:_a_systematic_review_of_the_evidence-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The slow and long-acting nonstimulant
      <a href="/wiki/Atomoxetine" title="Atomoxetine">
       atomoxetine
      </a>
      (Strattera), is primarily a
      <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor">
       norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor
      </a>
      and, to a lesser extent, a
      <a href="/wiki/Dopamine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Dopamine reuptake inhibitor">
       dopamine reuptake inhibitor
      </a>
      . It may be more effective for those with predominantly inattentive concentration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atom_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-atom-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is sometimes prescribed in adults who do not get enough
      <a href="/wiki/Vigilance_(psychology)" title="Vigilance (psychology)">
       vigilant
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Attentional_control" title="Attentional control">
       concentration
      </a>
      response from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed_Amphetamine_Salts" title="Mixed Amphetamine Salts">
       mixed amphetamine salts
      </a>
      (Adderall) or get too many side effects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also approved for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADHD" title="ADHD">
       ADHD
      </a>
      by the US
      <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">
       Food and Drug Administration
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">
       Prognosis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prognosis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In some cases, children who enjoy learning may develop a sense of fear when faced with structured or planned work, especially long or group-based that requires extended focus, even if they thoroughly understand the topic. Children with ADHD may be at greater risk of academic failures and early withdrawal from school.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adhd_in_adulthood_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-adhd_in_adulthood-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Teachers and parents may make incorrect assumptions about the behaviours and attitudes of a child with ADHD-PI, and may provide them with frequent and erroneous
      <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">
       negative feedback
      </a>
      (e.g. "careless", "you're irresponsible", "you're immature", "you're lazy", "you don't care/show any effort", "you just aren't trying", etc.).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-not_lazy_stupid_or_crazy_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-not_lazy_stupid_or_crazy-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The inattentive children may realize on some level that they are somehow different internally from their peers. However, they are also likely to accept and internalize the continuous negative feedback, creating a negative
      <a href="/wiki/Self-image" title="Self-image">
       self-image
      </a>
      that becomes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-reinforcing" title="Self-reinforcing">
       self-reinforcing
      </a>
      . If these children progress into adulthood undiagnosed or untreated, their inattentiveness, ongoing frustrations, and poor self-image frequently create numerous and severe problems maintaining healthy relationships, succeeding in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postsecondary" title="Postsecondary">
       postsecondary
      </a>
      schooling, or succeeding in the workplace. These problems can compound frustrations and low
      <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">
       self-esteem
      </a>
      , and will often lead to the development of secondary pathologies including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anxiety_disorders" title="Anxiety disorders">
       anxiety disorders
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_promiscuity" title="Sexual promiscuity">
       sexual promiscuity
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mood_disorders" title="Mood disorders">
       mood disorders
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">
       substance abuse
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adhd_in_adulthood_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-adhd_in_adulthood-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been suggested
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-add_college_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-add_college-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that some of the symptoms of ADHD present in childhood appear to be less overt in adulthood. This is likely due to an adult's ability to make
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive">
       cognitive
      </a>
      adjustments and develop
      <a href="/wiki/Compensation_(psychology)" title="Compensation (psychology)">
       compensating
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coping_skill" title="Coping skill">
       coping skills
      </a>
      to minimize the impact of inattentive or hyperactive symptoms. However, the core problems of ADHD do not disappear with age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adhd_in_adulthood_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-adhd_in_adulthood-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some researchers have suggested that individuals with reduced or less overt hyperactivity symptoms should receive the ADHD-combined diagnosis. Hallowell and Ratey (2005) suggest
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that the manifestation of hyperactivity simply changes with adolescence and adulthood, becoming a more generalized restlessness or tendency to fidget.
     </p>
     <p>
      A meta-analysis of 37 studies on cognitive differences between those with ADHD-Predominantly Inattentive type and ADHD-Combined type found that "the ADHD-C subtype performed better than the ADHD-PI subtype in the areas of processing speed, attention, performance IQ, memory, and fluency. The ADHD-PI subtype performed better than the ADHD-C group on measures of flexibility, working memory, visual/spatial ability, motor ability, and language. Both the ADHD-C and ADHD-PI groups were found to perform more poorly than the control group on measures of inhibition, however, there was no difference found between the two groups. Furthermore the ADHD-C and ADHD-PI subtypes did not differ on measures of sustained attention."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane.2C_B._2004_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lane.2C_B._2004-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">
       Epidemiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      It is difficult to say exactly how many children worldwide have ADHD because different countries have used different ways of diagnosing it, while some do not diagnose it at all. In the UK, diagnosis is based on quite a narrow set of symptoms, and about 0.51% of children are thought to have attention or hyperactivity problems. In comparison, professionals in the U.S. used a much broader definition of the term ADHD until recently.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This meant up to 10% of children in the U.S. were described as having ADHD. Current estimates suggest that ADHD is present throughout the world in about 15% of the population.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      About five times more boys than girls are diagnosed with ADHD. Boys are seen as the prototypical ADHD child, therefore they are more often diagnosed with ADHD than girls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This may be partly because of the particular ways they express their difficulties. Boys and girls both have attention problems, but boys are more likely to be overactive and difficult to manage.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Children from all cultures and social groups are diagnosed with ADHD. However, children from certain backgrounds may be particularly likely to be diagnosed with ADHD, because of different expectations about how they should behave.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      It is therefore important to ensure that a child's cultural background is understood and taken into account as part of the assessment.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder_predominantly_inattentive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders#DSM-III_.281980.29" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">
       DSM-III
      </a>
      , sluggishness, drowsiness, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daydreaming" title="Daydreaming">
       daydreaming
      </a>
      were listed as characteristics of ADD without hyperactivity. These symptoms were removed from the ADHD criteria in DSM-IV even though many of those with ADHD were found to have these symptoms along with hyperactive symptoms. These distinct symptoms in the absence of hyperactivity though were described in a separate category called
      <a href="/wiki/Sluggish_cognitive_tempo" title="Sluggish cognitive tempo">
       sluggish cognitive tempo
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
