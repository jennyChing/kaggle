     <p>
      <b>
       Pierre-Jules Renard
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Jules Renard
      </b>
      (February 22, 1864  May 22, 1910) was a
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       French
      </a>
      author and member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Goncourt" title="Acadmie Goncourt">
       Acadmie Goncourt
      </a>
      , most famous for the works
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Poil_de_carotte" title="Poil de carotte">
        Poil de carotte
       </a>
      </i>
      (Carrot Top) (1894) and
      <i>
       Les Histoires Naturelles
      </i>
      (Nature Stories) (1896). Among his other works are
      <i>
       Le Plaisir de rompre
      </i>
      (The Pleasure of Breaking) (1898) and
      <i>
       Huit jours  la campagne
      </i>
      (A Week in the Country) (1906).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jules_Renard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The child of Franois Renard and Anna-Rose Colin, Renard was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2lons-du-Maine" title="Chlons-du-Maine">
       Chlons-du-Maine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mayenne" title="Mayenne">
       Mayenne
      </a>
      where his father was working on the construction of a railroad. Renard grew up in
      <a href="/wiki/Chitry-les-Mines" title="Chitry-les-Mines">
       Chitry-les-Mines
      </a>
      , (
      <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%A8vre" title="Nivre">
       Nivre
      </a>
      ). He had three older siblings including Amlie (b. 1858), who died at a young age. A second sister was also named Amlie (b. 1859). A third child, Maurice, was born before Pierre-Jules in 1862. Renard's childhood was characterized as difficult and sad ("un grand silence roux" or "a great ruddy silence"). Although he decided not to attend the prestigious
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_normale_sup%C3%A9rieure" title="cole normale suprieure">
       cole normale suprieure
      </a>
      , love of literature would eventually dominate his life. From 18851886, he served in the military in
      <a href="/wiki/Bourges" title="Bourges">
       Bourges
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">
       Life and career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jules_Renard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life and career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On April 28, 1888, Renard married Marie Morneau. He and his wife lived at 43 rue du Rocher in the 8th
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_arrondissement" title="Municipal arrondissement">
       Arrondissement
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
       Paris
      </a>
      . He began to frequent literary cafs and to contribute to Parisian newspapers. Among his steady friends were
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Capus" title="Alfred Capus">
       Alfred Capus
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Guitry" title="Lucien Guitry">
       Lucien Guitry
      </a>
      . Jules Renard wrote poems, short stories, short plays, novels and his famous
      <i>
       Poil de carotte
      </i>
      . He was elected mayor ("maire") of
      <a href="/wiki/Chitry" title="Chitry">
       Chitry
      </a>
      on May 15, 1904 as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">
       socialist
      </a>
      candidate and became a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Goncourt" title="Acadmie Goncourt">
       Acadmie Goncourt
      </a>
      in 1907, thanks to
      <a href="/wiki/Octave_Mirbeau" title="Octave Mirbeau">
       Octave Mirbeau
      </a>
      . He died of
      <a href="/wiki/Arteriosclerosis" title="Arteriosclerosis">
       arteriosclerosis
      </a>
      in Paris.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Work">
       Work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jules_Renard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Some of Jules Renard's works take their inspiration from the countryside he loved in the Nivre region. His character portraits are sharp, ironic and sometimes cruel (in his
      <i>
       Histoires naturelles
      </i>
      he humanizes animals and animalizes men) and he was an active supporter of
      <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">
       pacifism
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticlericalism" title="Anticlericalism">
       anticlericalism
      </a>
      (apparent in
      <i>
       La Bigote
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      His journal (1897 to 1910, published in 1925) is a masterpiece of introspection, irony, humor and nostalgia, and also provides an important glimpse into the literary life.
     </p>
     <p>
      The British writer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somerset_Maugham" title="Somerset Maugham">
       Somerset Maugham
      </a>
      was influenced to publish his own well-known journals by the example of Renard. In the introduction to his own work
      <i>
       A Writer's Notebook
      </i>
      , Maugham wrote an apt summary of the virtues of Renard's journal: "The journal is wonderfully good reading. It is extremely amusing. It is witty and subtle and often wise... Jules Renard jotted down neat retorts and clever phrases, epigrams, things seen, the sayings of people and the look of them, descriptions of scenery, effects of sunshine and shadow, everything, in short, that could be of use to him when he sat down to write for publication."
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Novelist" title="Novelist">
       novelist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Sorrentino" title="Gilbert Sorrentino">
       Gilbert Sorrentino
      </a>
      based his 1994 work
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Red_the_Fiend&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Red the Fiend (page does not exist)">
        Red the Fiend
       </a>
      </i>
      on Renard's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Poil_de_carotte" title="Poil de carotte">
        Poil de carotte
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      For a great part, the 2008 memoir
      <i>
       Nothing to Be Frightened Of
      </i>
      by British novelist
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_Barnes" title="Julian Barnes">
       Julian Barnes
      </a>
      is a homage to Jules Renard.
     </p>
     <p>
      Renard is one of several popular philosophers whose quotations appear on the roadsigns of
      <a href="/wiki/Himank#Road_signs" title="Himank">
       Project HIMANK
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">
       Ladakh
      </a>
      region of northern India. On one such sign in the
      <a href="/wiki/Nubra_Valley" title="Nubra Valley">
       Nubra Valley
      </a>
      , he is quoted as saying
      <i>
       Laziness is nothing more than the habit of resting before you get tired
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
