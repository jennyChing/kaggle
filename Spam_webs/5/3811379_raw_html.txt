      <p>
       <strong>
        High noon for the General Assembly
       </strong>
       <br/>
       High noon Sunday starts what could be a marathon session in the Illinois House and Senate as the two chambers meet to run through a slew of bills, resolutions and other measures on concurrence before a midnight deadline. The Senate could take up all kinds of issues as could the House, but one thing hasnt come up is a comprehensive way to fill a more than $3 billion deficit in the budget majority Democrats passed this past week. Governor Bruce Rauner said Friday he wont sign quote phony budget bills and wont support tax hikes without structural reforms. The Governor also said he doesnt plan to call an immediate special session. Instead he wants to meet with leaders and lawmakers as they can to work out solutions for the coming fiscal year that begins July 1.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Strike and lockout ban passes both chambers
       </strong>
       <br/>
       The Senate concurs with the House on a measure that, if it becomes law, would remove the ability for a union to strike or for management to lockout workers if the two parties are at an impasse in contract negotiations. Supporters say it keeps state government operating. Opponents say it is a political bill that changes the rules with other options being available. During Senate debate Saturday, Republican State Senator Matt Murphy said the measure would replace the administration, who represents the taxpayer, with an unelected arbitrator to bring about binding arbitration.
      </p>
      <p>
       This is totally out of spite toward a governor who is trying to bring back into balance the relationship between the public employees who work for the people and the taxpayers who who pay for them.
      </p>
      <p>
       Democratic Senator Don Harmon, who carried the bill in the Senate, said the strikes or the potential of a strike, forces moderation for both sides. But Harmon said the moderating influence disappears when the management side of the bargaining table is courting a potential strike.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Rhetoric vs reality; Senators debate Rauners words and actions
       </strong>
       <br/>
       Democrats used quotes Governor Bruce Rauner made several months before he was even elected in an effort to pass changes to union contract negotiations for the entire state. But Republican Senator Dale Righter said thats old rhetoric. During debate Saturday, Righter said Rauner, as Governor, has laid out his proposals to the union, which have not been countered. Righter said the Union should file a labor grievance instead of trying to change the rules during negotiations. Democratic Senator Don Harmon quoted Rauner the candidate who said there may need to be a shutdown. With a contract expiring soon, Harmon said time is of the essence and the measure would keep state departments open and functioning. Righter then quoted Harmon from earlier in their debate
      </p>
      <p>
       Senator Harmon himself said the right to strike, just like the right to lockout, is a fundamental balance between the two. Go home and answer to the other union members who may or may not be affected by this and say yes, I decided by fiat of state law that I would take away the right to strike.
      </p>
      <p>
       Harmon said the measure is a minimal way to resolve an impasse while keeping open state government for taxpayers into what will be a long summer of negotiations. Opponents of the measure said its actually an issue of the Unions not willing to work with the new Republican Governor. The measure passed the Senate on concurrence and now heads to Governors desk.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Chicago pension Senate Bill 777 passes
       </strong>
       <br/>
       Expect more debate about further delaying the City of Chicagos pension burden after a measure opponents say does just that passed the House Saturday. Confronted with previous passed measures that Republican Jeanne Ives claims caused the Windy Citys pension underfunding problems by quote kicking the can down the road, Democratic Representative Barbara Flynn Currie said its time to fix the problems.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thats what Senate Bill 777 is all about. Lets fix a problem that is facing the people of the City of Chicago let us help them sort out their issues, pay for their pensions but not to the tune of a six-hundred million tax increase year after year after year.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ives said this is the City of Chicagos problem, not the taxpayers of the state of Illinois. The measure includes provisions for revenues from a possible casino to go to pensions, something Ives said is appropriate, given the bill number.
      </p>
      <p>
       This is SB 777 becuase youre hoping for a string of lucky sevens in a casino thats a fantasy idea right now to make this work. It couldnt have been perfect for you to pick this bill number for this bill.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ives says currie is betting on a casino to cash in new revenues and isnt willing to do the hard work to pay for the obligation. Supporters ultimately said the relief doesnt take the city completely off the hook. They also says provisions in the measure increases benefits for survivors of deceased public safety pensioners. Opponents say Chicago knew the time to pay their obligations was up and they didnt plan for it appropriately. The measure passed the House and now heads to Senate for concurrence.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Incentives measure for convention space passes House
       </strong>
       <br/>
       Money from the Tourism Promotion Fund can go to municipalities or their convention center authorities for various projectsif it brings in outside businesswith a measure that passed the House Saturday. Opponents said it subsidizes the public sector at the disadvantage of the private sector. During House Debate Democrat Kenneth Dunkin said he worries the measure would take dollars meant to promote tourism in the whole state and give it to local governments. Democratic Sponsor Fred Crespo tried to ease those concerns.
      </p>
      <p>
       Potential loss of local distributive funds, property tax freezes, so theyre looking for other ways, to hey, assistance to generate other revenues. This is one of the ideas that we came up with. The key for me was as i talked with them was it had to be revenue neutral. We cant tap into existing funds. So the way we drafted this was they have to demonstrate new revenue to the state.
      </p>
      <p>
       Crespo says the measure levels the playing field and encourages outside businesses coming to the state. Republican Steven Andersson says thats not what the bill would do.
      </p>
      <p>
       It allows public venues to offer incentives and not worry about paying for them. Private venues, in order to attract this same business, will have to do the same thing, but they have to factor in the expenses of doing so into their overall profit and loss. Not so with public venues who can now just be reimbursed.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Bipartisan support, and opposition, to incentives measure
       </strong>
       <br/>
       Crespo, says there are at least nine different institutions, some he worked directly with in crafting the legislation, that would benefit from the program. But Republican David Harris said the measure is subject to appropriation and nothing has been appropriated. Harris says its a poor piece of legislation. Democrat Jack Franks says every deal has to stand on its own in the private sector. Franks stood in strict opposition.
      </p>
      <p>
       Understand what we are asking for is private industry to subsidize their publictheir public competitors. I dont see how that benefits anyone.
      </p>
      <p>
       However Republicans Joe Sosnowski and Margo McDermed stood in support, saying right now the business climate is already bad and this would help areas that are struggling. The measure now heads back to the Senate for concurrence. Thats expected Sunday, the final day for simple majorities to pass legislation.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        PTSD for Medical Marijuana
       </strong>
       <br/>
       Post Traumatic Stress Disorder could soon be added to the list of eligible ailments allowed for treatment in the Medical Cannabis Pilot Program. If signed by the Governor Senate Bill 33 would also protect an approved medical cannabis patients Firearm Owner Identification Card. Democrat Representative Lou Lang said the measure is common sense.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now you all now Im not a big fan of guns but this is about fundamental fairness. These people dont want to go out and buy a gun, they just dont want to lose their FOID cards.
      </p>
      <p>
       Senate Bill 33 passed 67 to 36 and now heads to the Governors desk. Lang passed other measures that would extend the pilot program for four years after the first dispensaries open. The program got a late start in the transition from Governor Pat Quinn to Governor Bruce Rauner earlier this year  the second year of a four year program. So far, there are no operating dispensaries.
      </p>
