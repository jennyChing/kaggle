       <h1 class="main-title" xmlns:fn="http://www.w3.org/2005/xpath-functions">
        How to Boil Eggs Perfectly Every Time
       </h1>
       <h2 class="subhead" xmlns:fn="http://www.w3.org/2005/xpath-functions">
        Boiling an egg may not be rocket science, but timing is important because the trick to perfectly cooked eggs is actually NOT to boil the eggs at all. Here are some simple guidelines for cooking soft-, medium-, and hard-cooked eggs.
       </h2>
       <div class="row" xmlns:fn="http://www.w3.org/2005/xpath-functions">
        <div class="nine columns">
         <!--Lead Image-->
         <a href="/videos/how-to-boil-an-egg.aspx">
          <img alt="how to boil an egg" src="/CMS/uploadedImages/Images/Cooking/Articles/Issues_81-90/fc83kt071-02_xlg.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <div class="caption">
          Watch our
          <a href="http://www.finecooking.com/videos/how-to-boil-an-egg.aspx" target="_self" title="Test Kitchen video">
           Test Kitchen video
          </a>
          to learn how to time your cooking to get perfectly soft-, medium-, or hard-cooked eggs.
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="three columns">
         <p class="attribution">
          <em>
           by Allison Ehri Kreitler
          </em>
         </p>
         <p class="attribution">
          <em>
           fromFine Cooking
          </em>
          <br/>
          Issue 83
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="row first article_content" xmlns:fn="http://www.w3.org/2005/xpath-functions">
        <div class="twelve columns">
         <div>
          <h5>
           Getting started
          </h5>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           Put the eggs in a saucepan and add enough cold water to cover them by about 1 inch. Set the pan over medium-high heat and as soon as the water reaches a brisk simmer, start timing. As the eggs cook, adjust the heat as needed to maintain a brisk simmer. (Though we talk about hard-boiled eggsand were using that term herethe fact is that cooking eggs in boiling water cracks the shell and makes the eggs tough and rubbery. A simmer works much better.)
          </p>
          <p>
           <font color="#1b6f79">
            <strong>
             From Fine Cooking's Test Kitchen:
            </strong>
           </font>
           Watch our
           <a href="/videos/how-to-boil-an-egg.aspx" target="_self" title="How to Boil an Egg video">
            How to Boil an Egg video
           </a>
           to see an expert turn out soft-, medium-, and hard-cooked eggs without overcooking them.
          </p>
          <!--Vertical Image-->
          <div class="image-vertical">
           <a>
            <img alt="soft-cooked egg" src="/CMS/uploadedImages/Images/Cooking/Articles/Issues_81-90/fc83kt071-04_ld.jpg"/>
           </a>
           <div class="caption">
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <font color="#1b6f79">
            <strong>
             Soft boiled: 2 minutes
            </strong>
           </font>
           The white is solid, but the yolk is still runny. Serve in an egg cup for breakfast. Use the side of a small spoon to crack and remove the pointed end of the egg, making a hole in the shell large enough to fit the spoon. Or use egg scissors, if you have them.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <div class="ym" id="ym_813551370984169144">
          </div>
          <script type="text/javascript">

          <!--Vertical Image-->
          <div class="image-vertical">
           <a>
            <img alt="medium cooked egg" src="/CMS/uploadedImages/Images/Cooking/Articles/Issues_81-90/fc83kt071-03_ld.jpg"/>
           </a>
           <div class="caption">
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <font color="#1b6f79">
            <strong>
             Medium boiled: 4-1/2 minutes
            </strong>
           </font>
           The yolk is solid but still dark orange-yellow, moist, and dense in the middle. Beautiful and delicious quartered on a salad.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>

          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div style="display:none;">
          <!--Vertical Image-->
          <div class="image-vertical">
           <a>
            <img alt="hard cooked egg" src="/CMS/uploadedImages/Images/Cooking/Articles/Issues_81-90/fc83kt071-02_ld.jpg"/>
           </a>
           <div class="caption">
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           <font color="#1b6f79">
            <strong>
             Hard boiled: 8 minutes
            </strong>
           </font>
           The yolk is completely solid, light yellow, and crumbly, with no sign of the telltale green or gray ring around the yolk thats caused by overcooking. Perfect for egg salad or deviled eggs. Need more ideas for what to do with hard-cooked eggs?
           <a href="/slideshows/leftover-hard-boiled-egg-recipes.aspx" target="_self" title="View the slideshow Hard-Cooked, Super Easy">
            View the slideshow Hard-Boiled, Super Easy
           </a>
           to get recipe ideas.
          </p>
          <div class="ym" id="ym_813551370984169144">
          </div>
          <script type="text/javascript">

          </script>
          <h5>
           Peeling eggs
          </h5>
          <p>
           When the eggs are cooked, carefully pour out most of the hot water, leaving the eggs in the pan. Set the pan in the sink under cool running water for a few minutes until the eggs are barely warm. If the shells are stubborn, try peeling them under running water. The fresher the egg, the more attached the shell, so for boiling, older eggs are preferable.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div style="display:none;">
          <div class="panel">
           <h5>
            The boil-and-walk-away method
           </h5>
           <p>
            For another way to hard-boil eggs, begin as directed at left with the eggs in cold water, but once the water reaches a brisk simmer, turn off the heat and let the eggs sit uncovered in the hot water for at least 10 minutes and up to 30 minutesthe water cools gradually, preventing the eggs from overcooking. This is a great method when youre multitasking and cant pay careful attention to the eggs.
           </p>
          </div>
