                      Hebrew Inked Celebs: David Beckham
                     </a>
                    </h1>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content">
                     <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Beckham">
                      David Beckham
                     </a>
                     , the celebrity football player and bearer of several multi-language tattoos, got a Hebrew tattoo to display his love of his wife Victoria. Or did he?
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/Sux4H7TwnnI/AAAAAAAAAUU/H59Wr5p3-d4/s1600-h/beckham-tattoo.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5398822130885369458" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/Sux4H7TwnnI/AAAAAAAAAUU/H59Wr5p3-d4/s320/beckham-tattoo.jpg" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 320px; height: 133px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     This tattoo is a correct rendition of a bible verse
                     <a href="http://scripturetext.com/songs/6-3.htm">
                      <span class="redheading">
                       Song of Solomon 6:3
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     , it says
                     <span style="font-weight: bold;">
                      "I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine. He browses among the lilies"
                     </span>
                     .
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Does something seems amiss? You bet. This tattoo speaks of a girl's love to her male beloved. It unmistakeably refers to a man. The bad Hebrew tattoo strikes again.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Guys (the straight ones), do keep away from this verse!

                     <a href="http://www.badhebrew.com/2009/10/abstract-art-of-hebrew-tattoo.html">
                      The Abstract Art of Hebrew Tattoo
                     </a>
                    </h1>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content">
                     Today we have something... different:
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuoNuHhGVOI/AAAAAAAAAUM/KtPdVrVQkgM/s1600-h/love.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5398142189299979490" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuoNuHhGVOI/AAAAAAAAAUM/KtPdVrVQkgM/s320/love.gif" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 240px; height: 320px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Where is the bad Hebrew tattoo, you ask? What's the deal with this piece of abstract expressionism, you say?
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     This is our Hebrew tattoo. Yes really!
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     In fact, this tattoo that was supposed to say "Love" in Hebrew. It seems that the artist got somewhat carried away with tweaking the script, rendering the letters totally irrecognizable. Ugly, too.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Unless you
                     <span style="font-style: italic;">
                      really
                     </span>
                     know what you're looking for, this tattoo is for all intents and purposes just a random collection of lines.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Now, this is how a sane person  writes "Love" (pronounced "Ahava" in Hebrew):
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuoNtjrUVwI/AAAAAAAAAT8/bLJytK-ZS4Q/s1600-h/love-correct.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5398142179679164162" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuoNtjrUVwI/AAAAAAAAAT8/bLJytK-ZS4Q/s320/love-correct.gif" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 169px; height: 81px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Do you see the resemblance now? No? Here's a visual aid.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Take a few minutes, squint, turn your head this way and that way, it'll come to you eventually. Maybe.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuoNtyg8cCI/AAAAAAAAAUE/OJLE2l07xao/s1600-h/love-overlay.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5398142183662186530" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuoNtyg8cCI/AAAAAAAAAUE/OJLE2l07xao/s320/love-overlay.gif" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 240px; height: 320px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     By the way, in Hebrew, you're not supposed to cut a word in half either!

                    <h1 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://www.badhebrew.com/2009/10/this-is-why-unique-is-overrated.html">
                      This is why Unique is Overrated
                     </a>
                    </h1>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content">
                     Today we have a true feat of originality.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     This girl wanted the expression "
                     <span style="font-weight: bold;">
                      We are all born unique, but most die a copy
                     </span>
                     ", written in Hebrew across her back. Those of you who regularly visit Bad Hebrew Tattoos can probably tell, this kind of thing never ends well.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I must admit that I was delighted to find a tattoo disaster that isn't of religious nature! And boy, does this one deliver...
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/Sudas7NH9oI/AAAAAAAAATg/n0vagoDn8-0/s1600-h/como-copias.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5397382406280312450" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/Sudas7NH9oI/AAAAAAAAATg/n0vagoDn8-0/s320/como-copias.gif" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 296px; height: 275px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Basically, her tattoo says:
                     <br/>
                     <span style="font-weight: bold;">
                      "We are allborn only but many stamping tool as to copying"
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                     </span>
                     This Hebrew tattoo has many mistakes, but the worst one, by far, is the translation of the word "die".
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     In English die means:
                     <span style="font-style: italic;">
                      To cease to live; undergo the complete and permanent cessation of all vital functions; become dead.
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Unfortunately, it isn't the only meaning of "die". There are quite a few others. Such as:
                     <span style="font-style: italic;">
                      An engraved stamp for impressing a design upon some softer material, as in coining money.
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Our miserable victim somehow picked the wrong meaning of die. What actually got translated to Hebrew is the stamping tool.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Other errors include:
                     <ol>
                      <li>
                       Conjoined words. There should be a space between "We are all" and "born".
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       "Unique" translated as "Only". These two words are of close meaning in English, in Hebrew you can't use one instead of the other.
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       "Copying" - The tattoo says "Maatik", which is the act of copying, taking place right now.
                       <br/>
                      </li>
                     </ol>
                     <br/>
                     This is how you do it right:
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SudgrBJUPNI/AAAAAAAAATw/pcf3v8oqaLk/s1600-h/born-unique-die-copy.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5397388970584980690" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SudgrBJUPNI/AAAAAAAAATw/pcf3v8oqaLk/s320/born-unique-die-copy.gif" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 320px; height: 33px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     The summary of today's lesson: Unless you speak fluent Hebrew, never attempt translating a complex expression all by yourself. Automatic translation services don't count as qualified help either.

                     </a>
                    </h1>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content">
                     Tattooing your loved one's name in Hebrew is a very romantic gesture. Getting it wrong - not so much.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuILfYs6ZlI/AAAAAAAAATI/RirskRUamXw/s1600-h/brian-angie.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5395887937377953362" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuILfYs6ZlI/AAAAAAAAATI/RirskRUamXw/s320/brian-angie.gif" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 320px; height: 227px;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Today's victim wanted a Hebrew tattoo of his own name (Brian, on the right) and the name of his girl (Angie - on the left), unfortunately, he managed to misspell them both.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Brian - There's only one mistake, but a crucial one. He used the regular Nun insead of the final form (Nun Sofit). For letters which have a final form, the final form should always be used at the end of a word.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Angie - Some creative spelling here. 3 mistakes in one small word, phenomenal.
                     <br/>
                     <ol>
                      <li>
                       In English, G can be read two ways, as in "game" or as in "gentle". In old Hebrew, the sound  G as in "gentle" or "Angie", didn't exist. In modern Hebrew it was artificially added by putting an apostrophe right after the letter Gimel. Do you see an apostrophe in that tattoo?
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       The author had a hard time figuring out the "ie" part of "Angie", and decided to use the Nikkud (vowels - look for dots in the tattoo) as if they were letters. Bad idea. In Hebrew you only apply vowels to letters, you
                       <span style="font-style: italic;">
                        never
                       </span>
                       use them as a standalone, never apply them to an empty space. Overall, don't attempt Nikkud unless you know exactly what you're doing. Most native Hebrew speakers don't dare mess with it, so you shouldn't either!
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Instead of that Nikkud disaster, the letter Yod should be used for the "ie" in "Angie".
                      </li>
                     </ol>
                     <br/>
                     This is how Brian is supposed to be written in proper Hebrew:
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SuIWeoN-AZI/AAAAAAAAATQ/TyvPmBrr7bg/s1600-h/brian.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">

                     </a>

                     A word of caution. YHWH is one of the most potentially offensive Hebrew tattoos. It's God's most sacred name and mustn't be used in vain. Jewish people don't pronounce it as is, not even in prayer, so think good and hard before you decide to tattoo YHWH on yourself.

                     </div>

                     Today's victim thinks he has the name "Patrick" tattooed in Hebrew on his arm. He is quite wrong.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SszMGvQmYYI/AAAAAAAAAQ4/BFy0G3m9T1I/s1600-h/PATRICK.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">

                     </a>
                    </h1>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content">
                     "Worship" is not an easy word to translate, there is a certain gap in meaning between English and Hebrew that pretty much guarantees that you'll end up with something wonky. Your translation could end up as "Job" or "Ritual" or many other things that are not quite right.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>

                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__uESgwNhwQs/SspANNhCNsI/AAAAAAAAAQw/W9xMpSkvr6A/s1600-h/pure.gif" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">

                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Not a very manly tattoo, but not such a bad thing either. It's more of an oddity. I've no idea how anyone managed to connect this word with "Worship".
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Now, this is my translation of "Worship", it literally means "God's Worship". When it comes to Hebrew, you want to spell out exactly who it is that you're worshiping.

                     <br/>

                     <br/>
