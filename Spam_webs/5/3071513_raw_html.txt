     <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
      Egypt
     </h2>
     <div class="paragraph" style="display:block;">
      Visiting Egypt was a new experience in a few ways for me. For one, it was my first time setting afoot in Africa, and visiing the fabled Sahara desert. And more interestingly, it was the first time I signed up to travel in an organised tour, rather than venturing out backpacker-style.
      <br/>
      <span>
      </span>
      <br/>
      <span>
      </span>
      From the start, I was already in for a pleasant surprise, for the agency had given us passes to a premium lounge to wait for our flight. It had a decent buffet, free flow of drinks and alcohol, and internet and magazines to boot! I would have stayed longer, if not for the anticipation of heading off to Egypt.
      <br/>
      <span>
      </span>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       <u>
        Travelling back in time to the Ancient Egyptian Empire
       </u>
      </strong>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      <span>
      </span>
      The itinerary was jam packed with sights to see and things to do. Upon touching down in Cairo, we started off with a whirlwind tour of ancient Egyptian history at the Egyptian Museum (with a quick glance at
      <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-middle-east-20558185" target="_blank" title="">
       protestersoccupying Tahrir Square
      </a>
      ), and then it was off to the Great Pyramids of Giza to get our minds blown. We had a quick rundown on the local scam artistry, before getting out of the safezonethat was the bus. We were warned not to allow any stranger to help us take any photo, or let them tie scarves around us, or even snap a photo with their camels in the background. All these would be grounds for incessant demands for tips. This would be a recurring theme that surface over and over again throughout our journey.
      <br/>
      <span>
      </span>
      <br/>
      <span>
      </span>
      From the Great Pyramid, it was a short drive to a vantage point where we could see all three pyramids lined up and ready for a postcard shot. Shaking off the relentless vendors, we took our photos and went on a camel ride, which turned out to a ananticlimactic"hop and a skip" to a random point 10 meters away, and then back to the start point. Ah well, it was probably for the best. It felt like the camels were way too unsteady and overworked. From there, we headed to the Sphinx and enjoyed the first of many brilliant sunsets that would blaze across the horizon every evening.
      <br/>

     <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
      The following day started with an early wake up call, as we staggered to the airport for an early morning flight to Aswan. We passed by the Aswan High Dam, a massive marvel of engineering that controls the seasonal Nile flooding and generates a whole lot of electrical power. After a few disinterested photos, we board a boat to the island temple of Philae, which kinda reminded me of an Indiana Jones set, complete with a lake teeming with crocs!
      <br/>
      <br/>
      In the afternoon. we checked into our Nile Cruise, our home for the next 3 nights until it reached Luxor. The next day, we embarked on a 3 hour bus ride way down south to Abu Simbel, to see the imposing temple of the samje name. Ramses II built it to send a stern warning to the Nubian invaders, and till today, his threat still lingers in the 4 hardened statues staring outwards.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      The cruise is probably worth a mention, as we took in the sights of two colossal temples, Kom Ombo and Edfu. Mere words (well actually, it's my listless writing) do not do justice to the magnificence of the temples, such is the vision and grandeur of these hallowed halls built to impress all for eternity. Of course, having a lively and knowledgeable guide brings the writings on the wall to life, quite literally!

     <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
      Upon reaching Luxor, we went into Ancient Egypt overload mode. Our first view of the city was literally a bird's eye view from a hot air balloon, which took off from the desert, stayed up for the sunrise over the horizon, then floated across the fertileriverbanksof the Nile and eventually settling down onsomeone'sfarm. The ride was thoroughly enjoyable, but the landing was memorable. You see, we landed on a barren piece of farmland (which was no easy feat!), only to be accosted by, in turns, a group of young lads, then an old lady with a stick, and lastly by a young man. And they didn't seem happy to see their private land being used as a landing strip. Eventually our van arrived and we were hustled out by the balloon operators, who worked quickly to keep the balloon. But later on our guide assured us that it was all an'act'. In fact, landing on some poor farmer's land is akin to striking lottery, as they would demand compensation for damaged crops and disturbing their grounds. Theangrierthey get, the more they can demand from the hot air balloon company.Well I'll be damned!
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Back on solid ground, we started for the Valley of the Kings, within which lie the burial tombs of over a hundred pharaohs, each tomb containing the material wealth and sacred knowledge during their respective reigns. King Tut's tomb is here too! And don't worry, the legend of the curse has been
      <a href="http://history.howstuffworks.com/history-vs-myth/king-tut-tomb1.htm" style="" target="_blank" title="">
       thoroughly and scientifically debunked
      </a>
      . Nearby is the Temple of Queen Hatshepsut, which comes with a
      <a href="http://www.eyelid.co.uk/Hatshepsut.htm" style="" target="_blank" title="">
       back-story
      </a>
      of a woman who ruled in a man's position, an eventual struggle for power, and the fury of a vengeful young pharaoh.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Luxor is the city for two important temples, the sprawling Karnak Temple, and the multi-religious Luxor Temple. Even though we were suffering from Egyptofatigue (don't Google it, I just made that up), the temples were still impressive places to explore. Karnak was the
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karnak" style="" target="_blank" title="">
       film set for countless movies
      </a>
      , such is its scale and expanse, as it had been built upon and expanded by a string of pharaohs. Luxor temple, on the other hand, is a lot smaller, but quirky in it's own way - it has a 12th century mosque built atop the ruins of the ancient temple, and in the far end, a section of the temple had been converted into a Roman church. It says something about the history of Egypt, and how it's fortunes had risen and waned over the pastmillennium.

     <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
      <strong>
       <u>
        The fun activities start!
       </u>
      </strong>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      After Luxor, the itinerary certainly got more active. We flew to Sharm El Sheikh, a classy resort townby the Red Sea, to even out our tans and catch some coral reefs in action. And boy, the waters are really clear and teeming with life! However, we didn't travel all the way to the Sinai Peninsular to soak up the sun. Nooooo. We went to scale the heights of Mount Sinai, in the footsteps of Moses, and catch the sunrise from the summit. And that, I have a lot to say about.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      First off, we woke up at 1am to prepare for the climb. The estimate was 3.5 hours up, so we started our ascent at 2, led by a Bedouin guide who didn't seem bothered by the cold, or the slippery sand, or even the steep slopes. Nope, he just kept on walking, and walking, and walking. Like
      <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_rTJ1M07yw3w/TUQCelNH-fI/AAAAAAAAEEs/KOxk-UhDKLc/s1600/energizer-bunny.jpg" target="_blank" title="">
       this little guy.
      </a>
      Fortunately, the entrepreneurial locals set up 4 rest stations/cafeterias along the way to give weary climbers a respite from the cold, the fatigue and to catch up with the guide. And for a pretty penny, you could rent a camel to ride up near the summit, so you would be fresh and ready to climb up 750 stairs. Yes, stairs, the dangerous gravelly kind without railings or safety nets. I cannot emphasize this enough, a slip and I might fall a few a few meters down a steep slope if I'm lucky. If not... Well, they really should have given us parachutes for a falsesenseof assurance.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      My wonder is directed at the locals, who restock their cafeterias every day. While the prices may be daylight robbery (really, $1 forusing a makeshift toilet?!), the combined donkey and human effort to bring up bottled water and canned drinks is admirable. And after the scrambling up the last few steps, we were greeted by the picturesque scenery of the mountain range beneath us. in the distance, the sun peeked out gingerly over the horizon, and then gradually emerging over the mountains, to rapturous applause from a captive audience. This was unlike the hot air balloon experience. We had put in an incredible amount of effort for the climb, and the sunrise was a crowning reward to ourendeavour.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      The magical moment over, we had to climb down. Now here's the part you don't realise at first... Climbing up was pretty easy, you don't get to see how high it really is. Climbing down, on the other hand, was terrifying. The steps are steep, people are slipping down from the well-worn surfaces, and the path is so narrow everyone is pushing onwards to get down quick. My only advice is to look down at the stairs and go at it, one step at a time.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Mythighswere ached so much the next two days, it hurtevery timewe had to board or disembark the tour bus.

      The last major highlight was the Bahariya Oasis and the 4WD drive out into the White Desert. The oasis is was pretty mundane - think a large town in the middle of the desert, not a Aladdinesque watering hole with palm trees and a group of wild animals lapping from it. TheWhiteDesert was something else though. Driving out into the Sahara for almost 3 hours, we came across a variety of landscapes - the volcanic rock strewn over the Black Desert, the mysterious Crystal Mountain with tiny crystals scattered all over the sand, the unearthly white rocky floor of the White Desert, the steep dune filled with soft silky sand, and the windswept mushroom rock formations that popped out right in the middle of nowhere. At night, we had a Bedouin camp BBQ dinner in the middle of the desert under a moonless starry sky. I swore that was my best star gazing ever, such that pointing out satellites and the Milky Way was almost effortless.And with all that sensory overload, somehow I still vividly taking a leak in the desert, with the sunset (and everyone else) in the far distance. Weird.

      <br/>
      <br/>
      In hindsight, taking a tour was probably the best means to visit the Egypt you would expect to experience, e.g. visiting the Valley of the Kings, taking a lazy cruise down the Nile, going on excursions into the middle of nowhere, useful tips to avoid geting ripped off or scammed etc. Even if I did it without a package tour, I would possibly have to pay even more to sign up for a local short tour in Egypt. Dynasty Travel and Temotours, the local agent, planned a comprehensive and exciting itinerary worth ever penny, and coming from me, it says a lot! Unless you just want to experience the daily life in an Egyptian city, a package tour is probably the best way to see Egypt.
      <br/>

      The itinerary wasundoubtedlyvery comprehensive and well planned, pampering the lazy slob in me, yet exciting theinquisitivetraveller's spirit looking for an adventure.
