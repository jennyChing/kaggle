      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Google Cardboard Is Google's Awesomely Weird Answer To Virtual Reality
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">
        Comment Now
       </a>
       <div class="comments_follow followable_block">
        <div class="followable_block initializing" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Google Cardboard Is Google's Awesomely Weird Answer To Virtual Reality&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/1360-26477" data-target-name="" data-target-type="article" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow" data-type-advoice="false">
         <div class="follow_unireg">
          <a class="follow_unireg_link" href="javascript://follow">
           <span class="option_follow">
            <span class="icon">
            </span>
            <span class="text">
             Follow Comments
            </span>
           </span>
           <span class="option_following">
            <span class="icon">

             Following Comments
            </span>
           </span>
           <span class="option_unfollow">

            <span class="text">

            </span>
           </span>

          <span class="follow_count">
          </span>
          <span class="loadingstate">
           <img alt="" class="loadingstateimg" height="16" src="http://i.forbesimg.com/assets/img/loading_spinners/16px_on_transparent.gif" width="16"/>
          </span>

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <figure class="featured_image">
         <img alt="" itemprop="image" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/erikkain/files/2014/06/Screenshot-92.png"/>
         <figcaption>
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         Theres a lot going on in the virtual reality space lately.
        </p>
        <p>
         The up-and-coming Oculus Rift VR headset was recently
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/briansolomon/2014/03/25/facebook-buys-oculus-virtual-reality-gaming-startup-for-2-billion/">
          purchased by Facebook
         </a>
         to the tune of $2 billion. And PlayStation 4 maker
         <a href="/companies/sony/">
          Sony
         </a>
         <span class="quotecard_hook" data-exchange="null" data-naturalid="fred/company/4017" data-quotes-closing="0.0" data-quotes-now="0.0" data-ticker="null" data-type="organization">
          <span class="wrapper">
           <a href="/companies/sony/">
            Sony
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         announced their own VR headset, Project Morpheus,
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/davidthier/2014/03/18/sony-announces-virtual-reality-headset-for-ps4/">
          this year at GDC
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Everyones been waiting for the other big players in gadgets and video games to unveil their own, and while
         <a href="/companies/microsoft/">
          Microsoft
         </a>
         <span class="quotecard_hook" data-exchange="NASDAQ" data-naturalid="fred/company/2854" data-quotes-closing="45.53" data-quotes-now="45.74" data-ticker="MSFT" data-type="organization">
          <span class="wrapper">
           <a href="/companies/microsoft/">
            Microsoft
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         didnt show anything at E3,
         <a href="/companies/google/">
          Google
         </a>
         <span class="quotecard_hook" data-exchange="NASDAQ" data-naturalid="fred/company/1821" data-quotes-closing="571.71" data-quotes-now="587.8725" data-ticker="GOOGL" data-type="organization">
          <span class="wrapper">
           <a href="/companies/google/">
            Google
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         revealed their own VR solution
         <a href="https://www.google.com/events/io">
          today at I/O 2014
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Googles answer to VR? Cardboard.
        </p>
        <p>
         Or, rather, a DIY cardboard headset equipped with cheap lenses into which you slide your smartphone. Simply
         <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.google.samples.apps.cardboarddemo">
          download the Cardboard app
         </a>
         and youre ready to roll, all for a few bucks in materials.
        </p>
        <p>
         Which islets face itabout as weird an idea as anyone could dream up, but also sort of brilliant; a truly open solution to virtual reality that hearkens back to Googles roots and commitment to open development. And one that sidesteps a lot of the challenges facing other VR gadgets by utilizing the ubiquitous smartphone.
        </p>
        <p>
         The actual construction of the cardboard VR headset will probably deter many would-be virtual-reality-goers, but it really doesnt look difficult at all for anyone with a knack for putting stuff together. Google has all the instructions here, and the materials you need are pretty basic:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="ingredients" class=" size-full wp-image-26482" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/erikkain/files/2014/06/ingredients.png"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Almost certainly kits and fully-assembled versions of this will be available for sale withindays? Hours? Soon, in any case. I suspect well even see more expensive, less disposable iterations pop up. After all, this is sort of the point: What Google is doing with Cardboard is opening up the possibilities of VR to basically anyone.
        </p>
        <p>
         They even have an experimental toolkit available so developers can start playing around with Android-based VR basically right now. You can read more about that, check out instructions, and more
         <a href="https://developers.google.com/cardboard/">
          at the Cardboard website.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Its all pretty ingenious.
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/facebook-ipo/">
          Facebook
         </a>
         buys Oculus for $2 billion, Sony must have spent millions in development of the Morpheus headset, and Google says, Hey check out this cardboard thing-a-muh-whatsit, it can turn your phone into a VR headset for pennies on the dollar.
        </p>
        <p>
         I havent tried to build one yet, though I think my Galaxy Note 3 would be both A) a great device for VR and B) probably require larger dimensions than the standard Cardboard design. But from what the internet tells me, the thing actually works remarkably well.
         <a href="http://gizmodo.com/turn-your-android-into-a-virtual-reality-headset-with-g-1596026538">
          Heres Gizmodos Brent Rose
         </a>
         :
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          You hold the box up to your face, and suddenly youre looking at a horizontal row of applications. To scroll back and forth, you just turn your head. Oh and you see that little washer thats held to the side? Thats how you click on things! You slide it down in its little cardboard slot and it pops back up. It feels like a old school view-finder.
         </p>
         <p>
          I used it to look into the Pixar-like world of
          <a href="http://gizmodo.com/how-moto-tech-and-pixar-brains-turn-phones-into-storyti-1536727724">
           Windy Day
          </a>
          , and it was incredibly immersive. Responsive, too. The gyroscopes in the phone worked very well, and as I turned my head, the world turned with me with no lag (that I was able to detect in the 30 seconds I was in there).
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         <img alt="Cardboard 2" class=" size-full wp-image-26481" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/erikkain/files/2014/06/Cardboard-2.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         While this may not be the innovation that brings big, expansive, immersive worlds to virtual reality gaming, it may be the innovation that brings virtual reality gaming to consumers much more quickly than other solutions. The bar to development and distribution is supremely low.
        </p>
        <p>
         While the major challenge facing Morpheus and Oculus is will be getting the device into consumers hands at a reasonable price, Cardboard will face no such obstacle. Simply getting the right screens has been one of the biggest hurdles for Oculus; but for Cardboard, the screens are already here. Consumers wont have to buy them twice.
        </p>
        <p>
         And while a consumer base is essential for development of a robust game catalogthe vicious circle of consumer base and contentdevelopment on Android of simple, casual VR applications and games will be cheap and low-risk.
        </p>

         <div class="comment_content">
          <p>
           Eric,
          </p>
          <p>
           I have a Note 3 as well and just finished my homebuilt cardboard. Here are some notes to assist you when you build yours.
          </p>
          <p>
           1) I had a difficult time finding the proper cardboard. I used a Elmers tri-fold display which proved too thick. You can make it work but you should look for something thinner. Maybe ordering the kraft board listed on the cardboard is a better idea.
          </p>
          <p>
           2) The note 3 will fit, barely. I had to cut out holes to keep the volume buttons and power from being actuated while in the unit. The good news is it will not fall out.
          </p>
          <p>
           3) One of the demos Windy Day the 3d is off giving you a dual image rather than a 3d effect. This may be due to the larger screen size and the cardboard design may need to be modified by widening the distance between the lenses.
          </p>
          <p>
           All in all its a fun project. It might need some tweaks if you cut your own. Hopefully someone will put out a kit similar to what Google put out at IO.
          </p>
