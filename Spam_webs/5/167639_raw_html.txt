      <b>
       State terrorism
      </b>
      refers to acts of

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=State_terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definition_of_terrorism" title="Definition of terrorism">
       Definition of terrorism
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terror" title="Terror">
       Terror
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      There is neither an academic nor an international legal consensus regarding the proper definition of the word "terrorism".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson-38_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Williamson-38-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many scholars believe that the actions of governments can be labeled "terrorism".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, using the term 'terrorism' to mean violent action used with the predominant intention of causing terror,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_James_(academic)" title="Paul James (academic)">
       Paul James
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Friedman" title="Jonathan Friedman">
       Jonathan Friedman
      </a>
      distinguish between state terrorism against
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-combatants" title="Non-combatants">
       non-combatants
      </a>
      and state terrorism against
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combatants" title="Combatants">
       combatants
      </a>
      , including '
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_and_Awe" title="Shock and Awe">
       Shock and Awe
      </a>
      ' tactics:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        Shock and Awe as a subcategory of rapid dominance is the name given to massive intervention designed to strike terror into the minds of the enemy. It is a form of state-terrorism. The concept was however developed long before the Second Gulf War by Harlan Ullman as chair of a forum of retired military personnel.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          9
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      However, others, including governments, international organizations, private institutions and scholars, believe that the term is only applicable to the actions of
      <a href="/wiki/Violent_non-state_actor" title="Violent non-state actor">
       violent non-state actors
      </a>
      . Historically, the term terrorism was used to refer to actions taken by governments against their own citizens whereas now it is more often perceived as targeting of non-combatants as part of a strategy directed
      <i>
       against
      </i>
      governments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Williamson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Historian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Commager" title="Henry Commager">
       Henry Commager
      </a>
      wrote that "Even when definitions of terrorism allow for
      <i>
       state terrorism
      </i>
      , state actions in this area tend to be seen through the prism of war or national self-defense, not terror.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While states may accuse other states of
      <a href="/wiki/State-sponsored_terrorism" title="State-sponsored terrorism">
       state-sponsored terrorism
      </a>
      when they support insurgencies, individuals who accuse their governments of terrorism are seen as radicals, because actions by legitimate governments are not generally seen as illegitimate. Academic writing tends to follow the definitions accepted by states.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most states use the term "terrorism" for non-state actors only.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Schmid-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Online" title="Encyclopdia Britannica Online">
       Encyclopdia Britannica Online
      </a>
      defines terrorism generally as "the systematic use of violence to create a general climate of fear in a population and thereby to bring about a particular political objective", and states that "terrorism is not legally defined in all jurisdictions." The encyclopedia adds that "[e]stablishment terrorism, often called state or state-sponsored terrorism, is employed by governments -- or more often by factions within governments -- against that government's citizens, against factions within the government, or against foreign governments or groups."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-britannica-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While the most common modern usage of the word terrorism refers to civilian-victimizing political violence by
      <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">
       insurgents
      </a>
      or conspirators,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      several scholars make a broader interpretation of the nature of terrorism that encompasses the concepts of state terrorism and state-sponsored terrorism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Stohl" title="Michael Stohl">
       Michael Stohl
      </a>
      argues, "The use of terror tactics is common in international relations and the state has been and remains a more likely employer of terrorism within the international system than insurgents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Stohl clarifies, however, that "[n]ot all acts of state violence are terrorism. It is important to understand that in terrorism the violence threatened or perpetrated, has purposes broader than simple physical harm to a victim. The audience of the act or threat of violence is more important than the immediate victim."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scholar
      <a href="/wiki/C._Augustus_Martin" title="C. Augustus Martin">
       Gus Martin
      </a>
      describes state terrorism as terrorism "committed by governments and quasi-governmental agencies and personnel against perceived threats", which can be directed against both domestic and foreign targets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Martin-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
       Noam Chomsky
      </a>
      defines state terrorism as "terrorism practised by states (or governments) and their agents and allies".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jeffrey_A._Sluka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jeffrey A. Sluka (page does not exist)">
       Jeffrey A. Sluka
      </a>
      has described Noam Chomsky and
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_S._Herman" title="Edward S. Herman">
       Edward S. Herman
      </a>
      as pioneers in academic studies about state terrorism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Stohl and
      <a href="/wiki/George_A._Lopez" title="George A. Lopez">
       George A. Lopez
      </a>
      have designated three categories of state terrorism, based on the openness/secrecy with which the alleged terrorist acts are performed, and whether states directly perform the acts, support them, or acquiesce to them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=State_terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      wrote critically of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terror" title="Terror">
       terror
      </a>
      employed by
      <a href="/wiki/Tyrant" title="Tyrant">
       tyrants
      </a>
      against their subjects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The earliest use of the word
      <i>
       terrorism
      </i>
      identified by the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
      </i>
      is a 1795 reference to tyrannical state behavior, the "
      <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">
       reign of terrorism
      </a>
      " in France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In that same year,
      <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">
       Edmund Burke
      </a>
      famously decried the "thousands of those hell-hounds called terrorists" who he believed threatened Europe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">
       Reign of Terror
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobin_Club" title="Jacobin Club">
       Jacobin
      </a>
      government and other factions of the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      used the apparatus of the state to kill and intimidate political opponents, and the Oxford English Dictionary includes as one definition of terrorism "Government by intimidation carried out by the party in power in France between 1789-1794".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teichman_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-teichman-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The original general meaning of terrorism was of terrorism by the state, as reflected in the 1798 supplement of the Dictionnaire of the
      <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Acadmie franaise">
       Acadmie franaise
      </a>
      , which described terrorism as
      <i>
       systeme
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       regime de la terreur
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dr. Myra Williamson wrote: "The meaning of terrorism has undergone a transformation. During the reign of terror a regime or system of terrorism was used as an instrument of governance, wielded by a recently established revolutionary state against the enemies of the people. Now the term terrorism" is commonly used to describe terrorist acts committed by
      <i>
       non-state or subnational entities
      </i>
      against a state." (italics in original)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Williamson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Later examples of state terrorism include the police state measures employed by the Soviet Union beginning in the 1930s, and by Germany's
      <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">
       Nazi regime
      </a>
      in the 1930s and 1940s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Igor Primoratz, "Both [the Nazis and Soviets] sought to impose total political control on society. Such a radical aim could only be pursued by a similarly radical method: by terrorism directed by an extremely powerful political police at an atomized and defenseless population. Its success was due largely to its arbitrary character  to the unpredictability of its choice of victims. In both countries, the regime first suppressed all opposition; when it no longer had any opposition to speak of, political police took to persecuting potential and objective opponents. In the Soviet Union, it was eventually unleashed on victims chosen at random."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Military actions primarily directed against non-combatant targets have also been referred to as state terrorism. For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Guernica" title="Bombing of Guernica">
       bombing of Guernica
      </a>
      has been called an act of terrorism,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as the
      <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">
       surprise attack on Pearl Harbor
      </a>
      . Other examples of state terrorism may include the World War II bombings of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">
       London
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden" title="Bombing of Dresden">
       Dresden
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Chongqing" title="Bombing of Chongqing">
       Chongqing
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_Bombing_of_Hiroshima" title="Atomic Bombing of Hiroshima">
       Hiroshima
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
         <span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (July 2011)">
          verification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometimes regarded as an act of terrorism was the peace-time
      <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_Rainbow_Warrior" title="Sinking of the Rainbow Warrior">
       sinking of the Rainbow Warrior
      </a>
      , a ship owned by
      <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">
       Greenpeace
      </a>
      , which occurred while in port at
      <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">
       Auckland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      on July 10, 1985. The bomb detonation killed
      <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pereira" title="Fernando Pereira">
       Fernando Pereira
      </a>
      , a Portuguese photographer. The organization who committed the attack, the DGSE, is a branch of
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      's intelligence services. Ironically,
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      had been allies since French missionaries settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Akaroa" title="Akaroa">
       Akaroa
      </a>
      , in 1835. The agents responsible pled guilty to
      <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">
       manslaughter
      </a>
      as part of a plea deal and were sentenced to ten years in prison, but were secretly released early to France under an agreement between the two countries' governments.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another example is the British
      <a href="/wiki/Military_Reaction_Force" title="Military Reaction Force">
       Military Reaction Force
      </a>
      in Northern Ireland during the 1970s which murdered innocent civilians from the Catholic community in order to stir up ethnic hatred and "take the heat off the army".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray44-45_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-murray44-45-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 201213, a former MRF member using the covername 'Simon Cursey' gave a number of interviews and published the book MRF Shadow Troop, about his time in the unit: "We opened fire at any small group in hard areas [...] armed or not  it didn't matter. We targeted specific groups that were always up to no good. These types were sympathisers and supporters, assisting the IRA movement. As far as we were concerned they were guilty by association and party to terrorist activities, leaving themselves wide open to the ultimate punishment from us".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailymail2012_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dailymail2012-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2013, a BBC Panorama documentary was aired about the MRF. It drew on information from seven former members, as well as a number of other sources: Soldier H said "We operated initially with them thinking that we were the
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">
       UVF
      </a>
      ", to which Soldier F added: "We wanted to cause confusion".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ware_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ware-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In June 1972, he was succeeded as commander by Captain James 'Hamish' McGregor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panorama_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-panorama-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 2014, the wake of the Panorama program the
      <a href="/wiki/Police_Service_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Police Service of Northern Ireland">
       Police Service of Northern Ireland
      </a>
      (PSNI) opened an investigation into the matter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an earlier review of the program the position of the PSNI was that none of the statements by soldiers in the programme could be taken as an admission of criminality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_that_terrorism_is_not_committed_by_states">
       Arguments that terrorism is not committed by states
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=State_terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arguments that terrorism is not committed by states">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Discussions of terrorism in social sciences and philosophy tend to apply to
      <a href="/wiki/Violent_non-state_actor" title="Violent non-state actor">
       violent non-state actors
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Chairman of the United Nations Counter-Terrorism Committee has stated that the twelve previous international conventions on terrorism had never referred to state terrorism, which was not an international legal concept, and that when states abuse their powers they should be judged against international conventions dealing with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">
       war crimes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_law" title="International human rights law">
       international human rights law
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/International_humanitarian_law" title="International humanitarian law">
       international humanitarian law
      </a>
      , rather than against international anti-terrorism statutes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a similar vein,
      <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">
       Kofi Annan
      </a>
      , at the time
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Secretary-General" title="United Nations Secretary-General">
       United Nations Secretary-General
      </a>
      , stated that it is "time to set aside debates on so-called 'state terrorism'. The
      <a href="/wiki/Use_of_force_by_states" title="Use of force by states">
       use of force by states
      </a>
      is already regulated under international law".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Annan added, "...regardless of the differences between governments on the question of definition of terrorism, what is clear and what we can all agree on is any deliberate attack on innocent civilians [or non-combatants], regardless of one's cause, is unacceptable and fits into the definition of terrorism."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Hoffman" title="Bruce Hoffman">
       Dr. Bruce Hoffman
      </a>
      has argued that failing to differentiate between state and non-state violence ignores the fact that there is a fundamental qualitative difference between the two types of violence. Hoffman argues that even in war, there are rules and accepted norms of behavior that prohibit certain types of weapons and tactics and outlaw attacks on specific categories of targets. For instance, rules codified in the
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">
       Geneva
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_(1899_and_1907)" title="Hague Conventions (1899 and 1907)">
       Hague Conventions
      </a>
      on warfare prohibit taking
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civilians" title="Civilians">
       civilians
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Hostage" title="Hostage">
       hostages
      </a>
      , outlaw
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reprisals" title="Reprisals">
       reprisals
      </a>
      against either civilians or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/POWs" title="POWs">
       POWs
      </a>
      , recognize
      <a href="/wiki/Neutral_territory" title="Neutral territory">
       neutral territory
      </a>
      , etc. Hoffman states that even the most cursory review of terrorist tactics and targets over the past quarter century reveals that terrorists have violated all these rules. Hoffman also states that when states transgress these rules of war the term war crime is used to describe such acts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffman_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hoffman-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Laqueur" title="Walter Laqueur">
       Walter Laqueur
      </a>
      has stated that those who argue that state terrorism should be included in studies of terrorism ignore the fact that The very existence of a
      <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">
       state
      </a>
      is based on its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monopoly_on_force" title="Monopoly on force">
       monopoly of power
      </a>
      . If it were different, states would not have the right, nor be in a position, to maintain that minimum of order on which all civilized life rests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakeley_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blakeley-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Calling the concept a 
      <a href="/wiki/Red_herring" title="Red herring">
       red herring
      </a>
       he stated: This argument has been used by the terrorists themselves, arguing that there is no difference between their activities and those by governments and states. It has also been employed by some sympathizers, and rests on the deliberate obfuscation between all kinds of violence...
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laqueur_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Laqueur-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
