          <h1 class="entry-title">
           <a href="/inspire/color-contrast" rel="bookmark">
            Color Contrast for Better Readability
           </a>
          </h1>
          <div class="blog-post-meta clear">
           <span class="breadcrumbs">
            <a href="/inspire">
             Inspire
            </a>
            //
           </span>
           <time class="published" datetime="2015-03-03T10:24:00-05:00" pubdate="">
            Mar 3, 2015
           </time>
           <a class="comment-count" data-disqus-identifier="3588" href="/inspire/color-contrast#disqus_thread">
            Comments
           </a>
          </div>
         </header>
         <div class="entry-content wysiwyg">
          <p>
           <img alt="article introduction image contains the words 'color contrast'" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-color-contrast.png" style="display: block; margin: 60px auto; height: 180px; width: 600px;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           When you create color palettes for your web design projects, are you testing the color combinations for contrast? If you're not, you might not be considering the eventual readability of the design and thus losing potential audience.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ive been working on a process to help me ensure good color contrast and readability in my projects. Thanks to some helpful color contrast accessibility tools, I think I have something that is working and wanted to share with others in the event that you find it helpful, too.
          </p>
          <p>
           I should mention that Im far from an accessibility expert. My goal hereis to simply show that a little bit of effort can go a long way when it comes toselecting colors with optimal readability in mind.
           <a href="http://www.w3.org/TR/UNDERSTANDING-WCAG20/visual-audio-contrast-contrast.html">
            Check out W3C
           </a>
           for a more thorough explanation. Also,
           <a href="http://contrastrebellion.com/">
            check out Contrast Rebellion
           </a>
           for an interesting look at the contrastproblem.
          </p>
          <h3>
           1. Establish a color palette (with tints, tones, and shades)
          </h3>
          <p>
           While you can use color contrast tools to help you establish a color palette, you can also use the tools to help find good options within an existing palette. In this case, Im using a pre-existing color palette and showing how I'm using tints, tones, and shades to help create more color contrast options.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="the color palette" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/tts-wheel.png" style="width: 400px; height: 200px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            My working color palette. From previous article,
            <a href="http://viget.com/inspire/add-colors-to-your-palette-with-color-mixing">
             Add Colors To Your Palette With Color Mixing
            </a>
            .
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="example of tints, tones, and shades" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/tts-concept.png" style="width: 650px; height: 276px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Establishing tints, tones, and shades. From previous article,
            <a href="http://viget.com/inspire/tints-tones-shades">
             From Darkness to Light: Color Versatility Using Tints, Tones, and Shades
            </a>
            .
           </em>
          </p>
          <h3>
           2. Find a good color contrast analyzer
          </h3>
          <p>
           There are plentyof good color contrast testing tools available on the web. Find one that works for you and use it to test background and foreground color combinations.Here are some options:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <a href="http://jxnblk.com/colorable/">
             Colorable
            </a>
            <a href="http://jxnblk.com/colorable/demos/text/">
             (Demo)
            </a>
            by Brent Jackson (*new favorite*
            <a href="https://twitter.com/JimJones/status/568851961203683328">
             h/t @JimJones
            </a>
            )
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://juicystudio.com/services/luminositycontrastratio.php">
             Luminosity Colour Contrast Ratio Analyser
            </a>
            by Juicy Studio
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://snook.ca/technical/colour_contrast/colour.html#fg=33FF33,bg=333333">
             Colour Contrast Check
            </a>
            by Jonathan Snook
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/">
             Color Contrast Checker
            </a>
            by WebAIM
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://www.checkmycolours.com/">
             Check My Colours
            </a>
            by Giovanni Scala
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://colorsafe.co/">
             Color Safe
            </a>
            byDonielle Berg &amp;Adrian Rapp
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           <a href="http://jxnblk.com/colorable/demos/text/">
            <img alt="Brent Jackson's Colorable" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/colorable.png" style="width: 600px; height: 326px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Brent Jackson's
            <a href="http://jxnblk.com/colorable/demos/text/">
             Colorable (Demo)
            </a>
           </em>
          </p>
          <h3>
           3. Examine body text contrast
          </h3>
          <p>
           First, it's helpful to establish good body text values. I usually start with a neutral color palette andaim for the lightest gray with a AAA (Section 508 compliant) rating (here's a good plain language explanation of ratings,
           <a href="http://www.inqbation.com/wcag-2-0-level-a-aa-aaa-versus-section-508-compliance/">
            WCAG 2.0 Level A/AA/AAA versus Section 508
           </a>
           .) The AAA rating ensures optimal readability while some brightness allows forsoftness in the text.Note that the color names are custom names that I've assigned to the colors(from previous article,
           <a href="http://viget.com/inspire/naming-colors">
            Giving Colors More Colorful Names
           </a>
           .)
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="testing text colors" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-text-1.png" style="width: 602px; height: 335px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Testing a neutral color palette as text on a white background (from previous article:
            <a href="http://viget.com/inspire/shades-of-gray-yes-really">
             Shades of Gray  Yes, Really
            </a>
            .)
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="best text options" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-text-2.png" style="width: 405px; height: 120px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            #373D3F or "Raven" is my lightest gray within a AAA accessibility rating.
           </em>
          </p>
          <h3>
           4. Evaluate buttonand linkluminosity
          </h3>
          <p>
           The next step is to find a good color option for buttons and links (the actions). I take a slightly different approach here. Instead of going for AAA, Im looking for AA (a reasonable standard to strive for)so that I can get a brighter color to contrast from the static text and draw attention to important links.For these purposes, I'm testing white (#FFFFFF) in combination with various colors.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="testing button and link colors options" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-link-1.png" style="width: 601px; height: 891px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            The blues and reds have a higher success rate while the yellows and greens not so much.
           </em>
           <span class="caption">
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="best button and link color options" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-link-2.png" style="width: 605px; height: 226px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            I go with "Darkest Alice" (#107896) for good combination of contrast and luminosity. "Ruby" (#C02F1D) is also a decent option.
           </em>
          </p>
          <h3>
           5. Establish various color combinations
          </h3>
          <p>
           Its good to identify some additional color combinationsfor attention-grabbing call-outs and other possible needs.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="color sample 1" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-promo-1.png" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; width: 400px; height: 370px;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            My base example with dark gray "Raven" text and a bright "Darkest Alice" blue for links and buttons.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="color sample 2" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-promo-2.png" style="width: 400px; height: 370px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Example with darks on a lighter background.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="color sample 3" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-promo-3.png" style="width: 400px; height: 370px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Example with lights on a darker background.
           </em>
          </p>
          <h3>
           6. Document in your style guide
          </h3>
          <p>
           The last step is to keep a reference guide handy with your test results while adding notes to your style guide. Your clients and audience will be impressed that your colors are chosen forthoughtful reasons-- optimal readability -- and you'll sail through QA testing with additional options should you need them.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="sample documentation for a style guide" class="no-border" src="http://viget.com/uploads/image/blog/cc-documentation.png" style="width: 600px; height: 373px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Sample documentation for a style guide.
           </em>
          </p>
          <h4>
           Summary
          </h4>
          <p>
           That's really all there is to it, but you could break this down into three even simpler chunks if you like:
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Reading Text:
            </strong>
            For reading purposes, find a high contrast pairing for most of your body copy (the heavy lifting).
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Action Links:
            </strong>
            For links, explore colors that are both luminous and high contrast to make it clear where actions are. (If you've done this right, there will be some contrast between your reading text and action links.)
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Extra, Extra!:
            </strong>
            Create and document various color combinations for call-outs intended to draw extra attention.
           </li>
          </ol>
