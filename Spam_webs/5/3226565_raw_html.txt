     <p>
      <b>
       Burushaski
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
           b
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'our' in 'courier'">
           r
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'oo' in 'foot'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'sh' in 'shy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'a' in 'bad'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">
           i
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burushaski_language" title="Burushaski language">
       Burushaski
      </a>
      :
      <span dir="rtl" lang="bsk" xml:lang="bsk">
       <i>
        burask
       </i>
       /
       <span class="Nastaliq" style="font-size: 125%; font-family: 'Jameel Noori Nastaleeq', 'Urdu Typesetting', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu', 'Hussaini Nastaleeq', 'AlQalam Taj Nastaleeq', IranNastaliq, 'Nafees Nastaleeq', 'Nafees Nastaleeq v1.01', Nafees, 'Pak Nastaleeq', 'PDMS_Jauhar', 'Alvi Lahori Nastaleeq';" title="Urdu Nastaliq">
        
       </span>
      </span>
      ), the language of the
      <a href="/wiki/Burusho_people" title="Burusho people">
       Burusho people
      </a>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">
       language isolate
      </a>
      spoken in northern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilgit%E2%80%93Baltistan" title="GilgitBaltistan">
       GilgitBaltistan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
       Pakistan
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2000, Burushaski was spoken by some 87,000 people in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunza%E2%80%93Nagar_District" title="HunzaNagar District">
       HunzaNagar District
      </a>
      , northern
      <a href="/wiki/Gilgit_District" title="Gilgit District">
       Gilgit District
      </a>
      , and in the
      <a href="/wiki/Yasin_Valley" title="Yasin Valley">
       Yasin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ishkoman_Valley" title="Ishkoman Valley">
       Ishkoman
      </a>
      valleys of northern
      <a href="/wiki/Ghizer_District" title="Ghizer District">
       Ghizer District
      </a>
      . Their native region is located in northern GilgitBaltistan and borders
      <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">
       Afghanistan
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pamir_corridor" title="Pamir corridor">
       Pamir corridor
      </a>
      to the north. Burushaski is also spoken by about 300 people in
      <a href="/wiki/Srinagar" title="Srinagar">
       Srinagar
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Jammu_and_Kashmir" title="Jammu and Kashmir">
       Jammu and Kashmir
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other names for the language are
      <i>
       Biltum
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Khajuna
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Kunjut
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Brushaski
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Burucaki
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Burucaski
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Burushaki
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Burushki
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Brugaski
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Brushas
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Werchikwar
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Mia:ski
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Today, Burushaski contains numerous
      <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
       loanwords
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">
       Urdu
      </a>
      (including
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
       Persian
      </a>
      words received via Urdu), and from the neighbouring
      <a href="/wiki/Dardic_languages" title="Dardic languages">
       Dardic languages
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Shina_language" title="Shina language">
       Shina
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Khowar_language" title="Khowar language">
       Khowar
      </a>
      , as well as a few from
      <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
       Turkic languages
      </a>
      , from the neighboring
      <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">
       Sino-Tibetan
      </a>
      language
      <a href="/wiki/Balti_language" title="Balti language">
       Balti
      </a>
      , and from the neighboring
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_languages" title="Eastern Iranian languages">
       Eastern Iranian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Wakhi_language" title="Wakhi language">
       Wakhi
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">
       Pashto
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the original vocabulary remains largely intact. The Dardic languages also contain large numbers of loanwords from Burushaski.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
       Classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      No generally accepted connection has been demonstrated between Burushaski and any other language or language family. Several attempts have been made to establish a genealogical relationship between Burushaski and the
      <a href="/wiki/North_Caucasian_languages" title="North Caucasian languages">
       Caucasic
      </a>
      languages,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/Yeniseian_languages" title="Yeniseian languages">
       Yeniseian languages
      </a>
      in a family called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Karasuk_languages" title="Karasuk languages">
        Karasuk
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as a non-Indo-Iranian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European" title="Indo-European">
       Indo-European
      </a>
      language,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or to include Burushaski in the
      <a href="/wiki/Den%C3%A9%E2%80%93Caucasian_languages" title="DenCaucasian languages">
       DenCaucasian
      </a>
      proposal, which includes both Caucasic and Yeniseian.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      None of these efforts has been accepted by scholarly consensus. In 2008
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_Vajda" title="Edward Vajda">
       Edward Vajda
      </a>
      attempted to demonstrate
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Merritt_Ruhlen" title="Merritt Ruhlen">
       Merritt Ruhlen
      </a>
      's proposal
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that Yeniseian was most closely related to
      <a href="/wiki/Na-Dene_languages" title="Na-Dene languages">
       Na-Dene
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Den%C3%A9%E2%80%93Yeniseian_languages" title="DenYeniseian languages">
       DenYeniseian
      </a>
      family, but the evidence adduced has not been extended to Burushaski. The Indo-Europeanist
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_P._Hamp" title="Eric P. Hamp">
       Eric P. Hamp
      </a>
      has proposed a correlation between Burushaski and
      <a href="/wiki/Indo-Hittite" title="Indo-Hittite">
       Indo-Hittite
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamp2013_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hamp2013-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ilija Casule proposed
      <a href="/wiki/Phrygian_language" title="Phrygian language">
       Phrygian
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following Berger (1956), the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language" title="The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language">
        American Heritage
       </a>
      </i>
      dictionaries suggested that the word
      <i>
       *abel
      </i>
      apple, the only name for a fruit (tree) reconstructed for
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
       Proto-Indo-European
      </a>
      , may have been borrowed from a language ancestral to Burushaski. (Today "apple" and "apple tree" are
      <i>
       balt
      </i>
      in Burushaski.)
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">
       Varieties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Varieties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Burushaski is spoken in main valleys:
      <a href="/wiki/Hunza_Valley" title="Hunza Valley">
       Hunza
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nagar_Valley" title="Nagar Valley">
       Nagar
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Yasin_Valley" title="Yasin Valley">
       Yasin
      </a>
      . The varieties of Hunza and Nagar diverge slightly, but are clearly dialects of a single language. The Yasin variety, also known by the Khowar exonym
      <i>
       Werchikwar
      </i>
      , is much more divergent. Intelligibility between Hunza-Nagar and Yasin is difficult, and Yasin is sometimes considered a distinct language.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Yasin is the least affected by
      <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
       contact with neighboring languages
      </a>
      , though speakers are bilingual in
      <a href="/wiki/Khowar_language" title="Khowar language">
       Khowar
      </a>
      . Yasin is spoken by a quarter of speakers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Burushaski is a predominantly spoken rather than written language. Occasionally the
      <a href="/wiki/Urdu_alphabet" title="Urdu alphabet">
       Urdu alphabet
      </a>
      is used, but no fixed
      <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
       orthography
      </a>
      exists. Adu Wazir Shafi has written a book "Burushaski Razon" using a
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
       Latin script
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In May 2011,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Piar_Karim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Piar Karim (page does not exist)">
       Piar Karim
      </a>
      , a native speaker and a linguist at the University of the North Texas, with his fellow linguist Tyler Utt, devised a Roman orthography they called Girmiyar Ghattayar (G.Gh.) Huruupuc 'letters for reading and writing'. In this orthography: there are five short vowels {a e i o u} pronounced as in Italian or Spanish; long vowels are shown by double letters. The consonants {b d f g h j k l m n n g p r s t w y z} have roughly the same values as in English ({g} is always hardas in give). {c} [cat 'stop'] represents a coronal affricates; its basic value is [ts]. {d t} are intermediate between alveolar and retroflex places of articulation, while {dd} [ddang 'sleep', {tt} [ttal 'pigeon'] represent dentals (n.b. not geminates). {gh} is a voiced velar fricative [] [ar 'song' ]. {h} represents aspiration in {ch} [chil 'water'], {crh} [char 'sprinkle'], {cyh} [cyhat 'short'], kh [khapun 'spoon'], ph [phin 'fly'], th [tham 'all'], tth [ttham 'king'}. {q} [qaw 'call'] is a voiceless uvular stop. {r} represents retroflex articulation in {cr} [crat 'cleavage'], {crh} [crhap 'throw'], {sr} [sraar 'drunk'], {zr} [zraasr 'pull'], {rw} [arwa 'father']. {} represents the voiced retroflex glide peculiar to Burushaski. {x} [xam 'gravy'] is a voiceless velar fricative [x]. {y} denotes palatal articulation in {cy} [cyaq 'chew'], cyh [cyhu 'bare feet'], sy [syan 'awake'].
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">
       Tibetan
      </a>
      sources record a
      <i>
       Bru-a
      </i>
      language of the Gilgit valley, which appears to have been Burushaski. Although Burushaski may once have been a significant
      <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">
       literary language
      </a>
      , no
      <i>
       Bru-a
      </i>
      manuscripts are known to have survived.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Linguists working on Burushaski use various makeshift transcriptions based on the Latin alphabet, most commonly that by Berger (see below), in their publications.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
       Phonology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phonology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Burushaski primarily has five vowels, /i e a o u/. Various contractions result in long vowels; stressed vowels (marked with acute accents in Berger's transcription) tend to be longer and less "open" than unstressed ones (
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [i e a o u]
      </span>
      as opposed to
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [    ]
      </span>
      ). Long vowels also occur in loans and in a few onomatopoeic words (Grune 1998). All vowels have nasal counterparts in Hunza (in some expressive words) and in Nager (also in proper names and a few other words).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
       Grammar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grammar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Burushaski is a
      <a href="/wiki/Double-marking_language" title="Double-marking language">
       double-marking language
      </a>
      and word order is generally
      <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" title="Subjectobjectverb">
       subjectobjectverb
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
       Nouns
      </a>
      in Burushaski are divided into four
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
       genders
      </a>
      : human masculine, human feminine, countable objects, and uncountable ones (similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_noun" title="Mass noun">
       mass nouns
      </a>
      ). The assignment of a noun to a particular gender is largely predictable. Some words can belong both to the countable and to the uncountable class, producing differences in meaning. For example, when countable,
      <i>
       balt
      </i>
      means apple but when uncountable, it means apple tree (Grune 1998).
     </p>
     <p>
      Noun
      <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
       morphology
      </a>
      consists of the noun stem, a
      <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">
       possessive
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefix_(linguistics)" title="Prefix (linguistics)">
       prefix
      </a>
      (mandatory for some nouns, and thus an example of inherent possession), and
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
       number
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
       case
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
       suffixes
      </a>
      . Distinctions in number are singular, plural, indefinite, and grouped. Cases include
      <a href="/wiki/Absolutive_case" title="Absolutive case">
       absolutive
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ergative_case" title="Ergative case">
       ergative
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">
       oblique
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
       genitive
      </a>
      , and several
      <a href="/wiki/Locative_case" title="Locative case">
       locatives
      </a>
      ; the latter indicate both location and direction and may be compounded.
     </p>
     <p>
      Burushaski
      <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
       verbs
      </a>
      have three basic stems: past tense, present tense, and consecutive. The past stem is the citation form and is also used for
      <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
       imperatives
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nominalization" title="Nominalization">
       nominalization
      </a>
      ; the consecutive stem is similar to a past participle and is used for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" title="Grammatical conjunction">
       coordination
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
       Agreement
      </a>
      on the verb has both
      <a href="/wiki/Nominative%E2%80%93accusative_language" title="Nominativeaccusative language">
       nominative
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ergative%E2%80%93absolutive_language" title="Ergativeabsolutive language">
       ergative
      </a>
      features: transitive verbs mark both the subject and the object of a clause, while intransitive verbs mark their sole argument as both a subject and an object.

      Altogether, a verb can take up to four prefixes and six suffixes.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">
       Nouns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nouns">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_classes">
       Noun classes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Noun classes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Burushaski, there are four
      <a href="/wiki/Noun_class" title="Noun class">
       noun classes
      </a>
      , similar to declensional classes in
      <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
       Indo-European languages
      </a>
      , but unlike Indo-European, the nominal classes in Burushaski are associated with four grammatical "genders":
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        m
       </b>
       = male human beings, gods and spirits
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        f
       </b>
       = female human beings and spirits
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        x
       </b>
       = animals,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countable" title="Countable">
        countable
       </a>
       nouns
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        y
       </b>
       = abstract concepts, fluids, uncountable nouns
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Below, the abbreviation "
      <b>
       h
      </b>
      " will stand for the combination of the m- and f-classes, while "
      <b>
       hx
      </b>
      " will stand for the combination of the m-, f- and x-classes. Nouns in the x-class typically refer to countable, non-human beings or things, for example animals, fruit, stones, eggs, or coins; conversely, nouns in the y-class are as a rule uncountable abstractions or mass nouns, such as rice, fire, water, snow, wool, etc.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, these rules are not universal  countable objects in the y-class are sometimes encountered, e.g.
      <i>
       ha
      </i>
      , 'house'. Related words can subtly change their meanings when used in different classes  for example,
      <i>
       bay
      </i>
      , when a member of the x-class, means salt in clumps, but when in the y-class, it means powdered salt. Fruit trees are understood collectively and placed in the y-class, but their individual fruits belong to the x-class. Objects made of particular materials can belong to either the x- or the y- class: stone and wood are in the x-class, but metal and leather in the y-class. The
      <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
       article
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjectives" title="Adjectives">
       adjectives
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">
       numerals
      </a>
      and other attributes must be in
      <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
       agreement
      </a>
      with the noun class of their subject.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pluralisation">
       Pluralisation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pluralisation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are two
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
       numbers
      </a>
      in Burushaski: singular and
      <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
       plural
      </a>
      . The singular is unmarked, while the plural is expressed by means of suffix, which vary depending on the class of the noun:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        h-class
       </b>
       : possible suffixes -ting, -aro, -daro, -taro, -tsaro
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        h- and x-class
       </b>
       : possible suffixes -o, -io, -ko, -iko, -juko; -ono, -u; -i, -ai; -ts, -uts, -muts, -umuts; -nts, -ants, -ints, -iants, -ingants, -ents, -onts
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        y-class
       </b>
       : possible suffixes -ng, -ang, -ing, -iang; -eng, -ong, -ongo; -ming, -ing, -iing, -miing, -iang (Nagar dialect)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Some nouns admit two or three different prefixes, while others have no distinctive suffix, and occur only in the plural, e.g.
      <i>
       bras
      </i>
      'rice',
      <i>
       gur
      </i>
      'wheat',
      <i>
       bishk
      </i>
      , 'fur', (cf.
      <a href="/wiki/Plurale_tantum" title="Plurale tantum">
       plurale tantum
      </a>
      ). On the other hand, there are also nouns which have identical forms in the singular and plural, e.g.
      <i>
       hagr
      </i>
      'horse(s)'. Adjectives have a unique plural suffix, whose form depends on the class of the noun they modify, e.g.
      <i>
       burm
      </i>
      'white' gives the x-class plural
      <i>
       burum-io
      </i>
      and the y-class plural
      <i>
       burm-ing
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples of pluralisation in Burushaski:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        wazir
       </i>
       (m), pl.
       <i>
        wazirishu
       </i>
       'vizier, minister'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        hir
       </i>
       (m), pl.
       <i>
        huri
       </i>
       'man' (stress shifts)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        gus
       </i>
       (f), pl.
       <i>
        gushnga
       </i>
       'woman' (stress shifts)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        dasn
       </i>
       (f), pl.
       <i>
        daseyoo
       </i>
       'girl', 'unmarried woman'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        huk
       </i>
       (x), pl.
       <i>
        huk
       </i>
       'dog'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        thely
       </i>
       (x), pl.
       <i>
        til
       </i>
       'walnut'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        thely
       </i>
       (y), pl.
       <i>
        theleng
       </i>
       'walnut tree'
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Declension">
       Declension
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Declension">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Burushaski is an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergativity" title="Ergativity">
       ergative
      </a>
      language. It has five primary
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case_(linguistics)" title="Case (linguistics)">
       cases
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable">
      <tr style="background:#c0f0f0;">
       <th>
        Case
       </th>
       <th>
        Suffix
       </th>
       <th>
        Function
       </th>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Absolutive_case" title="Absolutive case">
         Absolutive
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        unmarked
       </td>
       <td>
        The subject of intransitive verbs and the object of transitive ones.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Ergative_case" title="Ergative case">
         Ergative
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -e
        </i>
       </td>
       <td>
        The subject of transitive verbs.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">
         Oblique
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -e
        </i>
        ;
        <i>
         -mo
        </i>
        (f)
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">
         Genitive
        </a>
        ; the basis of secondary case endings
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
         Dative
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -r
        </i>
       </td>
       <td>
        Dative,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allative" title="Allative">
         allative
        </a>
        .
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Ablative_case" title="Ablative case">
         Ablative
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -um
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -m
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -mo
        </i>
       </td>
       <td>
        Indicates separation (e.g. 'from where?')
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      The case suffixes are appended to the plural suffix, e.g.
      <i>
       Husiniukutse
      </i>
      , 'the people of Hussein' (ergative plural). The genitive ending is irregular, /mo/, for singular f-class nouns, but /-e/ in all others (identical to the ergative ending). The dative ending, /-ar/, /-r/ is attached to the genitive ending for singular f-class nouns, but to the stem for all others. Examples:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        hir-e
       </i>
       'the man's',
       <i>
        gus-mo
       </i>
       'the woman's' (gen.)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        hir-ar
       </i>
       'to the man',
       <i>
        gus-mu-r
       </i>
       'to the woman' (dat.)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The genitive is placed before the thing possessed:
      <i>
       Hunzue tham
      </i>
      , 'the Emir of Hunza.'
     </p>
     <p>
      The endings of the secondary cases are formed from a secondary case suffix (or infix) and one of the primary endings /-e/, /-ar/ or /-um/. These endings are directional, /-e/ being locative (answering 'where?'), /-ar/ being terminative (answering 'where to?'), and /-um/ being ablative (answering 'where from?'). The infixes, and their basic meanings, are as follows:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <i>
        -ts-
       </i>
       'at'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -ul-
       </i>
       'in'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -a-
       </i>
       'on; with'
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -al-
       </i>
       'near' (only in the Hunza dialect)
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      From these, the following secondary or compound cases are formed:
     </p>

      <tr style="background:#c0f0f0;">

       </th>

       </th>

        Ablative
       </th>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         -ts-
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ts-e
        </i>
        'at'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ts-ar
        </i>
        'to'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ts-um
        </i>
        'from'
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         -ul-
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ul-e
        </i>
        'in'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ul-ar
        </i>
        'into'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -ul-um
        </i>
        'out of'
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         -a-
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -a-e
        </i>
        'on','with'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -a-ar
        </i>
        'up to'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>

       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         -al-
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -al-e
        </i>
        'near'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -al-ar
        </i>
        'to'
       </td>
       <td>
        <i>
         -al-um

        'from'

     <p>
      The regular endings /-ul-e/ and /-ul-ar/ are archaic and are now replaced by /-ul-o/ and /-ar-ulo/ respectively.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns_and_pronominal_prefixes">
       Pronouns and pronominal prefixes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pronouns and pronominal prefixes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Nouns indicating parts of the body and kinship terms are accompanied by an obligatory pronominal prefix. Thus, one cannot simply say 'mother' or 'arm' in Burushaski, but only 'my arm', 'your mother', 'his father', etc. For example, the root
      <i>
       mi
      </i>
      'mother', is never found in isolation, instead one finds:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        i-mi
       </i>
       'his mother',
       <i>
        mu-mi
       </i>
       'their mother' (3f sg.),
       <i>
        u-mi
       </i>
       'your mother' (3h pl.),
       <i>
        u-mi-tsaro
       </i>
       'their mothers'(3h pl.).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The pronominal, or personal, prefixes agree with the person, number and  in the third person, the class of their noun. A summary of the basic forms is given in the following table:
     </p>

       </th>

       </th>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        1st person
       </td>
       <td style="text-align:center;">
        <i>

        </i>
       </td>

        </i>
       </td>
      </tr>

     <p>
      Personal pronouns in Burushaski distinguish proximal and distal forms, e.g.
      <i>
       khin
      </i>
      'he, this one here', but
      <i>

      </i>
      , 'he, that one there'. In the oblique, there are additional abbreviated forms.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The verbal morphology of Burushaski is extremely complicated and rich in forms. Many sound changes can take place, including
      <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
       assimilation
      </a>
      ,

     <p>
      The personal affixes are also used when the noun occupies the role of the subject or the object, e.g.
      <i>
       hir i-r-i-mi
      </i>
      'the man died'. With intransitive verbs, the subject function is indicated by both a prefix and a suffix, as in:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        gu-ir--u-m-a
       </i>
       "you will die" (future)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        i-ghurts-i-m-i
       </i>
       "he sank" (preterite)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Personal prefixes do not occur in all verbs and all tenses. Some verbs do not admit personal prefixes, others still do so only under certain circumstances. Personal prefixes used with intransitive verbs often express a volitional function, with prefixed forms indicating an action contrary to the intention of the subject. For example:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        hur-i-m-i
       </i>
       "he sat down" (volitional action without prefix)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        i-r-i-m-i
       </i>
       "he died" (involuntary action with prefix)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        ghurts-i-mi
       </i>
       "he went willingly underwater", "he dove" (without prefix)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        i-ghurts-i-m-i
       </i>
       "he went unwillingly underwater", "he sank" (with prefix)
      </li>
     </ul>

        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burushaski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The d-prefix">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A number of verbs  mostly according to their root form  are found with the d-prefix in position 2, which occurs before a consonant according to
      <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
       vowel harmony
      </a>
      . The precise semantic function of the d-prefix is unclear. With primary transitive verbs the d-prefix, always without personal prefixes, forms regular intransitives. Examples:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        i-phalt-i-mi
       </i>
       'he breaks it open' (transitive)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        du-phalt-as
       </i>
       'to break open, to explode' (intransitive)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A master's thesis research work of a native speaker of Burushaski: Middle Voice Construction in Burushaski: From the Perspective of a Native Speaker of the Hunza Dialect claims that the [dd-] verbal prefix is an overt morphological middle marker for MV constructions, while the [n-] verbal prefixis a morphological marker for passive voice. The data primarily come from the Hunza dialect of Burushaski, but analogous phenomena can be observed in otherdialects. This research is based on a corpus of 120 dd-prefix verbs. This research has showed that position {-2} on the verb template is occupied by voice-marker in Burushaski. The author argues that the middle marker is a semantic category of its own and that it is clearly distinguished from the reflexive marker in this language. The middle marker (MM) means the grammatical device used to indicate that the two semantic roles of Initiator and Endpoint refer to a single holistic entity (Kemmer 1993: 47). In the view of that definition,I look at a middle marked verb in Burushaski and illustration follows the example
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       hiles dd-i-il-imi 'the boy soaked'
      </li>
     </ul>
