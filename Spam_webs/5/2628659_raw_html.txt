        <p>
         Saving Summer is the theme of our #SundaySupper today and its something thats pretty near and dear to my heart. My garden is constantly over flowing with fruits and veggies that I cant use up fast enough. Last year we had an over abundance of banana peppers and blueberries. This year it has been strawberries, blueberries and corn. Yes, corn. Welcome to my world. I live in Missouri and its pretty abundant here.
         <br/>
         <span id="more-25258">
         </span>
         <br/>
         I actually remember the first time I told
         <a href="http://familyfoodie.com/" target="_blank">
          Isabel
         </a>
         , our fearless leader, that I actually grow corn in my garden. She laughed and laughed. I guess that does seem kinda strange to someone from Florida but here its a household staple. Its everywhere. You dont even have to go far from my house to find corn field after corn field. No
         <em>
          really
         </em>
         . Less than 5 minutes.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="corn field" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-25458" height="360" src="http://dailydishrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/corn-field.jpg" width="540"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Since I have such an abundance of corn this year, I thought it only fitting to make that the subject of my #SundaySupper post. We made Corn Cob Jelly. This delicious Corn Cob Jelly is light, sweet and tastes a whole lot like honey. I saw this recipe last year from my friend Pam from
         <a href="http://blueberriesandblessings.com/" target="_blank">
          Blueberries and Blessings
         </a>
         and set about making it nearly immediately.
        </p>
        <p>
         The first time wetriedmaking Corn Cob Jelly, it never did firm up all the way. At first, I was disappointed but then realized it had turned into a delicious, thick syrup for waffles and pancakes. It was great, but it definitely lacked the jelly consistency that I was looking for. At least I now know how to make Corn Cob Syrup and yes I have made a few batches.
        </p>
        <p>
         However what I wanted was Jelly, and I was determined to get some Corn Cob Jelly this year. So I tweaked therecipe, mixing it with a few others after doing some research. It all related to the sugar amount. The less sugar I used, the more syrupy myliquid would be, and no amount of letting it sit in the fridge or anywhere was changing that. The balance seems to be the amount of cups of water should equal the amount of cups of sugar added. That seems to work pretty well. I seem to get about 3 1/2 cups of liquid after straining each time I make this, so thats what I used in sugar. This held true whether I used regular fruit pectin or its low sugar counterpart. So use whichever you prefer.
        </p>
        <p>
         While I certainly have an over abundance of corn growing, it isnt generally ready all at the same time. So, my other trick is to freeze the corn cobs after removing the kernels for other uses. I might only pull 2 or 3 a day, so I pop them in a freezer bag after Ive removed the kernels. When I am ready towhip up a batch of Corn Cob Jelly, I just allow a little more time when I am boiling to let the flavor into the water. Ive had no problems doing this, so if youre like me and dont typically have 12 corn cobs all at the same time, freezing works.
        </p>
        <p>
         Oh and one more fun little trick. When I remove the corn cobs from the water, I allow them to dry out completely. They make AWESOME fire starters for those fall campfires or fire pits. Well, either that or you can share it with any small rodents you have living in your house.
        </p>

        <div class="easyrecipe" data-rating="#rating#">
         <link href="http://dailydishrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Corn-Cob-Jelly-300x199.jpg" itemprop="image"/>
         <div class="item ERName">
          Easy Canning Recipe: Corn Cob Jelly #SundaySupper
         </div>
         <div class="ERClear">
         </div>
         <div class="ERHead">
          Author:
          <span class="author">
           www.dailydishrecipes.com
          </span>
         </div>
         <div class="ERHead">
          Serves:
          <span class="yield">
           3-4 jelly jars
          </span>
         </div>
         <div class="ERSummary summary">
          Sweet, golden and with a flavor that almost resembles honey. Was born to be put on toast, english muffins and anywhere jelly can go.
         </div>
         <div class="ERIngredients">
          <div class="ERIngredientsHeader">
           Ingredients
          </div>
          <ul class="ingredients">
           <li class="ingredient">
            12 corn cobs (kernels removed)
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient">
            1 package (1-3/4 ounces) powdered fruit pectin
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient">
            3 1/2-4 cups sugar
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient">
            a drop or two of yellow food coloring
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient">
            3 1/2-4 cups of water from boiling the corn cobs.
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
         <div class="ERInstructions">
          <div class="ERInstructionsHeader">
           Instructions
          </div>
          <div class="instructions">
           <ol>
            <li class="instruction">
             Remove corn kernels from cobs and save for another recipe or use as a side dish for dinner.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             In a large pot, drop corncobs with about 6-8 cups of water; bring to a boil.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Boil uncovered for about 10 minutes.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Throw away the cobs and strain the liquid to remove extra pieces, etc.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Reserve 3 1/2 cups of liquid. You can throw out the rest.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Place the corn cob water back into the large pot and stir in the pectin. Bring to a strong boil.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Add the sugar and bring back to a boil.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Skim the foam off the top and add a few drops of yellow food coloring if youd like.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Ladle the liquid into jelly jars, leaving about 1/2 to 1 inch head-space
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Place the seal and rings on the jar and process the jelly in a water bath for about 10-15 minutes.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Remove and set aside. As they cool, listen for the jars to pop.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             If for some reason they do not pop, just put that jar into the refrigerator and use it within 2 weeks.
            </li>
            <li class="instruction">
             Shelf life (for popped jars) is 1-2 years
            </li>
           </ol>
          </div>
