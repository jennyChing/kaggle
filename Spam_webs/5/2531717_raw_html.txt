        <header class="entry-header">
         <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
          Quite Possibly the Most Dishonest Seattle Times Editorial Ever
         </h1>
         <p class="entry-meta">
          by
          <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
           <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://horsesass.org/author/admin-2/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
            <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
             Goldy
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
          
          <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-08-16T09:34:14+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
           Saturday, 8/16/14
          </time>
          ,
          <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-08-16T09:34:14+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
           9:34 am
          </time>
         </p>
        </header>
        <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_56261" style="width: 1034px">
          <img alt="McBride Farm" class="size-large wp-image-56261" height="871" src="http://horsesass.org/wp-content/uploads/Screen-Shot-2014-08-16-at-9.44.31-AM-1024x871.png" width="1024"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           <small>
            <a href="http://www.zillow.com/homes/for_sale/Issaquah-WA/5302_rid/days_sort/47.558984,-122.027434,47.556953,-122.03097_rect/17_zm/1_fr/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.zillow.com/homes/for_sale/Issaquah-WA/5302_rid/days_sort/47.558984,-122.027434,47.556953,-122.03097_rect/17_zm/1_fr/', 'Screenshot from Zillow of the McBride Farm']);" target="_blank">
             Screenshot from Zillow of the McBride Farm
            </a>
           </small>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          I know, I know, some people just wont link to a post with
          <a href="http://horsesass.org/fuck-you-frank-blethen-fuck-you-up-the-fucking-fuckhole/" target="_blank">
           too many f-bombs in it
          </a>
          , so for you faint ofheart, heres a non-foul-mouthed take down of what has got to be
          <a href="http://seattletimes.com/html/editorials/2024313850_estatetaxeditxml.html" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://seattletimes.com/html/editorials/2024313850_estatetaxeditxml.html', 'one of the most dishonest Seattle Times editorialsever']);" target="_blank">
           one of the most dishonest
           <em>
            Seattle Times
           </em>
           editorials
           <strong>
            ever
           </strong>
          </a>
          .Which is saying a lot. Because the
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          has an impressive track record of dishonest editorials.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           NEWS that
           <strong>
            the last family farm in Issaquah
           </strong>
           is being sold for residential development is a reminder of one of the subtle ills of our tax system: a
           <strong>
            death tax
           </strong>
           that forces many farm families and business owners either to liquidate their assets, or go through enormous and costly gyrations to avoid it.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://seattletimes.com/html/localnews/2024280138_mcbridefarmxml.html" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://seattletimes.com/html/localnews/2024280138_mcbridefarmxml.html', 'The story of the McBride family']);" target="_blank">
            The story of the McBride family
           </a>
           , recounted by Seattle Times reporter Erin Heffernan, shows us
           <strong>
            what we lose as a result of the estate tax
           </strong>
            in this case, the last working farm in a fast-growing suburb.
           <strong>
            Twelve acres of open space farmed by a single family since 1883
           </strong>
           will soon become a subdivision. The family had to sell, explained Jim McBride. There wasnt any other thing for us to do. All my parents wealth was in that land, and
           <strong>
            we couldnt afford to pay the taxes that come with inheriting it at the current property value
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Credulously read this
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          editorial, and you would think the McBridefamilywasforced off the land of the last working farm in Issaquah thanks tothe death of their patriarch and a stupidly punitive estate tax.Except, everything about this editorial is wrong:
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Working family farms are
           <strong>
            entirely exempt
           </strong>
           from the Washingtons estate tax, while99.4 percent of family farms pay no federal estate tax at all; the number of family farms liquidated to pay the federal estate tax is estimated near zero.
          </li>
          <li>
           The McBride property is actually
           <strong>
            not a working farm
           </strong>
           , and apparently hasnot been for quite some time.
          </li>
          <li>
           Regardless of whether or not it is a farm, family patriarch Ralph McBrides estateis
           <strong>
            too small to be subject
           </strong>
           to either the state or federal estate tax.
          </li>
          <li>
           RalphMcBride
           <strong>
            is not dead
           </strong>
           , so there is no estate yet to tax.
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
          You wouldnt know any of this from reading the editorial. Because the editors dont want you to know it. But lets be clear, the McBrides did not sell the family farm to pay off an estate tax.
         </p>
         <p>
          First of all, the federal estate tax exempts the first
          <strong>
           $5.25 million.
          </strong>
          So if, as Jim McBride is quoted, all his parents wealth was in that land, and they sold the property for
          <strong>
           $4.5 million
          </strong>
          (as the
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          <a href="http://seattletimes.com/html/localnews/2024280138_mcbridefarmxml.html" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://seattletimes.com/html/localnews/2024280138_mcbridefarmxml.html', 'reports']);" target="_blank">
           reports
          </a>
          ), then there would be no federal estate tax to pay. The US Department of Agriculture reports that
          <a href="http://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-economy/federal-tax-issues/federal-estate-taxes.aspx#.U-6DwVZOixI" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-economy/federal-tax-issues/federal-estate-taxes.aspx#.U-6DwVZOixI', 'only 0.6 percent']);" target="_blank">
           only 0.6 percent
          </a>
          of family farms end up paying any estate tax. And thanks to other provisions benefiting farms, such as a special use valuation and a 15-year payment plan, the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities estimatesthe number of family-owned farms forced to liquidate to pay the federal estate tax at 
          <a href="http://www.cbpp.org/cms/?fa=view&amp;id=2655" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cbpp.org/cms/?fa=view&amp;id=2655', 'virtually none']);" target="_blank">
           virtually none
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          But what about that pernicious Washington estate tax? Heres what the
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          has the audacity to tell its readers:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           Washington states tax is especially punitive. The rate of up to 20 percent is the highest in the country  on top of a federal rate of 40 percent. The typical state exemption for the first $2 million of estate value is
           <strong>
            hardly enough for a farm
           </strong>
           or prosperous business, despite reforms by the 2013 Legislature.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Except, Washington state law
          <strong>
           <a href="http://dor.wa.gov/content/findtaxesandrates/othertaxes/tax_farmestatetaxfaq.aspx" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://dor.wa.gov/content/findtaxesandrates/othertaxes/tax_farmestatetaxfaq.aspx', 'exempts the value of working farms entirely']);" target="_blank">
            exempts the value of working farms
            <em>
             entirely
            </em>
           </a>
          </strong>
          . All of it. If this had beena working farm for five out of the past eight years, then the McBrides would inherit it Washington-estate-tax-free, whatever its value. No exceptions. Hard to see what is especially punitive about that.
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course, as Ill explain in a moment, the McBride property is likely
          <strong>
           <em>
            not
           </em>
           a working farm
          </strong>
          .Not that it matters, because 97-year-old Ralph McBrides land holdings comprised only a portion of the $4.5 million deal, far below the $2 million threshold for non-farm assetssubject to Washingtons estate tax.
         </p>
         <p>
          It didnt take more than a few seconds on Zillow to locate the four properties the extended McBride family sold to developer Wescott Holdings
          <a href="http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23203-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787725_zpid/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23203-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787725_zpid/', 'here']);" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23217-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787788_zpid/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23217-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787788_zpid/', 'here']);" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23231-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787789_zpid/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23231-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787789_zpid/', 'here']);" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          , and
          <a href="http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23317-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787790_zpid/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/23317-SE-48th-St-Issaquah-WA-98029/48787790_zpid/', 'here']);" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          and according to the King County Assessors Office only one property wasstill owned by Ralph McBride, a 7.55 acre parcel with an
          <a href="http://info.kingcounty.gov/Assessor/eRealProperty/Detail.aspx?ParcelNbr=2224069006" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://info.kingcounty.gov/Assessor/eRealProperty/Detail.aspx?ParcelNbr=2224069006', 'assessed taxable value of only $666,000']);" target="_blank">
           assessed taxable value of only $666,000
          </a>
          . The other lots werealready owned by Ralphs heirs.
         </p>
         <p>
          All my parents wealth was in that land, Jim McBride told the
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          . So a tiny bit of fact-checking would have demonstrated that Ralph McBrides estate couldnt possibly be subject to either the state or federal estate tax, regardless of its classification. If anything, the family
          <em>
           increased
          </em>
          their chance of paying the Washington estate tax by selling his property at a premium before his death (though Im guessing the deal is structured in a way to avoid this).
         </p>
         <p>
          So why did the McBrides sell the farm?
          <a href="http://www.issaquahpress.com/2014/07/08/last-harvest-development-ends-four-generations-of-issaquah-farmers/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.issaquahpress.com/2014/07/08/last-harvest-development-ends-four-generations-of-issaquah-farmers/', 'Heres what they told the Issaquah Press']);" target="_blank">
           Heres what they told the
           <em>
            Issaquah Press
           </em>
          </a>
          :
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           Age,
           <strong>
            skyrocketing property taxes
           </strong>
           and nearby development caused the family to vacate the 660-acre [sic] section of land at the end of June, bringing an end to one of the final remnants of Issaquahs rural past.  The farm just kind of petered out as the development began, [Celia] McBride said, referring to the build out of the Klahanie area in 1985.
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, the construction didnt stop there. Residential neighborhoods sprang up to surround the farm, leading to complaints about noise from the animals and financial concerns.
          </p>
          <p>
           
           <strong>
            The property taxes became outrageous
           </strong>
           , she said. My dad got older, my mom got tired and now the land is going to be a development.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          The property tax on James McBrides parcel was $7,575.38 for 2014. The McBride familys combined tax bill was over $26,000 across the four properties. So, yeah, maybe they were just tired of paying it. Though looking at the parcels on Zillow, Im guessing that the family got offered a premium to sell the four parcels together, rather than the per acre price they could have demanded for Ralphs 7.5 acres alone. Thats the way these development deals often work.
         </p>
         <p>
          Regardless, you cant liquidate the family farm to pay the estate tax if you dont have a family farm.
         </p>
         <p>
          Look at the
          <a href="http://www.zillow.com/homes/for_sale/Issaquah-WA/5302_rid/days_sort/47.559122,-122.027923,47.557091,-122.031458_rect/17_zm/1_fr/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.zillow.com/homes/for_sale/Issaquah-WA/5302_rid/days_sort/47.559122,-122.027923,47.557091,-122.031458_rect/17_zm/1_fr/', 'aerial map']);" target="_blank">
           aerial map
          </a>
          on Zillow. Two of the lots are heavily wooded, with maybe five or six acres of potential cropland between the four. According to the Agriculture Council of America, the average US farm is 441 acres, so it would behardly viable to farm a plot this small
          <a href="https://www.google.com/maps/@47.557729,-122.030027,4277m/data=!3m1!1e3" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.google.com/maps/@47.557729,-122.030027,4277m/data=!3m1!1e3', 'in the midst of fast appreciating suburban developments']);" target="_blank">
           in the midst of fast appreciating suburban developments
          </a>
          . Indeed, King County characterizes all four parcels as urban residential, not rural or agricultural, and assigns single family as the properties highest and best use. The narrative in both the
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Issaquah Press
          </em>
          describes a 660-acre farm that was gradually divided and sold off over generations. Yes, Ralph McBride continued to raise chickens and tend a garden. But so do lots of people, and it doesnt make them farmers.
         </p>
         <p>
          The McBride property may in fact have been the last working farm in Issaquahthats hard to know for surebut it clearly ceased to be a working farm years ago. So all Issaquah is really losing with the developmentof the McBride properties is some open space and a tiny fragment of its history. If you find this loss upsetting, blame the land use policies.
         </p>
         <p>
          Still,farm or not, the
          <em>
           Seattle Times
          </em>
          thesis is demonstrably wrong. If it is a working farm, then it is exempt from the estate tax. Ifits not exempt from the estate tax, then it is clearly not a farm. There is simply no way the estate tax cost the McBrides the family farm.
         </p>
         <p>
          But what else can the estate-tax-hating Blethens do but
          <strong>

            <p>
             Sensible Estate Tax Act of 2014  Amends the Internal Revenue Code to:
             <br/>
             (1) establish new estate tax rates of between 41% (for estates with a value in excess of $1,000,000) and 55% (for estates with a value in excess of $10 million),
             <br/>
             (2) allow a $1 million estate tax exclusion, and
             <br/>
             (3) provide for an inflation adjustment to such amounts for decedents dying after 2014. Restores the estate tax credit for any estate, inheritance, legacy, or succession taxes paid to a state (expired after 2004). Repeals the deduction currently allowed for such taxes. Sets forth estate valuation rules for certain transfers of nonbusiness assets and limits estate tax discounts for certain individuals with minority interests in a business acquired from a decedent. Requires that the value of the basis in any property acquired from a decedent or by gift be consistent with the basis as determined for estate and gift tax purposes. Requires executors of estates and donors of gifts required to file a gift tax return to disclose to the Secretary of the Treasury, and to recipients of any interest in an estate or a gift, information identifying the value of each interest received. Expands rules for valuing assets in grantor retained annuity trusts to require that:
             <br/>
             (1) the right to receive fixed amounts from an annuity last for a term of not less than 10 years and that such fixed amounts not decrease during the first 10 years of the annuity term, and
             <br/>
             (2) the remainder interest have a value greater than zero when transferred. Terminates the generation-skipping transfer exemption for certain long-term trusts (perpetual dynasty trusts) 90 years after the establishment of such trusts.
