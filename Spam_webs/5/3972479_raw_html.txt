     <p>
      <b>
       Mary Frith
      </b>
      (c. 1584 26 July 1659), alias
      <b>
       Moll
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       Mal
      </b>
      )
      <b>
       Cutpurse
      </b>
      , was a notorious
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pickpocket" title="Pickpocket">
       pickpocket
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Fence_(criminal)" title="Fence (criminal)">
       fence
      </a>
      of the London
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_underworld" title="Criminal underworld">
       underworld
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_of_nicknames">
       Meaning of nicknames
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Frith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meaning of nicknames">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The name Moll Cutpurse was a pun: Moll, apart from being a nickname for Mary, was a common name for a young woman usually of disreputable character. Cutpurse denoted her reputation as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thief" title="Thief">
       thief
      </a>
      who would cut purses to steal the contents.
     </p>
     <p>
      The other name by which she was known, "The Roaring Girl" is taken from "roaring boys", young gentlemen who caroused in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taverns" title="Taverns">
       taverns
      </a>
      and then picked fights on the street.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="An_eccentric_life">
       An eccentric life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Frith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: An eccentric life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The facts of her life are extremely confusing, with many exaggerations and myths attached to her name.
      <i>
       The Life of Mrs Mary Frith
      </i>
      , a sensationalised biography written in 1662, three years after her death, helped to perpetuate many of these myths.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mary Frith was born in the mid-1580s to a shoemaker and a housewife. Marys uncle, who was a minister and her fathers brother, once attempted to reform her at a young age by sending her to New England. However, she jumped overboard before the ship set sail, and refused to go near her uncle again.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hist-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mary presented herself in public in a
      <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(clothing)" title="Doublet (clothing)">
       doublet
      </a>
      and baggy
      <a href="/wiki/Breeches" title="Breeches">
       breeches
      </a>
      , smoking a pipe and swearing if she felt like it. She was recorded as having been burned on her hand four times, a common punishment for thieves, and was at one time sentenced to do penance standing in a white sheet at St. Pauls Cross during the Sunday morning sermon. It did little good, since she still wore mens clothing, and she set mirrors up all around her house to stroke her vanity. Her house was actually rather feminine, thanks to the efforts of her three full-time maids. She kept parrots and bred mastiffs. Her dogs were particularly special to her: each had its own bed with sheets and blankets, and she prepared their food herself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hist-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is believed that she first came to prominence in 1600 when she was indicted in
      <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">
       Middlesex
      </a>
      for stealing 2s 11d on 26 August of that year. It is at that point she began to gain notoriety. In the following years, two plays were written about her. First the 1610 drama
      <i>
       The Madde Pranckes of Mery Mall of the Bankside
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Day_(dramatist)" title="John Day (dramatist)">
       John Day
      </a>
      , the text of which is now lost. Another play (that has survived) came a year later by
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">
       Thomas Middleton
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Dekker_(poet)" title="Thomas Dekker (poet)">
       Thomas Dekker
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Roaring_Girl" title="The Roaring Girl">
        The Roaring Girl
       </a>
      </i>
      . Both works dwelt on her scandalous behaviour, especially that of dressing in men's attire and did not show her in an especially favourable light, though the surviving play is fairly complimentary to her by contemporary standards.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, Mary seems to have been given a fair amount of freedom in a society that so frowned upon women who acted unconventionally. In 1611 Frith even performed (in men's clothing, as always) at the
      <a href="/wiki/Fortune_Playhouse" title="Fortune Playhouse">
       Fortune Theatre
      </a>
      . On stage she bantered with the audience and sang songs while playing the
      <a href="/wiki/Lute" title="Lute">
       lute
      </a>
      . It can be assumed that the banter and song were somewhat obscene, but by merely performing in public at all she was defying convention.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Once a showman named William Banks bet Mary 20 pounds that she wouldnt ride from Charing Cross to Shoreditch dressed as a man. Not only did she win the bet, she rode flaunting a banner and blowing a trumpet as well. She also rode
      <a href="/wiki/Marocco" title="Marocco">
       Marocco
      </a>
      , a famous performing animal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hist-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Such public actions led to some reprisal. Frith was arrested for being dressed indecently on 25 December 1611 and accused of being involved in prostitution. On 9 February 1612 Mary was required to do a penance for her "evil living" at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Paul%27s_Cross" title="St. Paul's Cross">
       St. Paul's Cross
      </a>
      . She put on a performance then, according to a letter by John Chamberland to Dudley Carlton. In his letter, Chamberland observes, "She wept bitterly and seemed very penitent, but it is since doubted she was maudlin drunk, being discovered to have tippled of three-quarters of sack".
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She married Lewknor Markham (possibly the son of playwright
      <a href="/wiki/Gervase_Markham" title="Gervase Markham">
       Gervase Markham
      </a>
      ) on 23 March 1614. It has been alleged that the marriage was little more than a clever charade. Evidence shows that the whole thing was contracted to give Frith a counter when suits against her referred to her as a "spinster".
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It turned out that society had some reason for its disapproval; by the 1620s she was, according to her own account, working as a fence and a pimp. She not only procured young women for men, but also respectable male lovers for middle-class wives. In one case where a wife confessed on her deathbed infidelity with lovers that Mary provided, Mary supposedly convinced the woman's lovers to send money for the maintenance of the children that were probably theirs. It is important to note that, at the time, women who dressed in men's attire on a regular basis were generally considered to be "sexually riotous and uncontrolled", but Mary herself claimed to be uninterested in sex.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She is recorded as being released on 21 June 1644 from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bethlem_Hospital" title="Bethlem Hospital">
       Bethlem Hospital
      </a>
      after being cured of insanity,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which may or not be related to the (possibly
      <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">
       apocryphal
      </a>
      ) story that she robbed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Fairfax" title="General Fairfax">
       General Fairfax
      </a>
      and shot him in the arm during the
      <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">
       Civil War
      </a>
      . It was said that to escape the gallows and
      <a href="/wiki/Newgate_Prison" title="Newgate Prison">
       Newgate Prison
      </a>
      she paid a 2000-pound bribe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She died of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dropsy" title="Dropsy">
       dropsy
      </a>
      on 26 July 1659 on
      <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">
       Fleet Street
      </a>
      in London.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODNB-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
