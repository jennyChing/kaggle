              <span>
              </span>
              <h1>
               Working With an Alzheimer's Health Care Team
              </h1>
             </header>
             <aside class="disclaimer">
             </aside>
             <h2>
              Managing medical appointments and health care plans for a loved one with Alzheimer's is a major concern for caregivers. Find ways to tackle the task effectively and compassionately.
             </h2>
             <div class="bottom">
              <div class="byline">
               By
               <a href="/contributing-writers-and-editors.aspx">
                Jennifer Warner
               </a>
              </div>
              <div class="reviewedby">
               Medically reviewed by
               <a href="/medical-reviewers.aspx">
                Farrokh Sohrabi, MD
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="clear">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div id="video-player-top">
            </div>
            <aside class="aside">
            </aside>
            <p>
             One of the biggest tasks for you as an
             <a href="/health-report/alzheimers-caregiver-guide.aspx">
              Alzheimers caregiver
             </a>
             is to manage your loved ones medical needs. From juggling doctors appointments to making long-term plans, the tasks can become overwhelming. But there are strategies and resources available to help caregivers make this difficult job a little easier.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Understanding the Caregivers Role on the Health Care Team
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             The caregiver is the essential person in the chain of Alzheimers care, says James Noble, MD, MS, a neurologist and assistant clinical professor of neurology at Columbia University Medical Center in New York City.
            </p>
            <p>
             Dr. Noble says that despite all he does as a neurologist to prescribe a good treatment plan, its useless if the person with Alzheimers cant remember to follow it. Thats where the caregiver comes in. He views a caregiver as not just the supervisor of a loved ones Alzheimers health care team, but also as a critical member of the team.
            </p>
            <p>
             An Alzheimer's health care team may grow as the disease progresses to include a wide variety of health care providers, such as a:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Primary care doctor
             </li>
             <li>
              Neuropsychologist
             </li>
             <li>
              Neurologist
             </li>
             <li>
              Geriatrician
             </li>
             <li>
              Geriatric psychiatrist
             </li>
             <li>
              Occupational therapist
             </li>
             <li>
              Clinical social worker
             </li>
             <li>
              Speech therapist
             </li>
             <li>
              Home health aide
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             Because of this, it's understandable that a 2013 study found that the health care costs associated with treating forms of dementia, like Alzheimers disease, are similar to the costs associated with treating heart disease and cancer.
            </p>
            <p>
             From the caregiver's perspective, all of that responsibility can be exhausting, says Mary OHara, LCSW, a clinical social worker and assistant director of education at the Cognitive Neurology and Alzheimers Disease Center at Northwestern Universitys Feinberg School of Medicine in Evanston, Ill. Even though caregivers are leading that health care team, there are so many services they can rely on and consult with," she says. Consider these support services as extensions of a health care team: a clinical social worker, a care manager, and a
             <a href="/health-report/alzheimers-caregiver-guide/caregiver-support.aspx">
              caregiver support group
             </a>
             , such as those offered by the
             <a href="http://www.alz.org/apps/we_can_help/support_groups.asp" rel="nofollow" target="_new">
              Alzheimers Association
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Planning Ahead
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             As a caregiver and advocate for a loved one with Alzheimers, its important to plan ahead when working with his or her Alzheimers health care team.
            </p>
            <p>
             As hard as it is to
             <a href="/health-report/alzheimers-caregiver-guide/family-history.aspx">
              have a discussion about the future
             </a>
             , its so important to have it sooner rather than later because at a certain point the person with Alzheimers cant be a part of those conversations in the same way, OHara says.
            </p>
            <p>
             Noble adds that setting treatment goals in the early stages of Alzheimers can make it easier for the entire health care team to be on the same page. The caregiver can play an important role in saying, Here are the general principles that this person lives their life by, and here is what they want to happen for them, he says.
            </p>
            <p>
             Once the disease advances, the role of the caregiver increases in scope because he or she has to speak for the person with Alzheimers. You definitely are the persons voice when he or she no longer has that voice, OHara says.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Making the Most of Every Appointment
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Its important for you to go with your loved one to all medical appointments with the health care team. To get the most out of each visit, take some time beforehand to write down your questions and concerns about your loved ones symptoms and behavior.
            </p>
            <p>
             Noble says its helpful if a caregiver can bring up concerns like, Heres a list of whats changed since the last time we saw you  and he adds that there's no such thing as a silly question. Unless you or your loved one specifically addresses any new problems, they may not be on the teams radar.
            </p>
            <p>
             Some common issues can be easily handled. For instance, the Alzheimer's doctor can ease an upset stomach related to Alzheimer's medications by modifying the dose. After new questions and concerns are addressed, Noble suggests focusing on a goal-oriented discussion.
            </p>
            <p>
             OHara says appointments are also a great opportunity to get referrals for additional help or resources. Questions like, Are there any suggestions to help with this behavior? or Is there anyone else I can talk to? can get the ball rolling. If a health care provider cant refer you directly to someone who can help, he or she may be able to recommend a social worker or local organization.
            </p>
            <p>
             At the end of each appointment, ask the health care provider to recap the important points. Write down any changes to the treatment plan or follow-up steps that are needed.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Discussing Treatment Options
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Although there's no cure for Alzheimers, people with the disease may take medications to treat the symptoms of Alzheimer's, or other medical conditions they may have.
            </p>
            <p>
             When youre discussing treatment options, though, Noble says its important to be realistic about the expectations and to consider the potential risks as well as the benefits.
            </p>
            <p>
             Clinical trials may be an opportunity to get a new treatment for Alzheimers before the government approves it. Clinical trials are research studies used to determine whether new treatments are safe and effective for widespread use. But Noble says a person with Alzheimers shouldnt expect to be cured as a result of participating in a clinical trial. There's a chance that a person in the trial will only receive a placebo, and theres also a risk of potential harm, depending on the specific treatment and the phase of the trial.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Getting Help When You Need It
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             At some point, you may need help
             <a href="/health-report/alzheimers-caregiver-guide/help-your-loved-one.aspx">
              caring for your loved one with Alzheimers
             </a>
             . Some caregivers may want help in the early stages, while others may seek help in the later stages of the disease.
            </p>
            <p>
             Either way, OHara says it pays to go slowly when introducing a new caregiver to a person with Alzheimers. Make sure its a good fit for the person, she says. Its good if they share an interest, like history or another hobby, so they can talk about things together. You want a companion who can be part of that conversation and get to know the person, she explains.
            </p>
            <p>
             OHara recommends introducing a new caregiver slowly, perhaps two days a week, and planning something enjoyable for them to do together, like going to a museum. When theyre sharing an experience together, thats creating a relationship and companionship, she says.
            </p>
