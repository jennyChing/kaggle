           <div style="margin:10px;">
            <p>
             There is probably one word that enters your mind when you discover underwear that masks the smell of farts: Finally.
            </p>
            <p>
             This is totally real.
            </p>
            <p>
             The
             <a href="http://myshreddies.com/about/flatulence/" target="_blank">
              Shreddies motto
             </a>
             is Fart with confidence, and with good reason. They are built with a special patented filter carbon cloth which effectively removes odors from flatulence and other continence problems, and even includes an
             <a href="http://myshreddies.com/store/incontinence" target="_blank">
              incontinence model
             </a>
             for people with severe disorders and handicaps (or even children who refuse to wash). But even for the rest of us, this handy clothing is developed to make it easier to exhale without worrying about destroying your conversation or stink-bombing the subway car.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://whattheeff.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/3020388-inline-about-diagram1.jpg">
              <img alt="shreddies diagram" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-136" height="182" src="http://whattheeff.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/3020388-inline-about-diagram1-300x182.jpg" width="300"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             Designed by Paul OLeary, a graduate of Loughborough University with a degree in industrial design, shreddies use the same technology used in chemical warfare suits called Zorflex, an activated carbon cloth which is used as the main flatulence filter. From the Shreddies website,
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              Findings from research by De Montfort University were presented at the 86th Textile Institute World Conference; the fabric was found to remove sulphide and ethyl mercaptan so effectively that it can filter odors 200 times the strength of the average flatus emission. The fabric remains effective for the life of the garment (2-3 years) providing the care instructions are followed correctly.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             Dr. Hilary gives a great explanation below:
             <br/>
             <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/h3d_yVtw2dE?rel=0" width="560">
             </iframe>
            </p>
            <p>
             The only drawback to this wonderful invention? These babies go for $30 a pop! (pun intended)
            </p>
