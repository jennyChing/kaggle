    &lt;div class="post-meta"&gt;
	
		&lt;span class="post-date"&gt;&lt;a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/institutional-aspects-of-the-iran-deal.html" title="Institutional Aspects of the Iran Deal"&gt;2015-07-14&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/span&gt;
		
		&lt;span class="date-sep"&gt; / &lt;/span&gt;
			
		&lt;span class="post-author"&gt;&lt;a href="http://duckofminerva.com/author/sweinberger" title="Posts by Seth Weinberger" rel="author"&gt;Seth Weinberger&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/span&gt;
		
		&lt;span class="date-sep"&gt; / &lt;/span&gt;
		
		&lt;a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/institutional-aspects-of-the-iran-deal.html#respond"&gt;&lt;span class="dsq-postid" rel="27285 http://duckofminerva.com/?p=27285"&gt;&lt;span class="comment"&gt;0 Comments&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;

&lt;/div&gt; &lt;!-- /post-header --&gt;
									                                    	    
&lt;div class="post-content"&gt;
	    		            			            	                                                                                            
		&lt;p&gt;I woke up this morning to read (a few hours behind most of you&#8230;one of the few downsides to living in the Pacific Northwest is living behind the news cycle!) about the finalizing of &lt;a href="http://apps.washingtonpost.com/g/documents/world/full-text-of-the-iran-nuclear-deal/1651/"&gt;a nuclear deal&lt;/a&gt; between the E3/EU+3 and Iran. I&#8217;ll leave it to others to analyze whether the deal is a good one and whether it will indeed limit the ability of Iran to develop a nuclear weapon. &lt;a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/the-worst-agreement-in-us-diplomatic-history/2015/07/02/960e8cf2-20e8-11e5-aeb9-a411a84c9d55_story.html"&gt;Charles Krauthammer hates it&lt;/a&gt;. &lt;a href="http://www.slate.com/articles/news_and_politics/foreigners/2015/07/iran_and_united_states_nuclear_deal_why_this_historical_deal_is_what_we.html"&gt;Joe Cirincione loves it&lt;/a&gt;. &lt;a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/07/iran-nuclear-weapons-deal-obama/398465/"&gt;Jeffery Goldberg isn&#8217;t quite sure what he thinks of it&lt;/a&gt;. My own thinking tends towards the agreement isn&#8217;t spectacular, but that it might be the least worst option of military strikes (unlikely to have a meaningful, lasting impact and almost certain to increase Iran&#8217;s resolve to develop an extant weapon) and indefinite sanctions (a degrading commodity that have limited impact).&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Still, rather than focus on the efficacy of the agreement and its details, I&#8217;d like to talk about a different aspect: what we can learn about Iran&#8217;s intentions to comply with the agreement or build a nuclear weapon. My first published work, &#8220;&lt;a href="http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09636410390447635#.VaVLNvl3cvk"&gt;Institutional Signaling and the Origins of the Cold War,&lt;/a&gt;&#8221; addressed the ability of states to use international agreements and organizations to force other states to reveal privately held information about their preferences and intentions (as my institution has a subscription to Taylor and Francis, I&#8217;m not sure if the article is behind a paywall. If it is, you can also find it &lt;a href="http://www.academia.edu/174817/Institutional_Signaling_and_the_Origins_of_the_Cold_War"&gt;here &lt;/a&gt;or e-mail me and I&#8217;ll send it to you). To make a long article short, I argue that:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;blockquote&gt;&lt;p&gt;the process of negotiating and creating international institutions plays a critical role in enabling states to send and evoke credible statements of preference. Institutions, by virtue of their abilityto impose costs on states as a result of compliance with the rules and obligations,provide a means of generating signals that will be accepted as credible by the policymakers of a given state. Those signals will be interpreted as revealing vital information about the true nature and interests of other states.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/blockquote&gt;

    <p>
     Waking up the other day
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/welcome-new-guest-bloggers.html">
      to discover that I was a duck

    <p>
     I have spent much of my time over the past few weeks trying to digest the
     <a href="http://www.ohchr.org/Documents/HRBodies/HRCouncil/CoIGaza/A-HRC-29-52_en.doc">
      United Nations report
     </a>
     on Operation Protective Edge, which accuses both the Israeli Defence Force (IDF) and Hamas of committing war crimes during the conflict in Gaza last summer. Much of the media coverage has focused on the large numbers of civilians who were killed. Palestinian groups stand accused offiring approximately 4,881 rockets and 1,753 mortars at Israel, killing six civilians and injuring more than 1,600 people in the process.The report alsoexpresses particular concern at the high casualty rate in Gaza, confirming that 2,251 Palestinians  including 1,462 civilians, 551 children  were killed in just 51 days of conflict. What has been overlookedin most of the coverage, however, is what the report has to say about the way in which the IDF sought to reclassify civilians as combatantsby creating sterile combat zones through the use of warnings. The assumption being that anyone who remained within a specific area after receiving a warningwould qualify as a legitimate target.

    <h2 class="post-title">
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/you-are-doing-it-wrong.html" rel="bookmark" title="You Are Doing It Wrong">
      You Are Doing It Wrong
     </a>
    </h2>
    <div class="post-meta">
     <span class="post-date">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/you-are-doing-it-wrong.html" title="You Are Doing It Wrong">
       2015-07-10
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <span class="post-author">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/author/jbusby" rel="author" title="Posts by Josh Busby">
       Josh Busby
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/you-are-doing-it-wrong.html#comments">
      <span class="dsq-postid" rel="27241 http://duckofminerva.com/?p=27241">
       8 Comments
      </span>
     </a>
    </div>
   </div>
   <!-- /post-header -->
   <div class="post-content">
    <p>
     The former Greek finance minister Yanis Varoufakis (also a former colleague) has
     <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/yanis-varoufakis-germany-fiscally-waterboarded-wall-street-global-minotaur-2015-7" target="_blank">
      compared
     </a>
     Greeces situation to fiscal waterboarding at the hands of its creditors. Thomas Piketty has
     <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/wp/2015/07/06/thomas-piketty-accuses-germany-of-forgetting-history-as-it-lectures-greece/" target="_blank">
      accused
     </a>
     Germany of forgetting its own post-World War II experience with debt relief.When I read some of the appeals to Germany and the European Union on Greeces debt situation, I thought as an act of strategic framing thatGreece/Syriza (and to a lesser extent those of its supporters like Piketty) might be going about it wrong.
    </p>
    <p>
     As I wrote about in my first
     <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Movements-Foreign-Cambridge-International-Relations/dp/0521125669" target="_blank">
      book
     </a>
     <em>
      Moral Movements and Foreign Policy, i
     </em>
     n the late 1990s, the Jubilee 2000 campaign galvanized a global movement to write-off the debts of the worlds poorest countries. That movement succeeded in persuading key creditors, including Germany, as well as the World Bank and IMF to embrace (or at least grudgingly implement) debt relief.
    </p>
    <p>
     Now, the stakes in the current situation are very different. Then, Germany was only owed about $6 billion by developing countries, and unlike Greece with the common currency, Germanys own economic situation was scarcely implicated in the economies of the developing world. That said, there are some parallels here that I think are potentially relevant, the traditional reluctance by the German finance ministry and elites, particularly among Christian Democrats, to write-off debt being among them.

     </a>

      has decided
     </a>
     that because Germany was the beneficiary of debt relief in 1953 that they should extend the same privilege to Greece today:
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      When I hear the Germans say that they maintain a very moral stance about debt and strongly believe that debts must be repaid, then I think: What a huge joke! Germany is the country that has never repaid its debts. It has no standing to lecture other nations.
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     Before explaining why this is both normatively and positivelymisguided I would like to clear some brush by mentioning two things. First, everyone (including me!) agrees that Greeces debt must be written down. In fact, a gradual disposal of Greeces debt has been a part of bailout program since 2010and more of it will be discharged in the future. Greece has not paid back a single cent on net. In the meantime the debt is being financed through rollovers whose interest is mostly being paid by the rest of Europe while Greece has received fiscal transfers equivalent to more than 100% of GDP. So it is not an accurate characterization of the situation to say that the Greek economy is being squeezed in order to pay back debt; it is being squeezed because its level of spending was not matched by its level of productivity. And in some ways it still is not, although it is now quite close.
    </p>
    <p>
     Second, while it would be very nice to have an international bankruptcy mechanism that would allow us to discharge debt and reorganize national economies in an orderly fashion, governments are unlikely to cede sovereignty over this issue for understandable reasons. So
     <em>
      ad hoc
     </em>
     bargaining is what were stuck with for the foreseeable future.
    </p>
    <p>

     </a>
    </p>

      Greece: a Shakespearean Tragedy
     </a>
    </h2>
    <div class="post-meta">
     <span class="post-date">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/greece-a-shakespearean-tragedy.html" title="Greece: a Shakespearean Tragedy">
       2015-07-03

     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <span class="post-author">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/author/jstacey" rel="author" title="Posts by Jeffrey Stacey">
       Jeffrey Stacey
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/07/greece-a-shakespearean-tragedy.html#comments">

   <div class="post-content">
    <p>
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/greek-bailout.jpg">
      <img alt="greek-bailout" class=" size-medium wp-image-27202 aligncenter" height="203" src="http://duckofminerva.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/greek-bailout-300x203.jpg" width="300"/>
     </a>
    </p>
    <p>
     In the Greek bailout episode the Greek government has been behaving much like the self-pitying Antonio from The Merchant of Venice, while the EU has been posing as a rather heavy-handed Shylock. Despite being aware of the damaging consequences of a Greek default and potential exit from the Eurozone, the EU seems intent of having its pound of flesh. By subjecting Greece to additional austerity provisions, it may be risking the revival of the Euro financial crisisthis time with serious geostrategic implications.
    </p>
    <p>
     For five years the Greek people have been dealing with a series of austerity measures that have crippled their economic prospects. The Greek economy has contracted a jaw-dropping 25% during this period, forcing Greece into a deep recession that now borders on depression, with a 26% unemployment rate and a debt level of 180% of GDP. The resulting loss of jobs and livelihoods has been staggering; tens of thousands of Greeks are barely getting by.
    </p>
    <p>
     But on the eve of its default this week the Greek government capitulated and at the 11th hour informed the EU it would accept additional austerity after all, only to be told by the EU that its offer had expired. Adding insult to injury, a senior EU official stated The previous program has expired. So now we need to start new negotiations as regards a new program. Tragically, Greece may no longer be in the Eurozone by then.

    </h2>
    <div class="post-meta">
     <span class="post-date">

   <div class="post-content">
    <p>
     In her seminal 1997 article,
     <a href="https://www.foreignaffairs.com/articles/1997-01-01/power-shift">
      Power Shift,
     </a>
     Jessica Mathews argued that a power shift was underway in international politics marked by a redistribution of power from states to non-state actorsmostly businesses and non-governmental organizations (NGOs). Almost two decades later, NGOs are trying to foster a new sort of power shift, this time transferring power from the global north to the global south. This power shift was one of the substantive topics of discussion at the
     <a href="http://www.bisa.ac.uk/">
      BISA
     </a>
     <a href="http://bisa-ngo-workinggroup.org/">
      NGO Working Group
     </a>
     workshop I wrote about last week. The
     <a href="http://www.bond.org.uk/">
      BOND
     </a>
     <a href="http://www.bond.org.uk/data/files/reports/Fast_Forward_0515.pdf">
      report
     </a>
     and subsequent presentations by academics,
     <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/">
      Amnesty International
     </a>
     and
     <a href="http://www.everychild.org.uk/what-we-do/family-every-child">
      Family for Every Child
     </a>
     elaborated various perspectives on the nature and perceived extent of the power shift.
    </p>
    <p>
     From the practitioners point of view, the perceived power shift is occurring as humanitarian, development and advocacy NGOs, often founded and headquartered in the global North, commit to four primary activities: (1) relocating their headquarters and operations to the global South; (2) supporting capacity development in the global South by transferring skills, knowledge and resources; (3) gradually withdrawing from service delivery to permit local actors to take over these roles; and (4) where Northern NGOs (NNGOs) remain primary actors, enhancing participation in all stages of program planning, implementation and evaluation.
     <a class="more-link" href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/ngo-power-shift-2-0-3.html#more-27193">
      Continue reading
     </a>
    </p>

      Foreign Affairs,

     <a href="http://www.thechicagocouncil.org/issue/public-opinion" target="_blank">
      data
     </a>
     (currently SPSS format but other file types to be uploaded). In light of the Lacour scandal, we wanted to make that data widely available to scholars as soon as possible. I thought Id use this post to talk about the challenges of reinvigorating those surveys.

    <p>
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/Vermont_Fall1-620x282.jpg">
      <img alt="Vermont_Fall1-620x282" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-27160" height="282" src="http://duckofminerva.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/Vermont_Fall1-620x282.jpg" width="620"/>

     <br/>
     Charli and I, along with a few other colleagues here in the
     <a href="https://www.fivecolleges.edu/international/people">
      Five Colleges
     </a>
     are hosting this years
     <a href="http://www.isanet.org/Conferences/ISSS-ISAC-Springfield-2015/Call">
      joint annual conference
     </a>
     of the International Security Studies Section and International Security and Arms Control Section of ISA and APSA.  The conference will be held Oct. 8  10  fall break weekend and peak fall foliage in New England!
    </p>
    <p>
     The conference theme is Global Trends in War and Political Violence. Over the past century, we have witnessed episodes of extreme interstate and intrastate violence as well as periods of relative stability and decline in war and armed conflict. Were looking for proposals from diverse theoretical, methodological, epistemological, and geographical approaches to examine the broad trends in interstate war, intrastate war, and political violence over the past century, the current state of these issues, and what future trends might look like. In particular, wed love to see proposals that address the following sets of questions: What are the long-term trends on war, conflict, and political violence? What explains these long-term trends and how are these patterns changing? How effective are the norms, institutions, and practices designed to control and mitigate war and political violence? What are the likely future trends, and in particular, how will violence manifest itself in an era that seems likely to be characterized by further globalization, urbanization, civil war, and the emergence of new and varied non-state actors? And, how are factors such as climate change, resource scarcity, demographic stress, ethnic and religious strife, social and economic inequality, and environmental degradation, affecting, and likely to affect, longer-term trends in war and political violence?
    </p>
    <p>
     Of course, were also accepting proposals on a wider range of international security issues as well.
    </p>
    <p>
     The submission deadline is July 1.  All submissions should be made through the
     <a href="http://www.isanet.org/Conferences/ISSS-ISAC-Springfield-2015/Submissions">
      conference website portal
     </a>
     .
    </p>

    <h2 class="post-title">
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/deterrence-in-cyberspace-and-the-opm-hack.html" rel="bookmark" title="Deterrence in Cyberspace and the OPM Hack">
      Deterrence in Cyberspace and the OPM Hack
     </a>
    </h2>
    <div class="post-meta">
     <span class="post-date">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/deterrence-in-cyberspace-and-the-opm-hack.html" title="Deterrence in Cyberspace and the OPM Hack">
       2015-06-24
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <span class="post-author">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/author/hroff-perkins" rel="author" title="Posts by Heather Roff">
       Heather Roff
      </a>
     </span>

      /

     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/deterrence-in-cyberspace-and-the-opm-hack.html#comments">

   </div>
   <!-- /post-header -->
   <div class="post-content">
    <p>
     I have yet to weigh in on the recent hack on the Office of Personnel Management (OPM). Mostly this is due to two reasons. First is the obvious one for an academic: it is summer! But the second, well, that is due to the fact that as most cyber events go, this one continues to unfold. When we learned of the OPM hack
     <a href="http://www.npr.org/2015/06/05/412177006/opm-hack-exposes-records-of-4-million-federal-employees">
      earlier
     </a>
     this month, the initial figures were 4 million records. That is, 4 million present and former government employees personal records were compromised. This week, weve learned that it is more like
     <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/hack-defined-as-two-distinct-breaches-1435158334">
      18 million
     </a>
     . While
     <a href="http://relationsinternational.com/four-million-records-the-meaning-of-massive-cyber-intrusions/">
      some
     </a>
     argue that this hack is not something to be worried about,
     <a href="http://www.darkreading.com/threat-intelligence/why-china-wants-your-sensitive-data/a/d-id/1321024?">
      others
     </a>
     are less sanguine. The truth of the matter is, we really dont know. Coming out on one side or the other is a bit premature. The hack could be state-sponsored, where the data is squirreled away in a foreign intelligence agency. Or it could be state-sponsored, but the data could be sold off to high bidders on the
     <a href="http://www.wnd.com/2015/06/for-sale-hacked-u-s-fed-data-at-darknet/">
      darknet
     </a>
     . Right now, it is too early to tell.
    </p>
    <p>
     What I would like to discuss, however, is what the OPM hackand many recent others like the
     <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Technology/anthem-hack-impacted-millions-customers/story?id=29212840">
      Anthem
     </a>
     hackshow in relation to thinking about cybersecurity and cyber deterrence. Deterrence as any IR scholar knows is about getting ones adversary to
     <em>
      not
     </em>
     undertake some action or behavior. Its about keeping the status quo. When it comes to cyber-deterrence, though, we are left with serious questions about this simple concept. Foremost amongst them is: Deterrence from what? All hacking? Data theft? Infrastructure damage?
     <em>
      Critical
     </em>
     infrastructure damage? What is the status quo? The new
     <a href="http://www.defense.gov/home/features/2015/0415_cyber-strategy/Final_2015_DoD_CYBER_STRATEGY_for_web.pdf">
      cybersecurity strategy
     </a>
     released by the DoD in April is of little help. It merely states that the DoD wants to deter states and non-state actors from conducting cyberattacks against U.S. interests (10). Yet this is pretty vague. What counts as a U.S. interest?
    </p>

     <a class="more-link" href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/deterrence-in-cyberspace-and-the-opm-hack.html#more-27153">

    <p>
     Last week, this duck crossed the pond to attend the

     (BISA)

      NGO Working Group
     </a>
     workshop on Non-governmental Organizations (NGOs) in Global Governance. The workshop convened scholars of NGOs as well as NGO practitioners to consider the practice and changing landscape of global governance as well as the role of NGOs therein. A highlight was an interactive session during which we discussed the recent
     <a href="http://www.bond.org.uk/" target="_blank">
      BOND
     </a>
     the UK membership body for organizations working in international developmentreport

      <a href="http://www.bond.org.uk/data/files/reports/Fast_Forward_0515.pdf" target="_blank">
       Fast Forward: The Changing Role of UK-based INGOs

     The rich discussion generated plenty of ideas to talk about, but today I focus on just one: How can academics support and strengthen data collection and research methodology in NGOs?
    </p>
    <p>
     This question, posed by BOND, is not unlike the
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/04/thinking-about-the-intellectual-future-of-higher-education.html#more-26855" target="_blank">
      heated debates
     </a>
     currently occurring in the discipline on the policy relevance and public value of political science. Yet, to date, we have mostly debated how to be more policy relevant for policy-makers (who often
     <a href="http://community.apsanet.org/Advocacy/home/" target="_blank">
      fund
     </a>
     our research), rather than how to work with less powerful groups like NGOs. What the Working Group discussion made clear though was that NGOs need us! The challenge is to figure out how to transfer capacities and skills to enable and support the work of NGOs. Heres the NGO practitioner wish list:

    <h2 class="post-title">
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/why-it-matters-whether-the-charleston-attack-was-terrorism.html" rel="bookmark" title="Why It Matters Whether the Charleston Attack Was Terrorism">
      Why It Matters Whether the Charleston Attack Was Terrorism
     </a>
    </h2>
    <div class="post-meta">
     <span class="post-date">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/why-it-matters-whether-the-charleston-attack-was-terrorism.html" title="Why It Matters Whether the Charleston Attack Was Terrorism">
       2015-06-22
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <span class="post-author">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/author/sweinberger" rel="author" title="Posts by Seth Weinberger">
       Seth Weinberger
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/why-it-matters-whether-the-charleston-attack-was-terrorism.html#comments">
      <span class="dsq-postid" rel="27140 http://duckofminerva.com/?p=27140">
       13 Comments
      </span>
     </a>
    </div>
   </div>
   <!-- /post-header -->
   <div class="post-content">
    <p>
     As a professor of international relations, I often have to radically adjust my syllabi from semester to semester. International politics changes so frequently that last years hot button issue is often no longer relevant the next time I teach a class. I offer my course on Terrorism every other year and its on the agenda for this coming fall. The last time I taught this class was Spring 2014. ISIS had just emerged on to the scene (the closest thing to
     <a href="http://www.washingtoninstitute.org/policy-analysis/view/al-qaeda-announces-an-islamic-state-in-syria">
      a formal announcement of ISISs existence
     </a>
     was April 2013, and only announced
     <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/world/2014/jun/29/isis-iraq-caliphate-delcaration-war">
      the establishment of the caliphate
     </a>
     in July 2014, after the class had concluded) and
     <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/world/middle_east/al-qaeda-disavows-any-ties-with-radical-islamist-isis-group-in-syria-iraq/2014/02/03/2c9afc3a-8cef-11e3-98ab-fe5228217bd1_story.html">
      the split between al Qaeda and ISIS
     </a>
     occurred during the semester. So, one of the things Im doing this summer is preparing my syllabus and refocusing it to deal more with ISIS. However, last weeks tragic and disgusting massacre in Charleston has given me something else to incorporate into the class. I already spend a day on domestic terrorism, but given all the reporting on
     <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2015/06/18/was-the-charleston-massacre-an-act-of-terrorism/">
      whether
     </a>
     Dylann Roofs heinous act should be
     <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2015/06/was-the-charleston-attack-terrorism/396329/">
      considered
     </a>
     <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/06/19/us/charleston-shooting-terrorism-or-hate-crime.html">
      terrorism
     </a>
     , Im probably going to work that question directly into the class.
    </p>
    <p>
     Theres been enough written about whether the attack should be classified as terrorism. I tend to think it shouldits clear that Roof was not simply trying to kill a select group of people but rather send a message (he
     <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/charleston-church-shooting-suspect-dylann-roof-in-police-custody-1434642237">
      left one person alive
     </a>
     to ensure his message would get out) in
     <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/06/21/us/dylann-storm-roof-photos-website-charleston-church-shooting.html">
      an attempt to create political change
     </a>
     . To me and many others, thats terrorism. But Im more interested in why it matters what we call it. Nine peoples lives have been horrifically cut short, so why should we dicker over terminology?
    </p>
    <p>
     <a class="more-link" href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/why-it-matters-whether-the-charleston-attack-was-terrorism.html#more-27140">

    <h2 class="post-title">
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/much-ado-about-nothing-very-intellectually-or-politically-important.html" rel="bookmark" title="Much Ado About Nothing (Very Intellectually or Politically Important)?">
      Much Ado About Nothing (Very Intellectually or Politically Important)?
     </a>
    </h2>
    <div class="post-meta">
     <span class="post-date">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/much-ado-about-nothing-very-intellectually-or-politically-important.html" title="Much Ado About Nothing (Very Intellectually or Politically Important)?">
       2015-06-18
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <span class="post-author">
      <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/author/amurdie" rel="author" title="Posts by amurdie">
       amurdie
      </a>
     </span>
     <span class="date-sep">
      /
     </span>
     <a href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/much-ado-about-nothing-very-intellectually-or-politically-important.html#comments">
      <span class="dsq-postid" rel="27134 http://duckofminerva.com/?p=27134">
       5 Comments
      </span>
     </a>
    </div>
   </div>
   <!-- /post-header -->
   <div class="post-content">
    <p>
     <em>
      The following is a guest post by
      <a href="http://polisci.indiana.edu/faculty/profiles/isaac.shtml" target="_blank">
       Jeffrey C. Isaac
      </a>
      , theJames H. Rudy Professor of Political Science at Indiana University, Bloomington.
     </em>
    </p>
    <p>
    </p>
    <p>
     What constitutes important political science research? This question has been much discussed lately in connection with When Contact Changes Minds: An Experiment on Transmission of Support for Gay Equality, an article by Michael J. LaCour and Donald P. Green published in
     <em>
      <a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/content/346/6215/1366" target="_blank">
       Science
      </a>
     </em>
     magazine.
    </p>
    <p>
     The reason for the attention is straightforward: because the piece was apparently based on fraudulent data, the article has become a veritable scandal. In the face of strong evidence that the articles lead author had engaged in repeated and willful misrepresentations, co-author Donald Green, a distinguished senior scholar, issued a retraction and dissociated himself from the piece, and
     <em>
      Science
     </em>
     magazine itself later followed with a retraction of its own.
    </p>
    <p>
     Fraud is almost always a serious ethical infraction and in some cases it constitutes a crime. In social science, and in the scholarly disciplines more generally, fraud, plagiarism, and other forms of deliberate misrepresentation are particularly egregious.
    </p>
    <p>
     Commentary on the scandal has centered on three questions: (1) how could LaCour behave in such an unprofessional manner, get away with it, persuade a senior scholar to sign on to tainted research, and have the work published in a top peer-reviewed scientific journal, without being exposed until after publication? (2) what kinds of collaborative research processes are involved in situations like this, involving scholars on opposite sides of a continent who are not well acquainted with one another, how common are such practices, and how common should they be? (3) what does this episode say about political science as a serious science that possesses the resources to critically evaluate and judge scientific contributions, to expose error much less fraud, and to credibly distinguish valid from invalid, and important from unimportant, knowledge claims?
    </p>
    <p>
     Each of these questions is important. But I would like here to press a fourth: what
     <em>
      is
     </em>
     political science, such that its practitioners might believe themselves to have something interesting to say about politics?
    </p>
    <p>
     <a class="more-link" href="http://duckofminerva.com/2015/06/much-ado-about-nothing-very-intellectually-or-politically-important.html#more-27134">
