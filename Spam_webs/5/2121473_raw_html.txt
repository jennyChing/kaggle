        <h2>
         About the film
        </h2>
        <p class="standfirst">
         An experimental account of the historical growth of personal freedom and civil rights in Britain using prints, animated sequences, and re-enactment.
        </p>
       </div>
       <a name="details">
       </a>
       <div class="panel">
        <h2>
         Details
        </h2>
        <dl class="details">
         <dt>
          Release year
         </dt>
         <dd>
          1946
         </dd>

         </dt>
         <dd>

         </dd>
         <dt>

         </dt>

        </h2>

         <br/>

        </p>
       </div>
       <a name="trivia">
       </a>
       <div class="panel">
        <h2>
         Trivia
        </h2>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <em>
           Magna Carta
          </em>
          was put into production in 1942, but was still unfinished in 1944 when the driving force behind it,
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Guedalla">
           Phillip Guedalla
          </a>
          (Chair of the British Council Film Committee), died. It is not thought that the film was ever completely finished or distributed. This copy of the film is thus believed to be an unfinished experiment, and the title credits state it to be An Experimental Treatment.
         </li>
         <li>
          Though there are no definite individual credits for
          <em>
           Magna Carta
          </em>
          , the British Council were considering actor
          <a href="http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0873754/">
           Cecil Trouncer
          </a>
          to be narrator and historian
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._M._Trevelyan">
           George Trevelyan
          </a>
          to write the script.
          <a href="http://www.screenonline.org.uk/people/id/581940/index.html">
           Mary Field
          </a>
          was also involved in production.
         </li>
         <li>
          Phillip Guedalla, himself a historical writer, was keen to produce a film on this topic, but had opinions on its format from the outset. He wrote to another member of the British Council FIlm Department, It might be necessary to reconstruct the signing of
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magna_Carta">
           Magna Carta
          </a>
          itself, though I hope this could be done with a minimum of false beards.
         </li>
         <li>
          The animations in
          <em>
           Magna Carta
          </em>
          were produced by
          <a href="http://www.animatormag.com/archive/issue-19/issue-19-page-25/%23.T6BSLMRYu0E">
           Gaumont-British Animation
          </a>
          , a studio set up by
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Arthur_Rank,_1st_Baron_Rank">
           J. Arthur Rank
          </a>
          in order to challenge
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney">
           Disney
          </a>
          s supremacy in the animation market.
          <em>
           Magna Carta
          </em>
          is thought to be one of the earliest examples of its work. Famed animator
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hand_(animator)">
           David Hand
          </a>
          , supervising director on Disneys
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow_White_and_the_Seven_Dwarfs_(1937_film)">
           <em>
            Snow White and the Seven Dwarves
           </em>
          </a>
          <em>
          </em>
          (1937)
          <em>
          </em>
          and
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bambi">
           <em>
            Bambi
           </em>
          </a>
          (1942) also worked on
          <em>
           Magna Carta
          </em>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
