   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Cheese-eating surrender monkeys
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheese_eating_surrender_monkeys&amp;redirect=no" title="Cheese eating surrender monkeys">
       Cheese eating surrender monkeys
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      "
      <b>
       Cheese-eating surrender monkeys
      </b>
      ", sometimes shortened to "
      <b>
       surrender monkeys
      </b>
      ", is a pejorative term for the
      <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">
       French people
      </a>
      . The term was coined in 1995, by
      <a href="/wiki/Ken_Keeler" title="Ken Keeler">
       Ken Keeler
      </a>
      , a writer for the television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">
        The Simpsons
       </a>
      </i>
      . The term has entered two Oxford quotation dictionaries. In February 2003, during the early phases of the
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">
       Iraq War
      </a>
      , the term was used by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">
        National Review
       </a>
      </i>
      journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Jonah_Goldberg" title="Jonah Goldberg">
       Jonah Goldberg
      </a>
      . In April 1999, he previously used the term.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
       Origin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheese-eating_surrender_monkeys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term "cheese-eating surrender monkeys" first appeared in "
      <a href="/wiki/%27Round_Springfield" title="'Round Springfield">
       'Round Springfield
      </a>
      ," an April 1995 episode of the American animated television show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">
        The Simpsons
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardianYoungeHenley_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-guardianYoungeHenley-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the episode, budget cuts at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Springfield_Elementary_School" title="Springfield Elementary School">
       Springfield Elementary School
      </a>
      force the Scottish janitor,
      <a href="/wiki/Groundskeeper_Willie" title="Groundskeeper Willie">
       Groundskeeper Willie
      </a>
      , to teach French. Expressing his disdain for the French people, he says to his French class in his Scottish accent: "Bonjoooouuurrr, ya cheese-eatin' surrender monkeys!"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETurner200454_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETurner200454-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERichmond_.26_Coffman1997173_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichmond_.26_Coffman1997173-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">
       audio commentary
      </a>
      for the episode, executive producer
      <a href="/wiki/Al_Jean" title="Al Jean">
       Al Jean
      </a>
      said the line was "probably" written by
      <i>
       The Simpsons
      </i>
      staff writer
      <a href="/wiki/Ken_Keeler" title="Ken Keeler">
       Ken Keeler
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jean-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 2012, Keeler confirmed this in an interview, and stated that he considers it to be his best contribution to the show.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keeler_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Keeler-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Jean commented that the staff did not expect the term to become widely used and never intended it as any kind of genuine political statement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jean-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When "'Round Springfield" was dubbed in French, the line became "Rendez-vous, singes mangeurs de fromage" ("Surrender, you cheese-eating monkeys").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benm_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-benm-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use">
       Use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheese-eating_surrender_monkeys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Use of the term has grown outside of the United States, particularly in the United Kingdom, where
      <i>
       The Simpsons
      </i>
      is extremely popular.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term was used by
      <a href="/wiki/Jonah_Goldberg" title="Jonah Goldberg">
       Jonah Goldberg
      </a>
      , a conservative American
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">
        National Review
       </a>
      </i>
      journalist in April 1999, as the title of a column called "Top Ten Reasons to Hate the French".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldberg1_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldberg1-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the run-up to and during the
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">
       Iraq War
      </a>
      , Goldberg reprised the phrase to criticize European nations and France in particular for not joining the United States in its invasion and occupation of Iraq.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardianYoungeHenley_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-guardianYoungeHenley-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Macintyre" title="Ben Macintyre">
       Ben Macintyre
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
        Times of London
       </a>
      </i>
      wrote in August 2007, that the term is "perhaps the most famous" of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_neologisms_on_The_Simpsons" title="List of neologisms on The Simpsons">
       coinages from
       <i>
        The Simpsons
       </i>
      </a>
      and it "has gone on to become a journalistic clich."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benm_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-benm-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term was used by the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">
        New York Post
       </a>
      </i>
      (as "Surrender Monkeys") as the headline for its December 7, 2006, front page, referring to the
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq_Study_Group" title="Iraq Study Group">
       Iraq Study Group
      </a>
      and its recommendation that U.S. soldiers be withdrawn from
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">
       Iraq
      </a>
      by January 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBP_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PBP-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Articles in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">
        Daily Mail
       </a>
      </i>
      (2005 &amp; 2009) used the term to describe France's "attitude problem"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the "muted" European reaction to the
      <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Osama_bin_Laden" title="Death of Osama bin Laden">
       death of Osama bin Laden
      </a>
      ;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
        The Daily Telegraph
       </a>
      </i>
      (November 2010) cited it in relation to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-French_military_cooperation" title="Anglo-French military cooperation">
       Anglo-French military cooperation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2013,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">
        The Independent
       </a>
      </i>
      suggested an evolution away from the term, in a headline about
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French-American_relations" title="French-American relations">
       French-American relations
      </a>
      over the
      <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Civil_War" title="Syrian Civil War">
       Syrian Civil War
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term was used on an episode of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Top_Gear_(2002_TV_series)" title="Top Gear (2002 TV series)">
        Top Gear
       </a>
      </i>
      , on 4 June 2006, when former host
      <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Clarkson" title="Jeremy Clarkson">
       Jeremy Clarkson
      </a>
      used the term to describe the manufacturers of the
      <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C6" title="Citron C6">
       Citron C6
      </a>
      . The term was previously used on
      <i>
       Top Gear
      </i>
      in June 2003, when
      <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Clarkson" title="Jeremy Clarkson">
       Jeremy Clarkson
      </a>
      used it to describe the handling of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renault_Clio_V6" title="Renault Clio V6">
       Renault Clio V6
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2005, the phrase was used by
      <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Farage" title="Nigel Farage">
       Nigel Farage
      </a>
      , who said of the thenFrench President,
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">
       Jacques Chirac
      </a>
      , "No cheese-eating surrender monkey, he", in his unflattering comparison to thenPrime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">
       Tony Blair
      </a>
      , during a European Parliament session. The term was selected by
      <a href="/wiki/Ned_Sherrin" title="Ned Sherrin">
       Ned Sherrin
      </a>
      , for inclusion in the
      <i>
       Oxford Dictionary of Humorous Quotations
      </i>
      , being introduced in the third edition in 2005.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also included in the
      <i>
       Oxford Dictionary of Modern Quotations
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term was used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Parliament" title="Australian Parliament">
       Australian Parliament
      </a>
      , on 6 March 2014, when Opposition Leader
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Shorten" title="Bill Shorten">
       Bill Shorten
      </a>
      described the
      <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Australia" title="Government of Australia">
       Government of Australia
      </a>
      as "the cheese-eating surrender monkeys of Australian jobs".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Upon being asked to withdraw the comment, Shorten claimed the line was borrowed from an American politician, whom he could not name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term "surrender monkeys" was again used in Australian politics, on 28 July 2014, by Immigration Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Scott_Morrison_(politician)" title="Scott Morrison (politician)">
       Scott Morrison
      </a>
      , describing the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_Party_of_Australia" title="Labor Party of Australia">
       Labor
      </a>
      and Greens position on asylum seekers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
