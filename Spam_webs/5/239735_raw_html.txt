         <p>
          At the University of Southern California, a robot is teaching an autistic child.  At the University of California and the University of Washington, the same thing is happening.  A new breed of robots have been designed to work with and teach children.  They are discovering that these robots seem to have the ability to connect with autistic children in a unique and beneficial way.  You can read a complete article about this phenomomen on
          <a href="http://www.ndtv.com/article/sci-tech/students-meet-your-new-teacher-mr-robot-36799" rel="nofollow">
           NDTV
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Some people argue that robots should not be used to teach children.  I have to disagree.  As a parent of an autistic child, I know first hand how difficult it can be to connect with your autistic child and to develop appropriate teaching methods for your child.  Although our children have a lot of similarities, they also tend to have their own way of learning.
         </p>
         <p>
          To find a fun and creative way to reach our children is a goal we all have.  When we find something that will engage and teach our child, we support it.  No matter what critics may say.
         </p>
         <p>
          I dont have a University near me that is using robots with autistic children.  I wish I did.  I can vision my son interacting and learning from such a robot.  Our local firestation has a robot fire truck.  It operates and talks by remote control.  It is usually controlled by a fireman that sits in the background, away from the robot.  My son loves the robot fire truck.
         </p>
         <p>
          Most of the advanced robots working with the children are fully autonomous, and learn themselves by teaching.  Their artificial intelligence is designed to learn and recognize repetitive behaviours, signs of upset or confusion.  As parents, we watch for these signs all the time.  They are an important factor in our childs learning.  We are always teaching others to watch for these signs.  More often then we care for, the professionals that work with our children miss these signs when working with out child.  Imagine how our child would respond to a robot that can pick up on these behaviors and adapt its teaching methods immediately to accommodate our child.  I say Hooray! for technology and Hooray! for robots.
         </p>
