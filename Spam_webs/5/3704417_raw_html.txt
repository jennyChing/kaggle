     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <b>
        Future History
       </b>
      </i>
      , by
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
       Robert A. Heinlein
      </a>
      , describes a projected future of the human race from the middle of the 20th century through the early 23rd century. The term
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      was coined by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_W._Campbell,_Jr." title="John W. Campbell, Jr.">
       John W. Campbell, Jr.
      </a>
      in the February 1941 issue of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astounding_Science_Fiction" title="Astounding Science Fiction">
        Astounding Science Fiction
       </a>
      </i>
      . Campbell published an early draft of Heinlein's chart of the series in the March 1941 issue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Heinlein wrote most of the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      stories early in his career, between 1939 and 1941 and between 1945 and 1950. Most of the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      stories written prior to 1967 are collected in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Past_Through_Tomorrow" title="The Past Through Tomorrow">
        The Past Through Tomorrow
       </a>
      </i>
      , which also contains the final version of the chart. That collection does not include
      <i>
       Universe
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Common Sense
      </i>
      ; they were published separately as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Orphans_of_the_Sky" title="Orphans of the Sky">
        Orphans of the Sky
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      was nominated for the
      <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">
       Hugo Award
      </a>
      for Best All-Time Series in 1966, along with the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">
        Barsoom
       </a>
      </i>
      series by
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Rice_Burroughs" title="Edgar Rice Burroughs">
       Edgar Rice Burroughs
      </a>
      , the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lensman" title="Lensman">
        Lensman
       </a>
      </i>
      series by
      <a href="/wiki/E._E._Smith" title="E. E. Smith">
       E. E. Smith
      </a>
      , the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Foundation_Series" title="The Foundation Series">
        Foundation
       </a>
      </i>
      series by
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
       Isaac Asimov
      </a>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">
        The Lord of the Rings
       </a>
      </i>
      series by
      <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">
       J. R. R. Tolkien
      </a>
      , but lost to Asimov's
      <i>
       Foundation
      </i>
      series.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Future_History_(Heinlein)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For the most part,
      <i>
       The Past Through Tomorrow
      </i>
      defines a core group of stories that are clearly within the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      series. However, Heinlein scholars generally agree that some stories not included in the anthology belong to the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      series, and that some included are only weakly linked to it.
     </p>
     <p>
      James Gifford
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      adds
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Enough_for_Love" title="Time Enough for Love">
        Time Enough for Love
       </a>
      </i>
      , which was published after
      <i>
       The Past Through Tomorrow
      </i>
      , and also
      <a href="/wiki/Let_There_Be_Light_(Heinlein_short_story)" title="Let There Be Light (Heinlein short story)">
       "Let There Be Light"
      </a>
      , which was not included in
      <i>
       The Past Through Tomorrow
      </i>
      , possibly because the collection editor disliked it or because Heinlein himself considered it to be inferior. However, he considers
      <i>
       Time Enough for Love
      </i>
      to be a borderline case. He considers
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Number_of_the_Beast_(novel)" title="The Number of the Beast (novel)">
        The Number of the Beast
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Cat_Who_Walks_Through_Walls" title="The Cat Who Walks Through Walls">
        The Cat Who Walks Through Walls
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/To_Sail_Beyond_the_Sunset" title="To Sail Beyond the Sunset">
        To Sail Beyond the Sunset
       </a>
      </i>
      to be too weakly linked to the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      to be included.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bill Patterson
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      includes
      <i>
       To Sail Beyond the Sunset
      </i>
      , on the theory that the discrepancies between it and the rest of the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      are explained by assigning it to the same "bundle of related timelines" in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_as_Myth" title="World as Myth">
       World as Myth
      </a>
      multiverse. However, he lists a number of stories that he believes were never really intended to be part of
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      , even though they were included in
      <i>
       The Past Through Tomorrow
      </i>
      : "
      <a href="/wiki/Life-Line" title="Life-Line">
       Life-Line
      </a>
      " (which was written before Heinlein published the Future History chart), "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Menace_from_Earth" title="The Menace from Earth">
       The Menace from Earth
      </a>
      ", "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/We_Also_Walk_Dogs" title="We Also Walk Dogs">
       We Also Walk Dogs
      </a>
      ", and the stories originally published in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_Evening_Post" title="Saturday Evening Post">
        Saturday Evening Post
       </a>
      </i>
      ("
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Jockey_(short_story)" title="Space Jockey (short story)">
       Space Jockey
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/It%27s_Great_to_Be_Back!" title="It's Great to Be Back!">
       It's Great to Be Back!
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Hills_of_Earth" title="The Green Hills of Earth">
       The Green Hills of Earth
      </a>
      ", and "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Pits_of_Luna" title="The Black Pits of Luna">
       The Black Pits of Luna
      </a>
      "). He agrees with Gifford that "Let There Be Light" should be included. The story
      <a href="/wiki/%22%E2%80%94And_He_Built_a_Crooked_House%E2%80%94%22" title='"And He Built a Crooked House"'>
       "And He Built a Crooked House"
      </a>
      was included only in the pre-war chart and never since.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Heinlein_juveniles" title="Heinlein juveniles">
       Heinlein juveniles
      </a>
      do not hew closely to the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      outline. Gifford states that "Although the twelve juvenile novels are not completely inconsistent with the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      , neither do they form a thorough match with that series for adult readers. It is not often recognized that they are a reasonably consistent 'Future History' of their own... At least one major story specified in the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      chart, the revolution on Venus, ended up being told in the framework of the juveniles as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Between_Planets" title="Between Planets">
        Between Planets
       </a>
      </i>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Variable_Star" title="Variable Star">
        Variable Star
       </a>
      </i>
      , written by
      <a href="/wiki/Spider_Robinson" title="Spider Robinson">
       Spider Robinson
      </a>
      from Heinlein's detailed outline, incorporates some elements of both the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      (such as references to Nehemiah Scudder) and the universe of the Heinlein juveniles (for example, torch ships and faster-than-light telepathic communication between twins). The adult short story "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Long_Watch" title="The Long Watch">
       The Long Watch
      </a>
      ", included in
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      story collections, connects to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Cadet" title="Space Cadet">
        Space Cadet
       </a>
      </i>
      through the character of (John) Ezra Dahlquist, the central character of the first, memorialized in the second.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stories_never_written">
       Stories never written
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Future_History_(Heinlein)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stories never written">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The chart published in the collection
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Revolt_in_2100" title="Revolt in 2100">
        Revolt in 2100
       </a>
      </i>
      includes several unwritten stories, which Heinlein describes in a postscript. "Fire Down Below," about a revolution in Antarctica, would have been set in the early 21st century. Three more unwritten stories fill in the history from just before "
      <a href="/wiki/Logic_of_Empire" title="Logic of Empire">
       Logic of Empire
      </a>
      " in the early 21st century through the beginning of "
      <a href="/wiki/If_This_Goes_On%E2%80%94" title="If This Goes On">
       If This Goes On
      </a>
      ". "The Sound of His Wings" covers Nehemiah Scudder's early life as a television evangelist through his rise to power as the First Prophet. "Eclipse" describes independence movements on Mars and Venus. "The Stone Pillow" details the rise of the resistance movement from the early days of the theocracy through the beginning of "
      <a href="/wiki/If_This_Goes_On%E2%80%94" title="If This Goes On">
       If This Goes On
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      These stories were key points in the
      <i>
       Future History
      </i>
      , so Heinlein gave a rough description of Nehemiah Scudder which made his reign easy to visualizea combination of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">
       John Calvin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">
       Girolamo Savonarola
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Franklin_Rutherford" title="Joseph Franklin Rutherford">
       Joseph Franklin Rutherford
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Huey_Long" title="Huey Long">
       Huey Long
      </a>
      . His rise to power began when one of his flock, the widow of a wealthy man who would have disapproved of Scudder, died and left him enough money to establish a television station. He then teamed up with an ex-
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">
       Senator
      </a>
      and hired a major
      <a href="/wiki/Advertising_agency" title="Advertising agency">
       advertising agency
      </a>
      . He was soon famous even off-worldmany
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonded_labor" title="Bonded labor">
       bonded laborers
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Logic_of_Empire" title="Logic of Empire">
       Venus
      </a>
      saw him as a messianic figure. He had muscle as wella recreation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">
       Ku Klux Klan
      </a>
      in everything but name. "Blood at the polls and blood in the streets, but Scudder won the election. The next election was never held." Though this period was integral to the human diaspora that would follow several hundred years later, Heinlein stated that he was never able to write them because they featured Scudder prominently; he "dislike(d) him too much".
     </p>
     <p>
      Nehemiah Scudder already appears in Heinlein's earliest novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/For_Us,_The_Living:_A_Comedy_of_Customs" title="For Us, The Living: A Comedy of Customs">
        For Us, The Living: A Comedy of Customs
       </a>
      </i>
      (written 19381939, though first published in 2003). Scudder's early career as depicted in that book is virtually identical with the abovebut with the crucial difference that in the earlier version Scudder is stopped at the last moment by the counter-mobilization of
      <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">
       Libertarians
      </a>
      , and despite mass voter intimidation carries only
      <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">
       Tennessee
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">
       Alabama
      </a>
      . In fact, the Libertarian regime seen in full bloom in that book's 2086 came into being in direct reaction to Scudder's attempt to impose Puritanical mores on the entire American society.
     </p>
