Bl\u00e9riot became the first person to fly across the English Channel in an airplane. This week, three highly modern airplanes attempted the same feat, for a much smaller place in history: Which would be the first fully electric two-engined airplane to make the leap from Europe to the isle of Great Britain? As of this afternoon, it appears at least two planes crossed successfully, but there\u2019s now a dispute over which was first.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.popsci.com\/technology\/article\/2013-06\/paris-air-show-2013?image=0\u0022\u003EAirbus E-Fan\u003C\/a\u003E looks like the tiny, electric-smartcar version of an A-10 fighter. Powered only by batteries, it has two fans situated behind the cockpit, promising up to 45 minutes of flight time with a top speed of 137 mph. Today, the E-Fan cross the channel in about 40 minutes. Here\u2019s a gif of it landing in England:\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cdiv\u003E\u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/gfycat.com\/QuestionableSlightAnkolewatusi\u0022 class=\u0022embedly-card\u0022\u003E\u003C\/a\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThere\u2019s a good chance that the E-Fan\u2019s channel crossing may be less historic than thought. Last night, pilot Hugues Duwal appeared to cross the channel in a tiny Columban Cri-Cri. It\u2019s name comes \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.popsci.com\/archive-viewer?id=hAEAAAAAMBAJ\u0026amp;pg=96\u0022\u003Efrom French for \u201ccricket\u201d\u003C\/a\u003E, and it is an unbelievably tiny airplane. If the E-Fan is a Smart Car, the Cri-Cri designed in the 1970s, resembles nothing so much as a \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.peelengineering.com\/peels\/peel-trident\u0022\u003EPeel Trident\u003C\/a\u003E, the world\u2019s second-smallest car. The Cri-Cri is a half-sized cockpit put into the middle of a quarter-scale plane, with two small engines powering propellers that stick out in front of the cockpit like catfish whiskers. The wingspan is just 16 feet, so flying the whole plane is like \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.aopa.org\/News-and-Video\/All-News\/2011\/January\/1\/Big-fun-in-a-small-package\u0022\u003Ewearing giant wings\u003C\/a\u003E. If Duwal\u2019s flight was indeed successful, and his CriCri was indeed an \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/web.archive.org\/web\/20100629192653\/http:\/\/classic.eads.net\/1024\/en\/pressdb\/pressdb\/20100618_eads_cri-cri.html\u0022\u003Eelectric one\u003C\/a\u003E and not the two-stroke engines common to Cri-Cris, then he may have under the cover of darkness snuck past Airbus and into the history books, or at least the books of minor aviation feats.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003E\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cdiv class=\u0022media media-element-container media-default full-width\u0022\u003E\u003Cimg class=\u0022full-width\u0022 typeof=\u0022foaf:Image\u0022 width=\u0022600\u0022 height=\u0022255\u0022 alt=\u0022Hugues Duwal In His Electric Cri-Cri\u0022 src=\u0022http:\/\/www.popsci.com\/sites\/popsci.com\/files\/styles\/medium_1x_\/public\/cri-cri_electric.png?itok=i98ywuc5\u0022 \/\u003E\u003Cdiv class=\u0022field-title\u0022\u003E\u003Cp\u003EHugues Duwal In His Electric Cri-Cri\u003C\/p\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003Cdiv class=\u0022field-credit\u0022\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAnubis2202, via Wikimedia Commons \u003Ca href=\u0022https:\/\/creativecommons.org\/licenses\/by-sa\/3.0\/deed.en\u0022\u003ECC BY-SA 3.0\u003C\/a\u003E\u003C\/p\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003Cp\u003EHilariously, a third electric plane was also supposed to cross the channel this week. The Pipistrel Alpha Electro (actual prototype name: \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.pipistrel.si\/plane\/wattsup\/overview\u0022\u003EWATTsUP\u003C\/a\u003E) was supposed to cross the channel on Wednesday, but Siemens, who provided the engine to the Electro, recalled it, claiming that the engine \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.australianflying.com.au\/news\/privateer-trumps-airbus-in-electric-battle-for-channel-crossing\u0022\u003Ewasn\u2019t approved for over-water flight\u003C\/a\u003E. In what can only be an unrelated matter, Siemens also provided the engines for the E-Fan.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EWhile this is absurd theatrics for a minor first, it\u2019s a promising sign for the future of electric aviation, and hearkens back to the early days of human flight, with multiple claims of firsts.               <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Robotic Flight Simulator
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                DLR
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              When it comes to flying, one of the ways to become a better pilot is through
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/tags/flight-simulators">
               flight simulations
              </a>
              , but these can often feel unrealistic. To fix that, researchers in Germany have developed a flight simulator that uses a robotic arm to make virtual flying seem real. As the pilot flies his way around a virtual sky complete with takeoffs and landings, the arm moves the simulator cockpit up and down and all around. The researchers hope this technology will soon be used for pilot training around the world.
             </p>
             <p>
              Watch a video of the robo simulator at our sister publication,
              <a href="http://www.flyingmag.com/training/recurrent-trainingsimulators/video-amazing-robot-controlled-flight-simulator">
