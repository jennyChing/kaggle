        <p>
         <em>
          <strong>
           Resignations of prominent Liberal Party members last month highlight deep rifts within the party. They bring to ten the areas of failure which taken together suggest the party is in terminal decline.
           <a href="http://www.independentaustralia.net/profile-on/alan-austin,67">
            Alan Austin
           </a>
           reports.
          </strong>
         </em>
        </p>
        <h4>
         <strong>
          1. Resignations and defections
         </strong>
        </h4>
        <p>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Hamilton-Smith" target="_blank">
          Martin Hamilton-Smith
         </a>
         is a former leader of the South Australian Liberal Party and
         <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/world/2014/may/27/south-australian-liberal-mp-martin-hamilton-smith-defects">
          regarded
         </a>
         as its most capable parliamentarian. This week he joined a long list of respected MPs and elder statesmen who have found they can no longer stay within the Liberal Party of Australia as it currently operates.
        </p>
        <p>
         Hamilton-Smith is now an independent member of South Australias Labor cabinet.
        </p>
        <p>
         One of the more notable Queensland Liberal National MPs was
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Davis_(politician)">
          Dr Chris Davis
         </a>
         , a past president of the Australian Medical AssociationsQueensland branch and chairman of theRoyal Australasian College of Physicians.
        </p>
        <p>
         He could no longer stomach the direction Liberal National Party (LNP) premier Campbell Newman was taking the government. He protested over several issues, including
         <a href="http://www.independentaustralia.net/politics/politics-display/newman-government-kills-queenslands-corruption-watchdog,6473" target="_blank">
          changes to
         </a>
         the Crime and Misconduct Commission, and finally

         But first, a short detour through history.

        <p>
         It is not only minor parties such as the
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Democrats" target="_blank">
          Democrats
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Nation_(Australia)">
          One Nation
         </a>
         thathave disappeared after periods of fame.
        </p>
        <p>
         Australias first dominant Federal party was the
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectionist_Party">
          Protectionist Party
         </a>
         , formed in 1889. It held government from 1901 to 1904 and from 1905 to 1908 with
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Barton" target="_blank">
          Edmund Barton
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Deakin" target="_blank">
          Alfred Deakin
         </a>
         as prime ministers.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Nationalist Party, formed by
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billy_Hughes" target="_blank">
          Billy Hughes
         </a>
         in 1917, governed for almost 13 years from 1917 to 1929.
        </p>
        <p>
         The United Australia Party ruled for most of the period from 1931 to 1941.
        </p>
        <p>
         All have since disappeared, although major policies continued in other party structures.
        </p>
        <p>
         Reasons for their demise vary. Commonalities appear to be internal divisions, shifting away from serving mainstream community interests and the presence of a strong alternative political force.
        </p>
        <p>
         So the fact that the Liberal Party has been successful for some decades is no guarantee it will remain. Indeed, there are definite signs that its downfall may be imminent.
        </p>
