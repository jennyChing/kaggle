           <h1 class="entry-title margin-bottom-30">
            Antarcticas amazing striped icebergs
           </h1>
           <div class="entry-meta margin-bottom-30 hidden-print" style="display:none">
            <span class="posted-on">
             <time class="entry-date published pull-left" datetime="2010-09-10T15:04:40+00:00">
              September 10, 2010
             </time>
             <time class="updated hidden" datetime="2013-03-20T16:55:55+00:00">
              March 20, 2013
             </time>
            </span>
            <span class="byline">
             <span class="author vcard">
              <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.zmescience.com/author/osamik/" rel="author">
               Mihai Andrei
              </a>
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
           <!-- .entry-meta -->
          </header>
          <!-- .entry-header -->
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            <a href="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/iceberg01_428x269_to_468x312.jpg">
             <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3532" height="294" src="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/iceberg01_428x269_to_468x312.jpg" title="iceberg01_428x269_to_468x312" width="468"/>
            </a>
            When you think of
            <a href="http://www.zmescience.com/tag/antarctica">
             Antarctica
            </a>
            , pristine w
            <a href="http://www.zmescience.com/ecology/environmental-issues/iceberg-twice-the-size-of-manhattan-greenland-3143223/">
             hite icebergs
            </a>
            usually come to mind; probably the last thing youd expect are humbug-like striped icebergs, with blue, green, yellow and even brown.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <a href="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/striped-iceberg4.jpg">
             <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3534" height="640" src="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/striped-iceberg4.jpg" title="striped-iceberg4" width="466"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            The different colours appear due to various reasons, but generally speaking they appear because some layers of ice form in special conditions. Blue stripes are the most common, and they appear when crevices are filled with water and they freeze so fast that no bubbles are formed. However, things are different with the other ones.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/marbled_iceberg4.jpg">
             <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3537" height="314" src="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/marbled_iceberg4.jpg" title="marbled_iceberg4" width="472"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            Green appears because the water that freezes is extremely
            <a href="http://www.zmescience.com/ecology/environmental-issues/algae-bloom-arctic-region-0062012/">
             rich in algae
            </a>
            , hence the colour. Brown, yellow, and even black stripes are caused by sediments picked up along the way when the
            <a href="http://www.zmescience.com/tag/ice-sheet">
             ice sheet
            </a>
            was sliding downhill.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/striped-iceberg.jpg">
             <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3535" height="338" src="http://cdn.zmescience.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/striped-iceberg.jpg" title="striped iceberg" width="450"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p class="entry-stuff clear margin-bottom-15 hidden-print single-post-ads-between center-block text-center">
            <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
            </script>

           </p>
           <p>
            Theyre not exactly rare, but quite uncommon still. It was Norwegian sailor Oyvind Tangen, on board a research ship that first photographed and described
            <a href="http://www.zmescience.com/research/studies/warmer-oceans-lead-to-faster-glacier-melting-321313/">
             these glaciers
            </a>
            (from what I was able to find, Im not quite sure however). Anyway, theyre an astonishing sight by any standards, and its easy to understand why sailors have pictured them as humbugs. If youre ever lucky enough to go to Antarctica, keep an eye out  you just might see one of these awesome wonders !
