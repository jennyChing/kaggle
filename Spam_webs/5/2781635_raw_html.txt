            and National Geographic television associate producer
            <b>
             J.J. Kelley
            </b>
            have spent a year of their lives together trekking
           </p>
           <p>
            through land and water. That includes the five months they spent on
           </p>
           <p>
            the Appalachian Trail, where they first met 400
           </p>
           <p>
            miles into what each of them thought would be a solo hike. In 2006, with
           </p>
           <p>
            mountain bikes and cameras in tow, they headed to Alaska for their second
           </p>
           <p>
            adventure: a 1200-mile bike ride from the Pacific Ocean to the Arctic, out of
           </p>
           <p>
            which came
            <i>
             <a href="http://www.dudesonmedia.com/PEDAL_TO_THE_MIDNIGHT_SUN.html">
              Pedal to the Midnight Sun
             </a>
            </i>
            , their first documentary film.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            Their second
           </p>
           <p>
            film,
            <i>
             <a href="http://www.dudesonmedia.com/PADDLE_TO_SEATTLE.html">
              Paddle to Seattle
             </a>
            </i>
            , tells the story of their most recent journey: 96
           </p>
           <p>
            days spent paddling from Skagway to Seattle through the Inside Passage in sea kayaks that they crafted by hand from pygmy wooden kits. The film is
            <a href="http://www.dudesonmedia.com/SCREENINGS.html">
             currently screening
            </a>
            at film
           </p>
           <p>
            festivals, and
            <i>
             <a href="http://www.paddlermagazine.com/media/dvd-book-reviews/in-real-time.html">
              Paddler
             </a>
            </i>
            <span style="font-style: normal;">
             <a href="http://www.paddlermagazine.com/media/dvd-book-reviews/in-real-time.html">
              magazine
             </a>
             dubbed it the best feature film about
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            paddling produced in the last decade. It has won the 2009 Port Townsend Film
           </p>
           <p>
            Festival Audience Award and took home the Best Documentary prize at the Minneapolis Underground Film Festival last week. Its screening this week at the Anchorage Film Festival, and will be screened at National Geographic headquarters in Washington D.C. on February 16th, 2010. I spoke with them about planning their adventures, refrigeration tactics, and adjusting to life after being out on the open water for over three months.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             <i>
              What led you to paddle the Inside Passage?
             </i>
             <br/>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             J.J.:
            </b>
            Josh said a long time ago, Theres no reason that you
           </p>
           <p>
            shouldnt be able to do what you love in life. And theres no reason that
           </p>
           <p>
            shouldnt be a viable option for you to be prosperous. For us it was these
           </p>
           <p>
            trips, going out and experiencing these other places and then just learning how other people and animals inhabit our planet. The more time you have an
           </p>
           <p>
            opportunity to see all thats out there, the hungrier it makes you to continue
           </p>
           <p>
            to follow that path. That longing brought us to the sea-kayaking trip.
            <span style="">
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              Did you become acquainted with the outdoors while growing
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            up?
            <br/>
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             Josh:
            </b>
            I grew up on a hobby farm where we had chicken and
           </p>
           <p>
            goats and all kinds of farm animals. We had a fair amount of property and
           </p>
           <p>
            forest around the house. Ive always been real close to nature. This was a
           </p>
           <p>
            natural progression to become more comfortable in it.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <i>
             <b>
              How did you prepare to paddle from Juneau to Seattle?
             </b>
            </i>
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Josh:
            </b>
            We both did a fair amount of sea kayaking beforehand.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its low impact enough that we more or less just eased into it. The first few
           </p>
           <p>
            days we were doing five miles a day as opposed to halfway though the trip when
           </p>
           <p>
            we were doing fifteen to twenty-mile days.
           </p>
           <p>
            <!--EndFragment-->
           </p>
           <p>
            <span id="more-2577">
            </span>
            <br/>
            <b>
             <i>
              What was your diet like?
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Josh: A lot of fat. We always had a lot of butter. I fell in love with mayonnaise. It had lots of calories in it. With the water
           </p>
           <p>
            being 45 degrees, you could keep a block of cheese for two weeks; you could
           </p>
           <p>
            carry a pound of butter. We even started carrying half-and-half for our coffee.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             J.J.:
            </b>
            We ate 5,000 to 6,000 calories a day. We ate fish.
           </p>
           <p>
            Josh did a lot of the fishing. Wed eat one or two of those a day. They were
           </p>
           <p>
            small rockfish that hed catch with a hand line that hed reel down and pull up
           </p>
           <p>
            and bring it to shore. Hed slice it in half and start a fire near the tide
           </p>
           <p>
            line. Wed cook it there, eat it there and as the high tide came up it would
           </p>
           <p>
            wash away the scent.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <i>
             <b>
              How did you interact with the communities along the way?
             </b>
            </i>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             Josh:
            </b>
            We wanted to be a part of the community. When we come
           </p>
           <p>
            into a town we help out wherever we could just to get involved somehow and
           </p>
           <p>
            people liked that. Halfway through the trip we volunteered for a swim race that
           </p>
           <p>
            took place in the ocean. We were safety boaters in our kayaks. We stopped at a lighthouse along
           </p>
           <p>
            the way south, which was completely unplanned. We ended up spending three or
           </p>
           <p>
            four days volunteering.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             J.J.:
            </b>
            We felt privileged anytime we got to stay somewhere
           </p>
           <p>
            and they shared their lives with us, so wed get groceries for them or make them
           </p>
           <p>
            dinner. Just small tiny things that were so easy for us to do but would allow
           </p>
           <p>
            us a view into their world. For us its never like were trying to overcome
           </p>
           <p>
            these wild places. We really truly do it because we enjoy it and we just try to
           </p>
           <p>
            make the connection to the place. People saw that we were just about having
           </p>
           <p>
            fun, not trying to prove anything.
            <br/>
            <i>
             <br/>
            </i>
            <b>
             <i>
              After you reached Seattle, what did you walk away with?
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             Josh:
            </b>
            Just having
           </p>
           <p>
            that time to reflect I think is the biggest thing to walk away from. You have a lot of time to
           </p>
           <p>
            think and reflect on all kinds of things, mostly what your future looks like,
           </p>
           <p>
            where you see yourself in a year from now or ten years from now.  When
           </p>
           <p>
            youre back at home doing your job and going through the motions, its really
           </p>
           <p>
            hard to step away sometimes even for an hour and to reflect on things. But when
           </p>
           <p>
            you have three months at eight hours a day, you get to know yourself very well.
           </p>
           <p>
            And thats huge just to go inside your brain to try to make yourself a better
           </p>
           <p>
            person. You learn just to be yourself.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             J.J.:
            </b>
            Knowing yourself better transpires into more
           </p>
           <p>
            confidence in yourself in your everyday life. Setting out to do a big goal that
           </p>
           <p>
            you follow through with also relates to that as well. If you can do Seattle,
           </p>
           <p>
            you can get a job at National Geographic, or you can build a cabinJosh just
           </p>
           <p>
            built a two-story cabin all by himself.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <i>
             <b>
              Whats next?
             </b>
            </i>
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             Josh:
            </b>
            There are definitely a number of trips that Id like
           </p>
           <p>
            to still do but its hard to lock down and start planning so soon. It seems
           </p>
           <p>
            were still shaking off the paddle to Seattle and just getting into it with the
           </p>
           <p>
            film.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <b>
             J.J.:
            </b>
            Our thought is to see where this project goes.
           </p>
           <p>
            Wed like to see this project grow. Were taking it around the country and
           </p>
           <p>
            showing folks what weve done. But we definitely talk a lot. We have a nice
           </p>
           <p>
            little short list of new trips wed like to do.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <i>
             [Trailer from
            </i>
            <a href="http://vimeo.com/user579113" style="text-decoration: underline;">
             <i>
              Ben Gottfried
             </i>
            </a>
            <i>
             on
            </i>
            <i>
             <a href="http://vimeo.com/" style="text-decoration: underline;">
              Vimeo
             </a>
            </i>
            ]
           </p>
