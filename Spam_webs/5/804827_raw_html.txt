                <h1 class="title">
                 Ancient Magic: The Illusions Created in Temples by Amazing Inventions
                </h1>
                <span class="rdf-meta element-hidden" content="Ancient Magic: The Illusions Created in Temples by Amazing Inventions" property="dc:title">
                </span>
                <span class="rdf-meta element-hidden" content="1" datatype="xsd:integer" property="sioc:num_replies">
                </span>
                <div class="content">
                 <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
                  <div class="field-items">
                   <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                    <p>
                     By
                     <a href="http://www.theepochtimes.com/n3/author/tara-macisaac/" target="_blank">
                      Tara MacIsaac
                     </a>
                     ,
                     <a href="http://www.theepochtimes.com/n3/about-us.html/" target="_blank" title="Epoch Times">
                      Epoch Times
                     </a>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Imagine youre in an ancient temple, dimly lit by rays of sunlight peaking in. A worshipper lights a fire on the altar, and steps back. A statue beside the altar begins to move! It pours libations to the god for whom the temple is built. A door behind the altar opens of its own accord, and the statue of that god appears.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     But behind themystique was the ingenuity of inventors,not the miracles of the gods.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Inventor Hero of Alexandria (1070 A.D.) described in Pneumatics how such devices worked. The statue stood on a pedestal connected to the altar by a tube. The tube extended from the altar, through the statue, and into a cup held by the statue. Water was poured into the pedestal through a hole that was then corked up.
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <img alt="Marvelous altar, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography." class="media-image" height="812" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/Marvelous-altar.jpg?itok=9SCz5-5L" style="width: 610px; height: 812px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="610"/>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <em>
                      Marvelous altar, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     When the altar fire was lit, the air would expand with the heat, and push into the pedestal through the tube. It would drive the water in the pedestal up through the tube, rising to the cup and pouring out.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The doors are made to open automatically by a similar mechanism. Instead of the water in this mechanism being pushed up through a tube toward a cup, the waterpushed intoa bucket. This bucket then becomes heavy, serving as a counterweight.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The bucket will be pulled down by the weight of the water, drawing on a cord that has been wound around cylinders and attached to the doors in such a way that when it is pulled, the doors will open.
                    </p>
                    <ul>
                     <li>
                      <a href="http://www.ancient-origins.net/artifacts-ancient-technology/6-advanced-ancient-inventions-beyond-modern-understanding-002652">
                       <strong>
                        6 Advanced Ancient Inventions Beyond Modern Understanding
                       </strong>
                      </a>
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <a href="http://www.ancient-origins.net/ancient-technology/ten-amazing-inventions-ancient-times-001539">
                       <strong>
                        Ten amazing inventions from ancient times
                       </strong>
                      </a>
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <a href="http://www.ancient-origins.net/artifacts-ancient-technology/five-da-vinci-inventions-could-revolutionized-history-020145">
                       <strong>
                        Five da Vinci inventions that could have revolutionized the history of technology
                       </strong>
                      </a>
                     </li>
                    </ul>
                    <p align="center">
                     <img alt="Mechanism for opening and closing the doors when a fire was lit upon the altar, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography." class="media-image" height="475" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/Mechanism-for-opening-and-closing-the-doors.jpg?itok=PWpkc_2R" style="width: 610px; height: 475px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="610"/>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <em>
                      Mechanism for opening and closing the doors when a fire was lit upon the altar, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <img alt="Temple doors opened when a fire was lit upon the altar, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography" class="media-image" height="791" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/Temple-doors-opened-when-a-fire-was-lit.jpg" style="width: 519px; height: 791px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="519"/>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <em>
                      Temple doors opened when a fire was lit upon the altar, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Further adding to the magic of the temples, large statues of the gods also sometimes had cavities within, which the priests could enter through secret passages to deliver oraclesthe statues of the gods would seem to talk.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Its all reminiscent of the modern tale, The Wizard of Oz, in which a normal middle-aged man uses similar ingenuity to appear as the great and powerful Wizard of Oz.
                    </p>
                    <ul>
                     <li>
                      <a href="http://www.ancient-origins.net/news-general/more-fifty-ancient-greek-inventions-brought-life-through-incredible-reconstructions">
                       <strong>
                        More than fifty ancient Greek inventions brought to life through incredible reconstructions
                       </strong>
                      </a>
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <a href="http://www.ancient-origins.net/ancient-technology/ancient-invention-steam-engine-hero-alexandria-001467">
                       <strong>
                        The ancient invention of the steam engine by the Hero of Alexandria
                       </strong>
                      </a>
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <a href="http://www.ancient-origins.net/ancient-technology/ancient-invention-water-clock-001818">
                       <strong>
                        The Ancient Invention of the Water Clock
                       </strong>
                      </a>
                     </li>
                    </ul>
                    <h2>
                     Did Demons Keep the Lamps Burning Unnaturally?
                    </h2>
                    <p align="center">
                     <img alt="Picture of an old lamp. " class="media-image" height="391" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/Picture-of-an-old-lamp.jpg?itok=pIiiQ0db" style="width: 610px; height: 391px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="610"/>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <em>
                      Picture of an old lamp.Photo source: BigStockPhoto
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Saint Augustine said the perpetually burning lamps in the temple of Jupiter Ammon in Egypt and other temples, as described by Plutarch, may have been kept aflame by the intervention of demons.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Father Athanasius Kircher (16021680), considered by some to be the father of Egyptology, quotes an Arab named Schiangia: In Egypt there was a field whose ditches were full of pitch and liquid bitumen. Philosophers, who understood the forces of nature, constructed canals which connected places like these with lamps hidden at the bottom of subterranean crypts. These lamps had wicks made of threads that could not burn. By this means the lamp, once lighted, burned eternally, because of the continuous influx of bitumen and the incombustibility of the wick.
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <img alt="A wood engraving of Athanasius Kircher (1602-1680). " class="media-image" height="688" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/Athanasius-Kircher.jpg" style="width: 482px; height: 688px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="482"/>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <em>
                      A wood engraving of Athanasius Kircher (1602-1680). (Library of Congress)
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     In the book
                     <a href="http://www.gutenberg.org/files/45235/45235-h/45235-h.htm" target="_blank">
                      Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography,
                     </a>
                     Albert A. Hopkins wrote that artifice of this nature may have been used to keep the flames described by Plutrarch alive, But the majority of them owed their peculiarity only to the precautions taken by the priests to feed them without being seen.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     He continued: It was only necessary, in fact, that the wick, which was made of asbestos threads or gold wire, should be kept intact, and that the body of the lamp should communicate with a reservoir placed in a neighboring apartment in such a way that the level of the oil should remain constant.
                    </p>
                    <h2>
                     The Magic of a Legendary Entrance
                    </h2>
                    <p>
                     Pliny the Elder (2379 A.D.), an ancient Roman scientist and historian, wrote that when the gates of the labyrinth of Thebes were opened, a noise like that of thunder would resound.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     This grand description may have an ingenious invention behind it. Hero of Alexandria described in Pneumatics how a trumpet can be made to automatically sound when a door is opened.
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <img alt="Apparatus for sounding a trumpet when the door of a temple was opened, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography." class="media-image" height="483" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/Apparatus-for-sounding-a-trumpet.jpg?itok=qrRCf9B9" style="width: 610px; height: 483px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="610"/>
                    </p>
                    <p align="center">
                     <em>
                      Apparatus for sounding a trumpet when the door of a temple was opened, pictured in the book Magic, Stage Illusions and Scientific Diversions Including Trick Photography.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     When the door opens, a system of pulleys, rods, and cords push a solid sphere part way into a vessel of water, as shown in Fig. 1 below. Air compressed by the water is forced through the trumpet, making the trumpet sound.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Featured image: Sketches of inventions by Leonardo da Vinci. Source: BigStockPhoto
                     </em>
                    </p>

                   <a href="/news-history-archaeology/ancient-korean-sites-baekje-kingdom-020423">
                    <span>
                     The little known Ancient Korean sites of the Baekje Kingdom receive Worldwide recognition
                    </span>
                   </a>
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                 <div class="views-field views-field-field-image">
                  <div class="field-content">
                   <a href="/news-history-archaeology/ancient-korean-sites-baekje-kingdom-020423">

                    </span>
                   </a>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 <div class="views-field views-field-body">
                  <div class="field-content">
                   <p>

                    <a href="http://whc.unesco.org/en/list/" target="_blank">
                     UNESCO World Heritage List
                    </a>
                    registered at Number 12 on the list which covers over 1000 ancient sites across the globe. The Korean sites, known as the Baekje Historic Areas, include locations in Iksan in North Jeolla, and Gongju and Buyeo in South Chungcheong. They include royal palaces and fortresses, tombs, temple sites and other archaeological sites
                   </p>

                    </span>

                    </span>
                   </a>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 <div class="views-field views-field-body">
                  <div class="field-content">
                   <p>

But after a certain time, when Cambodia and India become the cultural, religious and political part of each other, the movement started from Cambodia to India. The purpose of this movement was religion and politics. Even today one can see the pilgrims that are regularly visiting Indian Buddhist sites bringing with them goods and offerings from their home countries.

                   <a href="/news-history-archaeology/elaborate-native-american-burial-bobcat-funeral-mound-reserved-humans-020421">
                    <span>
                     Elaborate Native American burial of a bobcat in a funeral mound reserved for humans
                    </span>
                   </a>
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                 <div class="views-field views-field-field-image">
                  <div class="field-content">
                   <a href="/news-history-archaeology/elaborate-native-american-burial-bobcat-funeral-mound-reserved-humans-020421">
                    <span>
                     <img alt="A Hopewell culture burial mound from the Mound City Group in Ohio. " height="120" src="http://www.ancient-origins.net/sites/default/files/styles/thumbnail/public/field/image/Mound-City-ohio.jpg?itok=sJwpmQp_" typeof="foaf:Image" width="270"/>
                    </span>
                   </a>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 <div class="views-field views-field-body">
                  <div class="field-content">
                   <p>
                    About 2,000 years ago, the Hopewell people of western Illinois buried a bobcat on the outer edge of a burial mound that was usually reserved for humans. In a touching gestures, the creature was adorned with a necklace of bear teeth and sea shells. The mound is one of fourteen located on the top of a bluff overlooking the Illinois River, about 80 kilometres north of St Louis.
                   </p>
