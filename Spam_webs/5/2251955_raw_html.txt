     <p>
      "
      <b>
       Boogie Chillen'
      </b>
      " or "
      <b>
       Boogie Chillun
      </b>
      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        b
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">
       blues
      </a>
      song first recorded by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lee_Hooker" title="John Lee Hooker">
       John Lee Hooker
      </a>
      in 1948. It is a solo performance featuring Hooker's vocal, electric guitar, and rhythmic foot stomps. The lyrics are partly autobiographical and alternate between spoken and sung verses. The song was his debut record release and in 1949, it became the first "down-home" electric blues song to reach number one in the R&amp;B records chart.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hooker's song was part of a trend in the late 1940s to a new style of urban electric blues based on earlier
      <a href="/wiki/Delta_blues" title="Delta blues">
       Delta blues
      </a>
      idioms. Although it is called a boogie, it resembles early North Mississippi
      <a href="/wiki/Hill_country_blues" title="Hill country blues">
       Hill country blues
      </a>
      rather than the
      <a href="/wiki/Boogie-woogie" title="Boogie-woogie">
       boogie-woogie
      </a>
      piano-derived style of the 1930s and 1940s. Hooker gave credit to his stepfather, Will Moore, who taught him the rhythm of "Boogie Chillen'" ("chillen'" is a phonetical approximation of Hooker's pronunciation of "children") when he was a teenager. Some of the song's lyrics are derived from earlier blues songs.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hooker's guitar work on the song inspired several well-known guitarists to take up the instrument. With its driving style and focus on rhythm, it is also considered a
      <a href="/wiki/Origins_of_rock_and_roll" title="Origins of rock and roll">
       forerunner of rock and roll
      </a>
      . Music critic
      <a href="/wiki/Cub_Koda" title="Cub Koda">
       Cub Koda
      </a>
      calls the guitar figure from "Boogie Chillen'" "the riff that launched a million songs".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koda_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koda-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several rock musicians have patterned successful songs either directly or indirectly on Hooker's many versions of "Boogie Chillen'". These include songs by
      <a href="/wiki/Boogie_rock" title="Boogie rock">
       boogie rock
      </a>
      band
      <a href="/wiki/Canned_Heat" title="Canned Heat">
       Canned Heat
      </a>
      , who also recorded a well-received version with Hooker. One of
      <a href="/wiki/ZZ_Top" title="ZZ Top">
       ZZ Top
      </a>
      's best-known hits, "
      <a href="/wiki/La_Grange_(song)" title="La Grange (song)">
       La Grange
      </a>
      ", uses elements of the song, which led to legal action by the song's publisher and resulted in changes to American copyright law.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1943, Hooker moved to Detroit, Michigan, for employment opportunities in the city's
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_home_front_during_World_War_II" title="United States home front during World War II">
       wartime vehicle manufacturing factories
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There he was attracted to the music clubs along Hastings Street in
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Bottom,_Detroit" title="Black Bottom, Detroit">
       Black Bottom/Paradise Valley
      </a>
      , the cultural center of the city's black community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDahl1996115.E2.80.93116_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDahl1996115.E2.80.93116-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He recounts his experience in the narrative to "Boogie Chillen'":
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray200290_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray200290-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <div class="poem">
       <p>
        When I first come to town people, I was walkin' down Hastings Street
        <br/>
        I heard everybody talkin' about, Henry's Swing Club
        <br/>
        I decided I'd drop in there that night, and when I got there
        <br/>
        I say 'Yes, people!', yes they was really havin' a ball!
        <br/>
        Yes, I know
        <br/>
        Boogie chillen'!
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      By 1948, Hooker came to the attention of Elmer Barbee, a local record shop owner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008241_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008241-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Barbee arranged to have several demos recorded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008241_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008241-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He or Hooker later presented them to Bernie Besman, who ran the Detroit area's only professional record company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESax19911_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESax19911-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although Hooker had played mostly with an ensemble at that time, Besman decided to record him solo.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008244_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008244-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This put the attention solely on the singer/guitarist,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002118_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002118-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in contrast to the prevailing
      <a href="/wiki/Jump_blues" title="Jump blues">
       jump blues
      </a>
      style, which emphasized ensemble instrumentation. Recent hit singles by
      <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">
       Muddy Waters
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lightnin%27_Hopkins" title="Lightnin' Hopkins">
       Lightnin' Hopkins
      </a>
      had also used this stripped-down, electrified
      <a href="/wiki/Delta_blues" title="Delta blues">
       Delta blues
      </a>
      -inspired approach.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008242.E2.80.93243_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008242.E2.80.93243-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_lyrics">
       Composition and lyrics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition and lyrics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "Boogie Chillen'" is described by music critic Bill Dahl as "blues as primitive as anything then on the market; Hooker's dark, ruminative vocals were backed only by his own ringing, heavily amplified guitar and insistently pounding foot".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDahl1996115.E2.80.93116_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDahl1996115.E2.80.93116-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an interview, Hooker how he came up with "Boogie Chillen'":
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       I wrote that song in Detroit when I was sittin' around strummin' my guitar. The thing come into me, you know? I heard [my stepfather] Will Moore done [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
         sic
        </a>
       </i>
       ] it years and years before. I was a little kid from down South, and I heard him do a song like that, but he didn't call it 'Boogie Chillen.' But it had that beat, and I just kept that beat up and I called it 'Boogie Chillen.'
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEObrecht2000427_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEObrecht2000427-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      He performed the song in clubs before recording it and called it "Boogie Woogie" before settling on "Boogie Chillen'".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEObrecht2000406.2C_426.E2.80.93427_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEObrecht2000406.2C_426.E2.80.93427-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to musicologist
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Palmer_(writer)" title="Robert Palmer (writer)">
       Robert Palmer
      </a>
      , "The closest thing to it on records is 'Cottonfield Blues', recorded by
      <a href="/wiki/Garfield_Akers" title="Garfield Akers">
       Garfield Akers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Joe_Callicott" title="Mississippi Joe Callicott">
       Joe Callicott
      </a>
      , two guitarists from the hill country of northern Mississippi, in 1929. Essentially, it was a
      <a href="/wiki/Hill_country_blues" title="Hill country blues">
       backcountry
      </a>
      , pre-blues sort of musica droning, open-ended stomp without a fixed verse form that lent itself to building up to a cumulative, trancelike effect".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243.E2.80.93244_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243.E2.80.93244-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="515" data-file-width="347" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg/170px-John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg/255px-John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg/340px-John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Lee_Hooker_two.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hooker performing in 1972
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Hooker's vocal alternates between sung and spoken sections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002129_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002129-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Commenting on Hooker's vocal sections, music historian
      <a href="/wiki/Ted_Gioia" title="Ted Gioia">
       Ted Gioia
      </a>
      notes, "The song has almost no melody. Even less harmony. In fact, it is hard to call it a song. It's more like a bit of jive steam of consciousness in 4/4 time."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008238_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008238-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some of the lyrics are borrowed from earlier songs that date back to the beginning of the blues.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The opening line "My mama she didn't allow me to stay out all night long" has origins in "Mama Don't Allow", an old dance song.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several songs were recorded in the 1920s with similar titles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Boogie No. 3" by boogie-woogie pianist
      <a href="/wiki/Cow_Cow_Davenport" title="Cow Cow Davenport">
       Cow Cow Davenport
      </a>
      has sung and spoken sections and includes the lines, "I don't care what Grandma don't allow, play my music anyhow, Grandma's don't 'llow no music playin' in here".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hooker's first and second takes of the song include similar verses and the narrative about Henry's Swing Club, but do not include the crucial mid-song hook "Boogie, chillen'!" before the guitar break, which gives the song its lyrical identity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002131_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002131-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A key feature of the song is the driving guitar rhythmic figure centered around one chord, with "accents that fell fractionally ahead of the beat".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Music journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Shaar_Murray" title="Charles Shaar Murray">
       Charles Shaar Murray
      </a>
      describes it as a "rocking dance piece... its structure is utterly free-form, its basic beat is the jumping,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyrhythmic" title="Polyrhythmic">
       polyrhythmic
      </a>
      groove which he [Hooker] learned in the Delta".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002127_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002127-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an interview with
      <a href="/wiki/B.B._King" title="B.B. King">
       B.B. King
      </a>
      , Hooker confirmed that he used an
      <a href="/wiki/Open_G_tuning" title="Open G tuning">
       open G guitar tuning
      </a>
      technique for his guitar,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKostelanetzKing2005100_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKostelanetzKing2005100-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although he usually used a
      <a href="/wiki/Capo" title="Capo">
       capo
      </a>
      , raising the pitch to B (1948), A
      <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">
       
      </span>
      (1959), or A (1970).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarlson2005138_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarlson2005138-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also employed
      <a href="/wiki/Hammer-on" title="Hammer-on">
       hammer-on
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pull-off" title="Pull-off">
       pull-off
      </a>
      techniques, which are described as "a slurred ascending bass line played on the fifth string [tonic]" by music writer Lenny Carlson.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarlson2005138_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarlson2005138-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although it is titled a "boogie", it does not resemble the earlier
      <a href="/wiki/Boogie-woogie" title="Boogie-woogie">
       boogie-woogie
      </a>
      style.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Boogie-woogie is based on a left-hand piano
      <a href="/wiki/Ostinato" title="Ostinato">
       ostinato
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Bassline#Walking_bass" title="Bassline">
       walking-bass
      </a>
      line and, as performed on guitar, forms the popular 1940s instrumental "
      <a href="/wiki/Guitar_Boogie_(song)" title="Guitar Boogie (song)">
       Guitar Boogie
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        c
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rather than being derivative, Hooker's boogie becomes "as overwhelmingly personal a piece as anything ever done in the blues".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002128_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002128-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_release">
       Recording and release
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recording and release">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In September 1948, Besman arranged recording sessions for Hooker at United Sound studios in Detroit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002118_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002118-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several songs were recorded with Hooker's vocals and amplified guitar.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESax19911_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESax19911-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To make the sound fuller, a microphone was set up in a
      <a href="/wiki/Pallet" title="Pallet">
       pallet
      </a>
      that was placed under Hooker's foot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002121.E2.80.93122_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002121.E2.80.93122-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Besman's account, a primitive echo-chamber effect was created by feeding Hooker's foot-stomp rhythm into a speaker in a toilet bowl, which in turn was miked and returned to a speaker in the studio in front of Hooker's guitar, thus giving it a "big" or more ambient sound.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002121.E2.80.93122_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002121.E2.80.93122-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Three takes of Hooker's performance were recorded, the last providing the master for "Boogie Chillen'".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008245_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008245-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Even though Besman had his own record label, Sensation Records, he licensed "Boogie Chillen'" to Los Angeles-based
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_Records" title="Modern Records">
       Modern Records
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESax19911_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESax19911-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On November 3, 1948, it was released nationally and Hooker commented on its immediate appeal: "The thing caught afire. It was ringin' all around the country. When it come out, every juke box you went to, every place you went to... they were playing it there".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because of the response, Nashville, Tennessee, radio station
      <a href="/wiki/WLAC" title="WLAC">
       WLAC
      </a>
      , a 50,000 watt
      <a href="/wiki/Clear-channel_station" title="Clear-channel station">
       clear-channel station
      </a>
      that reached fifteen states and Canada, played the song ten times in a row during one broadcast night.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESullivan2013609_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESullivan2013609-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It entered the
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      Race Records chart on January 8, 1949, where it remained for eighteen weeks, and reached number one on February 19, 1949.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhitburn1988194_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitburn1988194-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It became the most popular race record of 1949
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhitburn1988597_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitburn1988597-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and reportedly sold "several hundred thousand"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFancourt19881_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFancourt19881-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to one million
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShadwick2001303_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShadwick2001303-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      copies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        d
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an experience similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">
       Muddy Waters
      </a>
      ' 1950 hit "
      <a href="/wiki/Rollin%27_Stone" title="Rollin' Stone">
       Rollin' Stone
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGordon2002101_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGordon2002101-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the song's popularity allowed Hooker to give up his factory job and concentrate on music.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981243-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_influence">
       Early influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early influence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Besides its commercial success, "Boogie Chillen'" had a big impact on blues and R&amp;B musicians.
      <a href="/wiki/B.B._King" title="B.B. King">
       B.B. King
      </a>
      , who was a
      <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">
       disc jockey
      </a>
      at Memphis, Tennessee, radio station
      <a href="/wiki/WDIA" title="WDIA">
       WDIA
      </a>
      at the time, regularly featured Hooker's song.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2001245_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2001245-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He recalled:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       [There was] hardly anybody around who was playing at that time didn't play 'Boogie Chillen.' That's just how heavy it was... I, for one, and many others [musicians] who would go out and playif you didn't play 'Boogie Chillen' at that time, people probably look at you and wonder what was wrong with you. It was such a big record.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135-38">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         34
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Murray likens the song to "the R&amp;B equivalent of
      <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">
       punk rock
      </a>
      " or superficially simple enough not to intimidate beginners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002134_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002134-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It interested the eleven-year-old
      <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">
       Bo Diddley
      </a>
      : "I think the first record I paid attention to was John Lee Hooker's 'Boogie Chillen,'... When I found John Lee Hooker on the radio, I said, 'If that guy can play, I know I can.' I mean John Lee's got a hell of a style".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an interview,
      <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Guy" title="Buddy Guy">
       Buddy Guy
      </a>
      described learning to play "Boogie Chillen'" at age thirteen: "that was the first thing I thought I learned how to play that I knew sounded right when someone would listen."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135_38-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Guy later recorded a version with
      <a href="/wiki/Junior_Wells" title="Junior Wells">
       Junior Wells
      </a>
      for their 1981 album
      <i>
       Alone &amp; Acoustic
      </i>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Collins" title="Albert Collins">
       Albert Collins
      </a>
      also recalled that it was the first song he learned to play.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135_38-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133.E2.80.93135-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The success of "Boogie Chillen'" brought numerous offers for John Lee Hooker to record for other record companies. Because he received little remuneration from the sales of his record, Hooker readily accepted the opportunities to generate income.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnglish200752_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnglish200752-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This led to his recording using a variety of pseudonyms, including Texas Slim, Little Pork Chops, Delta John, Birmingham Sam, the Boogie Man, Johnny Williams, John Lee Booker, John Lee Cooker, and others for such labels as
      <a href="/wiki/King_Records_(United_States)" title="King Records (United States)">
       King
      </a>
      , Danceland,
      <a href="/wiki/Regent_Records_(US)" title="Regent Records (US)">
       Regent
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Records" title="Savoy Records">
       Savoy
      </a>
      , Acorn, Prize, Staff,
      <a href="/wiki/Gotham_Records" title="Gotham Records">
       Gotham
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gone_Records" title="Gone Records">
       Gone
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">
       Chess
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Swing_Time_Records" title="Swing Time Records">
       Swing Time
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDahl1996115.E2.80.93116_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDahl1996115.E2.80.93116-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Hooker_versions">
       Later Hooker versions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later Hooker versions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The demand for "Boogie Chillen'" remained high enough for Hooker to re-record the song several times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440_18-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1950, he recorded a faster version with different lyrics as "Boogie Chillen' #2" for Bernie Besman's Sensation label (also issued by Regal).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002133-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Modern Records released an edited version in 1952 titled "New Boogie Chillun". After Hooker began his association with
      <a href="/wiki/Vee-Jay_Records" title="Vee-Jay Records">
       Vee-Jay Records
      </a>
      , he recorded "Boogie Chillun" in 1959, which closely follows the original single.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray2002131_fn_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray2002131_fn-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because of the similarity, the 1959 version is sometimes misidentified as the 1948 version and vice versa (at 2:36, the Vee-Jay version is about a half of a minute shorter than the original).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hooker_n_Heat_cover.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="280" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Hooker_n_Heat_cover.jpg/240px-Hooker_n_Heat_cover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4a/Hooker_n_Heat_cover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4a/Hooker_n_Heat_cover.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hooker_n_Heat_cover.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Hooker 'n Heat
        </i>
        album cover (front)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
         <a href="#cite_note-44">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          e
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first two takes from the September 1948 Detroit recording session began appearing on various compilation albums in the 1970s, sometimes with the titles "John Lee's Original Boogie" and "Henry's Swing Club".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESax19911_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESax19911-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, Modern and its associated labels including
      <a href="/wiki/Kent_Records" title="Kent Records">
       Kent
      </a>
      and Crown reissued the song several times.
     </p>
     <p>
      From the 1960s onwards, Hooker recorded several studio and live renditions of "Boogie Chillen'",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnterberger1996117_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnterberger1996117-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with guest musicians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">
       Eric Clapton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">
       the Rolling Stones
      </a>
      . In 1970, he recorded an updated version of the song, titled "Boogie Chillen' No. 2", with the
      <a href="/wiki/Blues_rock" title="Blues rock">
       blues rock
      </a>
      group Canned Heat for their joint album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hooker_%27n_Heat" title="Hooker 'n Heat">
        Hooker 'n Heat
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planner_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Planner-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Blues historian Gerard Herzhaft describes the performance as a "memorable one".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440_18-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerzhaft1992440-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It combines Hooker's vocal and Canned Heat's signature boogie rock backing, as heard in the group's jam song "Fried Hockey Boogie" (itself an adaptation of "Boogie Chillen'").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koda_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koda-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite being over eleven minutes long with extended guitar and harmonica solos, it remains as "full of the same swagger as the original".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planner_46-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Planner-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_and_legacy">
       Recognition and legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recognition and legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1985, Hooker's 1948 recording of "Boogie Chillen'" was inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/Blues_Foundation" title="Blues Foundation">
       Blues Foundation
      </a>
      Hall of Fame. Writing for the Foundation, blues historian
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_O%27Neal" title="Jim O'Neal">
       Jim O'Neal
      </a>
      noted it was "the first down-home electric blues record to achieve No. 1 chart status and its success, together with that of the Hooker hits that followed, inspired record companies to search out the new electric generation of country bluesmen".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Neal_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-O.27Neal-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1999, it received a
      <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Hall_of_Fame_Award" title="Grammy Hall of Fame Award">
       Grammy Hall of Fame Award
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is included in the
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">
       Rock and Roll Hall of Fame
      </a>
      list of the "500 Songs That Shaped Rock and Roll".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Boogie Chillen'" was added to the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/National_Recording_Registry" title="National Recording Registry">
       National Recording Registry
      </a>
      in 2008, which noted that "the driving rhythm and confessional lyrics have guaranteed its place as an influential and enduring blues classic".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Authors
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Dawson" title="Jim Dawson">
       Jim Dawson
      </a>
      and Steve Propes included it in their 1992 book
      <i>
       What Was the First Rock 'n' Roll Record?
      </i>
      , identifying it among the precursors of rock and roll.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDawsonPropes1992eBook_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDawsonPropes1992eBook-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      "Boogie Chillen'" has inspired several songs, beginning in 1953, when
      <a href="/wiki/Junior_Parker" title="Junior Parker">
       Junior Parker
      </a>
      recorded his interpretation titled "Feelin' Good".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981244_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalmer1981244-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It became Parker's first hit for
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_Records" title="Sun Records">
       Sun Records
      </a>
      and was subsequently recorded by
      <a href="/wiki/James_Cotton" title="James Cotton">
       James Cotton
      </a>
      in 1967 and by
      <a href="/wiki/Magic_Sam" title="Magic Sam">
       Magic Sam
      </a>
      as "I Feel So Good (I Wanna Boogie)" for his influential 1967 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/West_Side_Soul" title="West Side Soul">
        West Side Soul
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koda_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koda-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A version by
      <a href="/wiki/Slim_Harpo" title="Slim Harpo">
       Slim Harpo
      </a>
      , titled "Boogie Chillun", appeared on his 1970 album
      <i>
       Slim Harpo Knew the Blues
      </i>
      using a similar arrangement to his 1966 hit "
      <a href="/wiki/Shake_Your_Hips" title="Shake Your Hips">
       Shake Your Hips
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other songs that borrow from "Boogie Chillen'" or "Boogie Chillen' No. 2", either directly or indirectly, include the radio hits "
      <a href="/wiki/On_the_Road_Again_(Canned_Heat_song)" title="On the Road Again (Canned Heat song)">
       On the Road Again
      </a>
      " by Canned Heat in 1968, "
      <a href="/wiki/Spirit_in_the_Sky" title="Spirit in the Sky">
       Spirit in the Sky
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Greenbaum" title="Norman Greenbaum">
       Norman Greenbaum
      </a>
      in 1970, and "La Grange" by ZZ Top in 1973.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koda_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koda-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarson2006167_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarson2006167-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_issues">
       Copyright issues
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boogie_Chillen%27&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Copyright issues">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1991, Bernie Besman, as the song's publisher, La Cienega Music, brought legal action against ZZ Top for copyright infringement for their song "La Grange".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnglish200752_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnglish200752-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Writer Timothy English notes that of the various Hooker recordings of "Boogie Chillen'", the one released in 1971 with Canned Heat "has the most elements in common with 'La Grange', including the guitar pattern and the 'howl, howl, howl' vocal line".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnglish200752.E2.80.9353_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnglish200752.E2.80.9353-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The case wound its way through the American legal system (including an appeal to the U.S. Supreme Court).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008251_56-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008251-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the ruling did not favor the publisher, the U.S. Congress was persuaded to amend the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Act_of_1976" title="Copyright Act of 1976">
       Copyright Act
      </a>
      in 1998 to protect many songs recorded before 1978 from entering the public domain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnglish200753_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnglish200753-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ZZ Top settled out of court in 1997,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnglish200753_57-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnglish200753-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but Hooker again gained no financial reward from his songBesman had obtained Hooker's rights to the song years earlier.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008251_56-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008251-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Gioia noted, "Nonetheless, his [John Lee Hooker's 1948] spontaneous performance in a recording studio had led to a substantial change in
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_States" title="Copyright law of the United States">
       U.S. intellectual property law
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGioia2008251_56-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGioia2008251-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
