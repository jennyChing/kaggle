          <h1 id="page-title">
           20 popular American Wilderness Areas
          </h1>
         </header>
         <div id="content">
          <div class="region region-content">
           <div class="block block-system block-odd block-count-7 block-region-content" id="block-system-main">
            <div class="block-inner clearfix">
             <div class="block-content content">
              <article about="/20-popular-american-wilderness-areas" class="article article-type-page odd article-full clearfix" id="article-107970" role="article" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">
               <div class="article-content">
                <span class="print-link">
                </span>
                <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
                 <div class="field-items">
                  <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     <img alt="" class="media-image media-image-right" height="360" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/WATW.MaroonBells-SnowmassWilderness.mary_.jpg?itok=8Yuf0Ri4" style="width: 400px; height: 300px; float: right; margin: 10px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="480"/>
                     Every year,
                    </span>
                    <a href="http://www.fs.fed.us/recreation/programs/nvum/nvum_national_summary_fy2011.pdf">
                     almost eight million visits
                    </a>
                    are made to Americas 757 Designated Wilderness Areas. Some of these are backcountry camping trips to places far off the beaten path, while others are day hikes in natural areas neighboring large cities.
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     With half of all wilderness areas within a day's drive of America's 30 largest cities, according to the
                    </span>
                    <a href="http://www.wilderness.net/NWPS/threatsOveruse">
                     Wilderness Institute
                    </a>
                    , more and more people are discovering all the joys that wilderness areas offer. Almost 25% of visits made to wilderness are made by those living within 25 miles, according to the U.S. Forest Service.
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Wilderness visits are part of the reason these areas are protected, but visitors are also responsible for caring for these places too. Recreationists of all kinds are encouraged to observe
                     <a href="/node/351">
                      leave no trace
                     </a>
                     principles, and to try to spread their visits out throughout the week to avert overcrowded weekends.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     In honor of the
                     <a href="http://wilderness.org/article/wilderness-act">
                      50th anniversary of the Wilderness Act
                     </a>
                     ,
                    </span>
                    we invite you to learn more about some of the nations most popular wilderness areas. Are any of them near you?
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     1. Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness, Minnesota
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     This wilderness area is considered one of the most visited in all of America. Located at the Canadian border, Boundary Waters has almost 2,200 backcountry camping sites, mostly accessible only by canoe. Fishing is popular as well, with dozens of species contributing to the diversity of life here - including
                    </span>
                    wolves, otters, moose, beavers, frogs and bats. Wilderness Society founder
                    <a href="http://wilderness.org/bios/founders/ernest-oberholtzer">
                     Ernest Oberholtzer
                    </a>
                    was one of the people primarily responsible for its protection under the
                    <a href="http://wilderness.org/article/wilderness-act">
                     Wilderness Act
                    </a>
                    50 years ago.
                    <strong>
                     At risk!
                    </strong>
                    Sadly, a new push to open
                    <a href="https://www.savetheboundarywaters.org/">
                     sulfide mining
                    </a>
                    operations near Boundary Waters could put the wilderness area at risk. Several companies are currently conducting mineral explorations just outside the southern boundary of the wilderness. If such companies are allowed to open mines, pollution from sulfuric acid, mercury and toxic metals could contaminate the lakes and rivers that flow into the Boundary Waters. This could have deep impacts on water quality, harming wildlife and fish in this wilderness area that so many Americans fought so hard to preserve.
                    <a href="https://www.savetheboundarywaters.org/">
                     Learn more
                    </a>
                    and view a
                    <a href="https://www.savetheboundarywaters.org/quest-clean-water">
                     short video
                    </a>
                    about the
                    <a href="http://SavetheBoundaryWaters.org">
                     controversial mining initiative in Boundary Waters.
                    </a>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/BWCAW.Greg%20Walters.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr, Greg Walters
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     2. Sandia Mountain Wilderness, New Mexico
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Just outside Albuquerque, Sandias popular trails can offer surprising solitude - if hikers are willing to climb side trails. On the western slopes of the small, granite Sandia Mountain range hikers may spy numerous birds of prey like raptors, golden eagles and various hawks. The Sandia Peak Aerial Tramway transports visitors to the peak for 2.7 miles, making it one of the longest trams in the world.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="673" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/Sandia%20Mountain%20Wilderness.trevinator3000.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,trevinator3000
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     3. Pusch Ridge Wilderness, Arizona
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Stunning views and beautiful rock formations are conveniently accessed by trails outside Tucson. Pusch Ridge is a prominent landmark in the Santa Catalina Mountains, with elevations from 2,800 to 8,800 feet. Its cooler temperatures are an attraction in summer, but heavy foot traffic is threatening to wildlife.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/Pusch%20Ridge%20Wilderness.Miguel%20Vieira_0.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,Miguel Vieira
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     4. Maroon Bells - Snowmass Wilderness, Colorado
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     This Wilderness contains six of Colorados legendary fourteeners, including the two known as the deadly bells and Snowmass Mountain. Every year thousands of mountaineers brave difficult climbs to alpine lakes, like Maroon Lake, whose reflection of Maroon Bells is one of the states most photographic scenes. Protected by the Wilderness Act 50 years ago, this wilderness area also hosts natural hot springs at Conundrum Creek. This may be the only Wilderness that has a shuttle bus for summer crowds.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/MaroonBells-SnowmassWilderness.mary_.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 768px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,
                    </em>
                    <em>
                     mary
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     5. Sangre de Cristo Wilderness, Colorado
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Four fourteeners are in the long and narrow Sangre de Cristo Wilderness, including the most challenging Crestone Needle. Most trails are rough and end at one of its 60 alpine lakes. Large aspen stands visible today are a result of heavy fires purposely set in the early 1900's to clear pasture land, expose minerals, and produce charcoal. Great Sand Dunes National Preserve is offers skiing in winter, splashing in sandy creeks in spring, and
                     <a href="/node/107899">
                      dark starry skies
                     </a>
                     year-round. Six insects protected here are found nowhere else on Earth, including the beautiful Great Sand Dunes Tiger Beetle. The area held religious significance to early Spanish settlers, thus the meaning of its name: "Blood of Christ".
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="2000" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/GreatSandDunesNP.NPS_.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 683px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="3000"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     photo: Great Sand Dunes. credit:
                    </em>
                    <em>
                     NPS
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     6. Mount Evans Wilderness, Colorado
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Mount Evans has two fourteeners, including the one closest to the city of Denver. To access the heart of the area, visitor must drive the highest paved road on the continent, the Mount Evans Scenic Byway. Bighorn sheep and mountain goats roam its peaks. The area also has regions of watery arctic tundra, which are rare south of the Arctic Circle.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="683" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/MountEvansW.Casey%20McCallister.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 683px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,
                    </em>
                    <em>
                     Casey McCallister
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     7. Alpine Lakes Wilderness, Washington
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Forests of fir and hemlock trees greet visitors in the rugged, glacier-carved North Cascades, less than 50 miles from Seattle . This wilderness area boasts over 700 mountain lakes, including Enchantment
                    </span>
                    , Mason, Melakwa, Snoqualmie
                    <a href="http://www.wta.org/go-hiking/seasonal-hikes/summer-destinations/alpine-lakes-hikes%20">
                     and more
                    </a>
                    . The area also contains headwaters for the Skykomish, Snoqualmie, Wenatchee and Yakima Rivers, and therefore is significant for water use in the region. One of the most popular outdoor destinations in its state, Alpine Lakes contains part of the
                    <a href="/node/107500">
                     Pacific Crest Trail
                    </a>
                    as well as Cashmere Crags, one of the best rock-climbing locales in the West. Visitors should be mindful of the many regulations in place to protect this fragile but well-liked wilderness area. A
                    <a href="/node/107272">
                     proposed bill
                    </a>
                    , supported by The Wilderness Society, would expand the wilderness area.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/AlpineLakesWilderness.Jessie%20Hey.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 768px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,Jessie Hey
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     8. Three Sisters Wilderness, Oregon
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     The second largest Wilderness area in Oregon was designated 50 years ago. The area is named for its three major volcanoes, and includes other geologic features like
                    </span>
                    Rock Mesa, Collier Cone, and Yapoah Crater as well as lava flows and tubes. The large glaciers like Collier are exemplary of the Pacific Northwests glaciation. The headwaters of the Wild and Scenic Whychus Creek invite brook and rainbow trout for fishers and hikers can trek a section of the
                    <a href="/node/107500">
                     Pacific Crest Trail
                    </a>
                    . Alpine meadows and waterfalls are irresistible attractions to its many visitors, some of which travel from the nearby city of Eugene.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/ThreeSistersWilderness.David%20Silverman.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr, David Silverman
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     9. Mark O Hatfield Wilderness, Oregon
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     On the northern side of Mount Hood is an area just south of the Columbia River Gorge and near enough to Portland that it attracts many visitors. Along with hiking on the
                     <a href="/node/107500">
                      Pacific Crest Trail
                     </a>
                     , this wilderness also hosts rafting in Herman Creek, Eagle Creek and Tanner Creek and camping along waterfall-strewn Eagle Creek Trail. Mossy cliffs, forested plateaus and meadow ridges offer views of wildlife and majestic scenes of the Cascade Range.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="577" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/EagleCreekTrailMarkHatfieldW.Patrick%20M.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 577px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit:Flickr, Patrick M
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     10. Desolation Wilderness, California
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Desolation Wilderness is anything but desolate. Its easily accessible from major cities in central California and therefore one of the most backpacked wildernesses in the country. Wrights, Echo, Eagle Falls and Fallen Leaf trailheads are the most heavily used, but there are important
                    </span>
                    <a href="http://www.fs.usda.gov/detail/eldorado/specialplaces/?cid=fsbdev7_019062">
                     regulations
                    </a>
                    throughout. Just southwest of Lake Tahoe, Desolation has 130 lakes of its own as well as many streams and waterfalls. The
                    <a href="/node/107500">
                     Pacific Crest Trail
                    </a>
                    crosses Dicks Pass here, which snow restricts access to until about mid-July.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="683" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/DesolationWilderness.Vlad%20Butsky.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,Vlad Butsky
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     11. John Muir Wilderness, California
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Running along the crest of the Sierra Nevadas is a wilderness filled with snow-capped granite peaks and expansive meadows. The John Muir Wilderness was designated 50 years ago and is named after the revered advocate for wildlands. It has the largest contiguous area above 10,000 feet in the continental U.S. Wildlife includes marmots, pikas, Clark's nutcrackers, Golden Trout and black bears. With both the
                     <a href="/node/107037">
                      John Muir Trail
                     </a>
                     and the
                     <a href="/node/107500">
                      Pacific Crest Trail
                     </a>
                    </span>
                    , this wilderness is one of the very top visited in the country.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="683" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/John%20Muir%20Wilderness.Tom%20Hilton.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr, Tom Hilton
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     12. Ansel Adams Wilderness, California
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Just north of John Muir and at the southern boundary of Yosemite National Park is an area designated 50 years ago as Minarets Wilderness, named after the metavolcanic Minaret mountains that remain an attraction for rock climbers. In the 80s it was renamed after
                     <a href="/node/276">
                      Ansel Adams
                     </a>
                     , the famous photographer of the Sierra Nevada and a former council member of the Wilderness Society. East of Ansel Adams Ritter Range is the most visited section: the Middle Fork of the San Joaquin River, which contains Devils Postpile National Monument. The Middle Fork flows from Thousand Island Lake, one of the largest backcountry lakes in the Sierra. The
                     <a href="/node/107037">
                      John Muir
                     </a>
                     and
                     <a href="/node/107500">
                      Pacific Crest Trails
                     </a>
                     run through her as well, making it a hotspot for day hiking.
                    </span>
                    View stunning photos of this area in the new
                    <a href="http://wilderness.org/ansel-adams-wilderness-photography-book-showcases-adams-enduring-legacy">
                     Ansel Adams Wilderness book
                    </a>
                    .
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="681" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/AnselAdamsW.ilya_ktsn.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,ilya_ktsn
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     13. Mount Rose Wilderness, Nevada
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Mount Rose is a wilderness that borders on being urban, with parts running right to the edge of Reno. Proximity to the city leads many visitors to trails like Mount Rose, which receives over
                    </span>
                    100 people daily. Other trails include Tahoe Rim, which encircles Lake Tahoe, and those that lead more serious hikers up the highest peak in the Carson Range, Mount Rose.
                    <a href="/node/107938">
                     Wildflowers
                    </a>
                    and wildlife are attractions here as well.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/snowshoeingMountRoseW.Michael%20D.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 768px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,Michael D
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     14. Mount Timpanogos Wilderness, Utah
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Utahs Timpanogos has as reputation for its fantastic mountain climbing. People on weekend Timp Hikes can get into the thousands, so parking can be difficult at trailheads. In summer, alpine meadows are covered in
                     <a href="/node/107938">
                      wildflowers
                     </a>
                     like forget-me-nots, buttercups, bluebells and columbine. In forests of aspen, conifers, oak and maple visitors may spy Rocky Mountain goats, mule deer, elk and moose.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="851" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/MountTimpanogoswithballoon.a4gpa_0.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 681px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1280"/>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <em>
                     photo:Mount Timpanogos.credit: Flickr,a4gpa.
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     15. High Uintas Wilderness, Utah
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Close to the greater Salt Lake City area, High Uintas is loved to death by hoards of avid rock climbers, fall hunters and hikers on Kings Peak. The Uinta Mountains, named for the Uintaat natives, are the highest peaks in the state and home to cougars, black bears, bighorn sheep, river otters and 75% of Utah's bird species. Hundreds of lakes feed cold rivers that carve through canyons, forming the headwaters of the state.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/HighUintasW.Todd%20Slagter.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 768px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr, Todd Slagter
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     16. Linville Gorge Wilderness, North Carolina
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     "The Grand Canyon of North Carolina" is
                    </span>
                    one of the most scenic river gorges in the eastern U.S. Its rough terrain prevented clear-cutting so it remains one of only a few old growth forests in the Blue Ridge Mountains range. Linville is the only gorge in the state without a road in the bottom and the only one in the nation designated as wilderness by the
                    <a href="http://wilderness.org/article/wilderness-act">
                     Act
                    </a>
                    that has never changed in acreage. Visitors are drawn to rock climbing at Jonas Ridge, scenic viewing at Linville Falls as well as backpacking, hunting and fishing. Those seeking more solitude are advised to explore the southern half of the wilderness or the rough northern section around Brushy Ridge.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="768" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/Linville%20GorgeW.James%20Wilson.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 768px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr, James Wilson
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     17. Shining Rock Wilderness, North Carolina
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Shining Rocks name comes from a white quartz outcropping near the peak of Shining Rock Mountain. The original 13,400 acres designated fifty years ago has since expanded to 18,483 acres. The largest Wilderness in the state, Middle Prong Wilderness lies at its southwestern border. Strewn with streams, its West Fork of Pigeon River runs to Cold Mountain, the namesake of a novel and film set during the Civil War. Logging and wildfires during the first half of last century are evidenced by grassy "balds" today. Despite hard-to-follow trails, this wilderness remains one of the most visited in the state, perhaps because it is the closest to the city of Asheville.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="693" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/ShiningRockW.anoldent.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 693px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,
                    </em>
                    <em>
                     anoldent
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     18. Shenandoah Wilderness, Virginia
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     Shenandoah National Park is 40% wilderness, which includes about 175 miles of trails. Its proximity to the legendary
                     <a href="/node/107652">
                      Appalachian Trail
                     </a>
                     as well as Skyline Drive means it is a well-visited one. Visitors would never guess that its abundant forests were once nearly completely cleared. Thankfully, today they are home to many wild creatures, including deer, bears, bobcats, wild turkeys and about 200 other species of birds.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="1728" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/Shenandoah%20Wilderness.wildernessnet.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 768px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="2304"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: wilderness.net
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     19. Absaroka - Beartooth Wilderness, Montana and Wyoming
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     The Beartooth Range has many high peaks, including one that looks like a bear's tooth. Granite Peak, Montana's tallest, towers over the vast Beartooth Plateau and alpine lakes are small but plentiful. The neighboring Absaroka mountain range, named for what the Crow Indians called themselves, has dense forests and expansive meadows with trout-rich streams. It extends to the northern boundary of Yellowstone National Park, offering over 700 miles of trails for ultimate backpacking adventures.
                    </span>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="522" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/BeartoothWilderness.Mouser%20Williams.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1024"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: Flickr,Mouser Williams
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <h3 dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     20. Selway - Bitterroot Wilderness, Idaho and Montana
                    </span>
                   </h3>
                   <p dir="ltr">
                    <span id="docs-internal-guid-59aa3c6b-4377-05e5-ae47-9a46cc0f1ddf">
                     A dirt road divides Frank Church-River of No Return Wilderness from Selway-Bitterroot, the third largest Wilderness outside of Alaska. Inside, the Bitterroot Mountains rise at the border of Idaho and Montana and the Wild and Scenic Selway River is Idahos premier whitewater river. Among its 1,800 miles of rugged trails through coniferous canyons and valleys, many remain sparse. This makes it ideal habitat for the plentiful Selway elk herd,
                    </span>
                    as well as recently reintroduced packs of the grey wolf. This area offers the quintessentially wild experience, so it is legendary among backpackers, as well as delightfully expansive - both reasons why it is one of the most visited.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <img alt="" class="media-image" height="2796" src="http://wilderness.org/sites/default/files/Selway-BitterrootW.Marty%20Almquist.jpg" style="width: 1024px; height: 675px;" typeof="foaf:Image" width="4240"/>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <em>
                     credit: wilderness.net,Marty Almquist
                    </em>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    The volume of American wilderness visitors indicates how prized these areas truly are. But for places treasured for their removal from hindrances of man, a delicate balance must be struck. When it comes to wilderness, popularity has its risks. For one, crowds decrease the opportunities for solitude that wilderness so uniquely offers. They also can lead to undesired impacts on fragile areas, such as trampling of plants, campfire dangers, tree damage, wildlife disturbance and littering.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    These areas are protected in part so they can be enjoyed, but visitors are also responsible for caring for them. Regardless of how you plant to revel in their beauty, please observe
                    <a href="/node/351">
                     leave no trace
                    </a>
                    principles during your visit. Visiting throughout the weekalso helps prevent overcrowded weekends.
                   </p>
                   <h3>
                    <a href="/node/351">
                     Get our leave no trace tips here.
                    </a>
                   </h3>
