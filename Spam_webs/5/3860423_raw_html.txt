     <p>
      <b>
       Siddha Yoga
      </b>
      is a spiritual path founded by
      <a href="/wiki/Muktananda" title="Muktananda">
       Muktananda
      </a>
      (19081982). The present spiritual head of the Siddha Yoga path is
      <a href="/wiki/Gurumayi_Chidvilasananda" title="Gurumayi Chidvilasananda">
       Gurumayi Chidvilasananda
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A fundamental characteristic of the Siddha Yoga path is shaktipat-diksha, literally translated as initiation by descent of divine power, though which a seekers Kundalini Shakti is awakened by the Guru. Once active, this inner power is said to support the seekers steady efforts to attain self-realization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ashrams and meditation centers provide places to learn and practice Siddha Yoga. The two main
      <a href="/wiki/Ashram" title="Ashram">
       ashrams
      </a>
      are: Gurudev Siddha Peeth in Ganeshpuri, India, and Shree Muktananda Ashram in upstate New York. There are meditation centers in a number of countries, including India, the United States,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      , France, Belgium, Germany, Italy, Canada, Mexico,
      <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
       Brazil
      </a>
      and Japan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vision_and_mission_statement">
       Vision and mission statement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siddha_Yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Vision and mission statement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Siddha Yoga vision statement reads,
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       For everyone, everywhere, to realize the presence of divinity in themselves and creation, the cessation of all miseries and suffering, and the attainment of supreme bliss.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddhayoga.org_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-siddhayoga.org-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The Siddha Yoga mission statement reads,
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       To constantly impart the knowledge of the Self. (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiva_Sutra" title="Shiva Sutra">
        Shiva Sutras
       </a>
       III.28)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddhayoga.org_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-siddhayoga.org-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siddha_Yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Muktananda's spiritual teacher,
      <a href="/wiki/Bhagawan_Nityananda" title="Bhagawan Nityananda">
       Bhagawan Nityananda
      </a>
      , has been widely regarded throughout India as a
      <a href="/wiki/Siddha" title="Siddha">
       Siddha
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Guru" title="Guru">
       Guru
      </a>
      and as an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avadhut" title="Avadhut">
       Avadhut
      </a>
      since the mid-20th century. Born in
      <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">
       South India
      </a>
      , he first came to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganeshpuri" title="Ganeshpuri">
       Ganeshpuri
      </a>
      , a small village located 82 kilometers north of
      <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">
       Mumbai
      </a>
      , in 1936, settling there in a small hut built for him by the caretakers of the local
      <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">
       Shiva
      </a>
      temple. As his visitors and devotees increased in number, the hut expanded into an ashram.
     </p>
     <p>
      In his autobiography,
      <i>
       Play of Consciousness,
      </i>
      Muktananda describes how he received
      <a href="/wiki/Shaktipat" title="Shaktipat">
       shaktipat
      </a>
      initiation from Nityananda on August 15, 1947, and how he attained
      <a href="/wiki/Enlightenment_(spiritual)" title="Enlightenment (spiritual)">
       God-realization
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Moksha" title="Moksha">
       moksha
      </a>
      after nine more years of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadhana" title="Sadhana">
       sadhana
      </a>
      and discipleship.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muktananda_1971_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Muktananda_1971-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nityananda installed Muktananda in a small three-room dwelling in Gavdevi, a mile from Ganeshpuri. After his death in 1961, Nityananda's Ganeshpuri ashram was converted into a
      <a href="/wiki/Samadhi" title="Samadhi">
       samadhi shrine
      </a>
      and has subsequently become a renowned temple and pilgrimage site. Under Muktananda's leadership the three-room dwelling in Gavdevi also expanded into a flourishing ashram and international retreat site (Sri Gurudev Ashram, now
      <a href="/wiki/Gurudev_Siddha_Peeth" title="Gurudev Siddha Peeth">
       Gurudev Siddha Peeth
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From August 27 to 30, 1974, Muktananda led the first Shaktipat Intensive in Aspen, Colorado.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Through Shaktipat Intensives, created by Muktananda, participants are said to receive shaktipat initiation (the awakening of Kundalini Shakti that is said to reside within a person) and to deepen their practice of Siddha Yoga meditation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Historically, Shaktipat initiation had been reserved for the few who had done many years of spiritual service and practices; Muktananda offered this initiation to newcomers and yogis alike.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1974, Muktananda founded the SYDA Foundation, an organization designated to protect, preserve and facilitate the dissemination of the Siddha Yoga teachings. In 1975 Muktananda founded the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siddha_Yoga_Ashram_in_Oakland" title="Siddha Yoga Ashram in Oakland">
       Siddha Yoga Ashram in Oakland
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">
       San Francisco Bay Area
      </a>
      , and in 1976 he established Shree Nityananda Ashram (now
      <a href="/wiki/Shree_Muktananda_Ashram" title="Shree Muktananda Ashram">
       Shree Muktananda Ashram
      </a>
      ) in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catskills_Mountains" title="Catskills Mountains">
       Catskills Mountains
      </a>
      , north of
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      . His fame increased to the point that he was made the subject of a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">
        New York
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine article ("Hanging out with the Guru") and a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine article ("Instant Energy"), both in 1976.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Muktananda also created The Prison Project in 1979, designed to making the teachings, practices and experience of the Siddha Yoga path available to incarcerated seekers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of Muktananda's earliest and principal disciples was Malti Shetty, a young woman from Mumbai who accompanied him as his
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English language
      </a>
      interpreter on his second and third World Tours. In May 1982, Muktananda installed Shetty (now known as Gurumayi Chidvilasananda or Gurumayi) and her brother Subhash Shetty (now known as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mahamandaleshwar_Nityananda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mahamandaleshwar Nityananda (page does not exist)">
       Mahamandaleshwar Nityananda
      </a>
      ) as co-Gurus and spiritual leaders of the Siddha Yoga path. Muktananda died on October 2, 1982.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1983 William Rodarmor wrote an article for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/CoEvolution_Quarterly" title="CoEvolution Quarterly">
        CoEvolution Quarterly
       </a>
      </i>
      charging that Muktananda had engaged in behavior at odds with his own teachings and with wider societal norms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodarmor_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rodarmor-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1985, Nityananda stepped down amid controversy and two years later started his own organization, Shanti Mandir.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Chidvilasananda continued in her appointed role and has been the sole spiritual leader of Siddha Yoga since then.
      <a href="/wiki/Lis_Harris" title="Lis Harris">
       Lis Harris
      </a>
      repeated and extended Rodarmor's allegations in an article in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">
        The New Yorker
       </a>
      </i>
      (1994).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1996 former devotees started a website entitled 'Leaving Siddha Yoga' to express their grievances against Siddha Yoga.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An article by Sarah Caldwell in the
      <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">
       academic journal
      </a>
      <i>
       Nova Religio
      </i>
      (2001) argued that Muktananda was both an enlightened spiritual teacher and a practitioner of Shakta Tantrism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1992 Chidvilasananda founded the
      <a href="/wiki/Prasad" title="Prasad">
       PRASAD
      </a>
      Project, an independent, not-for-profit,
      <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">
       charitable organization
      </a>
      dedicated to providing impoverished communities in India with
      <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">
       medical care
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">
       dental care
      </a>
      , eye care, nutrition, education and community development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1997 she established the Muktabodha Institute, an independent non-profit foundation with its own publishing
      <a href="/wiki/Imprint_(trade_name)" title="Imprint (trade name)">
       imprint
      </a>
      , Agama Press, to foster and encourage the preservation and study of the ancient philosophical texts of India.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Practices">
       Practices
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siddha_Yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Practices">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Siddha Yoga practices are intended to help the seeker "touch and expand the inner mystical state, until over time he or she becomes established in his experience of yoga or oneness with God."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Siddha Yoga meditation, or the practice of turning the attention inward, involves silently focusing the attention on a
      <a href="/wiki/Mantra" title="Mantra">
       mantra
      </a>
      and on the flow of breath. The principal Siddha Yoga meditation mantra is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aum_namah_Shivaya" title="Aum namah Shivaya">
       Om Namah Shivaya
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Siddha Yoga chanting involves the use of music and sacred mantras "to enter into a dialogue with the divine." There are two main types of Siddha Yoga chants:
      <i>
       namasankirtana
      </i>
      (lyrical chanting of
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      mantras, typically the names of God), and
      <i>
       swadhyaya
      </i>
      (the chanting of longer Sanskrit scriptural texts). Scriptural texts chanted in Siddha Yoga ashrams and meditation centers include the morning and evening
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arati" title="Arati">
        Arati
       </a>
      </i>
      ; the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Guru_Gita" title="Guru Gita">
        Guru Gita
       </a>
      </i>
      , a hymn of 182 verses transmitted in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Skanda_Purana" title="Skanda Purana">
        Skanda Purana
       </a>
      </i>
      ;
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shri_Rudram_Chamakam" title="Shri Rudram Chamakam">
        Shree Rudram
       </a>
      </i>
      , an ancient hymn to
      <a href="/wiki/Rudra" title="Rudra">
       Rudra
      </a>
      (Shiva) preserved in the
      <i>
       Krishna
       <a href="/wiki/Yajurveda" title="Yajurveda">
        Yajurveda
       </a>
      </i>
      ; and the
      <i>
       Kundalini Stavaha
      </i>
      , an eight-stanza hymn to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kundalini_energy" title="Kundalini energy">
       Kundalini
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Siddha Yoga students can participate in
      <a href="/wiki/Satsang" title="Satsang">
       satsang
      </a>
      , group meetings or programs held weekly at Siddha Yoga ashrams and meditation centers. Satsangs typically include talks, chanting, and meditation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The SYDA Foundation offers a variety of courses and retreats throughout the year, including the meditation intensives first developed by Muktananda in the 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Siddha Yoga students engage in
      <a href="/wiki/Selfless_service" title="Selfless service">
       seva
      </a>
      , or "selfless service," as a spiritual practice. Students can practice seva through volunteer work at an ashram or a meditation center in their city. The work of the SYDA Foundation is carried out by the work of "sevites."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other Siddha Yoga practices include
      <a href="/wiki/Japa" title="Japa">
       japa
      </a>
      (mantra repetition), contemplation, and
      <a href="/wiki/Dakshina" title="Dakshina">
       dakshina
      </a>
      , the traditional practice of making a voluntary monetary offering to a saint as an expression of gratitude for the grace and teachings one is said to have received.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_Days">
       Holy Days
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siddha_Yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Holy Days">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Students of Siddha Yoga celebrate two major Hindu religious holy days:
      <a href="/wiki/Maha_Shivaratri" title="Maha Shivaratri">
       Maha Shivaratri
      </a>
      (celebrated two days before the new moon in February/March) and
      <a href="/wiki/Guru_Purnima" title="Guru Purnima">
       Guru Purnima
      </a>
      (celebrated on the full moon in JulyAugust). They also celebrate the birthdays of Muktananda and Chidvilasananda; Muktananda's divya diksha day (the day he received initiation); and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahasamadhi" title="Mahasamadhi">
       mahasamadhi
      </a>
      anniversaries of Muktananda and Bhagawan Nityananda.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scriptures">
       Scriptures
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siddha_Yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scriptures">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Siddha Yoga tradition draws its teachings from the yogic philosophy of
      <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">
       Vedanta
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kashmir_Shaivism" title="Kashmir Shaivism">
       Kashmir Shaivism
      </a>
      , and the Maharashtran poet-saints.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scriptures_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-scriptures-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Principal texts from the Vedantic tradition include the
      <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">
       Vedas
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">
       Upanishads
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">
       Bhagavad Gita
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viveka_Chudamani" title="Viveka Chudamani">
       Viveka Chudamani
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Shankaracharya" title="Shankaracharya">
       Shankaracharya
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Yoga_Vasistha" title="Yoga Vasistha">
       Yoga Vasistha
      </a>
      . Texts from the Kashmir Shaivite tradition include the
      <a href="/wiki/Shiva_Sutras_of_Vasugupta" title="Shiva Sutras of Vasugupta">
       Shiva Sutras of Vasugupta
      </a>
      , the Spanda Karikas of
      <a href="/wiki/Vasugupta" title="Vasugupta">
       Vasugupta
      </a>
      , the Prataybhijnahridayam, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vijnana_Bhairava" title="Vijnana Bhairava">
       Vijnana Bhairava
      </a>
      . Other texts referred to by Muktananda and Chidvilasananda include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dnyaneshwar" title="Dnyaneshwar">
       Jnaneshwari
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Yoga_Sutras_of_Patanjali" title="Yoga Sutras of Patanjali">
       Yoga Sutras of Patanjali
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Narada_Bhakti_Sutra" title="Narada Bhakti Sutra">
       Bhakti Sutras of Narada
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">
       Mahabharata
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">
       Ramayana
      </a>
      , and poet saints such as
      <a href="/wiki/Kabir" title="Kabir">
       Kabir
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tukaram" title="Tukaram">
       Tukaram Maharaj
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siddha_Yoga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "Siddha Yoga" ("perfect" or "perfected" yoga) is a Sanskrit term adopted by Muktananda to describe the path of
      <a href="/wiki/Self-realization" title="Self-realization">
       self-realization
      </a>
      that he embarked on under the guidance of his spiritual teacher, the Indian saint Bhagawan Nityananda. Muktananda regarded the path he learned from his teacher as a perfect path because it embraced all of the traditional yogas (
      <a href="/wiki/Jnana_yoga" title="Jnana yoga">
       jnana yoga
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karma_yoga" title="Karma yoga">
       karma yoga
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raja_yoga" title="Raja yoga">
       raja yoga
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Bhakti_yoga" title="Bhakti yoga">
       bhakti yoga
      </a>
      ), spontaneously bringing the disciple to perfection in each. In 1975 Muktananda founded the SYDA Foundation (Siddha Yoga Dham Associates) to administer the work of his global "meditation revolution."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      "Siddha Yoga" has been a registered
      <a href="/wiki/Service_mark" title="Service mark">
       service mark
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TESS_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TESS-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the SYDA Foundation, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">
       domestic non-profit corporation
      </a>
      , since 1977.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As an educational service mark, it is used in teaching and conducting workshops for individual spiritual development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TESS_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TESS-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The ancient generic Sanskrit term "Siddha Yoga" (or "perfected" yoga) is attested in the Third Tantra of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirumandhiram" title="Thirumandhiram">
       Tirumantiram
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirumoolar" title="Thirumoolar">
       Tirumular
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a Tamil poet of the 7th or 8th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A definition of "Siddha Yoga" is also offered by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swami_Shankar_Purushottam_Tirtha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swami Shankar Purushottam Tirtha (page does not exist)">
       Swami Shankar Purushottam Tirtha
      </a>
      , a yogi from the dual Tirtha/Siddhayoga lineage, who wrote two books on "Siddhayoga" in the early 1900s:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       The easy way of attaining it (salvation) is said to be Siddhayoga... Siddhayoga or Siddhimarga is that means by which yoga can be attained without difficulty... Siddhayoga is attained by the infusion of spiritual force through the good grace of a saintly preceptor... Siddhayoga or Siddhimarga is nothing but the knowledge of the unity of Self and Brahma...
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         34
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      A further definition of "Siddha Yoga" was offered in 1948 by Swami Purushottam Tirtha's disciple, Swami Vishnu Tirth:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       Therefore the
       <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">
        yoga
       </a>
       of Kundalini is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Mahayoga" title="Mahayoga">
        Mahayoga
       </a>
       . It is also sometimes called Siddhayoga because it can be acquired only through the favor of a perfect master (Siddha Guru), without any effort on the part of the initiated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         35
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       It has been said that through shaktipat Kundalini is soon awakened, and that Mahayoga or Siddhayoga is the direct outcome.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         36
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Muktananda himself defined a true Guru, or spiritual teacher, as "one who awakens the inner shakti Kundalini through shaktipat."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muktananda_1971_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Muktananda_1971-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
