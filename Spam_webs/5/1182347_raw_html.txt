             <figure class="caption">
              <img alt="" height="513" src="/sites/default/files/images/imported/KUCHNIA/Lazanki/full_lazanki_fot._grazyna_makara_770.jpg" width="770"/>
              <figcaption>
               azanki, photo: Grayna Makara
              </figcaption>
             </figure>
             <p class="article-header">
              Hated by some and beloved by others, this Polish lasagna can bring back bitter memories of communist canteens or the tastes of childhood.
             </p>
             <p>
              It is thought that the name of this square pasta is derived from the Italian lasagne. The similarities are obvious. Legend has it that azanki came to Poland in the 16th century through Bona Sforza, the Italian wife of the Polish king Sigismund I. The queen brought Italian ingredients to Poland, predominantly vegetables and pasta. The duality of Polish cuisine emerges in the 16th century Bona didnt accept the heavy and greasy Polish cuisine, based mainly on meat and  compared to that of her family  lacking in vegetables. Italian cooks took over the palace, unfortunately this wasnt met with universal approval  writes Christina Bockenheim in her book
              <strong>
               On the Polish Table
              </strong>
              . In contrast to other dishes from Italy, azanki survived until the 21st century, although its long history had been forgotten.
             </p>

                 <div class="field-name-title">
                  <h2 class="title-link">
                   <a href="/en/article/10-surprising-eating-habits-of-communist-poland">
                    10 Surprising Eating Habits of Communist Poland
                   </a>
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                 <div class="field-name-body">
                  <p>
                   <span class="more-link">
                    <a href="/en/article/10-surprising-eating-habits-of-communist-poland">
                     Read more 
                    </a>
                   </span>
                  </p>
                 </div>
                 <div class="field field-name-field-category field-type-taxonomy-term-reference field-label-hidden">
                  <div class="field-items">
                   <div class="field-item even">
                    <a href="/en/category/polish-cuisine" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" typeof="skos:Concept">
                     POLISH CUISINE
                    </a>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
             </p>
             <div id="ad-links">
              <script type="text/javascript">
               &lt;!--// &lt;![CDATA[
                OA_show('link_in_text');
                // ]]&gt; --&gt;
              </script>
             </div>
             <p>
              Today, azanki are made with pickled or fresh cabbage, fresh mushrooms, sausage, bacon and are often topped with lard. There are probably as many recipes as there are cooks, each one claiming to be the best. Although one can buy azanki made in a factory, home-made ones tend to taste better. The pasta itself often finds its way into Christmas Eve soup with mushroom or fish. The dish can also be sweetened with poppy seeds.
             </p>
             <p>
              200 years ago, azanki were made a little differently. Instead of mixing the pasta with extra ingredients and baking it, azanki were placed in layers over the ingredients, exactly like lasagne today. This method of preparation was used in the early 20th century by the famous culinary author Maria OchorowiczMonatowa, who described recipes with mushrooms, cabbage and cottage cheese.
             </p>
             <figure class="caption">
              <img alt="azanki, photo: Grayna Makara" height="513" src="/sites/default/files/images/imported/KUCHNIA/Lazanki/full_lazanki_fot._grazyna_makara_2_770.jpg" width="770"/>
              <figcaption>
               azanki, photo: Grayna Makara
              </figcaption>
             </figure>
             <p>
              Lviv cookbooks from the early nineteenth century describe recipes for azanki baked in a pastry with ham, butter and cream; the dish was called "pate with ham." Another recipe from Lviv at that time was for potato azanki. They were cut and dried like wheat pasta, cooked in water, broth or milk. Melted butter or parmesan cheese was added, sometimes with a pinch of saffron, and it was put into an oven. azanki can also be eaten with stockfish or with more sophisticated ingredients such as rabbit meat, deer, lamb, pheasant or partridge, and even with oysters and truffles, red wine and cayenne pepper. Other dishes included azanki with crayfish: the pasta was baked with crayfish butter, crayfish tails and cream. azanki with ham could also be stuffed with pork before being cooked on the grill. Parmesan cheese, home-made cheese and cottage cheese would often be used to cover the casserole of pasta or crumble.
             </p>
             <p>
              azanki can also be sweet: apples, pears, raspberries, currants, blueberries, cherries, plums, apricots, sour cherries, almonds, vanilla, spices were all tasty options, especially with a glass of good wine such as sweet Hungarian or Malaga. No amount of sugar should be spared!
             </p>
             <p>
              What recipes for azanki do you know?
             </p>
             <p>
              Magdalena Kasprzyk-Chevriaux , July 2014
             </p>
