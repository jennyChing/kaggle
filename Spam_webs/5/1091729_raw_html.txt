        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         A Sense of Duty in Startups
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-02-18T10:47:13+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          February 18, 2014
         </time>
         by
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.instigatorblog.com/author/byosko/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Ben Yoskovitz
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="entry-comments-link">
          <a href="http://www.instigatorblog.com/duty-in-startups/2014/02/18/#comments">
           <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="2857 http://www.instigatorblog.com/?p=2857">
            15 Comments
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <p>
         <img alt="fire truck" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2858" src="http://instigator.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/fire-truck.jpg" width="600"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive never read a blog post about duty and startups (although Im sure theyre out there), but its something Ive been thinking about for awhile. Some people (founders and employees) have a sense of duty towards the work they do. Others do not.
         <strong>
          And that sense of duty is one of the elements that separates awesome entrepreneurs and employees from everyone else.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Theres definitely a gray area between duty and responsibility. In fact you can Google duty vs. responsibility and get a lot of results. Heres an
         <a href="http://www.differencebetween.net/miscellaneous/difference-between-duties-and-responsibilities/">
          example
         </a>
         . I dont think everything it describes about duty completely applies to a startup founder or employee, but a lot of it does. I also found
         <a href="http://www.cpbj.com/article/20130828/TOPJOB/130829774/Duty-vs-responsibility">
          this article on duty vs. responsibility for CEOs
         </a>
         very apropos.
        </p>
        <p>
         Clearly, we (as founders and employees) have responsibilities. And most people in an early stage startup get their jobs done and handle their responsibilities. But its those that have a sense of duty
         <em>
          a higher moral standard that they hold themselves accountable to
         </em>
         that go beyond expectations and really make a difference.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          For me, duty is about caring. Or more specifically, caring to a level beyond the norm.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Those with a sense of duty are your go-to-guys, the founders youll back over and over, and the employees you want to hire right away when you start something new. Its cliche, but some guys will take a bullet for you, and others wont. And I wouldnt expect
         <em>
          most people
         </em>
         to take a bullet for you as a founder or fellow employee, but there are a few that just will.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When you find those people, hold onto them.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         That sense of duty gives them the ability to take their performance to another level. It gives them the internal fortitude to struggle through all the shit that youll face early on with your startup (and later too!) and push through.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          One last thing: founders have to be careful about abusing an employees sense of duty.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         It might be easy to rely on people over and over knowing that theyre compelled to help/work/struggle. In fact, if you want your startup to succeed, youll have to rely on those people! But as much as duty is something that comes from within a person, I believe it needs to be recognized, and peoples work needs to be rewarded. In my experience, most people that do have a sense of duty towards the work they do, dont need a lot of external validation, but they still need some. They dont need trinkets, baubles, or little perksthey need to know that the person theyre taking a bullet for / following blindly on an insane startup journey, recognizes their value.
        </p>

Now that Im writing this, I sound like a negative Nancy. Maybe thats just how I see the words and how theyve been attributed to my life experiences. Therefore, Ive always felt that the more responsibilities (or the more of important responsibilities to you) that you can accomplish, means the more success you will have. For example, if you accomplish 3 duties in one day, you may feel accomplished that they were completed, but only because they had to be. If you accomplished 3 responsibilities in one day, you will still feel accomplished, but because you wanted to complete these things and were able to do so with your own creativity and methods. Does this make any sense to anyone else, or is it just me?
            </p>

             Theres no doubt that when starting up a business, theres a fair share of duties and responsibilities involved. However, I feel that entrepreneurs and CEOs should encourage positive responsibilities with their partners and employees, as a sense of opportunity for success and growth, over duty, as a task that must be completed whether you like it or not in order to stay afloat.
