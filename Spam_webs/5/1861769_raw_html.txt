    Black Knight satellite

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Black Knight satellite
      </b>
      is claimed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theorists" title="Conspiracy theorists">
       conspiracy theorists
      </a>
      to be an object approximately 13,000 years old of
      <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
       extraterrestrial
      </a>
      origin orbiting Earth in near-
      <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">
       polar orbit
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redpath_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Redpath-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flockstra_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Flockstra-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stories_and_myths">
       Stories and myths
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Knight_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stories and myths">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The story has its origins in 1954 when newspapers including the
      <i>
       St. Louis Post Dispatch
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       San Francisco Examiner
      </i>
      ran stories attributed to retired naval aviation major and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UFO" title="UFO">
       UFO
      </a>
      researcher
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Keyhoe" title="Donald Keyhoe">
       Donald Keyhoe
      </a>
      saying that the US Air Force had reported that two satellites orbiting Earth had been detected. At this time no one had the technology to launch a satellite.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skep_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-skep-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 1960 there was a further claim by TIME that the US Navy had detected a dark object thought to be a Russian spy satellite in an orbit inclined at 79 from the equator with an
      <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
       orbital period
      </a>
      of 104.5 minutes. Its orbit was also highly eccentric with an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
       apogee
      </a>
      of 1,728km (1,074mi) and a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
       perigee
      </a>
      of only 216km (134mi). At the time the Navy was tracking a fragment of casing from the
      <a href="/wiki/Corona_(satellite)#Discoverer" title="Corona (satellite)">
       Discoverer VIII
      </a>
      satellite launch which has the same orbit, and it is believed to be a derelict US satellite that had gone astray.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skep_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-skep-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An object photographed in 1998 during the
      <a href="/wiki/STS-88" title="STS-88">
       STS-88
      </a>
      mission has been widely claimed to be this "alien artifact". However, it is more probable that the photographs are of a thermal blanket that had been reported as lost during an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
       EVA
      </a>
      , which was later confirmed by the astronaut who lost said object from the airlock.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redpath_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Redpath-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Martina Redpath of
      <a href="/wiki/Armagh_Planetarium" title="Armagh Planetarium">
       Armagh Planetarium
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Black Knight is a jumble of completely unrelated stories; reports of unusual science observations, authors promoting fringe ideas, classified spy satellites and people over-interpreting photos. These ingredients have chopped up, stirred together and stewed on the internet to one rambling and inconsistent dollop of myth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redpath_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Redpath-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
