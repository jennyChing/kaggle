             <h1 class="entry-title">
              How many hackers does it take to screw up a light bulb?
             </h1>
             <div class="post-info">
              <span class="date published time" title="2015-05-19T10:29:19+00:00">
               May 19, 2015
              </span>
              <span class="author vcard">
               <span class="fn">
                <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/author/btletstalk/" rel="author">
                 BT Lets Talk
                </a>
               </span>
              </span>
              <span class="post-comments">
               <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/2015/05/how-many-hackers-does-it-take-to-screw-up-a-light-bulb/#respond">
                <span class="number">
                 0
                </span>
               </a>
              </span>
             </div>
             <div class="entry-content">
              <p>
               <strong>
                By Richard Brooker, Chief Architect  IT Managed Services Practice at BT Global Services.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                The Internet of Things (IoT) is already taking shape around us.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                By 2020, theres going to be
                <a href="http://www.zonealarm.com/blog/2014/11/the-benefits-and-security-risks-of-the-internet-of-things/" target="_blank">
                 around 212 billion networked things
                </a>
                . Excellent news, right? Everything from our heaters to our hobs, our cars to our kettles, will all be joined up to the net, so we can sit back, relax and control everything from anywhere in the world. But is our rush to hook up our world putting us at further risk from hacking?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span id="more-15431">
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               Its hard to look at everyday objects and see them as something we need to protect from a security breach. After all, when was the last time you looked at your fridge and thought it was a threat? Or saw your kettle as anything other than your shortcut to your mission-critical cup of coffee? Or wondered if your dishwasher was up to something devious?
              </p>
              <p>
               It might seem ludicrous to think in these terms, but these everyday items could open the backdoor to our wider network of connected things  making it easier to steal our personal information or provide a platform from which bad things could be done without our knowledge.
              </p>
              <p>
               The trick to getting the very best from the IoT is thinking one step ahead of cyber sleuths.
              </p>
              <p>
               Heres a list of a just a few everyday objects that might need securing in tomorrows world (or today, if youre lucky).
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 Light bulbs
                </strong>
                 while they seem innocent enough,
                <a href="http://www.pcworld.com/article/2868884/new-security-risks-accompany-the-internet-of-things.html" target="_blank">
                 connected smart bulbs
                </a>
                are just around the corner. They allow you to set up a schedule for when theyre on and when theyre off, or you can switch them on and off whenever you please through your smartphone. The loophole? Because they connect via wi-fi, they contain your wireless password
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 TVs
                </strong>
                 the technology seen recently in a small way in games consoles and smartphones is now being put into TVs. These listening TVs use voice-activated controls. Pretty space age, but a
                <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/technology-31296188" target="_blank">
                 Samsung recently warned of a scenario where the technology recorded sensitive information and passed it on to third-parties
                </a>
                .
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 Home security systems
                </strong>
                 of course youre aware that your home security system needs to be unbreakable, but those that can be operated remotely are clearly
                <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/business-31157975" target="_blank">
                 vulnerable to unauthorised access
                </a>
                . Attackers can log on as you to find out when youre at home, when youre away and even watch video of you from anywhere in the world.
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 Medical devices
                </strong>
                 the new breed of
                <a href="http://www.zdnet.com/pictures/hacking-vulnerabilities-with-the-internet-of-things-risks-and-security-loopholes/7/" target="_blank">
                 implanted devices
                </a>
                , like those that control the insulin levels of diabetics, use wireless transmissions and if the signals between the insulin pump and the glucose monitor are hacked and hijacked, it could lead to the delivery of incorrect dosages  potentially fatal.
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 Cars
                </strong>
                
                <a href="http://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/2014/06/keeping-cars-safe/" target="_blank">
                 the cars we drive are rapidly becoming smartphones on wheels
                </a>
                embedded with SIM cards and wi-fi hotspots so there are plenty of ways for hackers to find a way in. They could hijack your smartphone app, unlock the car without the keys, or worse, lock you out and demand a ransom to let you back in (and thats just the beginning).
               </li>
              </ul>
              <p>
               The outlook is not bleak though, there are several steps being taken to make sure youre completely protected. The
               <a href="https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/press-releases/2015/01/ftc-report-internet-things-urges-companies-adopt-best-practices" target="_blank">
                Federal Trade Commission in America has called for best practice guidelines
               </a>
               , which will regulate the security measures companies use to protect products and their users. And it means there would be an IoT-specific watchdog to
               <a href="http://www.cio-today.com/article/index.php?story_id=12100006WREF" target="_blank">
                make sure companies build security into every connected object
               </a>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               Before IoT really takes off, you need the assurance that every smart device you buy will keep your private information safe. As companies, we have to decide the best way to protect your customers from attacks and make sure security is a high priority for your products. Are you doing enough?
              </p>
              <p>
               Chat to us today and see if theres anything more you can do to make sure you and your customers have peace of mind.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Find out more about
                <a href="http://www.globalservices.bt.com/uk/en/insights/internet-of-things" target="_blank">
                 BTs point of view on the Internet of Things
                </a>
                .
               </strong>
              </p>
