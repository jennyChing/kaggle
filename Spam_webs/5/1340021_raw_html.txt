     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
       chemistry
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">
       manufacturing
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       electrolysis
      </b>
      is a technique that uses a
      <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
       direct
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
       electric current
      </a>
      (DC) to drive an otherwise non-spontaneous chemical reaction. Electrolysis is commercially highly important as a stage in the
      <a href="#Industrial_uses">
       separation
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       elements
      </a>
      from naturally occurring sources such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ores" title="Ores">
       ores
      </a>
      using an
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolytic_cell" title="Electrolytic cell">
       electrolytic cell
      </a>
      . The voltage that is needed for electrolysis to occur is called the
      <a href="/wiki/Decomposition_potential" title="Decomposition potential">
       decomposition potential
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word electrolysis comes from the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">
        [lektron]
       </a>
      </span>
      "amber" and
      <i>
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        
       </span>
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">
        [lsis]
       </a>
      </span>
      "dissolution".
     </p>

      <li>
       1785 
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martinus_van_Marum" title="Martinus van Marum">
        Martinus van Marum
       </a>

        tin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
        zinc
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">
        antimony
       </a>
       from their salts using electrolysis.

         </span>

         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>

       <a href="/wiki/William_Nicholson_(chemist)" title="William Nicholson (chemist)">
        William Nicholson
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Carlisle" title="Anthony Carlisle">
        Anthony Carlisle

       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        water
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>

       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">

       <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">

      </li>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Electrolysis is the passing of a
      <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
       direct
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
       electric current
      </a>
      through an
      <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
       ionic
      </a>
      substance that is either molten or dissolved in a suitable solvent, producing chemical reactions at the electrodes and separation of materials.
     </p>
     <p>
      The main components required to achieve electrolysis are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       An
       <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
        electrolyte
       </a>
       : a
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">
        substance
       </a>
       , frequently an ion-conducting
       <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">
        polymer
       </a>
       that contains free
       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
        ions
       </a>
       , which carry
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
        electric current
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
        electrolyte
       </a>
       . If the
       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
        ions
       </a>
       are not mobile, as in a
       <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">
        solid salt
       </a>
       then electrolysis cannot occur.
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
        direct current
       </a>
       (DC) electrical supply: provides the
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       necessary to create or discharge the
       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
        ions
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
        electrolyte
       </a>
       . Electric current is carried by
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       in the external circuit.
      </li>
      <li>
       Two
       <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">
        electrodes
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">
        electrical conductors
       </a>
       that provide the physical interface between the
       <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
        electrolyte
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_circuit" title="Electrical circuit">
        electrical circuit
       </a>
       that provides the
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Electrodes of
      <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">
       metal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">
       graphite
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">
       semiconductor
      </a>
      material are widely used. Choice of suitable electrode depends on chemical reactivity between the electrode and electrolyte and manufacturing cost.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Process_of_electrolysis">
       Process of electrolysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Process of electrolysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The key process of electrolysis is the interchange of atoms and ions by the removal or addition of electrons from the external circuit. The desired products of electrolysis are often in a different physical state from the electrolyte and can be removed by some physical processes. For example, in the electrolysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">
       brine
      </a>
      to produce hydrogen and chlorine, the products are gaseous. These gaseous products bubble from the electrolyte and are collected.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilley2004_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tilley2004-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       2 NaCl + 2 H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O  2 NaOH + H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       + Cl
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      A liquid containing mobile ions (electrolyte) is produced by:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">
        Solvation
       </a>
       or reaction of an
       <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">
        ionic compound
       </a>
       with a
       <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">
        solvent
       </a>
       (such as water) to produce mobile ions
      </li>
      <li>
       An ionic compound is fused by heating
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      An electrical potential is applied across a pair of
      <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">
       electrodes
      </a>
      immersed in the electrolyte.
     </p>
     <p>
      Each electrode attracts ions that are of the opposite
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">
       charge
      </a>
      . Positively charged ions (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">
       cations
      </a>
      ) move towards the electron-providing (negative) cathode. Negatively charged ions (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">
       anions
      </a>
      ) move towards the electron-extracting (positive) anode.
     </p>
     <p>
      In this process
      <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
       electrons
      </a>
      are either absorbed or released. Neutral atoms gain or lose electrons and become charged ions that then pass into the electrolyte. The formation of uncharged atoms from ions is called discharging. When an ion gains or loses enough electrons to become uncharged (neutral) atoms, the newly formed atoms separate from the electrolyte. Positive metal ions like Cu
      <sup>
       ++
      </sup>
      deposit onto the cathode in a layer. The terms for this are
      <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">
       electroplating
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Electrowinning" title="Electrowinning">
       electrowinning
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrorefining" title="Electrorefining">
       electrorefining
      </a>
      . When an ion gains or loses electrons without becoming neutral, its electronic charge is altered in the process. In chemistry the loss of electrons is called oxidation while electron gain is called reduction.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation_and_reduction_at_the_electrodes">

      </span>
     </h3>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">

      <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">

     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_changes_during_electrolysis">
       Energy changes during electrolysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Energy changes during electrolysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The amount of electrical energy that must be added equals the change in
      <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">
       Gibbs free energy
      </a>
      of the reaction plus the losses in the system. The losses can (in theory) be arbitrarily close to zero, so the maximum
      <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">
       thermodynamic
      </a>
      efficiency equals the
      <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">
       enthalpy
      </a>
      change divided by the free energy change of the reaction. In most cases, the electric input is larger than the enthalpy change of the reaction, so some energy is released in the form of heat. In some cases, for instance, in the electrolysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      into hydrogen and oxygen at high temperature, the opposite is true and heat energy is absorbed. This heat is absorbed from the surroundings, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating_value" title="Heating value">
       heating value
      </a>
      of the produced hydrogen is higher than the electric input.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_techniques">
       Related techniques
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Related techniques">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The following techniques are related to electrolysis:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">
        Electrochemical cells
       </a>
       , including the hydrogen
       <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">
        fuel cell
       </a>
       , use differences in
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">
        Standard electrode potential
       </a>
       to generate an electrical potential that provides useful power. Though related via the interaction of ions and electrodes, electrolysis and the operation of electrochemical cells are quite distinct. A chemical cell should
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       be thought of as performing
       <i>
        electrolysis in reverse
       </i>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Faraday.27s_laws_of_electrolysis">
       Faraday's laws of electrolysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Faraday's laws of electrolysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_laws_of_electrolysis" title="Faraday's laws of electrolysis">
       Faraday's laws of electrolysis
      </a>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_law_of_electrolysis">
       First law of electrolysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: First law of electrolysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1832, Michael Faraday reported that the quantity of elements separated by passing an electric current through a molten or dissolved
      <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
       salt
      </a>
      is proportional to the quantity of electric charge passed through the circuit. This became the basis of the first law of electrolysis:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="m = k \cdot q" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/b/09b0afba8937cc81d5877921855e8e83.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Second_law_of_electrolysis">
       Second law of electrolysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Second law of electrolysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Faraday discovered that when the same amount of electricity is passed through different electrolytes/elements connected in series, the mass of substance liberated/deposited at the electrodes is directly proportional to their equivalent weights.
     </p>

     <p>
      In terms of electrolysis, this table should be interpreted as follows:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Oxidized species (often a cation) with a more negative cell potential are more difficult to reduce than oxidized species with a more positive cell potential. For example it is more difficult to reduce a sodium ion to a sodium metal than it is to reduce a zinc ion to a zinc metal.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Reduced species (often an anion) with a more positive cell potential are more difficult to oxidize than reduced species with a more negative cell potential. For example it is more difficult to oxidize sulfate anions than it is to oxidize bromide anions.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Using the
      <a href="/wiki/Nernst_equation" title="Nernst equation">
       Nernst equation
      </a>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Electrode_potential" title="Electrode potential">
       electrode potential
      </a>
      can be calculated for a specific concentration of ions, temperature and the number of electrons involved. For pure water (
      <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">
       pH
      </a>
      7):
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       the electrode potential for the reduction producing hydrogen is 0.41 V
      </li>
      <li>
       the electrode potential for the oxidation producing oxygen is +0.82 V.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Comparable figures calculated in a similar way, for 1M
      <a href="/wiki/Zinc_bromide" title="Zinc bromide">
       zinc bromide
      </a>
      , ZnBr
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      , are 0.76 V for the reduction to Zn metal and +1.10 V for the oxidation producing bromine. The conclusion from these figures is that hydrogen should be produced at the cathode and oxygen at the anode from the electrolysis of waterwhich is at variance with the experimental observation that zinc metal is deposited and bromine is produced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The explanation is that these calculated potentials only indicate the thermodynamically preferred reaction. In practice many other factors have to be taken into account such as the kinetics of some of the reaction steps involved. These factors together mean that a higher potential is required for the reduction and oxidation of water than predicted, and these are termed
      <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">
       overpotentials
      </a>
      . Experimentally it is known that
      <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">
       overpotentials
      </a>
      depend on the design of the cell and the nature of the electrodes.
     </p>
     <p>
      For the electrolysis of a neutral (pH 7) sodium chloride solution, the reduction of sodium ion is thermodynamically very difficult and water is reduced evolving hydrogen leaving hydroxide ions in solution. At the anode the oxidation of chlorine is observed rather than the oxidation of water since the overpotential for the oxidation of
      <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">
       chloride
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
       chlorine
      </a>
      is lower than the overpotential for the oxidation of
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxide_ion" title="Hydroxide ion">
       hydroxide ions
      </a>
      and dissolved
      <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
       chlorine
      </a>
      gas react further to form
      <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">
       hypochlorous acid
      </a>
      . The aqueous solutions resulting from this process is called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyzed_water" title="Electrolyzed water">
       electrolyzed water
      </a>
      and is used as a disinfectant and cleaning agent.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolysis_of_water">
       Electrolysis of water
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electrolysis of water">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">
       Electrolysis of water
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      One important use of electrolysis of water is to produce
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       2 H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O(l)  2 H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       (g) + O
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       (g); E
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       = -1.229 V
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Hydrogen can be used as a fuel for powering internal combustion engines by combustion or electric motors via hydrogen fuel cells (see
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_vehicle" title="Hydrogen vehicle">
        Hydrogen vehicle
       </a>
      </i>
      ). This has been suggested as one approach to shift economies of the world from the current state of almost complete dependence upon hydrocarbons for energy (
      <i>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">
        hydrogen economy
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      ) There are a wide range of conventional and emerging technologies being explored for the production of hydrogen from water including
      <a href="/wiki/Low_temperature_electrolysis" title="Low temperature electrolysis">
       low temperature electrolysis
      </a>
      , high temperature electrolysis and chemically assisted electrolysis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      of water electrolysis varies widely. The efficiency of an electrolyser is a measure of the enthalpy contained in the hydrogen (to undergo combustion with oxygen, or some other later reaction), compared with the input electrical energy. Heat/enthalpy values for hydrogen are well published in science and engineering texts, as 144 MJ/kg. Note that fuel cells (not electrolysers) cannot utilise this full amount of heat/enthalpy, which has led to some confusion when calculating efficiency values for both types of technology. In the reaction, some energy is lost as heat. Some reports quote efficiencies between 50% and 70% for alkaline electrolysers; however, much higher practical efficiencies are available with the use of PEM (
      <a href="/wiki/Polymer_electrolyte_membrane_electrolysis" title="Polymer electrolyte membrane electrolysis">
       Polymer Electrolyte Membrane electrolysis
      </a>
      ) and catalytic technology, such as 95% efficiency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carmo2013a_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-carmo2013a-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the US there is still an occasional erroneous tendency to use the 'Lower Heating Value' for efficiencies. This value (becoming obsolete) does not represent the total amount of energy within the hydrogen, hence the efficiency appears lower than when using the more accurately defined values. The theoretical maximum considers the total amount of energy required for the formation of the hydrogen and oxygen from water. Note that (in more broader contexts of energy efficiency), these values refer only to the efficiency of converting electrical energy into hydrogen's chemical energy; the energy lost in generating the electricity is not included.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NREL" title="NREL">
       NREL
      </a>
      estimated that 1kg of hydrogen (roughly equivalent to 3kg, or 4 L, of petroleum in energy terms) could be produced by wind powered electrolysis for between $5.55 in the near term and $2.27 in the long term.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRELElectrolysis_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NRELElectrolysis-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      About 4% of hydrogen gas produced worldwide is created by electrolysis, and normally used onsite. Hydrogen is used for the creation of ammonia for fertilizer via the
      <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">
       Haber process
      </a>
      , and converting heavy petroleum sources to lighter fractions via
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocracking" title="Hydrocracking">
       hydrocracking
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrocrystallization">
       Electrocrystallization
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charge-transfer_salt" title="Charge-transfer salt">
       charge-transfer salts
      </a>
      .
