       <h2 class="post-title">
        <span>
         7 Senior Pet Care Tips
        </span>
       </h2>
       <div class="entry clearfix">
        <img alt="seniordogwithcat" class="alignleft wp-post-image" src="http://i2.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/seniordogwithcat.jpg?resize=300%2C210"/>
        <div id="triberr_endorsement">
        </div>
        <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
         Senior pet care differs from other pet ages. Younger pets such as dogs and cats are more likely to get injured or eat something poisonous. Senior cats and dogs are more likely to suffer from illness, obesity, arthritis and mental deterioration. First, lets establish what is the age range of senior cats and dogs. It is felt that between the ages of 10-15 years, a cat or dog can be described as senior. Take a look at the infographics below about dog and cat ages vs human age in years.
        </h5>
        <h5>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/dogs-age.jpg">
          <img alt="dogs-age" class=" size-medium wp-image-581 alignright" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/dogs-age.jpg?resize=300%2C287"/>
         </a>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/catyearage.jpeg">
          <img alt="catyearage" class=" wp-image-570 alignleft" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/catyearage.jpeg?resize=220%2C263"/>
         </a>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5>
        </h5>
        <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
         As you can see, the old myth of dog years being equal to 7 human years is not completely true. Larger dogs age more rapidly than smaller dogs. Cat age is based on cats being 0-20 pounds average. The heavier cat above 20 pounds will age more quickly.
        </h5>
        <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
         So what are the care tips for senior pets? Do they differ between cats and dogs or are they the same?
        </h5>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #0000ff;">
          Senior Cats
         </span>
        </h3>
        <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
         Cats 7-10 years of age should be considered seniors. Cats over age 12 years are usually affected by arthritisand with this have increased pain and more difficulty getting around. Signs of this are decreased activity andlessenedlikelihood of jumping up on things such as counters and furniture. Dental disease can be a problem for older pets. By the time your cat is a senior, he may have lost some teeth and can experience pain and difficulty eating meals. Along with decreased eating can be weight loss. Senior cats often suffer from diseases of the kidney, thyroid, liver and heart. All of these diseases cause weight loss. Some cats are different and actually gain weight and become obese due to inactivity and being couch potatoes.
        </h5>
        <h4>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blog-CAT-WEARING-GLASSES.jpg">
          <img alt="blog-CAT-WEARING-GLASSES" class=" size-full wp-image-584 alignnone" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blog-CAT-WEARING-GLASSES.jpg?resize=240%2C171"/>
         </a>
        </h4>
        <h4>
         <span style="color: #0000ff;">
          Tips for senior cat care are:
         </span>
        </h4>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #0000ff;">
            <strong>
             Schedule regular visits with your veterinarian.
            </strong>
           </span>
           At least yearly your cat should be seen and include a body condition evaluation to determine if he is ideal body weight.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <span style="color: #0000ff;">
            <strong>
             Feed your older cat a diet with adequate protein levels.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Cats are carnivores and require meat as part of their diet. They require a higher protein level than dogs.Feed your cat a diet that ensures he stays atideal body weight.Obese cats have a higher incidence of diabetes, cancer, liver and skin diseases.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <span style="color: #0000ff;">
            <strong>
             Thinkabout fortifying your senior cats diet with supplements.
             <span style="color: #000000;">
              Consider adding f
             </span>
            </strong>
           </span>
           atty acids such as
           <strong>
            DHA
           </strong>
           and
           <strong>
            EPA
           </strong>
           as well as
           <strong>
            glucosamine
           </strong>
           and
           <strong>
            chondroitin
           </strong>
           .These are useful for cats with arthritis.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <span style="color: #0000ff;">
            <strong>
             Consider a special diet if your older cat has a disease.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Diets lower in sodium are sometimes used for cats with heart disease. Kidney disease requires controlling phosphorus, calcium, and other electrolyte levels. Restricted iodine intake is indicated for cats with hyperthyroidism. Ask your vets advice about special diets.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <span style="color: #0000ff;">
            <strong>
             Take care of your cats mouth.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Brushing your cats teeth or using cat dental treats keep your cats mouth healthy and clean.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <strong>
            <span style="color: #0000ff;">
             Stimulating surroundings is important for senior cats with mental decline.
            </span>
           </strong>
           Interactive toys may help with exercise and deter obesity. Supervised access to the outdoors will keep your cat safe.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <span style="color: #0000ff;">
            <strong>
             Provide your senior cat with special adaptations to his environment.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Cats with arthritis benefit from litter boxes with lower sides for easier access. They may even need puppy pads for toilet needs. Giving your cat a soft bed or blanket to rest on, makes him more comfortable. Food and water need to be easily accessible.
          </h5>
         </li>
        </ol>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #339966;">
          Senior Dogs
         </span>
        </h3>
        <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
         Your senior dog may develop
         <span class="glossify_term hovertip_target">
          arthritis
         </span>
         that causes him to move more slowly. He may tire more easily and have more difficulty walking as far or playing as long. He may have a harder time getting up or more difficulty finding a comfortable position for sleeping. He may not want to go up and down stairs. He may find it more difficult to get into and out of the car. Your dog may have lost some teeth and this can be painful, causing your dog to avoid or have difficulty eating his meal resulting in weight loss.Senior dogs can suffer from kidney, liver, heart disease and other conditions that result in weight loss. Some senior dogs may have the opposite problem and be less active with age and gain weight.
        </h5>
        <h4>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/o-DOG-READING-570.jpg">
          <img alt="o-DOG-READING-570" class=" wp-image-583 alignnone" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/susanspetblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/o-DOG-READING-570.jpg?resize=198%2C234"/>
         </a>
        </h4>
        <h4>
         <span style="color: #339966;">
          Tips for caring for your senior dog:
         </span>
        </h4>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
           <strong>
            <span style="color: #339966;">
             Schedule regular visits to your vet
            </span>
            .
           </strong>
           Your dog needs to be examined at least yearlyand include a body condition evaluation to determine if he is ideal body weight.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li style="text-align: justify;">
          <h5>
           <strong>
            <span style="color: #339966;">
             Feed your older dog a high-quality diet
            </span>
            .
           </strong>
           Choose a diet that is right for your dogs age and lifestyle.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li style="text-align: justify;">
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #339966;">
            <strong>
             Use special diets to keep your senior dog healthy
            </strong>
           </span>
           <strong>
            .
           </strong>
           Diets that are lower in calories and high in L-carnitine are available for overweight dogs. Lower sodium diets are sometimes helpfulfor dogs with heart disease. Kidney disease requires control of phosphorus, calcium, and other electrolyte levels.Ask your vet if a special diet is needed.Fortifying your senior dogs diet may be recommended. Fatty acids such as DHA,EPA, glucosamine and chondroitin help dogs with arthritis.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li style="text-align: justify;">
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #339966;">
            <strong>
             Take care of your dogs mouth.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Brushing your dogs teeth or using dental treats and toys will help keep the teeth clean.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li style="text-align: justify;">
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #339966;">
            <strong>
             Exercise your senior dog.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Exercise can help keep your older dogs joints and muscles healthier. Your dogs exercise needs to be adjusted to his breeds requirements and his age.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li style="text-align: justify;">
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #339966;">
            <strong>
             Provide plenty of toys to keep your senior dog occupied.
            </strong>
           </span>
           Food toys like Kong are useful for entertainment and weight loss. These activities help with mental decline as well.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li style="text-align: justify;">
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #339966;">
            <strong>
             Adapt the environment for your older dog.
            </strong>
           </span>
           With arthritis, he needs soft beddingsuch as a special dog bed or blanket on which to sleep. Ramps can be used to make stairs easier to navigate and car entry easier.
          </h5>
         </li>
        </ol>
        <h5 style="text-align: justify;">
         The last thing I want to address is increased mental decline or CDS (Cognitive Dysfunction Syndrome) in cats and dogs. This has just recently being discovered in dogs and is being studied in cats. It is thought to be caused by Alzheimers-related changes in the brain. Pets will have the following symptoms identified as:
        </h5>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #ff0000;">
          DISH
         </span>
        </h3>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #ff0000;">
            <strong>
             D
            </strong>
           </span>
           =
           <strong>
            d
           </strong>
           isorientation, confusion, not recognizing you.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #ff0000;">
            <strong>
             I
            </strong>
           </span>
           = reduced social
           <strong>
            i
           </strong>
           nteractions, apathy, and less interest in being petted.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #ff0000;">
            <strong>
             S
            </strong>
           </span>
           = changes in
           <strong>
            s
           </strong>
           leep patterns. A cat may sleep more during the day, but be up at night wandering the house, often crying out for no obvious reason. A dog will have an increased sleeping pattern and may show anxiety, shivering, and moaning when awake.
          </h5>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h5>
           <span style="color: #ff0000;">
            <strong>
             H
            </strong>
           </span>
           =
           <strong>
            h
           </strong>
           ouse soiling/
           <strong>
            h
           </strong>
           ousetraining. Cat may go outside the litterbox and dogs may soil in the house.
          </h5>
         </li>
        </ol>
        <hr/>
        <h5>
         I hope you have found this information helpful in caring for senior pets. The senior pet care tips for both dogs and cats are similar with someslight variations. Please comment below and tell me if you have senior pets and if this blog was helpful.
        </h5>
