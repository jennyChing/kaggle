       <h2 class="post-title">
        Generic Faith Doesnt Offend, But It Doesnt Save Either
       </h2>
       <h4 class="post-subtitle">
        When we talk about the particulars of the cross, or anything that invokes Jesus Christ, that is when the trouble hits
       </h4>
       <div class="post-info">
        Written by Toby B. Holt |
				Wednesday, February 19, 2014
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <div addthis:title="Generic Faith Doesnt Offend, But It Doesnt Save Either " addthis:url="http://theaquilareport.com/generic-faith-doesnt-offend-but-it-doesnt-save-either/" class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style ">
         <a class="addthis_button_facebook_like" fb:like:layout="button_count">
         </a>
         <a class="addthis_button_tweet">
         </a>
         <a class="addthis_button_pinterest_pinit">
         </a>
         <a class="addthis_counter addthis_pill_style">
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <em>
          You see, the world doesnt get offended about generic faith. For after all, thats what most people have. They have faith in faith, and hope in hope. And so when churches preach a generic message, they dont get in trouble. It is when we talk about
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           specifics
          </span>
          that the knives come out.
         </em>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Have faith.
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         My wife and I heard this phrase a lot while my daughter was in chemo. Oddly, our unbelieving friends said it as often as believers.
        </p>
        <p>
         In hindsight, I think our unbelieving friends were appealing to faith in the abstract. Faith devoid of a source, and short on specifics.
        </p>
        <p>
         The world doesnt mind that kind of faith. No one is offended by generic faith.
        </p>
        <p>
         Have you ever heard a sermon that was preached in the abstract? For example, how about a sermon regarding sacrifice that didnt point to Calvary or Christ. Such a sermon might go something like this:
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Sacrifice is good. We like it when people make sacrifices for us. So we should make sacrifices for them. Isnt sacrifice good? Isnt it good to give of oneself to make others happy?
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         Now, expand that for a half-hour. Imagine a whole sermon that deals with sacrifice only in the abstract. Or substitute another generic concept, like love, or peace.
        </p>
        <p>
         Imagine a sermon that deals with love as an
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          airy, floaty thing, independent of Christ
         </span>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Is that the sort of sermon you want to sit through?
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          You might say no. But the worlds answer is a resounding YES!
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         You see, the world doesnt get offended about generic faith. For after all, thats what most people have. They have faith in faith, and hope in hope. And so when churches preach a generic message, they dont get in trouble. It is when we talk about
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          specifics
         </span>
         that the knives come out.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is when we talk about the particulars of the cross, or anything that invokes Jesus Christ, that trouble hits.
        </p>
        <p>
         The specifics, specifics, specifics. They are what the world hates.
        </p>
        <p>
         With that said, the Bible is not a collection of abstract essays. It is not a blase greeting card from God. It is not sanitized by a focus-group. The beauty of Scripture is in its details. Its beauty is found in the concrete, historically verifiable (and sometimes hard) truth it contains.
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          Its beauty is that of a complete, detailed blueprint of redemption, not a mere compass.
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         And so we should preach it as such.
        </p>
        <p>
         If the Bible contains specifics and nuances, it is for our benefit. Even when we dont like their implications. Created beings do themselves no good when they discount, or bury, any part of the Creators revelation. God is revealed in every iota, on every page of His Word. His self-revelation is not generic, but carefully transmitted, to be carefully studied, and prayerfully understood.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          <b>
           Thank God that we have such specifics. And shame on a world that wants something less.
          </b>
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Toby B. Holt is a Teaching Elder in the Presbyterian Church in America.
         </em>
        </p>
