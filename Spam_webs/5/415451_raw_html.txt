     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Brighton and Rottingdean Seashore Electric Railway
      </b>
      was a unique coastline
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">
       railway
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">
       Brighton
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      that ran through the shallow coastal waters of the
      <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">
       English Channel
      </a>
      between 1896 and 1901.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_construction">
       Background and construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brighton_and_Rottingdean_Seashore_Electric_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Volk" title="Magnus Volk">
       Magnus Volk
      </a>
      , its owner, designer and engineer, had already been successful with the more conventional
      <a href="/wiki/Volk%27s_Electric_Railway" title="Volk's Electric Railway">
       Volk's Electric Railway
      </a>
      , which had then not been extended east of Paston Place. Facing unfavourable geography, Volk decided to construct a line through the surf from a pier at Paston Place to one at
      <a href="/wiki/Rottingdean" title="Rottingdean">
       Rottingdean
      </a>
      . This was also home to Volk's Seaplane Station which was used by his son
      <a href="/wiki/George_Herbert_Volk" title="George Herbert Volk">
       George Herbert Volk
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The railway itself consisted of two parallel
      <span class="nowrap">
       2ft
       <span class="frac">
        8
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </span>
       in
      </span>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       825mm
      </span>
      ) gauge tracks, billed as 18ft (5.5m) gauge, the measurement between the outermost rails.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The tracks were laid on concrete sleepers mortised into the bedrock. The single car used on the railway was a 45 by 22ft (13.7 by 6.7m) pier-like building which stood on four 23ft (7.0m)-long legs. The car weighed 45
      <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">
       long tons
      </a>
      (50
      <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">
       short tons
      </a>
      ; 46
      <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">
       t
      </a>
      ). Propulsion was by electric motor. It was officially named
      <i>
       Pioneer
      </i>
      , but many called it
      <i>
       Daddy Long-Legs
      </i>
      . Due to regulations then in place, a qualified sea captain was on board at all times, and the car was provided with lifeboats and other safety measures.
     </p>
     <p>
      Construction took two years from 1894 to 1896. The railway officially opened 28 November 1896, but was nearly destroyed by a storm the night of 4 December. Volk immediately set to rebuilding the railway including the
      <i>
       Pioneer
      </i>
      , which had been knocked on its side, and it reopened in July 1897.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_use">
       In use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brighton_and_Rottingdean_Seashore_Electric_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The railway was popular, but faced difficulties. The car was slowed considerably at high tide, but Volk could never afford to improve the motors. In 1900,
      <a href="/wiki/Groyne" title="Groyne">
       groynes
      </a>
      built near the railway were found to have led to underwater scouring under the sleepers and the railway was closed for two months while this was repaired. Immediately afterward, the council decided to build a beach protection barrier, which unfortunately required Volk to divert his line around the barrier. Without funds to do so, Volk closed the railway.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1901 the right-of-way was broken up for construction of the barrier. One further attempt was made to raise money for a conventional over-water viaduct along roughly the same route.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brighton_and_Rottingdean_Seashore_Electric_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="1024" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg/220px-VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg/330px-VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg/440px-VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VolkDaddyLongLegsModel.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Volk's prototype model of "Pioneer", in the
        <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Toy_and_Model_Museum" title="Brighton Toy and Model Museum">
         Brighton Toy and Model Museum
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The track, car and other structures were sold for scrap, but some of the concrete sleepers can still be viewed at low tide. Eventually Volk's Electric Railway was extended onshore, covering a portion of the same distance; it remains in operation.
     </p>
     <p>
      A model of the railway car is on display (along with a poster for the railway) in the foyer of the
      <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Toy_and_Model_Museum" title="Brighton Toy and Model Museum">
       Brighton Toy and Model Museum
      </a>
      .
     </p>
