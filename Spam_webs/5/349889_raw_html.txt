     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       depression
      </b>
      is a sustained, long-term downturn in economic activity in one or more economies. It is a more severe
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_downturn" title="Economic downturn">
       downturn
      </a>
      than an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_recession" title="Economic recession">
       economic recession
      </a>
      , which is a slowdown in economic activity over the course of a normal
      <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">
       business cycle
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A depression is an unusual and extreme form of recession. Depressions are characterized by their length, by abnormally large increases in
      <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">
       unemployment
      </a>
      , falls in the availability of
      <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">
       credit
      </a>
      (often due to some form of banking or financial crisis), shrinking output as buyers dry up and suppliers cut back on production and investment, large number of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bankruptcies" title="Bankruptcies">
       bankruptcies
      </a>
      including sovereign debt defaults, significantly reduced amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">
       trade
      </a>
      and commerce (especially international trade), as well as highly volatile relative currency value fluctuations (often due to currency
      <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">
       devaluations
      </a>
      ). Price
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deflation_(economics)" title="Deflation (economics)">
       deflation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis" title="Financial crisis">
       financial crises
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bank_failure" title="Bank failure">
       bank failures
      </a>
      are also common elements of a depression that do not normally occur during a recession.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
       Definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Economic_Research" title="National Bureau of Economic Research">
       National Bureau of Economic Research
      </a>
      determines contractions and expansions in the business cycle, but does not declare depressions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nber.org_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nber.org-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Generally, periods labeled depressions are marked by a substantial and sustained shortfall of the ability to purchase goods relative to the amount that could be produced using current resources and technology (
      <a href="/wiki/Potential_output" title="Potential output">
       potential output
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another proposed definition of
      <i>
       depression
      </i>
      includes two general rules:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       a decline in real GDP exceeding 10%, or
      </li>
      <li>
       a recession lasting 2 or more years.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      There are also differences in the duration of depression across definitions. Some economists refer only to the period when economic activity is declining. The more common use, however, also encompasses the time until economic activity has returned close to normal levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nber.org_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nber.org-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A recession is briefly defined as a period of declining economic activity spread across the economy (according to NBER). Under the first definition, each depression will always coincide with a recession, since the difference between a depression and a recession is the severity of the fall in economic activity. In other words, each depression is always a recession, sharing the same starting and ending dates and having the same duration.
     </p>
     <p>
      Under the second definition, depressions and recessions will always be distinct events however, having the same starting dates. This definition of depression implies that a recession and a depression will have different ending dates and thus distinct durations. Under this definition, the length of a depression will always be longer than that of the recession starting the same date.
     </p>
     <p>
      A useful example is the difference in the chronology of the Great Depression in the U.S. under the view of alternative definitions. Using the second definition of depression, most economists refer to the Great Depression, as the period between 1929 and 1941. On the other hand, using the first definition, the depression that started in August 1929 lasted until March 1933. Note that NBER, which publishes the recession (instead of depression) dates for the U.S. economy, has identified two recessions during that period. The first between August 1929 and March 1933 and the second starting in May 1937 and ending in June 1938.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
       Terminology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Today the term "depression" is most often associated with the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
      of the 1930s, but the term had been in use long before then. Indeed, an early major American economic crisis, the
      <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1819" title="Panic of 1819">
       Panic of 1819
      </a>
      , was described by then-president
      <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe" title="James Monroe">
       James Monroe
      </a>
      as "a depression",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hnn_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hnn-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the economic crisis immediately preceding the 1930s depression, the
      <a href="/wiki/Depression_of_1920%E2%80%9321" title="Depression of 192021">
       Depression of 192021
      </a>
      , was referred to as a "depression" by president
      <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">
       Calvin Coolidge
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      However, in the 19th and early 20th centuries,
      <i>
       financial
      </i>
      crises were traditionally referred to as "panics", e.g., the 'major'
      <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1907" title="Panic of 1907">
       Panic of 1907
      </a>
      , and the 'minor'
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panic_of_1910%E2%80%931911" title="Panic of 19101911">
       Panic of 19101911
      </a>
      , though the 1929 crisis was more commonly called "The Crash", and the term "panic" has since fallen out of use. At the time of the Great Depression (of the 1930s), the phrase "The Great Depression" had already been used to refer to the period 187396 (in the United Kingdom), or more narrowly 187379 (in the United States), which has since been renamed the
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Depression" title="Long Depression">
       Long Depression
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Common use of the phrase "The Great Depression" for the 1930s crisis is most frequently attributed to British economist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lionel_Robbins" title="Lionel Robbins">
       Lionel Robbins
      </a>
      , whose 1934 book
      <i>
       The Great Depression
      </i>
      is credited with 'formalizing' the phrase,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hnn_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hnn-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though US president
      <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">
       Herbert Hoover
      </a>
      is widely credited with having 'popularized' the term/phrase,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hnn_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hnn-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      informally referring to the downturn as a "depression", with such uses as "Economic depression cannot be cured by legislative action or executive pronouncement", (December 1930, Message to Congress) and "I need not recount to you that the world is passing through a great depression" (1931).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
       Occurrence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Due to the lack of an agreed definition and the strong negative associations, the characterization of any period as a "depression" is contentious. The term was frequently used for regional crises from the early 19th century until the 1930s, and for the more widespread crises of the 1870s and 1930s, but economic crises since 1945 have generally been referred to as "recessions", with the 1970s global crisis referred to as "
      <a href="/wiki/Stagflation" title="Stagflation">
       stagflation
      </a>
      ", but not a depression. The only two eras commonly referred to at the current time as "depressions" are the 1870s and 1930s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krug2010_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-krug2010-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To some degree this is simply a stylistic change, similar to the decline in the use of "panic" to refer to financial crises, but it does also reflect that the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_cycle" title="Economic cycle">
       economic cycle
      </a>
       both in the United States and in most OECD countries  though not in all  has been more moderate since 1945.
     </p>
     <p>
      There
      <i>
       have
      </i>
      been many periods of prolonged economic underperformance in particular countries/regions since 1945, detailed below, but terming these as "depressions" is controversial. The current economic cycle, which has comprised the most significant global crisis since the Great Depression, has at times been termed a depression,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krug2010_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-krug2010-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but this terminology is not widely used, with the episode instead being referred to by other terms, such as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">
       Great Recession
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_depressions">
       Notable depressions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notable depressions">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Depression">
       Great Depression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Great Depression">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The best-known depression was the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
      , which affected most national economies in the world throughout the 1930s. This depression is generally considered to have begun with the
      <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">
       Wall Street Crash of 1929
      </a>
      , and the crisis quickly spread to other national economies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Between 1929 and 1933, the
      <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_product" title="Gross national product">
       gross national product
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      decreased by 33% while the rate of
      <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">
       unemployment
      </a>
      increased to 25% (with industrial unemployment alone rising to approximately 35%  U.S. employment was still over 25% agricultural).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The probable causes of the Great Depression include the loose money policies of the Federal Reserve during the latter 1920s and the consequent misallocation of capital based on easy and inexpensive credit,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      although this is still hotly debated.
     </p>
     <p>
      A long-term effect of the Great Depression was the departure of every major currency from the
      <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">
       gold standard
      </a>
      , although the initial impetus for this was World War II (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_Accord" title="Bretton Woods Accord">
       Bretton Woods Accord
      </a>
      ). In any case, the world economy has simply outgrown the capacity of additions to the world gold supply to accommodate the increase in world population and increased trade without periodic, painful revaluations of any currencies tied to gold.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Long_Depression">
       Long Depression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Long Depression">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      For more details on this topic, see
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Depression" title="Long Depression">
       Long Depression
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2156" data-file-width="3290" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg/220px-Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg/330px-Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg/440px-Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tompkins_square_riot_1874.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        New York police violently attacking unemployed workers in
        <a href="/wiki/Tompkins_Square_Riot_(1874)" title="Tompkins Square Riot (1874)">

        , 1874.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Starting with the adoption of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">
       gold standard
      </a>
      in Britain and the United States, the
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Depression" title="Long Depression">
       Long Depression
      </a>
      (18731896) was indeed longer than what is now referred to as the Great Depression, but shallower. However, it was known as "the Great Depression" until the 1930s.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Panic_of_1837">
       Panic of 1837
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Panic of 1837">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1837" title="Panic of 1837">
       Panic of 1837
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Panic of 1837 was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis" title="Financial crisis">
       financial crisis
      </a>
      , built on a
      <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">
       speculative
      </a>
      real estate market.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-politonomist.com_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-politonomist.com-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The bubble burst on May 10, 1837 in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      , when every
      <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">
       bank
      </a>
      stopped payment in
      <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
       gold
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
       silver
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">
       coinage
      </a>
      . The Panic was followed by a five-year depression,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-politonomist.com_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-politonomist.com-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with the failure of
      <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">
       banks
      </a>
      and record high unemployment levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasner_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Glasner-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_Depression">
       Greek Depression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Greek Depression">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_sovereign-debt_crisis" title="European sovereign-debt crisis">
       European sovereign-debt crisis
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Beginning in 2009, Greece sank into a recession that, after two years,
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_government-debt_crisis" title="Greek government-debt crisis">
       became a depression
      </a>
      . The country saw an almost 20% drop in economic output, and unemployment soared to near 25%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Greece's high amounts of sovereign debt precipitated the crisis, and the poor performance of its economy since the introduction of severe austerity measures has slowed the entire eurozone's recovery. Greece's continuing troubles have led to discussions about
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_withdrawal_from_the_eurozone" title="Greek withdrawal from the eurozone">
       its departure from the eurozone
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_depressions">
       Other depressions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other depressions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Global">
       Global
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">
       1973 oil crisis
      </a>
      , coupled with the rising costs of maintenance of
      <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">
       welfare state
      </a>
      in most countries led to a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1973-75_recession" title="1973-75 recession">
       recession between 1973 and 1975
      </a>
      , followed by a period of
      <a href="/wiki/Stagflation" title="Stagflation">
       almost minimal growth and rising inflation and unemployment
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Early_1980s_recession" title="Early 1980s recession">
       1980-82 recession
      </a>
      marked the end of the period.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Savings_and_loan_crisis" title="Savings and loan crisis">
       savings &amp; loans
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Leveraged_buyout" title="Leveraged buyout">
       leveraged buyout
      </a>
      crises led to a severe depression in mid-to-late 1989, causing a
      <a href="/wiki/Early_1990s_recession" title="Early 1990s recession">
       recession in 1990-91
      </a>
      (also fueled by
      <a href="/wiki/1990_oil_price_shock" title="1990 oil price shock">
       the oil price crisis
      </a>
      ), whose effects lasted as late as 1994. This downturn is more remembered for its political effects:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Prime_Minister" title="British Prime Minister">
       British Prime Minister
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">
       Margaret Thatcher
      </a>
      had to resign in November 1990 as a result of the socioeconomic debacle caused by her later policies; and while his approval ratings were above 60%,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">
       U.S. President
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
       George H. W. Bush
      </a>
      lost the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1992" title="United States presidential election, 1992">
       1992 election
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_J._Clinton" title="Bill J. Clinton">
       Bill J. Clinton
      </a>
      because of the domestic malady marked by the depression and increasing urban decay.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2005, the
      <a href="/wiki/2000s_energy_crisis" title="2000s energy crisis">
       persistent oil price rises
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_overheating" title="Economic overheating">
       economic overheating
      </a>
      caused by deregulation led to a gradual deterioration of the world economy with inflation and unemployment rising as growth slowed down: The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Housing_bubble" title="Housing bubble">
       housing bubble
      </a>
      in the U.S. burst in 2007, and the American economy slipped into a
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">
       recession
      </a>
      . This in turn provoked the failure of many prominent financial institutions throughout 2008, most notably
      <a href="/wiki/Lehman_Brothers" title="Lehman Brothers">
       Lehman Brothers
      </a>
      , leading to the loss of millions of jobs. The bailouts made by many governments to save banks also led to an increase on the national deficits.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional">
       Regional
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depression_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regional">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Several Latin American countries had severe downturns in the 1980s: by the Kehoe and Prescott definition of a great depression as at least one year with output 20% below trend,
      <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Argentina" title="Economy of Argentina">
       Argentina
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Brazil#Stagnation.2C_inflation.2C_and_crisis.2C_1981-94" title="Economic history of Brazil">
       Brazil
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Chile" title="Economy of Chile">
       Chile
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Mexico#1982_crisis_and_recovery" title="Economic history of Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      experienced great depressions in the 1980s, and Argentina experienced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentine_economic_crisis_(1999-2002)" title="Argentine economic crisis (1999-2002)">
       another
      </a>
      in 19982002. Latin American countries fell once again into this in the early-to-mid 2010s, with the economies of Argentina, Venezuela and Brazil slipping into recession as a result of falling prices on commodities such as in the 1980s.
     </p>
     <p>
      This definition also includes the economic performance of
      <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_New_Zealand" title="Economy of New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      from 19741992 and
      <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Switzerland" title="Economy of Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      from 1973 to the present, although this designation for Switzerland has been controversial.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Over the period 19802000, Sub-Saharan Africa broadly suffered a fall in absolute income levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kick_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kick-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Communism">
       Post-Communism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      was almost twice as intense as the Great Depression in the countries of
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">
       Western Europe
      </a>
      and the United States in the 1930s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russia_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Russia-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Average
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">
       standards of living
      </a>
      registered a catastrophic fall in the early 1990s in many parts of the former
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">
       Eastern Bloc
      </a>
      - most notably, in
      <a href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_states" title="Post-Soviet states">
       post-Soviet states
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even before Russia's
      <a href="/wiki/1998_Russian_financial_crisis" title="1998 Russian financial crisis">
       financial crisis
      </a>
      of 1998,
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      's GDP was half of what it had been in the early 1990s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russia_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Russia-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some populations are still poorer today than they were in 1989 (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">
       Moldova
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
       Serbia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Central_Asia" title="Soviet Central Asia">
       Central Asia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">
       Caucasus
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

       <i>

         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">

         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The collapse of the Soviet planned economy and the
      <a href="/wiki/Shock_therapy_(economics)" title="Shock therapy (economics)">
       transition to market economy
      </a>
      resulted in catastrophic declines in GDP of about 45% during the 19901996 period
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">

      <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Finland" title="Economy of Finland">

      </a>
      around the breakup of the Soviet Union (19891994) as a great depression; this is partly attributed to the breakup of the Soviet Union, and partly to the Scandinavian banking crisis, which was also suffered, to a lesser degree, by Sweden and Norway.
     </p>
