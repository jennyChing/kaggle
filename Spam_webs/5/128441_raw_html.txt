        <font color="#666600">
         <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial Narrow" size="4">
          <strong>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            Equipment
           </font>
          </strong>
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </font>
        <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          The 
        few items you need to brew your own homemade beer can be found at your nearest 
        beermaking supply shop, or at most hardware stores.
          <br/>
          - One 10-gallon "food grade" plastic pail with lid. Cost: about 
        $12.00
          <br/>
          - Siphon hose. You'll need a 74" length of 5/16" "food grade" 
      vinyl tubing. Cost: $2.00
          <br/>
          - Hose clamp for siphon. Cost: $1.00
          <br/>
          - Twelve 2- liter plastic pop bottles, with lids.
         </b>
        </font>

           <br/>
           - Large pot, or turkey roaster.
          </font>
         </b>

        <font color="#666600">
         <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial Narrow" size="4">
          <strong>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            Ingredients
           </font>
          </strong>
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           <br/>
           <font color="#FFFFF0">
            -
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>
         <font color="#339966" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           Malt 
        Extract.
          </b>
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           One 40oz. can of any flavor you like ( light, dark, stout), or a 1.5kg "tall" 
        can of same. The 1.5kg can contains more malt extract so you can make a larger 
        batch or use the same method here to make a richer beer.
           <font color="#666600">
            <b>
             You can also buy 'pre-hopped' extract
            </b>
           </font>
           which will impart more of 
          a hop flavor to your beer.
           <br/>
           <font color="#FFFFF0">
            -
           </font>
          </b>
          <font color="#339966">
           <b>
            Yeast.
           </b>
          </font>
          <b>
           1 tsp brewers' yeast. Note: some malt comes with little packets of yeast included.
           <br/>
           <font color="#FFFFF0">
            -
           </font>
          </b>
          <font color="#339966">
           <b>
            Sugar
           </b>
          </font>
          <b>
           <font color="#339966">
            .
           </font>
           6 - 7 cups of regular white sugar, or 8 - 9 cups of corn sugar (preferred).
           <br/>
           <br/>
           For even better results, consider using two cans of the malt extract and not 
        using any sugar. This adds to the expense, but further enriches the taste 
        of the beer.
          </b>
         </font>
        </font>
        <p>
         <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           These 
        ingredients should cost between $10 - 15 depending on your choice of malt 
        extract. The yield will be about 23 liters of beer, which equals 65 - 70 bottles 
        or cans of beer, of the regular 345ml size.
          </b>
         </font>
         <font color="#666600" size="2">
          <b>
           <font color="#666600" size="2">

       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="10" height="33" valign="top">
        <b>
         <font color="#669966" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="4">
          How 
      to brew
         </font>
        </b>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" height="640" rowspan="2" valign="top" width="38">
       </td>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="9" height="402" valign="top">
        <font color="#666600">
         <font color="#666600">
          <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <strong>
            Sanitize
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <font color="#666600">
            <b>
             It has been said that 75% of brewing is good sanitation. 
        First, clean all equipment with warm, lightly soapy water. Rinse well to remove 
        soap residue. Then sanitize using household bleach at a quantity of 1 tbsp/gallon 
        of water. Or you can purchase
             <font color="#666600">
              <font color="#666600">
               <strong>
                a 
          no-rinse acid sanitizer
               </strong>
              </font>
             </font>
             <strong>
              such as StarSan, which 
      is effective and leaves no aftertaste.
             </strong>
            </b>
           </font>
           <strong>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <b>
             Brew
            </b>
           </strong>
          </font>
         </font>
        </font>
        <strong>
         <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           <br/>
           1.
          </b>
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           <font color="#666600">
            Pour 10 liters of 
                  fresh, cold water into the 10 gallon plastic pail (carboy). If the pail is 
                  new, wash it out first with a mixture of water and baking soda to remove the 
                  plastic smell.
           </font>
           <br/>
          </b>
          <font color="#FF6600">
           <b>
            2.
           </b>
          </font>
          <b>
           <font color="#666600">
            In your largest pot, bring 7 liters of water to a boil.
            <br/>
           </font>
          </b>
          <font color="#FF6600">
           <b>
            3.
           </b>
          </font>
          <b>
           <font color="#666600">
            Add one can of malt extract. 
                        Stir and cook uncovered for 20 minutes.
           </font>
           <br/>
           <font color="#FF6600">
            4.
           </font>
           <font color="#666600">
            Add the sugar and stir to dissolve.
           </font>
           <br/>
           <font color="#FF6600">
            5.
           </font>
           <font color="#666600">
            As soon as the sugar is dissolved, pour contents 
                          into the carboy. Pour, or 'splash', the contents quickly, which adds air to 
                          the mixture. The more air the yeast get initially, the better. It allows them 
                          to rapidly grow and get things going.
           </font>
           <br/>
           <font color="#FF6600">
            6.
           </font>
           <font color="#666600">
            Top up with bottled drinking water or tapwater 
                          until temperature is neutral. (If using tapwater, it is recommended to boil 
                          first to kill bacteria
           </font>
          </b>
          <b>
           <font color="#666600">
            .) Test using a clean, 
                            sanitized thermometer. The carboy will now be a little more than half full.
           </font>
           <br/>
           <font color="#FF6600">
            7.
           </font>
           <font color="#666600">
            Sprinkle in the yeast, and stir well. Cover 
                              with lid.
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>
         <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           (Set 
                                lid on loosely; if capped too tightly, a carboy can explode from the carbon 
                                dioxide gas that is produced.)
          </b>
         </font>
        </strong>
        <strong>
         <p>
          <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            Keep covered 
                                    and avoid unnecessary opening. The beer will be ready to bottle in 6- 10 days, 
                                    depending on ambient temperature of the room and amount of sugar used in the 
                                    brewing.
            <b>
             Room temperature should be 20-24 Celsius at the highest;
             <b>
              16-20 
                                Celsius
             </b>
             is better but it will take the beer a day or two longer to ferment.
            </b>
           </b>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            Test for readiness 
          with a hydrometer. Set hydrometer into the beer and spin it once to release 
          bubbles which cling to it and give a false reading. The "ready to bottle" 
        reading should be about 1.008 for dark beers and 1.010-1.015 for light beers. 
        If you don't have a hydrometer, you can judge readiness by tasting a sample 
        - it should not be sweet tasting. There should be little or no bubbling action 
        in the beer.
           </b>
          </font>
         </p>
        </strong>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="9" height="234" valign="top">
        <font color="#666600">
         <strong>
          <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           Bottle
          </font>
         </strong>
        </font>
        <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          <br/>
          <font color="#666600">
           Set the carboy on a sturdy table and the 12 2-liter bottles on the floor, with 
          newspaper underneath to catch drips or overflows. Using a funnel, put 2 level 
          teaspoons of sugar in each bottle.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Siphon the beer into the bottles, trying not to disturb the sediment on the 
      bottom of the carboy. (One method is to tape a plastic straw alongside the bottom 
      end of the siphon hose with 1" projecting beyond the end. The tip of the 
          straw can touch the bottom of the carboy without the siphon drawing up sediment.) 
          Tip the carboy as you near the bottom.
           <br/>
           It is important to not splash or agitate the beer too much when bottling as 
      any oxygen introduced can lead to oxidation and a cardboard taste.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           As you fill the bottles, keep the end of the siphon tube near the bottom of 
      the bottle to avoid frothing. It is essential that the bottles ar not completely 
      filled - leave an airspace. Screw the caps on tightly. Invert each bottle and 
      shake to dissolve sugar on the bottom. Set bottles in a warm area for the first 
      few days, then store in a dark, cool spot. You can drink the beer within a few 
      days of bottling, but it will improve with age.
          </font>
         </b>
        </font>
        <font size="2">
         <b>
          <font face="Arial Narrow">
           <br/>
           <a name="c">
           </a>
          </font>
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFF98" colspan="10" height="25" valign="top">
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="10" height="33" valign="top">
        <b>
         <font color="#669966" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="4">
          Enhancements
         </font>
        </b>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="10" height="78" valign="top">
        <font color="#666600">
         <b>
          <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           Once 
      you've made a few batches of your own homemade beer, it's fun to experiment 
      with different 'enhancements'. When adding your own personal touch, however, 
      caution is always a good idea; try things in small amounts so as not to over-do 
      it.
           <br/>
           Here are few suggestions:
          </font>
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" height="162" valign="top" width="38">
       </td>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="9" height="162" valign="top">
        <li>
         <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <font color="#FF6600">
           <strong>
            Molasses.
           </strong>
          </font>
          <b>
           Add a cup or two of molasses to the malt while it's cooking. Cut back 
              a bit on the sugar. This will impart a darker, more full-bodied taste 
              to the beer. Blackstrap molasses is darker and richer; Fancy molasses 
              is lighter.
          </b>
         </font>
        </li>
        <li>
         <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <strong>
           Licorice.
          </strong>
         </font>
         <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           Throw a few sticks of licorice into the malt while it's cooking. Leave 
        in for 5 - 10 minutes, then remove any unmelted pieces before pouring 
        the brew into the carboy.
          </b>
         </font>
        </li>
        <li>
         <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <font color="#FF9900">
           <strong>
            <font color="#FF6600">
             Hops.
            </font>
           </strong>
          </font>
          <b>
           Put whole hops (dried) or hop flakes into a cheesecloth bag and add 
        to the cooking malt. A handful is plenty. This will give a distinctive 
        hop flavor to the beer.
          </b>
         </font>
        </li>
        <li>
         <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <font color="#CC9900">
           <strong>
            <font color="#FF6600">
             Herb 
        teas.
            </font>
           </strong>
          </font>
          <b>
           Try adding 3 or 4 teabags of Celestial 
          Seasonings "Bengal Spice" tea, or "Apple Cinnamon Spice" 
        tea. Add to the cooking malt and remove after 10 minutes. This will 
        add a bit of spice to the taste of the beer, a very interesting flavor.
          </b>
         </font>
        </li>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFF98" colspan="10" height="25" valign="top">
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="10" height="267" valign="top">
        <strong>
         <font color="#666600">
          <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial Narrow" size="4">
           <b>
            <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
             Enjoy!
            </font>
           </b>
          </font>
         </font>
        </strong>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            Pour your beer 
        carefully to avoid disturbing the sediment. Re-capped partially full bottles 
        will retain their "fizz" for up to two weeks, so don't feel you 
        have to empty the bottle (unless you're looking for an excuse!). After two 
        weeks, unfinished bottles should be emptied.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            You may find that batches of homemade beer can vary in quality. Darker beers will work best 
        with this recipe for the most consistent results. If your beer is a little 
        short on "fizz" or falls a little short of your expectations, try 
        mixing it 50/50 with  commercial beer.
           </b>
          </font>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <font color="#666600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            If 
        the empty bottles are rinsed out immediately, washing them later will 
        be easy. Simply rinse with warm, lightly soapy water. Rinse well to remove 
        soap residue.
            <b>
             A mild bleach solution can also be used to clean the 
          bottles.
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </b>
            Remember, 
        your beer will continue to improve for months. It's a good idea to start 
        a second batch right away so you can get well enough ahead to enjoy fully 
        matured beer.
            <b>
             <br/>
             <a name="d">

            </b>
           </b>
          </font>
         </strong>
        </p>
