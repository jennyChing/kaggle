         <p>
          <em>
           Guest blogger Adam DuVander is the author of 
           <a href="http://oreilly.com/catalog/9781593272715">
            Map Scripting 101
           </a>
           , an example-driven guide to building interactive maps on multiple platforms. He also serves as executive editor of
           <a href="http://www.programmableweb.com/">
            ProgrammableWeb
           </a>
           .
          </em>
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <p>
          Maps took over the web in mid-2005, shortly after the
          <a href="http://conferences.oreillynet.com/where2005/">
           first Where 2.0 conference
          </a>
          . They quickly moved from fancy feature to necessary element of any site that contained even a trace of geographic content. Today were amidst another location and mapping revolution, with mobile making its impact on the web. And with it, were seeing even more geo services provided by both the old guard and innovative new mapping platforms.
         </p>
         <p>
          Though the map itself continues to be important, other geographic data is having a larger impact. Providers are making this data, such as driving directions and business listings, available in increasingly open ways.
         </p>
         <h2>
          The old guard
         </h2>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div style="float: right;width: 300px;height: auto;padding: 10px;margin: 0 0 12px 12px;border: 1px solid #ddd;font-style: italic;text-align: left">
          <a href="http://maps.forum.nu/v3/gm_customTiles.html">
           <img alt="This screen is from Marcelo Montagna's Custom Tile Layers with Opacity Google Maps demo." border="0" src="http://s.radar.oreilly.com/2010/09/03/custom-tiles-example.png" style="margin: 0 0 10px 0" width="300"/>
          </a>
          <br/>
          A screenshot from Marcelo Montagnas 
          <a href="http://maps.forum.nu/v3/gm_customTiles.html">
           Custom Tile Layers with Opacity
          </a>
           Google Maps demo.
         </div>
         <p>
          Google had the first mapping API and continues to keep its lead by adding useful new features. The companys
          <a href="http://code.google.com/apis/maps/documentation/javascript/">
           Maps V3
          </a>
          was originally optimized for mobile, but in May Google made it the go-to platform for the web as well. With this move, Google showed that the mobile web is at least as important as the web we access from our homes and offices.
         </p>
         <p>
          Another sign of mobiles influence on mobile appears in how Google is making some of its newest services available. Geocoding, driving directions and business listings are no longer confined to access via JavaScript. Instead, Google has made these all available via web services, giving developers the freedom to use the results in multiple ways, such as in a native smart phone application.
         </p>
         <p>
          Yahoo has done little to expand its mapping platform in recent years, though it is almost as old as Googles. Due to Yahoos more lenient terms, its auxiliary geo services, such as geocoding and static maps, get consistent interest from developers. And the company has made improvements, such as its next-generation geocoder,
          <a href="http://developer.yahoo.com/geo/placefinder/">
           PlaceFinder
          </a>
          , which it announced in June.
         </p>
         <p>
          Yet, with Yahoos tremendous potential, the mapping platform remains untouched. Theres hope, given the
          <a href="http://conversations.nokia.com/2010/05/24/nokia-and-yahoo-team-up-in-online-services/">
           recent deal with Nokia
          </a>
          to provide maps on Yahoo proper. Both Yahoo and Nokia are mum on whether the deal will extend to Yahoos developer platform, which makes me wonder if it will leave behind an industry it helped create.
         </p>
         <p>
          MapQuest is oft-forgotten by developers, though it has made some of the largest strides with its mapping platform in the last year. A sixth version of its
          <a href="http://developer.mapquest.com/web/products/featured/javascript">
           JavaScript API
          </a>
          , written from the ground up, recently came out of beta. The new platform takes advantage of the web services that MapQuest has released. Its an attempt to make the thin client code even thinner.
         </p>
         <p>
          One of these web services,
          <a href="http://open.mapquestapi.com/directions/">
           Directions
          </a>
          , made MapQuest a leader among mapping APIs. Launched a year ago, Directions marked the first time routing was available for free without being constricted with JavaScript-only access. Most recently MapQuest made the same service available built on top of
          <a href="http://www.openstreetmap.org/">
           OpenStreetMap
          </a>
          data.
         </p>
         <h2>
          The newcomers
         </h2>
         <p>
          Bing may seem like a strange newcomer, since Microsoft has had a mapping API for some time. Previously called Virtual Earth, it was re-branded in 2009 along with the launch of Microsofts new search engine. But its not just surface-level changes. Microsoft has
          <a href="http://www.microsoft.com/maps/developers/">
           continued to launch new developer services with Bing
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          In addition to the JavaScript SDK, Bing Maps can also be created with Silverlight, which makes for smoother transitions and animation. The Bing Maps site itself runs on Silverlight, and in June Microsoft launched the ability to create
          <a href="http://www.bing.com/community/blogs/maps/archive/2010/06/07/announcing-the-bing-map-app-sdk-and-3-new-bing-map-apps.aspx">
           map apps
          </a>
          , which can run on the main Bing Maps site.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://cloudmade.com/">
           CloudMade
          </a>
          is a company built upon OpenStreetMap, the project creating a wiki-like map that anyone can edit. Using this open data, CloudMades API gives you access to the Open Street Map tiles in a way that is more reliable  and style-able  than the project itself.
         </p>
         <p>
          CloudMades Map Style Editor lets you set colors for features, such as roads and parks. Then, make your own style available for embedding using the JavaScript API. CloudMade supplies much of the same power that super-users have when making map tiles server-side in a point-and-click interface.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Where have all the hackers gone?
         </h2>
         <p>
          With so many official mapping APIs available, its easy to forget that the map mashup culture was founded upon hacking. Paul Rademacher created
          <a href="http://www.housingmaps.com/">
           HousingMaps
          </a>
          to show Craigslist rentals and homes for sale on a Google Map before Google had an API. Adrian Holovaty made
          <a href="http://chicago.everyblock.com/crime/">
           Chicago Crime
          </a>
          to show crime data (which he scraped from the police bureaus website) on a hacked and embedded Google Map.
         </p>
         <p>
          Rademacher joined Google in 2005, created the
          <a href="http://earth.google.com/plugin/">
           Google Earth plugin
          </a>
          and now is part of the team that makes Google Maps. Holovatys Chicago Crime project became part of
          <a href="http://www.everyblock.com/">
           EveryBlock
          </a>
          , a local news aggregator that
          <a href="http://blog.everyblock.com/2009/aug/17/acquisition/">
           sold to MSNBC
          </a>
          last year. Ironically, EveryBlock doesnt use any mapping API, instead opting for using its own minimalist map tiles.
         </p>
         <p>
          The mapping hackers of 2010 have also gone server-side, away from the APIs. Using tools like
          <a href="http://mapnik.org/">
           Mapnik
          </a>
          , theyre styling their own maps, almost always with OpenStreetMap data. Sometimes its for fun, like Brett Campers
          <a href="http://8bitcity.com/map?New%20York">
           8-Bit City
          </a>
          . And when an earthquake struck Haiti,
          <a href="http://en.oreilly.com/where2010/public/schedule/detail/13201">
           map hackers responded
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <h2>
          Mapping the future
         </h2>
         <p>
          Mapping providers will likely make it easier to create your own customized maps. Already Google Maps V3 has simple styling via CSS-like code. And the process of creating OpenStreetMap tiles is greatly simplified by
          <a href="http://tiledrawer.com/">
           Tile Drawer
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          But its not just making the map itself that needs simplification, but storing and accessing the data on top of it. For years developers have had to set up their own databases of locations, which raises the bar for the type of developer who can use maps. Now there are tools like
          <a href="http://simplegeo.com/">
           SimpleGeo
          </a>
          to make the process easier. However, it would be useful to see these tools baked into the mapping APIs and we likely will soon.
         </p>
         <p>
          Similarly, we need easier ways of expressing data without just adding more markers. Graphic overlays, such as
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choropleth_map">
           choropleths
          </a>
          (regions shaded based on data) and heatmaps, are not accessible to most developers. The processes need to run on a server capable of geo-referencing the graphic it outputs. And services available to do this tend to charge. The
          <a href="http://radar.oreilly.com/gov2">
           open government
          </a>
          movement is already tied closely to mapping. Hopefully projects for the greater good will fill in feature gaps where mapping providers dont see business opportunities.
         </p>
         <p>
          Obviously, mobile will play a huge role in the future of mapping. Already weve seen an impact, yet there are far fewer sites taking advantage of the users location than could. Expect the next generation of
          <a href="http://radar.oreilly.com/2010/09/lets-pull-it-together.html">
           store locators
          </a>
          , for example, to be much more exciting. But thats just the beginning.
         </p>
