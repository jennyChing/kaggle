     <div class="story-header-left twoCol">
      <h1 class="title" itemprop="name">
       Homegrown brands lead India's mobile revolution
      </h1>
      <div class="source" itemprop="author" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
       <span itemprop="name">
        Gauri Bhatia, special to CNBC.com
       </span>
      </div>
      <time class="datestamp" datetime="2014-06-25T19:06:02-0400" itemprop="datePublished">
       Wednesday, 25 Jun 2014 |  7:06  PM ET
      </time>
      <span class="sourcelogo" id="sourcelogo-cnbccom" itemprop="sourceOrganization">
       CNBC.com
      </span>
     </div>
     <div class="story-header-right">
      <div>
       <div class="share-count-container hide">
        <div class="share-total">
        </div>
        <div class="share-text">
         SHARES
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="gigyasharebar" id="social_bar_top">
       </div>
      </div>
      <a data-comments-label="Article" data-disqus-identifier="cnbcnewsstory_101712474" href="#">
      </a>
     </div>
    </div>
    <section class="cols2">
     <div class="unit col1">
      <article>
       <div class="story">
        <div class="content" id="article_body">
         <div class="group-container ">
          <div class="embed-container image" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
           <img alt="A packaged Micromax Informatics Ltd. smartphone is displayed at a wholesale smartphone outlet at Gaffar Market in New Delhi, India" height="298" src="http://fm.cnbc.com/applications/cnbc.com/resources/img/editorial/2014/05/28/101712493-166357483.530x298.jpg?v=1401335338" width="530"/>
           <meta content="http://fm.cnbc.com/applications/cnbc.com/resources/img/editorial/2014/05/28/101712493-166357483.jpg?v=1401335338" itemprop="image"/>
           <meta content="width='530'" itemprop="width"/>
           <meta content="height='298'" itemprop="height"/>
           <meta content="2014-05-28T23:48:58-0400" itemprop="datePublished"/>
           <meta content="2014-05-28T23:47:50-0400" itemprop="dateCreated"/>
           <meta content="2014-05-28T23:48:58-0400" itemprop="dateModified"/>
           <meta content="2014-05-28T23:47:50-0400" itemprop="uploadDate"/>
           <meta content="2099-12-31" itemprop="expires"/>
           <!-- TODO: defaulting to 2099 until we get the expiry date from toolbelt -->
           <meta content="jpg" itemprop="encodingFormat"/>
           <meta content="Prashanth Vishwanathan | Bloomberg | Getty Images" itemprop="author"/>
           <meta content="CNBC" itemprop="publisher"/>
           <meta content="A packaged Micromax Informatics Ltd. smartphone is displayed at a wholesale smartphone outlet at Gaffar Market in New Delhi, India" itemprop="caption"/>
           <div class="attribution">
            Prashanth Vishwanathan | Bloomberg | Getty Images
           </div>
           <div class="caption">
            A packaged Micromax Informatics Ltd. smartphone is displayed at a wholesale smartphone outlet at Gaffar Market in New Delhi, India
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="group" itemprop="articleBody">
           <p>
            India's homegrown mobile phone brands are giving global heavyweights a run for their money as they mop up market share in the country's $13 billion mobile phone market.
           </p>
           <p>
            Until five years ago there wasn't a single recognizable local brand in India's handset market. However, cut-throat pricing, aggressive marketing and localization changed that; today the top-10 space is dominated by local newcomers including
            <strong>
             Micromax
            </strong>
            ,
            <strong>
             Karbonn
            </strong>
            and
            <strong>
             Lava
            </strong>
            , which together account for over 30 percent of the smartphone market. Micromax's 17.5 percent share is second only to
            <a class="inline_quotes" data-gdsid="10007514" data-inline-quote-symbol="593-KR" href="http://data.cnbc.com/quotes/593-KR" target="_self">
             Samsung
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             "With handsets manufactured in China, these guys started a mobile revolution in India," said Neil Shah, director at technology research firm Counterpoint.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span class="label-read-more">
             Read More
            </span>
            <a href="/2014/05/19/narendra-modi-election-the-prosand-cons-of-indias-stock-market.html" target="_self">
             India stocks are hot-the question is for how long
             <br/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             Local players began gaining traction in 2008-2009 when the smartphone revolution reached China, leading China's feature phone manufacturers to search for new markets. India, which was undergoing a telecom revolution, seemed like a perfect market as policy changes reduced call tariffs and removed import barriers.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             India's handset industry, among the world's fastest growing, jumped 20 percent to 226 million units in 2013 according to Counterpoint. It's the second-largest handset market globally after China.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            "The time was ripe for regional brands," said Ankur Bisen, senior vice president at consulting firm
            <strong>
             Technopak
            </strong>
            , and Indian entrepreneurs gained market share by offering phones at discounts of over 30 percent to the lowest-priced foreign brand.
           </p>
           <p>
            <span class="label-read-more">
             Read More
            </span>
            <a href="/2014/05/28/can-indias-new-finance-minister-deliver.html" target="_self">
             Can India's new finance minister deliver?
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             "We are not interested in the tip of the pyramid, but the base. 90 percent of phones selling in India are selling at less than 5,000 rupees (around $85), if [major international] brands want to ignore that [segment] it's their problem, we cannot," Shashin Devsare, executive director at Karbonn Mobiles told CNBC.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             For Lava, the handset market was an "exciting space" when the company began in 2009.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             "We decided to leverage the China ecosystem and took a bottom-up approach. Our research showed that no one was servicing the tier-three and tier-four markets," SN Rai, one of Lava's founders, told CNBC.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span class="label-read-more">
             Read More
            </span>
            <a href="/2014/05/21/under-modi-can-indias-economy-take-on-china.html" target="_self">
             Under Modi, can India's economy take on China?
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             These homegrown brands quickly climbed the value chain and within a few years started selling smart phones, creating a category called "affordable premium". According to Counterpoint's Shah, most Indian smartphones sell for 4,000 to 20,000 rupees ($70 to $340), about $100 less than the iPhone or Galaxy; some even boast similar features.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             Indian players were also quick to customize their phones to customers' needs, investing in research and development, design and software design centers to develop new apps.
            </span>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="group-container last">
          <div class="player embed-container cnbcvideo">
           <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="player" data-src="http://player.cnbc.com/cnbc_global?byGuid=3000248809&amp;endCardGuids=3000383239|3000383217|3000383353" frameborder="0" height="298" id="player_1_0" mozallowfullscreen="" msallowfullscreen="" oallowfullscreen="" scrolling="no" webkitallowfullscreen="" width="533">
            <p>
             This is where future smartphone growth lies...
            </p>
            <p>
             As China sees an ease in demand, India is where future growth will be for the smartphone industry, according to a new report. CNBC's Julia Wood reports.
            </p>
           </iframe>
          </div>
          <div class="group" itemprop="articleBody">
           <p>
            For instance, the
            <strong>
             Firstouch
            </strong>
            phone which hit the market in May allows users to translate English text to an Indian language and vice versa. It also comes with a keyboard designed for Indian alphabets.
           </p>
           <p>
            "Only 10 percent of the Indian population knows Englishso we are targeting those who love their own language and are not comfortable with English," said Firstouch co-founder Rakesh Deshmukh.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Nuisance value
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            While these Indian players are focused on the low-end market, they still spend big on advertising and marketing. From hiring big-name brand ambassadors to sponsoring big-ticket cricket tournaments, they have created "nuisance value."
           </p>
           <p>
            <span class="label-read-more">
             Read More
            </span>
            <a href="/2014/05/23/american-businesses-chase-big-markets-in-india.html" target="_self">
             US businesses chase India's billion-plus consumers
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            "We were very bold in our marketing spending more than 20 percent of revenue initially. We were audacious and [as a result] had people asking 'what is Karbonn?'," said Karbonn's Devsare.
           </p>
           <p>
            Karbonn is not alone. Last October, Micromax signed Hollywood star Hugh Jackman as its brand ambassador. Meanwhile, Lava has become the official sponsors of English football club Liverpool in India.
           </p>
           <p>
            Their marketing push also aims to capture foreign markets. Some Indian players have reached Russia and the Middle East and aim to break into Europe and the U.S.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Survival of the fittest
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            While Indian brands have managed to woo consumers in such a small span of time  with the leader in the pack Micromax clocking sales of over a billion dollars in the fiscal year ended March 2014  the smaller of the 80-odd players, those with annual revenues of less than $200 million,  could fall by the way side in the next three to four years. They need to grow at a feverish pace, even at 100 percent annually, to survive, say experts.
           </p>
           <p>
            <span class="label-read-more">
             Read More
            </span>
            <a href="/2014/05/25/are-emerging-market-equities-finally-catching-up.html" target="_self">
             Are emerging market equities finally catching up?
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            If not, they may have to opt out of the handset market and look to sell smart watches instead, say industry watchers.
           </p>
           <p>
            But even for the established brands, foreign competition is hotting up, with international players like
            <a class="inline_quotes" data-gdsid="2463" data-inline-quote-symbol="992-HK" href="http://data.cnbc.com/quotes/992-HK" target="_self">
             Lenovo
            </a>
            and
            <a class="inline_quotes" data-gdsid="2220" data-inline-quote-symbol="6657-KR" href="http://data.cnbc.com/quotes/6657-KR" target="_self">
             LG
            </a>
            launching phones at very competitive price points, threatening the very unique selling proposition that has made these local players so successful, say experts.
           </p>
           <p>
            "This [new international launches] could slow down the growth of domestic brands and the growing middle class with disposable income would go after an international brand rather than local brand unless brands like Micromax prove themselves on a global stage and become a global brand, " said Shah.
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
