     <p>
      A
      <b>
       one-hit wonder
      </b>
      is a person or act known mainly for only a single success.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term is most often used to describe music performers with only one Top 40
      <a href="/wiki/Hit_single" title="Hit single">
       hit single
      </a>
      or for having one
      <a href="/wiki/Signature_song" title="Signature song">
       signature song
      </a>
      which overshadows their other work.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the term is used as well to describe other, related phenomena such as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_company" title="Software company">
       software company
      </a>
      which only has one widely successful release, or for an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsperson" title="Sportsperson">
       athlete
      </a>
      , known for only one major career event.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music_industry">
       Music industry
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-hit_wonder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Music industry">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">
        Billboard
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine journalist Wayne Jancik's book
      <i>
       The Billboard Book of One-Hit Wonders
      </i>
      (published in 1997 and covering the period from 1955, the start of the rock and roll era, to 1992) defines a one-hit wonder objectively as "an act that has won a position on [the] national, pop, Top 40 just once."
     </p>
     <p>
      However, this formal definition can therefore include acts with greater success outside their lone pop hit (and who are thus not typically considered one-hit wonders), while at the same time excluding acts who have multiple hits which have been overshadowed by one
      <a href="/wiki/Signature_song" title="Signature song">
       signature song
      </a>
      , or those performers who never actually hit the top 40, but, had exactly one song achieve mainstream popularity in some other fashion (and who are thus considered one-hit wonders). One-hit wonders are usually relative to a given market, either a country or sometimes a genre; a performer may be a one-hit wonder in one but have multiple hits (or no hits) in another.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="VH1.27s_list_of_.22100_greatest_one-hit_wonders.22">
       VH1's list of "100 greatest one-hit wonders"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-hit_wonder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: VH1's list of &quot;100 greatest one-hit wonders&quot;">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2002, the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      cable network
      <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">
       VH1
      </a>
      aired a countdown of the VH1's 100 Greatest One-hit Wonders, hosted by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Shatner" title="William Shatner">
       William Shatner
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [

     </p>
     <p>
      It listed musicians with only one American hit, regardless of international success, which has been substantial and long-lived for musicians like
      <a href="/wiki/A-ha" title="A-ha">
       A-ha
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nena" title="Nena">
       Nena
      </a>
      (see below). (Under Jancik's criteria, A-ha,
      <a href="/wiki/Falco_(musician)" title="Falco (musician)">
       Falco
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_Ice" title="Vanilla Ice">
       Vanilla Ice
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gerardo_Mej%C3%ADa" title="Gerardo Meja">
       Gerardo
      </a>
      would not qualify for the list, as all four had additional hits in the top 20 outside their signature hits; they are nonetheless commonly considered one-hit wonders because those other hits did not survive in
      <a href="/wiki/Recurrent_rotation" title="Recurrent rotation">
       recurrent rotation
      </a>
      ). Los del Ro likewise had two Top 40 hits, though both were versions of "Macarena."
     </p>
     <p>
      The countdown also omitted acts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">
       Jimi Hendrix
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">
       Grateful Dead
      </a>
      who, while technically charting with only one single, became too well known for their entire bodies of work to merit inclusion on the list.
     </p>

       Classical music one-hit wonders
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-hit_wonder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classical music one-hit wonders">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Classical composers such as
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Pachelbel" title="Johann Pachelbel">
       Johann Pachelbel

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pachelbel%27s_Canon" title="Pachelbel's Canon">
        Pachelbel's Canon
       </a>
      </i>

      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vox_Records" title="Vox Records">
       Vox Records
      </a>
      have both released albums of classical one-hit wonders.      <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_music">
       Outside music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-hit_wonder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Outside music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       one-hit wonder
      </i>
      is occasionally applied to other media.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       one-hit wonder
      </i>
      is often applied to either highly potent specific varieties of substances, such as certain strains of
      <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">
       cannabis
      </a>
      that require only one "hit" (a single inhalation of smoke), or a "hit" of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">
       LSD
      </a>
      (a single dose), to achieve the desired
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoactive" title="Psychoactive">
       psychoactive
      </a>
      effects, or a person with a significantly low tolerance to such drugs that it only takes a single "hit" to achieve desired effects.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_sports">
       In sports
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-hit_wonder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In sports">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the sports world, there are several athletes known to casual sports fans for one event in their careers. Examples include
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Mazeroski" title="Bill Mazeroski">
       Bill Mazeroski
      </a>
      , who is the only player in
      <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">
       Major League Baseball
      </a>
      history to end a seventh game of the
      <a href="/wiki/1960_World_Series" title="1960 World Series">
       World Series
      </a>
      with a
      <a href="/wiki/Walk-off_home_run" title="Walk-off home run">
       walk-off home run
      </a>
      (however, Mazeroski is a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum">
       Baseball Hall of Fame
      </a>
      , due primarily to his status as one of the greatest defensive
      <a href="/wiki/Infielder" title="Infielder">
       infielders
      </a>
      of all time);
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Henderson" title="Paul Henderson">
       Paul Henderson
      </a>
      , a Canadian
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">
       ice hockey
      </a>
      player who scored the deciding goal in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1972_Summit_Series" title="1972 Summit Series">
       1972 Summit Series
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Jones_(linebacker)" title="Mike Jones (linebacker)">
       Mike Jones
      </a>
      , who tackled
      <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Dyson" title="Kevin Dyson">
       Kevin Dyson
      </a>
      at the one-yard line on
      <a href="/wiki/Final_play_of_Super_Bowl_XXXIV" title="Final play of Super Bowl XXXIV">
       the final play
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXXIV" title="Super Bowl XXXIV">
       Super Bowl XXXIV
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/David_Tyree" title="David Tyree">
       David Tyree
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Wide_receiver" title="Wide receiver">
       wide receiver
      </a>
      who became famous for a
      <a href="/wiki/Helmet_Catch" title="Helmet Catch">
       helmet-assisted catch
      </a>
      during the waning moments of
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLII" title="Super Bowl XLII">
       Super Bowl XLII
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Timmy_Smith" title="Timmy Smith">
       Timmy Smith
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rypien" title="Mark Rypien">
       Mark Rypien
      </a>
      , both
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">
       Washington Redskins
      </a>
      stars that ended up out of football shortly after winning Super Bowls
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXII" title="Super Bowl XXII">
       XXII
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXVI" title="Super Bowl XXVI">
       XXVI
      </a>
      respectively;
      <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">
       NASCAR
      </a>
      driver
      <a href="/wiki/Derrike_Cope" title="Derrike Cope">
       Derrike Cope
      </a>
      , who won the 1990
      <a href="/wiki/Daytona_500" title="Daytona 500">
       Daytona 500
      </a>
      in a surprise victory after
      <a href="/wiki/Dale_Earnhardt" title="Dale Earnhardt">
       Dale Earnhardt
      </a>
      blew a tire on the last lap;
      <a href="/wiki/Buster_Douglas" title="Buster Douglas">
       Buster Douglas
      </a>
      , who was the first boxer to ever knock
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Tyson" title="Mike Tyson">
       Mike Tyson
      </a>
      out; and
      <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Glass" title="Jimmy Glass">
       Jimmy Glass
      </a>
      , an
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       English
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">
       football
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Goalkeeper_(association_football)" title="Goalkeeper (association football)">
       goalkeeper
      </a>
      , who is remembered for scoring a goal in the last seconds of the final day of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998-99_in_English_football" title="1998-99 in English football">
       1998-99
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division" title="Football League Third Division">
       English Third Division
      </a>
      that kept
      <a href="/wiki/Carlisle_United_F.C." title="Carlisle United F.C.">
       his club
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/The_Football_League" title="The Football League">
       The Football League
      </a>
      . His subsequently released biography was titled
      <i>
       One-Hit Wonder
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Some athletes have become remembered for a single mistake in their careers as opposed to a triumphant moment. Notable examples of such athletes include
      <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">
       Buffalo Bills
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Placekicker" title="Placekicker">
       placekicker
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Scott_Norwood" title="Scott Norwood">
       Scott Norwood
      </a>
      , who is infamous for
      <a href="/wiki/Wide_Right_(Buffalo_Bills)" title="Wide Right (Buffalo Bills)">
       a missed 47-yard field goal
      </a>
      that cost his team the
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXV" title="Super Bowl XXV">
       Super Bowl
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Buckner" title="Bill Buckner">
       Bill Buckner
      </a>
      , who at the
      <a href="/wiki/1986_World_Series" title="1986 World Series">
       1986 World Series
      </a>
      made a fielding error in which a baseball rolled through his legs; and
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Marshall_(American_football)" title="Jim Marshall (American football)">
       Jim Marshall
      </a>
      , who, as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Vikings" title="Minnesota Vikings">
       Minnesota Vikings
      </a>
      , ran 66 yards (mistakenly into his own end zone) and scored a safety for the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_49ers" title="San Francisco 49ers">
       San Francisco 49ers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In tennis, the term "one-slam wonder" can be referred towards players who have either won only one Grand Slam singles title during his or her career, or players who have currently only won one Grand Slam singles title but have the potential to win even more in their careers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Andy_Roddick" title="Andy Roddick">
       Andy Roddick
      </a>
      is said to have enjoyed a successful tennis career, despite winning only one Grand Slam singles title  the
      <a href="/wiki/2003_US_Open_(tennis)" title="2003 US Open (tennis)">
       2003 US Open
      </a>
       in his entire career.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other players who won only one Grand Slam singles title in their entire career include
      <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Moy%C3%A1" title="Carlos Moy">
       Carlos Moy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petr_Korda" title="Petr Korda">
       Petr Korda
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gast%C3%B3n_Gaudio" title="Gastn Gaudio">
       Gastn Gaudio
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Johansson" title="Thomas Johansson">
       Thomas Johansson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Costa" title="Albert Costa">
       Albert Costa
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_Ferrero" title="Juan Carlos Ferrero">
       Juan Carlos Ferrero
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Anastasia_Myskina" title="Anastasia Myskina">
       Anastasia Myskina
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gabriela_Sabatini" title="Gabriela Sabatini">
       Gabriela Sabatini
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jana_Novotn%C3%A1" title="Jana Novotn">
       Jana Novotn
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Chang" title="Michael Chang">
       Michael Chang
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term "
      <a href="/wiki/Cup_of_coffee" title="Cup of coffee">
       cup of coffee
      </a>
      " is used to describe a baseball or ice hockey player who has only a short stint (i.e., long enough to drink a cup of coffee and not do much else) in
      <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">
       Major League Baseball
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">
       National Hockey League
      </a>
      respectively and then spend the rest of their careers in the
      <a href="/wiki/Minor_league" title="Minor league">
       minor leagues
      </a>
      .
     </p>
