     <p>
      A
      <b>
       polydactyl cat
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">
       cat
      </a>
      with a
      <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">
       congenital physical anomaly
      </a>
      called
      <a href="/wiki/Polydactyly" title="Polydactyly">
       polydactyly
      </a>
      (or polydactylism, also known as hyperdactyly), that causes the cat to be born with more than the usual number of toes on one or more of its
      <a href="/wiki/Paw" title="Paw">
       paws
      </a>
      . Cats with this genetically inherited trait are most commonly found along the East Coast of
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      (in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      ) and in
      <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">
       South West England
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
       Wales
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
       Occurrence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Polydactyl_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The true polydactyly is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital" title="Congenital">
       congenital
      </a>
      abnormality,
      <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">
       genetically
      </a>
      inherited as an
      <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">
       autosomal
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominance_relationship" title="Dominance relationship">
       dominant
      </a>
      trait of the ZRS cis element of the Pd gene with incomplete
      <a href="/wiki/Penetrance" title="Penetrance">
       penetrance
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lettice_978.E2.80.93985_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lettice_978.E2.80.93985-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Howietoes.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="720" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Howietoes.jpg/220px-Howietoes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Howietoes.jpg/330px-Howietoes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Howietoes.jpg/440px-Howietoes.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Howietoes.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Kitten with 23 toes
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Normal cats have a total of 18 toes, with five toes on each front paw and four toes on each hind paw; polydactyl cats may have as many as eight digits on their front and/or hind paws. Jake, a Canadian polydactyl cat with 28 toes, was recognised by
      <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">
       Guinness World Records
      </a>
      as having the highest number of toes on a cat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Various combinations of anywhere from four to seven toes per paw are common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lange_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lange-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Polydactyly is most commonly found on the front paws only, it is rare for a cat to have polydactyl hind paws only, and polydactyly of all four paws is even less common.
     </p>
     <p>
      The nickname "double-pawed cat" is a misnomer since there is a specific double paw condition, although this condition may be interrelated with polydactyly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-messybeast_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-messybeast-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Feline radial hypoplasia
      <i>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Squitten" title="Squitten">
        squitten
       </a>
       )
      </i>
      is a mimic of polydactyly and is considered a severe condition. Radial hypoplasia may cause the formation of extra jointed toes, but it is not a result of the gene normally associated with polydactyls. It thus does not cause the "mitten cat" or "thumb cat" condition where the extra toes occur separated from the normal ones just like a
      <a href="/wiki/Dewclaw" title="Dewclaw">
       dewclaw
      </a>
      , usually associated with an additional pad which makes them look like an underdeveloped foot sticking out near the base of the normal toes. Rather, radial hypoplasia-related extra toes are immediately adjacent to the normal ones, giving the cat overly large, flat feet colloquially known as "
      <a href="/wiki/Patty" title="Patty">
       patty
      </a>
      feet" or "hamburger feet". Though this looks less serious than true polydactyly (as the feet appear "normal" apart from having one or two extra toes), breeding such cats will eventually result in severely crippled offspring. Cats used in polydactyl breeding programs can be screened by x-ray for indicators of radial hypoplasia, and cats suspected to have radial hypoplasia should not be used for breeding.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_folklore">
       History and folklore
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Polydactyl_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and folklore">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The condition seems to be most commonly found in cats along the East Coast of
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      (in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      ) and in
      <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">
       South West England
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
       Wales
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingston-upon-Hull" title="Kingston-upon-Hull">
       Kingston-upon-Hull
      </a>
      . Polydactyl cats have been extremely popular as
      <a href="/wiki/Ship%27s_cat" title="Ship's cat">
       ship's cats
      </a>
      . Although there is some controversy over whether the most common variant of the trait originated as a mutation in
      <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
       New England
      </a>
      or was brought there from
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       Britain
      </a>
      , there seems to be agreement that it spread widely as a result of cats carried on ships originating in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">
       Boston, Massachusetts
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Prevalence" title="Prevalence">
       prevalence
      </a>
      of polydactyly among the cat population of various ports correlates with the dates when they first established trade with Boston.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-messybeast_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-messybeast-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-straightdope-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Contributing to the spread of polydactyl cats by this means,
      <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">
       sailors
      </a>
      were long known to value polydactyl cats especially for their extraordinary climbing and hunting abilities as an aid in controlling shipboard
      <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">
       rodents
      </a>
      . Some sailors also considered them to be extremely
      <a href="/wiki/Luck" title="Luck">
       good luck
      </a>
      when at sea.
     </p>
     <p>
      Genetic work studying the DNA basis of the condition however indicates that many different mutations can all lead to polydactyly
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lettice_978.E2.80.93985_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lettice_978.E2.80.93985-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and since samples from the UK and USA were shown to possess different mutations it seems likely that these cats have originated multiple times independently, rather than spreading from a single origin.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">
       Nobel Prize
      </a>
      -winning author
      <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">
       Ernest Hemingway
      </a>
      was a famous
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aficionado#English" title="wiktionary:aficionado">
       aficionado
      </a>
      of polydactyl cats, after being first given a six-toed cat by a ship's captain. Upon Hemingway's death in 1961, his
      <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway_House" title="Ernest Hemingway House">
       former home
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">
       Key West, Florida
      </a>
      , became a museum and a home for his cats, and it currently houses approximately fifty descendants of his cats (about half of which are polydactyl). Because of his love for these animals, polydactyl cats are sometimes referred to as "Hemingway Cats".
     </p>
     <p>
      Some sources state that these cats are rare in Europe because they were killed as witches'
      <a href="/wiki/Familiar_spirit" title="Familiar spirit">
       familiars
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-straightdope_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-straightdope-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but other sources indicate that they are quite common in southern Britain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-messybeast_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-messybeast-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">
       Naming
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Polydactyl_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nicknames for polydactyl cats include "
      <i>
       conch cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       boxing cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       mitten cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       mitten-foot cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       snowshoe cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       thumb cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       six-fingered cats
      </i>
      ", "
      <i>
       Cardi-cats
      </i>
      ", and "
      <i>
       Hemingway cats
      </i>
      ". Two specific breeds recognized by some but not all cat fancier clubs are the American Polydactyl and
      <a href="/wiki/Maine_Coon" title="Maine Coon">
       Maine Coon
      </a>
      Polydactyl, and named regional populations include the Boston thumb cat, Cardi-cat, Ithacat, and Vermont snowshoe cat.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">
       Breeding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Polydactyl_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      American Polydactyl cats are bred as a specific cat breed, with specific physical and behavioral characteristics in addition to extra digits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-centralpets_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-centralpets-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The American Polydactyl is not to be confused with the pedigree
      <a href="/wiki/Maine_Coon" title="Maine Coon">
       Maine Coon
      </a>
      polydactyl. The polydactyl form of the Maine Coon is being reinstated by some breeders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poly_mc_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-poly_mc-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A particular strain of polydactyl cats native to
      <a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">
       Ithaca, New York
      </a>
      is known as the Ithacat. Polydactyls are very common in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardigan,_Wales" title="Cardigan, Wales">
       Cardigan
      </a>
      area of Wales, where they are known as "Cardi-cats."
     </p>

     <p>
      The database PolyTrak comprises 1800 Maine Coons in total (as of July 2014). Out of them about 1200 are polydactyl.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PolyTrak_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PolyTrak-8">

        </span>

        <span>

      The PolyTrak database service is a repository of information for the Maine Coon polydactyl cat. Details on numbers of toes are given as well as of catteries, sires and dames. Physiological characteristics of each polydactyl individual are also stored. There is no medical injury mentioned of any polydactyl Maine Coon individual. Thus PolyTrak could help to confirm on a broad empirical base, that the polydactyl Maine Coon (Hemingway mutant) has no negative healthy effects at all.

     <p>
      In the case of preaxial polydactyly of the Maine Coon cat (Hemingway mutant) a mutation of the cis-regulatory element ZRS (ZPA regulator sequence) is associated. ZRS is a noncoding element, 800.000 basepairs remote to the target gene Shh. An
      <a href="/wiki/Ectopia_(medicine)" title="Ectopia (medicine)">
       ectopic
      </a>
      expression of Shh is seen on the anterior side of the limb. Normally Shh is expressed in an organiser region, called the
      <a href="/wiki/Zone_of_polarizing_activity" title="Zone of polarizing activity">
       zone of polarizing activity
      </a>
      (ZPA) on the posterior limb side. From there it diffuses anteriorly, laterally to the growth direction of the limb. In the mutant mirroring smaller ectopic expression in a new organiser region is seen on the posterior side of the limb. This ectopic expression causes cell proliferation delivering the raw material for one or more new digits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lettice-2003_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lettice-2003-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lettice-2008_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lettice-2008-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lettice_2012_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lettice_2012-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the study of genetic causes of polydactyly limb patterning models are used to simulate the
      <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">
       congenital disorder
      </a>
      at the limb, being able to explain the development paths of polydactyly.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biased_polyphenism_of_a_spontaneous_complex_variation">
       Biased polyphenism of a spontaneous complex variation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Polydactyl_cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biased polyphenism of a spontaneous complex variation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Polydactyly is a spontaneous complex phenotypic variation, developed in one generation. In the concrete preaxial form of the Hemingway mutant the variation is induced by a single point mutation in a noncoding cis-regulatory element for Shh. In an extensive phenotypic variation like this, one or more complete digits at each single limb are developed including nerves, blood vessels, muscles and ligaments. The physiology of the digits can be perfect. This complex phenotypic result cannot be explained by the mutation alone. The mutation can only induce the variation. In the consequence of the mutation thousands of events, each different from the wildtype, occur on different organisation layers, i.e. expression changes of other genes, cell-cell signal exchange, cell differentiation, cell and tissue growth. The summarized small random changes on all layers build the raw material and the process steps for the generation of the plastic variation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lange_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lange-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The mentioned form of polydactyly of the Hemingway mutant shows a biased variation. In a recent empirical study first the number of extra toes of 375 mutant
      <a href="/wiki/Maine_Coon" title="Maine Coon">
       Maine Coon
      </a>
      cats were variable (
      <a href="/wiki/Polyphenism" title="Polyphenism">
       polyphenism
      </a>
      ) and second, the number of extra toes followed a discontinuous statistical distribution. They were not equally distributed as one might expect of an identical single point mutation. The example demonstrates, that the variation is not explained completely by the mutation alone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lange_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lange-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Maine Coon (wildtype: 18 toes) in some cases exhibits polydactyly with 18 toes, were the first toe at the forehand is elongated forming a three phalangeal thumb. More often 20 toes appear, and in diminishing order we see 22, 24 or 26 toes per individual. Also odd numbers of toes are seen.
     </p>
