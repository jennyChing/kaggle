      <b>
       Eyjafjallajkull
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
       Icelandic
      </a>
      pronunciation
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Icelandic" title="Help:IPA for Icelandic">
        [ejafjatlajktl]
       </a>
      </span>
      <small class="nowrap metadata">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/File:Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull-bjarmason.ogg" title="File:Eyjafjallajkull-bjarmason.ogg">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/>
       </a>
       <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull-bjarmason.ogg" title="Eyjafjallajkull-bjarmason.ogg">
        listen
       </a>
       )
      </small>
      ), English
      <b>
       Eyjafjalla Glacier
      </b>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is one of the smaller
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">
       ice caps
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
       Iceland
      </a>
      , situated to the north of
      <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B3gar" title="Skgar">
       Skgar
      </a>
      and to the west of
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="Mrdalsjkull">
       Mrdalsjkull
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">
       ice cap
      </a>
      covers the
      <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">
       caldera
      </a>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
       volcano
      </a>
      with a summit elevation of 1,651 metres (5,417ft). The volcano has erupted relatively frequently since the
      <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">
       last glacial period
      </a>
      , most recently
      <a href="/wiki/2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajkull">
       in 2010
      </a>
      .

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
       Geography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Eyjafjallajkull consists of a volcano completely covered by an ice cap. The ice cap covers an area of about 100 square kilometres (39sqmi), feeding many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outlet_glacier" title="Outlet glacier">
       outlet glaciers
      </a>
      . The main outlet glaciers are to the north; Ggjkull, flowing into Lni, and Steinholtsjkull, flowing into Steinholtsln.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vefur1_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vefur1-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1967 there was a massive landslide on the Steinholtsjkull glacial tongue. On 16 January 1967 at 13:47:55 (or 1:47:55 PM) there was an explosion on the glacier. It can be timed because the seismometers in Kirkjubjarklaustur monitored the movement. When about 15,000,000 cubic metres (529,720,001 cubic feet) of material hit the glacier a massive amount of air, ice, and water began to move from under the glacier out into the lagoon at the foot of the glacier.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vefur1_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-vefur1-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The mountain itself, a
      <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">
       stratovolcano
      </a>
      , stands 1,651 metres (5,417ft) at its highest point, and has a
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">
       crater
      </a>
      34 kilometres (1.92.5mi) in diameter, open to the north. The crater rim has three main peaks, being (clockwise from the north-east) Gunasteinn, 1,500 metres (4,900ft) (approx), Hmundur, 1,651 metres (5,417ft) and Goasteinn, 1,497 metres (4,911ft). The south face of the mountain was once part of Iceland's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">
       Atlantic
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline">
       coastline
      </a>
      , from which, over thousands of years, the sea has retreated some 5 kilometres (3.1mi). The former coastline now consists of sheer cliffs with many
      <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">
       waterfalls
      </a>
      , of which the best known is
      <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B3gafoss" title="Skgafoss">
       Skgafoss
      </a>
      . In strong winds, the water of the smaller falls can even be blown up the mountain. The area between the mountain and the present coast is a relatively flat strand, 2 to 5km (1 to 3 miles) wide, called Eyjafjll.
     </p>
     <p>
      The volcano is fed by a
      <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
       magma chamber
      </a>
      under the mountain, which in turn derives from the
      <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
       tectonic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">
       divergence
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">
       Mid-Atlantic Ridge
      </a>
      . It is part of a chain of volcanoes stretching across Iceland. Its nearest active neighbours are
      <a href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">
       Katla
      </a>
      , to the northeast, and
      <a href="/wiki/Eldfell" title="Eldfell">
       Eldfell
      </a>
      , on
      <a href="/wiki/Heimaey" title="Heimaey">
       Heimaey
      </a>
      , to the southwest. The volcano is thought to be related to Katla geologically, in that eruptions of Eyjafjallajkull have generally been followed by eruptions of Katla.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iceland_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_from_S%C3%B3lheimaj%C3%B6kull.JPG">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iceland_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_from_S%C3%B3lheimaj%C3%B6kull.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Eyjafjallajkull in March 2006, viewed from a recreation area on the Slheimajkull, a glacier on the
        <a href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">
         Katla
        </a>
        volcano
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Eyjafjallajkull volcano erupted in 920, 1612 and again from 1821 to 1823 when it caused a
      <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake_outburst_flood" title="Glacial lake outburst flood">
       glacial lake outburst flood
      </a>
      (or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kulhlaup" title="Jkulhlaup">
        jkulhlaup
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It
      <a href="/wiki/2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajkull">
       erupted three times in 2010
      </a>
      on 20 March, AprilMay, and June. The March event forced a brief evacuation of around 500 local people,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but the 14 April eruption was ten to twenty times more powerful and caused
      <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallajkull eruption">
       substantial disruption to air traffic
      </a>
      across
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      . It caused the cancellation of thousands of flights across Europe and to Iceland.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The name means "
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
       glacier
      </a>
      " (or more properly here "
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">
       ice cap
      </a>
      ") of the Eyjafjll. The word
      <i>
       jkull
      </i>
      , meaning glacier or ice cap, is a
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognate
      </a>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
       Middle English
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       ikel
      </i>
      surviving in the
      <i>
       -icle
      </i>
      of English
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/icicle" title="wikt:icicle">
        icicle
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eyjafjll
      </i>
      is the name given to the southern side of the volcanic massif together with the small mountains which form the foot of the volcano.
     </p>
     <p>
      The name
      <i>
       Eyjafjll
      </i>
      is made up of the words
      <i>
       eyja
      </i>
      (genitive plural of
      <i>
       ey
      </i>
      , meaning
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eyot" title="wikt:eyot">
        eyot
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/island" title="wikt:island">
        island
       </a>
      </i>
      ), and the plural word
      <i>
       fjll
      </i>
      , meaning
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fell#Etymology_3" title="wikt:fell">
        fells
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mountain" title="wikt:mountain">
        mountains
       </a>
      </i>
      ), and together literally means: "the mountains of the islands". The name probably refers to the close-by archipelago of
      <a href="/wiki/Vestmannaeyjar" title="Vestmannaeyjar">
       Vestmannaeyjar
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       fjalla
      </i>
      is the genitive plural of
      <i>
       fjll
      </i>
      , and so
      <i>
       Eyjafjalla
      </i>
      is the genitive form of
      <i>
       Eyjafjll
      </i>
      and means: "of the Eyjafjll". A literal part-by-part translation of
      <i>
       Eyjafjallajkull
      </i>
      would thus be "Islands' Mountains' ice cap".
     </p>
     <p>
      Eyjafjallajkull is sometimes referred to by the
      <a href="/wiki/Numeronym" title="Numeronym">
       numeronym
      </a>
      "E15".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">
       Geology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="726" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg/220px-Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg/330px-Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg/440px-Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volcanic_system_of_Iceland-Map-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Active volcanic areas and systems in Iceland
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">
       stratovolcano
      </a>
      , whose vents follow an east-west trend, is composed of
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       basalt
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">
       andesite
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
       lavas
      </a>
      . Most of its historical eruptions have been
      <a href="/wiki/Explosive_eruption" title="Explosive eruption">
       explosive
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However,
      <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">
       fissure vents
      </a>
      occur on both (mainly the west) sides of the volcano.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1821_to_1823_eruptions">
       1821 to 1823 eruptions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1821 to 1823 eruptions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Some damage was caused by a minor eruption in 1821.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larsen_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-larsen-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Notably, the ash released from the eruption contained a large fraction of
      <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">
       fluoride
      </a>
      , which in high doses may harm the bone structure of cattle, horses, sheep and humans. The eruption also caused some small and medium
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacier_run" title="Glacier run">
       glacier runs
      </a>
      and flooding in nearby rivers
      <a href="/wiki/Markarflj%C3%B3t" title="Markarfljt">
       Markarfljt
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Holts%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Holts (page does not exist)">
       Holts
      </a>
      . The eruptive phase started on 19 and 20 December 1821 by a series of explosive eruptions and continued over the next several days. The sources describe heavy
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ash_fall" title="Ash fall">
       ash fall
      </a>
      in the area around the volcano, especially to the south and west.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larsen_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-larsen-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After that event the sequence of eruptions continued on a more subdued level until June 1822.
     </p>
     <p>
      From the end of June until the beginning of August 1822, another sequence of explosive eruptions followed. The eruption columns were shot to considerable heights, with ashfall in both the far north of the country, in
      <a href="/wiki/Eyjafj%C3%B6r%C3%B0ur" title="Eyjafjrur">
       Eyjafjrur
      </a>
      , and in the southwest, on the peninsula of
      <a href="/wiki/Seltjarnarnes" title="Seltjarnarnes">
       Seltjarnarnes
      </a>
      near
      <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavk">
       Reykjavk
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The period from August to December 1822 seemed quieter, but farmers attributed the death of cattle and sheep in the Eyjafjrur area to poisoning from this eruption, which modern analysis identifies as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluoride_poisoning" title="Fluoride poisoning">
       fluoride poisoning
      </a>
      . Some small glacier runs occurred in the river Holts. A bigger one flooded the plains near the river Markarfljt. The sources do not indicate the exact date.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1823, some men went hiking up on Eyjafjallajkull to inspect the
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">
       craters
      </a>
      . They discovered a
      <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">
       fissure vent
      </a>
      near the summit
      <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">
       caldera
      </a>
      a bit to the west of
      <i>
       Gunasteinn
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In early 1823, the nearby volcano
      <a href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">
       Katla
      </a>
      under the
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="Mrdalsjkull">
       Mrdalsjkull
      </a>
      ice cap erupted and at the same time steam columns were seen on the summit of Eyjafjallajkull.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ash of Eyjafjallajkulls 1821 eruptions is to be found all over the south of Iceland. It is dark grey in colour, small-grained and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermediate_rock" title="Intermediate rock">
       intermediate rock
      </a>
      containing about 2840%
      <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">
       silicon dioxide
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallajkull eruption">

     <p>
      In April 2010, almost three thousand small
      <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
       earthquakes
      </a>
      were detected near the volcano, all having a depth of 710 kilometres (4.36.2mi).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 26 February 2010, unusual seismic activity along with rapid expansion of the Earth's crust was registered by the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meteorological_Institute_of_Iceland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meteorological Institute of Iceland (page does not exist)">
       Meteorological Institute of Iceland
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This gave
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysicist" title="Geophysicist">
       geophysicists
      </a>
      evidence that magma was pouring from underneath the crust into the
      <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
       magma chamber
      </a>
      of the Eyjafjallajkull volcano and that pressure stemming from the process caused the huge crustal displacement at orvaldseyri farm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The seismic activity continued to increase and from 35 March, close to 3,000 earthquakes were measured at the epicenter of the volcano.
     </p>
     <p>
      The eruption is thought to have begun on 20 March 2010, about 8 kilometres (5.0mi) east of the top crater of the volcano, on
      <a href="/wiki/Fimmv%C3%B6r%C3%B0uh%C3%A1ls" title="Fimmvruhls">
       Fimmvruhls
      </a>
      , the high neck between Eyjafjallajkull and the neighbouring icecap, Mrdalsjkull. This first eruption, in the form of a
      <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">
       fissure vent
      </a>
      , did not occur under the glacier and was smaller in scale than had been expected by some geologists. The fissure opened on the north side of Fimmvruhls, directly across the popular hiking trail between Skgar, south of the pass, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3rsm%C3%B6rk" title="rsmrk">
       rsmrk
      </a>
      , immediately to the north.
     </p>

     <p>
      On 14 April 2010 Eyjafjallajkull resumed erupting after a brief pause, this time from the top crater in the centre of the glacier, causing
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kulhlaup" title="Jkulhlaup">
        jkulhlaup
       </a>
      </i>
      (also known as
      <a href="/wiki/Meltwater" title="Meltwater">
       meltwater
      </a>
      floods) to rush down the nearby rivers, and requiring 800 people to be evacuated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC8621581_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC8621581-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This eruption was explosive in nature, due to melt water getting into the volcanic vent. It was estimated to be ten to twenty times larger than the previous one in
      <a href="/wiki/Fimmv%C3%B6r%C3%B0uh%C3%A1ls" title="Fimmvruhls">
       Fimmvruhls
      </a>
      . This second eruption threw volcanic ash several kilometres up in the atmosphere which led to
      <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallajkull eruption">
       air travel disruption
      </a>
      in northwest Europe for six days from 15 April to 21 April 2010 and also in May 2010,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2011)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      including the closure of
      <a href="/wiki/Airspace" title="Airspace">
       airspace
      </a>
      over many parts of Europe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The eruptions also created
      <a href="/wiki/Dirty_thunderstorm" title="Dirty thunderstorm">
       electrical storms
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 23 May 2010, the London Volcanic Ash Advisory Commission declared the eruption to have stopped, but stated that they were continuing to monitor the volcano.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The volcano continued to have several earthquakes daily, with
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanologist" title="Volcanologist">
       Volcanologists
      </a>
      watching the volcano closely.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en.vedur.is_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-en.vedur.is-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of August 2010, Eyjafjallajkull was considered dormant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_Katla">
       Relationship to Katla
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship to Katla">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="917" data-file-width="2133" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png/400px-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png/600px-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png/800px-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_en_Katla.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Cross section through Eyjafjll and Katla
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Eyjafjallajkull lies 25km (16mi) west of another subglacial volcano,
      <a href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" title="Katla volcano">
       Katla
      </a>
      , under the
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="Mrdalsjkull">
       Mrdalsjkull
      </a>
      ice cap, which is much more active and known for its powerful
      <a href="/wiki/Subglacial_eruption" title="Subglacial eruption">
       subglacial eruptions
      </a>
      and its large magma chamber.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each of the eruptions of Eyjafjallajkull in 920, 1612, and 18211823 has preceded an eruption of Katla.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icelandprepares_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-icelandprepares-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Katla has not displayed any unusual activity (such as expansion of the crust or seismic activity) during the 2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallajkull, though geologists have been concerned about the general instability of Katla since 1999. Some geophysicists in Iceland believe that the Eyjafjallajkull eruption may trigger an eruption of Katla, which would cause major flooding due to melting of glacial ice and send up massive plumes of ash.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icelandprepares_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-icelandprepares-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 20 April 2010, Icelandic President
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%93lafur_Ragnar_Gr%C3%ADmsson" title="lafur Ragnar Grmsson">
       lafur Grmsson
      </a>
      said "the time for Katla to erupt is coming close...we [Iceland] have prepared...it is high time for European governments and airline authorities all over the world to start planning for the eventual Katla eruption".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Volcanologists continue to monitor Katla, aware that any eruption from Katla following an eruption from Eyjafjallajkull has historically occurred within months of an Eyjafjallajkull eruption. The Icelandic Meteorological Office updates its website with reports of quakes both at Eyjafjallajkull and Katla.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en.vedur.is_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-en.vedur.is-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A small eruption of Katla was confirmed on 8 July 2011, after a harmonic tremor was detected. The event produced cracks on Katla's glacier and a
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kulhlaup" title="Jkulhlaup">
       jkulhlaup
      </a>
      that destroyed a bridge on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring_Road_(Iceland)" title="Ring Road (Iceland)">
       Ring Road
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
