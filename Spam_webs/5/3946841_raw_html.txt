     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2004 American
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_film" title="Romantic film">
       romantic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">
       science-fiction
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Comedy-drama" title="Comedy-drama">
       comedy-drama
      </a>
      film about an estranged couple who have erased each other from their memories, written by
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Kaufman" title="Charlie Kaufman">
       Charlie Kaufman
      </a>
      and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Gondry" title="Michel Gondry">
       Michel Gondry
      </a>
      . The film uses elements of
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">
       science fiction
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Psychological_thriller" title="Psychological thriller">
       psychological thriller
      </a>
      , and a
      <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_narrative" title="Nonlinear narrative">
       nonlinear narrative
      </a>
      to explore the nature of
      <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">
       memory
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_love" title="Romantic love">
       romantic love
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It opened in North America on March 19, 2004, and grossed over $70million worldwide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BOM-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Kaufman and Gondry wrote the story with
      <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bismuth" title="Pierre Bismuth">
       Pierre Bismuth
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">
       ensemble cast
      </a>
      includes
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Carrey" title="Jim Carrey">
       Jim Carrey
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kate_Winslet" title="Kate Winslet">
       Kate Winslet
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kirsten_Dunst" title="Kirsten Dunst">
       Kirsten Dunst
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Ruffalo" title="Mark Ruffalo">
       Mark Ruffalo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Wood" title="Elijah Wood">
       Elijah Wood
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Wilkinson_(actor)" title="Tom Wilkinson (actor)">
       Tom Wilkinson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Adams_(actress)" title="Jane Adams (actress)">
       Jane Adams
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/David_Cross" title="David Cross">
       David Cross
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The film opened to high acclaim from film critics, with much praise centering around its acting and writing. It won the
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay">
       Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay
      </a>
      and has a
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">
       cult following
      </a>
      . Winslet also received an
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">
       Academy Award nomination for Best Actress
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The title of the film is a quotation from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eloisa_to_Abelard" title="Eloisa to Abelard">
        Eloisa to Abelard
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
       Alexander Pope
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eternal_Sunshine_of_the_Spotless_Mind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The emotionally withdrawn Joel Barish and the unrestrained free-spirit Clementine Kruczynski begin a relationship on a
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Rail_Road" title="Long Island Rail Road">
       Long Island Rail Road
      </a>
      train from
      <a href="/wiki/Montauk,_New_York" title="Montauk, New York">
       Montauk, New York
      </a>
      . They are almost immediately drawn to each other despite their radically different personalities.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although they do not realize it at the time, Joel and Clementine are, in fact, former lovers, now separated after having spent two years together. After a fight, Clementine hires the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      firm Lacuna, Inc. to erase all of her memories of their relationship. Upon discovering this, Joel is devastated and decides to undergo the procedure himself, a process that takes place while he sleeps.
     </p>
     <p>
      Much of the film takes place in Joel's mind. As his memories are erased, he tries to evade the erasing effects of the procedure by hiding Clementine in remote parts of his unconscious. Joel finds himself revisiting them
      <a href="/wiki/Reverse_chronology" title="Reverse chronology">
       in reverse
      </a>
      . Upon seeing happier times of love with Clementine from earlier in their relationship, he struggles to preserve at least some memory of her and his love for her. Despite his efforts, the memories are slowly erased, with the last memory of Clementine telling him, "Meet me in Montauk."
     </p>
     <p>
      In separate but related
      <a href="/wiki/Story_arc" title="Story arc">
       story arcs
      </a>
      occurring during Joel's memory erasure, the employees of Lacuna are revealed to be more than peripheral characters. Patrick, one of the Lacuna technicians performing the erasure, is dating Clementine while viewing Joel's memories, and copying aspects of their relationship in order to seduce her. Mary, the Lacuna receptionist, is dating the other memory-erasing technician, Stan. As it turns out, Mary had an affair with Dr. Howard Mierzwiak, the married doctor who heads the companya relationship which she agreed to have erased from her memory when it was discovered by his wife. Once Mary learns this, she quits her job, confronts Stan about his knowledge of her relationship with Howard (which he denies knowing about), steals the company's records, and sends them out to all the company's clients.
     </p>
     <p>
      Joel and Clementine come upon their Lacuna records shortly after re-encountering each other on the train. They react with shock and bewilderment, given that they have no clear memory of having known each other, let alone having had a relationship and having had their memories erased. Joel beckons Clementine to start over; Clementine initially resists, pointing out it could go the same way. Joel accepts this, and they decide to attempt a relationship anyway, starting their life together anew.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
       Critical reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eternal_Sunshine_of_the_Spotless_Mind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind
      </i>
      was met with overwhelming acclaim, earning a
      <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">
       Rotten Tomatoes
      </a>
      approval rating of 93%. Winslet's and Carrey's performances were generally praised.
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
       Roger Ebert
      </a>
      commented, "Despite jumping through the deliberately disorienting hoops of its story,
      <i>
       Eternal Sunshine
      </i>
      has an emotional center, and that's what makes it work."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ebert later included the film in his "Great Movies" series.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly,
      <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">
       A. O. Scott
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      praised the film for being "cerebral, formally and conceptually complicated, dense with literary allusions and as unabashedly romantic as any movie youll ever see."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Out_(company)" title="Time Out (company)">
        Time Out
       </a>
      </i>
      summed up their review by saying, "the formidable Gondry/Kaufman/Carrey axis works marvel after marvel in expressing the bewildering beauty and existential horror of being trapped inside one's own addled mind, and in allegorising the self-preserving amnesia of a broken but hopeful heart."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006, in issue 201 of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">
        Empire
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind
      </i>
      was voted #83 in their
      <i>
       201 Greatest Movies of All Time
      </i>
      poll as voted for by readers. That same year, Winslet's performance as Clementine was included in
      <i>
       Premiere
      </i>
      magazine's 100 Greatest Performances of All Time at #81. Claudia Puig, reviewer in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">
        USA Today
       </a>
      </i>
      said about her performance that "Winslet is wonderful as a free spirit whose hair color changes along with her moods. She hasn't had such a meaty role in a while, and she plays it just right."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Carol Vernallis points out that Gondry's experience in directing
      <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">
       music videos
      </a>
      contributed to the film's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mise-en-sc%C3%A8ne" title="Mise-en-scne">
       mise-en-scne
      </a>
      and sound design. Vernallis describes some threads of the visual, aural and musical motifs throughout the film, and how some motifs can work in
      <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">
       counterpoint
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2009,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(magazine)" title="Time Out (magazine)">
        Time Out New York
       </a>
      </i>
      ranked the film as the third-best of the decade:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       In the past, both director Michel Gondry's kindergarten arts-and-crafts aesthetic and Charlie Kaufman's Mbius-striptease scripts have come off as insufferably twee and gimmicky. So why does this existential meta-rom-com always leave us teary-eyed and genuinely moved?...[T]he duo finally finds the right combination of high-concept and humanity here, taking the what-if idea of a company that lobotomizes the lovelorn into territory that's funny, painful, poetic and unsettlingly weird.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">
        Entertainment Weekly
       </a>
      </i>
      put it on its end-of-the-decade, "best-of" list, saying, "Only the bizarre and byzantine brain of Charlie Kaufman could turn this 2004 story about erasing all memories of love into one of the most romantic movies of the decade."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>

        </span>

       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>

      </sup>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paste_Magazine" title="Paste Magazine">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
