                  <h2 class="short-teaser">
                   Syfys 12 Monkeys TV series looks to the past to save a future it, itself, is dooming.
                  </h2>
                  <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                     <p>
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      will be premiering some time in the not so distant future on Syfy and
                      <a href="/tv/12-monkeys/242699/syfys-12-monkeys-series-premiere-review">
                       Michael Ahr promises it will be time well spent
                      </a>
                      . The series comes from a film made in the not so distant past,
                      <em>
                       <b>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </b>
                      </em>
                      , directed by the always timely Terry Gilliam. The cartoonist from
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        Monty Pythons Flying Circus
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      wrenched a time capsule called
                      <strong>
                       <em>
                        La Jete
                       </em>
                      </strong>
                      that director Chris Marker buried in a time tunnel in France. The combined chronometrical energy of these three temporal forces threatens to create a loop in time.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      The time loop, sometimes called the predestination paradox, the idea that you can go back in time to change to present, is probably as old as regret itself. The first caveman pulling a woolly mammoth tusk from his ass wished he could have crawled under a rock and gone back to sleep hungry that morning. All three times the
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      are uncaged, they teach the same lesson, one that already infected us by having been told.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        The Simpsons
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      taught us that you should never change anything from the past because the present will only be worse. Stephen King tried to stop the Kennedy Assassination over and over in and on
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        11/22/63
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      . It didnt work. Christopher Reeve tried to go back in time for love. Captain Kirk went back in time to kill the woman he loved. It wasnt that the beloved president was better off dead or that peace shouldnt be given a chance, but the laws of physics are cruel.
                     </p>
                     <div class="file file-image file-image-jpeg file-content-full-width" id="file-253067">
                      <div class="content">
                       <img alt="" src="http://cdn2.denofgeek.us/sites/denofgeekus/files/styles/insert_main_wide_image/public/0/67//12_monkeys_3.jpg?itok=hxChTXAC" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <p>
                      Time abhors a vacuum and fills it with the most noxious of alternatives. This series, the third telling of a warning weve been ignoring since
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        T2
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      , has a theoretical probability of taking as much of a chance with the future of mankind as the testing of the supercollider. Earth could have been sucked into a manmade black hole then. Now it is threatened by an irreversible time loop.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      A time loop is different from The Time Warp, though just as danceable. All three versions of the story of James Cole depict multiple time loops that somehow contribute to the very thing he wants to avoid, the end of humanity.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      In Gilliams
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      , James Cole is chosen to go back to a time he feels a connection to. He has a recurring memory of seeing a women in anguish over the death of a man. When Cole goes back in time to 1990, he tells budding scientist Jeffrey Goines about a virus, saying it has the potential to kill the entire human race. This gives Goines the idea, so when Cole comes back in 1996, Goines thanks Cole for giving him the inspiration to create the virus. However, Cole only knew about the virus in 1990 because he lived through it 1997.
                     </p>
                     <div class="file file-image file-image-jpeg file-content-full-width" id="file-253063">
                      <div class="content">
                       <img alt="" src="http://cdn3.denofgeek.us/sites/denofgeekus/files/styles/insert_main_wide_image/public/0/63//la-jetee-zone-books002.jpg?itok=xDHUCZwG" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <p>
                      The survivors in
                      <strong>
                       <em>
                        La Jete
                       </em>
                      </strong>
                      live underground in the Palais de Chaillot galleries. Scientists are researching time travel in order "to call past and future to the rescue of the present," but everyone they send on trips to the future become unhinged mentally. They pick the convict because he is obsessed with a memory from before the war. He noticed a woman, played by Hlne Chatelain, right before he saw a man die on the jetty at Orly Airport.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      The scientists try a few times to sling the convict into the past. Each time they cast their loop wider. The man meets the woman from his past and they begin to fall in love. The next time the convict comes into the films present, our future, the future worlds past, the scientists throw him into the future to se if he can find any help there. Geeks from tomorrow give the convict a power unit that can regenerate the pasts society.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      Having saved the present, the convict is set to be executed. People from the future get in touch with him to offer him a place in that time, but he chooses to go back in time, to the woman from his past that he was falling in love with. The people from his time period send an assassin to carry out his execution and he realizes that the person he saw die as a child was his future, future self.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      In each story, the people who propel the subject into the past cast wider and wider circles. The experimenters are playing god and take bigger and bigger risks. It even takes longer now to tell the story. Each version gets longer.
                      <strong>
                       <em>
                        La Jete
                       </em>
                      </strong>
                      has a running time of 29 minutes.
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      runs 131 minutes and Christ only knows how long the
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      Syfy series will run.
                     </p>
                     <div class="file file-image file-image-jpeg file-content-full-width" id="file-253064">
                      <div class="content">
                       <img alt="" src="http://cdn3.denofgeek.us/sites/denofgeekus/files/styles/insert_main_wide_image/public/0/64//jettee.jpg?itok=vVBjq3v6" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <p>
                      Maybe thats the point. Perhaps, this time, James Cole will save humanity without having to sacrifice his own life. There is something about ape movies that seem biblical. Maybe the drugs trigger something in my collective unconscious. Im already on record as firmly believing that King Kong died for our sins. If Kong was Christ, then James Cole is JC, back to save the world from a future that must be. It just has to be. It is preordained, predicted and foretold as if it was written in the Book of Revelations itself.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      If Kong is King of Kings, the army of the
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      could be his disciples. John Connor from the
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        Terminator
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      franchise shares initials and goals with Cole. Another JC caught in a time loop with the future in the balance. But the future can never be balanced by proxy. Ask Homer Simpson, who lost out on a perfect world with donut rainstorms in the quest for perfect toast.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      could be one of Jesus temptations, as he was waiting to die on the cross in
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        The Last Temptation of Christ
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      .
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        La Jete
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      was made in 1962. The 1995 film
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      came 33 years later. Coincidence? Probably. Gilliam was planning to make Dickens
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        A Tale of Two Cities
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      into a film when executive producer Robert Kosberg threw David and Janet Peoples script through his window.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      James Cole is a convict. He is volunteered for future duty. You get the feeling that the alternative would be much worse. Jesus was imprisoned and purged. The depictions of Coles torture in the photoplay
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        La Jete
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      are more effective on a human level than in the subsequent movies. The still image, one moment frozen in time, gives more relatable detail than anything more modern. In
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        La Jete
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      , it really looks like it would suck to be him. Sure, you can see that James Cole is in pain, but in a place like that, the world that Gilliam created, its cool just to be there.
                     </p>
                     <div class="file file-image file-image-jpeg file-content-full-width" id="file-253062">
                      <div class="content">
                       <img alt="" src="http://cdn3.denofgeek.us/sites/denofgeekus/files/styles/insert_main_wide_image/public/0/62//12-monkeys-la0006356062-19950526.jpg?itok=cngmuPIo" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <p>
                      Im not saying that Gilliams prison is anywhere near a low security tennis camp. Its a shithole. Livestock kept behind chicken wire have it easier. But there are so many distractions just because the setting is so vast. In
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        La Jete
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      , Chris Marker creates a stark and ugly place where time stands still. Every frame is a self-contained universe of encroaching claustrophobia.
                      <em>
                       <strong>
                        12 Monkeys
                       </strong>
                      </em>
                      is too vast.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      Cole is considered crazy and dangerous. He doubts his own memories. Is he who he is or the reflection of another trauma? Gilliam shook the foundation of trauma induced reality in
                      <strong>
                       <em>
                        Brazil.
                       </em>
                      </strong>
                      Terry Gilliam is immensely far-reaching in his artistic quests and is always looking at a bigger picture. Gilliam creates worlds, universes, as a singular cinematic visionary on the scale of Stanley Kubrick, Akira Kurosawa, or Orson Welles. He is an auteur.
