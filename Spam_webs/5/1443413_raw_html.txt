             Kathy Belville could go on for days about the hoarding stories shes heard over the years.
            </p>
            <p>
             And not much surprises her. As partner at the
             <a data-cms-ai="1" href="http://www.builderonline.com/local-housing-data/pacific/san-diego-carlsbad-san-marcos-ca/employment.aspx" target="_blank" title="See 15 charts and data on San Diego, CA">
              San Diego
             </a>
             -based branch of law firm Kimball, Tirey, and St. John, her office averages about three calls a day from owners asking what to do when faced with a hoarding tenant.
            </p>
            <p>
             Owners have long dealt with "pack rats"; from overflowing boxes to the sheer volume of weight pressing down on a floor, the problem is nothing new.But after its recent attention in the media via TV shows like
             <em>
              Hoarders
             </em>
             , and after becoming a mental disorder in May by the American Psychiatric Association 
             <a data-cms-ai="1" href="http://www.multifamilyexecutive.com/accessible-housing/developers-join-equal-rights-center-program-to-improve-housing-accessibility.aspx?dfpzone=people_and_organizations.commentary" target="_blank">
              thereby becoming a protected class
             </a>
              hoarding is a trending topic.
            </p>
            <p>
             Operators can easily teeter on the lines of a fair housing violation if they dont handle the problem correctly.
            </p>
            <p>
             Its something that we need to be a little bit more aware of, Belville says. It became much more clear to the general public when they started having television programs about it, when people could actually see what its like and how it affects someones life. So they can understand why a landlord has a problem [with the lifestyle].
            </p>
            <p>
             Kimball, Tirey, and St. John handles the largest amount of defense cases throughout California, and disability filings alone make up about 40 percent of the states
             <a data-cms-ai="1" href="http://www.multifamilyexecutive.com/fair-housing/top-10-cities-for-filed-fair-housing-act-complaints-042913.aspx" target="_blank">
              fair housing complaints
             </a>
             . But the cases could be avoided, so long as property managers are delicate in how they approach the situation.
            </p>
            <p>
             Our goal is not to have those cases, Belville says. Our goal is to resolve them earlier so our clients dont end up being sued by a fair housing agency.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Unit Recognition
              <br/>
             </strong>
             Recognizing a hoarder can happen either during their tenancy, or once the resident moves out. Theres not much that a manager can do in being proactive to prevent it, but early recognition will help alleviate long-term issues.
            </p>
            <p>
             When [property managers] become aware of a problem, what they can do is try to avoid there being damage or further accumulation of [items], Belville says.
            </p>
            <p>
             Evan Knupp, partner at
             <a data-cms-ai="1" href="http://www.builderonline.com/local-housing-data/great-lakes/milwaukee-waukesha-west-allis-wi/employment.aspx" target="_blank" title="See 15 charts and data on Milwaukee, WI">
              Milwaukee
             </a>
             , Wi.-based law firm Roney and Knupp, has dealt with more than one instance of evicting a hoarding tenant, and the signs are all the same. In one case, the hoarding came to his clients attention when extermination was attempted in the unit, but the exterminators could not do their job because there was so much stuff in their way. In most cases, though, not being allowed in the unit in the first place is a tell-tale sign that something is amiss.
            </p>
            <p>
             If you have a sudden
             <a data-cms-ai="1" href="http://www.multifamilyexecutive.com/property-management/bloodsucking-bed-bugs-bedevil-managers.aspx" target="_blank">
              bug infestation
             </a>
             or rodent problem in a building, or perhaps if theres a unit that youre never able to get into because [the resident] refused access, I would pay attention to those key indicators, says Shailene Casio-Smith, vice president of business development at Austin-based FirstService Residential Realty (FSRR).
            </p>
            <p>
             But unless a landlord is already aware of the health and safety issue that exists in the unit, they dont have power to force entry. Tenants should be given prior notice before a landlord does repairs or enters their unit; only if there is an emergency can owners take the legal route or call the cops to get access.
            </p>
            <p>
             If theres water pouring out of an upstairs unit and the downstairs tenant wont let you in, thats an emergency, Belville says.
            </p>
            <p>
             Once the problem is discovered, landlords can ask tenants to clean up and follow up with an inspection, with proper notice. Knupp suggests planning ahead with placing actionable items in an up-to-date lease to make sure tenants keep a reasonably tidy unit that poses no fire hazard or safety threats.
            </p>
            <p>
             The tough thing in Wisconsin is, if you cant evict someone based on non-payment of rent, you really need to have a provision in the lease that theyre violating, Knupp says. It needs to be such a substantial violation that it warrants an eviction.
            </p>
            <p>
             Jumping to an eviction is where things get tricky, though.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reasonable Accommodation
             </strong>
             <br/>
             By claiming the disability, a tenant is asking for reasonable accommodations from their community, but it has to be reasonable under the given circumstances. That reasonability is gauged by the courts on whether or not the accommodation burdens the landlords.
            </p>
            <p>
             Now the trend with fair housing advocates is to say that a landlord could be responsible to accommodate if they knew or should have known that the person was disabled and in need of an accommodation, Belville says.
            </p>
            <p>
             So while you cant give a tenant a three-day notice to move out once you discover their hoarding tendencies, depending on the state, its necessary to help them in their efforts to bring the condition of their unit up to proper safety.That can be as simple as allowing them to take the necessary steps to get help, and giving them a timeline to come up to compliance with reasonable rules. Their disability will generally allow them more clean-up time after the owners request.
            </p>
            <p>
             Its when the resident dont bring the unit up to compliance, however, or refuses to, that the owner has more leverage to evict after documenting all communication and evidence that theyve worked with the tenant to no avail.
            </p>
            <p>
             It is disruptive to their neighbors and theyre violating their lease in regards to keeping their unit habitable, Smith says.
            </p>
            <p>
             The timeline for compliance varies based on the severity in the situation. One of Belvilles recent clients in California, for instance, was concerned about a stack of paper found in and around a gas wall heater. Upon removing the paper and cleaning the area during a follow-up inspection, the tenant let the owner know that they would just continue to put more paper in the spot once they left, creating a fire hazard.
            </p>
            <p>
             So we had to give them a notice, Belville says. [Reasonable accommodation] relies upon some level of cooperation.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Quick Clean Up
              <br/>
             </strong>
             After an eviction, the clean-up of a unit that housed a hoarder can be time consuming, depending on the degree of the issue.
            </p>
            <p>
             Most units require a full paint job, full carpet cleaning or replacement, and a professional removal of everything from the apartment. More serious cases require cleaning beyond the surface areasvents need to be cleaned for air quality, and the unit needs the services of an exterminator. The landlord also has the right to bill them for the cost of clean-up and damages, if its already outlined in the lease agreement.
            </p>
            <p>
             With good planning, though, cleaning up a unit after dealing with a hoarder wont take up too much time.
            </p>
            <p>
             [Turnover] would only extend it by a day or so depending on your vendors and resources, how quickly theyre able to schedule you, Smith says.
            </p>
            <p>
             Operators should also continue to be alert with new tenants in making sure their unit stays habitable.
            </p>
            <p>
             The landlord cant be the house beautiful police making everything great, Belville says. But when there are health and safety issues, the landlord has a responsibility to other residents  to try and resolve those issues.
            </p>
            <p>
             <em>
              -Linsey Isaacs is an assistant editor with Multifamily Executive magazine. Follow her on twitter

               @LinseyI
              </a>
              to continue this conversation.
             </em>
            </p>
