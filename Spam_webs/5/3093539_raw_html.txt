        <h2>
         Five time-suckers you should give up now

        <hr/>
        <p>
         We get it: IT workers are busy. With technology touching pretty much every aspect of work, the folks in charge are finally starting get how central you are to business. But pair your newfound importance with a tight IT labor market, and youre probably juggling a lot of tasks.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         And all too often, those day-to-day tasks and IT emergencies keep you from focusing on the longer-term initiatives. I have a simple solution to your time management problems: quit your job. Actually, don't do that. But do quit the parts that are holding you back from accomplishing actual work.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1. Quit multitasking.
         </strong>
         Admit it, you feel like Superman when youre juggling multiple projects, emails and 10 other things. But
         <a href="http://psychology.about.com/od/cognitivepsychology/a/costs-of-multitasking.htm">
          your brain disagrees
         </a>
         . When you jump back and forth between tasks, youre slow to recover and frankly, dumber. Youll do your best, most efficient work when you complete one task at a time.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          2. Quit being nice.
         </strong>
         The most effective people dont let others dictate their agendas. Being a little selfish with your time will free you to accomplish long-term goals. You shouldnt hand off your more tedious tasks to your team, but you know that co-worker who always leans on you for things he should figure out? Try making yourself unavailable or slow to respondchances are hes pretty resourceful when he has to be.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Being productively selfish also means blocking your best time of day on your calendar to accomplish work. Leave your less alert times of day for meetings or routine taskssay, while youre fighting through your post-lunch food coma. (Just dont try that one when meeting with your companys CEO!)
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          3. Speaking of meetingsjust quit going to them.
         </strong>
         Do you really need to attend every meeting on your calendar, or can you just email a quick status update to the people involved? Unless your meeting is a good tool for collaborating, scrap it. If thats not possible, respectfully request that the group schedule your part of the meeting first and bow out after you're no longer needed.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          4. Quit monitoring email.
         </strong>
         That little pop-up email preview is your enemy, and letting it control your day is the biggest time management mistake you can make. Turn it off and set a couple of specific periods every day for reading and responding to email. Let people know that if something is truly right-this-second urgent, they should call or text. Freeing yourself from constant interruptions will allow you to focus and really power through work. Bonus: youll be surprised at how many issues resolve themselves during your email hiatus.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          5. Most importantly, quit working late!
         </strong>
         Its a common trap: you have too much work to do, so you stay late. However, study after study has shown that well-rested workers who take breaks accomplish more. And lets face it, during those long hours you sometimes indulge in online shopping or check your personal email. Those things can be bigger time-suckers than you think, and avoiding them can save you oodles of timemaybe even enough to go outside or eat dinner away from your computer screen.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Not sure what your time traps are? The
         <a href="https://www.rescuetime.com/">
          Rescue Time app
         </a>
         can help you identify exactly how you spend your time, helping you conquer your time-suckers. It's also quick and easy to install. Now how's that for time management?
        </p>
