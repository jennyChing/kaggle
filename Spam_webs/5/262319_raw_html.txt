     <p>
      A
      <b>
       relationship breakup
      </b>
      , often referred to simply as a
      <b>
       breakup
      </b>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is the termination of an
      <a href="/wiki/Intimate_relationship" title="Intimate relationship">
       intimate relationship
      </a>
      by any means other than death. The act is commonly termed "dumping [someone]" in slang when it is initiated by one partner.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The term is less likely to be applied to a
      <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">
       married couple
      </a>
      , where a breakup is typically called a
      <a href="/wiki/Legal_separation" title="Legal separation">
       separation
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">
       divorce
      </a>
      . When a couple engaged to be married breaks up, it is typically called a "broken engagement".
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Susie_Orbach" title="Susie Orbach">
       Susie Orbach
      </a>
      (1992) has argued that the dissolution of dating and cohabiting relationships can be as painful as or more painful than divorce because these nonmarital relationships are less socially recognized.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Models">
       Models
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breakup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Models">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological" title="Psychological">
       psychological
      </a>
      models have been proposed to explain the process of a relationship breakup, many suggesting that 'relationship dissolution occurs in stages'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stages_leading_to_a_breakup">
       Stages leading to a breakup
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breakup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stages leading to a breakup">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      L. Lee
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      proposes that there are five stages ultimately leading up to a breakup.
     </p>
     <div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         Dissatisfaction
        </b>
         one or both partners grow dissatisfied with the relationship
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Exposure
        </b>
         both partners mutually become aware of the problems in the relationship
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Negotiation
        </b>
         both partners attempt to negotiate a solution to problems
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Resolution and transformation
        </b>
         both partners apply the outcome of their negotiation
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Termination
        </b>
         proposed resolution fails to rectify issues and no further solutions are accepted or applied
       </li>
      </ol>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_of_a_breakup">
       Cycle of a breakup
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breakup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cycle of a breakup">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Steve Duck outlines a six-stage cycle of relationship breakup,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      including
     </p>
     <div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         Dissatisfaction
        </b>
        with the relationship
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Social withdrawal
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Discussion
        </b>
        of the reasons for the discontentment
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Going public
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Tidying up
        </b>
        the memories
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Recreating
        </b>
        sense of one's own social value
       </li>
      </ol>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_that_predict_a_breakup_before_marriage">
       Factors that predict a breakup before marriage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breakup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Factors that predict a breakup before marriage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Hill, Rubin and Peplau
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      identify 5 factors that predicted breakup before marriage:
     </p>
     <div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         Unequal involvement
        </b>
        in the relationship
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Age difference
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Educational aspirations
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Intelligence
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Physical attractiveness
        </b>
       </li>
      </ol>
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uncoupling_theory">
       Uncoupling theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breakup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uncoupling theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1976, sociologist Diane Vaughan proposed an "uncoupling theory," where there exists a "turning point" in the dynamics of relationship breakup - 'a precise moment when they "knew the relationship was over," when "everything went dead inside"' - followed by a transition period in which one partner unconsciously knows the relationship is going to end, but holds on to it for an extended period, even for years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Vaughan-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Vaughan considered that the process of breakup was asymmetrical for initiator and respondent: the former 'has begun mourning the loss of the relationship and has undertaken something tantamount to a rehearsal, mentally and, to varying degrees, experientially, of a life apart from the partner'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The latter then has to play catch-up: 'to make their own transition out of the relationship, partners must redefine initiator and relationship negatively, legitimating the dissolution'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As a result, for Vaughan 'getting out of a relationship includes a redefinition of self at several levels: in the private thoughts of the individual, between partners, and in the larger social context in which the relationship exists'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan.2C_p._6_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Vaughan.2C_p._6-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She considered that 'uncoupling is complete when the partners have defined themselves and are defined by others as separate and independent of each other - when being partners is no longer a major source of identity'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaughan.2C_p._6_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Vaughan.2C_p._6-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
