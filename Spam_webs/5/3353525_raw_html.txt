      <article about="/content/making-college-savings-easy-abc" class="node node-article see-through clearfix" id="node-45" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">
       <span class="rdf-meta element-hidden" content="Making college savings as easy as ABC" property="dc:title">
       </span>
       <div class="content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          THE BACKGROUND:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Lets face it: If you have children, or plan to have children, youve probably thought about the cost of college. Its a source of major stresspaying for college seems to get more expensive every year. The good news is that you can make paying for it more manageable. The sooner you begin saving, the betterand its more than possible to save what you need to help make college happen!
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          THE BREAKDOWN:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres our biggest piece of guidance and the secret to saving for education: Open and regularly contribute to a 529 college savings plan account.
         <em>
          Fidelity Viewpoints
         </em>
         <sup>
          
         </sup>
         "
         <a href="https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/abcs-of-college-savings-plans">
          The ABC's of 529 College Savings Plans
         </a>
         " breaks down the process, and heres why you should consider it:
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Its made to send people to college.
         </strong>
         <br/>
         529 college savings plans are designed specifically to help families pay for college costs like tuition, fees, books, supplies, and any approved equipment someone may need to enroll. A 529 plan even allows you to build up resources to help pay for room and board. When funds from a 529 are used for these things, you dont even pay federal taxes on investment gains (no capital gains tax, ordinary income tax, or Medicare surtax).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Its flexible.
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Each 529 college savings plan offers its own range of investment options. You can invest in an age-based portfolio that adjusts its investment risk based on time left until college, or you can manage your investments based on your desire to be more conservative or aggressive. There are lots of options available to make the plan work the way you need it to. If you change your mind, you can change your existing investments once each year.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          It wont hurt your childs chances for financial aid.
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Worried that saving for college will hurt the chances of receiving financial aid? A 529 college savings plan is considered a parental asset, which means it has less of an impact on financial aid plans.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          You can contribute as much as you want.
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Unlike some other college savings plans, a 529 plan has no income or age restrictions and no upper limit on the annual contribution. (Heads up: Once the 529 plan account savings for a particular child reach a certain valuetypically more than $300,000 [this varies by state]further contributions are not permitted. There is also a maximum per beneficiary.)
        </p>
        <p>
         Understanding the ins and outs of a 529 college savings plan may help you unlock one of the biggest bangs for your college-savings bucks.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Read on to find out more about:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Who may want to consider investing
          </strong>
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Some expert guidance on 529 plans
          </strong>
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           The potential tax benefits
          </strong>
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Some myths and truths of a 529 plan
          </strong>
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           How to get started
          </strong>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Who may want to consider investing
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Anyone with children (or grandchildren) may want to consider investing in a 529 college savings plan, no matter how old the children are now. The sooner you start, the longer you have to take advantage of tax-deferred growth and generous contribution limits.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          How to get started
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Anyone can open and fund a 529 college savings plan, including parents, grandparents, relatives, and family friends. Typically, a parent or grandparent opens the account and names a child or loved one as the beneficiary. Each plan is sponsored by an individual state, often along with a financial services company that manages the plan.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Some advice on 529 plans
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Think carefully about how you invest your savings: A strategy thats too aggressive for your time frame could put you at risk for losses that you may not have time to earn back. Being too conservative can also be a risk, because you might not make enough to cover your costs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Consider an age-based strategy for investing to the plan:
         </strong>
         If you dont want to actively manage your investments, an age-based strategy will automatically adjust the mix of assets to decrease the amount of risk in your investment as your child gets closer to starting college. Its like cruise control for college savings!
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Consider setting up automatic contributions:
         </strong>
         Regular, disciplined saving is the best way to grow the amount you put away for your future collegian. Many plans allow you to set up an automatic contribution right from your bank account or even from your paycheck. This discipline is the most important factor in growing your savings, because even if you arent able to make larger lump sum contributions to a 529 college savings plan, it will still grow.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Keep your other financial priorities in mind:
         </strong>
         Retirement saving should be a priority. You cant borrow money to pay for retirement, but you can for college. However, if saving for college is one of your financial goals, choosing to invest in a 529 college savings plan may be one of the smartest decisions you can make to help pay for college costs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          THE BOTTOM LINE:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Some myths and truths of the 529 plan
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Here are some of the most common myths, and the actual truths, about 529 college savings plans:
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Myth:
         </strong>
         If I don't use my 529 college savings plan savings for higher education, I lose the money.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Truth:
         </strong>
         The money is still yours! Youll pay a 10% penalty and ordinary income taxes on the earnings if you dont spend it on qualified higher education costs. But if you transfer the account to another child who plans to go to college, you keep every penny.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Myth:
         </strong>
         I can only invest in my own states plan.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Truth:
         </strong>
         You should consider your own state plan and any tax or other advantages that plan may have. However, you can invest in most plans, no matter what state you are in! Most plans have no state residency requirements for either the account owner or the beneficiary. The other good news? Most plans have no restrictions on where (i.e., in which state) your child can go to college.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Myth:
         </strong>
         Once I choose a 529 college savings plan and its underlying investments, I cant make changes to the plan.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Truth:
         </strong>
         You will still have choices, even after you start a 529 plan. If you want, you can typically roll over your 529 account savings to another college savings plan. And dont forget the flexibility: Investments within your plan can also be changed once per calendar year or when you change beneficiaries.
        </p>
