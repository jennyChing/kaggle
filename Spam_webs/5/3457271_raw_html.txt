      <div id="triberr_endorsement">
      </div>
      <p>
       The internet revolutionized the way people communicated and now the Internet of Things (IoT) may soon revolutionize the way your household appliances communicate.
       <a href="http://www.cisco.com/web/solutions/trends/iot/overview.html">
        The IoT
       </a>
       is a network of physical objects  anything from your refrigerator to your garage door opener  accessed through the Internet. Once connected, these objects can interact with the internal state of other objects and the external environment.
      </p>
      <h2>
       How It Will Work
      </h2>
      <p>
       There arent many objects connected right now, but companies like Panasonic, LG, and Sharp are working to make the IoT much more commonplace. To do so they have formed, along with other companies like HTC and Cisco, the Allseen Alliance. The Allseen Alliances goal is create a software language that would enable all kinds of devices to talk to one another.
      </p>
      <p>
       Smart appliances already exist, but right now they lack the ability to communicate with one another. Rob Chandhok, the senior vice president of Qualcomm, a company part of the Allseen Alliance,
       <a href="http://www.npr.org/2013/12/13/250536995/tech-companies-take-step-toward-the-internet-of-things">
        recently told
       </a>
       NPR about the current situation.
      </p>
      <p>
       It has been a forest of things that didnt talk to each other, Chandhok said. The consumer experience up till now has been fragmented and therefore not very interesting or useful.
      </p>
      <p>
       The common language for physical objects the Allseen Alliance is trying to create hopes to glue together all of those fragments, making it so just about everything in your house  except for maybe your
       <a href="http://www.amishoutletstore.com/">
        Amish furniture
       </a>
        will be interconnected.
       <span id="more-26724">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       The source code of the IoTs so-called common language will be open to anybody who wants to work on it. With so many people collaborating on the project, it will be difficult for anybody to add a vulnerability to the system. Of course, an open source code does not mean anybody could hack into your homes system and turn up your thermostat while you sleep. The manufacturers will still be able to add security features to prevent such intrusions.
      </p>
      <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_26727" style="width: 650px">
       <img alt="Nabaztag rabbits were an early form of connecting your appliances. They came with small RFID tags that you could attach to objects in order to track them." class="size-full wp-image-26727" height="480" src="http://www.dailywaffle.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/medium_3067221675.jpg" width="640"/>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">
        Nabaztag rabbits were an early form of connecting your appliances. They came with small RFID tags that you could attach to objects in order to track them.
       </p>
      </div>
      <h2>
       What It Will Look Like
      </h2>
      <p>
       If the Allseen Alliance  or any similar project  is successful, then your house will be able to function as
       <a href="http://www.wired.com/2013/05/internet-of-things-2/all/">
        one interconnected unit
       </a>
       . When your alarm clock goes off in the morning, it could tell your shower to turn on and your coffeepot to start brewing. Your favorite music could start playing when you walk into the kitchen. When you leave for work, your AC could automatically shut off when you leave and start up again an hour before you get home.
      </p>
      <p>
       If someone tries to break into your house while youre at work, an electronic lock on your door could set off an alarm and a camera could take pictures of the potential thief. The alert, with the pictures, could be sent straight to your smartphone.
      </p>
      <p>
       When you leave your Jetsons-esque abode to go on vacation, your hotel room could download your prearranged settings and have the lights, blinds and TV set just the way you like it before you even walk in.
      </p>
      <p>
       The potential for the IoT isnt just limited to the domestic sphere, either. It could change the way factories work, since all the machines on a production line will be able to communicate with one another and provide feedback on potential malfunctions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Though the IoT seems close to being a reality, many things still have to happen for it to come to fruition. Even if the common language comes together, its useless unless the majority of objects speak it. Only having a few household objects on the system wont be worth the trouble, and retrofitting most old appliances wont be possible. Still, even with a long way to go, having a smart fridge may soon be as common as having a smartphone.
      </p>
