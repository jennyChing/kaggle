     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">
        Censorship
       </a>
       of music
      </b>
      is the practice of restricting free access to
      <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">
       musical works
      </a>
      . This
      <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">
       censorship
      </a>
      may stem from a wide variety of motivations, including moral, political, military or religious reasons. Censorship can range from the complete government-enforced legal prohibition of a musical work, to private, voluntary removal of content when a musical work appears in a certain context. An example of this latter form of censorship is the
      <a href="/wiki/Radio_edit" title="Radio edit">
       radio edit
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship_of_pop_music">
       Censorship of pop music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Censorship_of_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Censorship of pop music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In order to allow songs to be played wherever possible, it is common to censor particular words, particularly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Profanities" title="Profanities">
       profanities
      </a>
      . Some music labels or artists produce censored versions themselves, sometimes with alternative lyrics, to comply with the rules set by various radio and television programs. Some stations decide to censor them themselves using one of several methods:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Blanking
       </b>
       ; when the volume is silenced for all or part of the word.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Bleeping
       </b>
       ; playing a noise, usually a "beep", over all or part of the word.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Resampling
       </b>
       ; using a like-sounding portion of vocals and music to override the offending word.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Resinging
       </b>
       ; Replacing the offensive word/phrase with a more appropriate word/phrase.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Backmasking
       </b>
       ; taking the offensive word and reversing the audio, sometimes the whole audio is reversed (often because it is a home-made job), but more usually only the vocal track is reversed.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Repeating
       </b>
       ; repeating the word said just before the explicit word was used.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Skipping
       </b>
       ; deleting the curse word from the song without a time delay.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Disc scratching
       </b>
       ; in
       <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">
        hip hop
       </a>
       , scratching on the word, making it sound like another word, or make the word said faster or slower.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        RoboVoicing
       </b>
       ; making the word totally non-understandable by overpowering a robotic voice effect (usually used as a last resort for home-made jobs).
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Distorting
       </b>
       ; Usually in
       <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">
        hip hop
       </a>
       , less offensive words such as "shit" or others are distorted. It is usually done by shifting down the pitch.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Airplay_censorship">
       Airplay censorship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Censorship_of_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Airplay censorship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An early example of censorship of music on the radio is from the 1940s.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Formby,_Jr." title="George Formby, Jr.">
       George Formby's
      </a>
      "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/When_I%27m_Cleaning_Windows" title="When I'm Cleaning Windows">
       When I'm Cleaning Windows
      </a>
      " was banned from
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      radio due to the "smutty lyrics", though Formby's wife Beryl managed to change BBC's opinion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The ostensibly offending lyrics were:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        The blushing bride she looks divine
        <br/>
        The bridegroom he is doing fine
        <br/>
        I'd rather have his job than mine
        <br/>
        When I'm cleaning windows
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Another example of censorship is when, in 1956, ABC radio refused to play Billie Holiday's "Love for Sale" because the lyrics are about prostitution, but "Love For Sale" would be on the radio again. ABC also made Cole Porter change the lyric of "I Get A Kick Out Of You", which was a hit for Frank Sinatra. Porter's original stated "I get no kick from cocaine". The cleaned-up version was "I get perfume from Spain".
     </p>
     <p>
      Another example is when the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones" title="Rolling Stones">
       Rolling Stones
      </a>
      appeared on the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="Ed Sullivan Show">
        Ed Sullivan Show
       </a>
      </i>
      . Sullivan asked them to sing their hit song "Let's Spend the Night Together", but he asked them to change the lyrics to "Let's Spend Some Time Together" so it would be considered more appropriate.
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to its position as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_broadcaster" title="Public broadcaster">
       public broadcaster
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">
       BBC Radio
      </a>
      has a policy of not playing songs that contain
      <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">
       product placement
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Davies" title="Ray Davies">
       Ray Davies
      </a>
      of the British rock band
      <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">
       The Kinks
      </a>
      was forced to travel back to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      during an American tour in order to change references to
      <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">
       Coca-Cola
      </a>
      to "cherry cola" from their hit song "
      <a href="/wiki/Lola_(song)" title="Lola (song)">
       Lola
      </a>
      " in order to allow it to be given airplay in the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship_due_to_copyright_infringement">
       Censorship due to copyright infringement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Censorship_of_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Censorship due to copyright infringement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1991,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Upright_v._Warner" title="Grand Upright v. Warner">
       Grand Upright v. Warner
      </a>
      clarified that
      <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">
       sampling
      </a>
      without permission from the original material's copyright holders is prohibited in the United States as
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">
       copyright infringement
      </a>
      . As a result, there are a few cases, particularly in
      <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">
       hip hop music
      </a>
      , where record labels are forced to reissue material with anything ruled to infringe on existing copyrights removed. An early example, before the 1991 US court case, is the recall of the European release 1987
      <i>
       (What the Fuck Is Going On?)
      </i>
      by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Justified_Ancients_of_Mu-Mu" title="The Justified Ancients of Mu-Mu">
       The Justified Ancients of Mu-Mu
      </a>
      . The album, full of unauthorized sampling, was recalled under the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_Copyright_Protection_Society" title="Mechanical Copyright Protection Society">
       Mechanical Copyright Protection Society
      </a>
      by a complaint from the band
      <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">
       ABBA
      </a>
      . An edited version, with long breaks of silence, was released afterward.
      <a href="/wiki/Megadeth" title="Megadeth">
       Megadeth
      </a>
      's 1985 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Killing_Is_My_Business..._and_Business_Is_Good!" title="Killing Is My Business... and Business Is Good!">
        Killing Is My Business... and Business Is Good!
       </a>
      </i>
      originally contained a cover of "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/These_Boots_Were_Made_For_Walking" title="These Boots Were Made For Walking">
       These Boots
      </a>
      " with some lyrics changed; however, in the 2002 re-release and remaster, due to a lawsuit concerning the changed lyrics, the changed lines were replaced with censor bleeps. The 1988 album
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Who_Killed_The_JAMs" title="Who Killed The JAMs">
        Who Killed The JAMs
       </a>
      </i>
      contained photographs of the previous aforementioned album being destroyed. A recent example of an album being altered due to copyright infringement is the recall and rerelease of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">
       The Notorious B.I.G.
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">
        Ready to Die
       </a>
      </i>
      , in which samples of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ohio_Players" title="The Ohio Players">
       The Ohio Players
      </a>
      music were omitted due to a 2006 American court case.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_censorship">
       Political censorship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Censorship_of_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political censorship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although not common in most
      <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
       democratic
      </a>
      societies, more authoritarian governments censor music deemed critical of the government, the military, stores, TV stations, or other authorities. In many societies without a well established free press, popular music is one of the few avenues to express and share ideas, even when those ideas are encoded in otherwise innocuous song lyrics.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mizik_rasin" title="Mizik rasin">
        mizik rasin
       </a>
      </i>
      band in
      <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
       Haiti
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/RAM_(band)" title="RAM (band)">
       RAM
      </a>
      , first played a song called "
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A8y" title="Fy">
       Fy
      </a>
      " in 1992. The song lyrics, from a traditional
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">
        vodou
       </a>
      </i>
      song, describe a leaf falling to the ground, but were widely understood as a song of support for the exiled President
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Bertrand_Aristide" title="Jean-Bertrand Aristide">
       Jean-Bertrand Aristide
      </a>
      . The song was banned throughout the country by the military regime of
      <a href="/wiki/Raoul_C%C3%A9dras" title="Raoul Cdras">
       Raoul Cdras
      </a>
      until he fled the country in September 1994 and Aristide was restored to the presidency. Censorship of music was also common in Communist countries such as the USSR.
     </p>
     <p>
      The line in
      <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">
       Eminem
      </a>
      's song "
      <a href="/wiki/Mosh_(song)" title="Mosh (song)">
       Mosh
      </a>
      " off of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Encore_(Eminem_album)" title="Encore (Eminem album)">
        Encore
       </a>
      </i>
      "Strap
      <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">
       Bush
      </a>
      with an
      <a href="/wiki/AK-47" title="AK-47">
       AK-47
      </a>
      , let him go fight his own war, let him impress daddy that way" the words "Bush" and "AK-47" were censored in the television broadcast version of the music video as well as the censored version sold in such stores as
      <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">
       Walmart
      </a>
      by blanking out the words still playing the background music, but was not edited on the uncensored version of the album or the internet broadcast version.
     </p>

     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/Publicity" title="Publicity">

      </a>
      or a
      <a href="/wiki/Parental_Advisory" title="Parental Advisory">
       Parental Advisory
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">

      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_audiovisual_entertainment_affected_by_the_September_11,_2001_attacks" title="List of audiovisual entertainment affected by the September 11, 2001 attacks">

      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">

      </a>
      's greatest hits album was delayed until after
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_trial_of_Michael_Jackson" title="2005 trial of Michael Jackson">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Sarah_McLachlan" title="Sarah McLachlan">
       Sarah McLachlan
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Building_a_Mystery" title="Building a Mystery">
       Building a Mystery
      </a>

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/The_Future_(Leonard_Cohen_album)" title="The Future (Leonard Cohen album)">

      <a href="/wiki/Cascada" title="Cascada">

      </a>

       The Black Eyed Peas
      </a>
      censor the word "fuck", but not other profanities, such as "shit" or "nigga", such as in the song "
      <a href="/wiki/Imma_Be" title="Imma Be">
       Imma Be
      </a>
      "; "motherfucking crew" has been censored as "mothermother crew", and the syllable "fuck" in the line "Imma be fucking her". This was censored by "Imma be freaking her". Similarly,
      <a href="/wiki/Robbie_Williams" title="Robbie Williams">
       Robbie Williams
      </a>
      ' song "Bodies" has the word "fucking" bleeped near the start of the song, where he says "UK and entropy, I feel like it's fucking me".
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Simple_Plan" title="Simple Plan">
       Simple Plan
      </a>
      's song "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Your_Love_is_a_Lie" title="Your Love is a Lie">
       Your Love is a Lie
      </a>
      ", the word "fucked" was partially censored in the line "And do you think about me when he fucked you?" The radio edit still replaced the word "fucked" with "touches" even though the word "fuck" was censored.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Mya's My Love Is Like... Wo, the word "ass" was censored in the line "My ass is like wo" even in the "unedited version" of the video. Taylor Swift's Teardrops on My Guitar has two versions: the uncensored version which has the line "I laugh 'cause he's so damn funny" (

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Censorship_of_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The total censorship of a song is often reported in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">
       mass media
      </a>
      and often has the effect of drawing
      <a href="/wiki/Streisand_effect" title="Streisand effect">
       more attention
      </a>
      to the song than it would have received had it not been banned.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/1993_in_music" title="1993 in music">
       1993
      </a>
      , when
      <a href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)" title="Nirvana (band)">
       Nirvana
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/In_Utero_(album)" title="In Utero (album)">
        In Utero (album)
       </a>
      </i>
      album was released, it was forced to be censored by their label as well as by distributors
      <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">
       Walmart
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kmart" title="Kmart">
       Kmart
      </a>
      . Nirvana's frontman,
      <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Cobain" title="Kurt Cobain">
       Kurt Cobain
      </a>
      , responded by saying, "I just feel bad for all the kids who are forced to buy their music from big chain stores and have to have the edited music." The name of the song "
      <a href="/wiki/Rape_Me" title="Rape Me">
       Rape Me
      </a>
      " was changed to "Waif Me" for these stores. The name change only appears on the back cover. The original title is still stated in the liner notes and the album insert.
     </p>
