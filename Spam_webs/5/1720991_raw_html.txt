        <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/" target="_blank" title="cdc">
         CDC

        and the
        <a href="http://www2.epa.gov/" target="_blank" title="epa">
         EPA
        </a>
        , lead poisoning is still a thing.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://poshonabudget.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/icon-english.jpg">
         <img alt="It's Lead Poisoning Prevention Week " class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-55500" height="297" src="http://poshonabudget.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/icon-english.jpg" width="252"/>
        </a>

       <h2>
        Its Lead Poisoning Prevention Week
       </h2>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Lead can still be found in all parts of our environment;
        </strong>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         air
        </li>
        <li>
         soil
        </li>
        <li>
         water
        </li>
        <li>
         paint
        </li>
        <li>
         ceramics
        </li>
        <li>
         pipes
        </li>
        <li>
         gasoline
        </li>
        <li>
         batteries
        </li>
        <li>
         and more places.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Why is lead so bad?
        </strong>
        <br/>
        Lead can affect almost every organ and system in your body.
       </p>
       <p>
        Children six years old and younger are most susceptible to the effects of lead.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Lead in the blood of children can result in:
        </li>
        <li>
         Behavior and learning problems
        </li>
        <li>
         Lower IQ and Hyperactivity
        </li>
        <li>
         Slowed growth
        </li>
        <li>
         Hearing Problems
        </li>
        <li>
         Anemia
        </li>
        <li>
         In rare cases, ingestion of lead can cause seizures, coma and even death.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
       </p>
       <h3>
        Where to look for Lead in your home:
       </h3>
       <ul>
        <li>
         House paint before 1978. Even if the paint is not peeling, it can be a problem. Lead paint is very dangerous when it is being stripped or sanded. These actions release fine lead dust into the air. Infants and children living in pre-1960s housing (when paint often contained lead) have the highest risk of lead poisoning. Small children often swallow paint chips or dust from lead-based paint.
        </li>
        <li>
         Toys and furniture painted before 1976.
        </li>
        <li>
         Painted toys and decorations made outside the U.S.
        </li>
        <li>
         Lead bullets, fishing sinkers, curtain weights.
        </li>
        <li>
         Plumbing, pipes, and faucets. Lead can be found in drinking water in homes containing pipes that were connected with lead
         <a href="http://www.nlm.nih.gov/medlineplus/ency/article/002822.htm">
          solder
         </a>
         . Although new building codes require lead-free solder, lead is still found in some modern faucets.
        </li>
        <li>
         Soil contaminated by decades of car exhaust or years of house paint scrapings. Lead is more common in soil near highways and houses.
        </li>
        <li>
         Hobbies involving soldering, stained glass, jewelry making, pottery glazing, and miniature lead figures (always look at labels).
        </li>
        <li>
         Childrens paint sets and art supplies (always look at labels).
        </li>
        <li>
         Pewter pitchers and dinnerware.
        </li>
        <li>
         Storage batteries.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h4>
        Read more on the health effects of lead
       </h4>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://cfpub.epa.gov/ncea/isa/recordisplay.cfm?deid=242655#Download">
          EPAs Integrated Science Assessment for Lead
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/toxfaqs/TF.asp?id=93&amp;tid=22">
          Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR).
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         Other federal agencies, such as the
         <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/nceh/lead/nlppw.htm">
          Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
         </a>
         and the
         <a href="http://portal.hud.gov/hudportal/HUD?src=/program_offices/healthy_homes/healthyhomes/lead">
          Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)
         </a>
         , also conduct various education and awareness events during Lead Poisoning Prevention Week.
         <p>
          For more information about Lead Poisoning Prevention Week or lead poisoning in general, contact the
          <a href="http://www2.epa.gov/lead/forms/lead-hotline-national-lead-information-center">
           National Lead Information Center
          </a>
          at 1 (800) 424-LEAD.
         </p>
        </li>
       </ul>
