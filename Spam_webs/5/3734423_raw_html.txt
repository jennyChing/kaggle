       <h1 class="post-title entry-title">
        Study reveals unhappiest cities in the U.S.
       </h1>
       <p class="byline">
        Media Release | July 21, 2014
       </p>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <a href="http://news.ubc.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/pmsa_blup_adj_all.jpg">
          <img alt="pmsa_blup_adj_all" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-163003" height="350" src="http://news.ubc.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/pmsa_blup_adj_all.jpg" width="770"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <div class="image-caption">
         A map of the U.S. which shows each metropolitan and rural areas adjusted life satisfaction.
        </div>
        <p>
         New research identifies the unhappiest cities in the U.S., but finds that some young people are still willing to relocate to them for a good job opportunity or lower housing prices.
        </p>
        <p>
         The analysis, co-authored by Joshua Gottlieb of the University of British Columbias
         <a href="http://www.economics.ubc.ca/">
          Vancouver School of Economics
         </a>
         , suggests people may be deciding to trade happiness for other gains.
        </p>
        <p>
         The working paper 
         <a href="http://www.nber.org/papers/w20291">
          Unhappy Cities
         </a>
         , released last week by the U.S.
         <a href="http://www.nber.org/">
          National Bureau of Economic Research
         </a>
         , relies on a large survey that asks respondents about their satisfaction with life. This measure, which is often interpreted as a measure of happiness, indicates that individuals may willingly endure less happiness in exchange for higher incomes or lower housing costs.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_162999" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://news.ubc.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/josh-gottlieb-150.jpg">
          <img alt="Prof. Joshua Gottlieb" class="size-full wp-image-162999" height="150" src="http://news.ubc.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/josh-gottlieb-150.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Prof. Joshua Gottlieb
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         Our research indicates that people care about more than happiness alone, so other factors may encourage them to stay in a city despite their unhappiness, says Gottlieb. This means that researchers and policy-makers should not consider an increase in reported happiness as an overriding objective.
        </p>
        <p>
         Gottlieb and his co-authors investigated which regions of the U.S. tend to report lower life satisfaction, and found that residents of declining cities appear less happy than those who live in other parts of the U.S. Long-term residents of these cities appear equally as unhappy as newer residents, suggesting that the citys unhappiness persists over time. Historical data indicate that cities currently in decline were also unhappy in their more prosperous past.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Read the full study
          <a href="http://www.nber.org/papers/w20291">
           here
          </a>
          . High resolution images available upon request.
         </strong>
         Gottliebs co-authors for Unhappy Cities are
         <a href="http://www.harvard.edu/">
          Harvard University
         </a>
         s Edward Glaeser and Oren Ziv.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          BACKGROUND
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Top 10 happiest metropolitan areas with a population greater than 1 million (as of 2010):
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         1. Richmond-Petersburg, VA
         <br/>
         2. Norfolk-Virginia Beach-Newport News, VA
         <br/>
         3. Washington, DC
         <br/>
         4. Raleigh-Durham, NC
         <br/>
         5. Atlanta, GA
         <br/>
         6. Houston, TX
         <br/>
         7. Jacksonville, FL
         <br/>
         8. Nashville, TN
         <br/>
