     <div class="hatnote">
      "Order of the Virtues" redirects here. For the Egyptian award, see

     <p>
      <b>
       Ordo Virtutum
      </b>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
       Latin
      </a>
      for
      <i>
       Order of the Virtues
      </i>
      ) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">
       allegorical
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Morality_play" title="Morality play">
       morality play
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_drama" title="Liturgical drama">
       liturgical drama
      </a>
      , by
      <a href="/wiki/Hildegard_of_Bingen" title="Hildegard of Bingen">
       Hildegard of Bingen
      </a>
      , composed c. 1151. It is the earliest morality play by more than a century, and the only
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">
       Medieval
      </a>
      musical drama to survive with an attribution for both the text and the music.
     </p>
     <p>
      A short version of
      <i>
       Ordo Virtutum
      </i>
      without music appears at the end of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scivias" title="Scivias">
        Scivias
       </a>
      </i>
      , Hildegard's most famous account of her visions. It is also included in some manuscripts of the
      <i>
       Symphonia armoniae celestium revelationum
      </i>
      ("Symphony of the Harmony of Celestial Revelations"), a cycle of more than 70 liturgical songs. It may have been performed by the convent nuns at the dedication of the St.
      <a href="/wiki/Rupertsberg" title="Rupertsberg">
       Rupertsberg
      </a>
      church in 1152
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or possibly before the Mass for the
      <a href="/wiki/Consecrated_virgin" title="Consecrated virgin">
       Consecration of Virgins
      </a>
      at the convent .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordo_Virtutum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ordo Virtutum is about the struggle for a human soul, or Anima, between the Virtues and the Devil. The piece can be divided as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Part I: Prologue in which the Virtues are introduced to the Patriarchs and Prophets who marvel at the Virtues.
     </p>
     <p>
      Part II: We hear the complaints of souls that are imprisoned in bodies. The (for now) happy Soul enters and her voice contrasts with the unhappy souls. However, the Soul is too eager to skip life and go straight to Heaven. When the Virtues tell her that she has to live first, the Devil seduces her away to worldly things.
     </p>
     <p>
      Part III: The Virtues take turns identifying and describing themselves while the Devil occasionally interrupts and expresses opposing views and insults.
     </p>
     <p>
      Part IV: The Soul returns, repentant. Once the Virtues have accepted her back, they turn on the Devil, whom they bind, and then God is praised.
     </p>
     <p>
      Part V: A procession of all the characters.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roles">
       Roles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordo_Virtutum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Roles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Soul (female voice)
     </p>
     <p>
      The Virtues (sung by 17 solo female voices): Humility (Queen of the Virtues), Hope, Chastity, Innocence, Contempt of the World, Celestial Love, Discipline? (the name is scratched out in the manuscript) Modesty, Mercy, Victory, Discretion, Patience, Knowledge of God, Charity, Fear of God, Obedience, and Faith
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Chorus of the Prophets and Patriarchs (sung by a male chorus)
     </p>
     <p>
      Chorus of Souls (sung by a womens chorus)
     </p>
     <p>
      The Devil (a male voice)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Devil does not sing, he only yells or grunts because according to Hildegard, he cannot produce divine harmony.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been suggested that the soul represents Richardis von Stade, Hildegards friend and fellow nun, who had left to become abbess of another convent. Hildegard was upset by this appointment and tried to have it revoked, appealing even to
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eugene_III" title="Pope Eugene III">
       Pope Eugene III
      </a>
      . However, Hildegard was unsuccessful and Richardis departed, only to die shortly thereafter on October 29, 1151. Other scholarship propose an allusion to Hildegard's brother Bruno.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Before dying Richardis told her brother that she wanted to return to Hildegard, not unlike the returning, repentant Soul of Ordo Virtutum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_elements">
       Musical elements
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordo_Virtutum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Musical elements">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ordo Virtutum is written in
      <a href="/wiki/Verse_drama_and_dramatic_verse" title="Verse drama and dramatic verse">
       dramatic verse
      </a>
      and contains 82 different melodies, which are set more
      <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
       syllabically
      </a>
      than Hildegard's
      <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">
       liturgical
      </a>
      songs. All parts are sung in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plainchant" title="Plainchant">
       plainchant
      </a>
      except that of the Devil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palisca_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Palisca-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is an alternation between solo and chorus parts as well as melismatic versus syllabic lines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
