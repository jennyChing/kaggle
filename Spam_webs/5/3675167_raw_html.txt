   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Magnetic bearing
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      A
      <b>
       magnetic bearing
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">
       bearing
      </a>
      that supports a load using
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_levitation" title="Magnetic levitation">
       magnetic levitation
      </a>
      . Magnetic bearings support moving parts without physical contact. For instance, they are able to levitate a rotating shaft and permit relative motion with very low friction and no mechanical wear. Magnetic bearings support the highest speeds of all kinds of bearing and have no maximum relative speed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive magnetic bearings use permanent magnets and, therefore, do not require any input power but are difficult to design due to the limitations described by
      <a href="/wiki/Earnshaw%27s_theorem" title="Earnshaw's theorem">
       Earnshaw's theorem
      </a>
      . Techniques using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">
       diamagnetic
      </a>
      materials are relatively undeveloped and strongly depend on material characteristics. As a result, most magnetic bearings are active magnetic bearings, using
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">
       electromagnets
      </a>
      which require continuous power input and an active control system to keep the load stable. In a combined design, permanent magnets are often used to carry the static load and the active magnetic bearing is used when the levitated object deviates from its optimum position. Magnetic bearings typically require a back-up bearing in the case of power or control system failure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Magnetic bearings are used in several industrial applications such as electrical power generation, petroleum refinement, machine tool operation and natural gas handling. They are also used in the
      <a href="/wiki/Zippe-type_centrifuge" title="Zippe-type centrifuge">
       Zippe-type centrifuge
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_enrichment" title="Uranium enrichment">
       uranium enrichment
      </a>
      and in
      <a href="/wiki/Turbomolecular_pump" title="Turbomolecular pump">
       turbomolecular pumps
      </a>
      , where oil-lubricated bearings would be a source of contamination.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design">
       Design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_bearing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amb2.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1040" data-file-width="1280" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Amb2.svg/220px-Amb2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Amb2.svg/330px-Amb2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Amb2.svg/440px-Amb2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amb2.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Basic operation for a single axis
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An active magnetic bearing works on the principle of
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_suspension" title="Electromagnetic suspension">
       electromagnetic suspension
      </a>
      and consists of an
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">
       electromagnet
      </a>
      assembly, a set of power amplifiers which supply current to the electromagnets, a
      <a href="/wiki/Controller_(control_theory)" title="Controller (control theory)">
       controller
      </a>
      , and gap sensors with associated electronics to provide the feedback required to control the position of the rotor within the gap. The power amplifier supplies equal bias current to two pairs of electromagnets on opposite sides of a rotor. This constant tug-of-war is mediated by the controller, which offsets the bias current by equal and opposite perturbations of current as the rotor deviates from its center position.
     </p>
     <p>
      The gap sensors are usually inductive in nature and sense in a differential mode. The power amplifiers in a modern commercial application are solid state devices which operate in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulse_width_modulation" title="Pulse width modulation">
       pulse width modulation
      </a>
      configuration. The controller is usually a
      <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">
       microprocessor
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">
       digital signal processor
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Active bearings have several advantages: they do not suffer from wear, have low friction, and can often accommodate irregularities in the mass distribution automatically, allowing rotors to spin around their
      <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
       center of mass
      </a>
      with very low vibration.
     </p>
     <p>
      Two types of instabilities are typically present in magnetic bearings. Attractive magnets produce an unstable static force that decreases with increasing distance and increases at decreasing distances. This can cause the bearing to become unbalanced. Secondly, because magnetism is a
      <a href="/wiki/Conservative_force" title="Conservative force">
       conservative force
      </a>
      , it provides little damping; oscillations may cause loss of successful suspension if any driving forces are present.
     </p>

       Jesse Beams

      from the

       University of Virginia

      <a href="/wiki/Ultracentrifuge" title="Ultracentrifuge">
       ultracentrifuges
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">
       Manhattan Project
      </a>
      . However, magnetic bearings did not mature until advances in solid-state electronics and modern computer-based control technology with the work of Habermann
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>

        </span>

        <span>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6

      but these designs were not manufactured due to expensive costs of production, which used a laser guidance system. Estelle Croot's research was the subject of 3 Australian patents
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ipaustralia.com.au/applicant/croot-estelle/patents/" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
      and was funded by Nachi Fujikoshi, Nippon Seiko KK and Hitachi, and her calculations were used in other technologies that used rare earth magnets but the active magnetic bearings were only developed to the prototype stage.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kasarda
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      reviews the history of active magnetic bearings in depth. She notes that the first commercial application of active magnetic bearings was in
      <a href="/wiki/Turbomachinery" title="Turbomachinery">
       turbomachinery
      </a>
      . The active magnetic bearing allowed the elimination of oil reservoirs on compressors for the NOVA Gas Transmission Ltd. (NGTL)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_pipeline" title="Gas pipeline">
       gas pipelines
      </a>
      in Alberta, Canada. This reduced the fire hazard allowing a substantial reduction in insurance costs. The success of these magnetic bearing installations led NGTL to pioneer the research and development of a digital magnetic bearing control system as a replacement for the analog control systems supplied by the American company Magnetic Bearings Inc. In 1992, NGTL's magnetic bearing research group formed the company Revolve Technologies Inc.
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.skfmagneticbearings.com" rel="nofollow">
       [2]
      </a>
      for commercializing the digital magnetic bearing technology. The company was later purchased by
      <a href="/wiki/SKF" title="SKF">
       SKF
      </a>
      of Sweden. The French company
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=S2M&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="S2M (page does not exist)">
       S2M
      </a>
      , founded in 1976, was the first to commercially market active magnetic bearings. Extensive research on magnetic bearings continues at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">
       University of Virginia
      </a>
      in the Rotating Machinery and Controls Industrial Research Program
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.virginia.edu/romac/" rel="nofollow">
       [3]
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Starting from 1996 the Dutch oil and gas company NAM installed over a period of 10 years 20 large E-motor driven (with variable speed drive) gas compressors of 23 MW fully equipped with active magnetic bearings on both the E-motor and the compressor. These compressors are used in the Groningen gas field to extract the remaining gas from this large gas field and to increase the field capacity. The motor-compressor design was done by Siemens and the active magnetic bearings were delivered by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waukesha_Bearings_Corporation" title="Waukesha Bearings Corporation">
       Waukesha Bearings
      </a>
      (owned by
      <a href="/wiki/Dover_Corporation" title="Dover Corporation">
       Dover Corporation
      </a>
      ). (Originally these bearings were designed by Glacier, this company was later taken over by Federal Mogul and is now part of Waukesha Bearings.) By using active magnetic bearings and a direct drive between motor and compressor (without the gearbox in between) and applying dry gas seals, a full dry-dry (oil free) system has been installed. Applying active magnetic bearings in the driver and in the compressor (compared to the traditional configuration with a gearbox, plain bearings and a gasturbine-driver) results in a relatively simple system with a very wide operating range and high efficiencies (particularly at partial load). As done in the Groningen field, the full installation can additionally be placed outdoors without the need for a large compressor building.
     </p>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      pioneered hybrid magnetic bearing designs (US patent 5,111,102) in which
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnet" title="Permanent magnet">
       permanent magnets
      </a>
      provide the bias field and active control coils are used for stability and dynamic control. These designs using permanent magnets for bias fields are smaller and of lighter weight than purely electromagnetic bearings. The electronic control system is also smaller and requires less electrical power because the bias field is provided by the permanent magnets. One of the most successful applications of this technology was by
      <a href="/wiki/Danfoss" title="Danfoss">
       Danfoss
      </a>
      , a manufacturer of refrigerator turbopumps. Using permanent magnet bias magnetic bearings allows the compressors to operate at over 40,000 rpm instead of the traditional 3,600 rpm. The Danfoss compressors are an order of magnitude smaller and lighter in weight than the lower speed compressors that they replace. Thousands of compressors using this advanced magnetic bearing technology have been manufactured by Danfoss in Florida.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the development of the necessary components progressed, scientific interest in the field also increased, peaking in the first International Symposium on Magnetic Bearings held in 1988 in Zurich with the founding of the International Society of Magnetic Bearings by Prof. Schweitzer (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ETHZ" title="ETHZ">
       ETHZ
      </a>
      ), Prof. Allaire (University of Virginia), and Prof. Okada (Ibaraki University). Since then, the symposium has developed into a biennial conference series with a permanent portal on magnetic bearing technology
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.magneticbearings.org" rel="nofollow">
       [4]
      </a>
      where all symposium contributions are made available. The web portal is supported by the international research and industrial community. Joining the hall of fame and earning lifetime achievement awards in 2012 were Prof. Yohji Okada, Prof. Gerhard Schweitzer, and Michael Swann of Waukesha Magnetic Bearings
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.magneticbearings.org/?page_id=1132" rel="nofollow">
       [5]
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_bearing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Magnetic bearing advantages include very low and predictable friction, and the ability to run without lubrication and in a vacuum. Magnetic bearings are increasingly used in industrial machines such as compressors, turbines, pumps, motors and generators.
     </p>
     <p>
      Magnetic bearings are commonly used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-hour_meters" title="Watt-hour meters">
       watt-hour meters
      </a>
      by electric utilities to measure home power consumption. They are also used in high-precision instruments and to support equipment in a vacuum, for example in
      <a href="/wiki/Flywheel_energy_storage" title="Flywheel energy storage">
       flywheel energy storage
      </a>
      systems. A flywheel in a vacuum has very low wind resistance losses, but conventional bearings usually fail quickly in a vacuum due to poor lubrication. Magnetic bearings are also used to support
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maglev_train" title="Maglev train">
       maglev trains
      </a>
      in order to get low noise and smooth ride by eliminating physical contact surfaces. Disadvantages include high cost, heavy weight and relatively large size.
     </p>
     <p>
      A new application of magnetic bearings is in artificial hearts. The use of magnetic suspension in ventricular assist devices was pioneered by Prof. Paul Allaire and Prof. Houston Wood at the University of Virginia, culminating in the first magnetically suspended ventricular assist centrifugal pump (
      <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_assist_device" title="Ventricular assist device">
       VAD
      </a>
      ) in 1999.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_advances">
       Future advances
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_bearing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Future advances">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1032" data-file-width="1172" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg/220px-Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg/330px-Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg/440px-Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magnetic_Mirroring.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An axial homopolar electrodynamic bearing
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      With the use of an induction-based levitation system present in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maglev_(transport)" title="Maglev (transport)">
       maglev
      </a>
      technologies such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Inductrack" title="Inductrack">
       Inductrack
      </a>
      system, magnetic bearings could replace complex control systems by using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halbach_Array" title="Halbach Array">
       Halbach Arrays
      </a>
      and simple closed loop coils. These systems gain in simplicity, but are less advantageous with regard to
      <a href="/wiki/Eddy_current" title="Eddy current">
       eddy current
      </a>
      losses. For
      <a href="/wiki/Rotordynamics" title="Rotordynamics">
       rotating systems
      </a>
      it is possible to use homopolar magnet designs instead of multipole Halbach structures, which reduce losses considerably.
     </p>
     <p>
      An example that has bypassed the Earnshaw's theorem issues is the homopolar
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamic_bearings" title="Electrodynamic bearings">
       electrodynamic bearing
      </a>
      invented by Dr Torbjrn Lembke.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is a novel type of electromagnetic bearing based on a passive magnetic technology. It does not require any control electronics to operate and works because the electrical currents generated by motion cause a restoring force.
     </p>
