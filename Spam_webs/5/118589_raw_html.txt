     <p>
      <b>
       Jos Maria de Ea de Queiroz
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Ea de Queirs
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (
      <small>
       European Portuguese:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">
        [uz mi d s d kej]
       </a>
      </span>
      ; 25 November 1845  16 August 1900) is generally considered to be the greatest Portuguese writer in the
      <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">
       realist
      </a>
      style.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Zola" title="mile Zola">
       Zola
      </a>
      considered him to be far greater than
      <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">
       Flaubert
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The London
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
        Observer
       </a>
      </i>
      critics rank him with
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">
       Dickens
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honor de Balzac">
       Balzac
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">
       Tolstoy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template">
       <span style="white-space: nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot">
          <span title="since June 2015">
           dead link
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </span>
      </sup>
      Ea never officially rejected Catholicism, and in many of his private letters he even invokes Jesus and uses expressions typical of Catholics, but was very critical of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">
       Catholic Church
      </a>
      of his time, and of
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      in general (also
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">
       Protestant
      </a>
      churches) as is evident in some of his novels.
     </p>
     <p>
      During his lifetime, the spelling was "Ea de Queiroz" and this is the form that appears on many editions of his works; the modern standard
      <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
       Portuguese spelling
      </a>
      is "Ea de Queirs".
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Maria_de_E%C3%A7a_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ea de Queirs was born in
      <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Pvoa de Varzim">
       Pvoa de Varzim
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
       Portugal
      </a>
      , in 1845. An illegitimate child, he was officially recorded as the son of Jos Maria de Almeida Teixeira de Queirs and Carolina Augusta Pereira d'Ea.
     </p>
     <p>
      At age 16, he went to
      <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">
       Coimbra
      </a>
      to study
      <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">
       law
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Coimbra" title="University of Coimbra">
       University of Coimbra
      </a>
      ; there he met the poet
      <a href="/wiki/Antero_de_Quental" title="Antero de Quental">
       Antero de Quental
      </a>
      . Ea's first work was a series of prose poems, published in the
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gazeta_de_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gazeta de Portugal (page does not exist)">
        Gazeta de Portugal
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine, which eventually appeared in book form in a posthumous collection edited by
      <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Batalha_Reis" title="Jaime Batalha Reis">
       Batalha Reis
      </a>
      entitled
      <i>
       Prosas Brbaras
      </i>
      ("Barbarous texts"). He worked as a journalist at
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vora" title="vora">
       vora
      </a>
      , then returned to Lisbon and, with his former school friend
      <a href="/wiki/Ramalho_Ortig%C3%A3o" title="Ramalho Ortigo">
       Ramalho Ortigo
      </a>
      and others, created the
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Correspond%C3%AAncia_de_Fradique_Mendes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Correspondncia de Fradique Mendes (page does not exist)">
        Correspondence
       </a>
      </i>
      of the fictional adventurer
      <a href="/wiki/Fradique_Mendes" title="Fradique Mendes">
       Fradique Mendes
      </a>
      . This amusing work was first published in 1900.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg/220px-Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg/330px-Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg/440px-Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pra%C3%A7a_do_Almada_-_Estatua_a_E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Statue of Ea in Pvoa de Varzim; a couple of metres from his birthplace.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1869 and 1870, Ea de Queirs travelled to
      <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
       Egypt
      </a>
      and watched the opening of the
      <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">
       Suez Canal
      </a>
      , which inspired several of his works, most notably
      <i>
       O Mistrio da Estrada de Sintra
      </i>
      ("The Mystery of the Sintra Road", 1870), written in collaboration with Ramalho Ortigo, in which Fradique Mendes appears.
      <i>
       A Relquia
      </i>
      ("The Relic") was also written at this period but was published only in 1887. The work was strongly influenced by
      <i>
       Memorie di Giuda
      </i>
      ("Memoirs of Judas") by
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_Petruccelli_della_Gattina" title="Ferdinando Petruccelli della Gattina">
       Ferdinando Petruccelli della Gattina
      </a>
      , such as to lead some scholars to accuse the Portuguese writer of plagiarism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When he was later dispatched to
      <a href="/wiki/Leiria" title="Leiria">
       Leiria
      </a>
      to work as a municipal administrator, Ea de Queirs wrote his first realist novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/O_Crime_do_Padre_Amaro" title="O Crime do Padre Amaro">
        O Crime do Padre Amaro
       </a>
      </i>
      ("The Sin of Father Amaro"), which is set in the city and first appeared in 1875.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ea then worked in the Portuguese consular service and after two years' service at Havana was stationed, from late 1874 until April 1879, at 53 Grey Street,
      <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">
       Newcastle upon Tyne
      </a>
      , where there is a memorial plaque in his honour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His diplomatic duties involved the dispatch of detailed reports to the Portuguese foreign office concerning the unrest in the
      <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">
       Northumberland
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">
       Durham
      </a>
      coalfields - in which, as he points out, the miners earned twice as much as those in South Wales, along with free housing and a weekly supply of coal. The Newcastle years were among the most productive of his literary career. He published the second version of
      <i>
       O Crime de Padre Amaro
      </i>
      in 1876 and another celebrated novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cousin_Bazilio" title="Cousin Bazilio">
        O Primo Baslio
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Cousin Bazilio") in 1878, as well as working on a number of other projects. These included the first of his "Cartas de Londres" ("Letters from London") which were printed in the Lisbon daily newspaper
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Di%C3%A1rio_de_Not%C3%ADcias" title="Dirio de Notcias">
        Dirio de Notcias
       </a>
      </i>
      and afterwards appeared in book form as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cartas_de_Inglaterra" title="Cartas de Inglaterra">
        Cartas de Inglaterra
       </a>
      </i>
      . As early as 1878 he had at least given a name to his masterpiece
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Os_Maias" title="Os Maias">
        Os Maias
       </a>
      </i>
      ("The Maias"), though this was largely written during his later residence in
      <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">
       Bristol
      </a>
      and was published only in 1888. There is a plaque to Ea in that city and another was unveiled in Grey Street, Newcastle, in 2001 by the Portuguese ambassador.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ea, a cosmopolite widely read in English literature, was not enamoured of English society, but he was fascinated by its oddity. In
      <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">
       Bristol
      </a>
      he wrote: "Everything about this society is disagreeable to me - from its limited way of thinking to its indecent manner of cooking vegetables." As often happens when a writer is unhappy, the weather is endlessly bad. Nevertheless, he was rarely bored and was content to stay in England for some fifteen years. "I detest England, but this does not stop me from declaring that as a thinking nation, she is probably the foremost." It may be said that England acted as a constant stimulus and a corrective to Eas traditionally Portuguese Francophilia.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1888 he became Portuguese consul-general in
      <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
       Paris
      </a>
      . He lived at
      <a href="/wiki/Neuilly-sur-Seine" title="Neuilly-sur-Seine">
       Neuilly-sur-Seine
      </a>
      and continued to write journalism (
      <i>
       Ecos de Paris
      </i>
      , "Echos from Paris") as well as literary criticism. He died in 1900 of either
      <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">
       tuberculosis
      </a>
      or, according to numerous contemporary physicians,
      <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn's disease">
       Crohn's disease
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His son
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_E%C3%A7a_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="Antnio Ea de Queirs">
       Antnio Ea de Queirs
      </a>
      would hold government office under
      <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="Antnio de Oliveira Salazar">
       Antnio de Oliveira Salazar
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">
       Adaptations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Maria_de_E%C3%A7a_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adaptations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="border:1px solid #aaa;background-color:#f9f9f9">
      <tr>
       <td class="mbox-image">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="1376" data-file-width="1024" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/30px-Commons-logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/45px-Commons-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/59px-Commons-logo.svg.png 2x" width="30"/>
       </td>
       <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
        Wikimedia Commons has media related to
        <i>
         <b>
          <a class="extiw" href="//commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:E%C3%A7a_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="commons:Category:Ea de Queirs">
           Ea de Queirs
          </a>
         </b>
        </i>
        .
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      There have been two film versions of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/O_Crime_do_Padre_Amaro" title="O Crime do Padre Amaro">
        O Crime do Padre Amaro
       </a>
      </i>
      , a Mexican one in 2002 and a Portuguese version in 2005 which was edited out of a
      <a href="/wiki/Sociedade_Independente_de_Comunica%C3%A7%C3%A3o" title="Sociedade Independente de Comunicao">
       SIC
      </a>
      television series, released shortly after the film (the film was by then the most seen Portuguese movie ever, though very badly received by critics, but the TV series, maybe due to being a slightly longer version of the same thing seen by a big share of Portuguese population,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Box_office_flop" title="Box office flop">
       flopped
      </a>
      and was rather ignored by audiences and critics).
     </p>
     <p>
      Ea's works have been also adapted on Brazilian television. In 1988
      <a href="/wiki/Rede_Globo" title="Rede Globo">
       Rede Globo
      </a>
      produced
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cousin_Bazilio" title="Cousin Bazilio">
        O Primo Baslio
       </a>
      </i>
      in 35 episodes. Later, in 2007, a movie adaptation of the same novel was made by director
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Filho" title="Daniel Filho">
       Daniel Filho
      </a>
      . In 2001 Rede Globo produced an acclaimed adaptation of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Os_Maias" title="Os Maias">
        Os Maias
       </a>
      </i>
      as a television serial in 40 episodes.
     </p>
     <p>
      A movie adaptation of
      <i>
       O Mistrio da Estrada de Sintra
      </i>
      was produced in 2007.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The director had shortly before directed a series inspired in a
      <a href="/wiki/Whodunit" title="Whodunit">
       whodunit
      </a>
      involving the descendants of the original novel's characters (
      <i>
       Nome de Cdigo Sintra
      </i>
      , Code Name Sintra), and some of the historical flashback scenes (reporting to the book's events) of the series were used in the new movie. The movie was more centered on Ea's and Ramalho Ortigo's writing and publishing of the original serial and the controversy it created and less around the book's plot itself.
     </p>
