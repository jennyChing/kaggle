     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Pancho Villa Expedition
      </b>
      now known officially in the United States as the
      <b>
       Mexican Expedition
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history.army.mil_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-history.army.mil-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but originally referred to as the
      <b>
       "Punitive Expedition, U.S. Army"
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjp4_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-jjp4-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was a
      <a href="/wiki/Military_operation" title="Military operation">
       military operation
      </a>
      conducted by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
       United States Army
      </a>
      against the paramilitary forces of
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexican
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">
       revolutionary
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Pancho_Villa" title="Pancho Villa">
       Francisco "Pancho" Villa
      </a>
      from March 14, 1916, to February 7, 1917, during the
      <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">
       Mexican Revolution
      </a>
      19101920.
     </p>
     <p>
      The expedition was launched in retaliation for Villa's
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Columbus_(1916)" title="Battle of Columbus (1916)">
       attack
      </a>
      on the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_New_Mexico" title="Columbus, New Mexico">
       Columbus, New Mexico
      </a>
      , and was the most remembered event of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Border_War_(1910-1919)" title="Border War (1910-1919)">
       Border War
      </a>
      . The declared objective of the expedition by the
      <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">
       Wilson administration
      </a>
      was the capture of Villa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archives.gov1_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-archives.gov1-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite successfully locating and defeating the main body of Villa's command, responsible for the raid on Columbus, U.S. forces were unable to prevent Villa's escape and so the main objective of the U.S. incursion was not achieved.
     </p>
     <p>
      The active search for Villa ended after a month in the field when troops sent by
      <a href="/wiki/Venustiano_Carranza" title="Venustiano Carranza">
       Venustiano Carranza
      </a>
      , the head of the Constitutionalist faction of the revolution and now the head of the Mexican government, resisted the U.S. incursion. The Constitutionalist forces used arms at the town of Parral to resist passage of a U.S. Army column. The U.S. mission was changed to prevent further attacks on it by Mexican troops and to plan for war in the eventuality it broke out.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyrulik_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cyrulik-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When war was averted diplomatically, the expedition remained in Mexico until February 1917 to encourage Carranza's government to pursue Villa and prevent further raids across the border.
     </p>
     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pancho_Villa_Expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Columbus_(1916)" title="Battle of Columbus (1916)">
       Battle of Columbus (1916)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Trouble between the United States and Pancho Villa had been growing since October 1915, when the United States government officially recognized Villa's rival and former ally
      <a href="/wiki/Venustiano_Carranza" title="Venustiano Carranza">
       Venustiano Carranza
      </a>
      as head of the government of Mexico. The U.S. also provided rail transportation through the United States, from Eagle Pass, Texas to Douglas, Arizona, for the movement of more than 5,000 Carrancista forces to fight Villa at the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Agua_Prieta" title="Second Battle of Agua Prieta">
       Battle of Agua Prieta
      </a>
      ; Villa's seasoned
      <i>
       Divisin del Norte
      </i>
      was smashed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Feeling betrayed, Villa began attacking U.S. nationals and their property in northern Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua7273_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua7273-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On January 11, 1916, sixteen American employees of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASARCO" title="ASARCO">
       American Smelting and Refining Company
      </a>
      were removed from a train near
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Isabel,_Chihuahua" title="Santa Isabel, Chihuahua">
       Santa Isabel, Chihuahua
      </a>
      and summarily stripped and executed. Brigadier General
      <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">
       John J. Pershing
      </a>
      , commanding the district headquartered at
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bliss" title="Fort Bliss">
       Fort Bliss, Texas
      </a>
      , received information that Villa with a new force was on the border and about to make an attack that would force the United States to intervene, embarrassing the Carranza government.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Raids were so commonplace, however, that the rumor was not seen as credible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua7273_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua7273-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      But at about 4:00 am on March 9, 1916, Villa's troops attacked Columbus, New Mexico, and
      <a href="/wiki/Village_of_Columbus_and_Camp_Furlong" title="Village of Columbus and Camp Furlong">
       Camp Furlong
      </a>
      , the U.S. Army post there, where four troops of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/13th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="13th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       13th Cavalry Regiment
      </a>
      had been stationed since September 1912. Ten civilians and eight soldiers were killed in the attack, and two civilians and six soldiers wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The raiders burned the town, stole horses and mules, and seized
      <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">
       machine guns
      </a>
      , ammunition and merchandise, before fleeing back to Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua7273_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua7273-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, Villa's troops had suffered considerable losses, with at least sixty-seven dead and dozens more wounded. Many of the casualties were inflicted when the machine gun troop of the 13th Cavalry led by
      <a href="/wiki/Second_lieutenant" title="Second lieutenant">
       2nd Lt.
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/John_P._Lucas" title="John P. Lucas">
       John P. Lucas
      </a>
      set up its
      <a href="/wiki/Hotchkiss_M1909_Ben%C3%A9t%E2%80%93Merci%C3%A9_machine_gun" title="Hotchkiss M1909 BentMerci machine gun">
       Hotchkiss M1909 BentMerci machine guns
      </a>
      under fire along the north boundary of Camp Furlong, firing over 5,000 rounds apiece using the glow of burning buildings to illuminate targets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      About thirteen of Villa's wounded later died of their wounds, and five wounded Villistas taken prisoner by the Americans were tried and hanged for murder. Local lore in Columbus holds that the attack may have been caused by a merchant in Columbus who supplied Villa with arms and ammunition. Villa is said to have paid several thousand dollars in cash for the weapons, but the merchant refused to deliver them unless he was paid in gold, giving "cause" for the raid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua7273_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua7273-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The next day, acting on the recommendations of the commanders of his cavalry regiments, Southern Department commanding general
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Funston" title="Frederick Funston">
       Frederick Funston
      </a>
      recommended an immediate pursuit in force into Mexico. U.S. President
      <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">
       Woodrow Wilson
      </a>
      concurred, designating Pershing to command the force and releasing a statement to the press:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       An adequate force will be sent at once in pursuit of Villa with the single object of capturing him and putting a stop to his forays. This can and will be done in entirely friendly aid to the constituted authorities in Mexico and with scrupulous respect for the sovereignty of that Republic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Expedition">
       Expedition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pancho_Villa_Expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expedition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pursuit_phase">
       Pursuit phase
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pancho_Villa_Expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pursuit phase">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg">
        <img alt="VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1566" data-file-width="885" height="708" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg/400px-VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg/600px-VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg/800px-VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VillistasvstheUnitedStates1915-1920.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Pershing assembled an expeditionary force consisting primarily of cavalry and horse artillery, the cavalry units armed with M1909 machine guns,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1903_Springfield_rifle" title="M1903 Springfield rifle">
       M1903 Springfield rifles
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/M1911_pistol" title="M1911 pistol">
       M1911 semi-automatic pistols
      </a>
      . On March 15, 1916,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marcovitz2002_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Marcovitz2002-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      organized into a
      <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">
       provisional division
      </a>
      of three provisional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigades" title="Brigades">
       brigades
      </a>
      (four
      <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">
       regiments
      </a>
      of cavalry,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      two of infantry, and 3,900 men), the expedition crossed the border into Mexico to search for Villa, marching in two columns from Columbus and Culberson's Ranch.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 2nd Provisional Cavalry Brigade reached
      <a href="/wiki/Colonia_Dubl%C3%A1n" title="Colonia Dubln">
       Colonia Dubln
      </a>
      after dark on March 17, where Pershing established the main base of operations for the campaign. The
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="1st Reconnaissance Squadron">
       1st Aero Squadron
      </a>
      , which had departed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">
       San Antonio, Texas
      </a>
      , on March 13 by rail with eight
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curtiss_JN-3" title="Curtiss JN-3">
       Curtiss JN-3
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">
       airplanes
      </a>
      , flew the first aerial reconnaissance of the area from Columbus the day after it arrived, on March 16. The entire squadron flew to the advanced camp at Colonia Dubln on March 1920, losing two aircraft in the process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurst2008_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hurst2008-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Pershing immediately sent the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       7th Cavalry
      </a>
      (seven troops in two squadrons) south just after midnight on March 18 to begin the pursuit, followed by the
      <a href="/wiki/10th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="10th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       10th Cavalry
      </a>
      moving by rail two days later.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua96_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua96-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From March 20 to March 30, as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/11th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="11th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       11th Cavalry
      </a>
      arrived in Columbus by train from
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Oglethorpe_(Fort_Oglethorpe,_Georgia)" title="Fort Oglethorpe (Fort Oglethorpe, Georgia)">
       Fort Oglethorpe, Georgia
      </a>
      and then forced marched into Mexico, Pershing dispatched four additional "flying columns" through the mountainous territory into the gaps between the original three columns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Continuing winter weather through early April, particularly bitterly cold nights at high altitude, made both pursuit and logistics more difficult. An additional regiment of cavalry and two of infantry were added to the expedition in late April,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      bringing its total size to 4,800 men. Ultimately more than 10,000 menvirtually every available unit of the Regular Army and additional National Guard troopswere committed to the expedition either in Mexico or its supporting units at Columbus.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of disputes with the Carranza administration over the use of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico_North_Western_Railway" title="Mexico North Western Railway">
       Mexico North Western Railway
      </a>
      to supply Pershing's troops, the United States Army employed trucks to convoy supplies to the encampment where the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Signal_Corps" title="United States Army Signal Corps">
       Signal Corps
      </a>
      also set up
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_telegraph" title="Wireless telegraph">
       wireless telegraph
      </a>
      service from the border to Pershing's headquarters. This was the first use of truck convoys in a U. S. military operation and provided useful experience for World War I.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During this phase of the campaign Pershing maintained a small mobile headquarters of 30 men using a Dodge touring car for personal transportation, to keep abreast of the moving columns and control their movements, using aircraft of the 1st Aero Squadron as messengers. His headquarters advanced as far as the 1st Aero Squadron's field at
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Javier_de_Satev%C3%B3" title="San Francisco Javier de Satev">
       Satev
      </a>
      , southeast of Chihuahua City, before falling back at the end of April.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Villa had a six-day head start on the pursuit, all but ensuring that his forces would successfully break up into smaller bands and he would be able to hide in the trackless mountains. Nevertheless he was nearly caught by the forced marches of the pursuing cavalry columns when he recklessly paused in his retreat to attack a Carrancista garrison. The
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guerrero" title="Battle of Guerrero">
       Battle of Guerrero
      </a>
      was fought on March 29, 1916, after a 55-mile night march through the snowy
      <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">
       Sierra Madre
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">
       Colonel
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/George_A._Dodd" title="George A. Dodd">
       George A. Dodd
      </a>
      and 370 men of the 7th Cavalry. 360 Villistas had remained in Guerrero celebrating the victory won over the Carrancista garrison
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 160 more were in the next valley in nearby San Ysidro.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dodd's force was unexpected by the Villistas, who hastily dispersed when the U.S. troops appeared on the steep eastern bluffs overlooking the town. Dodd immediately attacked, sending one squadron west around the town to block escape routes and advancing with the other. A planned charge was thwarted when the fatigued horses were unable to attain the proper gait.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During a five-hour pursuit of fleeing Villista elements, over 75 of Villa's men were killed or wounded and he was forced to retreat into the mountains. Only five of the Americans were hurt, none of them fatally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The battle is considered the single most successful engagement of the expedition and possibly the closest Pershing's men came to capturing Villa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boot.2C_pg._199_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boot.2C_pg._199-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After advancing from
      <a href="/wiki/Namiquipa" title="Namiquipa">
       Namiquipa
      </a>
      on March 24 to San Diego del Monte,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua96_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua96-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the 10th Cavalry became isolated from Pershing's headquarters by a fierce snow storm on March 31. A squadron of the 10th marched toward Guerrero after receiving reports of the action there and at midday April 1 a
      <a href="/wiki/Meeting_engagement" title="Meeting engagement">
       meeting engagement
      </a>
      resulted with one of the retreating Villista groups, 150 strong, under Francisco Beltran at a ranch near Agua Caliente. Breaking up into even smaller groups and retreating over a wooded ridge, some of the Villistas attempted to defend themselves behind a stone wall, resulting in what was purported to be the first mounted cavalry charge by U.S. troops since 1898, led by Major
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Young_(United_States_Army)" title="Charles Young (United States Army)">
       Charles Young
      </a>
      . The pursuit lasted until darkness and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffalo_Soldiers" title="Buffalo Soldiers">
       Buffalo Soldiers
      </a>
      killed at least two Villistas left on the field and routed the remainder, without loss.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The action also was the first time the U.S. Army used
      <a href="/wiki/Plunging_fire" title="Plunging fire">
       plunging fire
      </a>
      by machine guns to support an attack.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The columns pushed deeper into Mexico, increasing tensions between the United States and the Carranza government. On April 12, 1916, Major
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Tompkins" title="Frank Tompkins">
       Frank Tompkins
      </a>
      and Troops K and M, 13th Cavalry, numbering 128 men, were
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Parral" title="Battle of Parral">
       attacked
      </a>
      by an estimated 500 Mexican troops as they were leaving the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Parral,_Chihuahua" title="Parral, Chihuahua">
       Parral
      </a>
      , 513 miles into Mexico and almost to the state of
      <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">
       Durango
      </a>
      , following violent protests by the civilian populace. Tompkins had been personally ordered to avoid a straight-up engagement with de facto government troops to prevent war between the countries
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and so used a rear guard to keep the Carrancistas at a distance during a retreat to his starting point, the fortified village of Santa Cruz de Villegas. Two Americans were killed in the skirmishing, one was missing from the rear guard, and another six were wounded, while the Carrancistas lost between fourteen and seventy men, according to conflicting accounts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annualreportwar_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-annualreportwar-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The battle marked a turning point in the campaign. Military opposition by Carranza forced a halt in further pursuit while diplomatic conversations took place by both nations to avoid war. Only four days earlier, on April 8, Army Chief of Staff General
      <a href="/wiki/Hugh_L._Scott" title="Hugh L. Scott">
       Hugh L. Scott
      </a>
      had expressed to Secretary of War
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton_Baker" title="Newton Baker">
       Newton Baker
      </a>
      that Pershing had virtually accomplished his mission and that it was "not dignified for the United States to be hunting one man in a foreign country." Baker concurred and so advised Wilson, but following the fight at Parral the administration refused to withdraw the expedition, not wanting to be seen as caving in to Mexican pressure during an election year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyrulik2_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cyrulik2-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Instead, on April 21 Pershing ordered a withdrawal of the four columns that had converged near Parral to
      <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Cuauht%C3%A9moc,_Chihuahua" title="Ciudad Cuauhtmoc, Chihuahua">
       San Antonio de Los Arenales
      </a>
      , and a week later assigned the cavalry regiments, including the newly arrived
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="5th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       5th Cavalry
      </a>
      , to five districts established in central Chihuahua in which to patrol and seek out the smaller bands.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2526_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-p2526-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While executing the withdrawal order, Dodd and a portion of the 7th Cavalry fought an engagement on April 22 with about 200 Villistas under Candelaro Cervantes at the small village of Tomochic. As the Americans entered the village, the Mexicans opened fire from the surrounding hills. Dodd first sent patrols out to engage the Villistas'
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_guard" title="Rear guard">
       rear guard
      </a>
      , to the east of Tomochic, and after they were "scattered" the main body was located on a plain, to the north of town, and brought into action. Skirmishing continued, but after dark the Villistas retreated and the Americans moved into Tomochic. The 7th Cavalry lost two men killed and four wounded, while Dodd reported his men had killed at least thirty Villistas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Patrol_district_actions">
       Patrol district actions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pancho_Villa_Expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Patrol district actions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The five districts that Pershing established west of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Central_Railway" title="Mexican Central Railway">
       Mexican Central Railway
      </a>
      on April 29, 1916 were:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Namiquipa District (10th Cavalry) south of the 30th parallel to Namiquipa;
      </li>
      <li>
       Bustillos District (13th Cavalry), below the eastern part of Namiquipa District around Laguna Bustillos to San Antonio de Los Arenales and Chihuahua City;
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Guerrero,_Chihuahua" title="Vicente Guerrero, Chihuahua">
        Guerrero
       </a>
       District (7th Cavalry), below the western part of Namiquipa District and west of the Bustillos and San Borja Districts;
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_de_Borja" title="San Francisco de Borja">
        San Borja
       </a>
       District (11th Cavalry), south of Bustillos District between the Guerrero and Satev Districts to Parral; and
      </li>
      <li>
       Satev District (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="5th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
        5th Cavalry
       </a>
       ), southeast of the Bustillos District and east of the San Borja District, south to Jimenez.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2526_42-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-p2526-42">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         31
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The next significant engagement took place May 5. A small Carrancista garrison at the silver mining town of
      <a href="/wiki/Cusihuiriachi" title="Cusihuiriachi">
       Cusihuiriachic
      </a>
      was attacked by Villa's forces on May 4, prompting the garrison commander to request help from U.S forces at nearby San Antonio. Six troops of the 11th Cavalry,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      its machine gun platoon, and a detachment of
      <a href="/wiki/Apache_Scouts" title="Apache Scouts">
       Apache Scouts
      </a>
      , totaling 14 officers and 319 men, began a night march under Major
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lee_Howze" title="Robert Lee Howze">
       Robert L. Howze
      </a>
      . Arriving at Cusihuirischic, Howze found that 140 Villistas under Julio Acosta had pulled back westward into the mountains to a ranch at Ojos Azules and that the garrison commander had received orders not to cooperate. Howze was delayed three hours in finding a guide and by the time he located the ranch and was deploying to attack, day had broken. When Acosta's guards and Howze's advance guard exchanged fire, Howze with Troop A immediately ordered a charge with pistols through the hacienda. Unable to deploy on line, the charge was made in column of fours and closed with the fleeing elements of Villistas. The other troops deployed to either side of the hacienda attempting to block escape and were supported by plunging fire from the machine gun troop.
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Katz" title="Friedrich Katz">
       Friedrich Katz
      </a>
      called the action the "greatest victory that the Punitive Expedition would achieve." Without a single casualty, the Americans killed forty-four Villistas and wounded many more. The survivors, including Acosta, were dispersed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Also on May 5, several hundred Mexican raiders, under a Villista officer,
      <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Springs_Raid" title="Glenn Springs Raid">
       attacked
      </a>
      the geographically isolated towns of
      <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Springs,_Texas" title="Glenn Springs, Texas">
       Glenn Springs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Boquillas,_Texas" title="Boquillas, Texas">
       Boquillas
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Bend_(Texas)" title="Big Bend (Texas)">
       Big Bend region
      </a>
      of Texas. At Glenn Springs the Mexicans overwhelmed a squad of just nine
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/14th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="14th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       14th Cavalry
      </a>
      troopers guarding the town, set fire to it, then rode on to Boquillas where they killed a boy, robbed the town and took two captives. Local commanders pursued the Mexicans 100 miles into the Mexican state of
      <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">
       Coahuila
      </a>
      to free the captives and regain the stolen property. On May 12, Major George T. Langhorne and two troops from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8th_Cavalry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="8th Cavalry Regiment (United States)">
       8th Cavalry
      </a>
      from Fort Bliss, Texas, soon reinforced by Colonel Frederick Sibley and Troops H and K of the 14th Cavalry from Fort Clark, rescued the captives at El Pino without a fight, and three days later a small detachment of cavalry encountered the raiders at Castillon. Five of the Villistas were killed and two wounded; the Americans had no casualties. The force returned to the United States May 21 after ten days in Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <span id="1stmotorraid">
      </span>
      On May 14, 2nd Lt.
      <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">
       George S. Patton
      </a>
      raided the San Miguelito Ranch, near Rubio, Chihuahua. Patton, a Pershing aide and a future
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      general, was out looking to buy some corn from the Mexicans when he came across the ranch of
      <a href="/wiki/Julio_C%C3%A1rdenas" title="Julio Crdenas">
       Julio Crdenas
      </a>
      , an important leader in the Villista military organization. With fifteen men and three
      <a href="/wiki/Dodge" title="Dodge">
       Dodge
      </a>
      touring cars, Patton led America's first motorised military action, in which Crdenas and two other men were shot dead. The young lieutenant then had the three Mexicans strapped to the hood of the cars and driven back to General Pershing's headquarters. Patton is said to have carved three notches into the twin
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colt_Single_Action_Army_handgun" title="Colt Single Action Army handgun">
       Colt Peacemakers
      </a>
      he carried, representing the men he killed that day. General Pershing nicknamed him the "Bandito".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militaryhistory.about.com_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-militaryhistory.about.com-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Villistas launched an attack of their own on May 25. This time a small force of ten men from the 7th Cavalry were out looking for stray
      <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">
       cattle
      </a>
      and correcting maps when they were ambushed by twenty rebels just south of Cruces. One American
      <a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">
       corporal
      </a>
      was killed and two other men were wounded, though they killed two of the "bandit leaders" and drove off the rest.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fp_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fp-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On June 2, Lieutenant James A. Shannon and twenty Apache scouts fought a small skirmish with some of Candelaro Cervantes' men after they stole a few horses from the 5th Cavalry. Shannon and the Apaches found the rebels' trail, which was a week old by then, and followed it for some time until finally catching up with the Mexicans near Las Varas Pass, about forty miles south of
      <a href="/wiki/Namiquipa" title="Namiquipa">
       Namiquipa
      </a>
      . Using the cover of darkness, Shannon and his scouts attacked the Villistas' hideout, killing one of them and wounding another without losses to themselves. The rebel who died was thought to be the leader as he carried a sword during the fight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another skirmish was fought on June 9, north of Pershing's headquarters and the city of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">
       Chihuahua
      </a>
      . Twenty men from the 13th Cavalry encountered an equally small force of Villistas and chased them through Santa Clara Canyon. Three of the Mexicans were killed and the rest escaped. There were no American casualties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fp_57-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-fp-57">

        </span>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On May 9, following a face-to-face meeting in
      <a href="/wiki/El_Paso,_Texas" title="El Paso, Texas">
       El Paso, Texas
      </a>
      with Carranza's Secretary of War and Navy, General
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_Obreg%C3%B3n" title="lvaro Obregn">
       lvaro Obregn
      </a>
      , Funston reacted to Carrancista threats to send a massive force against the expedition's supply lines and forcibly drive it out of Mexico by ordering Pershing to withdraw all his troops from San Antonio de Los Arenales to Colonia Dubln.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hs20_60-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hs20-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the order was rescinded on the evening of May 11 when no evidence of Carrancista troop movements was found, the southernmost supply depots had been closed and materiel sent north that could not easily be turned around.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pershing instead was ordered to halt in place at Namiquipa, making tactical dispositions of his forces there and on El Valle to the north.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However a gradual withdrawal of the expedition to Dubln was effectively underway when on May 19 units of the 10th and 11th Cavalry returned to the base to guard the supply lines with Columbus and conduct reconnaissance in the absence of the grounded 1st Aero Squadron. As the threat of war with the de facto government increased, the northward movement continued. Pershing's headquarters left Namiquipa on June 21, setting up again in Dubln, after which the advanced supply depot at Namiquipa closed June 23.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      June 29 found the expedition concentrated on the main base and a forward camp at El Valle 60 miles to the south.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The last and most costly engagement of the Mexican Expedition was fought on June 21 when 3 officers and 87 men of Troops C and K of the 10th Cavalry, sent separately to scout Carrancista dispositions reported along the Mexican Central Railway, combined into a single column and encountered a blocking force of 300 soldiers. They were soundly defeated at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Carrizal" title="Battle of Carrizal">
       Battle of Carrizal
      </a>
      , with Captain Charles T. Boyd, 1st Lt. Henry R. Adair, and ten enlisted men killed,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ten wounded and another 24 (23 soldiers and 1 civilian guide) taken prisoner. The remainder, including the sole surviving officer, Capt. Lewis S. Morey, were rescued four days later by a relief squadron of the 11th Cavalry. The Mexicans did not do much better; they reported the loss of 24 men killed and 43 wounded, including their commander, General Flix Uresti Gmez, while Pershing listed 42 Carrancistas killed and 51 wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjp94_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-jjp94-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When General Pershing learned of the battle he was furious and asked for permission to attack the Carrancista garrison of Chihuahua City. President Wilson refused, knowing that it would certainly start a war.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history.army.mil_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-history.army.mil-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annualreportwar_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-annualreportwar-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The action at Parral in April had made the destruction of Villa and his troops secondary to the objective of preventing further attacks on U.S. forces by Carrancistas and preparing contingencies in the event of war.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyrulik_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cyrulik-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The battle at Carrizal brought the countries to the brink of war and forced both governments to make immediate overt gestures clearly showing their intent to avoid it. Although the United States deployed 100,000 troops on the border, by July 4 the major crisis had passed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Punitive Expedition, U.S. Army remained at Colonia Dubln indefinitely as a fixed-base operation to be a negative incentive to the Carranza government to take seriously its obligation to catch Villa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Carranza government proved unequal to the task but nevertheless U.S. operations inside Mexico virtually ceased over the next six months.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A Joint High Commission for negotiations with the Carranza government was agreed upon in July, and the first of 52 sessions met on September 6 in
      <a href="/wiki/New_London,_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">
       New London, Connecticut
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the commission reached accord on all issues, the negotiations failed to result in a formal agreement for withdrawal of U.S. forces signed by the Mexican government. Despite this, Pershing was ordered on January 18, 1917 to prepare the expedition for return to the United States, which was executed between January 28 and February 5.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the expedition made a dozen successful contacts with Villista groups in the first two months of the campaign, killing many of his important subordinates and 169 of his men, all of whom had participated in the attack on Columbus,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jjp9497_74-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jjp9497-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it failed in its other major objective of capturing Villa. However, between the date of the American withdrawal and Villa's retirement in 1920, Villa's troops were unable to bring down the government or successfully raid the United States.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="National_Guard_call-ups">
       National Guard call-ups
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pancho_Villa_Expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National Guard call-ups">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Between June 1915 and June 1916 raiders from Mexico attacked persons on American soil 38 times, resulting in the deaths of 26 soldiers and 11 civilians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hua7273_8-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-hua7273-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reacting to the Glenn Springs raid, the Army transferred three regiments of regulars (the
      <a href="/wiki/14th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="14th Infantry Regiment (United States)">
       14th Infantry
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/21st_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="21st Infantry Regiment (United States)">
       21st Infantry
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/6th_Field_Artillery_Regiment" title="6th Field Artillery Regiment">
       6th Field Artillery
      </a>
      ) to the border and also called up
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Guard" title="United States National Guard">
       state militia
      </a>
      units from Texas,
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
       Arizona
      </a>
      , and New Mexico on May 8.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hs20_60-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hs20-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archives.gov2_75-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-archives.gov2-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On June 15, 1916, another
      <a href="/wiki/San_Ygnacio_Raid" title="San Ygnacio Raid">
       attempted raid
      </a>
      by Mexican border-crossers, this at
      <a href="/wiki/San_Ygnacio,_Texas" title="San Ygnacio, Texas">
       San Ygnacio, Texas
      </a>
      , 30 miles downstream from
      <a href="/wiki/Laredo,_Texas" title="Laredo, Texas">
       Laredo
      </a>
      , was repulsed by soldiers with casualties to both sides.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result, using powers granted by passage of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Defense_Act" title="National Defense Act">
       National Defense Act of 1916
      </a>
      , which established the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Guard" title="United States National Guard">
       United States National Guard
      </a>
      , Wilson on June 18 fully mobilized Guard units from the remainder of the states and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">
       District of Columbia
      </a>
      for duty on the border.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      More than 140,000 National Guard troops were called up,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but only two regiments, the 1st New Mexico Infantry and the
      <a href="/wiki/182nd_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="182nd Infantry Regiment (United States)">
       2nd Massachusetts Infantry
      </a>
      , were assigned to the Mexican Expedition, and those to guard the base at Columbus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Historian Clarence Clendenen asserts that although no Guard units crossed into Mexico at any time, individuals from the two regiments at Columbus did enter the country to perform various tasks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
       <a href="#cite_note-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Wide differences in proficiency existed between various guard units in training, leadership, and equipment, and for the most part units came to the border with only basic drilling as experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">
       <a href="#cite_note-81">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Units were initially assigned as static guards for railroad bridges and border crossing points, but as training made them more proficient, increasing responsibility for the patrolling the border resulted in encounters with smugglers and bandits who still posed an occasional threat. Records of the Utah National Guard indicate three skirmishes resulting in an exchange of fire after it arrived at Camp Stephen J. Little on the Arizona border in July 1916. The final action of the three, occurring January 26, 1917, resulted in an all-day border skirmish between Utah cavalrymen and Mexicans in which the guardsmen were reinforced and ten Mexicans were killed or wounded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82">
       <a href="#cite_note-82">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83">
       <a href="#cite_note-83">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        n 16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While incapable of conducting organized combat operations with other units, the border security mission proved an excellent training environment for the officers and men of the National Guard, who were again inducted into federal service after the United States
      <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_war_by_the_United_States" title="Declaration of war by the United States">
       declared war
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      in April 1917. Many National Guard leaders in both World Wars traced their first federal service to the Mexican Expedition. In their history of the call-up, Charles Harris and Louis Sadler reveal the significance of the call-up:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "Between June 1916 and April 1917 the guard received intensive field training. Units from different states were sometimes grouped into large provisional units. Not only did the men become more proficient, but many officers gained invaluable experience commanding large formations. At the same time the guard was receiving badly needed equipment and supplies. The great call-up transformed the national guard into a much more effective fighting force, for it was as close as the United States came to the large-scale military maneuvers in which European armies traditionally engaged."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84">
        <a href="#cite_note-84">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         68
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">
       Aftermath
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pancho_Villa_Expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After U.S. forces were withdrawn in January 1917, Pershing publicly claimed the expedition to be a success, which in light of the public declarations by President Wilson was clearly not the case since Villa eluded capture by the U.S. Army. Pershing complained privately to his family that Wilson had imposed too many restrictions, which made it impossible for him to fulfill that portion of his mission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-militaryhistory.about.com_55-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-militaryhistory.about.com-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He admitted to having been "outwitted and out-bluffed at every turn"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West2012_85-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-West2012-85">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and wrote that "when the true history is written, it will not be a very inspiring chapter for school children, or even grownups to contemplate. Having dashed into Mexico with the intention of eating the Mexicans raw, we turned back at the first repulse and are now sneaking home under cover, like a whipped curr with its tail between its legs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West2012_85-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-West2012-85">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Pershing was permitted to bring into New Mexico 527 Chinese refugees who had assisted him during the expedition, despite the ban on Chinese immigration at that time under the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Exclusion_Act" title="Chinese Exclusion Act">
       Chinese Exclusion Act
      </a>
      . The Chinese refugees, known as "Pershing's Chinese", were allowed to remain in the U.S. if they worked under the supervision of the military as cooks and servants on bases. In 1921, Congress passed Public Resolution 29, which allowed them to remain in the country permanently under the conditions of the 1892
      <a href="/wiki/Geary_Act" title="Geary Act">
       Geary Act
      </a>
      . Most of them settled in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">
       San Antonio
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">
       <a href="#cite_note-86">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Soldiers who took part in the Villa campaign were awarded the
      <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Service_Medal" title="Mexican Service Medal">
       Mexican Service Medal
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87">
       <a href="#cite_note-87">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
