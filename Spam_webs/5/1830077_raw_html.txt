      <h2 class="storytitle">
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/from-belle-epoch-to-disco-how-the-plaza-dressed-up-for-the-70s-in-american-hustle/" rel="bookmark">
        From Belle Epoch to Disco  How The Plaza Dressed Up for the 70s in American Hustle
       </a>
      </h2>
      <p>
       By Terry Trucco
      </p>
      <div class="meta">
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/category/in-the-news/" rel="category tag">
        In the News
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/category/on-screen/" rel="category tag">
        On Screen
       </a>
       | tags:
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/tag/american-hustle/" rel="tag">
        "American Hustle"
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/tag/heather-loeffler/" rel="tag">
        Heather Loeffler
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/tag/judy-becker/" rel="tag">
        Judy Becker
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/tag/judy-becker-production-designer/" rel="tag">
        Judy Becker production designer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/tag/the-plaza/" rel="tag">
        The Plaza
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/tag/the-plaza-in-movies/" rel="tag">
        The Plaza in movies
       </a>
       |  January 16, 2014
      </div>
      <div class="storycontent">
       <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_7861" style="width: 546px">
        <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/from-belle-epoch-to-disco-how-the-plaza-dressed-up-for-the-70s-in-american-hustle/american-hustle-wallpaper/" rel="attachment wp-att-7861">
         <img alt='Entering the "General Sherman Suite."' class="size-full wp-image-7861" height="358" src="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/wp-content/uploads/American-Hustle-wallpaper.jpg" width="536"/>
        </a>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Entering the General Sherman Suite.
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        From
        <em>
         North by Northwest
        </em>
        and
        <em>
         Sleepless in Seattle
        </em>
        to
        <em>
         Crocodile Dundee
        </em>
        , its hard to imagine a hotel with more Hollywood screen time than
        <a href="http://www.theplazany.com/" target="_blank">
         <strong>
          The Plaza
         </strong>
        </a>
        . Last year alone the hotel racked up vivid cameos in
        <em>
         The Great Gatsby
        </em>
        and
        <a href="http://www.americanhustle-movie.com/site/" target="_blank">
         <strong>
          <em>
           American Hustle.
          </em>
         </strong>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        How vivid? Both films snapped up Academy Award nominations for Production Design.
       </p>
       <p>
        With
        <em>
         American Hustle
        </em>
        burning through theaters with its roller-coaster riff on the Abscam scandal, I checked in with The Plaza to learn how the citys renowned Gilded Age hotel got down for the disco era.
        <span id="more-7843">
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        Turns out The Plaza only posed for exterior shots. (The hotels deep-dish 2009 renovation
       </p>
       <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_7852" style="width: 446px">
        <a href="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/from-belle-epoch-to-disco-how-the-plaza-dressed-up-for-the-70s-in-american-hustle/american-hustle-suite-1/" rel="attachment wp-att-7852">
         <img alt="Flocked wallpaper in a starring role." class="size-full wp-image-7852" height="288" src="http://blog.overnightnewyork.com/wp-content/uploads/American-Hustle-suite-1.jpg" width="436"/>
        </a>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Flocked wallpaper in a starring role.
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        wiped out all traces of the 1970s.) But the hotel worked closely with the production team to make certain no anachronisms, like Lexus SUVs or guests talking on cellphones, were anywhere in sight the day of the shoot. We closed off the front entrance and cleared our cars for a few hours in the early morning so their crew could come in and recreate the entrance a la 1970s. It was a lot of fun actually, a hotel spokesperson says.
       </p>
       <p>
        The hotel interiors, all dizzying hues and flocked wallpaper, were recreated in a studio in Woburn, Massachusetts by production designer
        <a href="http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0065473/" target="_blank">
         <strong>
          Judy Becker,
         </strong>
        </a>
        whose design credits include
        <em>
         Silver Linings Playbook, Brokeback Mountain
        </em>
        and
        <em>
         The Fighter
        </em>
        . The sets included two suites  the crummy suite too humble to impress bigwigs, and the sprawling General Sherman Suite, where the action unfolds.
       </p>
       <p>
        I asked Becker how she dreamed up  and kitted out  The Plaza for its Studio 54 turn.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          Did you study pictures of the Carter-era Plaza or did you simply imagine how those prewar rooms might have looked in the days of flocked wallpaper?
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        We did study pictures of The Plaza rooms during the 60s and 70s. We based our design on this research, then expanded beyond exact literalness to meet the needs of the story and the visual world we were creating.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          How difficult was it to recreate the two suites?
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Its always a challenge when building such a large set to create a realism that will draw the audience in. In that regard we paid enormous attention to detail, the degree of wear and tear the hotel would have had, period accurate dcor and decorative architectural details.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          The flocked wallpaper captures the zeitgeist of the movie. And I love that its navy instead of red.
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        I wanted a high contrast wallpaper so that the flocking would be noticeable and chose navy on a pale blue instead of black and white as more period accurate. Also, blue was a strong part of our palette. The palette we chose for the movie was primarily blue and yellow, based on the period and the glamorous world we were conveying.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          The wallpaper deserves its own credit. Was it made for the movie?
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        My decorator Heather Loeffler found a wallpaper manufacturer who was able to customize the colors of the flocked pattern. We experimented with the navy on several different shades of very pale blue and tested them with the cinematographer and costume designer before coming up with the final version.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          Are there any cool details a viewer might miss?
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        We added a thin line of gold leaf trim to most of the moldings when it seemed appropriate. The viewer might see not this consciously, but subliminally it adds a layer of luxury.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
