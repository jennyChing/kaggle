     <h1 style="max-width: 420px; padding-left: 15px;">
      Work May Ward Off Depression
     </h1>
     <div class="posted-in" style="padding-left: 15px;">
      <p class="text-info">
       <em>
        Posted on June 19, 2014, 6 a.m. in
        <a class="preferred-category" href="/list/news/depression/">
         Depression
        </a>
        <a href="/list/news/lifestyle/">
         Lifestyle
        </a>
        <a href="/list/news/mental_health/">
         Mental Health
        </a>
       </em>
      </p>
      <div class="article-blurb">
       <p class="text-info">
        <em>
         By offering mental health education and prevention programs, many workplaces offer solutions to reduce or eliminate depression among employees.
        </em>
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="pull-right hidden-xs" style="margin-left:10px;">
      <a class="abstract-img-link rgac" href="http://image.shutterstock.com/display_pic_with_logo/816064/134829716/stock-photo-attractive-blond-mature-business-woman-in-her-office-134829716.jpg">
       <img alt="Work May Ward Off Depression" border="0" class="abstract-main" height="200" src="/images/homefeature/140619_businesswoman.png" width="200"/>
      </a>
      <div class="shutterstock">
       <a href="http://image.shutterstock.com/display_pic_with_logo/816064/134829716/stock-photo-attractive-blond-mature-business-woman-in-her-office-134829716.jpg">
        Business woman - image from Shutterstock
       </a>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="visible-xs-block" style="margin-left:10px;">
      <a class="abstract-img-link rgac" href="http://image.shutterstock.com/display_pic_with_logo/816064/134829716/stock-photo-attractive-blond-mature-business-woman-in-her-office-134829716.jpg">
       <img alt="Work May Ward Off Depression" border="0" class="abstract-main" src="/images/homefeature/140619_businesswoman.png"/>
      </a>
      <div class="shutterstock pull-left">
       <a href="http://image.shutterstock.com/display_pic_with_logo/816064/134829716/stock-photo-attractive-blond-mature-business-woman-in-her-office-134829716.jpg">
        Business woman - image from Shutterstock
       </a>
      </div>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      <br/>
     </div>
     <div class="article-indent" style="padding-left: 15px;">
      <p>
       Many of us spend an estimated 60% or more of our waking hours at work. Samuel B Harvey, from the University of New South Wales (Australia), and colleagues completed a meta-analysis of studies of workplace interventions aimed at universal prevention of depression. Nine workplace-based randomized controlled trials (RCT) were identified. The majority of the included studies utilized cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) techniques. Data analysis revealed an overall standardized mean difference between the intervention and control groups was 0.16, sufficient to indicate a small positive effect. The study authors submit that: There is good quality evidence that universally delivered workplace mental health interventions can reduce the level of depression symptoms among workers, encouraging for: Evidence-based workplace interventions should be a key component of efforts to prevent the development of depression among adults.
      </p>
     </div>
     <a class="newslink" href="http://medicalxpress.com/news/2014-05-mental-health.html" target="_blank">
      View news source
     </a>
     <div class="clearfix">
     </div>
     <div class="attribution">
      <p>
       Leona Tan, Min-Jung Wang, Matthew Modini, Sadhbh Joyce, Arnstein Mykletun, Helen Christensen, Samuel B Harvey. Preventing the development of depression at work: a systematic review and meta-analysis of universal interventions in the workplace. BMC Medicine 2014, 12:74.
      </p>
     </div>

		ANTI-AGING TIP OF THE DAY 
	&lt;/h4&gt;
	&lt;div class="tipsy"&gt;
		&lt;div class="tiphd"&gt;

Tip #192 -  Stay Connected
&lt;/div&gt;

&lt;div&gt;

Researchers from the University of Chicago (Illinois, USA) report that social isolation may be detrimental to both mental and physical health.  The team analyzed data from the National Social Life, Health, and Aging Project, a nationwide US study involving 3,000 men and women, ages 57 to 85 years.  They arrived at three key findings regarding the relationships between health and different types of isolation:
&lt;br&gt;&lt;br&gt;
&bull; The researchers found that the most socially connected older adults are three times as likely to report very good or excellent health compared to those who are least connected, regardless of whether they feel isolated.
&lt;br&gt;&lt;br&gt;
&bull; The team found that older adults who feel least isolated are five times as likely to report very good or excellent health as those who feel most isolated, regardless of their actual level of social connectedness. 
&lt;br&gt;&lt;br&gt;
&bull; They determined that social disconnectedness is not related to mental health unless it brings feelings of loneliness and isolation. 
&lt;br&gt;&lt;br&gt;Separately, Rush University Medical Center (Illinois, USA) researchers studied 906 older men and women, testing their motor functions (including grip, pinch strength, balance, and walking) and surveying their social activity, for a period of 5 years.  Those study participants with less social activity were found to have a more rapid rate of motor function decline. Specifically, the team found that every one-point decrease in social activity corresponded to an increase in functional aging of 5 years, translating to a 40% higher risk of death and 65% higher risk of disability.
