      <p>
       Technology.am (Dec. 02, 2009)  On Tuesday, Google announced that it will allow publishers set a limit on the number of articles people are able to read for without charge through its search engine. The progress comes in the midst of an increasing row with media groups which assert the search engine is profiting from indexing online news pages.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="murdoch" class="left" height="300" src="http://www.technology.am/wp-content/uploads/2009/12/murdoch-256x300.jpg" title="murdoch" width="256"/>
       Rupert Murdoch, chairman and chief executive of News Corporation, has been a tough reviewer of Google  and other news aggregators  and is taking into consideration of removing his newspapers content from Googles search.
      </p>
      <p>
       He said: The people who only just pick up everything and run with it  steal our stories, we say they steal our stories  they just take them. Thats Google, thats Microsoft, thats Ask.com, a whole lot of people  they shouldnt have had it free all the time, and I think weve been asleep.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under this most recent proposal, Google said publishers can connect a First Click Free program that lets the Internet firm index website content however prevents Web surfers from having unlimited access once they arrive at the online locales.
      </p>
      <p>
       An Internet users first click leads to the preferred Web page, but attempts to inquire into deeper into a website are routed to fee or registration pages, according to Google.
      </p>
      <p>
       Previously, every click from a user would be treated as free, Google senior business product manager Josh Cohen said in a blog post.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now, weve restructured the program so that publishers can bind users to no more than five pages per day without registering or subscribing.
      </p>
      <p>
       The change announced on Tuesday means Google users might begin considering registration pages pop up when they click for a sixth time on any particular day at websites of publishers using First Click Free, according to Mr Cohen.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Google declaration came as the Mr Murdoch, talking at the two-day meeting hosted by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) to converse journalism in the internet age, said newspapers need to do a better job of persuading consumers that high quality reliable news and information does not come free.
      </p>
      <p>
       Good journalism is a costly commodity, said the 78-year-old, who repeated his intention to begin charging readers of News Corp. newspapers on the Web.
      </p>
