     <p>
      <b>
       Wheat gluten
      </b>
      , also called
      <b>
       seitan
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
       Japanese
      </a>
      : ),
      <b>
       wheat meat
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       gluten meat
      </b>
      , or simply
      <b>
       gluten
      </b>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">
       food
      </a>
      made from
      <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">
       gluten
      </a>
      , the main protein of
      <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">
       wheat
      </a>
      . It is made by washing wheat
      <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">
       flour
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">
       dough
      </a>
      with water until all the
      <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">
       starch
      </a>
      granules have been removed, leaving the sticky insoluble gluten as an elastic mass which is then cooked before being eaten.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Wheat gluten is an alternative to
      <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">
       soybean
      </a>
      -based foods such as
      <a href="/wiki/Tofu" title="Tofu">
       tofu
      </a>
      , which are sometimes used as
      <a href="/wiki/Meat_analogue" title="Meat analogue">
       meat substitutes
      </a>
      . Some types of wheat gluten have a chewy or stringy texture that resembles meat more than other substitutes. Wheat gluten is often used instead of meat in
      <a href="/wiki/Asian_cuisine" title="Asian cuisine">
       Asian
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian_cuisine" title="Vegetarian cuisine">
       vegetarian
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_cuisine" title="Buddhist cuisine">
       Buddhist
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Macrobiotic_diet" title="Macrobiotic diet">
       macrobiotic cuisines
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Mock_duck" title="Mock duck">
       Mock duck
      </a>
      is a common use for wheat gluten.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wheat gluten first appeared during the 6th century as an ingredient for Chinese noodles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sah_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sah-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has historically been popular in the cuisines of China, Japan and other
      <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">
       East
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asian
      </a>
      nations. In Asia, it is commonly found on the menus of restaurants catering primarily to
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhist
      </a>
      customers who do not eat meat.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheat_gluten_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word seitan is of Japanese origin and was coined in 1961 by
      <a href="/wiki/George_Ohsawa" title="George Ohsawa">
       George Ohsawa
      </a>
      , a Japanese advocate of the
      <a href="/wiki/Macrobiotic_diet" title="Macrobiotic diet">
       macrobiotic diet
      </a>
      . The name was given to a wheat gluten product created by Ohsawa's student Kiyoshi Mokutani. In 1962, wheat gluten was sold as seitan in Japan by Marushima Shoyu K.K. It was imported to the West in 1969 by the American company Erewhon. The earliest book that refers to wheat gluten as seitan is
      <i>
       Cooking Good Food
      </i>
      , a cookbook published in 1969.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sah_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sah-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheat_gluten_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Wheat gluten was invented in China, where it has been documented since the 6th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sah_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sah-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was widely consumed by the Chinese as a substitute for meat, especially among adherents of Buddhism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftp_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ftp-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The oldest reference to wheat gluten appears in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Qimin_Yaoshu" title="Qimin Yaoshu">
        Qimin Yaoshu
       </a>
      </i>
      , a Chinese agricultural encyclopedia written by Jia Sixie in 535. The encyclopedia mentions noodles prepared from wheat gluten called
      <i>
       bo duo
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sah_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sah-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Wheat gluten was known as
      <i>
       mian jin
      </i>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">
       Song dynasty
      </a>
      (9601279). Wheat gluten arrived in the West by the 18th century.
      <i>
       De Frumento
      </i>
      , an Italian treatise on wheat from 1745, describes the process of washing wheat flour dough in order to extract the gluten. John Imison wrote an English-language definition of wheat gluten in his
      <i>
       Elements of Science and Art
      </i>
      published in 1803. By the 1830s, Western doctors were recommending wheat gluten in diets for diabetics. In the United States, the
      <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">
       Seventh-day Adventists
      </a>
      promoted the consumption of wheat gluten beginning in the late 19th century. Sanitarium Foods, a company affiliated with
      <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_Kellogg" title="John Harvey Kellogg">
       John Harvey Kellogg
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_Creek_Sanitarium" title="Battle Creek Sanitarium">
       Battle Creek Sanitarium
      </a>
      , advertised wheat gluten in 1882.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sah_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-sah-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese">
       Chinese
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheat_gluten_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chinese">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Wheat gluten, called
      <i>
       min jn
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_character" title="Traditional Chinese character">
       traditional
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BA%B5" title="wiktionary:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AD%8B" title="wiktionary:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_character" title="Simplified Chinese character">
       simplified
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%A2" title="wiktionary:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AD%8B" title="wiktionary:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      , literally "dough tendon"; also spelled
      <i>
       mien chin
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       mien ching
      </i>
      ) is believed to have originated in ancient
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      , as a
      <a href="/wiki/Meat_analogue" title="Meat analogue">
       meat substitute
      </a>
      for adherents of
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      , particularly some
      <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">
       Mahayana
      </a>
      Buddhist monks, who are strict vegetarians (see
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_cuisine" title="Buddhist cuisine">
       Buddhist cuisine
      </a>
      ). One story attributes the invention of imitation meat to chefs who made it for Chinese emperors who, traditionally, observed a week of vegetarianism each year (cf.,
      <i>
       Vegetarian Paradise 2 Menu
      </i>
      , New York City
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <i>
       Harmony Restaurant Menu
      </i>
      , Philadelphia).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Min jn
      </i>
      is often
      <a href="/wiki/Deep_frying" title="Deep frying">
       deep fried
      </a>
      before being cooked in
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">
       Chinese cuisine
      </a>
      , which confers a crispy rind that enhances the texture of the gluten.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are three primary Chinese forms of wheat gluten:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <b>
        Oily/oil-fried gluten
       </b>
       (
       <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B2%B9" title="wikt:">
         
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BA%B5%E7%AD%8B" title="wikt:">
         
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        yu min jn
       </i>
       ): Raw gluten that has been torn into small bits, then deep-fried into small puffy balls of around 35cm in diameter and sold as "imitation abalone". They are golden brown in color, and
       <a href="/wiki/Braising" title="Braising">
        braised
       </a>
       or boiled in a savory soup or stew before eating. They are frequently paired with
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiitake_mushroom" title="Shiitake mushroom">
         xiang gu
        </a>
       </i>
       (black mushrooms).
       <ul>
        <li>
         Larger fried balls of gluten, called
         <i>
          min jn qi
         </i>
         (
         <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
          
         </span>
         ) or
         <i>
          min jn pao
         </i>
         (), which may be up to 5
         <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">
          inches
         </a>
         in diameter, are sometimes seen in
         <a href="/wiki/Asian_supermarket" title="Asian supermarket">
          Asian supermarkets
         </a>
         . These are often stuffed with meat or tofu mixtures and served as a dish called "gluten
         <a href="/wiki/Meatball" title="Meatball">
          meatballs
         </a>
         " (
         <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
          
         </span>
         ,
         <i>
          Min jn ro wn
         </i>
         ) or "gluten stuffed with meat" (
         <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
          
         </span>
         ,
         <i>
          min jn sa ro
         </i>
         ).
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Steamed gluten
       </b>
       (
       <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%92%B8" title="wikt:">
         
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BA%B5%E7%AD%8B" title="wikt:">
         
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        zhng min jn
       </i>
       ): Raw gluten that has been wrapped around itself to form a long
       <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">
        sausage
       </a>
       shape which is then steamed. This type of gluten has a dense texture and ranges from off-white to light greenish grey in color. It is torn open into strips before being used as an ingredient in recipes. When this sausage-shaped gluten is thickly sliced into medallions, the resulting form is called
       <i>
        min ln
       </i>
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BA%B5" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%BC%AA" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
       , literally "gluten wheels"). Larger blocks of steamed gluten are sometimes colored pink and sold as vegetarian "mock ham."
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Baked spongy gluten
       </b>
       (traditional:
       <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">
        <a class="extiw" href="//zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%83%A4%E9%BA%B8" title="zh:">
         
        </a>
       </span>
       ; simplified:
       <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
        <a class="extiw" href="//zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%83%A4%E9%BA%B8" title="zh:">
         
        </a>
       </span>
       ; pinyin:
       <i>
        ko f
       </i>
       ): Similar in texture to a
       <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">
        sponge
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        kao fu
       </i>
       (sometimes labeled in English as "bran puff") is made by
       <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">
        leavening
       </a>
       raw gluten, then baking or steaming it. These are sold as small blocks in Chinese markets and are then diced up and cooked. This type of gluten absorbs its cooking liquid like a sponge and is enjoyed for its "juicy" character. Chinese
       <i>
        kao fu
       </i>
       has a different texture than its Japanese counterpart,
       <i>
        yaki-fu
       </i>
       , due to the relatively larger air bubbles it contains.
       <i>
        Kao fu
       </i>
       is available in fresh, frozen, dehydrated, and canned forms.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      <i>
       Min jn
      </i>
      is also available in Asian grocery stores in canned and jarred forms, often
      <a href="/wiki/Marination" title="Marination">
       marinated
      </a>
      in combination with
      <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">
       peanuts
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">
       mushrooms
      </a>
      . Such canned and jarred gluten is commonly eaten as an accompaniment to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_congee" title="Rice congee">
       congee
      </a>
      (boiled rice porridge) as part of a traditional Chinese breakfast.
     </p>
     <p>
      Freshly prepared
      <i>
       min jn
      </i>
      can be difficult to find in Chinese restaurants other than those specializing in Buddhist or
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian_cuisine" title="Vegetarian cuisine">
       vegetarian cuisine
      </a>
      . Depending on its method of preparation and ingredients used, both fresh and preserved
      <i>
       min jn
      </i>
      can be used to simulate pork, poultry, beef, or even seafood.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">
       Japanese
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheat_gluten_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Japanese">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2752" data-file-width="3088" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG/220px-Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG/330px-Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG/440px-Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Japanese_Wheat_gluten_Fu.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Japanese dry baked wheat gluten
        <i>
         "Fu" (Yaki-Fu, )
        </i>
        looks like
        <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">
         bread
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">
       Japanese cuisine
      </a>
      , the traditional type of wheat gluten is called
      <i>
       fu
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
       
      </span>
      (
      <a class="extiw" href="//ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%BA%A9" title="ja:">
       ja
      </a>
      ), lit. "gluten"), originated from
      <a href="/wiki/Jiangnan" title="Jiangnan">
       Jiangnan
      </a>
      dialect for
      <i>
       <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
        ed khaw-fu
       </span>
      </i>
      . In Japan, the two main types of
      <i>
       fu
      </i>
      are most widely used in Buddhist vegetarian cooking (
      <i>
       Shojin ryori
      </i>
      ) and tea ceremony cuisine (
      <i>
       cha-kaiseki 
      </i>
      )(
      <a class="extiw" href="//ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%87%90%E7%9F%B3" title="ja:">
       ja
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      There are two main forms of
      <i>
       fu
      </i>
      , the raw
      <i>
       nama-fu
      </i>
      , and the dry
      <i>
       yaki-fu
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <b>
        Raw
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        nama-fu
       </i>
       <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       ): Solid gluten is mixed with
       <a href="/wiki/Glutinous_rice" title="Glutinous rice">
        glutinous rice
       </a>
       flour and
       <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">
        millet
       </a>
       and steamed in large blocks. It may be shaped and colored in a variety of ways, using ingredients such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mugwort" title="Mugwort">
        mugwort
       </a>
       . Popular shapes include autumn-colored maple leaves,
       <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">
        bunnies
       </a>
       , and other generally "various" forms. Such shapes and colors enhance the attractiveness of the cooked product since steamed gluten has an unappealing grey tone.
       <i>
        Nama-fu
       </i>
       is an important ingredient in
       <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_cuisine" title="Buddhist cuisine">
        Shjin-ryri
       </a>
       , the Buddhist vegetarian cuisine of Japan. It may also be used as an ingredient in
       <a href="/wiki/Wagashi" title="Wagashi">
        wagashi
       </a>
       , Japanese confectionery.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Fu-manj (
         <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
          
         </span>
         ) is a type of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manju_(confection)" title="Manju (confection)">
          manju
         </a>
         made from
         <i>
          nama-fu
         </i>
         . Solid gluten is sweetened and filled with various sweet fillings such as
         <a href="/wiki/Red_bean_paste" title="Red bean paste">
          red bean paste
         </a>
         . They are then wrapped in leaves and steamed in a manner similar to that used to prepare Chinese
         <a href="/wiki/Zongzi" title="Zongzi">
          zongzi
         </a>
         .
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Dry baked
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        yaki-fu
       </i>
       <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       or
       <i>
        sukiyaki-fu
       </i>
       <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       ): The gluten is leavened with baking powder and baked into long bread-like sticks. It is often sold in cut form, as hard dry discs resembling
       <a href="/wiki/Crouton" title="Crouton">
        croutons
       </a>
       or bread
       <a href="/wiki/Rusk" title="Rusk">
        rusk
       </a>
       . Yaki-fu is typically added to
       <a href="/wiki/Miso_soup" title="Miso soup">
        miso soup
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sukiyaki" title="Sukiyaki">
        sukiyaki
       </a>
       , where it absorbs some of the broth and acquires a fine texture that is lighter and fluffier than its Chinese equivalent. It is the most commonly available type of
       <i>
        fu
       </i>
       in Japanese supermarkets.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      In Japan, seitan, initially a rather salty macrobiotic seasoning that gradually evolved into a food, is not well known or widely available, despite the macrobiotic diet's Japanese origins. When used, the terms for this food are rendered in
      <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">
       katakana
      </a>
      as
      <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
       
      </span>
      (Romanized "gurutenmto," from the
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      "gluten meat"), or, rarely,
      <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
       
      </span>
      ("seitan"). Outside macrobiotic circles, these terms are virtually unknown in Japan, and they do not typically appear in Japanese dictionaries.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnamese">
       Vietnamese
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_Vietnam" title="Cuisine of Vietnam">
       Vietnam
      </a>
      , wheat gluten is called
      <i>
       m cng
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       m cn
      </i>
      , and is prepared in a similar fashion to Chinese
      <i>
       min jn
      </i>
      . Along with tofu, it is a part of the Buddhist

     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Macrobiotic">
       Macrobiotic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wheat_gluten_(food)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Macrobiotic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Seitan
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">
       neologism
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
       Japanese
      </a>
      origin, is a food made from wheat gluten in the
      <a href="/wiki/Macrobiotic_diet" title="Macrobiotic diet">
       macrobiotic
      </a>
      system of cooking and health, as formulated by the Japanese-born philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/George_Ohsawa" title="George Ohsawa">
       George Ohsawa
      </a>
      (18931966). According to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
      </i>
      , it is said to have been coined by Ohsawa in the early 1960s, but its etymology is uncertain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first syllable may be from  (sei, "be, become"),  (sei, "proper, correct"), or  (sei, "made of"), while the second syllable is from  (tan, from  (tanpaku, "protein")).
     </p>
     <p>
      The meaning of the word "seitan" has undergone a gradual evolution. The initial product, imported from Japan in 1969 was a very salty seasoning, the color of soy sauce, sold in a small glass jar or plastic pouch, to be used as a seasoning for brown rice. The name gradually came to refer to any wheat gluten seasoned with soy sauce. The people most responsible for this change are Nik and Joanne Amartseff, who invented Tan Pups in 1972 and John Weissman, who in 1974 invented Wheatmeat (first meatballs then cutlets made of seitan) in Boston. All worked for years to popularize these pioneering products at the Erewhon retail store, and developed a trademark on the Wheatmeat name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :191-194
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As prepared in macrobiotic practice, seitan consists of powdered wheat
      <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">
       gluten
      </a>
      , which is extracted from
      <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">
       whole wheat
      </a>
      flour by washing the flour and rinsing away the
      <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">
       starch
      </a>
      . (A lower quality product can be made from vital wheat gluten, also called powdered gluten or gluten flour). The wheat protein is then mixed with just enough water to form a stiff paste, which is then kneaded in order to produce a firm, stringy texture. The dough is then cut into pieces and cooked via steaming, boiling, frying, or other methods. While wheat gluten is itself rather flavorless, it holds a marinade very well and is usually simmered in a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dashi" title="Dashi">
        dashi
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Broth" title="Broth">
       broth
      </a>
      ) made from
      <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">
       soy sauce
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kombu" title="Kombu">
       kombu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">
       ginger
      </a>
      , and sometimes also
      <a href="/wiki/Sesame_oil" title="Sesame oil">
       sesame oil
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

          </i>
         </small>
        </span>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Since the mid-20th century, wheat gluten (generally known by its macrobiotic name, seitan) has been increasingly adopted by vegetarians in Western nations as a meat alternative.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is sold in block, strip and shaped forms in
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      , where it can be found in some supermarkets, Asian food markets,
      <a href="/wiki/Health_food_store" title="Health food store">
       health food stores
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">
       cooperatives
      </a>
      . Some companies also sell powdered gluten (marketed under the names "vital wheat gluten" or "gluten flour"), for those who wish to make their own gluten from scratch. It is important to distinguish the two; vital wheat gluten is the product used for making seitan, but it can be mislabeled as gluten flour. Properly-labeled gluten flour has more gluten than normal flour, but not enough for seitan. Properly-labeled vital wheat gluten typically contains 75% or more protein. The distinction is important, as gluten flour will produce a consistency more similar to dumplings than to seitan. Wheat gluten is also used by bakers to increase the chewiness of breads.
     </p>
     <p>
      The block form of seitan is often flavored with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiitake_mushroom" title="Shiitake mushroom">
       shiitake
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portobello_mushroom" title="Portobello mushroom">
       portobello
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">
       mushrooms
      </a>
      , fresh
      <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">
       coriander
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">
       onion
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Barbecue_sauce" title="Barbecue sauce">
       barbecue sauce
      </a>
      , or packed in a vegetable-based broth. In strip form, it can be packed to be eaten right out of the package as a high-protein snack. Shaped seitan products, in the form of "ribs" and patties, are frequently flavored with barbecue,
      <a href="/wiki/Teriyaki" title="Teriyaki">
       teriyaki
      </a>
      , or other savory
      <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">
       sauces
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Additional uses of wheat gluten include the partial use in products such as
      <a href="/wiki/Morningstar_Farms" title="Morningstar Farms">
       Morningstar Farms
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/LightLife" title="LightLife">
       LightLife
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gardein" title="Gardein">
       Gardein
      </a>
      , and, most famously,
      <a href="/wiki/Tofurkey" title="Tofurkey">
       Tofurkey
      </a>
      , which is known for its mock-turkey
      <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">
       Thanksgiving
      </a>
      meal. Wheat gluten is also used by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_African_Hebrew_Israelites_of_Jerusalem" title="The African Hebrew Israelites of Jerusalem">
       The African Hebrew Israelites of Jerusalem
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegan" title="Vegan">
       vegan
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">
       African American
      </a>
      religious sect that operates a chain of restaurants called Soul Vegetarian, to produce a vegetarian sandwich called the Garvey Burger. In North America, there are also several brand-name meat alternatives, such as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Protein_Chef&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Protein Chef (page does not exist)">
       Protein Chef
      </a>
      , which are used in the restaurant and food service markets.
     </p>

      by

      </a>
