            <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
             <a href="http://gurneyjourney.blogspot.ro/2015/07/transparency-and-reflections.html">
              Transparency and Reflections
             </a>
            </h3>
            <div class="post-header">
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="post-body entry-content">
             The blue and white placemats are printed with ads for local businesses.
             <div>
              <div>
               <table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; text-align: center;">
                <tbody>
                 <tr>
                  <td style="text-align: center;">
                   <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-UVWsYOtKEFI/VaRE08XgATI/AAAAAAAAb-M/dUQOCGDUOTE/s1600/Reflections.sm.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                    <img border="0" height="252" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-UVWsYOtKEFI/VaRE08XgATI/AAAAAAAAb-M/dUQOCGDUOTE/s400/Reflections.sm.jpg" width="400"/>
                   </a>
                  </td>
                 </tr>
                 <tr>
                  <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                   <i>
                    Reflections,
                   </i>
                   gouache (black, white, ultra blue, raw umber), 5x8 inches
                  </td>
                 </tr>
                </tbody>
               </table>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                The creamer and the juice glass distort the patterns of the placemats in different ways. One reflects them, and the other refracts them.
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                To the left of the base of the juice glass, there's an arc of light, a
                <a href="http://gurneyjourney.blogspot.com/2008/06/caustics.html">
                 caustic projection
                </a>
                of window light.
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-H4t2HPhMqL4/VaREz8N1tHI/AAAAAAAAb-E/SJG35r_GlT0/s1600/Diner%2Bin%2Bprog.sm.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="400" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-H4t2HPhMqL4/VaREz8N1tHI/AAAAAAAAb-E/SJG35r_GlT0/s400/Diner%2Bin%2Bprog.sm.JPG" width="266"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                Here's what I'm looking as I put in my order for the turkey burger deluxe (+ grilled onions, lettuce, tomato and a side of fries). When Ann brings the plates steaming from the kitchen, I lift up the art studio and set it on the napkin holder.
               </div>

             A lot of you have asked which is my favorite brand of gouache, and I have to answer that I can't say yet. I've been trying out a lot of different brands, such as:
             <br/>
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0044TTG76/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B0044TTG76&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=NVYLJEYU6PZW7WS3">

             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001G7PCTU/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B001G7PCTU&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=3AYUQS5LOAZQCVWC">
              Holbein
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000NB2IDS/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000NB2IDS&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=ZOFYQDGLI2KLKYBC">
              Winsor and Newton
             </a>
             ,

              Utrecht
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B004WTUP8C/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B004WTUP8C&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=RWECREN7QNZS6END">
              Lukas Gouache
             </a>

             all the time.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Adrian Weber, a fine-art product developer from the Caran D'Ache company in Geneva, wrote me with further answers to the questions I posed to representatives of all the gouache manufacturers. I had inquired what ingredients they used and what qualities they thought were important. (See their answers in my post about
             <a href="http://gurneyjourney.blogspot.com/2015/06/gouache-ingredients-info-from.html">
              Gouache Ingredients
             </a>
             )
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Mr. Weber started by offering three important "gouache criteria":
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "
             <b>
              A.      Opacity
             </b>
             : As you say, our definition of  gouache  (from Italian  guazzo ) means a rather opaque, covering  sauce  with a certain covering power on dark undergrounds."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "
             <b>
              B.      Water-solubility
             </b>
             : According to our definition, gouache needs to be water soluble and stay water soluble. We do not understand how other producers can label colours writing things like :  Acrylic Gouache . It cant be both! Either its acrylic and permanent after drying (I call it a  liquid plastic  that hardens when water evaporates ; polymerization), or its gouache and you can always dissolve layers underneath."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             <b>
              "C.      Matt surface
             </b>
             : Typical for a good gouache should be its matt aspect when dried. It should be close to the pure pigment powder aspect and by this make a distinction to satinated, creamy or oily aspects of other types. This characteristic makes gouache so unique and essential for studying and teaching  colour mixing  based on primaries etc.!"
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "To clarify this, I think you need to explain to your readers the terms  Gouache ,  Plakatfarbe [in English we'd say "poster paint"] , and  Tempera  that all mean a kind of opaque water colour."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "Even in our own range, weve known and still know different types of  gouache . We only speak for our own gouaches:"
             <br/>
             <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-JxiYuiOUkP8/VaO8b41M6LI/AAAAAAAAb9Y/FUPvxsey5iM/s1600/gouache%2Bpan%2Bsets.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em; text-align: center;">
              <img border="0" height="133" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-JxiYuiOUkP8/VaO8b41M6LI/AAAAAAAAb9Y/FUPvxsey5iM/s200/gouache%2Bpan%2Bsets.jpg" width="200"/>
             </a>
             <br/>
             <b>
              "1.       Gouache in tablets/cakes (dry)
             </b>
             <br/>
             To produce our dry gouache tablets, we extrude them with pressure from a highly pigmented, humide paste (unlike most other producer from dry powder similar to medical pills). In order to provide the right consistency and to make it extrudable, this gouache contains kaolin and calcium carbonite, not just as filler, but as  consistency provider . We use vegetable binders without plastifiers here."
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000NMBKEA/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000NMBKEA&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=SBCGLCLJKNZD3ZFQ">
              Retail info on pans sets here
             </a>
             <img alt="" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=gurnjour-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B000NMBKEA" style="border: none !important; margin: 0px !important;" width="1"/>
             <br/>
             <div>
              <br/>
             </div>
             <br/>
             <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-L_55TtFgoAk/VaO8daWDSFI/AAAAAAAAb9g/cyWyu6ltVeM/s1600/caran-d-ache-set-de-gouache-10-couleurs.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em; text-align: center;">
              <img border="0" height="93" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-L_55TtFgoAk/VaO8daWDSFI/AAAAAAAAb9g/cyWyu6ltVeM/s200/caran-d-ache-set-de-gouache-10-couleurs.jpg" width="200"/>
             </a>
             <br/>
             "2.
             <b>
              Gouache extra-fine in small tubes (consistency like tooth paste)
             </b>
             <br/>
             Gouache extra-fine is close to a water colour, as the binder is arabic gum and that the quantity of mineral filler is just minimal."
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0064N4TKO/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B0064N4TKO&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=QFBOWGHB67FDVIDD">

             <img alt="" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=gurnjour-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B0064N4TKO" style="border: none !important; margin: 0px !important;" width="1"/>

             "]--Thanks, Steve

               <img border="0" height="98" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-3gYCUJFGur4/VaO96Na_BYI/AAAAAAAAb90/Ph86JNNLePs/s200/Caran_dAche8c.jpg" width="200"/>

             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000NM8N2C/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000NM8N2C&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=RXYFXFNG6XLNNVAH">

             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B003NLEEVO/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B003NLEEVO&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=QBAWQ4SIRKS2TF5Y">

             <br/>
             "Last remark : Our philosophy is that a good gouache colour, despite its covering qualities, should still be luminous and powerful. This is the reason why we use a strong concentration and a high quality of pigments that we carefully refine in our mills and grinders in our Genevan factory."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "I often compare a colour with coffee or chocolate : Concentrated black coffee made with the best beans has got power and makes an  explosion of taste in your mouth and throat (similar effect on paper with highly concentrated watercolour without filler !). Now, some people like to add some milk in their coffee or producers add water to decrease the costs of the expensive coffee powder. Thats all ok, but if you add too much milk (filling material !) or too much water in your coffee, it loses its original power and taste, it just gets a characterless muddy sauce!"
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "In other words : You can always break down a highly concentrated colour and lighten or darken it, but you can never  pimp up  a colour with too much filler, white or similar ingredients."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             "Wishing you all the best for your great project, with kind regards from Geneva, Adrian"

              Iain McCaig
             </a>
             (concept artist for Guardians of the Galaxy and Harry Potter) said, "It's a humbling experience to watch any true Master perform. In James Gurney's case, he is so beguiling and unpretentious that it is nothing short of magic.  One moment, a blank page, the next--presto!  A living, breathing, feathered Tyrannosaur!

             </a>
            </h3>
            <div class="post-header">

             </div>
            </div>

          <span>
           Friday, July 10, 2015
          </span>
         </h2>
         <div class="date-posts">
          <div class="post-outer">
           <div class="post hentry">
            <a name="9217655094581217237">
            </a>
            <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
             <a href="http://gurneyjourney.blogspot.ro/2015/07/gj-book-club-speed-chapter-14-unity-of.html">
              GJ Book Club, Speed, Chapter 14: Unity of Mass
             </a>
            </h3>
            <div class="post-header">
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="post-body entry-content">
             <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-ZLAb2mdfmVU/VRVB7rNQWOI/AAAAAAAAajo/uqnG29BZfWY/s1600/GJ%2BFriday%2BBookClub.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em; text-align: center;">
              <img border="0" height="200" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-ZLAb2mdfmVU/VRVB7rNQWOI/AAAAAAAAajo/uqnG29BZfWY/s1600/GJ%2BFriday%2BBookClub.jpg" width="200"/>
             </a>
             On the GJ Book Club, we're looking at Chapter 14: "Unity of Mass" in Harold Speed's 1917 classic
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0486228703/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=0486228703&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=gurnjour-20&amp;linkId=TH2IGMDGSUC7MK3B">
              The Practice and Science of Drawing
             </a>
             . The following numbered paragraphs cite key points in boldface. If you would like to respond to a specific image or point, please precede your comment by the corresponding number.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             This is another core chapter of Speed's book, with invaluable advice on tonal organization.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               1. The modifications in the relative tone values of objects seen under different aspects of light and atmosphere are infinite and ever varying.
              </b>
             </span>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              In other words, the
             </span>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              color you mix on your palette for a given object can be greatly altered by the
             </span>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              light and atmosphere of a given situation.
             </span>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <br/>
             </span>
             <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
              <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-efSzFU2Ohqk/VZ_L4wrgGOI/AAAAAAAAb7k/UHLc9yop7HY/s1600/Giovanni%2BBattista%2BTiepolo.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
               <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-efSzFU2Ohqk/VZ_L4wrgGOI/AAAAAAAAb7k/UHLc9yop7HY/s320/Giovanni%2BBattista%2BTiepolo.jpg" width="261"/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               <br/>
              </b>
             </span>
             <b>
              <span style="text-align: justify;">
               2. The painting of flesh higher in tone than the sky was
              </span>
              <a href="https://www.blogger.com/null" id="Page_201" name="Page_201" style="text-align: justify;">
              </a>
             </b>
             <span class="pagenum" style="font-size: smaller; left: 1311px; position: absolute; text-align: right;">
              <b>
               201
              </b>
             </span>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               almost universal at many periods of art, and in portraits is still often seen.
              </b>
             </span>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              This is an artistic convention, which makes some sense compositionally, but it doesn't look naturalistic. That's a Tiepolo.
             </span>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <br/>
             </span>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               3. A black glass, by reducing the light, enables you to observe these relationships more accurately.
              </b>
             </span>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              He's referring to a
              <a href="http://gurneyjourney.blogspot.com/2008/01/lorrain-mirrors.html">
               Lorrain mirror
              </a>
              .
             </span>
             <br/>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               <br/>
              </b>
             </span>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               4.T
              </b>
             </span>
             <b>
              <span style="text-align: justify;">
               here should never be pure white or pure black masses in a
              </span>
              <span style="text-align: justify;">
              </span>
             </b>
             <br/>
             <b>
              <a href="https://www.blogger.com/null" id="Page_203" name="Page_203" style="text-align: justify;">
              </a>
             </b>
             <span class="pagenum" style="font-size: smaller; left: 1311px; position: absolute; text-align: right;">
              <b>
               203
              </b>
             </span>
             <span style="text-align: justify;">
              <b>
               picture.
              </b>
             </span>
             <div>
              This is one of the best reasons to mix your "blacks": you get color character in the darks that way. You can't go as low in value, but you don't want to. How often do pianists hit the lowest note on their keyboard? This power in reserve is also true of chroma. "Keep a shot in your locker," as Pyle and Dunn used to say.
             </div>
             <div>
              <br/>
              <div>
               <b>
                5. Speed continues: "
                <span style="text-align: justify;">
                 Also, the highest lights in nature are never without colour, and this will lower the tone; neither are the deepest darks colourless, and this will raise their tone.
                </span>
                <span style="text-align: justify;">
                 "
                </span>
               </b>
              </div>
              <div style="text-align: justify;">
               Keep in mind that highlights partake of both the color of the light source and the local color of the object. Solar highlights with a little cadmium yellow look brighter than pure white.
              </div>
              <div style="text-align: justify;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div style="text-align: justify;">
               <b>
                6. Painting light to dark vs. dark to light
               </b>
              </div>
              Interesting discussion here about painting sequence and value keying. What do you all think of his view of this?
              <div>
               <br/>
               <div>
                <b>
                 7. These tone values are only to be perceived in their true relationship by the eye contemplating a wide field of vision.
                </b>
               </div>
               When he says "wide field of vision," I believe he doesn't mean viewing angle, but rather scope or range of awareness.
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div>
               <b>
                8. Light and shade and half tone are the great enemies of colour, sullying, as they do, its purity; and to some extent to design also, destroying, as they do, the flatness of the picture.
               </b>
              </div>
              <div>
               Sometimes the strongest color effects can happen when the chiaroscuro of form lighting are minimized, and the objects are shown more or less as flat shapes. He says, "Large flat tones give a power and simplicity to a design, and a largeness and breadth of expression that are very valuable, besides showing up every little variety in the values used for your modelling; and thus enabling you to model with the least expenditure of tones."
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div>
               <b>
                9. The
               </b>
               <b style="text-align: justify;">
                nearer these tones are in the scale of values, the more reserved and quiet the impression created
               </b>
               <span style="text-align: justify;">
                , and the
               </span>
               <b style="text-align: justify;">
                further apart or greater the contrast, the more dramatic and intense the effect
               </b>
               <span style="text-align: justify;">
                .
               </span>
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div>
               Strong light and shade divides forms into light and shadow shapes, as he points out, but it also introduces cast shadows. All these things can complicate a picture unless you go out of your way to group values by shape welding.
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div>
               <b>
                10 Straight lines = flat tones. Curved lines = gradated tones.
               </b>
              </div>
              <div>
               I love the equivalency he draws here, which makes perfect sense, especially if you paint a still life of a polygonal vs. a rounded form. The lines and the tones are related.
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://madamepickwickartblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/02/watteau1-1024x772.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="301" src="http://madamepickwickartblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/02/watteau1-1024x772.jpg" width="400"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div>
               <b>
                11. Watteau created a dream country of his own.
               </b>
              </div>
              <div>
               Example above. So did Maxfield Parrish and Dulac and Moebius and Syd Mead.
              </div>
              <div>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-NKPak8_n6e0/VZ_iv9gSeqI/AAAAAAAAb70/DZREL88-ASQ/s1600/Watteau%2Bdiagram.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="300" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-NKPak8_n6e0/VZ_iv9gSeqI/AAAAAAAAb70/DZREL88-ASQ/s400/Watteau%2Bdiagram.jpg" width="400"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
               <b>
                12. Gradation
               </b>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
               Pencil diagrams like the ones Speed demonstrates are a valuable way to study old masters. He mentions the magic of Turner and Watteau for their use of large gradations, and I would add the Hudson River School painters, especially Church and Bierstadt.
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
               He also echoes Ruskin when he points out that these gradations should be looked for not only in large areas, but also in the rocks and leaves.
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both;">
               <span style="text-align: justify;">
                <b>
                 13.
                </b>
               </span>
               <b>
                The desire of so many artists in these days to cut loose from tradition and start all over again puts a very severe strain upon their intuitive faculties
               </b>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both;">
               An interesting point. He also says that the lack of a traditional system "keeps them occupied correcting things that more knowledge of some of the fundamental principles that don't really alter and that are the same in all schools would have saved them. Knowledge in art is like a railway built behind the pioneers who have gone before; it offers a point of departure for those who come after, further on into the unknown country of nature's secretsa help not lightly to be discarded."
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both;">
               <b>
                <br/>
               </b>
              </div>
              <b>
               14. But all artifice in art must be concealed, a picture obviously composed is badly composed.
              </b>
             </div>
             <div>
              <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-ltOXMhqPq-U/VRWv56-xlVI/AAAAAAAAakw/7qfFlNh3-9M/s1600/harold_speed_cover.jpg" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
               <img border="0" height="200" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-ltOXMhqPq-U/VRWv56-xlVI/AAAAAAAAakw/7qfFlNh3-9M/s200/harold_speed_cover.jpg" width="123"/>
              </a>
              This is traditionally know by the Latin line:
              <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ars%20est%20celare%20artem">
               A
              </a>
              <i>
               <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ars%20est%20celare%20artem">
                rs est celare artem
               </a>
               :
              </i>
              True art is the concealment of artifice.
             </div>
