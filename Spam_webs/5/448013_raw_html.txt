            Nowadays, there are several ways to render, or image and display, output printers, monitors, tablets, and smartphones (and Im sure Ive left a few out). In one way or another, they all gauge output density with some sort of resolution measurement, such as
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.techterms.com/definition/dpi" title="dots per inch">

            </a>
            (dpi) or
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.digicamguides.com/print/ppi-print-size.html" title="PPI and Print Size">
             pixels per inch
            </a>
            (ppi). Typically, though, we use
            <em>
             dpi
            </em>
            when referring to
            <em>
             printed
            </em>
            resolution, and when we use
            <em>
             ppi,
            </em>
            were talking about
            <em>
             image
            </em>
            , or display, resolution.
           </p>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
           </div>
           <div class="radlinks ads-half" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">
           </div>
           <div data-defer="onPageLoad" id="videoPlaceholderLarge">
           </div>
           <p>
            Its also important to point out that laser-class printers turn out entirely different size and shaped dots than do their inkjet counterparts. For example, a good, appropriately configured laser printers output is so clean, in terms of screen frequency and halftones, that you can (theoretically) use it as
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://desktoppub.about.com/od/prepress/g/cameraready.htm" title="Camera Ready">
             camera-ready art
            </a>
            , i.e.
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="4" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://desktoppub.about.com/cs/basic/g/separations.htm" title="Separations">
             color separations
            </a>
            , for reproduction on a printing press. (However, most designers would use the laser output for proofing, and then go one step further and output the color separations to film for printing on a high-end,
            <em>
             high
            </em>
            -resolution imaging, or typesetting, machine.)
           </p>
           <h3>
            <strong>
             DPI
            </strong>
           </h3>
           <p>
            Hard-copy output devicesprinters, imagesetters, slide recorders, and plottersprint at various rates of dots per inch. Most laser printers, for example, print at rates between 150 and 600 dpi, with some models hitting resolutions of 1,200 dpi and beyond. Imagesetters print from about 900 up 3,000 dpi and beyond. The benefit of higher resolutions is, of course, smaller, tighter dots. Smaller, more densely positioned dots mean more detail and subtletiessharper lines and curvesand, more importantly, cleaner
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="5" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://desktoppub.about.com/cs/halftones/a/halftones.htm" title="Halftones">
             halftone screens
            </a>
            and photographs.
           </p>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="2" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense2">
           </div>
           <h3>
            <strong>
             Bitmap (Raster) Graphics vs. Vector Graphics
            </strong>
           </h3>
           <p>
            While there are several different graphics files types, there are really only two kinds of computer graphics:
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="6" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://graphicssoft.about.com/od/glossary/g/bitmap.htm" title="Bitmap and Raster">
             bitmap
            </a>
            (or raster) and
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="7" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://graphicssoft.about.com/od/glossary/l/blvector.htm" title="Vector graphics">
             vector
            </a>
            . You are probably more familiar with bitmap graphics, such as JPEG and PNG, used in producing images and other graphics made up of halftonesthe types of graphics you would typically edit or create with Photoshop.
           </p>
           <p>
            When it comes to resolution, bitmaps are
            <em>
             device dependent
            </em>
            , meaning that they maintain the resolution of the device on which they were createdin this case, usually a digital camera, scanner, computer, tablet, smartphoneyou get the idea. Bitmaps consist of a pattern of tightly placed dots, and while you can increase or decrease the size of a bitmap image, doing so greatly affects the density of the dots, or the dpi. Stretching the image decreases the dpi and making the image smaller increases it.
           </p>
           <p>
            As you can imagine, changing the number of dots per inch can greatly affect the overall quality of the image. For more on bitmap images, check out this About.com 
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="8" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://printscan.about.com/od/printerscannerspecs/fl/Bitmap-Graphics-ndash-Digital-Rubber-Stamps.htm" title="Bitmap Graphics  Digital Rubber Stamps">
             Bitmap Images, the Digital Rubber Stamp
            </a>
            , article.
           </p>
           <p>
            Vector graphics (or more precisely, vector
            <em>
             drawings
            </em>
            ), on the other hand, are, rather than blobs of dots, drawn mathematically. Every line, curve, and fill are created via thousands, sometimes millions, of computer instructions. Vector drawings are edited and created in drawing programs, such as Adobe Illustrator.
           </p>
           <p>
            For more on vector drawings, check out this About.com 
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="9" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://printscan.about.com/od/printerscannerspecs/fl/Scalable-Resolutions-with-Vector-Graphics.htm" title="Scalable Resolutions with Vector Graphics">
             Scalable Resolutions with Vector Graphics
            </a>
             article.
           </p>
           <p>
            Rather than stretching and condensing fields of dots when you enlarge and reduce vectors, the vectors simply redraw themselvesat the resolution of the device on which they are being
            <em>
             reproduced
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             not
            </em>
            the resolution of the device on which they were created. In other words, a vector drawing will output optimally (in terms of resolution, anyway) at 72 or 96 dpi, and it can print at the resolution of the output device, the printersay 300, 600 dpi, or more, just as though it was painstakingly created to be produced at that specific dpi.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Conclusion
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            In any case, being mindful of the resolution and image type of the photos and drawings you use, whether you embed them in your documents or print them as standalone photographs, can not only save you some time and frustration, but it can also greatly improve the quality of what you print. For instance, inadvertently printing 72 dpi photos on a photo printer capable of
            <em>
             much
            </em>
            higher resolutions will often yield much lower quality images and documentswhich is certainly something to think about.
           </p>
