        <h1 class="title">
         How do Same-Sex Couples Interpret Eph. 5:21-33?
        </h1>
        <div class="post-meta clearfix">
         <p class="meta-info">
          Posted  by
          <a href="http://IMPACTmagazine.us/author/suzannedewitt/" rel="author" title="Posts by Suzanne Marie DeWitt">
           Suzanne Marie DeWitt
          </a>
          in
          <a href="http://IMPACTmagazine.us/category/love-sex-family/family-life/" rel="category tag">
           Family Life
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://IMPACTmagazine.us/category/featured/" rel="category tag">
           Featured
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://IMPACTmagazine.us/category/love-sex-family/love-sex/" rel="category tag">
           Love &amp; Sex
          </a>
         </p>
        </div>
        <!-- end .post-meta -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <script>
          &lt;!--
dynamicgoogletags.update();
//--&gt;
         </script>
         <p>
          <img alt="RomanCouple" class="aligncenter wp-image-5884" height="400" src="http://IMPACTmagazine.us/wp-content/uploads/RomanCouple.jpg" width="600"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-size: 60px; float: left; color: #e10069; line-height: 45px; padding-top: 3px; padding-right: 5px; font-family: Georgia, Times, serif;">
           <b>
            T
           </b>
          </span>
          his morning, Dolce and I received a lovely text from a young priest at the church we attended for some years before moving to Massachusetts. Its part of a socially conservative new denomination which strives to reunite the liturgical, evangelical, and charismatic Christan streams. Dolce and I were active in leadership and in the life of the church when our marriages went through the final death throes of their long-drawn out demise. As God knit the two of us closer and closer together, the church didnt know how to handle us.
         </p>
         <p>
          Understandably.
         </p>
         <p>
          It was a pleasure to read the request this young Father sent us today. He asked how Ephesians 5:21-33 can be understood and applied in same sex relationships. And he asked very respectfully.
         </p>
         <p>
          Heres the passage:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           21 Be subject to one another out of reverence for Christ. 22 Wives, be subject to your husbands as you are to the Lord. 23 For the husband is the head of the wife just as Christ is the head of the church, the body of which he is the Savior. 24 Just as the church is subject to Christ, so also wives ought to be, in everything, to their husbands. 25 Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her, 26 in order to make her holy by cleansing her with the washing of water by the word, 27 so as to present the church to himself in splendor, without a spot or wrinkle or anything of the kindyes, so that she may be holy and without blemish. 28 In the same way, husbands should love their wives as they do their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. 29 For no one ever hates his own body, but he nourishes and tenderly cares for it, just as Christ does for the church, 30 because we are members of his body. 31 For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and the two will become one flesh. 32 This is a great mystery, and I am applying it to Christ and the church. 33 Each of you, however, should love his wife as himself, and a wife should respect her husband.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          As with all scripture, there are multiple levels of application, intent, and meaning. First Ill address the contemporary cultural context. And for ease of discussion, Ill assume that Paul is the author.
         </p>
         <p>
          At the time of the missives writing, there were two forms of Roman marriage in places like Ephesus. Marriage was a contractual business that could come about by a variety of processes and ritualistic approaches. But the two contractual forms (in simplified essence) were that a woman and her property either remained subject to her father, or became subject to her husband. The forms ebbed and flowed in popularity over the years, but it would appear that in verses 22-24 Paul was clarifying that in leaving her father, the proper form of marriage was to become the property of her husband. He appeared to be stating that this form was the Christian form.
         </p>
         <p>
          This echoes the leaving and cleaving reference in verse 31.
         </p>
         <p>
          The second cultural issue is Pauls instructions for men. At the time, marriage was rarely romantic. Men who demonstrated romantic love for their women were viewed as emasculated sissies. Paul acted like Jesus again, instructing that this should change, and that men
          <em>
           should
          </em>
          love their wives.
         </p>
         <p>
          So it appears that he strove to move marriage from a contractual thing to a Christian thing. From a legal construct to a sacramental reality. From a set of rules to a rule of love.
         </p>
         <p>
          From this cultural perspective, the passage doesnt apply to same sex couples (or to modern heterosexual couples for that matter). In 2014 we dont view women as property to be transferred, were thankfully lacking the eras gender discrimination and bias, and we dont have the same contractual models of marriage to contend with.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its very clear that same-sex marriages were not something that existed, but then, the entire concept of marriage didnt mean what it does now. Fidelity was not assumed for men. Polygamy was common. All sorts of broken sexuality took place that nowhere near modeled the gorgeous scriptural images of marital love about which Solomon and Paul wrote.
         </p>
         <p>
          As far as a message to todays culture, I think that Paul is calling us all back to the sacredness of sexual union, and reminding us of how God wants us to view each other within marriage. He calls us to be subject to each other. To be faithful defenders of each other. To be one as He and the Father are one, in mystical union.
         </p>
         <p>
          How do I see this as applying to same sex couples? I guess the question is more how do I NOT see it applying?
         </p>
         <p>
          In good marriages of our era there is a sharing of responsibility and leadership. In Christian marriages which God brings together, the man may be deferred to as the head and final arbiter for times when consensus cannot be reached. This model can flourish and bear bountiful fruit.
         </p>
         <p>
          Smart same sex couples create a similar structure. Someone needs to be the head in the case of non-agreement. For Dolce and I, we are both heads of different parts of life. We are subject to each other, and God is the head of us both. Given that He put us together, and given that we defer all decisions to His will, the times when we are not of the same opinion are shockingly few.
         </p>
         <p>
          In marriages that humans bring together rather than God, it wont matter how much deference is given to the man as head. The fruit will be stunted and bitter.
         </p>

                 June 25, 2014
                </a>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="social-comment-body">
               <p>
                Before I proceed, Im not responding in an argumentative way.
               </p>
               <p>
                I think we can agree that Wives, be subject to your husbands as you are to the Lord is outdated thinking. However, that being said, there are still many Christians who believe in this kind of thinking, and whats more surprising is that there are many Christian women who believe in this. Which illustrates the fact that Christianity is divided into different sects and factions of people who hold opposing view points; youre wrong, Im right, youve misinterpret scripture, etc, etc.
               </p>
               <p>
                In Christian marriages which God brings together, the man may be deferred to as the head and final arbiter for times when consensus cannot be reached. This model can flourish and bear bountiful fruit. Smart same sex couples create a similar structure. Someone needs to be the head in the case of non-agreement. I disagree. For one, who decides who is the head? The oldest? The one who makes more money? Every decision we make, every reaction we have stems from individual history. I was raised in an psychological and physically abusive home by a mean, anger mother who believed she was the head. I promised myself I would never be in a marriage where I submit or defer consensus to someone else. And promised myself to not get into a relationship with a submissive person. We must make choices as a united couple and as individual too. But when they are both at odds, you should not surrender your convictions just because someone else is deemed head of the household. I have been in a relationship for 12 years and in that time, we have disagreed and neither of us has deferred to the other. In fact, when the answer is no, its no. I dont need to run the decision by God for clarification. I look at things logically, with reasoning and what consequences will entail. That is how my husband and I make decisions as a couple. Not by giving one the final say.
               </p>
               <p>
                In marriages that humans bring together rather than God, it wont matter how much deference is given to the man as head. The fruit will be stunted and bitter. This confuses me. Sometimes I think people give God too, too much credit for what was just pure chance. Do I feel God brought my husband and I together? No. Circumstances and chance and series of events lead me to him. Choices I made, not God. To say that God brought us together implies to me I had no choice. But Im sure people will disagree. What matters is that I dont believe God brought us together. We met, we decided, its our choice and our marriage has been fruitful and happy. Because there are many marriages that yield stunted and bitter fruit who believe God brought them together.
               </p>

              <div class="social-comment-body">
               <p>
                Steve Garza: No worries. Your respectful stance is apparent.
               </p>
               <p>
                I definitely get your point, and thats one of the reasons I chose terms like may (in the man -may- be deferred to as the head) and can (in This model -can- flourish). The piece I wrote was a response to a priest from a socially conservative denomination, which firmly believes in wives submitting. My point was to say that the model of deferring to the man in a heterosexual marriage MAY work, and the model CAN flourish (as opposed to WILL work and WILL flourish). Its conceivable. Far from guaranteed, but possible.
               </p>
               <p>
                My writing on this subject will always rotate around God as the axis, and that is why I wrote what I did about marriages that God brings together versus humans bringing together. I completely agree with your final sentence about marriages that yield bitter fruit. I used to think that -anyone- who got married was brought together by God, but what Ive come to realize is that is often not the case. We are a strong willed lot who often rush into relationships for all sorts of broken reasons rather than coming into them from places of wholeness. So I now look at the Biblical phrase what God has brought together let no man separate quite differently from how I viewed it in the past. I say AMEN to the idea that people who try to get in the way of a coupling ordained by God are headed for trouble. But I recognize that not all couplings which result in marriage are, in fact, ordained.
               </p>
               <p>
                You have said much that is profound about your family experience and the choices you have made because of them. And it sounds as if your relationship with your husband works well because you approached your choice of him as partner knowing what you wanted for healthy interaction. That is a wonderful thing. I myself am profoundly Catholic in my faith life and in my world view, and submission is a key part in that way of thinking. I submit daily to Gods will, I try to emulate Mary in saying yes, and I believe that there is great, great power in submission. The gift of submission yields amazing results. It doesnt necessarily get you what you -want- but the rewards are astounding.
               </p>
               <p>
                (This is of course not meant in any way to justify abuse or hyper control or other disordered situations)
               </p>
               <p>
                Ive prattled on, probably provoking more issues than answering them. But again, I do appreciate your having read the piece, and responding to it.
               </p>
