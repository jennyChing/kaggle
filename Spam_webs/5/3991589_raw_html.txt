            <span class="entry-caption thumbnail-caption">
             What We Learned From SnapChats 4.6M User Hack
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            On New Years Day, millions of Snapchat users discovered that their data had been compromised. Information from approximately 4.6 million users of the photo-sharing app was published online by hackers who wanted to demonstrate that Snapchats security was lacking. This is the latest in a headline-making series of (unrelated)
            <a href="http://www.foxnews.com/us/2013/12/22/debit-and-credit-cards-stolen-in-target-breach-reportedly-for-sale-in/" title="Debit and credit cards stolen in Target breach reportedly for sale in underground black markets">
             security breaches
            </a>
            ,
            <b>
            </b>
            but it wont be the last.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Millions of Snapchat Usernames, Phone Numbers Published Online
           </h2>
           <p>
            Usernames and phone numbers with the last two digits missing were published online on Reddit and the site SnapchatDB.info, which has since been suspended. A hacker or group of
            <a href="http://www.ibtimes.co.uk/photo-messaging-app-snapchat-hacked-4-6m-usernames-phone-numbers-leaked-1430746" title="Snapchat Hacked with 4.6M Usernames and Phone Numbers Leaked Online">
             hackers going by Lightcontact
            </a>
            claimed to be responsible. This came after months of warnings to Snapchat about the companys security features.
           </p>
           <p>
            In August of last year, the Australian company Gibson Security alerted Snapchat to the fact that it had a flaw that could be breached. Snapchat did not respond. Gibson Security repeated the warning on December 25
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            , to which Snapchat replied in a blog post that theyd made hacking their database more difficult. When the database was hacked a week later, the company responded with another
            <a href="http://blog.snapchat.com/post/72013106599/find-friends-abuse" title="Find Friends Abuse ">
             blog post
            </a>
            , saying it would allow users to opt out of the Find Friends feature which stores users phone numbers. It also promised to improve security in other ways. If users were expecting an apology for the breach, they didnt get one.
           </p>
           <p>
            Snapchat users can check if their information was leaked using
            <a href="http://lookup.gibsonsec.org/lookup" title="GS Lookup  Snapchat">
             this lookup tool
            </a>
            from Gibson Security.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Snapchat Popularity Soared Since September 2011 Debut
           </h2>
           <p>
            Snapchat has grown impressively in just a little over two years, with millions of users sharing millions of photos and videos every day. The company has no revenue, but its popularity has supposedly prompted two major companies to make
            <a href="http://www.technobuffalo.com/2013/11/15/snapchat-4-billion-google-offer/" title="Snapchat Allegedly Rejected $4B Buyout Offer From Google">
             multibillion dollar offers
            </a>
            : Facebook offered $3 billion on November 14th, and Google offered $4 billion a day later. Both offers were refused.
           </p>
           <p>
            A big draw of the multi-feature app is that it allows users to decide how long the recipient can see the photo  anywhere from 1 to 10 seconds  before its gone and deleted from Snapchats servers. Although not the companys stated intention, this feature allows users to share very personal messages with others.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Improve Your Online Security Protect Your Information
           </h2>
           <p>
            Theres not much users can do now if their info was leaked; even closing their Snapchat account wont help, since the information has already been published. But this is a good reminder  especially as storing data in the cloud becomes more popular  that nothing online is perfectly safe, and that its only a matter of time before the next big security breach occurs. Take steps now to lessen the chances that
            <i>
             your
            </i>
            personal information is leaked.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Change your passwords.
            </b>
            Most people use the same password, or a slight variation on one or two basic passwords, simply because its easier to remember. But improving password security is the first step in improving your overall security online. Use this
            <a href="http://techland.time.com/2011/06/29/two-minute-video-how-to-create-strong-online-passwords/" title="Two Minute Video: How to Create Strong Online Passwords">
             password recipe
            </a>
            <b>
            </b>
            to come up with unique words for all your accounts, or try one of the dozens of free password generators available, like this
            <a href="https://www.grc.com/passwords.htm" title="Perfect Passwords">
             Ultra High Security Password Generator
            </a>
            , also from Gibson Security.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Change your usernames.
            </b>
            Hacking one account can make it easier to hack others when the same information is used. For added privacy, choose usernames that dont include your real name.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Opt for two-step authentication when possible.
            </b>
            Some sites give you the option to add an extra step to the login process, such as a security code texted to your phone that you must enter before gaining access to your account. These can be tedious, but they do improve security.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Close your accounts before they are hacked.
            </b>
            If youve finally had enough, consider abstaining from life online altogether, or at least from the sites you can live without. Clear out your data and
            <a href="http://nakedlaw.avvo.com/social-media-2/how-to-break-up-with-facebook.html" title="How to Break Up With Facebook">
             shut down your accounts
            </a>
            for good.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
