   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Onomastics
   </h1>

     <p>
      <b>
       Onomastics
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       onomatology
      </b>
      is the study of the origin, history, and use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proper_name" title="Proper name">
       proper names
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <b>
       Onomastics
      </b>
      originates from the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Greek
      </a>
       (
      <i>
       onomastikos
      </i>
      ), which translates to "of or belonging to naming"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      from  (
      <i>
       noma
      </i>
      ) meaning "name".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
       Toponymy or toponomastics
      </a>
      , the study of place names, is one of the principal branches of onomastics.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthroponomastics" title="Anthroponomastics">
       Anthroponomastics
      </a>
      is the study of personal names. Onomastics can be helpful in data mining, with applications such as
      <a href="/wiki/Named-entity_recognition" title="Named-entity recognition">
       Named-entity recognition
      </a>
      , or recognition of the origin of names.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An
      <b>
       orthonym
      </b>
      is the proper name of the object in question, the object of onomastic study.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_studies">
       Gender studies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Onomastics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gender studies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Onomastics have been used extensively in
      <a href="/wiki/Gender_studies" title="Gender studies">
       gender studies
      </a>
      , to infer the gender of personal names in quantitative research. In most countries and cultures, the method is very accurate,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with a precision in the range 95-99%+.
     </p>
