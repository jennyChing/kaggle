     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
       Norse mythology
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       Geri
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       Freki
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      , both meaning "the ravenous" or "greedy one") are two wolves which are said to accompany the god
      <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">
       Odin
      </a>
      . They are attested in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">
        Poetic Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      , a collection of epic poetry compiled in the 13th century from earlier traditional sources, in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">
        Prose Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      , written in the 13th century by
      <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">
       Snorri Sturluson
      </a>
      , and in the poetry of
      <a href="/wiki/Skald" title="Skald">
       skalds
      </a>
      . The pair has been compared to similar figures found in
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
       Greek
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">
       Roman
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vedic_mythology" title="Vedic mythology">
       Vedic mythology
      </a>
      , and may also be connected to beliefs surrounding the
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
       Germanic
      </a>
      "wolf-warrior bands", the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Alfh%C3%A9%C3%B0nar" title="lfhnar">
       lfhnar
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geri_and_Freki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The names
      <i>
       Geri
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Freki
      </i>
      have been interpreted as meaning either "the greedy one" or "the ravenous one".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMEKANDLINDOW_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SIMEKANDLINDOW-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The name
      <i>
       Geri
      </i>
      can be traced back to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">
       Proto-Germanic
      </a>
      adjective
      <i>
       *geraz
      </i>
      , attested in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundian_language_(Germanic)" title="Burgundian language (Germanic)">
       Burgundian
      </a>
      <i>
       girs
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      <i>
       gerr
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
       Old High German
      </a>
      <i>
       ger
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       giri
      </i>
      , all of which mean "greedy".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL132_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OREL132-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The name
      <i>
       Freki
      </i>
      can be traced back to the Proto-Germanic adjective
      <i>
       *frekaz
      </i>
      , attested in
      <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
       Gothic
      </a>
      <i>
       faihu-friks
      </i>
      "covetous, avaricious", Old Norse
      <i>
       frekr
      </i>
      "greedy",
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
       Old English
      </a>
      <i>
       frec
      </i>
      "desirous, greedy, gluttonous, audacious" and Old High German
      <i>
       freh
      </i>
      "greedy".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OREL113_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OREL113-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lindow" title="John Lindow">
       John Lindow
      </a>
      interprets both Old Norse names as nominalized adjectives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LINDOW120AND139_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LINDOW120AND139-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lincoln" title="Bruce Lincoln">
       Bruce Lincoln
      </a>
      further traces
      <i>
       Geri
      </i>
      back to a
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
       Proto-Indo-European
      </a>
      stem
      <i>
       *gher-
      </i>
      , which is the same as that found in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Garmr" title="Garmr">
        Garmr
       </a>
      </i>
      , a name referring to the hound closely associated with the events of
      <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnark">
       Ragnark
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LINCOLN99_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LINCOLN99-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Attestations">
       Attestations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geri_and_Freki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attestations">

     </h2>
     <p>
      In the
      <i>
       Poetic Edda
      </i>
      poem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmnism%C3%A1l" title="Grmnisml">
        Grmnisml
       </a>
      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">
       Odin
      </a>
      (disguised as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Odin" title="List of names of Odin">
        Grmnir
       </a>
      </i>
      ) provides the young
      <a href="/wiki/Agnarr_Geirr%C3%B6%C3%B0sson" title="Agnarr Geirrsson">
       Agnarr
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Odin" title="List of names of Odin">

     <p>
      In the
      <i>
       Prose Edda
      </i>
      book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">
        Gylfaginning
       </a>
      </i>
      (chapter 38), the enthroned figure of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">
       High
      </a>
      explains that Odin gives all of the food on his table to his wolves Geri and Freki and that Odin requires no food, for wine is to him both meat and drink. High then quotes the above-mentioned stanza from the poem
      <i>
       Grmnisml
      </i>
      in support.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES33_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FAULKES33-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In chapter 75 of the
      <i>
       Prose Edda
      </i>
      book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1ldskaparm%C3%A1l" title="Skldskaparml">
        Skldskaparml
       </a>
      </i>
      a list of names for
      <a href="/wiki/Warg" title="Warg">
       wargs
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">
       wolves
      </a>
      is provided that includes both Geri and Freki.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES164_12-0">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_record">
       Archaeological record
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geri_and_Freki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archaeological record">

     <p>
      If the rider on horseback on the image on the
      <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6ksta_Runestone" title="Bksta Runestone">
       Bksta Runestone
      </a>
      has been correctly identified as Odin, then Geri and Freki are shown taking part in hunting an elk or moose.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SILEN88-91_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SILEN88-91-16">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">
       Theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geri_and_Freki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1217" data-file-width="1408" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg/220px-Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg/330px-Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg/440px-Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bronspl%C3%A5t_pressbleck_%C3%B6land_vendeltid.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vendel_era" title="Vendel era">
         Vendel era
        </a>
        bronze plate found on
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%96land" title="land">
         land
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
         Sweden
        </a>
        depicting a wolf-pelt warrior drawing a sword beside a dancing figure.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Freki
      </i>
      is also a name applied to the monstrous wolf
      <a href="/wiki/Fenrir" title="Fenrir">
       Fenrir
      </a>
      in the
      <i>
       Poetic Edda
      </i>
      poem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Vlusp">
        Vlusp
       </a>
      </i>
      . Folklorist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lindow" title="John Lindow">
       John Lindow
      </a>
      sees irony in the fact that Odin feeds one Freki at his dinner table and anotherFenrirwith his flesh during the events of
      <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnark">
       Ragnark
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LINDOW120_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LINDOW120-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Historian Michael Spiedel connects Geri and Freki with archaeological finds depicting figures wearing wolf-pelts and frequently found wolf-related names among the
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
       Germanic peoples
      </a>
      , including Wulfhroc ("Wolf-Frock"), Wolfhetan ("Wolf-Hide"), Isangrim ("Grey-Mask"), Scrutolf ("Garb-Wolf") and Wolfgang ("Wolf-Gait"), Wolfdregil ("Wolf-Runner"), and Vulfolaic ("Wolf-Dancer") and myths regarding wolf warriors from Norse mythology (such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Alfh%C3%A9%C3%B0nar" title="lfhnar">
       lfhnar
      </a>
      ). Spiegel believes this to point to the pan-Germanic wolf-warrior band cult centered around Odin that waned away after
      <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">
       Christianization
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPIEDEL24-38_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SPIEDEL24-38-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scholars have also noted
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">
       Indo-European
      </a>
      parallels to the wolves Geri and Freki as companions of a divinity. 19th century scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">
       Jacob Grimm
      </a>
      observed a connection between this aspect of Odin's character and the Greek
      <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">
       Apollo
      </a>
      , to whom both the wolf and the raven are sacred.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRIMM147_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GRIMM147-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Philologist
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bloomfield" title="Maurice Bloomfield">
       Maurice Bloomfield
      </a>
      further connected the pair with the two dogs of
      <a href="/wiki/Yama" title="Yama">
       Yama
      </a>
      in Vedic mythology, and saw them as a Germanic counterpart to a more general and widespread Indo-European "
      <a href="/wiki/Cerberus" title="Cerberus">
       Cerberus
      </a>
      "-theme.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Michael Speidel finds similar parallels in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">
       Vedic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Rudra" title="Rudra">
       Rudra
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">
       Roman
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">
       Mars
      </a>
      . Elaborating on the connection between wolves and figures of great power, he writes: "This is why Geri and Freki, the wolves at Woden's side, also glowered on the throne of the Anglo-Saxon kings. Wolf-warriors, like Geri and Freki, were not mere animals but mythical beings: as Woden's followers they bodied forth his might, and so did wolf-warriors."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPIEDEL24-38_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SPIEDEL24-38-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Heinrich" title="Bernd Heinrich">
       Bernd Heinrich
      </a>
      theorizes that Geri and Freki, along with Odin and his ravens
      <a href="/wiki/Huginn_and_Muninn" title="Huginn and Muninn">
       Huginn and Muninn
      </a>
      , reflect a
      <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">
       symbiosis
      </a>
      observed in the natural world among ravens, wolves, and humans on the hunt:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        In a biological symbiosis one organism typically shores up some weakness or deficiency of the other(s). As in such a symbiosis, Odin the father of all humans and gods, though in human form was imperfect by himself. As a separate entity he lacked depth perception (being one-eyed) and he was apparently also uninformed and forgetful. But his weaknesses were compensated by his ravens, Hugin (mind) and Munin (memory) who were part of him. They perched on his shoulders and reconnoitered to the ends of the earth each day to return in the evening and tell him the news. He also had two wolves at his side, and the man/god-raven-wolf association was like one single organism in which the ravens were the eyes, mind, and memory, and the wolves the providers of meat and nourishment. As god, Odin was the ethereal parthe only drank wine and spoke only in poetry. I wondered if the Odin myth was a metaphor that playfully and poetically encapsulates ancient knowledge of our prehistoric past as hunters in association with two allies to produce a powerful hunting alliance. It would reflect a past that we have long forgotten and whose meaning has been obscured and badly frayed as we abandoned our hunting cultures to become herders and agriculturists, to whom ravens act as competitors.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEINRICH-355_21-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-HEINRICH-355-21">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          21
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
     </blockquote>
