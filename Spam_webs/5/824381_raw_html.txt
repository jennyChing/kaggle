          The dying American dream, perfectly illustrated with Legos
         </b>

        <font color="">
         <br/>
         If youre suffering from Ice Bucket Challenge fatigue, heres another
         <a class="inline " href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t2XFh_tD2RA#t=11">
          <span>
           video worth watching
          </span>
         </a>
         : the dying American dream, perfectly illustrated in Lego bricks.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         The expanding wealth gap in America has been well-documented in the years since the recession. Data released today by
         <a class="inline " href="http://links.govdelivery.com/track?type=click&amp;enid=ZWFzPTEmbWFpbGluZ2lkPTIwMTQwODIxLjM1MjI3MTIxJm1lc3NhZ2VpZD1NREItUFJELUJVTC0yMDE0MDgyMS4zNTIyNzEyMSZkYXRhYmFzZWlkPTEwMDEmc2VyaWFsPTE3MDMxMjgxJmVtYWlsaWQ9bWFuZGl3QHlhaG9vLWluYy5jb20mdXNlcmlkPW1hbmRpd0B5YWhvby1pbmMuY29tJmZsPSZleHRyYT1NdWx0aXZhcmlhdGVJZD0mJiY=&amp;&amp;&amp;101&amp;&amp;&amp;http://www.census.gov/people/wealth/files/Wealth%20distribution%202000%20to%202011.pdf?eml=gd&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_source=govdelivery">
          <span>
           the U.S. Census Bureau
          </span>
         </a>
         shows that the wealthiest households in the U.S. were worth nearly 40 times as much as the poorest households in 2005, accounting for both debts and assets. By 2011, they were worth 87 times as much.
         <br/>
        </font>
       </div>
       <div>
        <div>
         <div>
          <div>
           <img align="right" alt="" border="0" src="http://r.m.yahoo.com/api/res/1.2/IAh3xXLmQq59soB7K7NPdw--/Zmk9ZmlsbDtoPTEyO3E9SGlnaDt3PTEy/https://s.yimg.com/vs/images/link.png"/>
           <a href="http://46070405.r.msn.com/?ld=b2bFEm3eXEeX_wh8N6GwU0gDVUCUwQp0Is7hTY-SKTh5KMVsOMer3ue4qxmyMbhdbJLfrxNUP9mXGHqsohGa0exw6_dgkJCKumW_iduF29FPGVeT0tlaE34ljcqZjbghb2qrwd-A&amp;u=bit.ly%2f1BcfZkH">
            <span class="ad-title">
             <font color="">
              Get Infinitely Wealthier!
             </font>
            </span>
           </a>
           <div>
            <font color="#8d8c8d">
             <span class="ad-grey">
              <font color="">
               http://bit.ly/1BcfZkH

             </span>
            </font>

       <div>
        <font color="">
         <br/>
         As troubling as that seems, the
         <a class="inline " href="http://www.brookings.edu/research/essays/2014/saving-horatio-alger">
          <span>
           three-minute video
          </span>
         </a>
         posted by Richard Reeves, an economist with the Brookings Institute, he captures an even more pressing concern  the startling difficulty that the countrys poorest households have at ever working their way up the income ladder.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         We can have a long argument about the gap between the rich and the poor, Reeves says. But I think we can all agree that we dont want to live in a society where where youre born determines so strongly your chances in life of where you end up.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Here are some of the highlights (see the data behind the video
         <a class="inline " href="http://www.brookings.edu/blogs/social-mobility-memos/posts/2014/08/21-data-behind-saving-horatio-alger-reeves">
          <span>
           here
          </span>
         </a>
         ):
         <br/>
         <br/>
         One out of every three children born into a poor household  defined as a dual-income family earning less than $31,500  in the U.S. will remain stuck in poverty. They have a less than 20% shot at making it to the middle class, and only one in 10 will make it to the top rung of the income ladder.
         <br/>
         <img alt="" border="0" src="http://r.m.yahoo.com/api/res/1.2/u0F3jehM0K7nYpgSWzdD8Q--/Y2w9cHJldmlldztmaT1maXQ7aD03MjtxPUhpZ2g7dz05Ng--/http://l.yimg.com/os/publish-images/finance/2014-08-21/cfff32e0-296d-11e4-b8ee-7dbf58bb57a0_Screen-Shot-2014-08-21-at-1-02-04-PM.png"/>
         <br/>
         Black Americans start out with even a bigger disadvantage. A mere 3% of black children born into poor households will make it to the top, while more than half will be stuck in poverty.
         <br/>
         <img alt="" border="0" src="http://r.m.yahoo.com/api/res/1.2/G5zEZzGfnwYLtKbONxIvTA--/Y2w9cHJldmlldztmaT1maXQ7aD03MjtxPUhpZ2g7dz05Ng--/http://l.yimg.com/os/publish-images/finance/2014-08-21/cff15030-296d-11e4-8f4b-bb48d69cd666_Screen-Shot-2014-08-21-at-1-02-16-PM.png"/>
         <br/>
         For white Americans, the outlook is significantly brighter.
         <br/>
         <img alt="" border="0" src="http://r.m.yahoo.com/api/res/1.2/4OK9c2L_ric5Gg6vt3kyoA--/Y2w9cHJldmlldztmaT1maXQ7aD03MjtxPUhpZ2g7dz05Ng--/http://l.yimg.com/os/publish-images/finance/2014-08-21/dd3e9e00-296d-11e4-b8ee-7dbf58bb57a0_Screen-Shot-2014-08-21-at-1-02-42-PM.png"/>
         <br/>
         But race, Reeves explains, will only get you so far up the income ladder. Education plays a much larger role.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Those without a high school degree are up against the worst odds if they want to work their way up. Only 1% of poor high school dropouts will make it to the top 20% of earners.
         <br/>
         <img alt="" border="0" src="http://r.m.yahoo.com/api/res/1.2/eKNOpTyPep2M6cw8MeqydA--/Y2w9cHJldmlldztmaT1maXQ7aD03MjtxPUhpZ2g7dz05Ng--/http://l.yimg.com/os/publish-images/finance/2014-08-21/dd10fe50-296d-11e4-a7e3-1f8f1bc59baa_Screen-Shot-2014-08-21-at-1-03-22-PM.png"/>
         <br/>
         Getting a college degree changes these odds significantly. College grads have a 20% probability of reaching the ranks of Americas highest-earning households. Fewer than one in five will remain stuck in poverty.
         <br/>
         <img alt="" border="0" src="http://r.m.yahoo.com/api/res/1.2/pa4gBTaC9GzZuJjTf6hGkw--/Y2w9cHJldmlldztmaT1maXQ7aD03MjtxPUhpZ2g7dz05Ng--/http://l.yimg.com/os/publish-images/finance/2014-08-21/e7c810e0-296d-11e4-b8ee-7dbf58bb57a0_Screen-Shot-2014-08-21-at-1-03-58-PM.png"/>
         <br/>
         Reeves, who oversees Brookings Center of Children and Family, has studied intergenerational mobility in families for years. At the root of Americas social immobility problem are fundamental differences in the opportunities available to poor and wealthy households.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Its the family theyre born into, the parenting they receive, the quality of their education, their prospects of getting into college, their connections to the labor market, he says. Many of these are very difficult for public policy [alone] to reach.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Some solutions include expanding early sex education  half of children born into unmarried poor households will stay poor  improving early childhood education and counseling young people about their options for college and scholarships. In
         <a class="inline " href="http://www.hamiltonproject.org/files/downloads_and_links/THP_13EconFacts_FINAL.pdf">
          <span>
           a report released
          </span>
         </a>
         last year by the Hamilton Project, a research arm of the Brookings Institute, researchers found that one of the biggest reasons low-income students dont pursue degrees is that they werent educated about scholarships and financial aid.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         I think its important for policy makers to realize that we need to be intervening at each stage of the life cycle, Reeves says. Theres no single solution to what is a multigenerational, multiyear complex problem.
         <br/>
         <br/>
