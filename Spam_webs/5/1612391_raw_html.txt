      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Hands off ... Alan Rosenberg, manager of the Church of Scientology's Paris branch, pushes back reporters as he leaves court." src="/content/dam/images/b/m/b/x/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.bm6z.png/1243372327487.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Hands off ... Alan Rosenberg, manager of the Church of Scientology's Paris branch, pushes back reporters as he leaves court.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Reuters
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       THE Church of Scientology has been accused of manipulating followers into handing over their savings in a trial that could result in the church being thrown out of France.
      </p>
      <p>
       One woman has claimed she was psychologically pressured into paying thousands of euros for lessons, books, drugs and an "electrometer", which the church says can measure a person's mental state.
      </p>
      <p>
       Charges of organised fraud, including claims of illegally prescribing drugs, were filed by the country's pharmaceutical association and two women who have accused Scientologists of causing their financial ruin.
      </p>
      <p>
       Scientology is not banned in France but has been considered a sect since 1995. It is a recognised religion in America, where it was founded in 1954 by L. Ron Hubbard, the science fiction writer. Followers include the Hollywood actors Tom Cruise and John Travolta.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       This is the second time the church has been the subject of a case in France. All other trials have been of individuals.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the plaintiffs, Aude-Claire Malton, 33, was allegedly approached by Scientologists in Paris in 1998 and offered a personality test. Prosecutors said the Scientologists' sole aim was to "claim their fortune" by "exercising a psychological hold" over her. It is alleged Ms Malton was gradually persuaded to hand over about 23,000 ($40,000) for such items as books, "life healing" lessons and "purification packs".
      </p>
      <p>
       Three former Scientologists retracted their complaints after reportedly reaching an out-of-court financial settlement. One was said to have received 33,000.
      </p>
      <p>
       The church's main structure in France, the AGES-Celebrity Centre, and its bookshop and six leaders, face charges of "organised fraud". The six, including Alain Rosenberg, 60, the centre's manager, could receive a 1 million fine and 10-year jail term if convicted. The church itself faces a 5 million fine and closure in the case, which has taken 10 years to come to court.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Church of Scientology said anti-cult organisations were trying to shut it down and called it "a trial for heresy".
      </p>
      <p>
       The Telegraph, London;
      </p>
      <p>
       Guardian News &amp; Media
      </p>
