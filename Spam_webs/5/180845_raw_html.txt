         <header class="entry-header">
          <h1 class="entry-title">
           New anti-littering campaign focuses on public shaming of people by collecting their DNA
          </h1>
          <div class="entry-meta">
          </div>
          <!-- .entry-meta -->
         </header>
         <!-- .entry-header -->
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           <strong>
            According to a recent global study, published in the journal
            <a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/content/347/6223/768.full#F1">
             <em>
              Science
             </em>
            </a>
            , 275 million metric tonnes (MT) of plastic waste, such as plastic bottles and bags, was produced in 2010 in 192 coastal countries, with 4.8 to 12.7 million MT entering the ocean.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_3523" style="width: 310px">
           <a href="http://www.spree.net.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/snapshot-report_stack-300DPI.png" rel="lightbox[post-3522]">
            <img alt="snapshot-report_stack-300DPI" class="wp-image-3523 size-medium" height="209" src="http://www.spree.net.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/snapshot-report_stack-300DPI-300x209.png" width="300"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Image credit: Parabon Nanolab
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           In the study, China was found to be
           <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/science/2015/feb/12/coastal-communities-dumping-8m-tonnes-of-plastic-in-oceans-every-year">
            one of the main culprits
           </a>
           as 1.32 to 3.53 million MT of its plastic waste reached the oceans in 2010.
          </p>
          <p>
           A Hong Kong environmental group decided to do something about it and end the out-of-control littering in the city.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Hong Kong Cleanup Initiative used DNA collected from discarded cigarette butts, gum and condoms to create renderings of the faces of people who littered.
          </p>
          <p>
           The campaign has a very interesting and descriptive name: The Face of Litter. It was officially launched on Earth Day and has thus far created 27 facial composites of litterers and splashed them across billboards around the city.
          </p>
          <p>
           Lets see how they did it. As part of this amazing project  coordinated by Hong Kong-based magazine
           <a href="http://www.ecozine.com/">
            <em>
             Ecozine
            </em>
           </a>
           and the
           <a href="https://www.tnc.org.hk/">
            Nature Conservancy
           </a>
            over 4,000 tonnes of litter was gathered from the streets of Hong Kong and DNA samples were sent to be analysed.
          </p>
          <p>
           Advertising firm Ogilvy &amp; Mathercame up with the public shaming part. With technology from the US-based
           <a href="http://parabon-nanolabs.com/">
            Parabon Nanolabs
           </a>
           , the campaign reconstructs the profile of litterers based on their DNA samples. Using its SnapShot technology, the company then identifies thousands of potential physical traits from perpetrators, including hair and eye colour, ethnicity and face shape.
          </p>
          <p>
           This campaign is one of a kind. Its interactive. Its innovative. Its our own science experiment that were using to create social change. Litter is such a major problem in Hong Kong and thanks to newly available DNA technology we can now put a face to this anonymous crime and get people to think twice about littering, said Reed Collins, chief creative officer at Ogilvy &amp; Mather Group, Hong Kong, said in a
           <a href="http://www.ogilvy.com/News/Press-Releases/April-2015-OM-Hong-Kong-uses-DNA-testing-to-put-a-face-to-litterbugs.aspx">
            statement
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           We are thrilled to be part of this innovative campaign, which is sure to have a positive impact on people and the community. Last year, during the six-week Hong Kong Cleanup Challenge, 418 teams comprising 51,064 participants, collected a total of 3,894,000 kgs of litter from city streets, coastal areas and country trails. Sadly, we suffer from a serious pick up after me mentality, and this simply must change, added Lisa Christensen, Founder and CEO of The Hong Kong Cleanup.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ogilvy will also be promoting a video that demonstrates the experiment and warns people not to litter at the risk of becoming the next face of the campaign.
          </p>
          <p>
           Amazing, right? Would you litter again if you knew that your face might end up on a giant billboard in your city? Think about it.
