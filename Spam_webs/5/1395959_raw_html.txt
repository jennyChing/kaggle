        <h1 class="entry-title">
         What is Social Listening?
        </h1>
        <span class="author">
         Posted by: Josephine Hardy
        </span>
       </div>
      </header>
      <!-- .entry-header -->
      <div class="entry-content">
       <p>
        Trillions of online conversations are occurring each day. While some of these conversations are protected, others are in the public domain, and are accessible to social data aggregators like GNIP and Topsy.
       </p>
       <p>
        Most social listening and analytics platforms use keywords to filter the large pool of available data so that particular data sets can be processed and analyzed. Collecting and organizing this data requires algorithms, and filtering applications, that most small businesses could not afford to accomplish on their own. Big businesses also prefer to outsource to companies like DataRank who specialize in social listening.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span id="more-290">
        </span>
       </p>
       <h3>
        Where Does The Data Come From?
       </h3>
       <p>
        Online conversation can be pulled from any public data source. These can include Social Media sources like Twitter, Facebook, Pinterest, and Instagram. They can an also include eCommerce sites like Walmart.com, Target.com, Amazon etc. Most businesses have a specific goal in mind when using social listening services. Some CPG brands, for example, may not have any interest in social media mentions, but do want eCommerce, forum and blog mentions to be tracked. Others may want to track a social media campaign, and therefore, require only social media data to be used.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Deriving Insight Through Social Listening
       </h3>
       <p>
        Social listening platforms dont just pull andorganize data from online conversation. The data needs to be analyzed for sentiment. (Is the comment negative or positive?) A variety of filtering tools are usually provided by the social listening companys platform. DataRank, for example, offers a varietyof services to help businesses understand and better convert data to actionable insights:
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Sentiment Analysis
        </em>
        Determines the attitude of the author of a specific piece of content with respect to the topic of interest. Comments and content can be referred to as Positive, Negative, Neutral or have no sentiment at all.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Theme Groups
        </em>
        A group of comments that fit within a certain theme for a topic; typically unique to the characteristics of a certain product or general consumer language. Frequently used theme groups include price, packaging, and performance and are created through a grouping of keywords.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Theme Correlation
        </em>
         The extent to which one theme groups overlaps with another. For example, when an individual mentions a Coupon and Price, we would see correlation between the Price and Coupon theme groups.
       </p>
       <p>
        You can read a bit more about what DataRank 2.0 is capable of achieving
        <a href="http://blog.datarank.com/announcing-datarank-2-0-future-social-listening/">
         here
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <h3>
        What Can Brand Monitoring Accomplish?
       </h3>
       <p>
        Social listening companies provide the service that allows businesses to monitor online conversation. The following list is asmall example of what is achievable fromusing a social listening company:
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Monitor Brand Health
        </li>
        <li>
         Establish a social media presence
        </li>
        <li>
         Analyze conversation about competitors
        </li>
        <li>
         Determine areas for brand growth
        </li>
        <li>
         Track social media engagement with a new product or service
        </li>
        <li>
         Analyze brand feedback to make focussed marketing decisions
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        Still a little unsure? We regularly post examples on our blog where we use data to analyze brands, or events. Check out
        <a href="http://blog.datarank.com/meeting-supply-demands-walmart-halloween-shoppers/">
         Meeting the Supply Demands of Walmart Halloween Shoppers
        </a>
        or
        <a href="http://blog.datarank.com/opi-vs-essie-summer-nail-polish-trends-2014/">
         OPI vs Essie: Summer Nail Polish Trends 2014
        </a>
        to see different examples of social listening at work.
       </p>

        DataRank also has
        <a href="https://datarank.com/casestudies">

        published on our website that illustrates how our social listening tool has had real-life positive impacts on businesses and brands.
