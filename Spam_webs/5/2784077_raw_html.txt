function updateCountdowns() {
	editbuttons = $('button.comment_edit:visible');
	if (editbuttons.length) {
		editbuttons.each(function() {
			timeremaining = $(this).data('editwindow-remaining');
			if (--timeremaining &lt; 0) {
				$(this).text('edit timer expired');
				$(this).fadeOut(); //no need for further time update; we only poll visibles
			} else {
				$(this).text('edit ('+timeremaining+' seconds remaining)');
				$(this).data('editwindow-remaining', timeremaining);
			}
		})
	} else {
		//no reason to keep this running when there's nothing to update
		clearInterval(countdownTimer);
	}
}

var countdownTimer = null;

$(document).bind('loginStatus', function(e,d){
	servertime = Number(d.unixtime);

	if (d.logged_in) {
		userID = d.user_id;

		handle.text(d.handle)
		handle.attr('href', handle.attr('href')+'/'+userID);
		//look for all the editable comments by this user
		$('div#commentcontainer li[data-user-id='+userID+']').each(function( index ) {
			commenttimestamp = Number($(this).data('comment-timestamp'));
			elapsedtime = servertime - commenttimestamp;
			if (elapsedtime &lt; editing_window_seconds) {
				secondsremaining = editing_window_seconds - elapsedtime;
				$(this).find('button.comment_edit').show().data('editwindow-remaining', secondsremaining);
			}
		});
		countdownTimer = setInterval(updateCountdowns, 1E3);
	} else {
		$("#user_login_control").html('&lt;a href="http://reason.com/users/login"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Login&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt; or &lt;a href="http://reason.com/users/register"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Sign-up&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;');

        <div class="entry postcontent">
         <p>
          <span class="addcaption pic right" style="width:300px">
           <img alt="lierne/Flickr" height="225" src="https://d1ai9qtk9p41kl.cloudfront.net/assets/db/14229136382727.jpg?h=225&amp;w=300" title="" width="300"/>
           <span class="caption">
            lierne/Flickr
           </span>
          </span>
          Every now
and then lawmaking goes delightfully, rather than horribly, wrong.
Today in this rare but beautiful category: a
          <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/news/volokh-conspiracy/wp/2015/02/02/this-would-be-the-perfect-crime-if-it-werent-so-pointless/" target="_blank">
           Flordia law meant to criminalize teen sexting
          </a>
          actually makes it impossible for the state to prosecute teens for
sending each other sexual images.
         </p>
         <p>
          Mark Joseph Stern at
          <em>
           Slate
          </em>
          <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/future_tense/2015/02/02/florida_teenagers_can_sext_with_legal_impunity_thanks_to_stupid_legislature.html?wpsrc=sh_all_dt_tw_top" target="_blank">
           explains
          </a>
          :
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           In 2011, the legislature passed a "sexting" statute barring
minors from sending images of nudity (their own or somebody elses)
to other minors. The first offense would qualify as only a civil
infraction; minors who violated the law would merely have to
perform court-ordered community service or pay a $60 fine. The
second and third offenses, however, would qualify as misdemeanors,
while the fourth offense would qualify as a felony.
          </p>
          <p>
           Under this law, Florida prosecutors thought they had a slam-dunk
case when they brought charges against a minor who texted a picture
of her own vagina to a classmate because she was "bored." But the
state quickly ran into a problem: Florida law doesnt give any
court jurisdiction of
           <em>
            civil
           </em>
           infractions by
juvenilesas opposed to criminal infractionsand the sexting
statute doesnt grant any court this kind of jurisdiction.
Accordingly, no court in the state currently has legal authority to
hear a case involving minors sexting. The prosecutors attempting to
prosecute the sexting teen got their case thrown out of court, a
decision an appeals court
           <a href="http://www.4dca.org/opinions/Jan%202015/01-07-15/4D13-2168.op.pdf" target="_blank">
            later affirmed
           </a>
           .
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Because no one can be convicted of a first offense, there's no
charging teens for subsequent offenses either, obviously. Now that
the state knows the statute stinks, however, it probably won't be
long until they amend it to legitimize their batshit insane
aims.
         </p>
         <p>
          Florida isn't the only state that thinks criminalizing
consensual communication between teens is somehow a worthwhile (and
constitutional) activity. In Pennsylvania, it's a crime for a minor
to share nude images of anyone ages 12-17, including
themselves.In Arizona, it's a misdemeanor for a juvenile to
send
          <em>
           or receive
          </em>
          a nude or sexually-explicit picture. And
these are just a few examples. I've been cataloguing a bunch of
recent instances of police treating teen sexting as a criminal
matter (results coming soon!) across the country. Of course, the
teen sextingpanic has been building for years nowhere's
Jacob Sullum writing
          <a href="http://reason.com/archives/2009/04/29/sexting-scare" target="_blank">
           about teen sexters arrested on child pornography
charges
          </a>
          in 2009 in Pennsylvania. More instances of such
silliness
          <a href="http://reason.com/blog/2013/10/29/san-diego-police-about-to-teach-local-te" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://reason.com/blog/2014/04/08/teen-sexting-can-ruin-your-reputation-an" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://reason.com/blog/2014/07/09/cops-want-to-give-teen-an-erection-and-p" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          , and
          <a href="http://reason.com/blog/2014/10/15/sexting-didnt-ruin-this-girls-life-but-a" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          .
         </p>
