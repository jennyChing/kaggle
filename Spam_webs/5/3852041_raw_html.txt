     <p>
      The
      <b>
       interplanetary internet
      </b>
      (based on
      <a href="/wiki/InterPlaNet" title="InterPlaNet">
       IPN
      </a>
      , also called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/InterPlaNet" title="InterPlaNet">
        InterPlaNet
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is a conceived
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">
       computer network
      </a>
      in space, consisting of a set of
      <a href="/wiki/Node_(networking)" title="Node (networking)">
       network nodes
      </a>
      that can communicate with each other.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceref_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-spaceref-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Communication would be greatly delayed by the great
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_space" title="Interplanetary space">
       interplanetary
      </a>
      distances, so the IPN needs a new set of
      <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" title="Communications protocol">
       protocols
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">
       technology
      </a>
      that are tolerant to large delays and errors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceref_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-spaceref-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the Internet as it is known today tends to be a busy
      <i>
       network of networks
      </i>
      with high traffic, negligible delay and errors, and a wired backbone, the interplanetary Internet is a
      <a href="/wiki/Store_and_forward" title="Store and forward">
       store and forward
      </a>
      <i>
       network of internets
      </i>
      that is often disconnected, has a wireless backbone fraught with error-prone links and delays ranging from tens of minutes to even hours, even when there is a connection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Space communication technology has steadily evolved from expensive, one-of-a-kind point-to-point architectures, to the re-use of technology on successive missions, to the development of standard protocols agreed upon by space agencies of many countries. This last phase has gone on since 1982 through the efforts of the
      <a href="/wiki/CCSDS" title="CCSDS">
       Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS)
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a body composed of the major space agencies of the world. It has 11 member agencies, 22 observer agencies, and over 100 industrial associates.
     </p>
     <p>
      The evolution of space data system standards has gone on in parallel with the evolution of the Internet, with conceptual cross-pollination where fruitful, but largely as a separate evolution. Since the late 1990s, familiar Internet protocols and CCSDS space link protocols have integrated and converged in several ways, for example, the successful
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FTP" title="FTP">
       FTP file transfer
      </a>
      to Earth-orbiting
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Technology_Research_Vehicle#STRV_1A_.26_1B" title="Space Technology Research Vehicle">
       STRV 1B
      </a>
      on January 2, 1996, which ran FTP over the CCSDS IPv4-like
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Communications_Protocol_Specifications" title="Space Communications Protocol Specifications">
       Space Communications Protocol Specifications
      </a>
      (SCPS) protocols.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Internet Protocol use without CCSDS has taken place on spacecraft, e.g., demonstrations on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UoSAT-12_satellite" title="UoSAT-12 satellite">
       UoSAT-12 satellite
      </a>
      , and operationally on the
      <a href="/wiki/Disaster_Monitoring_Constellation" title="Disaster Monitoring Constellation">
       Disaster Monitoring Constellation
      </a>
      . Having reached the era where networking and IP on board spacecraft have been shown to be feasible and reliable, a forward-looking study of the bigger picture was the next phase.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cerf%27s_Up-marquee-20071031.jpg">

       </a>

        meeting,
        <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">

     <p>
      The Interplanetary Internet study at NASA's
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">
       Jet Propulsion Laboratory
      </a>
      (JPL) was started by a team of scientists at JPL led by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinton_Cerf" title="Vinton Cerf">
       Vinton Cerf
      </a>
      and Adrian Hooke.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cerf is one of the pioneers of the Internet on Earth, and currently holds the position of distinguished visiting scientist at JPL. Hooke is one of the directors of the CCSDS.
     </p>
     <p>
      While IP-like SCPS protocols are feasible for short hops, such as ground station to orbiter,
      <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">
       rover
      </a>
      -to-lander, lander-to-orbiter, probe-to-flyby, and so on,
      <a href="/wiki/Delay-tolerant_networking" title="Delay-tolerant networking">
       delay-tolerant networking
      </a>
      is needed to get information from one region of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">
       solar system
      </a>
      to another. It becomes apparent that the concept of a "region" is a natural architectural factoring of the InterPlanetary Internet.
     </p>
     <p>
      A "region" is an area where the characteristics of communication are the same.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunset_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sunset-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Region characteristics include communications, security, the maintenance of resources, perhaps ownership, and other factors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunset_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sunset-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Interplanetary Internet is a "network of regional internets."
     </p>
     <p>
      What is needed then, is a standard way to achieve end-to-end communication through multiple regions in a disconnected, variable-delay environment using a generalized suite of protocols. Examples of regions might include the terrestrial Internet as a region, a region on the surface of the moon or Mars, or a ground-to-orbit region.
     </p>
     <p>
      The recognition of this requirement led to the concept of a "bundle" as a high-level way to address the generalized Store-and-Forward problem. Bundles are an area of new protocol development in the upper layers of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osi_model" title="Osi model">
       OSI model
      </a>
      , above the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osi_model#Layer_4:_transport_layer" title="Osi model">
       Transport Layer
      </a>
      with the goal of addressing the issue of bundling store-and-forward information so that it can reliably traverse radically dissimilar environments constituting a "network of regional internets."
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Delay-tolerant_networking" title="Delay-tolerant networking">
       Delay-tolerant networking
      </a>
      (DTN) was designed to enable standardized communications over long distances and through time delays. At its core is something called the Bundle Protocol (BP), which is similar to the Internet Protocol, or IP, that serves as the heart of the Internet here on Earth. The big difference between the regular Internet Protocol (IP) and the Bundle Protocol is that IP assumes a seamless end-to-end data path, while BP is built to account for errors and disconnections  glitches that commonly plague deep-space communications.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bundle Service Layering, implemented as the Bundling protocol suite for
      <a href="/wiki/Delay-tolerant_networking" title="Delay-tolerant networking">
       delay-tolerant networking
      </a>
      , will provide general purpose delay-tolerant protocol services in support of a range of applications: custody transfer, segmentation and reassembly, end-to-end reliability, end-to-end security, and end-to-end routing among them. The Bundle Protocol was first tested in space on the
      <a href="/wiki/UK-DMC" title="UK-DMC">
       UK-DMC satellite
      </a>
      in 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="900" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg/210px-Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg/315px-Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg/420px-Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg 2x" width="210"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deep_Impact_HRI.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(spacecraft)" title="Deep Impact (spacecraft)">
          Deep Impact
         </a>
        </i>
        mission
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An example of one of these end-to-end applications flown on a space mission is the
      <a href="/wiki/CCSDS_File_Delivery_Protocol" title="CCSDS File Delivery Protocol">
       CCSDS File Delivery Protocol
      </a>
      (CFDP), used on the comet mission,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(spacecraft)" title="Deep Impact (spacecraft)">
        Deep Impact
       </a>
      </i>
      . CFDP is an international standard for automatic, reliable file transfer in both directions. (CFDP should not be confused with
      <a href="/wiki/Coherent_file_distribution_protocol" title="Coherent file distribution protocol">
       Coherent File Distribution Protocol
      </a>
      , which unfortunately has the same acronym and is an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IETF" title="IETF">
       IETF
      </a>
      -documented experimental protocol for rapidly deploying files to multiple targets in a highly networked environment.)
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to reliably copying a file from one entity (such as a spacecraft or ground station) to another entity, CFDP has the capability to reliably transmit arbitrary small messages defined by the user, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">
       metadata
      </a>
      accompanying the file, and to reliably transmit commands relating to file system management that are to be executed automatically on the remote end-point entity (such as a spacecraft) upon successful reception of a file.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">
       Implementation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Implementation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The dormant InterPlanetary Internet Special Interest Group of the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_Society" title="Internet Society">
       Internet Society
      </a>
      has worked on defining protocols and standards that would make the IPN possible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Delay-Tolerant Networking Research Group (DTNRG) is the primary group researching
      <a href="/wiki/Delay-tolerant_networking" title="Delay-tolerant networking">
       Delay-tolerant networking
      </a>
      (DTN). Additional research efforts focus on various uses of the new technology.
     </p>
     <p>
      The canceled
      <a href="/wiki/Mars_Telecommunications_Orbiter" title="Mars Telecommunications Orbiter">
       Mars Telecommunications Orbiter
      </a>
      had been planned to establish an Interplanetary Internet link between Earth and Mars, in order to support other Mars missions. It would have used
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_communications" title="Optical communications">
       optical communications
      </a>
      using
      <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
       laser
      </a>
      beams for their lower
      <a href="/wiki/Ping_(networking_utility)" title="Ping (networking utility)">
       ping
      </a>
      rates than radiowaves. "Lasercom sends information using beams of light and optical elements, such as telescopes and optical amplifiers, rather than RF signals, amplifiers, and antennas"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NASA
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JPL" title="JPL">
       JPL
      </a>
      continued to test the DTN protocol with their Deep Impact Networking (DINET) experiment on board the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(spacecraft)" title="Deep Impact (spacecraft)">
        Deep Impact
       </a>
      </i>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/EPOXI" title="EPOXI">
       EPOXI
      </a>
      spacecraft in October, 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In May 2009, DTN was deployed to a payload on board the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">
       ISS
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      NASA and BioServe Space Technologies, a research group at the University of Colorado, have been continuously testing DTN on two Commercial Generic Bioprocessing Apparatus (CGBA) payloads. CGBA-4 and CGBA-5 serve as computational and communications platforms which are remotely controlled from BioServe's Payload Operations Control Center (POCC) in Boulder, CO.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In October 2012 ISS Station commander
      <a href="/wiki/Sunita_Williams" title="Sunita Williams">
       Sunita Williams
      </a>
      remotely operated Mocup (Meteron Operations and Communications Prototype), a "cat-sized"
      <a href="/wiki/Lego_Mindstorms" title="Lego Mindstorms">
       Lego Mindstorms
      </a>
      robot fitted with a
      <a href="/wiki/BeagleBoard" title="BeagleBoard">
       BeagleBoard
      </a>
      computer and webcam,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      located in the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Operations_Centre" title="European Space Operations Centre">
       European Space Operations Centre
      </a>
      in Germany in an experiment using DTN.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These initial experiments provide insight into future missions where DTN will enable the extension of networks into deep space to explore other planets and solar system points of interest. Seen as necessary for space exploration, DTN enables timeliness of data return from operating assets which results in reduced risk and cost, increased crew safety, and improved operational awareness and science return for NASA and additional space agencies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      DTN has several major arenas of application, in addition to the Interplanetary Internet, which include sensor networks, military and tactical communications, disaster recovery, hostile environments, mobile devices and remote outposts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As an example of a remote outpost, imagine an isolated Arctic village, or a faraway island, with electricity, one or more computers, but no communication connectivity. With the addition of a simple wireless hotspot in the village, plus DTN-enabled devices on, say, dog sleds or fishing boats, a resident would be able to check their e-mail or click on a Wikipedia article, and have their requests forwarded to the nearest networked location on the sled's or boat's next visit, and get the replies on its return.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_orbit">
       Earth orbit
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Earth orbit">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Earth orbit is sufficiently nearby that conventional protocols can be used. For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
       International Space Station
      </a>
      is connected to the regular terrestrial Internet. However, the space station also serves as a useful platform to develop, experiment, and implement systems that make up the interplanetary internet. NASA and the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
       European Space Agency
      </a>
      (ESA) have used an experimental version of interplanetary Internet to control an educational rover, placed at the European Space Operations Centre in Darmstadt, Germany, from the International Space Station. The experiment used the DTN protocol to demonstrate technology that one day may enable Internet-like communications that can support habitats or infrastructure on another planet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
