     <p>
      An Inglewood vet and leptospirosis victim is appealing to farmers to protect those around them by making sure their animals are vaccinated against the debilitating disease.
     </p>
     <p>
      Aaron Chambers' message to farmers to have their stock vaccinated is backed by 18 years of experience as a vet in Taranaki.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the wake of his illness, he's also appealing to lifestyle block owners who may not even know about leptospirosis to vaccinate their sheep, cattle, goats and pigs.
     </p>
     <p>
      "The disease is out there and you need to minimise your chances of getting it. Take it seriously. Wear shoes and put plaster on cuts."
     </p>
     <p>
      Chambers said leptospirosis was a disease that shouldn't occur in a first world country. "But we'll never get rid of it and vaccination is the key to controlling it.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Being a vet is a high-risk occupation for this disease.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Lepto is always in the back of our minds - but as one of those things that happen to other people," he said.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Inglewood Veterinary Services (IVS) vet said the practice's vets vaccinated virtually all their clients' dairy herds.
     </p>
     <p>
      While he thought vets throughout Taranaki performed the vaccinations, in other parts of the country farmers usually did them.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taranaki vets kept control of and certified farm leptospirosis risk management programmes because the disease posed such a high risk to farmers, their families, their employees, AB technicians, TB testers, vets and other contractors like stock truck drivers. "This means we know we have minimised the risk of leptospirosis infection to people and the dairy herd."
     </p>
     <p>
      The programme included staff education and hazard identification, which ensured farmers were meeting their Health and Safety in Employment Act obligations.
     </p>
     <p>
      If farmers did their own vaccinations, vets could not certify the elements of refrigerated storage and correct dosage, injection site and timing had been met. "If we run and implement the vaccination programme, we know it has been done correctly on all of our dairy farms. When most of my day is spent at the back end of a cow, knowing the cows' vaccination status is current is reassuring."
     </p>
     <p>
      Chambers said only a couple of IVS clients did not get their herds vaccinated. In the 2011-12 dairy season, the practice's leptospirosis vaccination rate of dairy calves, heifers and herds exceeded 98.5 per cent.
     </p>
     <p>
      "There are some farmers who don't do it. For some it's the cost, even though it's cheap. Others think it causes abortions, which is absolute nonsense."
     </p>
     <p>
      A trial 10 years ago had shown no evidence of a link between leptospirosis vaccinations and abortions. "It's a myth - an excuse not to do it."
     </p>
     <p>
      Vaccination of calves is due to start about now when they're three months' old and they receive a booster in four to six weeks, followed by an annual booster.
     </p>
     <p>
      Chambers said although vaccination of stock reduced the prevalence and spread of leptospirosis, farmers should realise it did not provide complete protection.
     </p>
     <p>
      They should also control carrier species like rats which could contaminate in-shed feeding supplies and calf meal. "Rats urinate on food which gets eaten - it's just a big cycle."
     </p>

     <p>
      Chambers caught the disease in August at the height of calving and when vets are most exposed to the spires that cause it. He thought he had a really bad dose of the flu. "We were under pressure and everyone at the practice had the flu." For five weeks he continued working until he returned home exhausted one day after inserting bloat bullets in about 400 cows. He went to bed and slept for three days. "I had the sweats, a constant headache, aching back and legs. I was tired - like I've never been."
     </p>
     <p>
      When he contacted his general practitioner in Waitara, he had to wait a week for an appointment, so he went instead to a New Plymouth clinic. During the discussion with the doctor, leptospirosis was considered. A blood test confirmed the diagnosis and treatment was a course of antibiotics and panadol. Now he's waiting for further results from the blood test in the hope it will show the infection's source - likely to be an unvaccinated cow.
     </p>
     <p>
      After the three-day sleep, he returned to work and spent the next week trying to fulfil his duties until "the boss (IVS general manager Greg Hall ) kicked me out".
     </p>
     <p>
      Hall said he wouldn't wish leptospirosis on anyone. "We're a team here and we understand that it's a disease picked up at work and it could have happened to any one of us."
     </p>
     <p>
      Chambers said at that time of year all the vets were "under the pump", but luckily two locums were just starting work.
     </p>
     <p>
      Grateful to have three weeks off, he returned to work a couple of weeks ago. "I'm just doing half days, otherwise I get hammered in the legs and back. I'll take it day by day, but it'll be a while before I'll be back fulltime."
     </p>
     <p>
      After four hours at work, he goes home and sleeps. "It's horrible, I've no energy to do anything. You lose motivation - it's a tiredness I've never experienced.
     </p>
     <p>
      "I've had a gutsful, to be honest. I've had a heap of time off work and I don't know how long that will go on and whether I'll have relapses."
     </p>
     <p>
      He's yet to see a clinician to discuss his prognosis, but expects it'll be months before he recovers and regains his stamina.
     </p>
     <p>
      A keen sportsman, he's previously undertaken adventure racing. Before illness struck, he was planning to run the ultra-marathon in next month's Round The Mountain Relay. "That was squashed before I even knew I had lepto," he said.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 40-year-old father of five children aged between 1 and 9 has to walk when he joins family touch rugby games. And he'd like to be able to mow his lawns without being exhausted.
     </p>
     <p>
      "I'm a pretty active sort of guy and now I've got no stamina. I'm not sure what's ahead and it's totally frustrating and frightening."
     </p>
     <p>
      Since he's had the illness, he's talked to what he says is an amazing number of people who have also had leptospirosis
     </p>
     <p>
      One is Bell Block farmer Justin Bishop, who said he'd had leptospirosis when he was about 20, but had had no relapses. "I was pretty crook for about three weeks." He also thought his father had had it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taranaki Federated Farmers president Bronwyn Muir said herd vaccination did not necessarily provide full protection from leptospirosis because the disease had many strains.
     </p>
     <p>
      She said the increased use of imported feed had led to a rise in rodents and bird life. She advised farmers to be careful about the way they handled feed and stored water, to keep roofs and gutters clean, and to clean out water tanks.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       AT A GLANCE
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      -New Zealand has the developed world's highest human rate of  leptospirosis - a zoonotic disease because it is passed from animals to  humans.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Humans can get leptospirosis from:
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      -Animal urine splashed in the eye and mouth, on cuts and grazes, or inhaled through the nose.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Contaminated water.
     </p>
     <p>
      -An infected animal being butchered.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Dagging and shearing infected sheep and trimming tails.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Preventing leptospirosis infection
       <br/>
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      -Vaccinate livestock and give an annual booster.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Control rats by traps and poison and by reducing available feed and rubbish.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Put waterproof plasters on cuts and grazes.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Reduce urine contamination.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Do not smoke in the cowshed.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Wear footwear in the cowshed, paddocks, yards and contaminated areas.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Wear gloves.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       The symptoms
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      -Extreme tiredness.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Fever.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Body aches and muscular pain.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Severe and persistent headaches.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Eye irritation.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Loss of appetite.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Abdominal pain.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Diarrhoea.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Vomiting.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       It might also cause:
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      -Body rashes.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Jaundice.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Liver and kidney failure.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Meningitis.
     </p>
     <p>
      -Leptospirosis victims might be unable to work and might need to be hospitalised.
     </p>
     <p>
      -The illness can be fatal.
     </p>
