        <p>
         Dont let anybody tell you differenteveryone has skipped a workout at some point. At Greatist, were firm believers in cutting yourself
         <a href="http://greatist.com/move/benefits-of-low-intensity-workouts">
          some slack
         </a>
         and taking time off from exercise when you need to. But we also know how easily three days off can snowball into six, then 10. Before you know it, youre asking that question weve all asked when the gym feels like a distant memory: How long does it take to lose my fitness?
        </p>
        <p>
         First, its important to remember that taking time off now and again is a good thingexercise inflicts a degree of stress on the body, and any good workout program includes a heck of a lot of rest days, especially if the exercise is
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/improve-workout-intensity-tips">
          very intense
         </a>
         . And there's a benefit to both "
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/18-scientifically-proven-ways-speed-recovery">
          active recovery
         </a>
         " and complete rest.
        </p>
        <div class="dfp-tag-wrapper dfp-ad-article-ad-mobile-inline" data-ad-dfp="/38507188/ROS_300x250_Mobile_Entry" data-ad-size="300x250" data-ad-slot="div-gpt-ad-1410556946418-0" data-dfp-g-id="article-ad-mobile-inline" data-restrict-context-to="medium,small,x-small">
        </div>
        <p>
         That said, use it or lose it is pretty much the rule. But exactly how much fitness youll lose depends on the length of your break and how fit you were to begin with.
        </p>
        <h3>
         If You Exercise on the Regular
        </h3>
        <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image" data-image-type="in-post-image-stock">
         <img alt="Athlete Lost Fitness" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/AthleteLostFitness.jpg?itok=A5vnGznw"/>
        </span>
        <p>
         Its a lot easier to bounce back from time off if youre someone who exercises five or six times a week, or if youve been exercising for a while.
        </p>
        <p>
         Generally speaking, if youve been working out several times a week for more than a year, your
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/how-stay-strong-and-prevent-muscle-loss">
          muscle memory
         </a>
         is solid
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20713720">
           Myonuclei acquired by overload exercise precede hypertrophy and are not lost on detraining
          </a>
          . Bruusgaard J.C., Johansen I.B., Egner I.M., et al. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America, 2010 Aug 24;107(34):15111-6.
         </cite>
         . In fact, with that strong of an exercise habit, scientists are quite willing to drop you in the athlete category. And for athletes, your fitness can deteriorate at different rates depending on whether you're looking at strength or cardiovascular losses.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Strength Loss
        </h4>
        <p>
         For most people, strength loss occurs after about two and a half to three weeks of inactivity, says
         <a href="http://mollygalbraith.com" style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
          Molly Galbraith
         </a>
         <span style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.175999999046326px; line-height:28.1599998474121px">
          , a certified strength and conditioning specialist and co-founder of
         </span>
         <a href="http://www.girlsgonestrong.com" style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
          Girls Gone Strong
         </a>
         <span style="letter-spacing:0.175999999046326px; line-height:28.1599979400635px">
          . But it depends on why you take the break.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         If you are sick, your body is overstressed, so youll start to lose strength after two to three weeks," she says. "If youre not sick, and especially if youre able to get in some movement and light exercise, you can probably take three, four, even five weeks off without significant strength loss.
        </p>
        <p>
         Science agrees.
         <em>
          Medicine and Science in Sports and Exercise
         </em>
         published a review of several studies on the subject that looked at runners, rowers, and power athletes. For all of these groups, muscular strength fibers appear not to change, even after a month of inactivity. But heres the kicker: While general strength doesnt change much in that period, specialized, sport-specific muscle fibers start to change in as little as two weeks without a workout
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11474330">
           Muscular characteristics of detraining in humans
          </a>
          . Mujika I., Padilla S. Medicine &amp; Science in Sports &amp; Exercise, 2001 Aug;33(8):1297-303.
         </cite>
         . For example, endurance athletes lose a significant amount of the extra
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/what-are-fast-and-slow-twitch-muscles">
          slow-twitch muscle fibers
         </a>
         that they worked so hard to accumulate, and the same thing happens for the power athletes and their hard-earned fast-twitch muscle fibers.
        </p>
        <p>
         Basically the body likes to hold onto strength for as long as it can, but skills that are very specialized for certain sports will decline faster. We're generalists, what can we say?
        </p>
        <h4>
         Cardio Loss
        </h4>
        <p>
         So what about all the cardio lovers out there who are more concerned with the strength of their heart and lungs? Sadly we lose this kind of conditioning a little more quickly than we lose strength. One study of endurance cyclists found that
         <span style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.176000013947487px; line-height:28.1599998474121px">
          four weeks of inactivity
         </span>
         resulted in a 20 percent decrease of their
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/ultimate-guide-running-lingo">
          VO2 max
         </a>
         , which measures a persons maximum capacity to take in, transport, and use oxygen during exercise
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8282588">
           Effects of detraining on endurance capacity and metabolic changes during prolonged exhaustive exercise
          </a>
          . Madsen K., Pedersen P.K., Djurhuus M.S., et al. Journal of Applied Physiology, 1993 Oct;75(4):1444-51.
         </cite>
         . The results were more or less confirmed by another study, which found that after 12 days of inactivity, VO2 max dropped by seven percent and enzymes in the blood associated with endurance performance decreased by 50 percent
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6511559">
           Time course of loss of adaptations after stopping prolonged intense endurance training
          </a>
          . Coyle E.F., Martin W.H. 3rd, Sinacore D.R., et al. Journal of applied physiology: respiratory, environmental and exercise physiology, 1984 Dec;57(6):1857-64.
         </cite>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         But keep your chin up. While your cardio conditioning does fall faster than your strength, its easier to regain,
         <span style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.176000013947487px; line-height:28.1600017547607px">
          Galbraith says
         </span>
         . So get back on that horse, cowboy.
        </p>
        <h3>
         If Youre Newer to Exercise
        </h3>
        <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image" data-image-type="in-post-image-stock">
         <img alt="Newbie Lost Fitness" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/NewbieLostFitness.jpg?itok=41pCDie5"/>
        </span>
        <p>
         Congratulations on your new-ish exercise habit! But if youve hit pause on your trips to the gym, dont take too long to hit play again. Consistency is key for building new habits, and its as true for the body as it is for the mind: If your body hasnt been enjoying exercise for long, it can be easier to lose the progress youve made.
        </p>
        <h4>
         <strong style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.175999999046326px; line-height:28.1599998474121px">
          Strength Loss
         </strong>
        </h4>
        <p>
         As far as strength goes, its best not to be too concerned about losing your headway, as those famous newbie gains make it somewhat easier to retain strength.
        </p>
        <p>
         For example, previously untrained folks who took a three-week break in the middle of a 15-week bench press program finished the course with similar strength levels as those who didnt take a break at all
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21771261">
           Effects of periodic and continued resistance training on muscle CSA and strength in previously untrained men
          </a>
          . Ogasawara R., Yasuda T., Sakamaki M., et al. Clinical Physiology and Functional Imaging, 2011 Sep;31(5):399-404.
         </cite>
         . One study even showed that six months after quitting a 4-month strength training program, up to 50 percent of the original strength gain was maintained
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2311599">
           Muscle hypertrophy and fast fiber type conversions in heavy resistance-trained women
          </a>
          . Staron R.S., Malicky E.S., Leonardi M.J., et al. European Journal of Applied Physiology. 1990;60(1):71-9.
         </cite>
         . It's also worth noting that among newbies, eccentric strength, that is, the strength used when lengthening a muscle or lowering a weight, may be harder to lose than concentric strength, which is when the muscle is contracted. A study of 13 previously untrained guys found that three months after ending a three-month training program, they had maintained their eccentric strength gains, but not their concentric strength
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15702342">
           Neuromuscular adaptations to detraining following resistance training in previously untrained subjects
          </a>
          . Andersen L.L., Andersen J.L., Magnusson S.P., et al. European Journal of Applied Physiology. 2005 Mar;93(5-6):511-8.
         </cite>
         .
        </p>
        <h4>
         <strong style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.175999999046326px; line-height:28.1599998474121px">
          Cardio Loss
         </strong>
        </h4>
        <p>
         Once again, cardio is a little more sensitive to time off. One of the best studies of the effects of detraining on recently acquired fitness gains found that VO2 max gains that were made in the last two months are completely lost after four weeks of inactivity
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11252068">
           Cardiorespiratory and metabolic characteristics of detraining in humans
          </a>
          . Mujika I., Padilla S. Medicine &amp; Science in Sports &amp; Exercise, 2001 Mar;33(3):413-21.
         </cite>
         .
        </p>
        <h3>
         Other Factors
        </h3>
        <p>
         While your fitness level is key to how quickly you get back to your fitness baseline, there are a few other variables that also come into play.
        </p>
        <p>
         First, age plays a role in your bounce-back time
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16046344">
           Short term and long term detraining: is there any difference between young-old and old people?
          </a>
          Toraman N.F. British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2005 Aug;39(8):561-4.
         </cite>
         . When looking at 41 people who were either 20 to 30 years old or 65 to 75 years old, the older subjects lost strength almost twice as fast as the whippersnappers during a six-month detraining period in one study
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10949019">
           Age and gender responses to strength training and detraining
          </a>
          . Lemmer J.T., Hurlbut D.E., Martel G.F., et al. Medicine &amp; Science in Sports &amp; Exercise, 2000 Aug;32(8):1505-12.
         </cite>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         And again, why youre taking the break is also a factor. When scientists
         <span style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.176000013947487px; line-height:28.1600017547607px">
          injected inactive volunteers with hormones that mimicked the stress of trauma or illness, they had a 28 percent decrease in strength over 28 days
         </span>
         a higher rate than average
         <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16984982">
           Atrophy and impaired muscle protein synthesis during prolonged inactivity and stress
          </a>
          . Paddon-Jones D., Sheffield-Moore M., Cree M.G., et al. Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism, 2006 Dec;91(12):4836-41.
         </cite>
         .
        </p>
        <h3>
         4 Ways to Make the Most of a Fitness Break
        </h3>
        <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image" data-image-type="in-post-image-stock">
         <img alt="Lost Fitness " class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/LostFitness.jpg?itok=E1mur_na"/>
        </span>
        <p>
         Whether you're on a relaxing
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/best-fitness-vacations-2014" style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.176000013947487px; line-height: 28.1600017547607px;">
          vacation
         </a>
         <span style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.176000013947487px; line-height:28.1600017547607px">
         </span>
         <span style="font-size:17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing:0.176000013947487px; line-height:28.1600017547607px">
          or stuck on the couch with an annoying chest infection,
         </span>
         there are a few ways to stay strong during downtime.
        </p>
        <section class="slideshow list-image-full" ng-controller="SlideshowListCtrl" ng-init="init()">
         <div class="slide">
          <div class="title-wrapper">
           <h4>
            1. Do Light Cardio
           </h4>
          </div>
          <div class="slide-body">
           <p>
            If youre able to take plenty of brisk walks, keeping your heart rate in the 120-ish range, then you should be able to stave off losing conditioning for a little longer, Galbraith says. Indeed, training a little will do a much better job of maintaining your gains than totally stopping, especially if youre able to squeeze in the odd cardio session thatll train you at the upper end of your VO2 max, like some quick intervals
            <cite class="citation-reference" data-cite-reference="">
             <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10999420">
              Detraining: loss of training-induced physiological and performance adaptations. Part II: Long term insufficient training stimulus
             </a>
             . Mujika I., Padilla S. The American Journal of Sports Medicine, 2000 Sep;30(3):145-54.
            </cite>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="slide">
          <div class="title-wrapper">
           <h4>
            2. Incorporate Some Resistance Training
           </h4>
          </div>
          <div class="slide-body">
           <p>
            <span style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
             There are plenty of reasons for taking a break, but if you have a localized injury, say in your ankle or wrist, dont use it as an excuse to completely stop exercising. Cross-train through injuries, if you can. Do some
             <a href="http://greatist.com/move/stay-fit-positive-while-injured">
              bodyweight exercises
             </a>
             , or see if you can try swimming, which is the go-to exercise for a lot of
             <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/take-it-easy-21-unexpected-low-impact-workouts">
              injured athletes
             </a>
             . Even a four-minute
             <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/best-tabata-moves">
              tabata
             </a>
             or two will make a huge difference in maintaining your strength.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
             Light, dynamic warmups are also a good way to help keep the body from getting too stiff and to slow the
             <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/kelly-starrett-mobility-injury-free">
              loss of mobility
             </a>
             without putting too much additional stress on an overstressed body, Galbraith says. But if youre sick from the neck downthink achy muscles, chest congestion, feverit may be best to rest, she adds.
            </span>
           </p>
          </div>

         <div class="slide">
          <div class="title-wrapper">
           <h4>
            3. Eat Right
           </h4>
          </div>
          <div class="slide-body">
           <p style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
            Exercise helps to control
            <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/can-exercise-help-treat-addiction">
             junk food cravings
            </a>
            , so you may need to try harder to avoid crappy food while youre not working out. Get lots of protein,
            <a href="http://greatist.com/eat/healthy-fats-best-foods-for-brain-health">
             healthy fats
            </a>
            , and
            <a href="http://greatist.com/health/what-glycemic-index">
             low-GI carbs
            </a>
            , and your body will thank you. Eating well will help you avoid any weight gain, which would make restarting fitness all the more challenging. And nutrient-dense foods will also speed up your recovery if youre injured or ill.
           </p>
           <p style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
            Galbraith also suggests raw honey for its antibacterial and antimicrobial properties, homemade
            <a href="http://greatist.com/grow/bone-broth-science">
             bone broths
            </a>
            for hydration, and garlic to lessen the severity of cold symptoms if you're under the weather.
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="slide">
          <div class="title-wrapper">
           <h4>
            4. Love Yourself
           </h4>
          </div>
          <div class="slide-body">
           <p>
            <span style="font-size: 17.6000003814697px; letter-spacing: 0.175999999046326px; line-height: 28.1599998474121px;">
             No, not
             <a href="http://greatist.com/happiness/masturbation-health-benefits">
              like that
             </a>
             . But its important not to judge yourself or lapse into self-loathing on account of taking some time off. The gym will be right there waiting for you when youre ready for it, but for now, do what you can and do what makes you
             <a href="http://greatist.com/grow/how-to-be-happy-long-term">
              happy
             </a>
             . If its seeing what life is like without exercising so darn much, you do you! Look in the mirror, say a
             <a href="http://greatist.com/grow/body-positive-mantras">
              body-positive mantra
             </a>
             , and know that youre perfect
             <a href="http://greatist.com/connect/you-are-not-your-diet">
              no matter how often
             </a>
             you hit the gym.
            </span>
           </p>
