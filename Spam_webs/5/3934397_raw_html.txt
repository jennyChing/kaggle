             <p>
              It seems my site has a few UK followers, and I was recently asked if I have any information on UK government grants for solar power. I tried to dig some up and organize it a bit. Heres what I found:
             </p>
             <p>
              Adopting various green or energy efficient means of powering your home can be a
              <strong>
               <a href="http://lolalovesgreen.com/solar-quote" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                powerfully lucrative endeavour
               </a>
              </strong>
              . Of course, the obvious and immediate benefit  not only for the planet itself and for your own wallet  is of loosening the shackles of big name energy companies; no small feat in our current financial climate.
             </p>
             <p>
              Such a decision cannot be taken lightly, however. Naturally, there are a great many installation
              <a href="http://lolalovesgreen.com/solar-power-pros-cons/" target="_blank" title="Solar Power Pros and Cons">
               costs to consider
              </a>
              , as well as the practicality of turning to solar power in the traditionally gloomy UK. Owing to this uncertainty, the government have launched a number of appealing deals to streamline the process. Predominantly, the offers are the Domestic Renewable Heat Incentive (RHI) and general Feed-In Tariffs (FITs) created in April of 2011, to ease the process, and help put money back in the pockets of UK householders.
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <a href="http://lolalovesgreen.com/solar-quote" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Click to find out what solar might be worth to
               <strong>
                you
               </strong>
              </a>
              !
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               How do these incentives work?
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p>
              Less is known about the RHI, given its young age, than the FITs; yet the fundamental process is the same for both schemes. In essence, the RHI allows all UK householders, private landowners and Registered Providers of Social Housing to earn up to 3,390 per year (paid in quarterly instalments over seven years) when they switch to a number of renewable energy sources; solar power included. The payments are set at a respectable rate, which will reflect future financial forecasts of renewable energies over the next two decades  meaning, even with fluctuation prices, inflation and economic uncertainty, any payments made to you will be based on a fair foundation.
             </p>
             <p>
              Solar power is often plagued with misconception. To function as an efficient energy production means, the panels do not need to be in the sights of constant, unabridged sunlight  which is often brought up as a concerned counter argument for UK home owners. In fact, studies display that a meagre 1m area on a standard house roof can be enough to generate enough energy to cater to the needs of an adult, throughout the UK year  seasons notwithstanding.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               How do I know if my home is eligible and where do I go from there?
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p>
              In order to begin the process, and source your eligibility date (i.e. the date at which an instalment becomes functional and able to generate financial and energy sustainability), you must contact your energy provider and discuss the matter further.
             </p>
             <p>
              This being said, the benefits of subscribing to a TIF can be large in scale. Primarily, the use of solar panels (or other renewable sources) will accumulate a generation tariff: your energy provider will provide you with a set rate of pay per energy unit that your home generates of its own accord. This government fixture will have a set deadline, or overall time, for paying back to you (a maximum of twenty years), but can still produce phenomenal savings.
             </p>
             <p>
              Second to this, is an export tariff  this details energy that you export back to the energy grid; basically any surplus power that is left over from supplying your own home. Again, this must be settled with your particular provider, however research shows that many households can be generating up to 50% more energy than the home needs; equating to 50% you can sell back to the grid, as part of a government funded grant.
             </p>
             <p>
              Given this double functionality, regardless of whether the home signs up to a standard government TIF or the new RHI schemes, the household will begin to save swathes of money on energy bills.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               Free installation of solar panels for pensioners
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p>
              Additionally, there exists the Green Deal scheme. This cashback system allows homeowners to reclaim a portion of their energy efficient investments. However, and unknown to many people, as part of the Green Deal pensioners are entitled to free installation of solar panels. Rationally speaking, pensioners will be less likely to afford the installation fee in the first place (the rough installation cost usually averages at around 5,000  a weighty cost for those who cannot work).
             </p>
             <p>
              In adopting this system, the house owner will not receive the FITs discussed above; this will go directly to the solar panel installation company themselves. Nevertheless, all energy savings and reductions in costs will still feed directly to the occupant of the home. The government greatly encourages the increased use of energy efficient, alternative methods of generating power in the archetypal household  even with the RHI and TIFs discussed, there are other, subtler grants available depending on specific circumstances, homes and generation techniques.
             </p>
             <p>
              Solar power may be the smartest move, though; with fairly simple installation and an assortment of quick, steady grants from the government readily available to any who inquire.
             </p>
