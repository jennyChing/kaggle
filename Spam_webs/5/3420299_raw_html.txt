       <h1 class="article-title">
        An Artist's Renderings of Our Future Cities (If Everything Goes Wrong)
       </h1>
       <section class="motherboard-portlet motherboard-portlet-about" id="yw0">
        <div class="author-info">
         <h3>
          Written by
          <a href="/author/DJPangburn">
           DJ Pangburn
          </a>
         </h3>
        </div>
       </section>
       <time class="meta-time" datetime="2014-08-28T19:20:00+00:00" pubdate="">
        August 28, 2014 // 02:20 PM EST
       </time>
       <section class="socials-header-one-column" data-description="Let Michael Kerbow take you on a tour of today's dystopian milieu, and extrapolate us all into a frightening techno-induced future." data-gatag="Side Widget" data-media="" data-title="An Artist's Renderings of Our Future Cities (If Everything Goes Wrong)" data-topic="" data-twitterhandle="motherboard" data-url="http://motherboard.vice.com/read/an-artists-renderings-of-our-future-cities-if-everything-goes-wrong" id="yw1">
        <div class="social-buttons">
         <a class="twitter sharing-item" data-share-type="twitter" href="#">
          <i class="site-icon twitter">
          </i>
         </a>
         <a class="facebook sharing-item" data-share-type="facebook" href="#">
          <i class="site-icon facebook">
          </i>
         </a>
        </div>
       </section>
       <div class="ad-sponsored-by single-col">
        <div class="motherboard-ad ad-banner" data-ref="sponsored_content_top">
        </div>
       </div>
       <article class="article-single" data-id="14851">
        <div class="wrapper article-content-wrapper">
         <div class="one-column">
          <div class="article-scroll-wrapper">
           <div class="article-content rich-text">
            <p>
             If you've never visualized dystopia, just look around. Visita mega farm in California, a body of water near an industrial plant, or simply walk around Manhattan and let the rapidly evolving island for the rich, jet-setting class seep into your consciousness.
            </p>
            <p>
             If that doesn't do the trick,San Francisco-based artist
             <a href="http://michaelkerbow.com/">
              Michael Kerbow
             </a>
             is here to takeyou on a tour oftoday'sdystopian milieu, and then extrapolate it all into atechno-induced future.
            </p>
            <p>
             Take Kerbow's
             <em>
             </em>
             <a href="http://michaelkerbow.com/series/portents">
              <em>
               Portents
              </em>
              series
             </a>
             . It'sa dark and mesmerizing window into humanity's near and distant futures. On a thematic level, itdeals variouslywith climate change, pollution, over-population, and humanity's Icarus and Tower of Babel-like drive to create new technologies; all toward perhaps someundefined and maybe even non-existent dream.
            </p>
            <p class="has-image">
             <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="819" data-original-width="1024" src="http://images.vice.com/motherboard/content-images/contentimage/14851/1409267183785323.jpg"/>
            </p>
            <p class="photo-credit">
             Their Refinement of the Decline. Image: Michael Kerbow
            </p>
            <p>
             It'sthe sort of work that science fiction fans dig,that wouldkeep Al Gore up at night (for good reason), and forcesskepticsinto paroxysms of climate change denial.
            </p>
            <p>
             In
             <em style="background-color: initial;">
              Gyre
             </em>
             , a nod perhaps to the
             <a href="http://motherboard.vice.com/blog/welcome-to-garbage-patch-state-where-plastic-rules" style="background-color: initial;">
              Pacific Trash Vortex
             </a>
             , cars spin in a gigantic whirlpool. Kerbow's pencil and paper work on
             <em style="background-color: initial;">
              A New Religion
             </em>
             resembles the
             <a href="http://motherboard.vice.com/read/death-of-an-alien-sex-machine" style="background-color: initial;">
              recently-passed H.R. Giger
             </a>
             , withmonotone, industrial grays thatdepictsome sort of a factory or plant growing heavenward.
            </p>
            <p class="has-image">
             <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="800" data-original-width="1000" src="http://images.vice.com/motherboard/content-images/contentimage/14851/1409321146036667.jpg"/>
            </p>
            <p class="photo-credit">
             Gyre (study). Image: Michael Kerbow.
            </p>
            <p>
             And with
             <em style="background-color: initial;">
              Diminishing Returns
             </em>
             ,an endless city surrounds a circular sink hole, conveying the idea that as distant as our urban environments and populations might grow, some entropic counterforcealways exerts a pressure on that expansion.
            </p>
            <p class="has-image">
             <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="800" data-original-width="1000" src="http://images.vice.com/motherboard/content-images/contentimage/14851/1409267543854114.jpg"/>
            </p>
            <p class="photo-credit">
             Diminishing Returns. Image: Michael Kerbow.
            </p>
            <p>
             Other times, like in his
             <em style="background-color: initial;">
             </em>
             <em style="background-color: initial;">
              <a href="http://michaelkerbow.com/series/consumption" style="background-color: initial;">
               Consumption
              </a>
              <a href="http://michaelkerbow.com/series/consumption">
              </a>
             </em>
             series,Kerbow creates surreal depictions of humanity's unparalleled capacity to consume biological life for food. Hismost recent series, the hyper-imaginative
             <a href="http://michaelkerbow.com/series/aberrations" style="background-color: initial;">
              <em>
               Aberrations
              </em>
             </a>
             , issurreal in a way that calls to mind thedarkly apocalyptic work of 15th century Dutch painter Hieronymus Bosch.
            </p>
            <p class="has-image">
             <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="958" data-original-width="1000" src="http://images.vice.com/motherboard/content-images/contentimage/14851/1409321109376740.jpg"/>
            </p>
            <p class="photo-credit">
             Churn. Image: Michael Kerbow.
            </p>
            <p>
             And then there's the
             <em>
              Hollow Pursuits
             </em>
             series,which
             <em>
             </em>
             envisions humans havingexploited and developed every bit of land to build skyscrapers and freeways, with the last frontier of development being subterranean Earth.
            </p>
            <p class="has-image">
             <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="1000" data-original-width="1000" src="http://images.vice.com/motherboard/content-images/contentimage/14851/1409321172734973.jpg"/>
            </p>
            <p class="photo-credit">
             Hollow Pursuits. Image: Michael Kerbow.
            </p>
            <p>
             By the looks of Diminishing Returns (above), it seems as though Earth's core is overheating, but that could just be the electric glow of one huge planetary city. What's also interesting here is that Kerbow flips the hollow Earth theory on its head: humans, not some magical force, are hollowingout the planet.
            </p>
            <p>
             Kerbow's a bit less convincing as a visionary dystopian when he fixates on freeways and piles of unused cars. Even so, these paintings
             <em>
              Churn
             </em>
             ,
             <em>
              Hive
             </em>
             , and
             <em>
              Compulsive Actions
             </em>
             <a href="http://michaelkerbow.com/series/aberrations" target="_blank">
              are still a wonder to look at
             </a>
             , and certainlyworthy as satirical commentary.
            </p>
            <p>
             Which is all to say, one can only hope Kerbow is proven wrong.
            </p>
            <p class="has-image">
             <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="813" data-original-width="1000" src="http://images.vice.com/motherboard/content-images/contentimage/14851/1409268024340555.jpg"/>
            </p>
            <p class="photo-credit">
             Compulsive Actions. Image: Michael Kerbow.
            </p>
           </div>
