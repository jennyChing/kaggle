So hence we\u2019re seeing some of the acquisitions and mergers that are in the market.\"\n\"Whether it\u2019s Netflix, and it will be other things tomorrow, our appetite for broadband is not abating. I think it would be a good alternate site for TPG as well.\"\niiNet claims to be number one in customer service. It has a high average Net Promoter Score of 58% \u2014 a rating that indicates how many customers a company has that are loyal and would refer the service to a friend \u2014 and its customer churn is at low levels by industry standards, according to the company's 2014 annual report.\nAs Nick Harris, a senior analyst at the stockbroking and wealth management firm, Morgans, told Mashable Australia, \"You\u2019ve got a good business with happy customers \u2014 that's quite a strategic asset to scale.\"\n3. Fear of the big, bad National Broadband Network\nAnother dynamic at play behind the prospective merger is the National Broadband Network (NBN) \u2014 a government-backed project to roll out fast broadband to 8 million Australian premises by 2020.\nBy getting their hands on iiNet, M2 and TPG are looking to buy an additional, hopefully more loyal customer base \u2014 a super-sizing that could help offset the costs of accessing the NBN.\n\"The customer is the battleground in all of this,\" Johnston said. \"When the NBN switch occurs, [the customer has] a choice at that point about whether you want to move. So if you\u2019re not happy with your existing provider, then there\u2019s a real risk to M2 and others that they will lose customers along the way.\"\n\nUnder the NBN, the price ISPs have to pay to access the new infrastructure and reach Australian consumers will be set. Therefore, ISPs are looking to build both the customer base and the client service framework now, so they are well-placed before the NBN comes fully online. \"Basically, the access costs go up under the NBN,\" Harris said. \"You need a big customer base to share that fixed cost against.\"\nAs mentioned, iiNet has a relationship with its subscribers that is better than most \u2014 potentially providing the type of sticky customer that would help a company like TPG or M2 thrive during the NBN transition.\nMichael Smith, chairman of iiNet, acknowledged this new reality on a media conference call on Wednesday. \"With the NBN and access to bandwidth becoming more equal,\" he said. \"The ability to be a standout customer brand becomes more important.\"\n4. The company just has a good vibe\nThis point is difficult to quantify: iiNet seems like a decent company.\nFirstly, it is well marketed. The company has a widely-recognisable, unthreateningly handsome Irish guy as its public face \u2014 Finn, the iiNet guy. The \"ultimate Internet nerd,\" as he is called, his interests apparently range from \"making lifesize macaroni celebrity statues\" to \"Cyber Karate.\"\nPeople have even watched his ad outtakes over 6,000 times.\n\nThe company has also been the beneficiary of public goodwill by being seen to step into the ring for its customers.\nWhether it is going to court in an attempt to prevent being forced to hand over the personal details of their customers in the Dallas Buyers Club case, or taking a stand against the government's mandatory data retention scheme, the company often seems to be on the side of the average Internet user.\n\"iiNet has really been a distinct brand for consumers in the way that it has stood up for consumer rights and debates around privacy, around metadata, around online copyright infringement,\" Choice's campaigns manager, Erin Turner, told the ABC. \"We'd hope they retain that. It makes them completely unique in the market at the moment.\"\nHaving a company with that kind of positive reputation on your team can only be a net-benefit for somewhat-faceless businesses like TPG and M2.\n5. Everyone is doing it\nIt takes capital to run an Internet business, and mergers have been one way Australian companies get their hands on it.\n\"Over time, the need to keep upgrading the technology to deliver these [Internet] services occurs,\" Johnston pointed out. \"At some point, people are going to need capital, so hence you\u2019re going to see consolidation as well.\"\nAnother factor is there are not many medium-sized ISPs like iiNet left for companies such as M2 or TPG to buy in Australia \u2014 and analysts do not expect those still around to last much longer. \"You\u2019ve see a lot of consolidation in the last five years, so there\u2019s not much left,\" Harris said.\nOn Wednesday, iiNet's chairman also talked up iiNet's position as one of the most popular takeover targets left in Australia. \"We\u2019re the best piece of fruit in the bowl,\" he said.\n6. Because coffee cam\nAnd last but not least: For no apparent reason, the company has a coffee cam streaming from its office twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week.              <section class="article-content">
              <p>
               It is not often a telecommunications merger gets much attention  but customer concern over the bidding war to acquire the Perth-based Internet service provider (ISP), iiNet, has made the potential deal one of the most high profile in Australia in recent years.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                See also:
                <a href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/27/m2-group-wants-iinet/">
                 Two Aussie Internet companies in battle to buy iiNet
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               To recap the story so far:
               <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/27/m2-group-wants-iinet/">
                Two Australian Internet companies are vying
               </a>
               to buy iiNet  TPG Telecom and M2 Group. In an indication of how muchit wants the company, TPG put A$1.4 billion cash on the table. M2 Group, in an attempt to outbid TPG, laid down a share-based offer of A$1.6 billion (on Tuesday evening, this had decreased to $1.57 billion
               <a href="http://www.afr.com/business/telecommunications/tpg-telecom-increases-bid-for-iinet-to-156-billion-20150505-ggugi0" target="_blank">
                due to fluctuations in the share price
               </a>
               ).
              </p>
              <p>
               The iiNet board were content with M2's bid, but allowed TPG a counter offer. On May 6, iiNet
               <a href="http://investor.iinet.com.au/IRM/ShowDownloadDoc.aspx?SiteId=140&amp;AnnounceGuid=8aaf38d7-6cd0-4e3c-9e1f-5d26efad5829" target="_blank">
                announced the revised TPG offer
               </a>
                which took the deal to a value of A$1.56 billion  was more favourable than M2's. Whether the underdog will come back to the table is yet to be seen.
              </p>
              <p>
               Unless you are a diehard observer of the machinations of the broadband market  and let's face it, who is  or you are an iiNet customer, you would be forgiven for wondering why everyone wants this company so bad.
              </p>
              <p>
               To help you figure out why iiNet is the most popular kid in school right now, here are a few theories:
              </p>
              <h2>
               1. Netflix, Netflix, Netflix
              </h2>
              <p>
               The public's insatiable desire for streaming video is not going away, and Australian internet companies like M2, TPG and iiNet need some help from their friends to make sure we are not watching videos buffer into eternity.
              </p>
              <p>
               To give you an idea of the size of our streaming appetite, take the experience of iiNet itself: After launching in Australia in March 2015, Netflix made up 3% of iiNet's network traffic. About four weeks later it grew to 25%,
               <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/20/netflix-traffic-explodes/">
                according to numbers the company's chief technology officer
               </a>
               shared in April. Not to mention, emerging digital businesses fromtelemedicine toonline education  will also put Australia's Internet infrastructure to the test.
              </p>
              <p>
               ISPs will have to keep up, and consolidation is one good way to do it.
              </p>
              <figure class="image">
               <p>
                <img alt="orange" class="" data-caption="Netflix's original series Orange is the New Black." data-credit-label="" data-credit-link="" data-credit-name="" data-credit-provider="Custom type" data-fragment="m!2fe6" data-image="http://rack.2.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA1LzA2L2ZlL29yYW5nZS44ZTY3YS5qcGcKcAl0aHVtYgkxMjAweDk2MDA-/de9b1791/3c5/orange.jpg" data-micro="1" data-width="960" src="http://rack.2.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA1LzA2L2ZlL29yYW5nZS44ZTY3YS5qcGcKcAl0aHVtYgkxMjAweDk2MDA-/de9b1791/3c5/orange.jpg" title="orange"/>
               </p>
               <figcaption class="image-caption">
                <p>
                 Netflix's original series Orange is the New Black.
                </p>
               </figcaption>
               <div class="image-credit">
               </div>
              </figure>
              <p>
               Stuart Johnston, leader of Technology, Media and Telecommunications at the consulting company, Deloitte, told
               <em>
                Mashable Australia
               </em>
               the moves by TPG and M2 to acquire iiNet are broadly reflective of this increasing usage and demand for broadband by consumers.
              </p>
              <p>
               "Theres a massive and ever increasing market for broadband data," he said. "To serve that market, it does really require the ability to have scale and infrastructure. So hence were seeing some of the acquisitions and mergers that are in the market."
              </p>
              <p>
               "Whether its Netflix, and it will be other things tomorrow, our appetite for broadband is not abating. Its increasing," he added.
              </p>
              <h2>
               2. iiNet has happy customers
              </h2>
              <p>
               In the process of courting iiNet, both TPG and M2 have paid lip service to the company's supposedly above-average customer service  both have indicated it is one thing that will sweeten the deal.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://m2.com.au/GetPdf.axd?id=645829" target="_blank">
                In a statement
               </a>
               to the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX) about its bid to acquire iiNet, M2 referred to the "the strength of the iiNet brand and its customer ethos."
              </p>
              <p>
               TPG Telecom chief executive David Teoh has
               <a href="http://www.smh.com.au/business/tpgs-david-teoh-offers-olive-branch-to-iinet-founder-michael-malone-20150324-1m6234.html" target="_blank">
                even talked about the greatness of iiNet's call centres
               </a>
               , saying: "The Cape Town call centre, they have fabulous people. I think it would be a good alternate site for TPG as well."
              </p>
              <p>
               iiNet
               <a href="http://www.iinet.net.au/about/customer-service/" target="_blank">
                claims to be number one in customer service
               </a>
               . It has a high average Net Promoter Score of 58%  a rating that indicates how many customers a company has that are loyal and would refer the service to a friend  and its customer churn is at low levels by industry standards,
               <a href="http://www.iinet.net.au/annual-reports/2014/iiNet_Annual_Report_2014-CEO_Report.pdf" target="_blank">
                according to the company's 2014 annual report
               </a>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               As Nick Harris, a senior analyst at the stockbroking and wealth management firm, Morgans, told
               <em>
                Mashable Australia
               </em>
               , "Youve got a good business with happy customers  that's quite a strategic asset to scale."
              </p>
              <h2>
               3. Fear of the big, bad National Broadband Network
              </h2>
              <p>
               Another dynamic at play behind the prospective merger is the National Broadband Network (NBN)  a government-backed project to roll out fast broadband to 8 million Australian premises by 2020.
              </p>
              <p>
               By getting their hands on iiNet, M2 and TPG are looking to buy an additional, hopefully more loyal customer base  a super-sizing that could help offset the costs of accessing the NBN.
              </p>
              <p>
               "The customer is the battleground in all of this," Johnston said. "When the NBN switch occurs, [the customer has] a choice at that point about whether you want to move. So if youre not happy with your existing provider, then theres a real risk to M2 and others that they will lose customers along the way."
              </p>
              <p>
               <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="480" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/ivWND9l0Dm4?enablejsapi=1&amp;" width="853">
               </iframe>
              </p>
              <p>
               Under the NBN, the price ISPs have to pay to access the new infrastructure and reach Australian consumers will be set. Therefore, ISPs are looking to build both the customer base and the client service framework now, so they are well-placed before the NBN comes fully online. "Basically, the access costs go up under the NBN," Harris said. "You need a big customer base to share that fixed cost against."
              </p>
              <p>
               As mentioned, iiNet has a relationship with its subscribers that is better than most  potentially providing the type of sticky customer that would help a company like TPG or M2 thrive during the NBN transition.
              </p>
              <p>
               Michael Smith, chairman of iiNet, acknowledged this new reality on a media conference call on Wednesday. "With the NBN and access to bandwidth becoming more equal," he said. "The ability to be a standout customer brand becomes more important."
              </p>
              <h2>
               4. The company just has a good vibe
              </h2>
              <p>
               This point is difficult to quantify: iiNet seems like a decent company.
              </p>
              <p>
               Firstly, it is well marketed. The company has a widely-recognisable, unthreateningly handsome Irish guy as its public face  Finn, the iiNet guy. The "ultimate Internet nerd," as he is called,
               <a href="https://www.facebook.com/theiiNetguy/info?tab=page_info" target="_blank">
                his interests apparently range from
               </a>
               "making lifesize macaroni celebrity statues" to "Cyber Karate."
              </p>
              <p>
               People have even watched his ad outtakes over 6,000 times.
              </p>
              <p>
               <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/7r7jLvlTBto?enablejsapi=1&amp;" width="560">
               </iframe>
              </p>
              <p>
               The company has also been the beneficiary of public goodwill by being seen to step into the ring for its customers.
              </p>
              <p>
               Whether it is going to court in an attempt to prevent being forced to hand over the personal details of their customers
               <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/07/dallas-buyers-club-iinet/">
                in the
                <em>
                 Dallas Buyers Club
                </em>
                case
               </a>
               , or
               <a href="http://blog.iinet.net.au/protecting-your-privacy/" target="_blank">
                taking a stand against the government's mandatory data retention scheme
               </a>
               , the company often seems to be on the side of the average Internet user.
              </p>
              <p>
               "iiNet has really been a distinct brand for consumers in the way that it has stood up for consumer rights and debates around privacy, around metadata, around online copyright infringement," Choice's campaigns manager, Erin Turner,
               <a href="http://www.abc.net.au/news/2015-03-13/tpgs-14-billion-iinet-takeover-raises-consumer-concerns/6312910" target="_blank">
                told the ABC
               </a>
               . "We'd hope they retain that. It makes them completely unique in the market at the moment."
              </p>
              <p>
               Having a company with that kind of positive reputation on your team can only be a net-benefit for somewhat-faceless businesses like TPG and M2.
              </p>
              <h2>
               5. Everyone is doing it
              </h2>
              <p>
               It takes capital to run an Internet business, and mergers have been one way Australian companies get their hands on it.
              </p>
              <p>
               "Over time, the need to keep upgrading the technology to deliver these [Internet] services occurs," Johnston pointed out. "At some point, people are going to need capital, so hence youre going to see consolidation as well."
              </p>
              <p>
               Another factor is there are not many medium-sized ISPs like iiNet left for companies such as M2 or TPG to buy in Australia  and analysts do not expect those still around to last much longer. "Youve see a lot of consolidation in the last five years, so theres not much left," Harris said.
              </p>
              <p>
               On Wednesday, iiNet's chairman also talked up iiNet's position as one of the most popular takeover targets left in Australia. "Were the best piece of fruit in the bowl," he said.
              </p>
              <h2>
               6. Because coffee cam
              </h2>
              <p>
               And last but not least: For no apparent reason, the company has a
               <a href="http://looking-glass.iinet.net.au/coffee/" target="_blank">
                coffee cam streaming from its office
               </a>
               twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week. That is the kind of intra-business transparency we can all get behind.
              </p>
              <figure class="image">
               <p>
                <img alt="coffee cam" data-caption="The infamous coffee cam." data-credit-label="" data-credit-link="" data-credit-name="iiNet" data-credit-provider="Custom type" data-fragment="m!8c07" data-image="http://rack.1.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzI5LzhmL2lpTmV0Y29mZmVlLjQxOWNiLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/fbc06d53/9a9/iiNetcoffeecam.jpg" data-micro="1" data-width="950" src="http://rack.1.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzI5LzhmL2lpTmV0Y29mZmVlLjQxOWNiLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/fbc06d53/9a9/iiNetcoffeecam.jpg" title="iiNetcoffeecam"/>
               </p>
               <figcaption class="image-caption">
                <p>
                 The infamous coffee cam.
                </p>
               </figcaption>

                </p>

              </figure>
              <h2>
               The final question
              </h2>
              <p>
               Of course, it is likely to be a combination of factors  from the threat of the NBN to the rise and rise of services like Netflix  that make iiNet so popular right now.
              </p>
              <p>
               Buying iiNet  which has around 975,000 broadband subscribers  would make either TPG or M2 Australia's second largest fixed-line Internet provider, behind Telstra. So it is after the merger honeymoon is over  whether with TPG or M2  that the more difficult questions will be asked of the new power-telco.
              </p>
              <p>
               Number one: will all those happy iiNet customers still be quite so satisfied once the company is no longer the friendly, neighbourhood ISP?
              </p>
              <p>
               iiNet's chairman moved to reassure subscribers on Wednesday. "Its like Volkswagen buying Porsche, he said, referring to TPG's bid. If you tried to turn Porsche into Volkswagen, you ruin the value of that brand."
              </p>
              <p>
               Whether that truism holds under TPG or M2 remains to be seen.
              </p>
