  <meta content="Two years after an outdated segment of ExxonMobil's Pegasus pipeline ruptured in Mayflower, Ark., releasing 378,000 gallons&nbsp;of Canadian heavy crude oil and water, more than 48,400 miles of similar, aged steel pipe is still in use, InsideClimate News reported Sunday (Apr. 3). The older pipe, not used in building new pipelines since 1970, was manufactured using low-frequency electric resistance welding. The welding method was phased out by 1970 because it left flaws in the steel, resulting in splits along lengthwise seams in the pipelines, the web site reported. The 48,400 miles of the older pipe still in use represents 25 percent of pipelines that carry oil, gasoline and other hazardous liquids. ExxonMobil&nbsp;still has 3,299 miles of the older pipe in its pipelines, according to 2013 statistics from the federal Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration. In addition to the Pegasus pipeline, which runs through Illinois, Missouri, Arkansas and Texas, the company owns a separate pipeline that runs from Longview, Texas, to Baton Rouge and St. James Parish. Enterprise Products, another company whose pipelines run through Louisiana, has 2,183 miles of the older pipe in its system. In a March 30 story, InsideClimate News reported that the pipeline safety agency..." name="Description"/>

          <div class="ndn_embed" data-config-distributor-id="90300" id="ndn-video-player-1">

          <p>
           Two years after an outdated segment of
           <a href="http://search.nola.com/exxonmobil%2C+mayflower/?date_range=all" target="_blank">
            ExxonMobil's Pegasus pipeline ruptured in Mayflower, Ark.
           </a>
           , releasing 378,000 gallonsof Canadian heavy crude oil and water, more than 48,400 miles of similar, aged steel pipe is still in use,
           <a href="http://insideclimatenews.org/news/03042015/flawed-steel-still-wide-use-oil-pipelines-exxonmobil-pegasus-spill-oil-sands" target="_blank">
            InsideClimate News reported
           </a>
           Sunday (Apr. 3).
          </p>
          <p>
           The older pipe, not used in building new pipelines since 1970, was manufactured using low-frequency electric resistance welding. The welding method was phased out by 1970 because it left flaws in the steel, resulting in splits along lengthwise seams in the pipelines,
           <a href="http://insideclimatenews.org/news/03042015/flawed-steel-still-wide-use-oil-pipelines-exxonmobil-pegasus-spill-oil-sands" target="_blank">
            the web site reported
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           The 48,400 miles of the older pipe still in use represents 25 percent of pipelines that carry oil, gasoline and other hazardous liquids.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://search.nola.com/exxonmobil/1/all/?date_range=all" target="_blank">
            ExxonMobil
           </a>
           still has 3,299 miles of the older pipe in its pipelines, according to 2013 statistics from the federal
           <a href="http://search.nola.com/Pipeline+and+Hazardous+Materials+Safety+Administration/1/all/?date_range=all" target="_blank">
            Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration
           </a>
           . In addition to the Pegasus pipeline, which runs through Illinois, Missouri, Arkansas and Texas, the company owns a separate pipeline that runs from Longview, Texas, to Baton Rouge and St. James Parish.
          </p>
          <p>
           Enterprise Products, another company whose pipelines run through Louisiana, has 2,183 miles of the older pipe in its system.
          </p>
          <p>
           In
           <a href="http://insideclimatenews.org/news/30032015/two-years-after-exxons-mayflower-spill-will-tougher-pipeline-rules-go-beyond-talk" target="_blank">
            a March 30 story, InsideClimate News
           </a>
           reported that the pipeline safety agency is considering new rules that would require about 95 percent of all hazardous liquid pipelines to be subject to stricter safety verification because of their age,location or other factors. The agency also was considering new guidelines for pre-1970 pipelines that carry natural gas, oil, gasoline and other hazardous liquids.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Pegasus pipe in Mayflower burst open on March 29, 2013, pouring diluted bitumen, also known as dilbit, into a residential cul-de-sac, requiring 22 families to be relocated.
          </p>
          <p>
           The pipe used in the pipeline was made in 1947 and 1948, and tests following the accident indicated brittle steel and manufacturing flaws may have been partially to blame for the 22-foot-long gash that formed in a seam in the pipe.
          </p>
          <p>
           The pipeline safety agency has fined ExxonMobil $2.66 million for safety violations related to the spill, but the company has appealed that decision.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            An earlier version of this story incorrectly listed the amount of oil and water released in the Pegasus spill as 378,000 barrels of oil and water. It's 378,000 gallons.
           </em>
          </p>
