     <p>
      <b>
       Military science fiction
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subgenre" title="Subgenre">
       subgenre
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
       science fiction
      </a>
      featuring the use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_fiction_technology" title="Science fiction technology">
       science fiction technology
      </a>
      , mainly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_fiction_weapons" title="Science fiction weapons">
       weapons
      </a>
      , for military purposes and principal characters that are members of a military organization involved in military activity; occurring sometimes in outer space or on a different planet or planets.
     </p>
     <p>
      A detailed description of the conflict, the tactics and weapons used for it, and the role of a military service and the individual members of that military organization forms the basis for a typical work of military science fiction. The stories often use features of actual past or current Earth conflicts, with countries being replaced by planets or galaxies of similar characteristics,
      <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">
       battleships
      </a>
      replaced by space battleships and certain events changed so that the author can extrapolate what might have occurred.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>
      Traditional military values of bravery, sacrifice, sense of duty, and camaraderie are emphasized, and the action is usually described from the point of view of a soldier or officer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waterson2008_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Waterson2008-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Typically, the technology is more advanced than that of the present and described in detail. In some stories, however, technology is fairly static, and weapons that would be familiar to present-day soldiers are used, but other aspects of society have changed. For example, women may be accepted as equal partners for combat roles. In many military sci-fi stories, technological advances are basic to plot development, but battles are often won more by cleverness or bravery than by technology.
     </p>
     <p>
      Several subsets of military science fiction overlap with
      <a href="/wiki/Space_opera" title="Space opera">
       space opera
      </a>
      , concentrating on large-scale space battles with futuristic weapons. At one extreme, the genre is used to speculate about future wars involving space travel, or the effects of such a war on humans; at the other, it consists of the use of military fiction plots with some superficial science fiction trappings. The term "military space opera" is occasionally used to denote this subgenre, as used for example by critic Sylvia Kelso when describing
      <a href="/wiki/Lois_McMaster_Bujold" title="Lois McMaster Bujold">
       Lois McMaster Bujold
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Vorkosigan_Saga" title="Vorkosigan Saga">
       Vorkosigan Saga
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renaissance251_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-renaissance251-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another example of military space opera would be the
      <a href="/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica" title="Battlestar Galactica">
       Battlestar Galactica
      </a>
      franchise, both traditional and more notably the reimagined series.
     </p>
     <p>
      The key distinction of military science fiction from space opera is that the principal characters in a space opera are not military personnel, but civilians or
      <a href="/wiki/Paramilitary" title="Paramilitary">
       paramilitary
      </a>
      . Military science fiction also does not necessarily always include an outer space or multi-planetary setting like space opera.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_science_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Precursors for military science fiction can be found in "future war" stories dating back at least to
      <a href="/wiki/George_Tomkyns_Chesney" title="George Tomkyns Chesney">
       George Chesney
      </a>
      's story "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_Dorking" title="The Battle of Dorking">
       The Battle of Dorking
      </a>
      " (1871).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-define_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-define-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other works of fiction followed, including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.G._Wells" title="H.G. Wells">
       H.G. Wells
      </a>
      's "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Land_Ironclads" title="The Land Ironclads">
       The Land Ironclads
      </a>
      ." Eventually, as science fiction became an established and separate genre, military science fiction established itself as a subgenre. One such work is
      <a href="/wiki/H._Beam_Piper" title="H. Beam Piper">
       H. Beam Piper
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Uller Uprising
      </i>
      (1952) (based on the events of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sepoy_Mutiny" title="Sepoy Mutiny">
       Sepoy Mutiny
      </a>
      ).
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
       Robert A. Heinlein
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Starship_Troopers" title="Starship Troopers">
        Starship Troopers
       </a>
      </i>
      (1959) is another work of military SF, along with
      <a href="/wiki/Gordon_R._Dickson" title="Gordon R. Dickson">
       Gordon Dickson
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsai" title="Dorsai">
        Dorsai
       </a>
      </i>
      (1960), and these are thought to be mostly responsible for popularizing this subgenre's popularity among young readers of the time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      resulted in veterans with combat experience deciding to write science fiction, including
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Haldeman" title="Joe Haldeman">
       Joe Haldeman
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/David_Drake" title="David Drake">
       David Drake
      </a>
      . Throughout the 1970s, works such as Haldeman's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Forever_War" title="The Forever War">
        The Forever War
       </a>
      </i>
      and Drake's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hammer%27s_Slammers" title="Hammer's Slammers">
        Hammer's Slammers
       </a>
      </i>
      helped increase the popularity of the genre.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-define_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-define-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Short stories also were popular, collected in books like
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Combat_SF" title="Combat SF">
        Combat SF
       </a>
      </i>
      , edited by
      <a href="/wiki/Gordon_R._Dickson" title="Gordon R. Dickson">
       Gordon R. Dickson
      </a>
      . This
      <a href="/wiki/Anthology" title="Anthology">
       anthology
      </a>
      includes one of the first
      <i>
       Hammer's Slammers
      </i>
      stories as well as one of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bolo_(tank)" title="Bolo (tank)">
        BOLO
       </a>
      </i>
      stories by
      <a href="/wiki/Keith_Laumer" title="Keith Laumer">
       Keith Laumer
      </a>
      and one of the
      <i>
       Berserker
      </i>
      stories by
      <a href="/wiki/Fred_Saberhagen" title="Fred Saberhagen">
       Fred Saberhagen
      </a>
      . This anthology seems to have been the first time SF-stories specifically dealing with war as a subject were collected and marketed as such. The series of anthologies with the group title
      <i>
       There Will be War
      </i>
      edited by Pournelle and
      <a href="/wiki/John_F._Carr" title="John F. Carr">
       John F. Carr
      </a>
      (nine volumes from 1983 through 1990) helped keep the category active, and encouraged new writers to add to it.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Viewpoints">

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/David_Drake" title="David Drake">
       David Drake
      </a>
      has often written of the horrors and futility of war. He has said, in the afterwords of several of his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hammer%27s_Slammers" title="Hammer's Slammers">
        Hammer's Slammers
       </a>
      </i>
      books (1979 and later), that one of his reasons for writing is to educate those people who have not experienced war, but who might have to make the decision to start or endorse a war (as policy makers or as voters) about what war is really like, and what the powers and limits of the military as an instrument of policy are.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/David_Weber" title="David Weber">
       David Weber
      </a>
      has said that:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "For me, military science-fiction is science-fiction which is written about a military situation with a fundamental understanding of how military lifestyles and characters differ from civilian lifestyles and characters. It is science-fiction which attempts to realistically portray the military within a science-fiction context. It is not 'bug shoots'. It is about human beings, and members of other species, caught up in warfare and carnage. It isn't an excuse for simplistic solutions to problems."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
