    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       trilemma
      </b>
      is a difficult choice from three options, each of which is (or appears) unacceptable or unfavourable.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are two
      <a href="/wiki/Logical_equivalence" title="Logical equivalence">
       logically equivalent
      </a>
      ways in which to express a trilemma: it can be expressed as a choice among three unfavourable options, one of which must be chosen, or as a choice among three favourable options, only two of which are possible at the same time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term derives from the much older term
      <a href="/wiki/Dilemma" title="Dilemma">
       dilemma
      </a>
      , a choice between two or more difficult or unfavourable alternatives.
     </p>
     <p>
      The earliest recorded use of the term was by the British preacher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_Henry_(clergyman)" title="Philip Henry (clergyman)">
       Philip Henry
      </a>
      in 1672, and later, apparently independently, by the preacher
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Watts" title="Isaac Watts">
       Isaac Watts
      </a>
      in 1725.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trilemmas_in_religion">
       Trilemmas in religion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Trilemmas in religion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Epicurus.27_trilemma">
       Epicurus' trilemma
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Epicurus' trilemma">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One of the earliest uses of the trilemma formulation is that of the Greek philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">
       Epicurus
      </a>
      , rejecting the idea of an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnipotent" title="Omnipotent">
       omnipotent
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnibenevolent" title="Omnibenevolent">
       omnibenevolent
      </a>
      God (as summarised by
      <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">
       David Hume
      </a>
      ):
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       If God is unable to prevent evil, then he is not all-powerful.
      </li>
      <li>
       If God is not willing to prevent evil, then he is not all-good.
      </li>
      <li>
       If God is both willing and able to prevent evil, then why does evil exist?
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Although traditionally ascribed to Epicurus, it has been suggested that it may actually be the work of an early
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_skepticism" title="Philosophical skepticism">
       skeptic
      </a>
      writer, possibly
      <a href="/wiki/Carneades" title="Carneades">
       Carneades
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In studies of philosophy, discussions and debates related to this trilemma are often referred to as being about the "
      <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_evil" title="Problem of evil">
       problem of evil
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Apologetic_trilemma">
       Apologetic trilemma
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apologetic trilemma">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis%27s_trilemma" title="Lewis's trilemma">
       Lewis's trilemma
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      One well known trilemma is sometimes used by
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_apologetics" title="Christian apologetics">
       Christian apologists
      </a>
      as a proof of the divinity of
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is most commonly known in the version by
      <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">
       C. S. Lewis
      </a>
      . It proceeds from the assumption that Jesus claimed to be God, and that therefore one of the following must be true:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <i>
        Lunatic
       </i>
       : Jesus was not God, but he mistakenly believed that he was.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Liar
       </i>
       : Jesus was not God, and he knew it, but he said so anyway.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Lord
       </i>
       : Jesus is God.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      The trilemma, usually in Lewis' formulation, is often used in works of popular apologetics, although it is almost totally absent from discussions about the status of Jesus by professional theologians and biblical scholars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In his 1993 book
      <i>
       The Metaphor of God Incarnate
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Hick" title="John Hick">
       John Hick
      </a>
      recalled having been taught this argument as a child, and states that New Testament scholars today do not support the view that Jesus claimed to be God.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trilemma in law">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22cruel_trilemma.22">
       The "cruel trilemma"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: The "cruel trilemma"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Ex_officio_oath" title="Ex officio oath">
       Ex officio oath
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The "cruel trilemma"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubenfeld_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rubenfeld-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_nation" title="English nation">
       English
      </a>
      ecclesiastical and judicial weapon
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fellman_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fellman-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      developed in the first half of the 17th century, and used as a form of coercion and persecution. The format was a religious oath to tell the truth, imposed upon the accused prior to questioning. The accused would find themselves trapped between:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       A breach of religious oath if they lied (taken extremely seriously in that era, a
       <a href="/wiki/Mortal_sin" title="Mortal sin">
        mortal sin
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubenfeld_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-rubenfeld-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Perjury" title="Perjury">
        perjury
       </a>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Self-incrimination" title="Self-incrimination">
        Self-incrimination
       </a>
       if they told the truth; or
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Contempt_of_court" title="Contempt of court">
        Contempt of court
       </a>
       if they said nothing and were silent.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Outcry over this process led to the foundation of the right to not incriminate oneself being established in
      <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">
       common law
      </a>
      and was the direct precursor of the
      <a href="/wiki/Right_to_silence" title="Right to silence">
       right to silence
      </a>
      and non-self-incrimination in the
      <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">
       Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnchhausen_trilemma" title="Mnchhausen trilemma">
       Mnchhausen trilemma
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">
       theory of knowledge
      </a>
      the Mnchhausen trilemma is a philosophical term coined to stress the impossibility to prove any
      <i>
       certain
      </i>
      truth even in the fields of logic and mathematics. Its name is going back to a logical proof of the German philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Albert" title="Hans Albert">
       Hans Albert
      </a>
      . This proof runs as follows: All of the only three possible attempts to get a certain justification must fail:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       All justifications in pursuit of
       <i>
        certain
       </i>
       knowledge have also to justify the means of their justification and doing so they have to justify anew the means of their justification. Therefore there can be no end. We are faced with the hopeless situation of an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infinite_regression" title="Infinite regression">
        infinite regression
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       One can stop at self-evidence or common sense or fundamental principles or speaking '
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex_cathedra" title="Ex cathedra">
        ex cathedra
       </a>
       ' or at any other evidence, but in doing so the intention to install
       <i>
        certain
       </i>
       justification is abandoned.
      </li>
      <li>
       The third horn of the trilemma is the application of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circular_argument" title="Circular argument">
        circular argument
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_trilemma_of_censorship">
       The trilemma of censorship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The trilemma of censorship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">
       Mill
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/On_Liberty" title="On Liberty">
        On Liberty
       </a>
      </i>
      , as a part of his argument against the suppression of free speech, he describes the trilemma facing those attempting to justify such suppression (although he does not refer to it as a trilemma Leo Parker-Rees (2009) identified it as such). If free speech is suppressed, the opinion suppressed is either:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       True  in which case society is robbed of the chance to exchange error for truth;
      </li>
      <li>
       False  in which case the opinion would create a 'livelier impression' of the truth, allowing people to justify the correct view;
      </li>
      <li>
       Half-true  in which case it would contain a forgotten element of the truth, that is important to rediscover, with the eventual aim of a synthesis of the conflicting opinions that is the whole truth.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trilemmas_in_economics">
       Trilemmas in economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trilemmas in economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22The_Uneasy_Triangle.22">
       "The Uneasy Triangle"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: "The Uneasy Triangle"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1952, the British magazine,
      <i>
       The Economist,
      </i>
      published a series of articles on an "Uneasy Triangle," which described "the three-cornered incompatibility between a stable price level, full employment, and . . . free collective bargaining." The context was the difficulty maintaining external balance without sacrificing two sacrosanct political values, jobs for all and unrestricted labor rights. Inflation resulting from labor militancy in the context of full employment put powerful downward pressure on the pound sterling. Runs on the pound triggered a long series of economically and politically disruptive "stop-go" policies (deflation followed by reflation).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      John Maynard Keynes had anticipated the severe problem associated with reconciling full employment with stable prices without sacrificing democracy and the associational rights of labor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The same incompatibilities were also elaborated on in Charles Lindblom's 1949 book,
      <i>
       Unions and Capitalism.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22Impossible_trinity.22">
       The "Impossible trinity"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title='Edit section: The "Impossible trinity"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Impossible_trinity" title="Impossible trinity">
       Impossible trinity
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1962 and 1963, a trilemma (or "impossible trinity") was introduced by economists
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mundell" title="Robert Mundell">
       Robert Mundell
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Fleming" title="Marcus Fleming">
       Marcus Fleming
      </a>
      in articles discussing the problems with creating a stable international financial system. It refers to the trade-offs among the following three goals: a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_exchange_rate" title="Fixed exchange rate">
       fixed exchange rate
      </a>
      , national independence in
      <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">
       monetary policy
      </a>
      , and capital mobility. According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Mundell%E2%80%93Fleming_model" title="MundellFleming model">
       MundellFleming model
      </a>
      of 1962 and 1963, a small, open economy cannot achieve all three of these policy goals at the same time: in pursuing any two of these goals, a nation must forgo the third.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wage_policy_trilemmas">
       Wage policy trilemmas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Wage policy trilemmas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1989 Peter Swenson posited the existence of "wage policy trilemmas" encountered by trade unions trying to achieve three egalitarian goals simultaneously. One involved attempts to compress wages within a bargaining sector while compressing wages between sectors and maximizing access to employment in the sector. A variant of this "horizontal" trilemma was the "vertical" wage policy trilemma associated with trying simultaneously to compress wages, increase the wage share of value added at the expense of profits, and maximize employment. These trilemmas helped explain instability in unions' wage policies and their political strategies seemingly designed to resolve the incompatibilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pinker_social_trilemma">
       The Pinker social trilemma
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The Pinker social trilemma">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">
       Steven Pinker
      </a>
      proposed another social trilemma in his books
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/How_the_Mind_Works" title="How the Mind Works">
        How the Mind Works
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Blank_Slate" title="The Blank Slate">
        The Blank Slate
       </a>
       :
      </i>
      that a society cannot be simultaneously "fair", "free" and "equal". If it is "fair", individuals who work harder will accumulate more wealth; if it is "free", parents will leave the bulk of their inheritance to their children; but then it will not be "equal", as people will begin life with different fortunes.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trilemmas_in_business">
       Trilemmas in business
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_project-management_trilemma">
       The project-management trilemma
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The project-management trilemma">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Project-triangle.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="455" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Project-triangle.svg/320px-Project-triangle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Project-triangle.svg/480px-Project-triangle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Project-triangle.svg/640px-Project-triangle.svg.png 2x" width="320"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Project-triangle.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Project_management_triangle" title="Project management triangle">
         project management triangle
        </a>
        as a "pick any two"
        <a href="/wiki/Euler_diagram" title="Euler diagram">
         Euler diagram
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
       Arthur C. Clarke
      </a>
      cited a management trilemma encountered when trying to achieve production quickly and cheaply while maintaining high quality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">
       software
      </a>
      industry, this means that one can pick any two of: fastest time to market, highest software quality (fewest defects), and lowest cost (headcount). This is the basis of the popular
      <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">
       project management
      </a>
      aphorism "Quick, Cheap, Good: Pick two," conceptualized as the
      <a href="/wiki/Project_management_triangle" title="Project management triangle">
       project management triangle
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trilemma_in_computing">
       Trilemma in computing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Trilemma in computing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/RAID" title="RAID">
       RAID
      </a>
      technology may offer two of the three desirable value: (relative) inexpensiveness, speed or reliability (RAID0 is fast and cheap, but unreliable; RAID60 is extremely expensive and reliable, with correct performance and so on). The phrase "fast, cheap, good: choose two".
     </p>
     <p>
      It had been
      <a href="/wiki/Pastiche" title="Pastiche">
       pastiched
      </a>
      in silent computing as "fast, cheap, quiet: choose two".
     </p>
     <p>
      Further trilemma in computing include the
      <a href="/wiki/CAP_theorem" title="CAP theorem">
       CAP theorem
      </a>
      about guarantees provided by distributed systems, and
      <a href="/wiki/Zooko%27s_triangle" title="Zooko's triangle">
       Zooko's triangle
      </a>
      for naming participants in network protocols.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Trilemma_of_the_Earth">
       The Trilemma of the Earth
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The Trilemma of the Earth">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Trilemma of the Earth (or 3E Trilemma) is a term used by scientists working on energy and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_protection" title="Environment protection">
       environment protection
      </a>
      . 3E Trilemma stands for Economy-Energy-Environment interaction.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       For the activation of
       <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">
        economic development
       </a>
       (E: Economy) to occur, we need to increase the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_expenditure" title="Energy expenditure">
        energy expenditure
       </a>
       (E: Energy) however this raises the environmental issue (E: Environment) of more emissions of
       <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">
        pollutant
       </a>
       gases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         17
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         18
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_energy" title="Clean energy">
       clean energy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">
       world population
      </a>
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_.C5.BDi.C5.BEek_trilemma">
       The iek trilemma
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: The iek trilemma">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Communist_3lemma.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="660" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Communist_3lemma.svg/200px-Communist_3lemma.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Communist_3lemma.svg/300px-Communist_3lemma.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Communist_3lemma.svg/400px-Communist_3lemma.svg.png 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Communist_3lemma.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Depiction of the iek Trilemma
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The iek trilemma is a humorous formulation on the incompatibility of certain personal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtues" title="Virtues">
       virtues
      </a>
      under a constraining
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideological" title="Ideological">
       ideological
      </a>
      framework. Often attributed to the philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Slavoj_%C5%BDi%C5%BEek" title="Slavoj iek">
       Slavoj iek
      </a>
      , it is actually quoted by him as the product of an anonymous source:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       One cannot but recall here a witty formula of life under a hard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_regime" title="Communist regime">
        Communist regime
       </a>
       : Of the three features  personal honesty, sincere support of the regime and intelligence  it was possible to combine only two, never all three. If one were honest and supportive, one was not very bright; if one were bright and supportive, one was not honest; if one were honest and bright, one was not supportive.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         19
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
