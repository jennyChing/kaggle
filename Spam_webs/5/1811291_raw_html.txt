     <h1>
      Heres the global perspective were missing: Earth is breathing
     </h1>
     <div class="post-inner clearfix">
      <div class="post-meta">
       <div class="post-meta-author">
        by
        <a href="http://matadornetwork.com/author/johnnelson/">
         John Nelson
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="post-meta-date">
        August 14, 2013
       </div>
       <div class="post-avatar">
        <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-100" height="100" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/authors/johnnelson.jpg" width="100"/>
       </div>
       <div class="post-share-wrapper post-share--vertical" data-post-id="216740" data-sticky="" data-sticky-max-width="991" data-sticky-offset-top="69" data-sticky-parent-contain="" data-sticky-parent-selector=".page-section">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="post-content">
       <p>
        <span style="text-transform:uppercase;">
         Heres a view
        </span>
        looking at one year of seasonal transformations on Earth, made possible by the tremendous folks of the
        <a href="HTTP://visibleearth.nasa.gov/view_cat.php?categoryID=1484">
         NASA Visible Earth
        </a>
        team. I downloaded the 12 cloud-free satellite imagery mosaics of Earth (Blue Marble Next Generation) at each month of the year. I wrapped them into some fun projections then stitched them together into a couple animated gifs.
       </p>
       <p>
        I of course had some expectation of what I would see as a result of animating these frames. But I didnt expect to be so mesmerized by them. I cant look away.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <noscript>
        <img alt='Animation: "A Breathing Earth"' src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/08/BreathingEarth.gif" title=""/>
       </noscript>
       <img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-216742 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/08/BreathingEarth.gif" height="640" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="619"/>
       <p>
       </p>
       <noscript>
        <img alt='Animation: "A Breathing Earth"' src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/08/breathing-earth2-matador-seo.gif" title=""/>
       </noscript>
       <img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-216741 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/08/breathing-earth2-matador-seo.gif" height="626" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="640"/>
       <p>
       </p>
       <center>
        <em>
         Images courtesy of
         <a href="http://uxblog.idvsolutions.com/2013/07/a-breathing-earth.html">
          IDV Solutions
         </a>
        </em>
       </center>
       <h5>
        Why?
       </h5>
       <p>
        Having spent much of my life living near the center of that
        <a href="HTTP://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLN-hqVuMmUAanICiytk2qTs_u7zEuHnKr">
         mitten-shaped peninsula
        </a>
        in North America, I have had a consistent seasonal metronome through which I track the years of my life. When I stitch together what can be an impersonal snapshot of an entire planet, all of a sudden I see a thing with a heartbeat. I can track one location throughout a year to compare the annual push and pull of snow and plant life there, while in my periphery I see the oscillating wave of life advancing and retreating, advancing and retreating. And Im reassured by it.
       </p>
       <p>
        Of course there are the global characteristics of climate and the nature of land to heat and cool more rapidly than water. The effects of warm currents feeding a surprisingly mild climate in the British Isles. The snowy head start of winter in high elevations like the Himalayas, Rockies, and Caucuses, that spread downward to join the later snowiness of lower elevations. The continental wave of growing grasses in African plains.
       </p>
       <p>
        But, overall, to me it looks like breathing. And my pixel is right at an interesting intersection of life and ice, where the longest night of the year feels like forever, and the longest day of the year is a like a battery strapped to my back. My winter was especially
        <a href="HTTP://uxblog.idvsolutions.com/2013/03/tribute-to-geographer.html">
         dark
        </a>
        . And my summer has been full of
        <a href="HTTP://uxblog.idvsolutions.com/2012/09/6-miles-in-michigan.html">
         blessings
        </a>
        . But I dont think either extreme would have been as memorable without the helpful (or painful) contrast of its opposite  all made possible by a 23.5 tilt.
       </p>
