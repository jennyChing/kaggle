     <p>
      The
      <b>
       <i>
        Yamato
       </i>
       -class battleships
      </b>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       <span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">
        ,
       </span>
       <i>
        Yamato-gata senkan
       </i>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">
         <span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">
          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </span>
      were
      <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">
       battleships
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">
       Imperial Japanese Navy
      </a>
      (IJN) constructed and operated during World War II. Displacing 72,000 long tons (73,000t) at full load, the vessels were the heaviest and most powerfully armed battleships ever constructed. The class carried the largest
      <a href="/wiki/Naval_artillery" title="Naval artillery">
       naval artillery
      </a>
      ever fitted to a warship, nine
      <a href="/wiki/40_cm/45_Type_94_naval_gun" title="40 cm/45 Type 94 naval gun">
       460-millimetre (18.1 in) naval guns
      </a>
      , each capable of firing 1,360kg (3,000lb) shells over 42km (26mi). Two battleships of the class (
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Yamato" title="Japanese battleship Yamato">
       <i>
        Yamato
       </i>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Musashi" title="Japanese battleship Musashi">
       <i>
        Musashi
       </i>
      </a>
      ) were completed, while a third (
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Shinano" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Shinano">
       <i>
        Shinano
       </i>
      </a>
      ) was converted to an
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">
       aircraft carrier
      </a>
      during construction.
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the threat of American submarines and aircraft carriers, both
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      spent the majority of their careers in naval bases at
      <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">
       Brunei
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chuuk_Lagoon" title="Chuuk Lagoon">
       Truk
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Kure,_Hiroshima" title="Kure, Hiroshima">
       Kure
      </a>
      deploying on several occasions in response to American raids on Japanese basesbefore participating in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leyte_Gulf" title="Battle of Leyte Gulf">
       Battle of Leyte Gulf
      </a>
      in October 1944, as part of Admiral Kurita's Centre Force.
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      was sunk during the battle by American carrier airplanes.
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      was sunk ten days after her commissioning in November 1944 by the submarine
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Archerfish_(SS-311)" title="USS Archerfish (SS-311)">
       USS
       <i>
        Archerfish
       </i>
      </a>
      , while
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      was sunk in April 1945 during
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Ten-Go" title="Operation Ten-Go">
       Operation
       <i>
        Ten-Go
       </i>
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The design of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      -class battleships was shaped by expansionist movements within the Japanese government, Japanese industrial power, and the need for a fleet powerful enough to intimidate likely adversaries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="423" data-file-width="650" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg/220px-1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg/330px-1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg/440px-1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1938_Japan_Navy_battleship.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Musashi
        </i>
        , August 1942, taken from the bow
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After the end of the First World War, many naviesincluding those of the United States, the United Kingdom, and
      <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">
       Imperial Japan
      </a>
      continued and expanded construction programs that had begun during the conflict. The enormous costs associated with these programs pressured their government leaders to begin a disarmament conference. On 8 July 1921, the United States' Secretary of State
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Evans_Hughes" title="Charles Evans Hughes">
       Charles Evans Hughes
      </a>
      invited delegations from the other major maritime powersFrance, Italy, Japan, and the United Kingdomto come to Washington, D.C. and discuss a possible end to the naval arms race. The subsequent
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Naval_Conference" title="Washington Naval Conference">
       Washington Naval Conference
      </a>
      resulted in the
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Naval_Treaty" title="Washington Naval Treaty">
       Washington Naval Treaty
      </a>
      . Along with many other provisions, it limited all future battleships to a
      <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(ship)#Standard_displacement" title="Displacement (ship)">
       standard displacement
      </a>
      of 35,000 long tons (36,000t; 39,000 short tons) and a maximum gun caliber of 16 inches (406mm). It also agreed that the five countries would not construct more capital ships for ten years and would not replace any ship that survived the treaty until it was at least twenty years old.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1930s, the Japanese government began a shift towards
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultranationalist" title="Ultranationalist">
       ultranationalist
      </a>
      militancy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This movement called for the expansion of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Empire" title="Japanese Empire">
       Japanese Empire
      </a>
      to include much of the Pacific Ocean and Southeast Asia. The maintenance of such an empirespanning 3,000 miles (4,800km) from China to
      <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">
       Midway Island
      </a>
      required a sizable fleet capable of sustained control of territory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although all of Japan's battleships built prior to the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class had been completed before 1921as the Washington Treaty had prevented any more from being completedall had been either reconstructed or significantly modernized, or both, in the 1930s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This modernization included, among other things, additional speed and firepower, which the Japanese intended to use to conquer and defend their aspired-to empire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When Japan withdrew from the
      <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">
       League of Nations
      </a>
      in 1934 over the
      <a href="/wiki/Mukden_Incident" title="Mukden Incident">
       Mukden Incident
      </a>
      , it also renounced all treaty obligations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Japan would no longer design battleships within the treaty limitations and was free to build warships larger than those of the other major maritime powers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Japan's intention to acquire resource-producing colonies in the Pacific and Southeast Asia would likely lead to confrontation with the United States,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      thus the U.S. became Japan's primary potential enemy. The U.S. possessed significantly greater industrial power than Japan, with 32.2% of worldwide industrial production compared to Japan's 3.5%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Furthermore, several leading members of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">
       United States Congress
      </a>
      had pledged "to outbuild Japan three to one in a naval race."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Consequently, as Japanese industrial output could not compete with American industrial power,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Japanese ship designers developed plans for new battleships individually superior to their counterparts in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each of these battleships would be capable of engaging multiple enemy capital ships simultaneously, eliminating the need to expend as much industrial effort as the U.S. on battleship construction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design">
       Design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Musashi,Bridge.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2998" data-file-width="2076" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Musashi%2CBridge.jpg/200px-Musashi%2CBridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Musashi%2CBridge.jpg/300px-Musashi%2CBridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Musashi%2CBridge.jpg/400px-Musashi%2CBridge.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Musashi,Bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Bridge of the
        <i>
         Musashi
        </i>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Preliminary studies for a new class of battleships began after Japan's departure from the
      <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">
       League of Nations
      </a>
      and its renunciation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Naval_Treaty" title="Washington Naval Treaty">
       Washington
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/London_Naval_Treaty" title="London Naval Treaty">
       London
      </a>
      naval treaties; from 1934 to 1936, 24 initial designs were put forth. These early plans varied greatly in armament, propulsion, endurance, and armor.
      <a href="/wiki/Main_battery" title="Main battery">
       Main batteries
      </a>
      fluctuated between 460mm (18.1in) and 406mm (16.0in) guns, while the secondary armaments were composed of differing numbers of 155mm (6.1in), 127mm (5.0in), and 25mm (0.98in) guns. Propulsion in most of the designs was a hybrid
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">
       diesel
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      combination, though one relied solely on diesel and another planned for only turbines. Endurance in the designs had, at 18kn (21mph; 33km/h), a low of 6,000nmi (11,000km) in design A-140-J
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      to a high of 9,200nmi (17,000km) in designs A-140A and A-140-B
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      . Armor varied between providing protection from the fire of 406mm guns to enough protection against 460mm guns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After these had been reviewed, two of the original twenty-four were finalized as possibilities, A-140-F
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      and A-140-F
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      . Differing primarily in their range (4,900nmi (9,100km) versus 7,200nmi (13,300km) at 16kn (18mph; 30km/h)), they were used in the formation of the final preliminary study, which was finished on 20 July 1936. Tweaks to that design resulted in the definitive design of March 1937,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke49-50_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke49-50-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which was put forth by Rear-Admiral
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fukuda_Keiji&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fukuda Keiji (page does not exist)">
       Fukuda Keiji
      </a>
      ;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      an endurance of 7,200nm was finally decided upon, and the hybrid diesel-turbine propulsion was abandoned in favor of just turbines. The diesels were removed from the design because of problems with the engines aboard the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_aircraft_carrier_Ry%C5%ABh%C5%8D" title="Japanese aircraft carrier Ryh">
        Taigei
       </a>
      </i>
      submarine tender.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke49-50_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke49-50-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their engines, which were similar to the ones that were going to be mounted in the new battleships, required a "major repair and maintenance effort"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke49_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke49-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to keep them running due to a "fundamental design defect".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke49_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke49-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, if the engines failed entirely, the 200mm (7.9in) armor that protected that area would severely hamper any attempt to replace them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The final design called for a standard displacement of 64,000 long tons (65,000t) and a full-load displacement of 69,988 long tons (71,111t),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke53_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke53-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      making the ships of the class the largest battleships yet designed, and the largest battleships ever constructed. The design called for a main armament of nine 460-millimetre (18.1in)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_guns" title="Naval guns">
       naval guns
      </a>
      , mounted in three, three-gun turretseach of which weighed more than a 1930s-era destroyer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The designs were quickly approved by Japanese Naval high command,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston122_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston122-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      over the objections of naval aviators, who argued for the construction of
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">
       aircraft carriers
      </a>
      rather than battleships.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        A 1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In all, five
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      -class battleships were planned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ships">
       Ships
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ships">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:YamatoClassBattleships.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/YamatoClassBattleships.jpg/220px-YamatoClassBattleships.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/YamatoClassBattleships.jpg/330px-YamatoClassBattleships.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/YamatoClassBattleships.jpg/440px-YamatoClassBattleships.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:YamatoClassBattleships.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Yamato
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         Musashi
        </i>
        anchored in the waters off of the
        <a href="/wiki/Chuuk_State" title="Chuuk State">
         Truk Islands
        </a>
        in 1943
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although five
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      -class vessels had been planned in 1937, only threetwo battleships and a converted aircraft carrierwere ever completed. All three vessels were built in extreme secrecy, to prevent American intelligence officials from learning of their existence and specifications;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      indeed, the United States'
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Naval_Intelligence" title="Office of Naval Intelligence">
       Office of Naval Intelligence
      </a>
      only became aware of
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      by name in late 1942. At this early time, their assumptions on the class's specifications were quite far off; while they were correct on their length, the class was given as having a beam of 110 feet (34m) (in actuality, it was about 127 feet (39m)) and a displacement of 40,00057,000 tons (in actuality, 69,000tons). In addition, the main armament of
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      -class was given as nine 16-inch (41cm) guns as late as July 1945, four months after
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      was sunk.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston128_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Johnston128-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jane%27s_Fighting_Ships" title="Jane's Fighting Ships">
        Jane's Fighting Ships
       </a>
      </i>
      and the Western media also misreported the specifications of the ships. In September 1944,
      <i>
       Jane's Fighting Ships
      </i>
      listed the displacement of both the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      as 45,000 tons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, both the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">
        New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">
       Associated Press
      </a>
      reported that the two ships displaced 45,000 tons with a speed of 30 knots,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and even after the sinking of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      in April 1945,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
        The Times
       </a>
      </i>
      of London continued to give 45,000 tons as the ship's displacement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nevertheless, the existence of the shipsand their supposed specificationsheavily influenced American naval engineers in the design of the
      <a href="/wiki/Montana-class_battleship" title="Montana-class battleship">
       <i>
        Montana
       </i>
       -class battleships
      </a>
      , all five of which were to be built to counter the firepower of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Yamato">
       <i>
        Yamato
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yamato">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Yamato" title="Japanese battleship Yamato">
       Japanese battleship Yamato
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="706" data-file-width="1176" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg/220px-Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg/330px-Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg/440px-Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yamato_during_Trial_Service.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Yamato
        </i>
        on trials in 1941
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Yamato" title="Japanese battleship Yamato">
       <i>
        Yamato
       </i>
      </a>
      was ordered in March 1937,
      <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">
       laid down
      </a>
      4 November 1937,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_naming_and_launching" title="Ship naming and launching">
       launched
      </a>
      8 August 1940, and commissioned 16 December 1941.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She underwent training exercises until 27 May 1942, when the vessel was deemed "operable" by Admiral
      <a href="/wiki/Isoroku_Yamamoto" title="Isoroku Yamamoto">
       Isoroku Yamamoto
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Joining the 1st Battleship Division,
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      served as the flagship of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Combined_Fleet" title="Japanese Combined Fleet">
       Japanese Combined Fleet
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">
       Battle of Midway
      </a>
      in June 1942, yet did not engage enemy forces during the battle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The next two years were spent intermittently between
      <a href="/wiki/Chuuk_Lagoon" title="Chuuk Lagoon">
       Truk
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kure" title="Kure">
       Kure
      </a>
      naval bases, with her sister-ship
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      replacing
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      as flagship of the Combined Fleet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During this time period,
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      , as part of the 1st Battleship Division, deployed on multiple occasions to counteract American carrier-raids on Japanese island bases. On 25 December 1943, she suffered major torpedo damage at the hands of
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Skate_(SS-305)" title="USS Skate (SS-305)">
       USS
       <i>
        Skate
       </i>
      </a>
      , and was forced to return to Kure for repairs and structural upgrades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1944following extensive antiaircraft and secondary battery upgrades
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      joined the Second Fleet in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Philippine_Sea" title="Battle of the Philippine Sea">
       Battle of the Philippine Sea
      </a>
      , serving as an escort to a Japanese Carrier Division.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In October 1944, as part of Vice Admiral
      <a href="/wiki/Takeo_Kurita" title="Takeo Kurita">
       Takeo Kurita
      </a>
      's Center Force for the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leyte_Gulf" title="Battle of Leyte Gulf">
       Battle of Leyte Gulf
      </a>
      , she used her naval artillery against an enemy vessel for the only time, helping sink the American escort carrier
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Gambier_Bay_(CVE-73)" title="USS Gambier Bay (CVE-73)">
       <i>
        Gambier Bay
       </i>
      </a>
      and the destroyer
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Johnston_(DD-557)" title="USS Johnston (DD-557)">
       USS
       <i>
        Johnston
       </i>
      </a>
      before she was forced away by torpedoes from
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Heermann_(DD-532)" title="USS Heermann (DD-532)">
       USS
       <i>
        Heermann
       </i>
      </a>
      , which put her out of combat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lightly damaged at Kure in March 1945, the ship was then rearmed in preparation for operations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord_21-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      was sunk 7 April 1945 by American carrier aircraft during
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Ten-Go" title="Operation Ten-Go">
       Operation Ten-Go
      </a>
      , receiving 10 torpedo and 7 bomb hits before capsizing; 2,498 of the 2,700 crew-members were lost, including Vice-Admiral
      <a href="/wiki/Seiichi_It%C5%8D" title="Seiichi It">
       Seiichi It
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston128_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Johnston128-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The sinking of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      was seen as a major American victory, and
      <a href="/wiki/Hanson_W._Baldwin" title="Hanson W. Baldwin">
       Hanson W. Baldwin
      </a>
      , the military editor of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      , wrote that "the sinking of the new Japanese battleship
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      ... is striking proofif any were neededof the fatal weakness of Japan in the air and at sea".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Musashi">
       <i>
        Musashi
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Musashi">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Musashi" title="Japanese battleship Musashi">
       Japanese battleship Musashi
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="583" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg/220px-Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg/330px-Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg/440px-Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Japanese_battleship_Musashi_cropped.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Musashi
        </i>
        departing Brunei in October 1944
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Musashi" title="Japanese battleship Musashi">
       <i>
        Musashi
       </i>
      </a>
      was ordered in March 1937, laid down 29 March 1938, launched 1 November 1940, and commissioned 5 August 1942. From September to December 1942, she was involved in surface and air-combat training exercises at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hashirajima" title="Hashirajima">
       Hashirajima
      </a>
      . On 11 February 1943,
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      relieved her sister ship
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      as flagship of the Combined Fleet. Until July 1944,
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      shifted between the naval bases of Truk, Yokosuka, Brunei, and Kure. On 29 March 1944, she sustained moderate damage near the bow from one torpedo fired by the American submarine
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Tunny_(SS-282)" title="USS Tunny (SS-282)">
       <i>
        Tunny
       </i>
      </a>
      . After repairs and refitting throughout April 1944,
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      joined the 1st Battleship Division in Okinawa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord2_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord2-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 1944, as part of the Second Fleet, the ship escorted Japanese aircraft carriers during the Battle of the Philippine Sea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord2_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord2-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In October 1944, she left Brunei as part of Admiral
      <a href="/wiki/Takeo_Kurita" title="Takeo Kurita">
       Takeo Kurita
      </a>
      's Centre Force during the Battle of Leyte Gulf.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      was sunk 24 October during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Sibuyan_Sea" title="Battle of the Sibuyan Sea">
       Battle of the Sibuyan Sea
      </a>
      , taking 17 bomb and 19 torpedo hits, with the loss of 1,023 of her 2,399-man crew.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

         Shinano

        in November 1944
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      , originally Warship Number 110, was laid down as the third member of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class, albeit with a slightly modified design. Most of the original armor values were slightly reduced, including the belt, deck, and turrets. The savings in weight this entailed meant that improvements could be made in other areas, including added protection for fire-control and lookout positions. In addition, the 12.7cm (5.0in) secondary armament on the first two
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      s was to have been replaced by the 10cm (3.9in)/65 caliber
      <a href="/wiki/10_cm/65_Type_98_naval_gun" title="10 cm/65 Type 98 naval gun">
       Type 98 gun
      </a>
      . Although smaller, this gun was superior to the 127mm, possessing a significantly greater
      <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">
       muzzle velocity
      </a>
      , maximum range, anti-aircraft ceiling and rate of fire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 1942, following the Japanese defeat at Midway, construction of
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      was suspended, and the hull was gradually rebuilt as an aircraft carrier.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord3_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord3-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She was designed as a 64,800-ton support vessel that would be capable of ferrying, repairing and replenishing the airfleets of other carriers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reynolds61_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reynolds61-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although she was originally scheduled for commissioning in early 1945,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the construction of the ship was accelerated after the Battle of the Philippine Sea;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reynolds284_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reynolds284-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      this resulted in
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      being launched on 5 October 1944 and commissioned a little more than a month later on 19 November.
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      departed Yokosuka for Kure nine days later. In the early morning on 29 November,
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      was hit by four torpedoes from the
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Archerfish_(SS-311)" title="USS Archerfish (SS-311)">
       USS
       <i>
        Archerfish
       </i>
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord3_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord3-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the damage seemed manageable, poor flooding control caused the vessel to list to starboard. Shortly before midday, she capsized and sank, taking 1,435 of her 2,400-man crew with her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfrecord3_42-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfrecord3-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To this day,
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      is the largest naval vessel to have been sunk by a submarine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Warships_Number_111_and_797">
       Warships Number 111 and 797
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Warships Number 111 and 797">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Warship Number 111, never named, was planned as the fourth member of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class and the second ship to incorporate the improvements of
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      . The ship's
      <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">
       keel
      </a>
      was laid after
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      's launch in August 1940 and construction continued until December 1941, when the Japanese began to question their ambitious
      <a href="/wiki/Capital_ship" title="Capital ship">
       capital ship
      </a>
      building programwith the coming of war, the resources essential in constructing the ship would become much harder to obtain. As a result, the hull of the fourth vessel, only about 30% complete, was taken apart and scrapped in 1942; materials from this were used in the conversions of
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Ise" title="Japanese battleship Ise">
       <i>
        Ise
       </i>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Hy%C5%ABga" title="Japanese battleship Hyga">
       <i>
        Hyga
       </i>
      </a>
      to hybrid
      <a href="/wiki/Ise-class_battleship#Conversion_to_aircraft_carriers" title="Ise-class battleship">
       battleship/aircraft carriers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston124_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Johnston124-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        A 2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The fifth vessel, Warship Number 797, was planned as an improved
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      but was never laid down. In addition to the modifications made to that ship, 797 would have removed the two 155mm (6.1in)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wing_turret" title="Wing turret">
       wing turrets
      </a>
      in favor of additional 100mm guns; authors William Garzke and Robert Dulin estimate that this would have allowed for 24 of these weapons.
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      was eventually modified in 1944 to something akin to this.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Armament">
       Armament
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Armament">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg/220px-Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg/330px-Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg/440px-Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yamato_model_AA_guns.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Yamato
        </i>
        's port-side anti-aircraft armament as depicted in the model of the ship at the
        <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Museum" title="Yamato Museum">
         'Yamato Museum'
        </a>
        in Kure
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although the primary armament of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class was officially designated as the 40cm/45 caliber (15.9in) Type 94,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navweaps_18.1_53-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-navweaps_18.1-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it actually took the form of nine
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/40_cm/45_Type_94" title="40 cm/45 Type 94">
       46 cm/45 caliber (18.1 in)
      </a>
      gunsthe largest guns ever fitted to a warship
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      mounted in three 3-gun turrets, each of which weighed 2,774 metric tons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson75_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson75-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each gun was 21.13 metres (69.3ft) long and weighed 147.3 metric tons (145.0 long tons).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      High-explosive armour-piercing shells were used which were capable of being fired 42.0 kilometres (26.1mi) at a rate of 1 to 2 per minute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navweaps_18.1_53-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-navweaps_18.1-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The main guns were also capable of firing 1,360kg (3,000lb)
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beehive_(anti-aircraft_shell)" title="Beehive (anti-aircraft shell)">
        3 Shiki tsjdan
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Common Type 3") anti-aircraft shells.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        A 3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A time fuze was used to set how far away the shells would explode (although they were commonly set to go off 1,000 metres (1,100yd) away). Upon detonation, each of these shells would release 900 incendiary-filled tubes in a 20 cone facing towards incoming aircraft; a bursting charge was then used to explode the shell itself so that more steel splinters were created, and then the tubes would ignite. The tubes would burn for five seconds at about 3,000C (5,430F) and would start a flame that was around 5 metres (16ft) long. Even though they comprised 40% of the total main ammunition load by 1944,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navweaps_18.1_53-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-navweaps_18.1-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       3 Shiki tsjdan
      </i>
      were rarely used in combat against enemy aircraft due to the severe damage the firing of these shells inflicted on the barrels of the main guns;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinberg54_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-steinberg54-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      indeed, one of the shells may have exploded early and disabled one of
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      's guns during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Sibuyan_Sea" title="Battle of the Sibuyan Sea">
       Battle of the Sibuyan Sea
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navweaps_18.1_53-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-navweaps_18.1-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The shells were intended to put up a
      <a href="/wiki/Barrage_(artillery)" title="Barrage (artillery)">
       barrage
      </a>
      of flame that any aircraft attempting to attack would have to navigate through. However, U.S. pilots considered these shells to be more of a pyrotechnics display than a competent anti-aircraft weapon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navweaps_18.1_53-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-navweaps_18.1-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the original design, the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class' secondary armament comprised twelve
      <a href="/wiki/15.5_cm/60_3rd_Year_Type_naval_gun" title="15.5 cm/60 3rd Year Type naval gun">
       15.5 cm/60 Type 3
      </a>
      guns mounted in four triple turrets (one forward, one aft, two midships),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson75_54-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson75-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and twelve
      <a href="/wiki/12.7_cm/40_Type_89_naval_gun" title="12.7 cm/40 Type 89 naval gun">
       12.7 cm/40 Type 89
      </a>
      guns in six double-turrets (three on each side amidships).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson75_54-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson75-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class originally carried twenty-four
      <a href="/wiki/Type_96_25_mm_AT/AA_Gun" title="Type 96 25 mm AT/AA Gun">
       25 mm Type 96
      </a>
      anti-aircraft guns, primarily mounted amidships.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson75_54-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson75-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1944,
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      the sole remaining member of the classunderwent significant anti-aircraft upgrades, with the configuration of secondary armament changed to six 15.5cm (6.1in) guns,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston180_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston180-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      twenty-four 12.7cm (5.0in) guns,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston180_58-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston180-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and one hundred and sixty-two 2.5cm (0.98in) antiaircraft guns,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston180_58-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston180-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in preparation for operations in Leyte Gulf.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson128_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson128-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The armament on
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      was quite different from that of her sister vessels due to her conversion. As the carrier was designed for a support role, significant anti-aircraft weaponry was installed on the vessel: sixteen 12.7cm (5.0in) guns,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfshinano_60-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfshinano-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      one hundred and twenty-five 2.5cm (0.98in) anti-aircraft guns,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfshinano_60-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfshinano-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and three hundred and thirty-six 5-inch (13cm) anti-aircraft rocket launchers in twelve twenty-eight barrel turrets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preston84_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-preston84-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      None of these guns were ever used against an enemy vessel or aircraft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preston84_61-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-preston84-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Armour">
       Armour
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Armour">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Belt_armor" title="Belt armor">
       Belt armor
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1213" data-file-width="782" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg/130px-Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg/195px-Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg/260px-Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg.png 2x" width="130"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yamato-armorsheme-DE_-_magazines_cut.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Protection schematics of the class at the rear turret. Here is
        <a href="/wiki/File:Yamato-armorsheme-DE.svg" title="File:Yamato-armorsheme-DE.svg">
         another cut
        </a>
        amidships
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Designed to engage multiple enemy battleships simultaneously,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schom.2C_p._270_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Schom.2C_p._270-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      s were fitted with heavy armour plating described by naval historian Mark Stille as providing "an unparalleled degree of protection in surface combat".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The main belt of armour along the side of the vessel was 410 millimetres (16in) thick,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with additional bulkheads 355 millimetres (14.0in) thick beyond the main-belt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Furthermore, the top hull shape was very advanced, the peculiar sideways curving effectively maximizing armor protection and structural rigidity while optimizing weight. The armour on the main-turrets surpassed even that of the main-belt, with plating 650 millimetres (26in) thick.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Armor plates in both the main belt and main turrets was made of Vickers Hardened, which was a face-hardened steel armor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke96_63-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke96-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Deck armour75 millimetres (3.0in) thickwas composed of a nickel-chromium-molybdenum alloy. Ballistics tests at the proving ground at Kamegabuki demonstrated the deck alloy to be superior to the homogeneous Vickers plates by 1015%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke96_63-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke96-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additional plating was designed by manipulating the chromium and nickel composition of the alloy. Higher contents of nickel allowed the plate to be rolled and bent without developing fracture properties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke96_63-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke96-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The relatively new procedure of
      <a href="/wiki/Arc_welding" title="Arc welding">
       arc welding
      </a>
      was used extensively throughout the ship, strengthening the durability of the armour plating.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzsimons2609_64-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fitzsimons2609-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Through this technique, the lower-side
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belt_armour" title="Belt armour">
       belt armour
      </a>
      , included in the ships as a response to gunnery experiments upon
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Tosa" title="Japanese battleship Tosa">
       <i>
        Tosa
       </i>
      </a>
      and the new Japanese Type 91 shell which could travel great lengths underwater,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke94_65-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke94-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was used to strengthen the hull structure of the entire vessel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzsimons2609_64-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-fitzsimons2609-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In total, the vessels of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class contained 1,147 watertight compartments,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzsimons2609_64-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-fitzsimons2609-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of which 1,065 were beneath the armoured deck.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fitzsimons2609_64-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-fitzsimons2609-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the armour of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class still suffered from several shortcomings, many of which would prove fatal in 194445.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-underwaterarmour_66-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-underwaterarmour-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In particular, poor jointing between the upper-belt and lower-belt armour created a weak-point just below the waterline, causing the class to be susceptible to air-dropped torpedoes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinberg54_57-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-steinberg54-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other structural weaknesses existed near the bow of the vessels, where the armour plating was generally thinner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinberg54_57-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-steinberg54-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The hull of the
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      was subject to even greater structural weaknesses, having been equipped with minimal armour and no watertight compartments at the time of her sinking.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfshinano_60-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-cfshinano-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Propulsion">
       Propulsion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Propulsion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class was fitted with 12 Kanpon Boilers, which powered quadruple
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
       steam turbines
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson74_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson74-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These, in turn, drove four 6-metre (20ft) propellers. This powerplant enabled the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class to achieve a top speed of 27 knots (50km/h).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With an indicated horsepower of 147,948 (110,325kW),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston123_6-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-johnston123-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class' ability to function alongside fast carriers was limited. In addition, the fuel consumption rate of both battleships was very high.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson128_59-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson128-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result, neither battleship was used in combat during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands_Campaign" title="Solomon Islands Campaign">
       Solomon Islands Campaign
      </a>
      or the minor battles during the "island hopping" period of 1943 and early 1944.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson128_59-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-jackson128-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The propulsion system of the
      <i>
       Shinano
      </i>
      was slightly improved, allowing the carrier to achieve a top speed of 28 knots (52km/h).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preston84_61-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-preston84-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Super_Yamato.22-class_battleships">
       "Super
       <i>
        Yamato
       </i>
       "-class battleships
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title='Edit section: "Super Yamato"-class battleships'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Design_A-150_battleship" title="Design A-150 battleship">
       Design A-150 battleship
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Two battleships of an entirely new, and larger, design were planned as a part of the 1942 fleet replenishment program. Designated as Design A-150 and initially named Warship Number 178 and Warship Number 179, plans for the ships began soon after the design of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class was finished, probably in 193839. Everything was "essentially completed" sometime in 1941, but with war on the horizon, work on the battleships was halted to fill a need for additional warships, such as aircraft carriers and cruisers, to replace war losses of those vital ships. The Japanese loss in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">
       Battle of Midway
      </a>
      , where four carriers were sunk (out of ten, to date, in the entire navy), made it certain that work on the ships would never begin. In the third volume of their
      <i>
       Battleships
      </i>
      series,
      <i>
       Axis and Neutral Battleships in World War II
      </i>
      , the authors William H. Garzke and Robert O. Dulin asserted that these ships would have been the "most powerful battleships in history" because of their massive 51cm (20in)
      <a href="/wiki/Main_battery" title="Main battery">
       main battery
      </a>
      and extensive anti-aircraft weaponry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway.27s_67-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Conway.27s-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke_68-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Similar to the fate of papers relating to the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class, most papers and all plans relating to the class were destroyed to prevent capture at the end of the war. It is known that the final design of the ships would have had an even greater firepower and size than the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      classa main battery of six 51cm (20in) guns in three twin turrets and secondary dual purpose armament consisting of twenty-four 10cm (3.9in) dual mounted guns (similar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Akizuki-class_destroyer" title="Akizuki-class destroyer">
       <i>
        Akizuki
       </i>
       -class
      </a>
      destroyers). The
      <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(ship)" title="Displacement (ship)">
       displacement
      </a>
      was to be bigger than the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      's, and a side
      <a href="/wiki/Belt_armor" title="Belt armor">
       armor belt
      </a>
      of 46cm (18in) was planned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conway.27s_67-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Conway.27s-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garzke_68-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Garzke-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction_of_records">
       Destruction of records
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Destruction of records">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yamato1945.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2026" data-file-width="4177" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Yamato1945.png/200px-Yamato1945.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Yamato1945.png/300px-Yamato1945.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Yamato1945.png/400px-Yamato1945.png 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yamato1945.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A drawing of
        <i>
         Yamato
        </i>
        , depicting her appearance circa 1945
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      On the eve of the Allies'
      <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Japan" title="Occupation of Japan">
       occupation of Japan
      </a>
      , special-service officers of the Imperial Japanese Navy destroyed virtually all records, drawings, and photographs of or relating to the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      -class battleships, leaving only fragmentary records of the design characteristics and other technical matters. The destruction of these documents was so efficient that until 1948 the only known images of the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      were those taken by
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      aircraft involved in the attacks on the two battleships. Although some additional photographs and information, from documents that were not destroyed, have come to light over the years, the loss of the majority of written records for the class has made extensive research into the
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      class somewhat difficult.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because of the lack of written records, information on the class largely came from interviews of Japanese officers following Japan's surrender.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, in October 1942, based upon a special request from
      <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">
       Adolf Hitler
      </a>
      , German Admiral
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wenneker" title="Paul Wenneker">
       Paul Wenneker
      </a>
      , attached to the German Naval Attache in Japan, was allowed to inspect a
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      -class battleship while it was undergoing maintenance in a dockyard, at which time Admiral Wenneker cabled a detailed description of the warship to Berlin. On 22 August 1943, Erich Groner, a German naval historian, and author of the book
      <i>
       Die Deutschen Kriegschiffe, 18151945
      </i>
      , was shown the report while at the "
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BChrer_Headquarters" title="Fhrer Headquarters">
       Fhrer Headquarters
      </a>
      ", and was directed to make an "interpretation" and then prepare a "design sketch drawing" of the Japanese battleship. The material was preserved by Erich Groner's wife, Mrs. H. Groner, and submitted to publishers in the 1950s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">
       Cultural significance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yamato-class_battleship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:YAMATO_Moder.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/YAMATO_Moder.JPG/220px-YAMATO_Moder.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/YAMATO_Moder.JPG/330px-YAMATO_Moder.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/YAMATO_Moder.JPG/440px-YAMATO_Moder.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:YAMATO_Moder.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Visitors to the
        <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Museum" title="Yamato Museum">
         Yamato Museum
        </a>
        examine the museum's 1:10 scale model of the battleship in 2006
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      From the time of their construction until the present day,
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Musashi
      </i>
      have carried a notable presence in Japanese culture,
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      in particular. Upon completion, the battleships represented the epitome of Imperial Japanese naval engineering. In addition, the two ships, due to their size, speed, and power, visibly embodied Japan's determination and readiness to defend its interests against the western powers, especially the United States.
      <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Fukudome" title="Shigeru Fukudome">
       Shigeru Fukudome
      </a>
      , chief of the Operations Section of the
      <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy_General_Staff" title="Imperial Japanese Navy General Staff">
       Imperial Japanese Navy General Staff
      </a>
      , described the two ships as "symbols of naval power that provided to officers and men alike a profound sense of confidence in their navy."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      , and especially the story of her sinking, has appeared often in
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_popular_culture" title="Japanese popular culture">
       Japanese popular culture
      </a>
      , such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">
       anime
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Battleship_Yamato" title="Space Battleship Yamato">
        Space Battleship Yamato
       </a>
      </i>
      and the 2005 film
      <a href="/wiki/Yamato_(film)" title="Yamato (film)">
       <i>
        Yamato
       </i>
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The appearances in popular culture usually portray the ship's last mission as a brave, selfless, but futile, symbolic effort by the participating Japanese sailors to defend their homeland. One of the reasons that the warship may have such significance in Japanese culture is that the word "Yamato" was often used as a poetic name for Japan. Thus, the end of battleship
      <i>
       Yamato
      </i>
      could serve as a metaphor for the end of the
      <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">
       Japanese empire
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
