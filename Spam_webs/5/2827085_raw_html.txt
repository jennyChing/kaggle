     <h1>
      9 experiences you can only have in Argentina
     </h1>
     <div class="post-inner clearfix">
      <div class="post-meta">
       <div class="post-meta-author">
        by
        <a href="http://matadornetwork.com/author/david-miller/">
         David Miller
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="post-meta-date">
        December 17, 2013
       </div>
       <div class="post-avatar">
        <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-100" height="100" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/authors/david-miller.jpg" width="100"/>
       </div>
       <div class="post-share-wrapper post-share--vertical" data-post-id="232999" data-sticky="" data-sticky-max-width="991" data-sticky-offset-top="69" data-sticky-parent-contain="" data-sticky-parent-selector=".page-section">

       <p>
        OUT OF THE last 10 years of my life, Ive lived in four different parts of
        <a href="http://www.intrepidtravel.com/argentina">
         Argentina
        </a>
        . Ive learned (and am still learning) how culturally, musically, and in terms of adventure sports objectives (mountains, rivers, waves), it can totally shatter your sense of whats possible.
       </p>
       <p>
        Area-wise, Argentina is the worlds eighth-largest country, but in terms of population it ranks only 32nd. There are huge tracts of open space across the pampas out to the Atlantic Patagonia, and all along the Andean Cordillera down to Tierra del Fuego. And then theres Buenos Aires: densely packed, in its own way seeming to go on forever along the Rio de la Plata, a place often likened more to a European city than its fellow Latin American capitals.
       </p>
       <p>
        The truth, though, is that Argentina has so many places and possibilities unlike anywhere else. Here are just a few experiences that stand out.
       </p>
       <noscript>
        <img alt="" src="http://cdn.matadornetwork.com.s3.amazonaws.com/assets/images/icons/mfinish.png" title=""/>
       </noscript>
       <img class=" lazy" data-original="http://cdn.matadornetwork.com.s3.amazonaws.com/assets/images/icons/mfinish.png" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png"/>

         </noscript>
         <img class="post-gallery-item-img lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/aconcagua.jpg" height="701" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="1024"/>

          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Trek / summit the highest mountain in the Western / Southern Hemisphere.
           </h2>
           <p>
            Aconcagua, at 22,841 feet, is the highest peak in the Americas, which means it's the highest in both the Southern and Western Hemispheres. It's also the tallest non-technical climb in the world, meaning there's a "walk up" route to the summit that does not necessitate technical mountaineering gear or ropework. That said, because of its extreme height / conditions, Aconcagua is still an inherently dangerous place, with people dying each year.
            <br/>
            <b>
             Photo:
            </b>
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/lucianocampagnolo/226205162/sizes/l/">
             .Luc
            </a>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>

         </noscript>
         <img class="post-gallery-item-img lazy" data-original="http://cdn2.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/piltri-shelter.jpg" height="683" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="1024"/>
         <div class="post-gallery-item-meta">

          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Drink homemade beer and eat Patagonian
            <em>
             cordero
            </em>
            deep in the backcountry.
           </h2>
           <p>
            The Patagonian region of Argentina has the unique and enviable tradition of
            <em>
             refugios
            </em>
            tended by
            <em>
             refugieros
            </em>
            . These are backcountry shelters with caretakers who live there, often year round, typically raising sheep, tending vegetable gardens, brewing homemade beer, and otherwise creating a homey atmosphere and level of comfort you can only dream about while deep in the mountains of other places.
            <br/>
            <b>
             Photo:
            </b>
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/nataliehg/3275121289/sizes/l/">
             NatalieHG
            </a>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="post-gallery-item">
         <noscript>
          <img alt="" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/ushuaia.jpg" title=""/>
         </noscript>
         <img class="post-gallery-item-img lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/ushuaia.jpg" height="498" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="750"/>
         <div class="post-gallery-item-meta">
          <div class="post-gallery-item-count">
           3
          </div>
          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Ski / explore in the southernmost city in the world.
           </h2>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Although Puerto Williams, a small Chilean town on the southern side of the Beagle Channel, is slightly further south, Ushuaiawith a population of some 60,000, and a full tourism infrastructure including airport and ski areasqualifies as the worlds southernmost city. Its the closest city to Antarcticaonly 1,000km away.
           </p>
           One unique thing about its ski area, Cerro Castor, is that you can ski at super low elevationsonly about 600 feet above sea level. Technically its the worlds southernmost ski resort, and has an exceptionally long ski season (June through October), with the lifts operating during the summer as well, providing access to the glacier.
           <br/>
           <b>
            Photo:
           </b>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/v1ctor/5353107345/sizes/l/">
            V1ctor Casale
           </a>

           <h2>
            Go to an after-after-after-party club the day after an all-nighter.
           </h2>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            There are hard-partying subsections of the population everywhere you go, from Tel Aviv to New York to Barcelona. But nowhere in the world is all-night dancing and celebrating such a widespread part of the culture as in Argentina. Most visitors are surprised to learn that from an early age, kids simply stay up during social events and parties, that there's no real "bedtime," and thus people grow up acculturated to partying what would be marathon hours for everyone else like it's no big deal.
           </p>
           As Kate Siobhan writes in Matador's nightlife guide,
           <a href="http://101places.com">
            101 Places
           </a>
           , "...at Miloca, you can keep on dancing and watch the sun come up at the same time. If 7 or 8am isnt quite enough for you, try Cocoliche, where the massive dance party keeps going until 1pm. Is 1pm not enough? Theres also Caix, a concrete dance haven where on Sundays, hardcore party people pay $50 to keep on going until (seriously?) 3pm."

          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Visit a true
            <em>
             milonga
            </em>
            .
           </h2>
           <p>
            Tango has spread and been glamorized worldwide, with people taking it all kinds of directionsfrom competitions to remixes to people setting "tango world records"all of which the innovators from the turn of the 19th century would've likely found amusing. You can find tango lessons and even clubs all over the globe, but the roots remain in the intimate
            <em>
             milongas
            </em>
            in Buenos Aires, where local people still come to dance with their partners week after week. Visiting a
            <em>
             milonga
            </em>
            , seeing the attitude and style of the dancersparticularly the partners who've been together for decadesis super inspiring.
            <br/>
            <b>
             Photo:
            </b>
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/libertinus/2225386624/sizes/o/">
             Libertinus
            </a>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="post-gallery-item">
         <noscript>
          <img alt="" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/superclasico-river-2-greatjambino.jpg" title=""/>
         </noscript>
         <img class="post-gallery-item-img lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/superclasico-river-2-greatjambino.jpg" height="768" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="1024"/>
         <div class="post-gallery-item-meta">
          <div class="post-gallery-item-count">
           6
          </div>
          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Attend a Superclsico.
           </h2>
           <p>
            The Superclsico is the one of the most passionate and storied traditions in all of sports, with River Plate hailing from a more affluent neighborhood, and Boca Juniors from the working class neighborhood of La Boca maintaining a fierce rivalry since 1908. Both teams have produced international stars, most notably Diego Maradona from Boca Juniors. The Superclsico is infamous for fans' continuous roaring and even making the stadiums and surrounding areas tremble due to the intensity of their movement / cheering. The teams switch off between the River Plate stadium (pictured above) and "La Bonbonera" in La Boca. Things get more than rowdy in the Superclsico: Avoid violence by staying away from fans baiting / instigating rival fans.
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="post-gallery-item">
         <noscript>
          <img alt="" src="http://cdn2.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/las-madres.jpg" title=""/>
         </noscript>
         <img class="post-gallery-item-img lazy" data-original="http://cdn2.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/las-madres.jpg" height="1024" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="768"/>
         <div class="post-gallery-item-meta">
          <div class="post-gallery-item-count">
           7
          </div>
          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Talk to women continuing one of the greatest, longest-running remembrances.
           </h2>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            In one of the darkest chapters of American history, Operation Condor was a period of state-organized terrorism in which the US collaborated with right-wing dictatorships to overthrow governments in violent coups, leading to prolonged war on left-wing political groups throughout nearly every country in South America. Of all the atrocities committed, none were so heinous as the forced "disappearance" of tens of thousands of Argentines, known as the "Desaparecidos."
           </p>
           Mothers of the
           <em>
            desaparecidos
           </em>
           eventually known as the Madres of Plaza del Mayobegan meeting in the plaza directly in front of the Casa Rosada in 1976 as a way of trying to find answers about their missing children and to organize in defiance of the government's terror campaign. Since that very first meeting of the mothers, the Madres of Plaza del Mayo have continued to meet every Thursday, walking around the plaza, sharing their testimony, and showing their resilience long after the war criminals from the dictatorship have been imprisoned.
           <br/>
           <b>
            Photo:
           </b>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/brianmka/2913704052/sizes/l/">
            BrianMKA
           </a>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>

         <img class="post-gallery-item-img lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2013/12/rio-de-la-plata.jpg" height="530" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="800"/>
         <div class="post-gallery-item-meta">

          </div>
          <div class="post-gallery-item-caption">
           <h2>
            Ferry across the world's widest river-mouth.
           </h2>
           <p>
            The Rio de la Plata, or "El Rio" as it's referred to by locals, is the world's widest river-mouth, over 200km wide before technically becoming the Atlantic Ocean. A massive estuary formed by the confluence of the Paran and Uruguay Rivers, Rio de la Plata is also the border between Uruguay and Argentina (Buenos Aires is visible along the crescent-shaped cutout on the right side). The ferries between Buenos Aires and Colonia, Uruguay are fun (several come and go each day) and take between one and three hours depending on the kind of ferry you're on. There are also daily ferries between Montevideo and Buenos Aires.
            <br/>

             eutrophication&amp;hypoxia

           </p>

           <h2>
            Drive the most isolated part of the worlds longest network of highways.
           </h2>
           <p>
            The Panamericana is the worlds longest network of interlinked roads, stretching some 30,000 miles from Prudhoe Bay in Alaska to Ushuaia in Argentina. The southernmost part of the route, Ruta 40, parallels the Andes through Santa Cruz province, with long unpaved sections through isolated terrain. This isnt like regular roadtripping: Prepare ahead of time, making sure you bring extra gas cans (you cant always count on gas stations), water, food, and camping supplies.
