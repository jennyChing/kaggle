         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Caponata alla siciliana
         </h1>
         <p class="p-meta">
          <span>
           In
           <a href="http://memoriediangelina.com/category/antipasti/" title="View all posts in: antipasti">
            antipasti
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://memoriediangelina.com/category/sicilia/" title="View all posts in: Sicilia">
            Sicilia
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://memoriediangelina.com/category/summer/" title="View all posts in: summer">
            summer
           </a>
           by Frank
          </span>
          <span>
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2011-06-18T19:57:00+00:00">
            18 June 2011
           </time>
          </span>
          <span>
           <a class="meta-comments" href="http://memoriediangelina.com/2011/06/18/caponata-alla-siciliana/#comments" title="Leave a comment on: Caponata alla siciliana">
            29 Comments
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <div class="entry-content content">
         <div class="at-above-post addthis_default_style addthis_toolbox at-wordpress-hide" data-title="Caponata alla siciliana" data-url="http://memoriediangelina.com/2011/06/18/caponata-alla-siciliana/">
         </div>
         <div class="at-above-post-recommended addthis_default_style addthis_toolbox at-wordpress-hide">
         </div>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          I have to admit, Sicilian food has always been something of a mystery to me. Many Sicilian dishes remind me of Angelinas Campanian cookingand Sicilians actually lay claim to
          <i>
           <a href="http://memoriediangelina.com/2010/08/21/parmigiana-di-melanzane/" title="Parmigiana di melanzane (Eggplant Parmesan)">
            melanzane alla parmigiana
           </a>
           ,
          </i>
          one of her signature dishesbut there is something different about some of the taste combinations youll find in Sicilian cookery, especially the tendency to combine sweet and sour.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          <strong>
           <em>
            La caponata
           </em>
          </strong>
          , one of the most famous Sicilian dishes, is a good example. Akind of southern cousin to the
          <i>
           ratatouille
          </i>
          of Provence, this dish stars eggplant, with celery co-starring and with tomato and onions in supporting roles, along with capers and olives, typically southern Italian flavor boosters. And, yes,it has a sweet-and-sour finishing touch that, if not overdone, perfectly balances out the flavors.
         </p>
         <h4 style="text-align: justify;">
          Ingredients
         </h4>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Serves 6 or more, as an antipasto or as part of a buffet
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           1 kilo (2 lbs.) eggplant, cut into small cubes
          </li>
          <li>
           1 bunch of celery, cut into bite-sized pieces
          </li>
          <li>
           1 large onion, thinly sliced
          </li>
          <li>
           1 small can of tomatoes or 250g (1/2 lb) fresh, skinned and seeded
          </li>
          <li>
           Salt and pepper
          </li>
          <li>
           A handful of capers
          </li>
          <li>
           A handful of pignoli nuts
          </li>
          <li>
           250g (1/2 lb.) of green olives, pitted and halved
          </li>
          <li>
           A sprig of fresh marjoram
          </li>
          <li>
           Olive oil
          </li>
          <li>
           Sugar and vinegar, to taste
          </li>
         </ul>
         <h4 style="text-align: justify;">
          Directions
         </h4>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          The first step in this recipe, which is not absolutely necessary but a good idea, especially for larger, more mature eggplant, is to remove the excess liquid from the eggplant by sprinking the eggplant cubes with salt and placing them in a colander, with a small plate on top to exert some gentle pressure and one below to catch the eggplant liquid as it drains. Let the eggplant steep for an hour or more.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          When you are ready to cook, gather up a handful of eggplant with a paper towel, give it a gentle squeeze to dry the cubes, and throw them into abundant olive oilabout 1 cm (1/2 in) deepin a large pot. (Enameled cast iron pots work particularly well.) Work in batches to avoid crowding the eggplant, removing them with a skimmer when the eggplant cubes are just lightly browned to a basket or bowl lined with paper towels to soak up the excess oil.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          When all your eggplant is done, there should be less oil in the pan, but still enough to cook with. Add the celery, saut for a few minutes until the celery is tender but still has some bite left in it. Season with salt and pepper as it cooks. Remove with a skimmer and set aside.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Now add the onion to the remaining oil in the pot and saut it gently until it is quite soft. Add the tomato. Simmer the tomato until it has melted and reduced to a nice, sauce-like consistency.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Now add back your eggplant and celery, along with the capers, pignoli, olives and the marjoram. Allow everything to simmer together for about 5-10 minutes. A minute or two before its done, add the sugar and vinegar, mix well (but gently!) and let it finish simmering. Turn off the heat and let the dishcool entirely before serving.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          <a href="http://i0.wp.com/memoriediangelina.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/06/Caponata-Plated1.jpg">
           <img alt="Caponata" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9979" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/memoriediangelina.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/06/Caponata-Plated1.jpg?resize=530%2C720"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <h4>
          Notes
         </h4>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          There are two main points to watch out for when making
          <em>
           caponata alla siciliana
          </em>
          . First, depending on the quality of the
          <strong>
           eggplant
          </strong>
          , it may become very soft and, if it gets stirred too often or too vigorously, it will turn to mush. You should be very gingerly when mixinga spatula will do a better job here than a spoon. It also helps to brown the eggplant over high heat; this way it will brown before getting too soft. The final simmering togetherof the ingredients should also lasta few minutes. The initial steeping of the eggplant in salt also helps. Besides removing the often bitter natural liquids in the eggplant, it produces a firmer texture. Recipes vary as to how long you should leave them to drain; some call for several hours, but with most eggplant an hour will do. In fact, even 30 minutes will help. The size and age of the eggplant will determine how long you will need. Smaller eggplant, by the way, tend to be firmer and are less needful of this initial step.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          The other point is
          <strong>
           greasiness
          </strong>
          , since everything gets cooked in abundant oil. Now, of course, one persons greasy is another persons unctuousness, and this dish will never be fat-free. But if you want to lower the oilyness (and the calorie count) there are a few tricks. First, as mentioned, fry your eggplant at high heat, this will limit the amount of oil that the eggplant will absorb. Make sure to sop up the excess oil with paper towels. The same goes for the celery. And once the dish has cooled, if you find the resulting dish is a bit too rich for your taste, you can either skim the excess oil off with a spoon or even (as I did last night) strain the entire thing over a sieve for just a minute or two to allow the excess oil to drain off. By the way, most recipes for
          <em>
           caponata alla siciliana
          </em>
          will tell you to fry the eggplant and celery separately, and then saut the onion in yet another pot. That strikes me as yet another way to wind up with a greasy dish; my one pot method works perfectly well and saves you some clean up afterwards.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Traditional recipes will tell you to de-string the
          <strong>
           celery
          </strong>
          and blanch it before sauting, because in the old days celery was very strongly flavored and had tough filaments. Modern celery (at least here in the US) is raised to be almost stringless and very mild in flavor. If in doubt, take a bite and see if these preliminaries are needed. The
          <strong>
           capers
          </strong>
          should be the kind packed in salt, in which case rinse them and, if they are very salty, you may want to soak them for a few minutes. But the kind packed in brine will do fine in a pinch. Of course, if you can find imported Sicilian capers, in particular the famous
          <a href="http://radicchioblog.com/2010/02/19/italian-capers-from-pantelleria-what-makes-them-so-special/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://radicchioblog.com/2010/02/19/italian-capers-from-pantelleria-what-makes-them-so-special/', 'capperi di Pantelleria');">
           capperi di Pantelleria
          </a>
          , that would be perfect. Since this is obviously a summer dish, fresh
          <strong>
           tomatoes
          </strong>
          rather than canned are traditional. But unless you are sure of the flavor and freshness of your tomatoes, to my mind canned is probably a better choice.Ive also seen recipes with considerably less celery and/or tomato. Other recipes add tomato paste for a redder version. In short, you can play with the proportions and ingredientsbut whatever you do, the eggplant should always be the star of the show!
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          How much
          <strong>
           sugar and vinegar
          </strong>
          , you ask? Well, I tend not to measure, but a couple of spoonful of sugar along with a splash of the vinegar (I prefer the white wine variety) should do you fine. Add more if you want a more pronounced sweet and sour flavor, but I like just a hint.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          <em>
           Caponata
          </em>
          is said to be of Spanish origin and, like many Italian dishes, has an interesting history, which you can read about
          <a href="http://www.cliffordawright.com/caw/food/entries/display.php/id/57/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cliffordawright.com/caw/food/entries/display.php/id/57/', 'here');">
           here
          </a>
          .
         </p>

            <ol class="instructions" id="zlrecipe-instructions-list">
             <li class="instruction" id="zlrecipe-instruction-0" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
              The first step in this recipe, which is not absolutely necessary but a good idea, especially for larger, more mature eggplant, is to remove the excess liquid from the eggplant by sprinking the eggplant cubes with salt and placing them in a colander, with a small plate on top to exert some gentle pressure and one below to catch the eggplant liquid as it drains. Let the eggplant steep for an hour or more.
             </li>
             <li class="instruction" id="zlrecipe-instruction-1" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
              When you are ready to cook, gather up a handful of eggplant with a paper  towel, give it a gentle squeeze to dry the cubes, and throw them into abundant olive oilabout 1 cm (1/2 in) deepin a large pot. (Enameled cast iron pots work particularly well.) Work in batches to avoid crowding the eggplant, removing them with a skimmer when the eggplant cubes are just lightly browned to a basket or bowl lined with paper towels to soak up the excess oil.
             </li>
             <li class="instruction" id="zlrecipe-instruction-2" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
              When all your eggplant is done, there should be less oil in the pan, but still enough to cook with. Add the celery, saut for a few minutes until the celery is tender but still has some 'bite' left in it. Season with salt and pepper as it cooks. Remove with a skimmer and set aside.
             </li>
             <li class="instruction" id="zlrecipe-instruction-3" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
              Now add the onion to the remaining oil in the pot and saut it gently until it is quite soft. Add the tomato. Simmer the tomato until it has melted and reduced to a nice, sauce-like consistency.
             </li>
             <li class="instruction" id="zlrecipe-instruction-4" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
              Now add back your eggplant and celery, along with the capers, pignoli, olives and the marjoram. Allow everything to simmer together for about 5-10 minutes. A minute or two before it's done, add the sugar and vinegar, mix well (but gently!) and let it finish simmering. Turn off the heat and let your caponata cool entirely before serving.
             </li>
            </ol>
