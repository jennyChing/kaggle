     <p>
      <b>
       Gerhard Berger
      </b>
      (born 27 August 1959) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_drivers_from_Austria" title="Formula One drivers from Austria">
       Austrian former Formula One racing driver
      </a>
      . He competed in Formula One for 14 seasons, twice finishing 3rd overall in the championship (
      <a href="/wiki/1988_Formula_One_season" title="1988 Formula One season">
       1988
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1994_Formula_One_season" title="1994 Formula One season">
       1994
      </a>
      ), both times driving for
      <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">
       Ferrari
      </a>
      . He won ten Grands Prix, achieved 48 podiums, 12 poles and 21 fastest laps. With 210 starts he is amongst the most experienced Formula One drivers of all time. He led 33 of the 210 races he competed in and retired from 95 of them. His first and last victories were also the first and last victories for the
      <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">
       Benetton
      </a>
      team, with eleven years separating them. He was also a race winner with Ferrari and with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_McLaren" title="Team McLaren">
       McLaren
      </a>
      . When at McLaren, Berger drove alongside
      <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">
       Ayrton Senna
      </a>
      , contributing to the team's
      <a href="/wiki/1990_Formula_One_season" title="1990 Formula One season">
       1990
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1991_Formula_One_season" title="1991 Formula One season">
       1991
      </a>
      constructors titles.
     </p>
     <p>
      Between 2006 and 2008 Berger owned 50% of the
      <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Toro_Rosso" title="Scuderia Toro Rosso">
       Scuderia Toro Rosso
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">
       Formula One
      </a>
      team.
     </p>

     <p>
      Gerhard Berger was born in
      <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rgl" title="Wrgl">
       Wrgl
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austria
      </a>
      . His father Johann worked in his own truck company with Gerhard later working for his father and before being promoted to a driver.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Berger, a multiple race winner in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       European
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_Three" title="Formula Three">
       Formula Three
      </a>
      , moved up to Formula One in
      <a href="/wiki/1984_Formula_One_season" title="1984 Formula One season">
       1984
      </a>
      driving for the
      <a href="/wiki/ATS_(wheels)" title="ATS (wheels)">
       ATS
      </a>
      team.
     </p>
     <p>
      Berger was fortunate to be alive after a serious road accident shortly after entering Formula One. A week after the 1984 season ended, he was driving home in his
      <a href="/wiki/BMW_3_Series_(E30)" title="BMW 3 Series (E30)">
       BMW 323i
      </a>
      through the hills above
      <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">
       Salzburg
      </a>
      when his car was rammed from behind, resulting in it cartwheeling off a cliff. Berger was thrown clear of the BMW through the rear window. By chance, the first car on the crash scene was occupied by two surgeons who specialised in back injuries. The doctors realised the extent of his injuries (a broken neck and some damaged bones in his back) and made sure he was not moved. Following emergency surgery, and a stay in hospital in
      <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">
       Innsbruck
      </a>
      , he made a full recovery.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A full season for
      <a href="/wiki/Arrows_Grand_Prix_International" title="Arrows Grand Prix International">
       Arrows
      </a>
      followed in
      <a href="/wiki/1985_Formula_One_season" title="1985 Formula One season">
       1985
      </a>
      , though both Berger and team mate
      <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Boutsen" title="Thierry Boutsen">
       Thierry Boutsen
      </a>
      were hampered by the
      <a href="/wiki/Arrows_A8" title="Arrows A8">
       Arrows A8
      </a>
      which wasn't the best chassis on the grid and didn't allow either to exploit the immense power of the
      <a href="/wiki/BMW_M12" title="BMW M12">
       BMW M12
      </a>
      engine. Berger would finish the season in 20th place having scored 3 points in the last two races of the year in
      <a href="/wiki/1985_South_African_Grand_Prix" title="1985 South African Grand Prix">
       South Africa
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1985_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1985 Australian Grand Prix">
       Australia
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      It was not until joining
      <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">
       Benetton
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/1986_Formula_One_season" title="1986 Formula One season">
       1986
      </a>
      that his F1 career took off. Finally in a car that allowed him to exploite both his talent and the exceptional power of the BMW turbo engine (1,400bhp (1,044kW; 1,419PS) in qualifying in 1986), coupled with a clever
      <a href="/wiki/Pirelli" title="Pirelli">
       Pirelli
      </a>
      tire strategy, Berger won his first Grand Prix in
      <a href="/wiki/1986_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="1986 Mexican Grand Prix">
       Mexico
      </a>
      using the
      <a href="/wiki/Benetton_B186" title="Benetton B186">
       Benetton B186
      </a>
      . Berger was also on course for an easy victory at his home race, the
      <a href="/wiki/1986_Austrian_Grand_Prix" title="1986 Austrian Grand Prix">
       1986 Austrian Grand Prix
      </a>
      before being forced into the pits from a comfortable lead with a flat battery. Berger out-qualified team-mate
      <a href="/wiki/Teo_Fabi" title="Teo Fabi">
       Teo Fabi
      </a>
      124 in 1986 though Fabi, who as a Rookie had qualified on the pole for the
      <a href="/wiki/1983_Indianapolis_500" title="1983 Indianapolis 500">
       1983 Indianapolis 500
      </a>
      , took pole at the faster
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%96sterreichring" title="sterreichring">
       sterreichring
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Nazionale_Monza" title="Autodromo Nazionale Monza">
       Monza
      </a>
      circuits. Berger established himself as a very fast driver in Formula One that year, recording the fastest speed trap of the season, clocking an incredible 351.22km/h (218.23mph) at the
      <a href="/wiki/1986_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1986 Italian Grand Prix">
       Italian Grand Prix
      </a>
      at Monza. This remains the fastest straight line speed attained by a turbocharged car in the first turbo era.
     </p>
     <p>
      Berger was also successful in
      <a href="/wiki/Group_A#Touring_Cars" title="Group A">
       Group A
      </a>
      touring cars during this time, racing for the highly rated
      <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">
       German
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Schnitzer_Motorsport" title="Schnitzer Motorsport">
       Schnitzer
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/BMW_Motorsport" title="BMW Motorsport">
       BMW
      </a>
      team, driving the highly competitive
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_E24" title="BMW E24">
       BMW 635 Csi
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Touring_Car_Championship" title="European Touring Car Championship">
       European Touring Car Championship
      </a>
      . He won the 1985
      <a href="/wiki/Spa_24_Hours" title="Spa 24 Hours">
       Spa 24 Hours
      </a>
      partnering
      <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
       Italian
      </a>
      touring car ace
      <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Ravaglia" title="Roberto Ravaglia">
       Roberto Ravaglia
      </a>
      and fellow
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">
       Formula One
      </a>
      driver,
      <a href="/wiki/Marc_Surer" title="Marc Surer">
       Marc Surer
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ferrari">
       Ferrari
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ferrari">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For the 1987 season, Berger signed for
      <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">
       Ferrari
      </a>
      , partnering the Italian
      <a href="/wiki/Michele_Alboreto" title="Michele Alboreto">
       Michele Alboreto
      </a>
      and replacing
      <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Johansson" title="Stefan Johansson">
       Stefan Johansson
      </a>
      . After mechanical failures robbed him of the chance to perform, Berger came on strongly in the latter half of 1987, winning the final two rounds of the season. The 1987
      <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_F1/87" title="Ferrari F1/87">
       Ferrari F1/87
      </a>
      benefitted from the work of ex-McLaren designer
      <a href="/wiki/John_Barnard" title="John Barnard">
       John Barnard
      </a>
      although the car was designed by Austrian Gustav Brunner. Ferrari were back competing for race honours for the first time since mid-1985 from the
      <a href="/wiki/1987_Hungarian_Grand_Prix" title="1987 Hungarian Grand Prix">
       Hungarian Grand Prix
      </a>
      onwards. Berger was running a close second to
      <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">
       Nigel Mansell
      </a>
      at the Hungaroring before he was forced to retire. At Estoril for the
      <a href="/wiki/1987_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1987 Portuguese Grand Prix">
       Portuguese Grand Prix
      </a>
      Berger dominated the race from pole position before spinning in the latter stages to finish second after pressure from
      <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">
       Alain Prost
      </a>
      . The Austrian then looked to be heading for a second consecutive victory at the Hermanos Rodriguez circuit for the
      <a href="/wiki/1987_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="1987 Mexican Grand Prix">
       Mexican Grand Prix
      </a>
      as he once again dominated the race but mechanical problems forced him to retire when leading. Success at both Suzuka and Adelaide for the
      <a href="/wiki/1987_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1987 Japanese Grand Prix">
       Japanese
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/1987_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1987 Australian Grand Prix">
       Australian Grands Prix
      </a>
      , including pole positions in both events and the fastest lap in
      <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Street_Circuit" title="Adelaide Street Circuit">
       Adelaide
      </a>
      , ensured Ferrari and Berger went into the winter season with boosted confidence and both team and driver were tipped as favourites for the 1988 Championship. Berger out-qualified team-mate Alboreto 124 in 1987.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg/220px-Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg/330px-Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg/440px-Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_Berger_1988_Canada.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Berger driving for
        <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">
         Ferrari
        </a>
        at the
        <a href="/wiki/1988_Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="1988 Canadian Grand Prix">
         1988 Canadian Grand Prix
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/1988_Formula_One_season" title="1988 Formula One season">
       1988
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">
       McLaren
      </a>
      team, with twice World Champion Alain Prost and the sensationally fast
      <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">
       Ayrton Senna
      </a>
      dominated the season, winning 15 of the 16 rounds. The Honda V6 turbo engine in the
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/4" title="McLaren MP4/4">
       McLaren MP4/4
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus" title="Team Lotus">
       Lotus
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Lotus_100T" title="Lotus 100T">
       100T
      </a>
      had superior fuel efficiency to the Ferrari, which caused both Berger and Alboreto to often drive much slower than possible in races. Berger was the only driver to break the McLaren stranglehold in 1988, winning the
      <a href="/wiki/1988_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1988 Italian Grand Prix">
       Italian Grand Prix
      </a>
      after Senna tangled with
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Schlesser" title="Jean-Louis Schlesser">
       Jean-Louis Schlesser
      </a>
      , who had temporarily replaced Nigel Mansell (who was sick with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken_pox" title="Chicken pox">
       chicken pox
      </a>
      ) in the
      <a href="/wiki/Williams_FW12" title="Williams FW12">
       Williams
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Judd_(engine)#CV" title="Judd (engine)">
       Judd
      </a>
      . Alboreto made it a Ferrari 12, finishing only half a second behind the Austrian. This was a particularly poignant victory for the team, as it came just weeks after the death of
      <a href="/wiki/Enzo_Ferrari" title="Enzo Ferrari">
       Enzo Ferrari
      </a>
      . Berger was also the only non-McLaren to take pole in 1988 when he sat on pole for the
      <a href="/wiki/1988_British_Grand_Prix" title="1988 British Grand Prix">
       British Grand Prix
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Silverstone_Circuit" title="Silverstone Circuit">
       Silverstone
      </a>
      . It was also the only race where both Ferrari cars were on the front row, with Alboreto qualifying only 0.199 behind his team mate.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrari_F1/87/88C" title="Ferrari F1/87/88C">
       Ferrari F1/87/88C
      </a>
      proved troublesome for both Berger and team-mate Alboreto especially in terms of fuel consumption from the 1.5 litre turbo engine (Ferrari, unlike the Japanese, had not built a new engine for the season to cope with the lower fuel limit of 150 litres and the lower 2.5 Bar turbo limit, instead fronting with an updated version of their 1987 engine). At the very wet British Grand Prix for instance, Berger led the opening laps from Senna having already attained pole position, consequently building a large cushion over the rest of the field before having to slow just to finish (at the halfway mark he was in 2nd place, 50 seconds in front of 3rd placed Nigel Mansell). Ultimately he finished in 9th position, having fallen from 5th after running out of fuel coming out of the Woodcote chicane on the very last lap, with Alboreto having run out of fuel 3 laps from the finish. In the space of 200m Berger was passed by
      <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">
       Nelson Piquet
      </a>
      (Lotus),
      <a href="/wiki/Derek_Warwick" title="Derek Warwick">
       Derek Warwick
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cheever" title="Eddie Cheever">
       Eddie Cheever
      </a>
      (Arrows) and
      <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">
       Riccardo Patrese
      </a>
      (Williams). Both Ferrari drivers were often forced to use the lowest turbo boost setting they had (approximately 2.0 bar) and reduce their engine's revs in order to get the cars to the finish of a race. Until changes were made to the Ferrari's V6 engine late in the season, this meant that Berger and Alboreto were often vulnerable to attack from the leading
      <a href="/wiki/Naturally_aspirated_engine" title="Naturally aspirated engine">
       atmospheric
      </a>
      cars, with Berger estimating that he could often only use 550bhp (410kW; 558PS) of the cars available 650bhp (485kW; 659PS) during a race if he wanted to finish.
     </p>
     <p>
      Berger was leading the final race of the season at
      <a href="/wiki/1988_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1988 Australian Grand Prix">
       Adelaide
      </a>
      ahead of Prost and Senna before he clashed with former Ferrari driver
      <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Arnoux" title="Ren Arnoux">
       Ren Arnoux
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Equipe_Ligier" title="Equipe Ligier">
       Ligier
      </a>
      while trying to lap the Frenchman. Before the race Berger, apparently with the full support of the team, told the McLaren drivers who qualified 1-2 in front of him that he was going to go all out and run as fast as he could without worrying about running out of fuel to give a good show for the fans. He did just that and after passing Senna for 2nd on lap 3, he set numerous fastest laps before finally catching and passing Prost on lap 14. From there he put some 5 seconds between himself and Prost before being taken out by Arnoux on lap 28. Berger out-qualified Alboreto in all 16 events for the 1988 Formula One calendar and in the eight races where they were both classified as finishers, finished behind Alboreto only once in
      <a href="/wiki/1988_French_Grand_Prix" title="1988 French Grand Prix">
       France
      </a>
      , spelling the end for the Italian after five seasons with the Maranello outfit.
     </p>
     <p>
      For 1989, Berger was joined at Ferrari by Nigel Mansell. The
      <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_640" title="Ferrari 640">
       Ferrari 640
      </a>
      was fast but fragile, thanks in part to its revolutionary semi-automatic gearbox design. At the
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Brazilian_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Brazilian Grand Prix">
       Brazilian Grand Prix
      </a>
      , Berger tangled with Senna at the very first corner as the Brazilian found himself trapped between
      <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">
       Patrese
      </a>
      's Williams to the left and Berger's Ferrari to the right, with the three drivers dicing for the lead. Berger was lucky to escape alive from a crash during the
      <a href="/wiki/1989_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="1989 San Marino Grand Prix">
       San Marino Grand Prix
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Enzo_e_Dino_Ferrari" title="Autodromo Enzo e Dino Ferrari">
       Imola
      </a>
      's Tamburello corner, where he went straight on into the wall at almost 180mph (290km/h). The car spun multiple times along the wall before catching fire. It took 16 seconds for a rescue crew to reach the Ferrari and put the fire out. Suffering remarkably few injuries, notably burns to his hands and some broken ribs, Berger was able to make it back to the cockpit at the
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Mexican Grand Prix">
       Mexican Grand Prix
      </a>
      after missing just the
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Monaco_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Monaco Grand Prix">
       Monaco Grand Prix
      </a>
      . Berger attributed this to Ferrari's unique semi-automatic paddle shift gearbox, saying that his return would have been delayed if Ferrari still used the traditional gear stick transmission used in all other F1 cars in 1989.
     </p>
     <p>
      With the exception of the
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Australian Grand Prix">
       Australian Grand Prix
      </a>
      where both Ferrari drivers had poor qualifying sessions, Berger featured in the top four in qualifying and races in all of the remaining Grands Prix but poor reliability and accidents meant that he only finished three races all year. His first points of the year didn't come until he finished in second place in Round 12 at the
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Italian Grand Prix">
       Italian Grand Prix
      </a>
      . He won the next race in
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Portuguese Grand Prix">
       Portugal
      </a>
      which was overshadowed by the accident involving Mansell and Senna, and then finished second at the next race in
      <a href="/wiki/1989_Spanish_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Spanish Grand Prix">
       Spain
      </a>
      . These races were in fact his only points scored for the year but was still enough to give him 7th in the championship after being 3rd in 1988. Despite improving form from Ferrari, soon after Alain Prost announced his intention to leave McLaren at the end of the season, Berger decided to sign for McLaren who, with their Honda V10 engines, were the best and fastest team in Grand Prix racing.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="McLaren">
       McLaren
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: McLaren">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From 1990 to 1992 Berger joined Ayrton Senna at McLaren. His arrival was met with great anticipation as he had proven great form for Benetton and Ferrari. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_(racing)" title="McLaren (racing)">
       McLaren
      </a>
      -
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_Racing_F1" title="Honda Racing F1">
       Honda
      </a>
      seemed to offer Berger a genuine chance at the world championship for the first time. However, although generally quicker than his predecessor Alain Prost in relation to Senna in qualifying (he often qualified 2nd behind Senna), he was rarely able to match the Brazilian's pace during races. He took three wins in these three seasons, gifted the
      <a href="/wiki/1991_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1991 Japanese Grand Prix">
       1991 Japanese Grand Prix
      </a>
      by Senna; as well as victories in
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="1992 Canadian Grand Prix">
       Canada
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1992 Australian Grand Prix">
       Australia
      </a>
      in 1992. During his three years at McLaren, Berger also obtained four pole positions and out-qualified Senna 8 times.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1044" data-file-width="1562" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg/220px-Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg/330px-Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg/440px-Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_Berger_1991_USA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Berger driving for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLaren_(racing)" title="McLaren (racing)">
         McLaren
        </a>
        at the
        <a href="/wiki/1991_United_States_Grand_Prix" title="1991 United States Grand Prix">
         1991 United States Grand Prix
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      His debut at McLaren ruffled feathers, as he outqualified Senna for the
      <a href="/wiki/1990_United_States_Grand_Prix" title="1990 United States Grand Prix">
       1990 United States Grand Prix
      </a>
      obtaining pole position. Initially, Berger complained of a lack of space in the
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/5" title="McLaren MP4/5">
       McLaren MP4/5
      </a>
      B for the 1990 season, which had been designed in 1988/89 for the shorter Senna and Prost. At the
      <a href="/wiki/1990_Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="1990 Canadian Grand Prix">
       1990 Canadian Grand Prix
      </a>
      Berger finished first, around 45 seconds ahead of the eventual winner Senna, but was penalized by one minute for creeping at the start before the green lights. Berger also started from pole position at the
      <a href="/wiki/1990_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="1990 Mexican Grand Prix">
       1990 Mexican Grand Prix
      </a>
      . In 1990 Berger failed to win a race for his new team, while team mate Senna won the driver's championship for the second time.
     </p>
     <p>
      Towards the end of 1991, Berger found his form in the
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/6" title="McLaren MP4/6">
       McLaren MP4/6
      </a>
      , out-qualifying teammate Senna at the
      <a href="/wiki/1991_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1991 Portuguese Grand Prix">
       Portuguese
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/1991_Spanish_Grand_Prix" title="1991 Spanish Grand Prix">
       Spanish
      </a>
      and the title deciding
      <a href="/wiki/1991_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1991 Japanese Grand Prix">
       Japanese
      </a>
      Grands Prix (claiming pole position in the latter two events).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1992, Berger finished a single point behind Senna in the Drivers' Championship using the
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/7A" title="McLaren MP4/7A">
       McLaren MP4/7A
      </a>
      . Berger won the
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="1992 Canadian Grand Prix">
       1992 Canadian Grand Prix
      </a>
      having overtaken
      <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">
       Riccardo Patrese
      </a>
      's Williams and attacking teammate Senna before the Brazilian retired. During the
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1992 Portuguese Grand Prix">
       1992 Portuguese Grand Prix
      </a>
      , Berger was involved in an incident with the Williams of Patrese. Patrese did not realize that Berger was heading towards the pitlane touched the McLaren's rear wheel and went airborne, narrowly missing a bridge, before coming to a halt in front of the armco of the pitlane. Berger then went on to win the
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1992 Australian Grand Prix">
       Australian Grand Prix
      </a>
      for the second time after a battle with Patrese. Berger therefore won his last race for McLaren and McLaren's last race with Honda engines. A lucrative offer from Ferrari proved too tempting for Berger who announced before the
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Belgian_Grand_Prix" title="1992 Belgian Grand Prix">
       1992 Belgian Grand Prix
      </a>
      that he would be returning to the struggling Italian outfit.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Ferrari">
       Return to Ferrari
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to Ferrari">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_Berger_Ferrari_1995.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="260" data-file-width="300" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Gerhard_Berger_Ferrari_1995.jpg/220px-Gerhard_Berger_Ferrari_1995.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Gerhard_Berger_Ferrari_1995.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Gerhard_Berger_Ferrari_1995.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_Berger_Ferrari_1995.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Berger driving a
        <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">
         Ferrari
        </a>
        at
        <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="Canadian Grand Prix">
         Montreal
        </a>
        in 1995
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Frustrated by the prospect of another year playing second-fiddle to Senna, Berger was persuaded by his countryman
      <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">
       Niki Lauda
      </a>
      to return to the Italian team in 1993 as Lauda felt Berger brought valuable experience with him from his partnership with Senna.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pg18_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pg18-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The active ride suspension made the car unpredictable to drive and the
      <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_F93A" title="Ferrari F93A">
       Ferrari F93A
      </a>
      failed to record a single victory and spent the majority of the season competing for the lower points. Berger's best result was third position at the
      <a href="/wiki/1993_Hungarian_Grand_Prix" title="1993 Hungarian Grand Prix">
       1993 Hungarian Grand Prix
      </a>
      . Berger had a number of spectacular incidents during the season including a clash with
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Andretti" title="Michael Andretti">
       Michael Andretti
      </a>
      at the start of the
      <a href="/wiki/1993_Brazilian_Grand_Prix" title="1993 Brazilian Grand Prix">
       1993 Brazilian Grand Prix
      </a>
      and a dramatic impact with the armco at the
      <a href="/wiki/1993_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1993 Portuguese Grand Prix">
       1993 Portuguese Grand Prix
      </a>
      having just exited the pits. The
      <a href="/wiki/1993_Monaco_Grand_Prix" title="1993 Monaco Grand Prix">
       1993 Monaco Grand Prix
      </a>
      was perhaps where Berger displayed his fighting qualities best of all, obtaining second fastest lap before he attempted an opportunistic move on
      <a href="/wiki/Damon_Hill" title="Damon Hill">
       Damon Hill
      </a>
      's Williams at Loews hairpin while challenging for second position. The Austrian was forced to retire.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1994, Berger recovered from the deaths of close friend Senna and countryman
      <a href="/wiki/Roland_Ratzenberger" title="Roland Ratzenberger">
       Roland Ratzenberger
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/1994_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="1994 San Marino Grand Prix">
       1994 San Marino Grand Prix
      </a>
      , to score an emotional win at
      <a href="/wiki/Hockenheimring" title="Hockenheimring">
       Hockenheim
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/1994_German_Grand_Prix" title="1994 German Grand Prix">
       1994 German Grand Prix
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_412_T1" title="Ferrari 412 T1">
       Ferrari 412T
      </a>
      , the first win for Ferrari since the
      <a href="/wiki/1990_Spanish_Grand_Prix" title="1990 Spanish Grand Prix">
       1990 Spanish Grand Prix
      </a>
      . Berger also scored two pole positions in 1994, the first at Hockenheim and then at Estoril for the
      <a href="/wiki/1994_Portuguese_Grand_Prix" title="1994 Portuguese Grand Prix">
       1994 Portuguese Grand Prix
      </a>
      where he retired from while leading. Berger was also in the lead at the final round at the
      <a href="/wiki/1994_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1994 Australian Grand Prix">
       1994 Australian Grand Prix
      </a>
      when he made a mistake, letting his former team-mate Nigel Mansell pass and win for Williams.
     </p>
     <p>
      A final season with the team in 1995 saw Berger score a number of podiums and was in a race winning position at the
      <a href="/wiki/1995_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="1995 San Marino Grand Prix">
       1995 San Marino Grand Prix
      </a>
      having qualified second before stalling in the pits but the fastest lap was a mere consolation. A particularly audacious pass on Damon Hill in Canada was just one example of his strong racecraft. Berger produced another aggressive drive for third having received a 10-second stop-go penalty for a jump start and then climbing up through the field from 14th position at the
      <a href="/wiki/1995_German_Grand_Prix" title="1995 German Grand Prix">
       1995 German Grand Prix
      </a>
      , making a number of over takings on the way. At
      <a href="/wiki/Monza" title="Monza">
       Monza
      </a>
      both Ferrari drivers were in contention for the win, when an on-board camera on
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Alesi" title="Jean Alesi">
       Jean Alesi
      </a>
      's Ferrari fell into the path of Berger and smashed the Austrian's front suspension after damaging Alesi's car. Overall, Berger out-qualified team-mate Alesi 125 in 1995. The departure of both Alesi and Berger from Ferrari to Benetton also spelt the end of the number 27 and 28 era for Ferrari that stretched back to 1981 with
      <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Villeneuve" title="Gilles Villeneuve">
       Gilles Villeneuve
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Didier_Pironi" title="Didier Pironi">
       Didier Pironi
      </a>
      only interrupted by the signing of 1989 world champion
      <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">
       Alain Prost
      </a>
      for the 1990 season.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years_with_Benetton">
       Final years with Benetton
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Final years with Benetton">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      With the arrival of
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">
       Michael Schumacher
      </a>
      at Ferrari in 1996, Berger moved back to
      <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">
       Benetton
      </a>
      , although the team lost the competitiveness of the previous years. He could have remained at Maranello, but felt that the new Ferrari V10 engine would take too much time to develop. Berger spent his final two years in the sport at the team he drove for in 1986 while also sporting a revised helmet design, with the Austrian flag being rotated horizontally. 1996 proved to be a disappointing season for the Austrian, the handling characteristics of the
      <a href="/wiki/Benetton_B196" title="Benetton B196">
       Benetton B196
      </a>
      not being to his taste. Berger nearly won the German Grand Prix for the second time in his career before his Renault V10 engine blew up, handing victory to Damon Hill in the Williams. Berger's main problem for the early part of 1996 was his driving position that prevented a smooth airflow into the airbox. Consequently, Berger was significantly slower down the straights than team-mate Jean Alesi.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title='What is meant by "driving position", and how exactly did this affect airflow to the airbox? Was his seat too high? (October 2014)'>
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Benetton_B197" title="Benetton B197">
       Benetton B197
      </a>
      had a lacklustre opening to the 1997 season at
      <a href="/wiki/1997_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1997 Australian Grand Prix">
       Melbourne
      </a>
      but the team and Berger bounced back at the
      <a href="/wiki/1997_Brazilian_Grand_Prix" title="1997 Brazilian Grand Prix">
       1997 Brazilian Grand Prix
      </a>
      , with Berger finishing second having overtaken
      <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika Hkkinen">
       Mika Hkkinen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">
       Michael Schumacher
      </a>
      . Berger then scored Benetton's final Grand Prix victory at the age of 37, again at
      <a href="/wiki/Hockenheimring" title="Hockenheimring">
       Hockenheim
      </a>
      , coming back after a 3 race layoff following illness and the death of his father in an aeroplane crash.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As well as the victory, Berger also claimed pole position and fastest lap during the
      <a href="/wiki/1997_German_Grand_Prix" title="1997 German Grand Prix">
       1997 German Grand Prix
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He announced his decision to leave Benetton at the end of the season during the same weekend. He eventually retired at the end of the season, his final race seeing him finish a very close fourth in Jerez only seconds behind the victor Mika Hkkinen. In his final season as a Formula One driver, Berger and Alesi out-qualified each other 7 times each with the Austrian missing three races due to illness. Rumours of a Ferrari return for 1998 were false.
      <a href="/wiki/Sauber" title="Sauber">
       Sauber
      </a>
      offered Berger a drive for 1998, but the Austrian turned down the offer for a number of reasons, not least because of the new regulations for grooved tyres which the Austrian did not find inspiring.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Berger_and_Senna">
       Berger and Senna
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Berger and Senna">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      During the years that Berger was at
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">
       McLaren
      </a>
      (19901992), he became most famous for his humorous side. Popular accounts tell of many ingenious practical jokes thought up by the Austrian to break through his serious, focused and unyielding teammate
      <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">
       Ayrton Senna
      </a>
      . Senna accepted the challenge and as both were spurred on by team manager
      <a href="/wiki/Ron_Dennis" title="Ron Dennis">
       Ron Dennis
      </a>
      , the practical joking escalated.
     </p>
     <p>
      Accounts tell of an incident at
      <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Nazionale_Monza" title="Autodromo Nazionale Monza">
       Monza
      </a>
      where in a joint helicopter ride Senna had been showing off his new tailor-made briefcase. Having been made of carbon fibre composite, Senna argued that it was virtually indestructible. Berger opened the door of the helicopter and threw the briefcase out, to Senna's disbelief. Berger asserted innocently that he only sought to test the hypothesis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Red_Bull_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Red_Bull-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Berger later recounted further events before the
      <a href="/wiki/1990_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1990 Australian Grand Prix">
       1990 Australian Grand Prix
      </a>
      . "After dinner, we started to throw people in the swimming pool, all dressed. As I was good at defending myself, I escaped from the bath, but many people got wet. Senna ran away to avoid us from getting him, however, later, I went to his room and he awkwardly threw a glass of water at me. For a
      <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">
       Tyrolean
      </a>
      , that was nothing, but it did mean that the game was now on. With a hose, we improvised an extension to the fire extinguisher and we put it under his room door at three in the morning. We invited some people to watch and when we pushed the lever, Senna flew out of the window like a rocket. It looked like a bomb had exploded inside the room. The confusion woke many people up, who started to scream at Senna for making so much noise. He was terribly embarrassed."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chegada_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chegada-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In an Australian hotel room Berger filled Senna's bed with frogs. Senna understandably infuriated, confronted Berger, who replied, "Did you find the snake?" This incident prompted retaliation by Senna, who then proceeded to put a strong smelling French cheese in the air conditioning unit of Berger's room.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Red_Bull_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Red_Bull-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On another occasion, Senna and Brazilian compatriot
      <a href="/wiki/Maur%C3%ADcio_Gugelmin" title="Maurcio Gugelmin">
       Maurcio Gugelmin
      </a>
      decided to fill Berger's shoes with shaving foam on a fast train ride to a dinner in Japan. The Austrian was forced to attend the dinner wearing a tuxedo with running shoes. A further incident followed in which Berger replaced Senna's passport photo with what Ron Dennis described as "an equivalent-sized piece of male genitalia" (although other media accounts refer to a black pair of breasts).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Senna's fame meant he rarely had his passport checked, but on a later trip to
      <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
       Argentina
      </a>
      , Berger's prank resulted in officials holding the Brazilian for 24 hours. As a response to this gag, Senna superglued all of Berger's credit cards together.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Red_Bull_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Red_Bull-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Berger's pranks continued at his new team for 1995,
      <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">
       Scuderia Ferrari
      </a>
      . Specifically, at the
      <a href="/wiki/Fiorano_Circuit" title="Fiorano Circuit">
       Fiorano Circuit
      </a>
      a day before the launch of that season's new F1 car, Berger and his then new teammate,
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Alesi" title="Jean Alesi">
       Jean Alesi
      </a>
      , decided to take a nearby roadcar for a spin. Unbeknownst to them, that roadcar was team director
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Todt" title="Jean Todt">
       Jean Todt
      </a>
      's new specially made
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lancia_Y10" title="Lancia Y10">
       Lancia Y10
      </a>
      . Because of the view that Alesi was driving conservatively, Berger decided to apply the handbrake causing the Lancia to flip upon their return to the pits, skidding upside down centimeters away from their yet-to-be unveiled new F1 car. Alesi was sent to the hospital. Later when Todt enquired about his roadcar, Berger conceded the two had put some "slight curb marks on the roof".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The strong connection between Senna and Berger has extended beyond the Brazilian's death in 1994. Berger later acted as an advisor to
      <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Senna" title="Bruno Senna">
       Bruno Senna
      </a>
      , Ayrton's nephew, who made his Formula One debut with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HRT_F1" title="HRT F1">
       HRT F1
      </a>
      Team in the 2010 World Championship, and subsequently switched to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Williams_F1" title="Williams F1">
       Williams
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="After_retirement_from_racing">
       After retirement from racing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: After retirement from racing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A hugely popular figure in Formula One, Berger was up until 2003 regularly seen in the pitlane in his new capacity as Competitions Director at
      <a href="/wiki/BMW_in_Formula_One" title="BMW in Formula One">
       BMW
      </a>
      , overseeing their successful return to Formula One in 2000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Berger was the first to drive a F1 car on the new Shanghai Grand Prix circuit when he demonstrated a 2003
      <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_F2003-GA" title="Ferrari F2003-GA">
       Ferrari F2003-GA
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      On 25 April 2004; 10 years after
      <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">
       Ayrton Senna
      </a>
      's death, Berger drove the JPS
      <a href="/wiki/Lotus_97T" title="Lotus 97T">
       Lotus Renault 97T
      </a>
      , which Senna had used in the
      <a href="/wiki/1985_Formula_One_season" title="1985 Formula One season">
       1985 championship
      </a>
      , for 3 laps at
      <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Enzo_e_Dino_Ferrari" title="Autodromo Enzo e Dino Ferrari">
       Imola
      </a>
      before the start of the
      <a href="/wiki/2004_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="2004 San Marino Grand Prix">
       2004 San Marino Grand Prix
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 2006, he acquired 50% of
      <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Toro_Rosso" title="Scuderia Toro Rosso">
       Scuderia Toro Rosso
      </a>
      in a business deal which saw Red Bull boss
      <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Mateschitz" title="Dietrich Mateschitz">
       Dietrich Mateschitz
      </a>
      purchase half of Berger Logistik, a road haulage company founded by Berger's father Johann in 1961. His Toro Rosso team won their first Grand Prix with
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Vettel" title="Sebastian Vettel">
       Sebastian Vettel
      </a>
      taking the win. He sold his share back to Mateschitz in November 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2012 Berger was appointed President of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIA" title="FIA">
       FIA
      </a>
      Single Seat Commission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Helmet">
       Helmet
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Berger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Helmet">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Berger's helmet originally was blue with yellow lines and azure and white details near the visor. In 1986, during his first spell at Benetton he used a different design that complimented the livery of the Benetton BMW. in 1987 his helmet is dark grey-blue featuring the Austrian flag on either side. For the 1995 Portuguese Grand Prix Berger sported a helmet featuring flags of multiple nations. The top was painted to resemble the Austrian flag and "NO WAR IN THE WORLD" at the rear. The special design was a result of a winning entrant for a one-off competition. In 1996 Berger modified his original design with the Austrian flag rotated horizontally.
     </p>
