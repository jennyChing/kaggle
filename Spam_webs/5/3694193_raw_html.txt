          <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-bottom:12.0pt">
           <i>
            <span style='font-size:
8.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             This story was updated at 1:40 p.m. ET.
            </span>
           </i>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-bottom:12.0pt">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;
font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Two Russian aerospace companies are teaming
up to build what they say will be the "world's first commercial space
station" ? an orbiting outpost open to private citizens, professional
astronauts and scientists. For a price.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Called
the Commercial Space Station, the orbiting space laboratory and hotel will be
able to host up to seven people at a time. It is being planned under a
partnership between the Russian companies Orbital Technologies and RSC Energia.
[Illustration:
Russia's Commercial Space Station]
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <div class="about_adsense" id="nointelliTXT">
           <script type="text/javascript">
            tmntag.adTag('ad_sense1');
           </script>
          </div>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
companies announced plans for the
           </span>
           <a href="/8785-spaceship-fly-people-private-space-stations.html">
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             new space station
            </span>
           </a>
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            today (Sept. 29) but
did not reveal an estimated cost. The space station is expected to launch sometime
between 2015 and 2016. The cost of individual trips may vary based on launch
vehicle, duration and purpose of missions.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            "Once
launched and operational, the CSS will provide a unique destination for
commercial, state and private spaceflight exploration missions,? said Orbital
Technologies chief executive Sergey Kostenko in a statement. "The CSS will
be a valuable addition to the global base of orbital assets."
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <b>
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             Space
hotel and orbiting lab
            </span>
           </b>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Medical
experiments and Earth observations are just some of the services the space
station is expected to offer. Space tourism, Kostenko said, is another big
field.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            ?We
also have proposals for the implementation of media projects," he added.
"And, of course, some parties are interested in short-duration stays on the
station for enjoyment.?
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Moscow-based
Orbital Technologies is a company of engineers, scientists and space industry
veterans dedicated to opening space for private enterprise, according to its
website.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            RSC
Energia is the largest contractor serving Russia's Federal Space Agency and has
extensive experience building space station modules and spacecraft. The company
built Russia's Salyut space stations, the multi-module Mir Space Station and
major Russian elements of the International Space Station. RSC Energia also
builds the Soyuz and Progress spacecraft that launch routinely to the
           </span>
           <a href="/8897-graphic-international-space-station.html">
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             International Space
Station
            </span>
           </a>
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            .
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Vitaly
Lopota, president of RSC Energia, said the company has 40 years of experience
building space stations and has been outlining plans for the Commercial Space
Station for more than 10 years.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
new space station would have an initial design life of about 15 years, Orbital
Technologies officials have said. Soyuz spacecraft would ferry crews to the
station, while unmanned Progress vehicles would keep it stocked with supplies.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
station will also fly in an orbit about 62 miles (100 km) from the
International Space Station and in a similar inclination, or tilt, to make any
transfers of crew or cargo between to two stations easier, the company said.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
company's description of the station do not specify its power systems, however
a spokesperson told SPACE.com the initial module would have systems similar to
the Zvezda service module on the International Space Station. The Russian
Zvezda service module is equipped with solar arrays and thrusters for attitude
control.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <b>
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             Other
private space stations
            </span>
           </b>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText" style="text-align:justify">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;
font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The Russian announcement comes amid a major
push for commercial space vehicles and rockets in the United States, where at
least one company ? the Las Vegas-based
           </span>
           <a href="/8217-private-moon-bases-hot-idea-space-pioneer.html">
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             Bigelow Aerospace
            </span>
           </a>
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            ? is planning
commercial space stations of its own.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Bigelow
Aerospace has already put two prototype space station modules in space, called
Genesis 1 and Genesis 2. The modules are inflatable craft that launched in 2006
and 2007 and both remain in orbit.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
company is aiming to have a full-scale
           </span>
           <a href="/7804-private-space-stations-edge-closer-reality.html">
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             private space station
in orbit
            </span>
           </a>
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            and ready to receive customers by 2015, according to past reports. If so,
Bigelow Aerospace's inflatable space station modules would snag the title of
the world's first commercial space station in orbit if it launches before
Russia's Commercial Space Station.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            Another
company, Excalibur Almaz in the Isle of Man, is planning to use spacecraft
originally designed for the Soviet-era Almaz space stations to offer weeklong
orbital spaceflights for paying customers.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
U.S. push for privately built spacecraft comes as NASA plans to use commercial
space vehicles to ferry astronauts to the International Space Station once the
agency's space shuttle fleet retires in 2011.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <b>
            <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
             Russian
space agency takes notice
            </span>
           </b>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
Commercial Space Station plan has also sparked interest in Russia's Federal
Space Agency.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            "We
consider the Commercial Space Station a very interesting project, encouraging
private participation," said Vitaly Davydov, deputy chief of the Federal
Space Agency. "It will attract private investment for the Russian space
industry.?
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
Russian space agency's chief, Alexey Krasnov, added that a commercial space
station could serve as a backup for International Space Station crews.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            "For
example, if a required maintenance procedure or a real emergency were to occur,
without the return of the ISS crew to Earth, habitants could use the CSS as a
safe haven,? Krasnov said.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            As
planned, the Commercial Space Station would use a universal docking system that
could accommodate spacecraft from Russia, the United States and Europe, Orbital
Technologies officials said. The station also is expected to accommodate
China's Shenzhou space capsules, which have flown three manned missions since
2003.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            The
space station could serve as a springboard for even more ambitious voyages into
deep space, Kostenko said.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-bottom:12.0pt">
           <span style='font-size:10.0pt;
font-family:"Arial","sans-serif"'>
            "A short stopover at our station will be
the perfect beginning of a manned circumlunar flight," he added.
"Deep-space manned exploration missions planned in the next decade are
also welcome to use the CSS as a waypoint and a supply station."
           </span>
          </p>
