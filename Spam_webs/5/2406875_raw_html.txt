     <p>
      <b>
       Captive import
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">
       marketing
      </a>
      term and a
      <a href="/wiki/Strategic_management" title="Strategic management">
       strategy
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
       automobile
      </a>
      that is foreign-built and sold under the name of an importer or by a domestic automaker through its own
      <a href="/wiki/Car_dealership" title="Car dealership">
       dealer
      </a>
      distribution system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The foreign car may be produced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">
       subsidiary
      </a>
      of the same company, be a
      <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">
       joint venture
      </a>
      with another firm, or acquired under
      <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">
       license
      </a>
      from a completely separate entity. The brand name used may be that of the domestic company, the foreign builder, or an unrelated marque entirely (this is one type of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Badge_engineering" title="Badge engineering">
       badge engineering
      </a>
      ).
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captive_import&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Captive import arrangements are usually made to increase the competitiveness of the domestic brand by filling a perceived
      <a href="/wiki/Target_market" title="Target market">
       target market
      </a>
      not currently served by its model lineup, that is either not practical or not economically feasible to fill from domestic production or a mutually beneficial agreement that helps automakers without a strong distribution network or a presence in a certain country to benefit from the distribution network and stronger brand image of an established automobile manufacturer in that location. One example is the agreement between
      <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">
       Chrysler
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Motors" title="Mitsubishi Motors">
       Mitsubishi Motors
      </a>
      that started in 1971, where Chrysler imported Mitsubishi-manufactured vehicles into the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      to fill a void in their compact lineup with vehicles such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Colt" title="Dodge Colt">
       Dodge Colt
      </a>
      . Mitsubishi would only start selling vehicles under their own name in the United States in 1982.
     </p>
     <p>
      In countries or regions where a foreign manufacturer might have a fully owned subsidiary that develops and manufactures vehicles or a strong manufacturing presence, a captive import can be a vehicle from the manufacturers' indigenous country or an affiliated manufacturer worldwide.
      <a href="/wiki/Holden" title="Holden">
       Holden
      </a>
      is the Australian subsidiary of American
      <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">
       General Motors
      </a>
      , and is considered to be a domestic manufacturer in Australia. Holden has also imported the Mexican-built
      <a href="/wiki/Holden_Suburban" title="Holden Suburban">
       Holden Suburban
      </a>
      , a variant of the North American
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Suburban" title="Chevrolet Suburban">
       Chevrolet Suburban
      </a>
      , along with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holden_Jackaroo" title="Holden Jackaroo">
       Holden Jackaroo
      </a>
      built by General Motors' Japanese affiliate
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isuzu" title="Isuzu">
       Isuzu
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      . An example of an Australian captive import is the
      <a href="/wiki/Holden_Barina" title="Holden Barina">
       Holden Barina
      </a>
      that has since 1985 been the
      <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Cultus" title="Suzuki Cultus">
       Suzuki Cultus
      </a>
      , two generations of the European
      <a href="/wiki/Opel_Corsa" title="Opel Corsa">
       Opel Corsa
      </a>
      , and is currently the Korean
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daewoo_Kalos" title="Daewoo Kalos">
       Daewoo Kalos
      </a>
      (marketed internationally as the
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Aveo" title="Chevrolet Aveo">
       Chevrolet Aveo
      </a>
      .) Because Holden in the past was a local car designer and manufacturer and still produces one Australian-designed and built model (the
      <a href="/wiki/Holden_Commodore" title="Holden Commodore">
       Holden Commodore
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      For countries that do not have native manufacturers or a development/manufacturing presence, a captive import is a vehicle not manufactured by the specific company that imported the vehicle but sold under its brand. Usually, the manufacturer of the vehicle might be an affiliate of the importer. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Forester" title="Chevrolet Forester">
       Chevrolet Forester
      </a>
      was sold in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">
       General Motors
      </a>
      , where its manufacturer,
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      -based
      <a href="/wiki/Subaru" title="Subaru">
       Subaru
      </a>
      does not have a sales presence and the importation agreement started during the period when the manufacturer of
      <a href="/wiki/Subaru" title="Subaru">
       Subaru
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fuji_Heavy_Industries" title="Fuji Heavy Industries">
       Fuji Heavy Industries
      </a>
      , was affiliated with
      <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">
       General Motors
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A vehicle manufactured in a country where the manufacturers' indigenous country has a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-trade_agreement" title="Free-trade agreement">
       free-trade agreement
      </a>
      with other countries in the same region such as the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAFTA" title="NAFTA">
       NAFTA
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASEAN" title="ASEAN">
       ASEAN
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      and manufactured in a plant fully or partially owned by that company should not be considered a captive import. The
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_Crown_Victoria" title="Ford Crown Victoria">
       Ford Crown Victoria
      </a>
      , which was known for livery use in the United States, particularly for taxi and police duty, was exclusively built in
      <a href="/wiki/Southwold,_Ontario" title="Southwold, Ontario">
       Southwold, Ontario
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Assembly" title="St. Thomas Assembly">
       St. Thomas Assembly
      </a>
      from 1992 to 2011. It is not considered a captive import in the U.S. because of the free-trade under the
      <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_Automotive_Products_Agreement" title="CanadaUnited States Automotive Products Agreement">
       CanadaUnited States Automotive Products Agreement
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="American_market">
       American market
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captive_import&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: American market">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the American market, captive imports "blurred national distinctions" because they have been designed and built elsewhere, but wear a domestic nameplate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shifting_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shifting-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The chief reason domestic automakers market captive imports is because "it is cheaper to import those cars than to produce them" in the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shifting_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-shifting-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Nash-Healey" title="Nash-Healey">
       Nash-Healey
      </a>
      two-seat
      <a href="/wiki/Sports_car" title="Sports car">
       sports car
      </a>
      was produced for the U.S. market between 1951 and 1954.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It combined a Nash Ambassador
      <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">
       drivetrain
      </a>
      with a European chassis and body and was a product of a partnership between
      <a href="/wiki/Nash-Kelvinator" title="Nash-Kelvinator">
       Nash-Kelvinator Corporation
      </a>
      and British automaker
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Healey" title="Donald Healey">
       Donald Healey
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After the first model year, the Nash-Healey was restyled and assembled by
      <a href="/wiki/Battista_Farina" title="Battista Farina">
       Pinin Farina
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
       Italy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Nash_Metropolitan" title="Nash Metropolitan">
       Nash Metropolitan
      </a>
      , sold in the U.S. from 1954 to 1962, a captive import for
      <a href="/wiki/Nash_Motors" title="Nash Motors">
       Nash Motors
      </a>
      (who designed it themselves, unlike most captive imports built by another company) produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Austin_Motor_Company" title="Austin Motor Company">
       Austin
      </a>
      in the UK specifically for sale in the U.S. By entering into a manufacturing arrangement, Nash would avoid the expense associated with tooling, body panels, and components.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When this two-seater sub-compact car was launched, it was the first time an American-designed car had been only built in Europe, having never been built in the United States. Unlike typical European cars of the era, its look was "American" and it had a design resemblance to the large or "senior" U.S.-built Nashes. It became one of the few small cars to sell well during the most bulk-obsessed period of U.S. automotive history.
     </p>
     <p>
      When
      <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">
       Mercedes-Benz
      </a>
      was seeking entry into the American market in the 1950s, the company signed a marketing agreement with
      <a href="/wiki/Studebaker" title="Studebaker">
       Studebaker
      </a>
      
      <a href="/wiki/Packard" title="Packard">
       Packard
      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_markets">
       Other markets
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captive_import&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other markets">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In Europe, there have been relatively few cases of captive imports, and most have been unsuccessful. The
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Venture" title="Chevrolet Venture">
       Chevrolet Venture
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Minivan" title="Minivan">
       minivan
      </a>
      was sold as the
      <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">
       Opel
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Motors" title="Vauxhall Motors">
       Vauxhall
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Motors_Sintra" title="General Motors Sintra">
       Sintra
      </a>
      in the late-1990s, but was not only not to European tastes, but also gained a bad reputation due to poor results in safety tests. The practice has been revived by
      <a href="/wiki/PSA_Peugeot_Citro%C3%ABn" title="PSA Peugeot Citron">
       PSA Peugeot Citron
      </a>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_4007" title="Peugeot 4007">
       Peugeot 4007
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_4008" title="Peugeot 4008">
       Peugeot 4008
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C-Crosser" title="Citron C-Crosser">
       Citron C-Crosser
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Citro%C3%ABn_C4_AirCross&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Citron C4 AirCross (page does not exist)">
       Citron C4 AirCross
      </a>
      , which are rebadged versions of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Outlander" title="Mitsubishi Outlander">
       Mitsubishi Outlander
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_ASX" title="Mitsubishi ASX">
       Mitsubishi ASX
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Brazil, the Australian-built
      <a href="/wiki/Holden_Commodore" title="Holden Commodore">
       Holden Commodore
      </a>
      has been sold since 1998 as Chevrolet Omega, replacing the
      <a href="/wiki/Opel_Omega#Omega" title="Opel Omega">
       locally built car
      </a>
      bearing the same name. Despite being well received by the press and public, sales are much worse than its locally built counterpart, simply because of its high price. However, it is used very often as official government cars. Chevrolet also rebranded the Argentine-built
      <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Escudo" title="Suzuki Escudo">
       Suzuki Vitara
      </a>
      as the Chevrolet Tracker after Suzuki stopped selling cars in Brazil, but it never achieved the same selling numbers from the original car.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Japan, where foreign car manufacturers have traditionally struggled to compete in the local market, even rebadging of U.S. models like the
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Cavalier" title="Chevrolet Cavalier">
       Chevrolet Cavalier
      </a>
      as a
      <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">
       Toyota
      </a>
      have failed to improve sales.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Australia, GM's
      <a href="/wiki/Holden" title="Holden">
       Holden
      </a>
      operation sold the 1975-84 Isuzu Bellett/Gemini, itself a license built version of the then current Opel Kadett, as the
      <a href="/wiki/Holden_Gemini" title="Holden Gemini">
       Holden Gemini
      </a>
      . The name was originally Holden-Isuzu Gemini, but after the initial TX series the Isuzu cobranding was dropped. Perhaps the original idea was to foster the Japanese-ness of the model at a time when that might have been seen by customers as a positive, the Nissan's 610 Bluebird being marketed as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datsun_180B_SSS" title="Datsun 180B SSS">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_LUV" title="Chevrolet LUV">
       Chevrolet LUV
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Suburban" title="Chevrolet Suburban">
       Chevrolet Suburban
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Holden_Suburban" title="Holden Suburban">
       Holden Suburban
      </a>
      with intentions to launch the full-sized SUV in a region that was used to having small to mid-sized SUVs, and the model was discontinued in 2001.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_failure">
       Reasons for failure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captive_import&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reasons for failure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Various reasons have been suggested as to why captive imports often fail. The question of exchange rates is clearly important, as a sudden shift can quickly raise prices to uncompetitive levels. Some models have been justly criticized for marginal quality, or being a bad match to the local driving environment. The commitment of domestic sales and service staffs to an unfamiliar vehicle has also often been questioned, particularly if the import is seen as reducing sales of other, more profitable vehicles in the lineup.
     </p>
     <p>
      Others fail due to no fault of their own; the
      <a href="/wiki/Sunbeam_Tiger" title="Sunbeam Tiger">
       Sunbeam Tiger
      </a>
      , for instance, an early 1960s example of the concept of an American
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_Windsor_engine" title="Ford Windsor engine">
       Ford Windsor engine
      </a>
      in a British (
      <a href="/wiki/Sunbeam_Alpine" title="Sunbeam Alpine">
       Sunbeam Alpine
      </a>
      ) body and chassis, enjoyed substantial success until Sunbeam became a captive import of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_Corporation" title="Chrysler Corporation">
       Chrysler Corporation
      </a>
      in North America. Chrysler could not be realistically expected to sell a car with a Ford engine, and Chrysler V8 engines all had the
      <a href="/wiki/Distributor" title="Distributor">
       distributor
      </a>
      positioned at the rear of the engine, unlike the front-mounted distributor of the Ford V8, making it impossible to fit the Chrysler engine into the Sunbeam engine bay without major and expensive revisions. Thus this niche of the automotive market was left to be filled with legendary success by the Ford engined
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelby_Cobra" title="Shelby Cobra">
       Shelby Cobra
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      There may be a deeper, structural issue at work, however. It could simply be that a domestic buyer is unlikely to want an import, and an import buyer is unlikely to enter a domestic showroom. A captive thus easily falls between two stools. This is probably why the practice of using a separate brand name, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Merkur" title="Merkur">
       Merkur
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">
       General Motors
      </a>
      ' short-lived
      <a href="/wiki/Geo_(automobile)" title="Geo (automobile)">
       Geo
      </a>
      , has ceased  the foreignness of the car is thus discreetly made less apparent.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions">
       Exceptions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captive_import&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exceptions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Not every vehicle that appears to be a captive import really is. A vehicle which is foreign-designed or badged but assembled in the market where it is sold does not fall into this category. Such vehicles are frequently the result of
      <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">
       joint venture
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Strategic_alliance" title="Strategic alliance">
       strategic alliance
      </a>
      arrangements between automakers.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/Renault_Alliance" title="Renault Alliance">
       Renault Alliance
      </a>
      , which was sold through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Motors_Corporation" title="American Motors Corporation">
       American Motors
      </a>
      (AMC) dealers in the 1980s, was actually assembled by AMC as part of the brief tie-up between the two companies. The 19851988
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Nova" title="Chevrolet Nova">
       Chevrolet Nova
      </a>
      and the later
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geo/Chevrolet_Prizm" title="Geo/Chevrolet Prizm">
       Geo Prizm
      </a>
      , though it was a
      <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">
       Toyota
      </a>
      design and shared the Chevrolet showroom with many captives, was built domestically by the GM/Toyota
      <a href="/wiki/NUMMI" title="NUMMI">
       NUMMI
      </a>
      joint venture. The
      <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Talon" title="Eagle Talon">
       Eagle Talon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Laser" title="Plymouth Laser">
       Plymouth Laser
      </a>
      , both sisters to the
      <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Eclipse" title="Mitsubishi Eclipse">
       Mitsubishi Eclipse
      </a>
      , were manufactured in the U.S. by
      <a href="/wiki/Diamond-Star_Motors" title="Diamond-Star Motors">
       Diamond-Star Motors
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">
       Chrysler
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Motors" title="Mitsubishi Motors">
       Mitsubishi Motors
      </a>
      joint venture. Australia's
      <a href="/wiki/Holden" title="Holden">
       Holden
      </a>
      , although it often shares planning and hardware with the rest of GM's global empire such as Opel and Isuzu, has generally preferred to assemble its versions of such vehicles locally.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rover_(car)" title="Rover (car)">
       Rover
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">
       Honda
      </a>
      have co-produced models for the European market, as have
      <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo" title="Alfa Romeo">
       Alfa Romeo
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">
       Nissan
      </a>
      . None of these would be considered imports.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States, a vehicle that is assembled in Canada or Mexico and is distributed domestically by a Big Three automaker is not considered a captive import. This is due to the integration of manufacturing operations by the Big 3 in these countries due to the hospitable trade environment created by the
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">
       North American Free Trade Agreement
      </a>
      (and before NAFTA, the US-Canada
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive_Products_Trade_Agreement" title="Automotive Products Trade Agreement">
       Auto Pact
      </a>
      ), coupled with the proximity of these nations to the U.S. Also, vehicles made and marketed by European automakers that were eventually acquired by the Big Three automakers, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">
       Land Rover
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Volvo_Cars" title="Volvo Cars">
       Volvo
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Saab_Automobile" title="Saab Automobile">
       Saab
      </a>
      , are generally not considered to be captive imports. The Opel vehicles sold in the 1960s and 1970s are exceptions to this rule because they were sold through the Buick distribution channel, while retaining the Opel brand name. Thus, they are captive imports.
     </p>
