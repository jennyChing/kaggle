           <p>
            (Bloomberg) -- Hundreds of thousands of Muslims vented
their anger in unison, shouting Allahu Akbar! as their
leader condemned supporters of the satirical French magazine
Charlie Hebdo after militants murdered five of its cartoonists.
           </p>
           <p>
            The protest against caricatures of Muhammad and the
policies of the U.S. and its allies was organized by the state
and televised live across the country for more than an hour, but
it wasnt in Iran or Pakistan. It was in Russia, where Vladimir
Putin came to power vowing to wipe out Muslim extremists,
even in the outhouse.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fifteen years on, Putin is now seeking to turn Muslim anger
to his advantage by pushing for a united front against what he
sees as a U.S.-led conspiracy to dominate the world. Putin is
also trying to neutralize the threat posed by the return of
Russian jihadis currently fighting for the Islamic State in
Syria and Iraq, a task complicated by his growing isolation over
the war in Ukraine, officials and analysts in Moscow say.
           </p>

           <p>
            The protest was an attempt to meld Muslim opinions with
Russian-wide views about the Western world, a lever to unite the
population around Putin, said Alexei Malashenko, a Middle East
analyst at the Moscow Carnegie Center.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Western Agents
           </h2>
           <p>
            The rally last month was held in Grozny, the capital of
Chechnya, where tens of thousands of people died in Russias two
wars against separatists. Ramzan Kadyrov, the Putin loyalist who
runs the region, told the crowd Western agents probably
organized the Charlie Hebdo killings to trigger a new wave
of Islamic State recruits for their war on Islam. Kadyrov, 38,
has said the U.S. secretly controls the Islamic State.
           </p>
           <p>
            Chechnya is the only one of the seven regions in the North
Caucasus Federal District where the death toll from battles with
extremists rose last year, to 52, according to the annual review
of violence in the area compiled by the Caucasian Knot, a
Moscow-based analysis and news group. Almost 10 million people,
mainly Muslims, live in the district, which borders Azerbaijan
and Georgia and is closer to Syria than Moscow.
           </p>
           <p>
            An estimated 3,069 people were killed in what Russia calls
its fight against terrorists in the North Caucasus in the last
five years and another 2,747 were wounded, according to a study
published today by the Caucasian Knot.
           </p>
           <p>
            Radicals from Chechnya have claimed credit for some of the
most brutal acts of terrorism in Russia. They include the 2002
hostage-taking at the Nord-Ost theater in Moscow, which led to
170 deaths, the 2004 seizure of a school in Beslan that resulted
in more than 380 fatalities and the dual suicide bombings in
Moscow subway stations in 2010 that killed at least 40.
           </p>
           <h2>
            1,500 Fighters
           </h2>
           <p>
            Putin is facing the threat of resurgent radical Islam on
Russias southern flank at a time when his security forces are
focused on Ukraine and the economy is reeling from sanctions and
plunging oil revenue. The ruble has lost almost half its value
against the dollar in the last year, adding to the strain.
           </p>
           <p>
            The issue became more urgent after militants staged the
deadliest attack in Chechnya in four years in December, when
they stormed a police outpost and seized buildings in Grozny,
culminating in an hours-long shootout that left more than 20
dead. The operation coincided with Putins annual address to the
nations political elite inside the Kremlin.
           </p>
           <p>
            These rebels have showed up in Chechnya again, Putin
said in his speech that day. Im sure local law-enforcement
authorities will take proper care of them.
           </p>
           <p>
            Russian officials are bracing for an upsurge in violence
across the North Caucasus now that the Islamic State has
declared Russia an enemy for arming Syrian President Bashar al-Assad, whos been locked in a civil war for four years. About
1,500 Russians, most from the North Caucasus, have fought for
the Sunni extremist group and many of them are starting to
return, according to the governments Anti-Terror Committee.
           </p>
           <h2>
            New Target
           </h2>
           <p>
            Russia is the Islamic States new target, Caucasian
Knot chief Grigory Shvedov said. The process has begun.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Islamic State, previously known as ISIS or ISIL,
declared itself a global caliphate with authority over all
Muslims last June, after killing thousands of people en route to
conquering vast swathes of Iraq and Syria. The U.S. and its
allies have been bombing its forces since August and President
Barack Obama is seeking new authority to widen the campaign.
           </p>
           <p>
            The group, which has tens of thousands of fighters in Syria
and Iraq, is establishing militant affiliates in countries such
as Algeria, Egypt and Libya, according to U.S. officials. At
least three emirs in the North Caucasus have pledged
allegiance to the movement in the past few months.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Warped Psyches
           </h2>
           <p>
            Its the most economically powerful and self-sufficient
terrorist entity weve ever seen, Ilya Rogachev, who runs the
Russian Foreign Ministrys office of new challenges and threats,
said in an interview. And now theyre bearing heavy losses, so
the outflow of militants is increasing. These people are coming
back with warped psyches, ready to solve problems through
violence.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Islamic States media arm last month released a video
in which a boy appears to execute two men accused of spying for
Russias Federal Security Service, or FSB. One said in an
alleged confession that he was gathering data about fighters
from Russia, while the other said he was sent to locate the
residence of the groups leader.
           </p>
           <p>
            In November, Kadyrov said the Islamic State commander known
as Omar the Chechen had been killed in Syria after threatening
to strike Russia. He didnt elaborate. The FSB didnt respond
immediately to an e-mailed request for comment on the threat
posed by the group.
           </p>
           <p>
            Leonid Reshetnikov, who ran the information and analysis
directorate at Russias Foreign Intelligence Service, or SVR,
from 2005 to 2009, said the video looks fake because theres no
blood and the confessions arent convincing.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Combat Component
           </h2>
           <p>
            Still, the Islamic States message is real: We will be as
merciless with your agents as we are with the Westerners,
Reshetnikov said in an interview.
           </p>
           <p>
            They will also try to activate the combat component in
the North Caucasus in the coming months, according to Evgeny
Ilyin, first deputy head of staff at the Anti-Terror Committee.
           </p>
           <p>
            The guys who return arent yesterdays schoolboys, but
hardened fighters who know how to use all kinds of weapons
necessary for terrorist activities, Ilyin said in an interview
in the Russian capital.
           </p>
           <p>
            President Putin visited Egypt, the most populous Arab
nation, for the first time in a decade on Feb. 9 to discuss the
Islamic State and other issues with his counterpart, Abdel
Fattah al-Sisi. While the group poses an unprecedented
threat, the U.S. and its allies should bear responsibility for
creating the conditions for its success, Putin told Egypts Al-Ahram newspaper.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Serious Signal
           </h2>
           <p>
            Even so, Rogachev of the Foreign Ministry said its
imperative that Russia, the U.S. and the European Union rebuild
ties and work together to neutralize the Islamic State. Russia
and the U.S. used to share intelligence on terrorist threats,
most recently during preparations for the Sochi Olympics, but
those ties have been severed over the war in Ukraine. Now,
Russia isnt always invited to the international meetings the
U.S. convenes to discuss the issue, according to Rogachev.
           </p>
           <p>
            Without such cooperation, attacks like the one on Charlie
Hebdo will only increase in severity and frequency, according to
Anatoly Kulikov, a former interior minister who commanded
Russian troops during the first war in Chechnya in the 1990s.
Kulikov now heads the Club of Russian Commanders, an advisory
body uniting more than 1,000 retired senior military officers.
           </p>
           <p>
            That was a very serious signal that we should stop
quarreling and join efforts, Kulikov said in an interview.
           </p>

Almost 10 million people,\nmainly Muslims, live in the district, which borders Azerbaijan\nand Georgia and is closer to Syria than Moscow. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;An estimated 3,069 people were killed in what Russia calls\nits fight against terrorists in the North Caucasus in the last\nfive years and another 2,747 were wounded, according to a study\npublished today by the Caucasian Knot. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Radicals from Chechnya have claimed credit for some of the\nmost brutal acts of terrorism in Russia. They include the 2002\nhostage-taking at the Nord-Ost theater in Moscow, which led to\n170 deaths, the 2004 seizure of a school in Beslan that resulted\nin more than 380 fatalities and the dual suicide bombings in\nMoscow subway stations in 2010 that killed at least 40. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;1,500 Fighters &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Putin is facing the threat of resurgent radical Islam on\nRussia&#x2019;s southern flank at a time when his security forces are\nfocused on Ukraine and the economy is reeling from sanctions and\nplunging oil revenue. The ruble has lost almost half its value\nagainst the dollar in the last year, adding to the strain. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The issue became more urgent after militants staged the\ndeadliest attack in Chechnya in four years in December, when\nthey stormed a police outpost and seized buildings in Grozny,\nculminating in an hours-long shootout that left more than 20\ndead. The operation coincided with Putin&#x2019;s annual address to the\nnation&#x2019;s political elite inside the Kremlin. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;These rebels have showed up in Chechnya again,&#x201D; Putin\nsaid in his speech that day. &#x201C;I&#x2019;m sure local law-enforcement\nauthorities will take proper care of them.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Russian officials are bracing for an upsurge in violence\nacross the North Caucasus now that the Islamic State has\ndeclared Russia an enemy for arming Syrian President Bashar al-Assad, who&#x2019;s been locked in a civil war for four years. About\n1,500 Russians, most from the North Caucasus, have fought for\nthe Sunni extremist group and many of them are starting to\nreturn, according to the government&#x2019;s Anti-Terror Committee. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;&#x2018;New Target&#x2019; &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;Russia is the Islamic State&#x2019;s new target,&#x201D; Caucasian\nKnot chief Grigory Shvedov said. &#x201C;The process has begun.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The Islamic State, previously known as ISIS or ISIL,\ndeclared itself a global caliphate with authority over all\nMuslims last June, after killing thousands of people en route to\nconquering vast swathes of Iraq and Syria. The U.S. and its\nallies have been bombing its forces since August and President\nBarack Obama is seeking new authority to widen the campaign. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The group, which has tens of thousands of fighters in Syria\nand Iraq, is establishing militant affiliates in countries such\nas Algeria, Egypt and Libya, according to U.S. officials. At\nleast three &#x201C;emirs&#x201D; in the North Caucasus have pledged\nallegiance to the movement in the past few months. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;&#x2018;Warped Psyches&#x2019; &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;It&#x2019;s the most economically powerful and self-sufficient\nterrorist entity we&#x2019;ve ever seen,&#x201D; Ilya Rogachev, who runs the\nRussian Foreign Ministry&#x2019;s office of new challenges and threats,\nsaid in an interview. &#x201C;And now they&#x2019;re bearing heavy losses, so\nthe outflow of militants is increasing. These people are coming\nback with warped psyches, ready to solve problems through\nviolence.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The Islamic State&#x2019;s media arm last month released a video\nin which a boy appears to execute two men accused of spying for\nRussia&#x2019;s Federal Security Service, or FSB. One said in an\nalleged confession that he was gathering data about fighters\nfrom Russia, while the other said he was sent to locate the\nresidence of the group&#x2019;s leader. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;In November, Kadyrov said the Islamic State commander known\nas Omar the Chechen had been killed in Syria after threatening\nto strike Russia. He didn&#x2019;t elaborate. The FSB didn&#x2019;t respond\nimmediately to an e-mailed request for comment on the threat\nposed by the group. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Leonid Reshetnikov, who ran the information and analysis\ndirectorate at Russia&#x2019;s Foreign Intelligence Service, or SVR,\nfrom 2005 to 2009, said the video looks fake because there&#x2019;s no\nblood and the confessions aren&#x2019;t convincing. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;&#x2018;Combat Component&#x2019; &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Still, the Islamic State&#x2019;s message is real: &#x201C;We will be as\nmerciless with your agents as we are with the Westerners,&#x201D;\nReshetnikov said in an interview. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;They will also try to &#x201C;activate the combat component&#x201D; in\nthe North Caucasus in the coming months, according to Evgeny\nIlyin, first deputy head of staff at the Anti-Terror Committee. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;The guys who return aren&#x2019;t yesterday&#x2019;s schoolboys, but\nhardened fighters who know how to use all kinds of weapons\nnecessary for terrorist activities,&#x201D; Ilyin said in an interview\nin the Russian capital. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;President Putin visited Egypt, the most populous Arab\nnation, for the first time in a decade on Feb. 9 to discuss the\nIslamic State and other issues with his counterpart, Abdel\nFattah al-Sisi. While the group poses an &#x201C;unprecedented&#x201D;\nthreat, the U.S. and its allies should bear responsibility for\ncreating the conditions for its success, Putin told Egypt&#x2019;s Al-Ahram newspaper. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;&#x2018;Serious Signal&#x2019; &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Even so, Rogachev of the Foreign Ministry said it&#x2019;s\nimperative that Russia, the U.S. and the European Union rebuild\nties and work together to neutralize the Islamic State. Russia\nand the U.S. used to share intelligence on terrorist threats,\nmost recently during preparations for the Sochi Olympics, but\nthose ties have been severed over the war in Ukraine. Now,\nRussia isn&#x2019;t always invited to the international meetings the\nU.S. convenes to discuss the issue, according to Rogachev. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Without such cooperation, attacks like the one on Charlie\nHebdo will only increase in severity and frequency, according to\nAnatoly Kulikov, a former interior minister who commanded\nRussian troops during the first war in Chechnya in the 1990s.\nKulikov now heads the Club of Russian Commanders, an advisory\nbody uniting more than 1,000 retired senior military officers. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;That was a very serious signal that we should stop\nquarreling and join efforts,&#x201D;
