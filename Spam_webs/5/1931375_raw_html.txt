                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     Miley Cyrus' VMA Performance Overshadows Her Environmental Activism
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-5207133561327389779" itemprop="articleBody">
                     Anyone who knows me will tell you that I usually don't do this, but I couldn't resist this time around. Yes, let's talk about celebrity gossip. Specifically, Miley Cyrus' "bizarre" performance this weekend at the VMAs has sparked a national conversation about former child stars and sexuality. Never mind that she's hardly the first 20 year old to display overt sexuality on-stage (admittedly in a VERY public forum) or that adults in the entertainment industry can manage their brand in the manner they see fit (for her, seems to be working). Personally, I find it a little bizarre, over-the-top and slightly distasteful. Do I really care. No.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a name="more">
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="border: 0; clear: right; float: left; height: auto; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 2em; width: auto;">
                      <script async="" src="http://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
                      </script>
                      <!-- In-Text 8/16 -->
                      <br/>
                      <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-0522499691570218" data-ad-slot="2698796069" style="display: inline-block; height: 250px; width: 300px;">
                      </ins>
                      <script>
                       (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
                      </script>
                     </div>
                     What I really want to write about is how celebrities, Miley Cyrus in this case, overshadow their activist or philanthropic messages with cheap tricks such as the one we all saw. You may not have been aware, but Miley Cyrus and some of her Disney and teen-pop pals are environmental activists. Their most famous contribution to the debate was a "We Are The World" type composition called "Send it On", released in 2009 and instantly one of the most popular iTunes downloads of its time. Take a look:
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/H7sTg9bhk2g" width="420">
                     </iframe>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The song was released as part of Disney's "
                     <a href="http://corporate.disney.go.com/responsibility/environment.html" target="_blank">
                      Project Green
                     </a>
                     ," one of the slew of corporate sponsored "green" initiatives to pop up in the past decade or so. Now we can debate the intention or efficacy of such initiatives, but in our mind, anything that contributes to an adult (no pun intended) discussion of humans' relationship with their environment is a good thing. We can also debate whether this song is actually about the environment. Disney swears it is, but one journalist argues that most will have trouble "
                     <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/environment/ethicallivingblog/2009/aug/14/disney-green-project" target="_blank">
                      connecting the dots
                     </a>
                     " between the lyrics, their message of community involvement and a more grassroots mission to work for the environment. Whether it's magnanimous or cynical, I don't really care. The message wasn't really intended for me, either; it was intended to reach the millions of boy/girl band fans under the age of 18. I think it's a good thing.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     But back to the point. Does "twerking," "grinding" and prancing about in a skin-colored two-piece detract from any social good that one might want to demonstrate? How exactly does a public, tabloid image relate to a social message one might want to spread? Perhaps the nearest analogy is George Clooney. His very public stance in favor of social justice in the developing world, one that culminated with his hands-on involvement in the Darfur crisis, took a hit with his affiliation with the Coffee Company "Nespresso." He was ambushed and fumbled his response at a press conference in which he was asked about said affiliation, the point being that Nespresso was related to questionable labor and farming practices in coffee producing countries (something that they have only recently tackled in the public forum, pledging to source an increasing quantity of their product from fair-trade sources). Since that moment, Clooney's philanthropic work has been on a much less public scale, partly for his own sanity I'm sure, but also to shift the focus of his efforts away from his public persona and onto the real work to be done.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     It remains to be seen whether Miley's VMA fun-time performance will affect her philanthropic work. It's already shifted the way that most unwitting spectators look at her.
