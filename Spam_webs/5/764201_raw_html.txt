   <p align="justify">
    The main ideas which underpin the calculus developed over a very long period of time indeed. The first steps were taken by Greek mathematicians.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    To the Greeks numbers were ratios of integers so the number line had "holes" in it. They got round this difficulty by using lengths, areas and volumes in addition to numbers for, to the Greeks, not all lengths were numbers.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="5">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Zeno_of_Elea.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Zeno_of_Elea',550,800); return false;">
     Zeno of Elea
    </a>
    , about 450 BC, gave a number of problems which were based on the infinite. For example he argued that motion is impossible:-
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     <i>
      If a body moves from A to B then before it reaches B it passes through the mid-point, say B
     </i>
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
     <i>
      of AB. Now to move to B
     </i>
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
     <i>
      it must first reach the mid-point B
     </i>
     <sub>
      2
     </sub>
     <i>
      of AB
     </i>
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
     <i>
      . Continue this argument to see that A must move through an infinite number of distances and so cannot move.
     </i>
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="7">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leucippus.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leucippus',550,800); return false;">
     Leucippus
    </a>
    ,
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Democritus.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Democritus',550,800); return false;">
     Democritus
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Antiphon.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Antiphon',550,800); return false;">
     Antiphon
    </a>
    all made contributions to the Greek method of exhaustion which was put on a scientific basis by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Eudoxus.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Eudoxus',550,800); return false;">
     Eudoxus
    </a>
    about 370 BC. The method of exhaustion is so called because one thinks of the areas measured expanding so that they account for more and more of the required area.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="10">
    </a>
    However
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Archimedes.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Archimedes',550,800); return false;">
     Archimedes
    </a>
    , around 225 BC, made one of the most significant of the Greek contributions. His first important advance was to show that the area of a segment of a parabola is
    <sup>
     4
    </sup>
    /
    <sub>
     3
    </sub>
    the area of a triangle with the same base and vertex and
    <sup>
     2
    </sup>
    /
    <sub>
     3
    </sub>
    of the area of the circumscribed parallelogram.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Archimedes.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Archimedes',550,800); return false;">
     Archimedes
    </a>
    constructed an infinite sequence of triangles starting with one of area
    <i>
     A
    </i>
    and continually adding further triangles between the existing ones and the parabola to get areas
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     ,
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     +
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     /
     <sub>
      4
     </sub>
     ,
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     +
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     /
     <sub>
      4
     </sub>
     +
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     /
     <sub>
      16
     </sub>
     ,
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     +
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     /
     <sub>
      4
     </sub>
     +
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     /
     <sub>
      16
     </sub>
     +
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     /
     <sub>
      64
     </sub>
     , ...
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    The area of the segment of the parabola is therefore
    <img align="right" src="../Diagrams/Archimedes'_diagram.jpeg"/>
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     (1 +
     <sup>
      1
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      4
     </sub>
     +
     <sup>
      1
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      4
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
     </sub>
     +
     <sup>
      1
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      4
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
     </sub>
     + ....) = (
     <sup>
      4
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      3
     </sub>
     )
     <i>
      A
     </i>
     .
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    This is the first known example of the summation of an infinite series.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s22">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Archimedes.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Archimedes',550,800); return false;">
     Archimedes
    </a>
    used the method of exhaustion to find an approximation to the area of a circle. This, of course, is an early example of integration which led to approximate values of .
    <br/>
   </p>
   <p align="right">
    <br/>
    Here is
    <b>
     Archimedes' diagram
    </b>
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <br/>
    <a name="s29">
    </a>
    Among other 'integrations' by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Archimedes.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Archimedes',550,800); return false;">
     Archimedes
    </a>
    were the volume and surface area of a sphere, the volume and area of a cone, the surface area of an ellipse, the volume of any segment of a paraboloid of revolution and a segment of an hyperboloid of revolution.
    <br/>
    <br clear="right"/>
    <br/>
    <a name="26">
    </a>
    No further progress was made until the 16
    <sup>
     th
    </sup>
    Century when mechanics began to drive mathematicians to examine problems such as centres of gravity.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Valerio.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Valerio',550,800); return false;">
     Luca Valerio
    </a>
    (1552-1618) published
    <i>
     De quadratura parabolae
    </i>
    in Rome (1606) which continued the Greek methods of attacking these type of area problems.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Kepler.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Kepler',550,800); return false;">
     Kepler
    </a>
    , in his work on planetary motion, had to find the area of sectors of an ellipse. His method consisted of thinking of areas as sums of lines, another crude form of integration, but
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Kepler.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Kepler',550,800); return false;">
     Kepler
    </a>
    had little time for Greek rigour and was rather lucky to obtain the correct answer after making two cancelling errors in this work.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="28">
    </a>
    Three mathematicians, born within three years of each other, were the next to make major contributions. They were
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Fermat.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Fermat',550,800); return false;">
     Fermat
    </a>
    ,
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Roberval.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Roberval',550,800); return false;">
     Roberval
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cavalieri.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cavalieri',550,800); return false;">
     Cavalieri
    </a>
    .
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cavalieri.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cavalieri',550,800); return false;">
     Cavalieri
    </a>
    was led to his 'method of indivisibles' by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Kepler.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Kepler',550,800); return false;">
     Kepler
    </a>
    's attempts at integration. He was not rigorous in his approach and it is hard to see clearly how he thought about his method. It appears that
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cavalieri.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cavalieri',550,800); return false;">
     Cavalieri
    </a>
    thought of an area as being made up of components which were lines and then summed his infinite number of 'indivisibles'. He showed, using these methods, that the integral of
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    <sup>
     <i>
      n
     </i>
    </sup>
    from 0 to
    <i>
     a
    </i>
    was
    <i>
     a
    </i>
    <sup>
     <i>
      n
     </i>
     +1
    </sup>
    /(
    <i>
     n
    </i>
    + 1) by showing the result for a number of values of
    <i>
     n
    </i>
    and inferring the general result.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s35">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Roberval.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Roberval',550,800); return false;">
     Roberval
    </a>
    considered problems of the same type but was much more rigorous than
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cavalieri.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cavalieri',550,800); return false;">
     Cavalieri
    </a>
    .
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Roberval.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Roberval',550,800); return false;">
     Roberval
    </a>
    looked at the area between a curve and a line as being made up of an infinite number of infinitely narrow rectangular strips. He applied this to the integral of
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    <sup>
     <i>
      m
     </i>
    </sup>
    from 0 to 1 which he showed had approximate value
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     (0
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
     </sup>
     + 1
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
     </sup>
     + 2
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
     </sup>
     + ... + (
     <i>
      n
     </i>
     -1)
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
     </sup>
     )/
     <i>
      n
     </i>
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      +1
     </sup>
     .
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s39">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Roberval.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Roberval',550,800); return false;">
     Roberval
    </a>
    then asserted that this tended to 1/(
    <i>
     m
    </i>
    + 1) as
    <i>
     n
    </i>
    tends to infinity, so calculating the area.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s41">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Fermat.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Fermat',550,800); return false;">
     Fermat
    </a>
    was also more rigorous in his approach but gave no proofs. He generalised the parabola and hyperbola:-
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     Parabola:
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>

       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>

       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     = (
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        x
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        b
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     )
     <sup>
      2
     </sup>
     to(
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        y
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        a
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     )
     <sup>
      <i>
       n
      </i>
     </sup>
     = (
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        x
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        b
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     )
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
     </sup>
    </p>
    <p align="justify">
     Hyperbola:
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        y
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        a
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     =
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        b
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        x
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     to(
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        y
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        a
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     )
     <sup>
      <i>
       n
      </i>
     </sup>
     = (
     <font size="+1">
      <sup>
       <i>
        b
       </i>
      </sup>
      /
      <sub>
       <i>
        x
       </i>
      </sub>
     </font>
     )
     <sup>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
     </sup>
     .
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    In the course of examining
    <font size="+1">
     <sup>
      <i>
       y
      </i>
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      <i>
       a
      </i>
     </sub>
    </font>
    = (
    <font size="+1">
     <sup>
      <i>
       x
      </i>
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      <i>
       b
      </i>
     </sub>
    </font>
    )
    <sup>
     <i>
      p
     </i>
    </sup>
    ,
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Fermat.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Fermat',550,800); return false;">
     Fermat
    </a>
    computed the sum of
    <i>
     r
    </i>
    <sup>
     <i>
      p
     </i>
    </sup>
    from
    <i>
     r
    </i>
    = 1 to
    <i>
     r
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     n
    </i>
    .
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="44">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Fermat.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Fermat',550,800); return false;">
     Fermat
    </a>
    also investigated maxima and minima by considering when the tangent to the curve was parallel to the
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    -axis. He wrote to
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Descartes.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Descartes',550,800); return false;">
     Descartes
    </a>
    giving the method essentially as used today, namely finding maxima and minima by calculating when the derivative of the function was 0. In fact, because of this work,
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Lagrange.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Lagrange',550,800); return false;">
     Lagrange
    </a>
    stated clearly that he considers
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Fermat.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Fermat',550,800); return false;">
     Fermat
    </a>
    to be the inventor of the calculus.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="46">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Descartes.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Descartes',550,800); return false;">
     Descartes
    </a>
    produced an important method of determining normals in
    <i>
     La Gomtrie
    </i>
    in 1637 based on double intersection.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/De_Beaune.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/De_Beaune',550,800); return false;">
     De Beaune
    </a>
    extended his methods and applied it to tangents where double intersection translates into double roots.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Hudde.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Hudde',550,800); return false;">
     Hudde
    </a>
    discovered a simpler method, known as
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Hudde.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Hudde',550,800); return false;">
     Hudde
    </a>
    's Rule, which basically involves the derivative. Descartes' method and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Hudde.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Hudde',550,800); return false;">
     Hudde
    </a>
    's Rule were important in influencing
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    .
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="48">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Huygens.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Huygens',550,800); return false;">
     Huygens
    </a>
    was critical of
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cavalieri.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cavalieri',550,800); return false;">
     Cavalieri
    </a>
    's proofs saying that what one needs is a proof which at least convinces one that a rigorous proof could be constructed.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Huygens.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Huygens',550,800); return false;">
     Huygens
    </a>
    was a major influence on
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    and so played a considerable part in producing a more satisfactory approach to the calculus.
    <br/>
    <img align="right" src="../Diagrams/Barrow's_triangle.jpeg"/>
    <br/>
    <a name="52">
    </a>
    The next major step was provided by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Torricelli.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Torricelli',550,800); return false;">
     Torricelli
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    .
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    gave a method of tangents to a curve where the tangent is given as the limit of a chord as the points approach each other known as
    <i>
     Barrow's differential triangle
    </i>
    .
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
   </p>
   <p align="right">
    <br/>
    Here is
    <b>
     Barrow's differential triangle
    </b>
    <br/>
    <br clear="right"/>
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s62">
    </a>
    Both
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Torricelli.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Torricelli',550,800); return false;">
     Torricelli
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    considered the problem of motion with variable speed. The derivative of the distance is velocity and the inverse operation takes one from the velocity to the distance. Hence an awareness of the inverse of differentiation began to evolve naturally and the idea that integral and derivative were inverses to each other were familiar to
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    . In fact, although
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    never explicitly stated the fundamental theorem of the calculus, he was working towards the result and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    was to continue with this direction and state the Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus explicitly.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="59">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Torricelli.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Torricelli',550,800); return false;">
     Torricelli
    </a>
    's work was continued in Italy by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Mengoli.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Mengoli',550,800); return false;">
     Mengoli
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Angeli.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Angeli',550,800); return false;">
     Angeli
    </a>
    .
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s66">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    wrote a tract on fluxions in October 1666. This was a work which was not published at the time but seen by many mathematicians and had a major influence on the direction the calculus was to take.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    thought of a particle tracing out a curve with two moving lines which were the coordinates. The horizontal velocity
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ' and the vertical velocity
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    ' were the fluxions of
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    and
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    associated with the flux of time. The fluents or
    <i>
     flowing quantities
    </i>
    were
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    and
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    themselves. With this fluxion notation
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    '/
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ' was the tangent to
    <i>
     f
    </i>
    (
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ,
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    ) = 0.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s68">
    </a>
    In his 1666 tract
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    discusses the converse problem, given the relationship between
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    and
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    '/
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ' find
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    . Hence the slope of the tangent was given for each
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    and when
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    '/
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ' =
    <i>
     f
    </i>
    (
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ) then
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    solves the problem by antidifferentiation. He also calculated areas by antidifferentiation and this work contains the first clear statement of the
    <i>
     Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus
    </i>
    .
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s70">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    had problems publishing his mathematical work.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    was in some way to blame for this since the publisher of
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    's work had gone bankrupt and publishers were, after this, wary of publishing mathematical works!
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    's work on
    <i>
     Analysis with infinite series
    </i>
    was written in 1669 and circulated in manuscript. It was not published until 1711. Similarly his
    <i>
     Method of fluxions and infinite series
    </i>
    was written in 1671 and published in English translation in 1736. The Latin original was not published until much later.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="67">
    </a>
    In these two works
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    calculated the series expansion for sin
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    and cos
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    and the expansion for what was actually the exponential function, although this function was not established until
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Euler.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Euler',550,800); return false;">
     Euler
    </a>
    introduced the present notation
    <i>
     e
    </i>
    <sup>
     <i>
      x
     </i>
    </sup>
    .
    <br/>
    <br/>
    <a name="69">
     You can see the
    </a>
    <a href="javascript:win0('Diagrams/SineTaylor.gif','Sine expansion',532,341,0,0,'')">
     series expansions for sine
    </a>
    and for
    <a .="" are="" called="" href="javascript:win0('Diagrams/CosineTaylor.gif','Cosine expansion',534,351,0,0,'')" now="" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Taylor',550,800); return false;" they="">
     Taylor
    </a>
    or
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Maclaurin.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Maclaurin',550,800); return false;">
     Maclaurin
    </a>
    series.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s76">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    's next mathematical work was
    <i>
     Tractatus de Quadratura Curvarum
    </i>
    which he wrote in 1693 but it was not published until 1704 when he published it as an Appendix to his
    <i>
     Optiks
    </i>
    . This work contains another approach which involves taking limits.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    says
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     <i>
      In the time in which x by flowing becomes
     </i>
     <i>
      x
     </i>
     +
     <i>
      o
     </i>
     <i>
      , the quantity x
      <sup>
       n
      </sup>
      becomes
     </i>
     (
     <i>
      x
     </i>
     +
     <i>
      o
     </i>
     )
     <sup>
      <i>
       n
      </i>
     </sup>
     <i>
      i.e. by the method of infinite series,
     </i>
    </p>
    <p align="justify">
     <i>
      x
     </i>
     <sup>
      <i>
       n
      </i>
     </sup>
     +
     <i>
      nox
     </i>
     <sup>
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      -1
     </sup>
     + (
     <i>
      nn
     </i>
     -
     <i>
      n
     </i>
     )/2
     <i>
      oox
     </i>
     <sup>
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      -2
     </sup>
     + . . .
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    At the end he lets the increment
    <i>
     o
    </i>
    vanish by 'taking limits'.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="79">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    learnt much on a European tour which led him to meet
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Huygens.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Huygens',550,800); return false;">
     Huygens
    </a>
    in Paris in 1672. He also met
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Hooke.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Hooke',550,800); return false;">
     Hooke
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Boyle.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Boyle',550,800); return false;">
     Boyle
    </a>
    in London in 1673 where he bought several mathematics books, including
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    's works.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    was to have a lengthy correspondence with
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Barrow.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Barrow',550,800); return false;">
     Barrow
    </a>
    . On returning to Paris
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    did some very fine work on the calculus, thinking of the foundations very differently from
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    .
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s86">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    considered variables changing with time.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    thought of variables
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ,
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    as ranging over sequences of infinitely close values. He introduced
    <i>
     dx
    </i>
    and
    <i>
     dy
    </i>
    as differences between successive values of these sequences.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    knew that
    <font size="+1">
     <sup>
      <i>
       dy
      </i>
     </sup>
     /
     <sub>
      <i>
       dx
      </i>
     </sub>
    </font>
    gives the tangent but he did not use it as a defining property.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s88">
    </a>
    For
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    integration consisted of finding fluents for a given fluxion so the fact that integration and differentiation were inverses was implied.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    used integration as a sum, in a rather similar way to
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cavalieri.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cavalieri',550,800); return false;">
     Cavalieri
    </a>
    . He was also happy to use 'infinitesimals' dx and dy where
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    used
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    ' and
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    ' which were finite velocities. Of course neither
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    nor
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    thought in terms of functions, however, but both always thought in terms of graphs. For
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    the calculus was geometrical while
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    took it towards analysis.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="s90">
    </a>
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    was very conscious that finding a good notation was of fundamental importance and thought a lot about it.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    , on the other hand, wrote more for himself and, as a consequence, tended to use whatever notation he thought of on the day.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    's notation of
    <i>
     d
    </i>
    and  highlighted the operator aspect which proved important in later developments. By 1675
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    had settled on the notation
   </p>
   <blockquote>
    <p align="justify">
     
     <i>
      y
     </i>
     <i>
      dy
     </i>
     =
     <i>
      y
     </i>
     <sup>
      2
     </sup>
     /2
    </p>
   </blockquote>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="89">
    </a>
    written exactly as it would be today. His results on the integral calculus were published in 1684 and 1686 under the name '
    <i>
     calculus summatorius
    </i>
    ', the name integral calculus was suggested by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Bernoulli_Jacob.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Bernoulli_Jacob',550,800); return false;">
     Jacob Bernoulli
    </a>
    in 1690.
   </p>
   <p align="justify">
    <a name="91">
    </a>
    After
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Newton.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Newton',550,800); return false;">
     Newton
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Leibniz.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Leibniz',550,800); return false;">
     Leibniz
    </a>
    the development of the calculus was continued by
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Bernoulli_Jacob.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Bernoulli_Jacob',550,800); return false;">
     Jacob Bernoulli
    </a>
    and
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Bernoulli_Johann.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Bernoulli_Johann',550,800); return false;">
     Johann Bernoulli
    </a>
    . However when
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Berkeley.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Berkeley',550,800); return false;">
     Berkeley
    </a>
    published his
    <i>
     Analyst
    </i>
    in 1734 attacking the lack of rigour in the calculus and disputing the logic on which it was based much effort was made to tighten the reasoning.
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Maclaurin.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Maclaurin',550,800); return false;">
     Maclaurin
    </a>
    attempted to put the calculus on a rigorous geometrical basis but the really satisfactory basis for the calculus had to wait for the work of
    <a href="../Mathematicians/Cauchy.html" onclick="javascript:win1('../Mathematicians/Cauchy',550,800); return false;">
     Cauchy
    </a>
    in the 19
    <sup>
     th
    </sup>
    Century.
   </p>
