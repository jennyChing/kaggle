     <p>
      <b>
       Quantum gravity
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       QG
      </b>
      ) is a field of
      <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">
       theoretical physics
      </a>
      that seeks to describe the force of
      <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
       gravity
      </a>
      according to the principles of
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
       quantum mechanics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The current understanding of
      <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
       gravity
      </a>
      is based on
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
       Albert Einstein
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">
       general theory of relativity
      </a>
      , which is formulated within the framework of
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_physics" title="Classical physics">
       classical physics
      </a>
      . On the other hand, the
      <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">
       nongravitational forces
      </a>
      are described within the framework of quantum mechanics, a radically different formalism for describing physical phenomena based on
      <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">
       probability
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The necessity of a quantum mechanical description of gravity follows from the fact that one cannot consistently couple a classical system to a quantum one.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although a quantum theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
       gravity
      </a>
      is needed in order to reconcile general relativity with the principles of quantum mechanics, difficulties arise when one attempts to apply the usual prescriptions of
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       quantum field theory
      </a>
      to the force of gravity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From a technical point of view, the problem is that the theory one gets in this way is not
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renormalizable" title="Renormalizable">
       renormalizable
      </a>
      and therefore cannot be used to make meaningful physical predictions. As a result, theorists have taken up more radical approaches to the problem of quantum gravity, the most popular approaches being
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       string theory
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">
       loop quantum gravity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A recent development is the
      <a href="/wiki/Causal_fermion_system" title="Causal fermion system">
       theory of causal fermion systems
      </a>
      which gives quantum mechanics, general relativity and quantum field theory as limiting cases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFSIntro_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CFSIntro-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PFP_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PFP-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srev_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-srev-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sector_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sector-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lepton_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lepton-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qft_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-qft-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Strictly speaking, the aim of quantum gravity is only to describe the quantum behavior of the gravitational field and should not be confused with the objective of
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Unified_Theory" title="Grand Unified Theory">
       unifying all fundamental interactions
      </a>
      into a single mathematical framework. Although some quantum gravity theories such as
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       string theory
      </a>
      try to unify gravity with the other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_force" title="Fundamental force">
       fundamental forces
      </a>
      , others such as
      <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">
       loop quantum gravity
      </a>
      make no such attempt; instead, they make an effort to quantize the gravitational field while it is kept separate from the other forces. A theory of quantum gravity that is also a
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Unified_Theory" title="Grand Unified Theory">
       grand unification
      </a>
      of all known interactions is sometimes referred to as a
      <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_everything" title="Theory of everything">
       theory of everything
      </a>
      (TOE).
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the difficulties of quantum gravity is that quantum gravitational effects are only expected to become apparent near the
      <a href="/wiki/Planck_scale" title="Planck scale">
       Planck scale
      </a>
      , a scale far smaller in distance (equivalently, far larger in energy) than what is currently accessible at high energy
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">
       particle accelerators
      </a>
      . As a result, quantum gravity is a mainly theoretical enterprise, although there are speculations about how quantum gravity effects might be observed in existing experiments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table class="wikitable" style="float:right; width:30%; margin:0em 1em 1em 1em;">
      <caption>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_physics" title="List of unsolved problems in physics">
        List of unsolved problems in physics
       </a>
      </caption>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <div style="padding:5px;">
         <i>
          How can the theory of
          <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
           quantum mechanics
          </a>
          be merged with the theory of
          <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
           general relativity
          </a>
          /
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
           gravitational force
          </a>
          and remain correct at microscopic length scales? What verifiable predictions does any theory of quantum gravity make?
         </i>
        </div>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:482px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quantum_gravity.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="1304" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Quantum_gravity.png/480px-Quantum_gravity.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Quantum_gravity.png/720px-Quantum_gravity.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Quantum_gravity.png/960px-Quantum_gravity.png 2x" width="480"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quantum_gravity.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Diagram showing where quantum gravity sits in the hierarchy of physics theories
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Much of the difficulty in meshing these theories at all energy scales comes from the different assumptions that these theories make on how the universe works.
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       Quantum field theory
      </a>
      depends on particle fields embedded in the flat space-time of
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special relativity
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       General relativity
      </a>
      models gravity as a curvature within
      <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
       space-time
      </a>
      that changes as a gravitational mass moves. Historically, the most obvious way of combining the two (such as treating gravity as simply another particle field) ran quickly into what is known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Renormalization" title="Renormalization">
       renormalization
      </a>
      problem. In the old-fashioned understanding of renormalization, gravity particles would attract each other and adding together all of the interactions results in many infinite values which cannot easily be cancelled out mathematically to yield sensible, finite results. This is in contrast with
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">
       quantum electrodynamics
      </a>
      where, given that the series still do not converge, the interactions sometimes evaluate to infinite results, but those are few enough in number to be removable via renormalization.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effective_field_theories">
       Effective field theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effective field theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Quantum gravity can be treated as an
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">
       effective field theory
      </a>
      . Effective quantum field theories come with some high-energy cutoff, beyond which we do not expect that the theory provides a good description of nature. The "infinities" then become large but finite quantities depending on this finite cutoff scale, and correspond to processes that involve very high energies near the fundamental cutoff. These quantities can then be absorbed into an infinite collection of coupling constants, and at energies well below the fundamental cutoff of the theory, to any desired precision; only a finite number of these coupling constants need to be measured in order to make legitimate quantum-mechanical predictions. This same logic works just as well for the highly successful theory of low-energy
      <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">
       pions
      </a>
      as for quantum gravity. Indeed, the first quantum-mechanical corrections to
      <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">
       graviton
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">
       scattering
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_gravitation" title="Newton's law of gravitation">
       Newton's law of gravitation
      </a>
      have been explicitly computed
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-effqg_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-effqg-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (although they are so astronomically small that we may never be able to measure them). In fact, gravity is in many ways a much better quantum field theory than the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">
       Standard Model
      </a>
      , since it appears to be valid all the way up to its cutoff at the
      <a href="/wiki/Planck_scale" title="Planck scale">
       Planck scale
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      While confirming that quantum mechanics and gravity are indeed consistent at reasonable energies, it is clear that near or above the fundamental cutoff of our effective quantum theory of gravity (the cutoff is generally assumed to be of the order of the Planck scale), a new model of nature will be needed. Specifically, the problem of combining quantum mechanics and gravity becomes an issue only at very high energies, and may well require a totally new kind of model.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_gravity_theory_for_the_highest_energy_scales">
       Quantum gravity theory for the highest energy scales
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quantum gravity theory for the highest energy scales">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The general approach to deriving a quantum gravity theory that is valid at even the highest energy scales is to assume that such a theory will be simple and elegant and, accordingly, to study symmetries and other clues offered by current theories that might suggest ways to combine them into a comprehensive, unified theory. One problem with this approach is that it is unknown whether quantum gravity will actually conform to a simple and elegant theory, as it should resolve the dual conundrums of
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special relativity
      </a>
      with regard to the
      <a href="/wiki/Mass#Equivalence_of_inertial_and_gravitational_masses" title="Mass">
       uniformity of acceleration and gravity
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      with regard to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_curvature" title="Spacetime curvature">
       spacetime curvature
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Such a theory is required in order to understand problems involving the combination of very high energy and very small dimensions of space, such as the behavior of
      <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">
       black holes
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">
       origin of the universe
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_mechanics_and_general_relativity">
       Quantum mechanics and general relativity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Quantum mechanics and general relativity">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">

         Gravity Probe B (GP-B)
        </a>
        has measured spacetime curvature near Earth to test related models in application of Einstein's general theory of relativity.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_graviton">
       The graviton
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The graviton">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">
       Graviton
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      At present, one of the deepest problems in theoretical physics is harmonizing the theory of
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      , which describes gravitation, and applications to large-scale structures (
      <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
       stars
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">
       planets
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">
       galaxies
      </a>
      ), with
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
       quantum mechanics
      </a>
      , which describes the other three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_forces" title="Fundamental forces">
       fundamental forces
      </a>
      acting on the
      <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
       atomic
      </a>
      scale. This problem must be put in the proper context, however. In particular, contrary to the popular claim that quantum mechanics and general relativity are fundamentally incompatible, one can demonstrate that the structure of general relativity essentially follows inevitably from the quantum mechanics of interacting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical" title="Theoretical">
       theoretical
      </a>
      spin-2 massless particles (called
      <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">
       gravitons
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraichnan1955_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kraichnan1955-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta1954_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gupta1954-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta1957_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gupta1957-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta1962_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gupta1962-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deser1970_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Deser1970-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While there is no concrete proof of the existence of gravitons, quantized theories of matter may necessitate their existence.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Supporting this theory is the observation that all
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_forces" title="Fundamental forces">
       fundamental forces
      </a>
      except gravity have one or more known
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messenger_particles" title="Messenger particles">
       messenger particles
      </a>
      , leading researchers to believe that at least one most likely does exist; they have dubbed this hypothetical particle the
      <i>
       graviton
      </i>
      . The predicted find would result in the classification of the graviton as a "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Force_particle" title="Force particle">
       force particle
      </a>
      " similar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
       photon
      </a>
      of the electromagnetic field. Many of the accepted notions of a unified theory of physics since the 1970s assume, and to some degree depend upon, the existence of the graviton. These include
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       string theory
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Superstring_theory" title="Superstring theory">
       superstring theory
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/M-theory" title="M-theory">
       M-theory
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">
       loop quantum gravity
      </a>
      . Detection of gravitons is thus vital to the validation of various lines of research to unify quantum mechanics and relativity theory.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_dilaton">
       The dilaton
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The dilaton">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Dilaton" title="Dilaton">
       Dilaton
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Dilaton" title="Dilaton">
       dilaton
      </a>
      made its first appearance in
      <a href="/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory" title="KaluzaKlein theory">
       KaluzaKlein theory
      </a>
      , a five-dimensional theory that combined
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
       gravitation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">
       electromagnetism
      </a>
      . Generally, it appears in
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       string theory
      </a>
      . More recently, it has appeared in the lower-dimensional many-bodied gravity problem
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      based on the field theoretic approach of
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jackiw" title="Roman Jackiw">
       Roman Jackiw
      </a>
      . The impetus arose from the fact that complete analytical solutions for the metric of a covariant
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      -body system have proven elusive in general relativity. To simplify the problem, the number of dimensions was lowered to
      <i>
       (1+1)
      </i>
      , i.e. one spatial dimension and one temporal dimension. This model problem, known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%3DT_model" title="R=T model">
       <i>
        R=T
       </i>
       theory
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (as opposed to the general
      <i>
       G=T
      </i>
      theory) was amenable to exact solutions in terms of a generalization of the
      <a href="/wiki/Lambert_W_function" title="Lambert W function">
       Lambert W function
      </a>
      . It was also found that the field equation governing the dilaton (derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">
       differential geometry
      </a>
      ) was the
      <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrdinger equation">
       Schrdinger equation
      </a>
      and consequently amenable to quantization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Thus, one had a theory which combined gravity, quantization and even the electromagnetic interaction, promising ingredients of a fundamental physical theory. It is worth noting that the outcome revealed a previously unknown and already existing
      <i>
       natural link
      </i>
      between general relativity and quantum mechanics. However, this theory needs to be generalized in
      <i>
       (2+1)
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       (3+1)
      </i>
      dimensions although, in principle, the field equations are amenable to such generalization as shown with the inclusion of a one-graviton process
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and yielding the correct Newtonian limit in
      <i>
       d
      </i>
      dimensions if a dilaton is included. However, it is not yet clear what the fully generalized field equation governing the dilaton in (3+1) dimensions should be. This is further complicated by the fact that gravitons can propagate in
      <i>
       (3+1)
      </i>
      dimensions and consequently that would imply gravitons and dilatons exist in the real world. Moreover, detection of the dilaton is expected to be even more elusive than the graviton. However, since this approach allows for the combination of gravitational, electromagnetic and quantum effects, their coupling could potentially lead to a means of vindicating the theory, through cosmology and perhaps even
      <i>
       experimentally
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nonrenormalizability_of_gravity">
       Nonrenormalizability of gravity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nonrenormalizability of gravity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Renormalization" title="Renormalization">
       Renormalization
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      General relativity, like
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">
       electromagnetism
      </a>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_field_theory" title="Classical field theory">
       classical field theory
      </a>
      . One might expect that, as with electromagnetism, there should be a corresponding
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       quantum field theory
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      However, gravity is perturbatively
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonrenormalizable" title="Nonrenormalizable">
       nonrenormalizable
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For a quantum field theory to be well-defined according to this understanding of the subject, it must be
      <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_freedom" title="Asymptotic freedom">
       asymptotically free
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymptotic_safety" title="Asymptotic safety">
       asymptotically safe
      </a>
      . The theory must be characterized by a choice of
      <i>
       finitely many
      </i>
      parameters, which could, in principle, be set by experiment. For example, in
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">
       quantum electrodynamics
      </a>
      , these parameters are the charge and mass of the electron, as measured at a particular energy scale.
     </p>
     <p>
      On the other hand, in quantizing gravity, there are
      <i>
       infinitely many independent parameters
      </i>
      (counterterm coefficients) needed to define the theory. For a given choice of those parameters, one could make sense of the theory, but since we can never do infinitely many experiments to fix the values of every parameter, we do not have a meaningful physical theory:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       At low energies, the logic of the
       <a href="/wiki/Renormalization_group" title="Renormalization group">
        renormalization group
       </a>
       tells us that, despite the unknown choices of these infinitely many parameters, quantum gravity will reduce to the usual Einstein theory of
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
        general relativity
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       On the other hand, if we could probe very high energies where quantum effects take over, then
       <i>
        every one
       </i>
       of the infinitely many unknown parameters would begin to matter, and we could make no predictions at all.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      As explained below, there is a way around this problem by treating QG as an
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">
       effective field theory
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Any meaningful theory of quantum gravity that makes sense and is predictive at all energy scales must have some deep principle that reduces the infinitely many unknown parameters to a finite number that can then be measured.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       One possibility is that normal
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory" title="Perturbation theory">
        perturbation theory
       </a>
       is not a reliable guide to the renormalizability of the theory, and that there really
       <i>
        is
       </i>
       a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_fixed_point" title="UV fixed point">
        UV fixed point
       </a>
       for gravity. Since this is a question of
       <a href="/wiki/Non-perturbative" title="Non-perturbative">
        non-perturbative
       </a>
       quantum field theory, it is difficult to find a reliable answer, but some people still pursue this option.
      </li>
      <li>
       Another possibility is that there are new symmetry principles that constrain the parameters and reduce them to a finite set. This is the route taken by
       <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
        string theory
       </a>
       , where all of the excitations of the string essentially manifest themselves as new symmetries.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="QG_as_an_effective_field_theory">
       QG as an effective field theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: QG as an effective field theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">
       Effective field theory
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In an
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">
       effective field theory
      </a>
      , all but the first few of the infinite set of parameters in a non-renormalizable theory are suppressed by huge energy scales and hence can be neglected when computing low-energy effects. Thus, at least in the low-energy regime, the model is indeed a predictive quantum field theory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-effqg_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-effqg-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (A very similar situation occurs for the very similar effective field theory of low-energy pions.) Furthermore, many theorists agree that even the Standard Model should really be regarded as an effective field theory as well, with "nonrenormalizable" interactions suppressed by large energy scales and whose effects have consequently not been observed experimentally.
     </p>
     <p>
      Recent work
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-effqg_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-effqg-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      has shown that by treating general relativity as an
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">
       effective field theory
      </a>
      , one can actually make legitimate predictions for quantum gravity, at least for low-energy phenomena. An example is the well-known calculation of the tiny first-order quantum-mechanical correction to the classical Newtonian gravitational potential between two masses.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacetime_background_dependence">
       Spacetime background dependence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spacetime background dependence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Background_independence" title="Background independence">
       Background independence
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A fundamental lesson of general relativity is that there is no fixed spacetime background, as found in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics" title="Newtonian mechanics">
       Newtonian mechanics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special relativity
      </a>
      ; the spacetime geometry is dynamic. While easy to grasp in principle, this is the hardest idea to understand about general relativity, and its consequences are profound and not fully explored, even at the classical level. To a certain extent, general relativity can be seen to be a
      <a href="/wiki/Relational_theory" title="Relational theory">
       relational theory
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which the only physically relevant information is the relationship between different events in space-time.
     </p>
     <p>
      On the other hand, quantum mechanics has depended since its inception on a fixed background (non-dynamic) structure. In the case of quantum mechanics, it is time that is given and not dynamic, just as in Newtonian classical mechanics. In relativistic quantum field theory, just as in classical field theory,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minkowski_spacetime" title="Minkowski spacetime">
       Minkowski spacetime
      </a>
      is the fixed background of the theory.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="String_theory">
       String theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: String theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Point%26string.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="800" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Point%26string.png/220px-Point%26string.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Point%26string.png/330px-Point%26string.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Point%26string.png/440px-Point%26string.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Point%26string.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Interaction in the subatomic world:
        <a href="/wiki/World_line" title="World line">
         world lines
        </a>
        of point-like
        <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">
         particles
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">
         Standard Model
        </a>
        or a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_sheet" title="World sheet">
         world sheet
        </a>
        swept up by closed
        <a href="/wiki/String_(physics)" title="String (physics)">
         strings
        </a>
        in string theory
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       String theory
      </a>
      can be seen as a generalization of
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       quantum field theory
      </a>
      where instead of point particles, string-like objects propagate in a fixed spacetime background, although the interactions among closed strings give rise to space-time in a dynamical way. Although string theory had its origins in the study of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quark_confinement" title="Quark confinement">
       quark confinement
      </a>
      and not of quantum gravity, it was soon discovered that the string spectrum contains the
      <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">
       graviton
      </a>
      , and that "condensation" of certain vibration modes of strings is equivalent to a modification of the original background. In this sense, string perturbation theory exhibits exactly the features one would expect of a
      <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory" title="Perturbation theory">
       perturbation theory
      </a>
      that may exhibit a strong dependence on asymptotics (as seen, for example, in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AdS/CFT" title="AdS/CFT">
       AdS/CFT
      </a>
      correspondence) which is a weak form of
      <a href="/wiki/Background_independence" title="Background independence">
       background dependence
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_independent_theories">
       Background independent theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Background independent theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">
       Loop quantum gravity
      </a>
      is the fruit of an effort to formulate a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Background-independent" title="Background-independent">
       background-independent
      </a>
      quantum theory.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Topological_quantum_field_theory" title="Topological quantum field theory">
       Topological quantum field theory
      </a>
      provided an example of background-independent quantum theory, but with no local degrees of freedom, and only finitely many degrees of freedom globally. This is inadequate to describe gravity in 3+1 dimensions, which has local degrees of freedom according to general relativity. In 2+1 dimensions, however, gravity is a topological field theory, and it has been successfully quantized in several different ways, including
      <a href="/wiki/Spin_network" title="Spin network">
       spin networks
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-classical_quantum_gravity">
       Semi-classical quantum gravity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Semi-classical quantum gravity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       Quantum field theory
      </a>
      on curved (non-Minkowskian) backgrounds, while not a full quantum theory of gravity, has shown many promising early results. In an analogous way to the development of quantum electrodynamics in the early part of the 20th century (when physicists considered quantum mechanics in classical electromagnetic fields), the consideration of quantum field theory on a curved background has led to predictions such as black hole radiation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Phenomena such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Unruh_effect" title="Unruh effect">
       Unruh effect
      </a>
      , in which particles exist in certain accelerating frames but not in stationary ones, do not pose any difficulty when considered on a curved background (the Unruh effect occurs even in flat Minkowskian backgrounds). The vacuum state is the state with the least energy (and may or may not contain particles). See
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory_in_curved_spacetime" title="Quantum field theory in curved spacetime">
       Quantum field theory in curved spacetime
      </a>
      for a more complete discussion.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Points_of_tension">
       Points of tension
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Points of tension">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are other points of tension between quantum mechanics and general relativity.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       First, classical general relativity breaks down at singularities, and quantum mechanics becomes inconsistent with general relativity in the neighborhood of singularities (however, no one is certain that classical general relativity applies near singularities in the first place).
      </li>
      <li>
       Second, it is not clear how to determine the gravitational field of a particle, since under the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle" title="Heisenberg uncertainty principle">
        Heisenberg uncertainty principle
       </a>
       of quantum mechanics its location and velocity cannot be known with certainty. The resolution of these points may come from a better understanding of general relativity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         25
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Third, there is the
       <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_time" title="Problem of time">
        problem of time
       </a>
       in quantum gravity. Time has a different meaning in quantum mechanics and general relativity and hence there are subtle issues to resolve when trying to formulate a theory which combines the two.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         26
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Candidate_theories">
       Candidate theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Candidate theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are a number of proposed quantum gravity theories.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Currently, there is still no complete and consistent quantum theory of gravity, and the candidate models still need to overcome major formal and conceptual problems. They also face the common problem that, as yet, there is no way to put quantum gravity predictions to experimental tests, although there is hope for this to change as future data from cosmological observations and particle physics experiments becomes available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="String_theory_2">
       String theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: String theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       String theory
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calabi-Yau.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="840" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Calabi-Yau.png/220px-Calabi-Yau.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Calabi-Yau.png/330px-Calabi-Yau.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Calabi-Yau.png/440px-Calabi-Yau.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calabi-Yau.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Projection of a
        <a href="/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold" title="CalabiYau manifold">
         CalabiYau manifold
        </a>
        , one of the ways of
        <a href="/wiki/Compactification_(physics)" title="Compactification (physics)">
         compactifying
        </a>
        the extra dimensions posited by string theory
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      One suggested starting point is ordinary quantum field theories which, after all, are successful in describing the other three basic fundamental forces in the context of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_model" title="Standard model">
       standard model
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elementary_particle_physics" title="Elementary particle physics">
       elementary particle physics
      </a>
      . However, while this leads to an acceptable
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">
       effective (quantum) field theory
      </a>
      of gravity at low energies,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      gravity turns out to be much more problematic at higher energies. Where, for ordinary field theories such as
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">
       quantum electrodynamics
      </a>
      , a technique known as
      <a href="/wiki/Renormalization" title="Renormalization">
       renormalization
      </a>
      is an integral part of deriving predictions which take into account higher-energy contributions,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      gravity turns out to be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonrenormalizable" title="Nonrenormalizable">
       nonrenormalizable
      </a>
      : at high energies, applying the recipes of ordinary quantum field theory yields models that are devoid of all predictive power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One attempt to overcome these limitations is to replace ordinary
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       quantum field theory
      </a>
      , which is based on the classical concept of a
      <a href="/wiki/Point_particle" title="Point particle">
       point particle
      </a>
      , with a quantum theory of one-dimensional extended objects:
      <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
       string theory
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the energies reached in current experiments, these strings are indistinguishable from point-like particles, but, crucially, different
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_mode" title="Normal mode">
       modes
      </a>
      of oscillation of one and the same type of fundamental string appear as particles with different (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric" title="Electric">
       electric
      </a>
      and other)
      <a href="/wiki/Charge_(physics)" title="Charge (physics)">
       charges
      </a>
      . In this way, string theory promises to be a
      <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_everything" title="Theory of everything">
       unified description
      </a>
      of all particles and interactions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The theory is successful in that one mode will always correspond to a
      <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">
       graviton
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messenger_particle" title="Messenger particle">
       messenger particle
      </a>
      of gravity; however, the price to pay are unusual features such as six extra dimensions of space in addition to the usual three for space and one for time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In what is called the
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_string_theory#1984.E2.80.931989:_first_superstring_revolution" title="History of string theory">
       second superstring revolution
      </a>
      , it was conjectured that both string theory and a unification of general relativity and
      <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">
       supersymmetry
      </a>
      known as
      <a href="/wiki/Supergravity" title="Supergravity">
       supergravity
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      form part of a hypothesized eleven-dimensional model known as
      <a href="/wiki/M-theory" title="M-theory">
       M-theory
      </a>
      , which would constitute a uniquely defined and consistent theory of quantum gravity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As presently understood, however, string theory admits a very large number (10
      <sup>
       500
      </sup>
      by some estimates) of consistent vacua, comprising the so-called "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/String_landscape" title="String landscape">
       string landscape
      </a>
      ". Sorting through this large family of solutions remains a major challenge.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Loop_quantum_gravity">
       Loop quantum gravity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Loop quantum gravity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">
       Loop quantum gravity
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spin_network.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="964" data-file-width="702" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Spin_network.svg/200px-Spin_network.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Spin_network.svg/300px-Spin_network.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Spin_network.svg/400px-Spin_network.svg.png 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spin_network.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Simple
        <a href="/wiki/Spin_network" title="Spin network">
         spin network
        </a>
        of the type used in loop quantum gravity
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Loop quantum gravity is based first of all on the idea to take seriously the insight of general relativity that spacetime is a dynamical field and therefore is a quantum object. The second idea is that the quantum discreteness that determines the particle-like behavior of other field theories (for instance, the photons of the electromagnetic field) also affects the structure of space.
     </p>
     <p>
      The main result of loop quantum gravity is the derivation of a granular structure of space at the Planck length. This is derived as follows. In the case of electromagnetism, the quantum operator representing the energy of each frequency of the field has discrete spectrum. Therefore the energy of each frequency is quantized, and the quanta are the photons. In the case of gravity, the operators representing the area and the volume of each surface or space region have discrete spectrum. Therefore area and volume of any portion of space are quantized, and the quanta are elementary quanta of space. It follows that spacetime has an elementary quantum granular structure at the Planck scale, which cuts-off the ultraviolet infinities of quantum field theory.
     </p>
     <p>
      The quantum state of spacetime is described in the theory by means of a mathematical structure called spin networks. Spin networks were initially introduced by
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Penrose" title="Roger Penrose">
       Roger Penrose
      </a>
      in abstract form, and later shown by
      <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Rovelli" title="Carlo Rovelli">
       Carlo Rovelli
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lee_Smolin" title="Lee Smolin">
       Lee Smolin
      </a>
      to derive naturally from a non perturbative quantization of general relativity. Spin networks do not represent quantum states of a field in spacetime: they represent directly quantum states of spacetime.
     </p>
     <p>
      The theory is based on the reformulation of general relativity known as
      <a href="/wiki/Ashtekar_variables" title="Ashtekar variables">
       Ashtekar variables
      </a>
      , which represent geometric gravity using mathematical analogues of
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">
       electric
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
       magnetic fields
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the quantum theory space is represented by a network structure called a
      <a href="/wiki/Spin_network" title="Spin network">
       spin network
      </a>
      , evolving over time in discrete steps.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The dynamics of the theory is today constructed in several versions. One version starts with the
      <a href="/wiki/Canonical_quantization" title="Canonical quantization">
       canonical quantization
      </a>
      of general relativity. The analogue of the
      <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrdinger equation">
       Schrdinger equation
      </a>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Wheeler%E2%80%93DeWitt_equation" title="WheelerDeWitt equation">
       WheelerDeWitt equation
      </a>
      , which can be defined in the theory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the covariant, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spinfoam" title="Spinfoam">
       spinfoam
      </a>
      formulation of the theory, the quantum dynamics is obtained via a sum over discrete versions of spacetime, called spinfoams. These represent histories of spin networks.
     </p>

      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Scale_relativity" title="Scale relativity">
       Scale relativity
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">

     <p>
      Most quantum gravity theories assume quantum laws as a starting point. However, in the framework of
      <a href="/wiki/Scale_relativity" title="Scale relativity">
       scale relativity
      </a>
      , this is not needed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The theory is an extension of
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      , including the relativity of scale transformations. It thus takes a geometrical approach to the problem, where quantum phenomena became a manifestation of the fractality of spacetime. This is similar to the geometrical interpretation of gravitation in general relativity, where gravitation become a manifestation of spacetime curvature instead of a force. Although much remains to be developed, validated predictions have already been obtained in physics, astrophysics and cosmology.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Weinberg.E2.80.93Witten_theorem">
       WeinbergWitten theorem
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: WeinbergWitten theorem">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       quantum field theory
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Weinberg%E2%80%93Witten_theorem" title="WeinbergWitten theorem">
       WeinbergWitten theorem
      </a>
      places some constraints on theories of
      <a href="/wiki/Composite_gravity" title="Composite gravity">
       composite gravity
      </a>
      /
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergent_gravity" title="Emergent gravity">
       emergent gravity
      </a>
      . However, recent developments attempt to show that if locality is only approximate and the
      <a href="/wiki/Holographic_principle" title="Holographic principle">
       holographic principle
      </a>
      is correct, the WeinbergWitten theorem would not be valid
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_tests">
       Experimental tests
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_gravity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Experimental tests">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As was emphasized above, quantum gravitational effects are extremely weak and therefore difficult to test. For this reason, the possibility of experimentally testing quantum gravity had not received much attention prior to the late 1990s. However, in the past decade, physicists have realized that evidence for quantum gravitational effects can guide the development of the theory. Since theoretical development has been slow, the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Phenomenological_quantum_gravity" title="Phenomenological quantum gravity">
       phenomenological quantum gravity
      </a>
      , which studies the possibility of experimental tests, has obtained increased attention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The most widely pursued possibilities for quantum gravity phenomenology include violations of
      <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_covariance" title="Lorentz covariance">
       Lorentz invariance
      </a>
      , imprints of quantum gravitational effects in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">
       cosmic microwave background
      </a>
      (in particular its polarization), and decoherence induced by fluctuations in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space-time_foam" title="Space-time foam">
       space-time foam
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/BICEP_and_Keck_Array" title="BICEP and Keck Array">
       BICEP2 experiment
      </a>
      detected what was initially thought to be primordial
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-modes" title="B-modes">
       B-mode polarization
      </a>
      caused by
      <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
       gravitational waves
      </a>
      in the early universe. If truly primordial, these waves were born as quantum fluctuations in gravity itself. Cosmologist Ken Olum (Tufts University) stated: "I think this is the only observational evidence that we have that actually shows that gravity is quantized....It's probably the only evidence of this that we will ever have."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SnT_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SnT-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
