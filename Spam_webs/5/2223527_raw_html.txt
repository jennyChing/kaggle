            <h4 style="text-align: justify;">
             <span style="color: #333333;">
              <b>
               What is Potash  Potash 101
              </b>
             </span>
            </h4>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             Potash is the name given for various mined salts that contain potassium in water soluble form.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             Potash is a key ingredient of fertiliser and is one of three
             <strong>
              non-substitutable
             </strong>
             major elements in fertilisers:
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: center;">
             <a href="http://www.nextminingboom.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/9/2013/07/POK_018.png" rel="lightbox[1437]">
              <img alt="POK_018" class="size-full wp-image-1448 aligncenter" height="360" src="http://www.nextminingboom.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/9/2013/07/POK_018.png" width="473"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             We have found
             <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ULLLmm6cCJ8" target="_blank">
              a great video on Potash
             </a>
             , (funnily enough titled Potash 101) that is well worth a watch
            </p>
            <h4 style="text-align: justify;">
             <span style="color: #333333;">
              <b>
               Not all potash is the same.
              </b>
             </span>
            </h4>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             The two most common minerals producing potash are
             <strong>
              Sylvinite
             </strong>
             and
             <strong>
              Carnallite.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             Sylvinite is a mixture of KCl (potassium chloride) and table salt, while carnallite is a mixture of potassium magnesium chloride and water.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             <strong>
              Sylvinite
             </strong>
             is the ideal form of potash  high grade ore that can be processed using a simple and cheap solution/evaporation method. This is the better kind of potash.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             <strong>
              Carnallite
             </strong>
             is the less desirable and more complicated ore from which to extract potash. Carnellite demands considerable energy to break down the magnesium component.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             <strong>
              POK has a mainly
              <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
               Sylvinite
              </span>
              deposit
             </strong>
             which is considered a significant advantage by the team at
             <strong>
              <em>
               The Next Mining Boom.
              </em>
             </strong>
            </p>
            <h4 style="text-align: justify;">
             <span style="color: #333333;">
              <b>
               What Potash is used for
              </b>
             </span>
            </h4>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             Potash actually has three main uses: fertilizer, livestock feed supplements and industrial processes.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             95% of worlds potash is used in fertilizers, while the rest is used for feed supplements and industrial production.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             Potash is a key ingredient in fertilizers that enhances water retention of plants, increases crop yields and plants disease resistance.
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             In feed supplements, the key function of potash is to contribute to animal growth and milk production. Potash is also used to produce glass, ceramics, soaps etc.
            </p>

            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             <strong>
              The Malthusian Trap
             </strong>
              named after the 19th century economist Thomas Malthus. It is the idea that populations outgrow the means to feed themselves. Malthus observed that plants and animals produced more offspring than could survive.
            </p>

             </a>
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: justify;">
             He argued that the potential existed for population to increase exponentially, while resources were finite, limiting the ability of society to increase food is inherently limited.
            </p>

             By operating in Utah, eager customers are quite literally on POKs doorstep  which is obviously cheaper and more efficient than importing from Canada.
