    <h1>
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
    </h1>
    <img border="" src="../../images/species/t/tomarctus-size.jpg"/>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Name:
     </b>
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     (Cut bear).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Phonetic:
     </b>
     Toe-mark-tus.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Named By:
     </b>
     Edward Drinker Cope - 1873.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Synonyms:
     </b>
     <i>
      Aelurodon francisi, Aelurodon
simulans.
     </i>
     <br/>
     <b>
      Classification:
     </b>
     Chordata, Mammalia, Carnivora,
Canidae, Borophaginae.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Species:
     </b>
     <i>
      T. brevirostris
     </i>
     (type), 
     <i>
      T. hippophaga.
     </i>
     <br/>
     <b>
      Diet:
     </b>
     Carnivore.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Size:
     </b>
     About 1.2 meters long.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Known locations:
     </b>
     Across North America.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Time period:
     </b>
     Aquitanian through to Langhian of the
Miocene, possibly later into the Miocene.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Fossil representation:
     </b>
     Multiple individuals.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     A
relative of such genera as
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/a/aelurodon.html">
      <i>
       Aelurodon
      </i>
     </a>
     and
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/b/borophagus.html">
      <i>
       Borophagus
      </i>
     </a>
     ,
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     is
another one of the hyena-like bone crushing dogs of the Miocene.
This description comes from the attachments for powerful jaw closing
muscles and short muzzle which means that when borophagine canids like
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     bit on something, it was closer to the
point of jaw
articulation. This in turn means that the force from the muscles is
focused so that it is concentrated upon a smaller area so that more
damage could be done to whatever is being bitten. This is exactly the
same with a modern hyena.
     <br/>
     The
high bite force of
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     is beyond that
necessary to kill an
animal, which leads to speculation that scavenging was just as an
important part of the diet as was hunting, perhaps even more so.
Scavengers however usually get to a carcass after most if not all of
the fleshy parts have been devoured, probably by the animal or animal
that made the kill. This would be no problem for an animal with jaws
like those of
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     however since it had the
necessary jaw
adaptations for cracking open the bones so that it could get at the
marrow within. Bone marrow by itself is one of the most nutritious
things in the natural world, and when encased in bone under the right
conditions it can last for months and even years after the death of an
animal. However, just because
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     was
well suited to being a
scavenger, it does not mean that they never killed their own prey.
     <br/>
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     is known to have existed for at least the first half of the Miocene,
and seems to have disappeared with the appearance of later borophagine
forms like Aelurodon. During the early half of the Miocene
     <i>
      Tomarctus
     </i>
     would have lived amongst other groups of carnivores such as
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/false-sabre-toothed-cats-the-nimravids-and-barbourofelids.html">
      false
sabre-toothed cats
     </a>
     , 
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/bear-dogs-of-the-amphicyonidae.html">
      bear
dogs
     </a>
     and
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/entelodonts.html">
      entelodonts
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Further reading
     </b>

H. Tedford &amp; B. E. Taylor - 1999.
     <br/>
     - Small mammal fossils from the Barstow Formation, California,
E. H. Lindsay - 1972.
    </p>
