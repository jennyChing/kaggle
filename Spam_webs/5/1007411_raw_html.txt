         <h1 class="entry-title">
          A Day in the Life of a Cambodian Rice Farmer
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="posted-on">
           Posted on
           <a href="http://www.justapack.com/day-in-life-cambodian-rice-farmer/" rel="bookmark">
            <time class="entry-date published" datetime="2015-01-16T19:44:58+00:00">
             January 16, 2015
            </time>
            <time class="updated" datetime="2015-07-03T09:07:17+00:00">
             July 3, 2015
            </time>
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="byline">
           by
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.justapack.com/author/just-a-pack/">
             Just a Pack
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <h1>
          <a href="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_085542_1-001.jpg">
           <img alt="20141203_085542_1-001" class="alignnone wp-image-5202 size-large" height="530" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_085542_1-001-e1434807169299-1100x583.jpg" width="1000"/>
          </a>
         </h1>
         <h3>
          I grab ten stalks of rice in my left hand and bunch them together. The sickle in my right hand saws through the plants clumsily. Sweat drips down my forehead and into my eyes; the humidity and the suns embrace are both relentless.
         </h3>
         <p>
          I straighten my back and shuffle over a few feet to grab another handful of rice stalks. The curved crescent blade cuts and saws again. I move on to the next group. I have been doing this for a hour and have fallen into an almost meditative trance. All that exists is the rice paddy, my tool, the sweat, the groaning protest of my muscles, and the task at hand. After cutting down as much as I can hold in my fist I walk out of the rice paddy and deposit my cuttings onto the ground, into the large pile I have been collecting. I stop to wipe sweat off of my face, stretch my muscles, and take in the beauty of my rural Cambodian surroundings.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Rice Farmer for a Day
         </h3>
         <p>
          Have you ever wondered where the rice you eat comes from? Could you live on $140 a year? Does your eight hour work day involve blistering heat, a sickle, bare feet on the earth, and a large straw hat?
         </p>
         <p>
          As of 2012 65% of the workforce (and 80% of the rural workforce) in Cambodia worked in agriculture, the majority of them rice farmers. We teamed up with
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           <a href="http://madmonkeyhostels.com/kampot/" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Mad Monkey Hostels in Kampot
            </strong>
           </a>
          </span>
          and took their
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           <a href="http://madmonkeyhostels.com/?h2o_tour=day-life-cambodian-farmer" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Farmer for a Day Tour
            </strong>
           </a>
          </span>
          to personally experience a small part of what life in rural Cambodia is like.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_081629_1.jpg">
           <img alt="20141203_081629_1" class="alignnone wp-image-2071 size-large" height="563" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_081629_1-1100x619.jpg" width="1000"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Our alarm went off at 6.30 am, approximately two and a half hours later than the average rice farmer awakens. We quickly got dressed and met up with Brian and Andrew, two of Mad Monkeys fabulous staff, to gulp down some coffee while discussing the upcoming day. Soon afterwards we rode out of the Mad Monkey Hostel in Kampot on our motorbikes, enjoying the wind on our faces, a welcome contrast to the already humid morning air. A few kilometers later we turned into a red dirt road adorned with layers of mud, endless potholes, and puddles. The road took us past seemingly infinite rice fields, basic Cambodian homes, and beautiful children on peddle bikes who greeted us with big smiles, hellos, and curious questions about who we were.
         </p>
         <p>
          We arrived at the farm we would be working at for the day, a bare wooden structure perched atop stilts which stood on a large cement slab in the midst of the rice fields. We were greeted by our guide and translator for the day, Limh, a young Cambodian man who grew up in the rice fields but now teaches computer classes in town. He introduced us to our host and owner of the farm, Lorng, whose toothy smile and wide happy eyes seemed to convey to us just how delighted and proud he was to welcome us to his home.
         </p>
         <figure class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_2073" style="width: 1000px;">
          <img alt="Our translator and guide Limh, and our host Lorng." class="wp-image-2073 size-large" height="563" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_081343-1100x619.jpg" width="1000"/>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           Our translator and guide Limh, and our host Lorng.
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>
          The cement slab, which served as the foundation for the farmers abode is where most activities are conducted outside of the rice fields throughout the day. Shaded and relatively cool in comparison to the baking wooden house above, it was adorned with a large multi-purpose table, a cooking station with a single propane burner, hammocks, and chairs. Countless dogs and chickens running about added to the rustic nature of our surroundings.
         </p>
         <p>
          After the introductions we walked a few houses away to enjoy a traditional Cambodian farmers breakfast which is composed of a large portion of rice noodle soup with a green sauce and small pieces of fish. Everything in the soup
          <strong>
           was homemade
          </strong>
          , including the noodles.
         </p>
         <figure class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_2075" style="width: 800px;">
          <a href="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/IMG_5156.jpg">
           <img alt="Cambodian Rice Farmer - Making Noodles" class="wp-image-2075 size-full" height="508" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/IMG_5156-e1421439107129.jpg" width="800"/>
          </a>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           Making rice noodle soup.
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>
          Once breakfast was in our hungry bellies we headed back to the farm, sickles and large hats were distributed, and off to the fields we went. Our host demonstrated a rice harvesting technique by grabbing a bunch of stalks in hand and deftly cutting through them in one swift motion of his sickle. We mimicked him to the best of our amateurish ability but found our actions clumsy and slow in comparison.
         </p>
         <p>
          After a hour of hacking and sweating we ended up with twenty large bundles of rice which were hauled back to the the shaded concrete slab beneath the house. We gathered our bundles on the ground there and Lorng showed us how to release the rice grains from the stalks. Lacking expensive modern day machinery we employed two manual techniques to complete the task. One involved standing on the bundles in bare feet while kneading the strands slowly. The dexterity, balance, and patience involved were a bit beyond us. The second technique, which is not only more efficient but also more fun, had us whipping and beating the bundles across a wooden board while the grains flew into the air and onto the slab.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once the rice was separated from the strands the ground was covered in brown grains. We swept it up, and collected the grains into a large wash basin. The entire large bowl was filled but for all of our labor we learned that the rice collected was only enough to feed the farmers family for a few days.
         </p>
         <figure class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_2076" style="width: 1000px;">
          <img alt="Cambodia Rice Farmer - At Work" class="wp-image-2076 size-large" height="563" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_091806-1100x619.jpg" width="1000"/>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           Amy, Andrew, and Lorng bringing in some of our harvest.
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>
          Covered in sweat and dust Lorng led us to a nearby pond where he filled up a bucket of water which we used to wash up a bit. It was unclear whether we were all that much cleaner after using the green pond water, but it was cool and refreshing.
         </p>
         <p>
          Returning to the farm house Lorngs young daughter prepared a modest lunch for us. Of course rice was served as the base of the meal and along with a topping of either a spicy fried chicken (likely one that was running around the farm just a day or two before) or a mixture of stir fried cabbage, carrots, and onions.
         </p>
         <p>
          After lunch we walked to a nearby home where we were taught by one of the local women how to make a traditional Cambodian dessert, sticky rice cooked in coconut cream with a banana filling. Portions of sticky rice were plopped down on squares of banana leaf and we were instructed to mash the rice down into a thin layer. We then placed a small banana on top and rolled the rice around it and ended up with something similar to a sushi roll. We then wrapped the banana leaf around the roll and folded in the open ends and tucked them under the roll. The parcels were brought off to be steamed for an hour or so. The result was a delicious, banana cream filled pocket of sweet rice that was quickly devoured by all.
         </p>
         <p>
          Well fed and a bit tired we bid farewell to Lorng and his family and drove our bikes out to Secret Lake located not too far from Kampot. We swam, relaxed, and drank beers, which was almost certainly in stark contrast to the farmers afternoon. While we had the luxury to laze around after a mere few hours of work, Lorng and his family were out in the fields, working in the hot humid environment with no real hopes of economic advancement, working simply to survive.
         </p>
         <figure class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_2077" style="width: 1000px;">
          <img alt="Cambodian Rice Farmer - Randi harvest" class="wp-image-2077 size-large" height="563" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/20141203_085542_1-1100x619.jpg" width="1000"/>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           Randi, Brian, Andrew, and Amy harvesting rice, sickles in hand.
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>
          The reality is that a typical Cambodian rice farmer makes around $1.75 a day for all the hard work they do, and most never advance past a point of subsistence farming. Lorng, and all the other farmers around the world just like him, work desperately in an environment most of us would not be able to tolerate. Which certainly makes us examine our own eating habits. The next time we feel we cant finish our food and decide to be wasteful we hope to pause for a moment and reflect back to our time in the rice fields.
         </p>
         <p>
          For more information on the financial earnings of a rice farmer in Cambodia please visit www.cdri.org.kh
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/IMG_5164.jpg">
           <img alt="Cambodia Rice Farmer - Rice in Hand" class="alignnone wp-image-2080 size-large" height="797" src="http://www.justapack.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/IMG_5164-e1421439538334-1100x877.jpg" width="1000"/>
          </a>
         </p>
