      <p>
       Delta Air Lines shook up the frequent-flier world Wednesday morning
       <a href="/story/travel/flights/2014/02/26/delta-frequent-flier/5815425/" title="http://www.usatoday.com/story/travel/flights/2014/02/26/delta-frequent-flier/5815425/">
        with news that it no longer plans to award miles based on the distance of flights
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        Instead, the carrier will award frequent-flier miles based on the fare paid. The more you pay, the more you'll earn.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        The formula for earning elite status will remain unchanged.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        But t
       </span>
       <span>
        he new formula for earning "redeemable miles"  miles that can be used for award tickets  has created a more complex system. As part of that change, D
       </span>
       <span>
        elta will now award miles based on a combination of a frequent-flier's elite status and the fare paid on each ticket.
       </span>
      </p>

        IN THE BLOGOSPHERE:
       </b>
       <a href="http://boardingarea.com/onemileatatime/2014/02/26/delta-skymiles-2015-revenue-based-program-details/" title="http://boardingarea.com/onemileatatime/2014/02/26/delta-skymiles-2015-revenue-based-program-details/">
        Delta SkyMiles 2015 Revenue Based Program Details
       </a>
       <span>
        (
        <i>
         One Mile at a Time
        </i>
        )
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        At the low end, a non-elite member of Delta's SkyMiles program will earn 5 miles per dollar spent. That gradually increases, with Silver members  those who accumulate at least 25,000 "elite-qualifying" miles per year -- earning 7 miles per dollar.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gold members (50,000 qualifying miles) earn 8 miles per dollar and Platinum members (75,000 qualifying miles) earn 9. At the top end, Delta's Diamond-level members  those who accumulate at least 125,000 "qualifying" miles per year  earn 11 miles per dollar spent. All members earn an extra 2 miles per dollar if using the Delta American Express card to buy their tickets.
      </p>
      <p>
       Delta spokesman Paul Skrbec tells Today in the Sky that the move is meant in part to better reward business travelers who fly on shorter routes that earn relatively few miles under the current system.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite their short flight distances, many of those routes typically command high fares for the last-minute tickets often purchased by corporate travelers.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, a last minute round-trip flight from Cincinnati-to-Hartford, Conn., typically costs more than $1,000. But the 661-mile each way flight would earn a non-elite Delta flier just 1,322 miles for round-trip travel, regardless of the fare paid. A Gold SkyMiles member who would earn a 100% mileage bonus would earn 2,644 miles, according to Delta.
      </p>
      <p>
       To put that in perspective, that means a non-elite customer would earn 1,322 miles round trip whether he or she paid $380 or $1,100.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, under the new system that will begin in 2015, a customer paying $1,100 for the round trip would earn anywhere from 5,500 miles (for a non-elite) to 8,800 miles for a Gold member or 12,100 miles for a Diamond member. A cheaper fare of $380 on that same route would earn between 1,900 and 4,180 miles. That's dramatically less than what would be earned on the higher fare, but is more than fliers earn using the current distance-based system.
      </p>
      <p>
       On longer routes, however, a quick analysis of nine routes shows customers will earn fewer miles unless they've booked expensive fares.
      </p>
      <p>
       On a route between New York JFK and Los Angeles, a non-elite would currently earn 4,950 miles round-trip egardless of the fare paid. A Gold member would earn 9,900 and a Diamond member 11,137,
       <a href="http://www.delta.com/content/www/en_US/skymiles/2015-skymiles-program.html" title="http://www.delta.com/content/www/en_US/skymiles/2015-skymiles-program.html">
        according to a Delta mileage calculation tool
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the system coming in 2015, those totals on a $500 round-trip fare would drop to 2,500 for the non-elite, 4,000 for the Gold flier and 5,500 for the Diamond. All are less than what's earned on the distance-based system currently in place.
      </p>
      <p>
       On a $1,100 fare, the totals would increase to 5,500 for the non-elite flier, to 8,800 for the Gold flier and 12,100 for the Diamond. Those totals are an improvement for the non-elite and the Diamond, but a slight decrease for the Gold.
      </p>
      <p>
       Brian Karimzad, director at Milecards.com, calculates that "the break-even math works out to be (that) a 25,000-mile flier on basic coach fares will need to spend about $4,500 in 2015 to earn the same number of miles as in 2014. A 50,000-mile flier needs to spend about $12,500, a 75,000-mile flier about $17,000 and a 125,000 mile flier about $25,000."
      </p>
