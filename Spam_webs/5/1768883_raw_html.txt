        <h1>
         5 Obnoxious Things That Won't Go Away in 2013
        </h1>
       </header>
       <footer class="lineDottedBottom">

       <section>
        <p>
         The new year is a time for taking stock of what we've learned from the past while making hopeful plans for the future. Unfortunately, those things don't always go together. Sometimes, despite our most fervent prayers, we've seen enough to know that certain things simply won't change. Here are five things you can still be sure you'll see in 2013.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/3/3/8/161338.jpg?v=1" height="82" width="550"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Including more columns by me. Tough break, guys. I have compromising photos of the O'Briens.
         </font>
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #5. People Equating Success With Intelligence
        </h2>
        <p>
         I was recently in a debate with
         <a href="https://twitter.com/DrunkSocialite">
          a friend
         </a>
         of mine about Ke$ha. My thoughts on Ke$ha are basically that she is a no-talent skank who can't sing, rap, write, or even rise to the level of being do-able. A complete zero. His position was that she is a millionaire and successful, so, therefore, she must be smart. My friend may be many things, but "right" is not one of them. In the court of Gladstone, the verdict was returned unanimously: She is a
         <i>
          successful
         </i>
         no-talent skank who does nothing for me sexually.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/3/3/5/161335.jpg?v=1" height="386" width="400"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 400px; text-align: right;">
          Getty
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "Y" is for "Yikes!"
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         You can be a huge success and not be smart, just as you can be an incredibly gifted failure. For every Ke$ha, there are a thousand girls blowing their wood shop teacher for a passing grade so they can cut class and go home and work on their atrocious rhymes about getting high behind the Dairy Queen. Fortune will smile on one of them, who will go on to make millions. The others will get their GED (maybe) and earn extra cash blowing dudes behind the Dairy Queen. That's just probability. It doesn't mean that they're all geniuses, or that only the brightest reached the top. It is a sickness of our culture that we automatically attribute other talents to people with money. Some of it is earned, and some people step in shit and then scrape that shit off their shoe and sell it for profit. In any event, if reality television is any indication, we're going to keep on attributing all sorts of talents to the momentarily successful this coming year.
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #4. People Who Step on Other People's Jokes
        </h2>
        <p>
         Before the Internet, you would only meet these people at bars and parties -- y'know, the places where people told jokes. For the kids -- jokes are things that people used to tell. You would use your voice to tell a short story with an unexpected ending that produced laughter. That last part was called the punchline. There were no GIFs, memes, or other shortcuts to humor.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/3/3/4/161334.jpg?v=1" height="350" width="325"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 325px; text-align: right;">
          Getty
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "OK, picture like a cat with like writing around it, and its grammar is bad and it's talking about eating cheeseburgers."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         But even though technology has changed the ways we share comedy, joke-steppers have remained. In fact, they're more prevalent than ever, because things like Facebook and Twitter carry our bits to larger audiences than the office Christmas party.
        </p>
        <p>
         Last month, before the Mayan apocalypse, I posted the following joke on
         <a href="http://twitter.com/WGladstone">
          my Twitter
         </a>
         :
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/3/3/9/161339.jpg?v=1" height="125" width="610"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         It became one of my biggest tweets (mostly because I was lucky enough to have the lovely
         <a href="https://twitter.com/pattonoswalt">
          Patton Oswalt
         </a>
         retweet it), and that exposure brought forth a bunch of replies. Too many people asked, "What's the line?" If they had to ask that question, I'm not sure what they thought the joke was. Understanding the reference to the old "The world ends tomorrow, baby" pickup line was the whole point.
        </p>
        <p>
         The reason I'm sure joke-steppers will remain part of 2013 is because it can happen to anyone -- even insufferably arrogant comedic geniuses like me. Sometimes our brains just don't work. I stepped on one of
         <a href="https://twitter.com/blainecapatch">
          Blaine Capatch's
         </a>
         jokes recently (you should also follow Blaine on Twitter) and have been shivering with uncontrolled douche chills for the last month since. Thank goodness it was offline and no one will ever know. Until I write about it. Hmm. Well, I had it coming.
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #3. Zealots Making Believers Look Foolish
        </h2>
        <p>
         Last month, over 20 children and teachers in Connecticut suffered a tragedy at the hands of unspeakable gun violence. I have nothing intelligent to say about these horrific events. It's the kind of thing I have a hard time looking directly at. A darkness I always know exists that robs me of sympathy for other "tragedies" like pop stars meeting their end as the result of self-inflicted wounds. It makes me quiet. It makes me cry.
        </p>
        <p>
         I wish that were true for everyone -- or at least for Mike Huckabee. On the same day as the shooting, the former pastor and Arkansas governor and present-day Fox News host
         <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2012/12/14/mike-huckabee-school-shooting_n_2303792.html">
          had this to say
         </a>
         :
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          "We ask why there is violence in our schools, but we have systematically removed God from our schools. Should we be so surprised that schools would become a place of carnage?"
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         Words like this are a far more damning statement for the future of humanity than the horrific sins of one crazed gunman. Everything about this statement is wrong, and so many good Christians would never dream of uttering such hurtful, nonsensical things. Using this tragedy to prove a religious point is unforgivable, but I will give Mike credit for one thing: He made me want to convert to Christianity so I can believe in a hell where he'll spend eternity.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/3/3/3/161333.jpg?v=1" height="465" width="400"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 400px; text-align: right;">
          Getty
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Also Easter eggs seem fun.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Aside from getting into the quagmire of the separation of church and state (and the constitutional scholars I'll ignore in the comments who will claim that no such protection exists in either our Constitution or the interpretive Supreme Court jurisprudence), it's just jaw-droppingly wrong. A belief that state-sponsored prayer to a higher power would make people bulletproof. I wonder what Mike thinks about those
         <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/communities/ondeadline/post/2012/08/breaking-shooting-at-temple-in-oak-creek-wis-/1">
          six devout Sihks
         </a>
         who were gunned down in a Wisconsin temple while at prayer last August? Of course, I don't
         <i>
          really
         </i>
         wonder, because I'm pretty sure Mike thinks that those people got what was coming to them for praying to the wrong god. Presumably, it's only the one true god, Jesus Christ, who can save young children from evil. That's probably why no sins and atrocities have ever been committed against small children while in Catholic schools.
        </p>
        <p>
         My point is not to beat up on Christianity, or any religion, but to shake my head in disgust at anyone who uses atrocities to push their religious agenda. It is a perversion of everything good about religion. Just like when Jerry Falwell and Pat Robertson
         <a href="http://www.snopes.com/rumors/falwell.asp">
          blamed 9/11 on gays, abortionists, and feminists
         </a>
         . It is a perversion, just like how those 9/11 terrorists corrupted Islam for their own evil agenda. It is a fanaticism that makes all the good-hearted people of faith look foolish for calling themselves members of the same club. It is the arrogance of believing in one true path to salvation, and it's not going anywhere in 2013.
        </p>
