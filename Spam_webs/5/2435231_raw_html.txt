          </li>

        <p id="p-1">
         Conserved sequences in the opsins of vertebrate and invertebrate
                           photoreceptors (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1" id="xref-ref-1-1">
          1
         </a>
         ), and homologous genes such as
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         involved in eye development across phyla (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-2" id="xref-ref-2-1">
          2
         </a>
         ), challenge the hypothesis
                           that the eyes of vertebrates and invertebrates had distinct
                           evolutionary origins (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3" id="xref-ref-3-1">
          3
         </a>
         ,
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4" id="xref-ref-4-1">
          4
         </a>
         ). This hypothesis was rooted in the dramatic
                           differences in embryogenesis, phototransduction, and optical imaging
                           mechanisms in the eyes of different species (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5" id="xref-ref-5-1">
          5
         </a>
         ). Sequencing data
                           suggest, however, that all metazoan photopigments probably had a common
                           origin, so while the photopigment from the eye spots of flatworms has
                           not been cloned, it would be surprising if it is not an opsin.
        </p>
        <p id="p-2">
         A photopigment, however, is not an eye. The evolution of eyes, as
                           complex organs, could still be polyphyletic. Consider one of the most
                           compelling cases of convergent evolution: the image-forming eyes of the
                           cephalopod mollusks and those of the vertebrates (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6" id="xref-ref-6-1">
          6
         </a>
         ). Though these eyes
                           look extraordinarily similar in design, these similarities are not
                           homologies. Neither primitive mollusks nor primitive vertebrates have
                           much more than an eye spot, suggesting independent evolution of the
                           camera eye in these two phyla starting from simple ancestral
                           photoreceptive structures. The embryonic origins corroborate this dual
                           ancestry (see Fig.
         <a class="xref-fig" href="#F1" id="xref-fig-1-1">
          1
         </a>
         ). In developing
                           vertebrates, the neural retina bulges out of the ventrolateral
                           forebrain as an optic vesicle, presses against the inner layer of the
                           overlying epidermis, and induces it to thicken and become a lens. The
                           lens then induces the covering epidermis to clear into corneal tissue.
                           The optic vesicle then involutes into an optic cup, the outer edges of
                           which form the ciliary body and iris (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7" id="xref-ref-7-1">
          7
         </a>
         ). In cephalopods, the embryonic
                           origin of the neural retina is a peripheral placode and the lens, iris,
                           and cornea form from successive folds of the ectoderm that encircle the
                           developing eye. The lens is acellular; it is made of long fingerlike
                           processes that coalesce into a central droplet (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-8" id="xref-ref-8-1">
          8
         </a>
         ). Thus, phylogenetic
                           and embryological considerations strongly suggest that the two eyes
                           must have evolved independently. Moreover, it seems highly unlikely
                           that the structural similarities in the adult are due to a conserved
                           developmental program. However, the expression of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         in
                           the development of the squid eye challenges this conclusion, as
                           reported in this issue (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9" id="xref-ref-9-1">
          9
         </a>
         ) from a collaboration between the
                           laboratories of Gehring and Piatigorsky.
        </p>

        <p id="p-3">
         In 1994, Walter Gehring and his colleagues showed that the
         <em>
          Drosophila eyeless
         </em>
         gene is a homolog of the vertebrate
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         gene (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10" id="xref-ref-10-1">
          10
         </a>
         ). Hypomorphic mutants in the
         <em>
          eyeless
         </em>
         (
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         ) gene in
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         compromise the development of the
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         eye disc, while
                           heterozygotes for a mutant in the mammalian
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         gene,
                           called
         <em>
          Small eye
         </em>
         (
         <em>
          Sey
         </em>
         ) have eye abnormalities
                           such as aniridia, and a reduction in eye size (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11" id="xref-ref-11-1">
          11
         </a>
         ). The idea that a
                           single developmental gene can govern eye formation in these two
                           species, when every previous indication was that the compound eyes of
                           insects and the camera eyes of vertebrates, if they shared anything,
                           shared only ancestral photochemistry, was startling. The same
                           laboratory then published an even more stunning result: the
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         gene of
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         , if misexpressed in other
                           imaginal discs such as the leg, wing, or antennal disc, transformed
                           parts of these structures into compound eyes, resulting in flies with
                           eyes growing all over their bodies (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-12" id="xref-ref-12-1">
          12
         </a>
         ). Hence, the
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         /
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         gene was shown to be both necessary
                           and sufficient (at least in some contexts) for eye formation. The mouse
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         gene under the same promoters also induces ectopic
                           compound eyes in fliesnot mouse eyes (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-12" id="xref-ref-12-2">
          12
         </a>
         )! That the mouse protein is
                           able to activate
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         genes, which carry out this
                           organogenic program, strongly argues for common developmental
                           mechanisms in eye formation that have been previously obscured by
                           morphological considerations.
        </p>
        <p id="p-4">
         The paper in this issue of the
         <em>
          Proceedings
         </em>
         (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9" id="xref-ref-9-2">
          9
         </a>
         ) extends the
                           hypothesis that
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         has an evolutionarily conserved and
                           critical role in eye development. It shows that there is a homolog of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         in squid that is expressed in the eye-forming region
                           and that squid
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         misexpression can lead to ectopic eye
                           formation in
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         . If the morphological similarities
                           between the eyes of cephalopods and vertebrates evolved twice, why then
                           do these eyes share a conserved transcription factor that might
                           regulate these processes? Is it a coincidence? The fly story makes this
                           unlikely. The hypothesis that there has been an evolutionary reason to
                           conserve the role of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         in eye development must therefore
                           be taken seriously. There are the conservative and bold forms of this
                           evolutionary hypothesis. In the bold version,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is an
                           organogenesis gene, a master regulator of eye development. In a more
                           conservative version,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is patterning gene, expressed in
                           the head, that has often been coopted to regulate particular aspects of
                           eye development. But what exactly is it that
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         does?
        </p>
        <p id="p-5">
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is one of nine members of a family of vertebrate
                           transcription factors that share a conserved paired box domain and a
                           homeodomain (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13" id="xref-ref-13-1">
          13
         </a>
         ).
         <em>
          Pax
         </em>
         genes are expressed in distinct
                           patterns during development, and where mutants are available, obvious
                           phenotypes consistent with the expression patterns often arise,
                           indicating the essential nature of these genes (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13" id="xref-ref-13-2">
          13
         </a>
         ). For example,
         <em>
          Pax-1
         </em>
         is involved with thymus and intervebral disc
                           development;
         <em>
          Pax-2
         </em>
         is involved in optic stalk and kidney
                           development;
         <em>
          Pax-3
         </em>
         is involved in neural crest development,
                           and so on (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13" id="xref-ref-13-3">
          13
         </a>
         ). In vertebrates,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is expressed not only
                           in the eye but also in the nasal placodes, diencephalon, and the
                           lateroventral hindbrain and spinal cord (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14" id="xref-ref-14-1">
          14
         </a>
         ,
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15" id="xref-ref-15-1">
          15
         </a>
         ). Homozygous
         <em>
          Sey
         </em>
         mutant mice die as embryos with defects in the formation
                           of the nose, forebrain, and spinal cord (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16" id="xref-ref-16-1">
          16
         </a>
         ). In flies, the
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         gene is expressed in the brain and ventral nerve cord, as
                           well as the eye disc, and null mutants do not survive. This indicates
                           that in flies,
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         is also involved in more than eye
                           development. In squid,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is expressed in the brain and
                           the arms as well as the eye tissue (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9" id="xref-ref-9-3">
          9
         </a>
         ). So, perhaps categorizing
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         as a regulator of eye formation is not doing it full
                           justice.
        </p>
        <p id="p-6">
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         has important roles in several aspects of eye
                           development (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17" id="xref-ref-17-1">
          17
         </a>
         ). For instance, it is a clear that there is an
                           autonomous defect in the lens-forming tissues of
         <em>
          Sey
         </em>
         homozygous mice and rats (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16" id="xref-ref-16-2">
          16
         </a>
         ,
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18" id="xref-ref-18-1">
          18
         </a>
         ).
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         mutant optic
                           vesicles can induce normal epidermis to form a lens, but wild-type
                           optic vesicles cannot induce a lens in mutant epidermis. Work in
                           vertebrates suggests that
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         regulates crystallin genes
                           in the lens (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19" id="xref-ref-19-1">
          19
         </a>
         ). Squid
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is expressed in the lens and
                           is postulated to regulate squid crystallins (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9" id="xref-ref-9-4">
          9
         </a>
         ). Interestingly, squid
                           crystallins and vertebrate crystallins are totally unrelated proteins
                           that originally had nothing to do with lens function (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20" id="xref-ref-20-1">
          20
         </a>
         ). This
                           suggests that
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         binding sites must have appeared
                           secondarily in the promoters of these different genes. Thus,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         would not be expected to regulate a common ancestral
                           lens protein. If it does have an ancestral regulatory role in eye
                           formation,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         might be predicted to control some
                           conserved genes in eyes. An obvious possibility is an opsin homolog
                           because, as suggested above, it is likely that opsins are truly
                           conserved elements of the photoreceptive organs. However,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is expressed in neither vertebrate nor cephalopod
                           photoreceptors (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9" id="xref-ref-9-5">
          9
         </a>
         ,
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21" id="xref-ref-21-1">
          21
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <p id="p-7">
         The fact that both the mouse and squid
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         -encoded
                           proteins can lead to ectopic eye formation in flies could be taken to
                           imply the retention of the ability of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         to fit into a
                           conserved hierarchy of eye gene regulation across the metazoan kingdom.
                           Yet, when all three species have such distinct structural and
                           functional components, this interpretation seems almost too amazing. A
                           more modest proposition is that
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is autoregulatory, as
                           many such transcription factors are. Thus, as the authors point out, it
                           is possible that the squid or mouse
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         gene activates the
                           native
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         gene in flies, which then regulates the downstream
                           fly genes (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9" id="xref-ref-9-6">
          9
         </a>
         ). The obvious experiment, which should be done, is to try
                           and rescue eye development in
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         nulls by misexpression of
                           the squid or mouse
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         genes. Another possibility is that
                           DNA binding specificity of the different
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         homologs is
                           similar, even though they regulate completely different genes in their
                           native species. Therefore, more work on the molecular mechanisms and
                           target specificity of these foreign gene inductions could reveal the
                           presence, or absence, of conserved pathways in the molecular hierarchy
                           of eye development.
        </p>
        <p id="p-8">
         Although zebrafish mutations that alter anterior neural patterning
                           affect both
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         expression and eye formation (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22" id="xref-ref-22-1">
          22
         </a>
         ), there
                           has been no demonstration that misexpression of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         in
                           vertebrates can lead to ectopic eyes (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21" id="xref-ref-21-2">
          21
         </a>
         ,
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23" id="xref-ref-23-1">
          23
         </a>
         ). Perhaps
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is higher in the regulatory pathway of eye development in flies than it
                           is in vertebrates, where optic vesicles clearly form in the homozygous
         <em>
          Sey
         </em>
         mice (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24" id="xref-ref-24-1">
          24
         </a>
         ). Another factor to consider in this regard is
                           the special developmental potential of the imaginal discs of
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         . Studies have shown that wing discs may, if
                           serially cultured in the abdomens of adult females, switch their fate
                           spontaneously and make compound eyes when forced to differentiate by
                           being removed from the host and inserted back into a larva about to
                           pupate (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25" id="xref-ref-25-1">
          25
         </a>
         ). Such spontaneous transdetermination from one fate to
                           another is a peculiarity of imaginal discs. As structures set aside for
                           later development, imaginal discs may repress inappropriate fates, but
                           not shut them down completely. In this way, discs may be likened to
                           pluripotential blast cells. Interestingly, much as ectopic expression
                           of
         <em>
          ey
         </em>
         causes the transdetermination of wing discs into eyes,
                           the ectopic expression of the
         <em>
          wingless
         </em>
         signaling molecule
                           leads to the transdetermination of leg discs into wings (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26" id="xref-ref-26-1">
          26
         </a>
         ). Thus,
                           misexpression of these genes, and presumably others, in
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         imaginal discs may shift their fate potential in
                           a way that is not possible in the vertebrate embryo, or any animal
                           where development of adult structures is direct rather than delayed.
                           The molecular hierarchy of early eye development is clearly an
                           intriguing problem. In
         <em>
          Drosophila
         </em>
         several genes are
                           involved, including
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         ,
         <em>
          sine-oculis
         </em>
         , and
         <em>
          eyes absent
         </em>
         . Strikingly, a vertebrate homolog of
         <em>
          sine-oculis
         </em>
         , called
         <em>
          Six-3
         </em>
         , is expressed in the
                           developing eye regions and may act upstream of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         , since
         <em>
          Sey
         </em>
         mutants have normal
         <em>
          Six-3
         </em>
         expression (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27" id="xref-ref-27-1">
          27
         </a>
         ).
                           The discovery of other eye regulation factors shared between flies and
                           vertebrates certainly strengthens the link between
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         and
                           eye organogenesis, but at the same time threatens the primacy of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         in this process.
        </p>
        <p id="p-9">
         To understand the special relationship between
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         and
                           eyes it will be important to discover the ancestral pattern of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         expression. Nematodes have
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         homologs
                           and have often been positioned in evolutionary trees near the
                           vertebrate/invertebrate branch point. The function of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         homologs in these animals may shed light on the
                           ancestral function of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         . Mutants in these genes have
                           defects in peripheral sense organs of the tail (
         <em>
          mab-18
         </em>
         ), or
                           abnormalities in the head (
         <em>
          vab-3
         </em>
         ) (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28" id="xref-ref-28-1">
          28
         </a>
         ,
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29" id="xref-ref-29-1">
          29
         </a>
         ). Butnematodes
                           do not have eyes or any known photoreceptive cells, and neither do
                           adult sea urchins, which express a respectable
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         gene in
                           their tube feet (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-30" id="xref-ref-30-1">
          30
         </a>
         )! Echinoderms, as deuterostomes, are usually placed
                           in evolutionary trees at the base of chordate evolution. The ribbon
                           worm, however, which has eyes associated with
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         expression, seems to be on the line that preceded the
                           vertebrate/invertebrate split (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31" id="xref-ref-31-1">
          31
         </a>
         ). If nematodes and echinoderms lost
                           their photosensory organs,
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         may have taken on new roles
                           in these species. On the other hand, the existing evolutionary data on
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         , taken together, may also support the idea that
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         is not really an organogenesis gene, but rather a
                           patterning gene for the head, the place where most, but not all,
                           animals have evolved eyes.
        </p>
        <p id="p-10">
         There may be too many unresolved questions to allow skeptics to buy
                           into the bold hypothesis at present, but neither can they rule it out.
                           It will therefore be interesting to continue the evolutionary survey of
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         expression and function, especially in organisms that
                           have either no eyes or very primitive eyes. For instance, there was
                           initial speculation that the primitive eye spots of flatworms expressed
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         (
         <a class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32" id="xref-ref-32-1">
          32
         </a>
         ). This would be useful to know, since flatworms
                           clearly represent a more ancestral state than the other animals so far
                           studied. What if these animals had eyes but no
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         , or
                           what if they had
         <em>
          Pax-6
         </em>
         but it wasnt expressed in their
                           eyes?
        </p>
