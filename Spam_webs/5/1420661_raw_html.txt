        Sitting disease is killing us  and exercise doesn't help
       </h1>

  an Italian company thats doing things a little differently
       </h2>

          <p class="bylineBody">

           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/journalists/olivia-parker/" rel="author" title="Olivia Parker">
            Olivia Parker

         <p class="comments">
          <img alt="Comments" src="/template/ver1-0/i/share/comments.gif"/>
          <a dsqid="11557470" href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/lifestyle/wellbeing/11557470/Sitting-disease-is-killing-us-and-exercise-doesnt-help.html#disqus_thread">

         </p>
         <div class="cl">
         </div>
        </div>
        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           Are you sitting comfortably? You wont be for long. Scientists have an unsettling update about an activity that 50-70 per cent of us spend around six hours a day doing. Putting your feet up, taking a load off: whatever we call it, the simple act of sitting has started to look a lot less appealing in recent years.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           Until new research last month by the Medical College of Wisconsin, one of the largest studies into the effects of our sedentary lifestyles was in 2012, when researchers from Loughborough University and the University of Leicester assessed nearly 800,000 people from a range of countries.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           They found incredibly
           <strong>
            <a href="http://www2.le.ac.uk/colleges/medbiopsych/research/diabetes-research-centre/diabetes-and-stratified-medicine">
             strong evidence linking excessive sitting to poor health
            </a>
           </strong>
           : compared to people who sit the least, those who spend most time in a chair have a 112 per cent higher risk of developing diabetes, a 147 per cent higher risk of suffering cardiovascular events such as strokes and a 49 per cent increased risk of death from any cause.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           <img alttext="The humble office chair could be on its way out" caption="The humble office chair could be on its way out" declared-caption="" height="386" localcaption="Computers and chairs at a modern workstation / office furniture / work station" name="office chairs" photographer="" refid="3278060" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03278/office_chairs_3278060b.jpg" version="b" width="620"/>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           <sup>
            The humble office chair could be on its way out
           </sup>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="body">
          <p>
           High cholesterol, lower back pain, colon cancer and slow blood circulation are just a few of the
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/health/news/10905484/Sitting-down-linked-to-higher-risk-of-cancer.html">
            <strong>
             problems caused by too much sitting
            </strong>
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Following this research the term sitting disease was coined for the array of inactivity-related conditions that are becoming ever more common. But beyond general advice, such as try to walk while on the phone or take the stairs instead of the lift, few practical solutions have emerged.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h3 class="was-h3">
           Exercise doesn't cure sitting disease
          </h3>
          <p>
           What is perhaps less obvious  and even more worrying  than the fact that inactivity is bad for us, is the recent
           <a href="http://www.mcw.edu/MCW-News-Center/In-The-News/Dr.-Kulinski-Finds-that-Time-Spent-Sitting-Increase-Risk-for-Heart-Disease.htm">
            <strong>
             Wisconsin discovery
            </strong>
           </a>
           <strong>
           </strong>
           that the health risks from sitting down cannot be entirely counteracted by exercise, as you may imagine.
          </p>
          <p>
           The study assessed the activity levels of more than 2,000 adults with an average age of 50. They were found to spend, on average, just over five hours a day in a chair, and for each hour that they werent standing, the levels of artery deposits in their blood vessels increased by 14 per cent. Most alarmingly, any improvement in fitness due to time at the gym was almost completely negated by staying still for a few hours.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3404815/">
            <strong>
             Active couch potato
            </strong>
           </a>
           <strong>
           </strong>
           is another recently invented term, and it describes so many of us with office jobs: we meet recommended exercise guidelines but still spend long periods sitting down, storing up health issues. This poses particularly difficult challenges for companies. Sedentary jobs have increased by 83 per cent since 1950, and the
           <strong>
            <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/foresight-projects">
             Governments Foresight programme
            </a>
           </strong>
           estimates that 
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/finance/jobs/9594263/Rise-in-presenteeism-amid-job-fears.html">
            <strong>
             presenteeism
            </strong>
           </a>
           , defined as the loss of productivity that occurs when people come to work ill,
           <a href="http://www.centreformentalhealth.org.uk/employment/presenteeism.aspx">
            <strong>
             costs the UK economy as much as 15.1billion every year
            </strong>
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alttext="Technogym cycle meeting table" caption="Technogym cycle meeting table" declared-caption="" height="387" name="Technogym-cycle-meeting-table" photographer="" refid="3278034" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03278/Technogym-cycle-me_3278034b.jpg" version="b" width="620"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <sup>
            Could meetings held on exercise bikes be the solution? This model has been developed by Technogym
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           Where a few years ago companies talked of the
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/finance/jobs/10514475/Green-economy-jobs-will-spring-up-across-all-sectors.html">
            <strong>
             green economy
            </strong>
           </a>
           , which prioritised environmental sustainability, survival in the business world now depends on the sustainability of an employees wellbeing. It is no longer enough to dole out discount gym memberships  movement and healthy living must be incorporated into daily business itself.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/health/news/10905484/Sitting-down-linked-to-higher-risk-of-cancer.html">
            <strong>
              Sitting down linked to higher risk of cancer
            </strong>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Reducing the amount of time you sit  by even an hour or two a day  could have a significant and positive impact on your future, says
           <a href="http://doctors.froedtert.com/PhysicianDirectory/Kulinski-Jacquelyn.htm">
            <strong>
             Dr Jacquelyn Kulinski
            </strong>
           </a>
           , assistant professor of cardiovascular medicine and a lead researcher in the Wisconsin study.
          </p>
          <p>
           But without replacing all our chairs with treadmills, what else can we do to save ourselves from sitting disease in a way thats appropriate for a work environment?
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h3 class="was-h3">
           The solution, according to Italian gym equipment company Technogym
          </h3>
          <p>
           One Italian company is working hard on an answer, and I visited their headquarters near Bologna to find out more.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.technogym.com">
            <strong>
             Technogym
            </strong>
           </a>
           , which was founded in 1983 by Nerio Alessandri, is best-known for building high-end exercise equipment found in thousands of gyms around the world. Beyond its product line, however, Technogym is light years ahead of most companies in recognising that wellness among employees is critical to a healthy business model.
          </p>
          <p>
           Alessandris instinct  a prescient one, as it turned out  was to improve his employees quality of life by championing the benefits of a typically Italian lifestyle at work: regular physical activity, correct nutrition and a positive mindset. In return, he hoped an active and happy staff would lead to vigorous business profits, an idea since proven by various academic papers. Most estimate the
           <a href="https://hbr.org/2010/12/whats-the-hard-return-on-employee-wellness-programs">
            <strong>
             hard return for companies that invest in staff wellbeing programmes to be around 3 for every 1 spent.
            </strong>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alttext="Nerio Alessandri, founder of Technogym, at the company's 'Wellness Village' in Italy" caption="Nerio Alessandri, founder of Technogym, at the company's 'Wellness Village' in Italy" copyright="Marco Onofri" declared-caption="" height="386" name="Nerio Alessandri Technogym" photographer="(c)Marco Onofri 2012" refid="3278014" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03278/Nerio_Alessandri_T_3278014b.jpg" version="b" width="620"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <sup>
            Nerio Alessandri, founder of Technogym, at the company's 'Wellness Village' in Italy [PHOTO: MARCO ONOFRI]
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           To showcase Alessandris theory, Technogym built itself a brand new headquarters in 2012. This
           <a href="http://www.technogym.com/gb/company/about-us/technogym-village/17588">
            <strong>
             Wellness Village
            </strong>
           </a>
           , nestled in the hills of Cesena, northern Italy, was opened by Bill Clinton and acts as a prototype for the concept that Technogym is now successfully marketing to other companies  a workplace that actively keeps you healthy.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/finance/jobs/9640237/Top-10-coolest-offices-in-UK.html">
            <strong>
              Top 10 coolest offices in the UK
            </strong>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           A total of 2,200 Technogym employees work from this huge, 150,000 sq m office. The building itself is remarkable  designed from easily disposable materials, it has a waved roof that lets in much more natural light than a traditional box-shaped building, and an energy-saving air-conditioning system that recycles natural air. Inside, beautiful staircases wind between the floors. Should anyone risk their colleagues opprobrium by taking the lift, they have to face lift doors that read take the stairs to burn calories.
          </p>
          <p>
           Perhaps the most obvious difference to a conventional workspace, however, is the seating. With the problem of our sedentary lifestyles in mind, Technogym designed what it calls
           <strong>
            
            <a href="http://www.technogym.com/gb/products/functional-training-equipment/wellness-ball/wellness-ball-active-sitting/wellness-ball-active-sitting/17506">
             wellness balls
            </a>
            
           </strong>
           . Almost all employees  uniformly elegant and in good shape  are using them instead of chairs, causing a general bouncing movement a little like a human arcade game.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alttext="Wellness balls - the future of office seating? " caption="Wellness balls - the future of office seating? " declared-caption="" height="387" name="Olivia-Parker-Technogym-Wellness-Ball" photographer="" refid="3278023" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03278/Olivia-Parker-Tech_3278023b.jpg" version="b" width="620"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <sup>
            Wellness balls  the future of office seating?
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           To me, the balls look like little more than overpriced yoga balls (retail price is an eye-watering 230 each), but I have to admit theyre a canny design for an office. The cover is antibacterial and the zip is extremely robust. The bottom third is coated in anti-slip material.
          </p>
          <p>
           Theres also a handle to carry it around, and a clever QR code that links to your phone and issues small sitting exercises to do through the day, or just to remind you when its time for a screen break. Best of all, sitting on the balls improves posture and keeps your body performing micro-movements that tighten the core and help reduce back problems. As a solution to the sitting problem, its not a bad one.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h3 class="was-h3">
           The healthiest office in the world?
          </h3>
          <p>
           Technogym has various other clever solutions for incorporating movement into the working day, including placing
           <a href="http://www.technogym.com/gb/products/functional-training-equipment/stretching-equipment/flexability/posterior/232">
            <strong>
             Flexability machines
            </strong>
           </a>
           (below)  designed to stretch muscles that suffer from sitting, such as the hip flexors  at common areas like water coolers and coffee percolators. They are also developing a new type of conference table, which lets four people sit on adapted exercise bikes, facing each other as they talk.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alttext="Olivia Parker tests out Flexability machines at Technogym headquarters" caption="Olivia Parker tests out Flexability machines at Technogym headquarters" declared-caption="" height="387" name="Olivia Parker Technogym" photographer="" refid="3278018" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03278/Olivia_Parker_Tech_3278018b.jpg" version="b" width="620"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <sup>
            Olivia tests out Flexability machines at Technogym headquarters
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           Technogym staff get a two-hour lunch break during which theyre encouraged to exercise in the buildings stunning gym, for which they pay just 50 (36) a year. Working out on an exercise machine produces enough energy to power its own interactive screen, and each person is connected virtually via their iPhone to the Technogym Ecosystem, a Cloud-based digital platform which allocates what the company calls Move points for all physical activity. These are charted on digital leader boards in the office and are regularly converted into (healthy) prizes.
          </p>
          <p>
           In the light and airy canteen (below), food is seasonal and healthy  no puddings allowed, although this is Italy, so coffee and olive oil are consumed liberally  with each dish listing both its calorific value and the number of Move points it equates to.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alttext="The healthy canteen at Technogym's headquarters, where all dishes list their calorific value" caption="The healthy canteen at Technogym's headquarters, where all dishes list their calorific value" declared-caption="" height="387" name="Technogym canteen" photographer="" refid="3278026" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03278/Technogym_canteen_3278026b.jpg" version="b" width="620"/>

           <sup>

           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           These ideas pay twofold dividends for Technogyms management. First, both absenteeism and presenteeism are reduced by ensuring that employees remain fit for the job. Second, the companys output benefits from improved concentration levels and better individual effectiveness.
          </p>
          <p>
           Technogym has capitalised on this by becoming a world leader in corporate wellness consultancy, and it now advises schools, universities, governments and, to date, 208 major UK companies, including Google and Merrill Lynch, and many smaller firms.
          </p>
          <p>
           There is still a long way to go, particularly in terms of understanding exactly why sitting is so bad for us. But the UK is slowly catching on to the importance of activity-promoting workplaces. For the last five years, we havent had a problem convincing companies, says Technogyms Enrico Capelletti, head of health. Rarely do you encounter companies that do not recognise what were talking about. Today it is about supporting them to take decisions.
          </p>

           Expect bouncing balls to invade your workspace any day now.
          </p>

                    updateCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                } else {
                    chooseNextArticle(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    createCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                }


                if (settings.articleId != "") {

                    $(renderTemplate()).appendTo("body");

                    $("#nextArticleWrapper a, #readNextOpenInner a").click(function () {
                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'click_headline', 'version_1']);

                        getNavPos($(this).attr("href"), "wayfarerReadNext");
                    });

                    bindUIActions();

                    var displayed = false;

                    setTimeout(function () {
                        if (!displayed) {
                            displayed == true;
                            readNextWidget.display();
                        }

                    }, 10000);

                    $(window).scroll(function () {
                        if (isScrolledIntoView("#tmg-related-links")) {
                            if (!displayed) {
                                displayed == true;
                                readNextWidget.display();
                            }
                        }

                        if(isScrolledIntoView("#shareBottom") || isScrolledIntoView("#newGigyaShareBottom")) {
                            if(settings.autoOpen) {
                                openArticleContainer();
                            }
                        }

                        if($(window).scrollTop() + $(window).height() == $(document).height()) {
                            if(settings.autoOpen) {
                                openArticleContainer();
                            }
                        }
                    });
                }
            };

            var display = function () {
                $(settings.widgetContainer).fadeIn(2000, function () {
                    //animation complete
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_closed', 'version_1']);
                });
            };

            var bindUIActions = function () {
                $(settings.openButton).mouseenter(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'hover_closed', 'version_1']);
                    openArticleContainer();
                });
                $(settings.widgetContainer).mouseleave(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    closeArticleContainer();
                });
                $(settings.closeButton).click(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'click_close', 'version_1']);
                    updateCookie(settings.articleId, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    closeArticleContainer();
                    settings.autoOpen = false;
                });
            };

            var openArticleContainer = function () {
                if (!settings.panelOpen) {
                	$("#readNextArticleContainer").show();//show/hide because height is calculated incorrectly for elements with display:none
                    $("#readNextArticleContainer").hide();
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_open', 'version_1']);
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                    settings.autoOpen = false; //set auto open to false once user has viewed this once
                }
                return false;
            };

            var closeArticleContainer = function () {
                if (settings.panelOpen) {
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                }
                return false;
            };


            var isScrolledIntoView = function(elem) {
                if($(elem).length) { //check if element exists
                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));
                } else {
                    return false;
                }
            };

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {
                    return index == arr.indexOf(el);
                });
            }

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
  </script>
