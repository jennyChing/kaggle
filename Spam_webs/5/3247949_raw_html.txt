          <a data-disqus-identifier="destructoid_172035" href="blogs/Ckarasu/of-evs-and-ivs-172035.phtml#disqus_thread" style="font-family:arial; font-size:14px ; color:#9dc668">

         <b style="margin-top:0px;font-size:30px; font-weight:bold; line-height:26px;">
          <a href="../../blogs/Ckarasu/of-evs-and-ivs-172035.phtml">
           Of EVs and IVs
          </a>
         </b>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <div id="findmeindablog">
          <div id="userblog_post_font" style="text-align:justify">
           <img src="http://bulk2.destructoid.com/ul/user/3/33448-172035-Gengarpokemon28332321200870jpg-620x.jpg"/>
           <br/>
           <strong>
            Pic only slightly related, but still awesome.
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           A while back, I read Mr. Sterling's review of Pokemon HeartGold/SoulSilver. In it, I recall him stating that HG/SS suffered (at least a little bit) because of the EV/IV aspect. I figured that, since I had the time, I would offer my point of view on the subject.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I will start off by saying that I do not think that his opinion is wrong. Just that I will offer a different perspective.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           In order to understand why he did not like those aspects, you must first understand what EVs and IVs are. EVs are essentially "extra points" you can earn for your pokemon's stats. Every Pokemon gives them out, and in different amounts. Once you earn 4 EV points in any given stat, that stat will go up by 1 extra point. So, if you beat 4 Machops (each gives 1 EV in attack), then your pokemon's attack will go up by 1 extra point when it levels up. You can have a total of 510 EVs earned, and only 255 can go into one stat. After that, your pokemon will stop earning EVs. That's about all for the basics of EVs.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           IVs are another aspect of the game, and these can be pretty tough to get the hang of. IV stands for Individual Values, and they determine how good your pokemon can be in any given stat (they determine how much your pokemon will get in any given stat, at maximum). The IVs for any given Pokemon are determined the moment you get an egg, or capture one from the wild. Each stat can have anywhere from 0 to 31 IVs in it(HP, ATK, DEF, SPDEF, SPE). For example: You catch a wild Aipom, and it has 0 IVs in every stat. That means that each of its stats are the lowest they can get, and it would be best to probably release it. In contrast, an Aipom with 31 IVs in every stat is fantastic, and will make a great Ambipom. Pretty confusing, I know. Hopefully, you got the gist of it.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           One other important, but easier to understand, aspect is that of natures. Natures determine what stat bonuses and penalties your pokemon gets while leveling up. Most natures will boost one stat (like Special Attack), while providing a penalty for another (Defense). The stat bonus is 10%, as is the penalty. Some natures do nothing to the pokemon's stats at all. While tough to keep track of at first, they can really determine how you will use your pokemon.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <img src="http://img.photobucket.com/albums/v190/nekomika/pmdbrt-machop.jpg?t=1272274182"/>
           <br/>
           <strong>
            Beat 4 of these to up your attack stat by one point!
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Now, as a person who is a bit of a nerd when it comes to pokemon, I constantly EV train my pokemon, and check their IVs. EV training is really simple, once you understand it. In fact, if your pokemon has something called Pokerus, the EVs it will get will be doubled. A Macho Brace can also be used to double the EVs you get again (at the cost of your speed being halved while wearing it). With those two, EV training can take as little as 3 hours (just a guesstimate. Probably much less). While training might take a while, it is pretty cool to see your pokemon grow, and to see the fruits of your training. I will agree that it is pretty lame that the game does nothing to help you keep track of the points, and relies on the player to figure it all out.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <img src="http://img.photobucket.com/albums/v81/knightkris/IVphrases3.gif"/>
           <br/>
           <strong>
            A chart showing what those sentences that appear under your pokemon's nature mean.
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Now, IVs are a different matter. Often times, I will catch a pokemon and go to find that, while it has a good nature, its IVs are awful. There isn't much you can really do to guarantee good IVs other than catching another pokemon, or breeding one and hoping the egg will result in a pokemon with good IVs. Legendaries take the breeding option away, so you have to reset a bunch of times to get a good one. It's certainly annoying at times, but you really only need good IVs in stats you are going to "use" (like speed and attack in a physical sweeper). Also, the game gives you a hint (see above picture) as to at least ones stat's IVs, so it's not ALL bad.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <img src="http://bulk2.destructoid.com/ul/user/3/33448-172035-naturechartpng-620x.jpg"/>
           <br/>
           <strong>
            Handy dandy chart for reference.
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Natures are, by far, the easiest aspect to control. All you need is a pokemon with Synchronize as an ability, and the nature you'd prefer. Put one as your lead pokemon, and there's a 50% any pokemon you catch will have that nature. Of course, the game does not tell you this (Boo!), so it's not exactly obvious. That's really all you need to know about natures.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Now, for my closing remarks:
           <br/>
           <br/>
           First off, be assured that you do not need to know any of this stuff in order to play single player. You can beat the Elite 4 with any pokemon you want to use. These are only things you need to know if you are going to play the game competitively. With that said, I will agree with Mr. Sterling that Nintendo should have made more of an effort to make people aware of these aspects. As it is, they make you rely on the internet far too much. Should they be removed from the games? No, I do not think so. While slightly annoying, they give you a reason to work for your team. They make you train, and it can be satisfying to see the fruit of your efforts. Pokemon is not a worse game because of these aspects, but it certainly is not for those who are not dedicated.
