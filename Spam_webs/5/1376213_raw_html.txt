             <p>
              Im currently spending most of my time explaining to students, hobbyists, professional developers and teachers how to build games using HTML5. I then recently thought: rather than keeping all these details for small audiences, wouldnt it be smarter to structure all I know to share it inside a post on my blog?
             </p>
             <p>
              This article is then based on my own experience. I will probably omit some crucial things some of you may know. But it will try to update this blog post along with my own new discovers and of course based on the feedbacks you will kindly provide in the comments or via
              <a href="http://twitter.com/davrous">
               Twitter
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               But why are so many people currently interested in HTML5 Gaming?
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Well, simply because today, we can
              <i>
               really
              </i>
              use HTML5 to target
              <b>
               <font color="#008000">
                multi-platforms
               </font>
              </b>
              using the same code: for
              <b>
               desktop
              </b>
              machines of course (with IE9/IE10, Firefox, Chrome, Opera &amp; Safari), for iOS &amp; Android
              <b>
               tablets
              </b>
              &amp;
              <b>
               phones
              </b>
              and
              <b>
               Windows 8
              </b>
              , and its future associated millions of tablets &amp; PC, is also warmly welcoming HTML5 Games! I then frequently see some HTML5 gaming projects ported to each of these platforms with almost no effort.
             </p>
             <p>
              Whats more, the modern JavaScript engines performance coupled with the GPU hardware-accelerated graphics layers enable us to build great games in HTML5 with confidence.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               <u>
                Note:
               </u>
              </b>
              we will only talk about the &lt;canvas&gt; tag of HTML5 and about SVG in this article.
             </p>
             <h2 id="partie1">
              Canvas &amp; SVG: 2 ways to draw on the screen
             </h2>
             <p>
              <a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/cfs-file.ashx/__key/communityserver-blogs-components-weblogfiles/00-00-01-10-46-metablogapi/4784.CanvasVsSVG_5F00_325C4B24.jpg">
               <img alt="Canvas contre SVG" border="0" height="300" src="http://blogs.msdn.com/cfs-file.ashx/__key/communityserver-blogs-components-weblogfiles/00-00-01-10-46-metablogapi/4784.CanvasVsSVG_5F00_thumb_5F00_05FCE7F5.jpg" style="border-left-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom-width: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; display: inline; padding-right: 0px; border-top-width: 0px" title="Canvas contre SVG" width="400"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              The first thing you need to understand before building your first HTML5 game is how to draw nice objects on the screen. There are 2 ways to do that and to better understand their differences, you should start by reading this article from Patrick Dengler (member of the SVG W3C Working Group):
              <a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/b/ie/archive/2011/04/22/thoughts-on-when-to-use-canvas-and-svg.aspx">
               Thoughts on when to use Canvas and SVG
              </a>
             </p>

             <p>
              Once you will have reviewed all this content, you will probably better understand why almost all HTML5 games are using Canvas rather than SVG. Canvas offers a well-known development model for games developers (low level drawing APIs) and is now very well hardware-accelerated by most of the recent browsers. Still SVG, and its vectors-based technology, is naturally better armed to scale across devices without loosing quality. You just need to use the magic ViewBox property of SVG which will handle that for you. Your games assets will then scale from 3 to 80 screens in a very easy way!
             </p>
             <p>
              Sometimes, it is even possible to achieve the exact same game using both technologies. For instance, here is the same simple game first using SVG then Canvas:
              <a href="http://david.blob.core.windows.net/html5graphics/010_SimpleGame_SVGVersion.htm">
               SVG Racketball
              </a>
              &amp;
              <a href="http://david.blob.core.windows.net/html5graphics/009_SimpleGame_CanvasVersion.htm">
               Canvas Racquetball
              </a>
              They are both issued from this MSDN documentation:
              <a href="http://msdn.microsoft.com/en-us/library/gg589521(v=VS.85).aspx">
               Programming Simple Games Using Canvas or SVG
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              In this case, analyzing the performance of each technology could help you to decide which one to use for your game. For instance, here is an 2 interesting experiments that will display 1000 small balls on your screen using SVG:
              <a href="http://themaninblue.com/experiment/AnimationBenchmark/svg/?particles=1000">

              </a>

              <a href="http://themaninblue.com/experiment/AnimationBenchmark/canvas/?particles=1000">
               1000 balls in Canvas
              </a>
              . Test both experiments in your favorite browser. In general, the Canvas performance is better for drawing a lot of objects on the screen in this special case. But this is generally also what wed like to achieve in our video games. To have a better overview of the performance comparison, here is an interesting diagram to look at:
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/cfs-file.ashx/__key/communityserver-blogs-components-weblogfiles/00-00-01-10-46-metablogapi/8611.PerformanceComparisonSVGCanvas_5F00_508F505A.jpg">
               <img alt="PerformanceComparisonSVGCanvas" border="0" height="360" src="http://blogs.msdn.com/cfs-file.ashx/__key/communityserver-blogs-components-weblogfiles/00-00-01-10-46-metablogapi/5852.PerformanceComparisonSVGCanvas_5F00_thumb_5F00_55BDEB71.jpg" style="border-left-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom-width: 0px; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin-left: auto; display: block; padding-right: 0px; border-top-width: 0px; margin-right: auto" title="PerformanceComparisonSVGCanvas" width="640"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              But nothing prevents you from
              <b>
               mixing both technologies
              </b>
              . Indeed, we could perfectly imagine using a first layer based on SVG to display the game menus with a second layer in background using a canvas for the game itself. You will then use what we call the browser compositing engine. However, you must pay some real attentions to final performance results because the browsers &amp; devices are far from being equals in this domain. Dont forget that the main interest of HTML5 is to target all platforms.
             </p>
             <p>
              My colleague David Catuhe has realized a couple of months ago a little brick breaker game using this composition principle:
              <a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/b/eternalcoding/archive/2011/09/06/write-a-small-game-using-html5-and-javascript-brikbrok.aspx">
               How to write a small game using HTML5 and JavaScriptBrikBrok
              </a>
              . The first layer is using SVG for the bricks to break and the background can display a tunnel effect using the canvas tag. You can perfectly mimic this concept to display the main game in background (hosting a plateformer or shootem up game, etc.) instead of the tunnel and your first layer will simply display the menus &amp; configuration options in SVG.

              <a href="http://www.designer-daily.com/10-cool-html5-games-and-how-to-create-your-own-23820" title="http://www.designer-daily.com/10-cool-html5-games-and-how-to-create-your-own-23820">
               10 cool HTML5 games and how to create your own
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              To conclude, Id like to recommend you 2 more advanced articles but very instructive to read. The first one is written by David Catuhe from Microsoft:
              <a href="http://blogs.msdn.com/b/eternalcoding/archive/2012/03/22/unleash-the-power-of-html-5-canvas-for-gaming-part-1.aspx">
               Unleash the power of HTML 5 Canvas for gaming
              </a>
              . He will share with you some optimization he has found while working on a demo like the one we were writing in the demo scene. The tips found could bring you some ideas for your own HTML5 games. The second one is written again by
              <b>
               <a href="http://www.html5rocks.com/profiles/#smus">
                Boris Smus
               </a>
              </b>
              from Google:
              <a href="http://www.html5rocks.com/en/tutorials/canvas/performance/">
               Improving HTML5 Canvas Performance
              </a>
              and it contains ton of good advices.
             </p>
             <h2 id="partie5">
              Some interesting online working games &amp; experience feedbacks
             </h2>
             <p>
              Again, this is my personal favorites. You will probably find other cool examples on the web to share. Whatever the game youll like, it is interesting to analyze how the developers made them. Thanks to the magic of the web, you just have to right-click &amp; view source. Even if the code will be probably minified, you can still learn a couple of interesting things by looking at the global architecture of the game.
             </p>
             <p>
              Lets start by some productions using the EaselJS framework. The first one was
              <a href="http://www.pirateslovedaisies.com/">
               Pirates Love Daisies
              </a>
              written by
              <a href="https://twitter.com/#!/gskinner">
               Grant Skinner
              </a>
              :
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.pirateslovedaisies.com/">
               <img alt="Le jeu Pirates Love Daisies  l'origine d'EaselJS" border="0" height="225" src="http://wscont1.apps.microsoft.com/winstore/1x/d86e5bea-360b-4baf-a4c4-0cf72711c1aa/Screenshot.1885.5811.png" style="border-left-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom-width: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; padding-right: 0px; border-top-width: 0px" title="Le jeu Pirates Love Daisies  l'origine d'EaselJS" width="400"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              Grant has started his EaselJS framework while building this HTML5 game.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               <u>
                Windows 8 note:
               </u>
              </b>
              youll notice youll find the very same game working inside Windows 8 in the Windows Store here:
              <a href="http://apps.microsoft.com/webpdp/en-us/app/pirates-love-daisies/a357342e-7444-4516-b0fe-29ad13b8ee0c">
               Pirates Love Daisies for Windows 8
              </a>
              . Indeed, if you have a HTML5 game working fine inside IE9 or IE10, building the Windows 8 version is almost only doing a simple copy and paste! The game will then directly works inside the Metro experience. But to make a real good Windows 8 game, you will have also to pay attention to the unique Windows 8 experience were building. This is described here:
              <a href="http://msdn.microsoft.com/library/windows/apps/hh868271/">
               Designing great games for Windows
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              Another game using EaselJS is
              <a href="http://www.bkom.com/robotgame/">
               BKOM ROBOT GAME
              </a>
              which uses 3D sprites I love:
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.bkom.com/robotgame/">
               <img alt="Un jeu de Robot avec sprites 3D utilisant EaselJS" border="0" height="241" src="http://blogs.msdn.com/cfs-file.ashx/__key/communityserver-blogs-components-weblogfiles/00-00-01-10-46-metablogapi/2541.image_5F00_15F45B32.png" style="border-left-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom-width: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; display: inline; padding-right: 0px; border-top-width: 0px" title="Un jeu de Robot avec sprites 3D utilisant EaselJS" width="400"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              The 2 most currently famous HTML5 games are of course
              <a href="http://chrome.angrybirds.com/">
               Angry Bird
              </a>
              (which uses the Box2D library I was mentioning above):
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://chrome.angrybirds.com/">
               <img alt="Le clbre Angry Birds en version HTML5" border="0" height="229" src="http://blogs.msdn.com/cfs-file.ashx/__key/communityserver-blogs-components-weblogfiles/00-00-01-10-46-metablogapi/4186.image_5F00_67257D77.png" style="border-left-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom-width: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; display: inline; padding-right: 0px; border-top-width: 0px" title="Le clbre Angry Birds en version HTML5" width="400"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              And
              <a href="http://www.cuttherope.ie/">
               Cut The Rope
              </a>
              :
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.cuttherope.ie/">
               <img alt="Cut The Rope en version HTML5" border="0" height="225" src="http://wscont2.apps.microsoft.com/winstore/1x/f390872d-9d9b-4c79-bb15-de4f019a20df/Screenshot.1914.5994.png" style="border-left-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom-width: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; padding-right: 0px; border-top-width: 0px" title="Cut The Rope en version HTML5" width="400"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              Youll find also a Windows 8 version of it in the Windows Store here:
              <a href="http://apps.microsoft.com/webpdp/en-us/app/cut-the-rope/2d85eb97-3ee6-4aff-9618-3f6cdfd68291">
               Cut The Rope for Windows 8
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              It was very simple to port into Windows 8. Here is the process:
             </p>
             <p>
              1  Copy/Paste the Web version into a HTML5 Metro Style App project for Windows 8
              <br/>
              2  Add support for touch
              <br/>
              3  Add support for the snapped view and pause the game
              <br/>
              4  Build some High Resolution assets (
              <strong>
               1366x768
              </strong>
              &amp;
              <strong>
               1920x1080
              </strong>
              ) to propose the best experience for all the incoming Windows 8 tablets &amp; PC.
             </p>
             <p>
              I also love the story around Cut The Rope. Its very interesting to read. This was a successful game written in Objective-C for the Apple iOS devices. It was ported into HTML5/Canvas and the developers who made this detail their feedback in this article:
              <a href="http://www.cuttherope.ie/dev/">
               Cut The Rope - Behind the scenes
              </a>
              . Have a look, youll probably learn some interesting points.
             </p>
             <p>
              Ive also add pleasure reading this experience feedback:
              <a href="http://www.wooga.com/2012/06/woogas-html5-adventure/">
               Woogas HTML5 Adventure
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              At last, here is a list of
              <b>
               40 more or less good HTML5 games
              </b>
              :
              <a href="http://www.hongkiat.com/blog/html5-web-games/?t=1320286727">
               40 Addictive Web Games Powered by HTML5
              </a>
              with some other available also here:
              <a href="http://html5games.com/">
               HTML5games.com
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              I now hope that all these resources will help you to build your
              <b>
               <font color="#008000">
                own HTML5 game
               </font>
              </b>
              . If youre doing so and/or if you have any suggestions to provide on good frameworks/tutorials/concepts youd like to share, please add a comment to this article!
             </p>
             <p>
