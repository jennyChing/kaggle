        <p>
         <strong>
          There are shocking secrets among Sicily's ancient relics, writes Luke Slattery.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The cornflake-shaped islet of Motya, serene in the shallow waters of its ice-green lagoon, dangles off a little-visited corner of south-west Sicily. As we approach by ferry, it seems little more than a nice spot for a stroll - you could circle it in half an hour - followed by lunch with a view of the sawtooth Egadi islands in the distance.
        </p>
        <div class="panel panel--related">
         <header class="header">
          <h2 class="heading">
           See Also
          </h2>
         </header>
         <ul>
          <li>
           <a href="/italy" title="Italy">
            Italy travel guide
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
        <p>
         Hours later, I am in a state of wonder, having witnessed scenes of extreme beauty and utter terror.
        </p>
        <figure class="media media--photo">
         <img alt="Taormina, the Teatro Greco (Greek theatre) and Mount Etna in the distance." src="/content/dam/images/3/9/7/j/4/image.related.articleLeadwide.520x294.393c9.png/1401469201000.jpg" title=""/>
         <figcaption class="media__caption">
          Taormina, the Teatro Greco (Greek theatre) and Mount Etna in the distance.
          <cite>
           Photo: AFP
          </cite>
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         I am in Sicily on an escorted group tour operated by British archaeologist Peter Sommer, led and designed by his colleague and fellow archaeologist, Dr Michael Metcalfe.
        </p>
        <p>
         We have already seen many of the island's famed antiquities, including the Doric temple complexes at coastal Agrigento and Selinunte, both of them set within folds of pasture strewn with spring flowers: saffron yellow, violet, and poppy red. After these big-ticket antiquities, tiny Motya comes as quite a surprise.
        </p>
        <p>
         As we stroll along the island's fringe, once hedged by a mighty defence wall, Metcalfe explains how Motya flourished as a trading centre in antiquity under the control of Tunis-based Carthage. Lying at the confluence of maritime trade routes and with a fertile interior suitable for agriculture, Sicily was an island of immeasurable wealth and a honey pot for covetous Mediterranean powers: the Phoenicians, who founded a necklace of trading colonies along her coast; the Greeks, who put down deeper roots with splendid cities such as Syracuse on the eastern seaboard; and later the Romans who, as was their way, conquered the lot.
        </p>
        <figure class="media media--photo">
         <img alt="Seafront area with restaurants at the historical area of Ortygia Syracuse." src="/content/dam/images/3/9/7/j/3/image.related.articleLeadwide.520x294.393c9.png/1401469201000.jpg" title=""/>
         <figcaption class="media__caption">
          Seafront area with restaurants at the historical area of Ortygia Syracuse.
          <cite>
           Photo: Alamy
          </cite>
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         Metcalfe stops at an archaeological dig on the island's north, a sanctuary where the burnt remains of children under the age of five have been found in what could be described as suspicious circumstances.
        </p>
        <p>
         "One of the reasons for the Roman distaste of the Carthaginians - Cato the Elder would end his speeches with the line 'Carthage must be destroyed' - can be found here," he says.
        </p>
        <p>
         "The Romans believed that the Carthaginians practised child sacrifice and new studies on the bones found here seem to confirm this. Usually it was the oldest child, sacrificed to the god Baal, although wealthy people were believed to buy infants and raise them as their own to be sacrificed."
        </p>
        <figure class="media media--photo">
         <img alt="Roman mosaic of hunters in Morgantina." src="/content/dam/images/3/9/7/i/f/image.related.articleLeadwide.520x294.393c9.png/1401469201000.jpg" title=""/>
         <figcaption class="media__caption">
          Roman mosaic of hunters in Morgantina.
          <cite>
           Photo: Alamy
          </cite>
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         There is silence, bar a faint breeze and a few notes of bird song.
        </p>
        <p>
         "The ancients may have looked like us," Metcalfe continues , "but they did things very differently."
        </p>
        <p>
         At a small museum dedicated to finds from this ancient island-city, I come across a cluster of grave markers found at the sanctuary.
        </p>
        <figure class="media media--portrait-photo">
         <img alt="An angel's figure in the Cathedral in Syracuse." src="/content/dam/images/3/9/7/h/r/image.related.articleLeadNarrow.300x0.393c9.png/1401469201000.jpg" title=""/>
         <figcaption class="media__caption">
          An angel's figure in the Cathedral in Syracuse.
          <cite>
           Photo: Getty Images
          </cite>
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         All bear images of children, carved in bas-relief from the soft local stone, in a naive and affecting style. These are not cookie-cutter icons but clumsy representations of real children with individuality. They seem to express something of the terrible weight of the offering.
        </p>
        <p>
         They look, to my eye, like dolls.
        </p>
        <p>
         A radical mood-shifter stands in another room of the museum: the famed Motya Charioteer.
        </p>
        <p>
         Little is known about this splendid example of male potency, carved from marble in a diaphanous full-length robe. Discovered lying face up in the ruins of the city, which was sacked in the late fourth century BC by Greeks from Syracuse, he probably represents a charioteer from the Panhellenic Games at Delphi proclaiming his victory.
        </p>
        <p>
         His sexually charged torso is modelled with a slight twist, as if he were showing some deft moves on the dance floor.
        </p>
        <p>
         "Just look at him," Metcalfe says. "He is lord of all he surveys."
        </p>
        <p>
         It is a great gift to find a statue of this quality, a male rival in the love-god stakes to the Venus de Milo, in such a sleepy out-of-the-way place.
        </p>
        <p>
         And, for a half an hour or so, we have him to ourselves.
        </p>
        <p>
         That night, after a fine meal with local wine at a vineyard overlooking the ocean, our home for a few nights, I think again of Motya. The bas-reliefs of the innocents dispatched to Baal float before me, but so too does the virile splendour of the charioteer.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is the psychological equivalent of over-eating. And for several hours sleep eludes me.
        </p>
        <p>
         I had expected Sicily to be a procession of fluted columns interspersed with walls of radiant mosaics from the period of Muslim and Norman rule.
        </p>
        <p>
         But my expectations are upended at every turn. Not one day passes without a surprise.
        </p>
        <p>
         The tour began almost a week earlier on the islet of Ortygia, a dollop of historical layer cake joined to the largely modern town of Syracuse, with a group meal - a meet and eat - at a restaurant called Dioniso.
        </p>
        <p>
         There are stuffed and battered sardines and beef braised in the indigenous nero d'avola red: an introduction to some of the gastronomic pleasures on our journey around the island.
        </p>
        <p>
         Our group includes a screenwriter from Florida, a Chicago chef, a former Pentagon official, a Belgian antiquarian, and a Melbourne barrister.
        </p>
        <p>
         The first day is spent in the limestone uplands near Syracuse at a late Bronze Age necropolis that was repopulated during the Byzantine period. Metcalfe shows us a small chapel daubed with the image of Christ Pantocrator in the rough-hewn apse of a grotto, the humble trace of a Christian community in flight from the Arab invader. It is an evocative introduction to the depth of Sicilian history and its great narrative of colonisation, conquest and competition.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Sicilian story is one long-arc epic tracing the clash of civilisations, and also their accommodation to one another.
        </p>
        <p>
         Back in Ortygia the next day, we admire one of the most vivid expressions of cultural continuity in Sicily, Italy - anywhere.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is the Duomo of Syracuse, a beautifully proportioned church with a theatrical Baroque-Rococo facade and a Norman battlement running along one side.
        </p>
        <p>
         But the stones hold deeper secrets, and there is a hint of their provenance in the limestone foundation slabs, which are clearly of great antiquity.
        </p>
        <p>
         Inside the church stands a string of austere Doric columns erected in the early fifth century before Christ. At that time the structure was a temple to Athena and it stood at the spiritual heart of a city colonised by Greeks from Corinth.
        </p>
        <p>
         By the seventh century after Christ, it had been transformed into a Byzantine church, its pagan roots preserved by the expedient practice of filling in the spaces between columns. On the afternoon we visit, Mass is being said and the church nave is full.
        </p>
        <p>
         The one "true" god is being worshipped, although he cohabits here with the many gods he never, at least in Sicily, entirely displaced.
        </p>
        <p>
         We visit the Syracusan catacombs, the city's museum, and its Greek theatre with views across the harbour. It is here, on the upper terraces, that Metcalfe, editor of the prestigious Blue Guide to Sicily, narrates one of the great war stories of the ancient world.
        </p>
        <p>
         We are standing in a superb ancient theatre, surveying a theatre of war. Metcalfe brings to life the shifting fortunes of the two-year land and naval conflict between Spartan-backed Syracuse and Athens: the sallies, the retreats, the sharp twists and turns. When the Athenians were defeated here, in 413, it was the beginning of the end for the city-state that fathered Western civilisation. A decade later, the incubator of tragedy and comedy, philosophy and democracy, had succumbed to Sparta.
        </p>
        <p>
         Metcalfe has a gift for narrative and we frequently gather to hear his war stories on our clockwise path around the island. That path takes us from Syracuse south to baroque Modica - a region popularised by the Montalbano detective series - then westward to Motya. The circuit then brings us north to Palermo via the Doric temple of Segesta and back east, cutting inland towards Taormina in the shadow of snow-capped Mount Etna.
        </p>
        <p>
         One of our archaeologist's most arresting tales is told at the site of ancient Himera, on the island's north. Metcalfe has just finished retelling the tragedy of this redoubtable Greek city's resistance to Carthage: one phase triumph; the next complete disaster. The mass grave of Greeks killed in that conflict lies hereabouts, discovered recently by archaeologists. And, as we walk through spring grass and wildflower carpeting the foundations of the Himeran temple of Victory, he explains at this evocative site how sacrifice was carried out in the ancient world.
        </p>
        <p>
         "One bull was good," he says. "A bull with gilded horns even better. The animal would have been led to its death by someone with a concealed knife. It was a procession, a festive occasion; the whole town would have been out. A small lock of the beast's hair would be set alight, smoke floating towards the gods, because they are - at least most of them - on high."
        </p>
        <p>
         Here, as at other sites from Greco-Roman antiquity, the memory of animal sacrifice - sometimes hundreds of beasts at a time - challenges the ideal of pure and poised classicism.
        </p>
        <p>
         The altar would have been a pool of blood and the air filled with the bellows of dying animals and the smell of smoking flesh.
        </p>
        <p>
         Despite the sanguinary nature of Metcalfe's description, not one of us is without an appetite that evening. Black pork, a local specialty, is on the menu at a vineyard high in the hills.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the morning, we angle back through the rich agricultural heart of Sicily, land sacred to Demeter, goddess of the harvest, and her daughter Persephone, giver of life and taker of life.
        </p>
        <p>
         We drop in to examine the splendid floor mosaics adorning a Roman villa outside Piazza Armerina, with its posse of pagan babes in bikinis.
        </p>
        <p>
         Nearby are the ruins of ancient Morgantina, a Greek city with a Roman sense of order and, on this clear crisp day, a lovely view of Mount Etna.
        </p>
        <p>
         A near-empty museum holds the striding goddess of Morgantina - yet another inscrutable mystery as there is doubt about her identity - repatriated recently from the Getty Museum in Los Angeles.
        </p>
        <p>
         "Would more people see the goddess at the Getty?" I ask our guide as we leave.
        </p>
        <p>
         "Yes," replies Metcalfe, who has strong views about the disastrous role of the antiquities trade. "But how wonderful to see her back in eternal Sicily!"
        </p>
        <p>
         In Sicily, I have learnt, the past is never really in the past. It is ever present.
        </p>
        <p>
