     <h1 class="article-title">
      8 Books That Will Inspire You to Travel
     </h1>
     <p class="article-excerpt">
      We've got your holiday reading list sorted.
     </p>
     <span class="article-details-group clearfix">
      <p class="article-details">
       <span class="author">

      </p>

     <p>
      Reading is one of the best ways to pass thetime when you travel, and books (or Kindles) areespecially usefulon long-haul flights and drives,orat those times whenWi-Fi is scarce. At the intersection of a love for reading and a love for travel are those stories thatactually inspire you to travel either by giving you new insightto a place,tellinga story that makes the destination integralor evoking images that you feel the pullto experience first-hand.
     </p>
     <p>
      Weve put together a list of books with characters who are just as travel-bugged as you are. If youre wondering what to turn toto inspireyour next trip, read on.
     </p>
     <p style="text-align: center;">
      <a href="http://awol.com.au/8-films-that-will-inspire-you-to-travel/7219">
       <strong>
        RELATED: 8 FILMS THAT WILL INSPIRE YOU TO TRAVEL
       </strong>
      </a>
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #1
      <em>
       The Beach
      </em>
       Alex Garland
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      Thailand
     </p>
     <a href="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq.jpg">
      <img alt="beachsq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7443" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/beachsq-320x320.jpg"/>
     </a>
     <p>
      Most young travellers want to find travel experiences off the beaten track  just like Richard, the British backpacker whos the protagonist of this novel.
     </p>
     <p>
      In search of a unique voyage in Thailand, he meets a man who gives him a map to a beach untouched by tourists in the Gulf of Thailand. Richards quest for the hidden beach takes him through jungle and waterfalls  all of which are exciting in their own right.
     </p>
     <p>
      When he gets to the beach he finds a community shut off from the Thai mainland  and the rest of the world. His experiences on the beach are very
      <em>
       Lord of the Flies
      </em>
      -esque. But Richards desperation for new experiences in different cultures (in this case, invented cultures) makes his tale worth going along for the ride.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #2
      <em>
       Norwegian Wood
      </em>
       Haruki Murakami
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      Japan
     </p>
     <img alt="nwsq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7445" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/nwsq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
      Flick through this Murakami delight on the
      <a href="http://www.japan-guide.com/e/e2018.html">
       Shinkansen
      </a>
      from Tokyo to Nagano over a lunch of
      <a href="http://www.japanfoodaddict.com/pork/katsu-sandwich/">
       katsu sando
      </a>
      from a
      <a href="http://www.japan-guide.com/e/e2071.html">
       konbini
      </a>
      . Thats the kind of thing Toru Watanabe, the narrator of
      <em>
       Norwegian Wood
      </em>
      , does. Hes a nomadic university student with a hunger for adventure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Unlike most Murakami novels,
      <em>
       Norwegian Wood
      </em>
      is steeped in reality. It is without the surrealism that permeates most of his plots. It focuses on Watanabes relationships with two very different women  the troubled Naoko and the outgoing and unusual Midori. His solution for difficult circumstances (including trying to choose between the two women) is to escape and take a train somewhere far away, to give himself time and space to think.
     </p>
     <p>
      As pensive and insightful as the Kyoto Imperial Palace Park itself,
      <em>
       Norwegian Wood
      </em>
      is perfect for travel. It wont completely consume you but it will entertain you and give you a literary peep into the strange and wonderful land that is Japan.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #3
      <em>
       Party Time: Who Runs China and How
      </em>
       Rowan Callick
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      China
     </p>
     <img alt="partytimesq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7447" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/partytimesq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
      You mightntthink that journalism and China go hand in hand, but Asia-Pacific Editor for
      <em>
       The Australian
      </em>
      Callick uses very thorough, yet engaging, journalism to explore China in this book.
     </p>
     <p>
      Callick has interviewed party members from every demographic: officials, millionaires, students, people from rural areas and cities, the old and the young. These members are the main drivers behind almost every cog in the China machine.
     </p>
     <p>
      Callick talks to critics too. Nobel Peace Laureate-turned-political prisoner Liu Xiaobo and artist and activist Ai Weiwei both cameo on the flip side of discussion around the Party. These countless voices give us a multi-dimensional perspective of the Communist Party.
     </p>
     <p>
      More informative than other works on this list,
      <em>
       Party Time
      </em>
      is probably best read for getting background info on the political sitch in China. Having said that, the political sitch is so complicated and overwhelming that its a good idea to read up on it before you head over.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #4
      <em>
       Shantaram
      </em>
       Gregory David Roberts
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      India
     </p>
     <img alt="shantsq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7448" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/shantsq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
      Anyone who has been to India can tell you it will change the way you see homelessness and poverty forever. Slums abound and the gap between rich and poor increases every day.
     </p>
     <p>
      But if youve ever wanted to get a taste of what its like to live in the slums, Roberts novel is the way to go. An Australian prison escapee, Roberts flees to India. There he lives in the slums, befriends locals and observes and immerses himself in the culture there. He learns how locals converse with one another and how conflicts are resolved. He witnesses outbreaks of cholera, firestorms and crime.
     </p>
     <p>
      Roberts rich experiences in Mumbai are deeply woven into the fabric of the book. Theres some
      <a href="http://www.shantaram.com/pages/interview_2.html">
       controversy
      </a>
      over the books depiction of events and people. Regardless, the feelings evoked by seeing everyday slum life through Roberts eyes  both colourful and tragic  are real.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #5
      <em>
       Istanbul: Memories and the City
      </em>
       Orhan Pamuk
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      Turkey
     </p>
     <img alt="istanbulsq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7449" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/istanbulsq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
      Pamuk is a hero in Turkey. When I went to Istanbul, there were posters promoting his new book like how Melbourne promoted the Emporium.
     </p>
     <p>
      His memoir perfectly captures Istanbul as a clash between two worlds: between the East and the West, and between the traditional and the modern. It has landmarks from Istanbul that youll want to visit: the Bosphorus and Beyoglu district. And youll learn a lot about Turkish history; the book is set when Turkey was trying to crack its way into the EU.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its also deeply personal, giving readers an insight into the life of a young man and the trajectory of his life between the 60s and the 80s. Pamuk intertwines stories from his own life  childhood dreams and family secrets  with the Ottoman villas and alleyways hidden in Istanbul.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #6
      <em>
       On The Road
      </em>
       Jack Kerouac
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      The USA
     </p>
     <img alt="ontheroadsq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7450" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/ontheroadsq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
     </p>
     <p>
      As aquintessential travel book,
      <em>
       On The Road
      </em>
      has inspired many a great American road trip (with detours a little south of the border).
     </p>
     <p>
      It follows narrator Sal Paradise and his restless maverick friend Dean Moriarty as they traverse across America  sometimes together, sometimes apart. Whenever they do cross paths, however, Deans spontaneity leads them to partying in ghost towns, sleeping with random women, and taking road trips in the middle of the night. Dean is the epitome of the travel bug. As Sal tells us, The bug was on me again, and the bugs name was Dean Moriarty.
     </p>
     <p>
      This book will make you want to road trip up and down the country, by road, by rail and by thumb. Kerouacs gritty tale will make you want to camp out in the south, party with fellow hitchhikers and drink whisky to stay warm overnight.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #7
      <em>
       In Patagonia
      </em>
       Bruce Chatwin
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      South America
     </p>
     <img alt="inpatagoniasq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7451" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/inpatagoniasq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
      Known as a bible to travellers through the loerhalf ofSouth America, Chatwins book will give you a historical insight into the American subcontinent. His language and frequent digressions (which mirror the meandering narrators ways) lure you into the depths of South America, where everything seems to be happening at once.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most compelling is his voice, which seduces readers as much as Patagonia itself.The book is structured into 97 separate section of varying length, diverting from the typical narrative and used to present a meditation on the nature of anytravellersnomadic life.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <h2>
      #8
      <em>
       In A Sunburned Country/Down Under
      </em>
       Bill Bryson
     </h2>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Destination:
      </strong>
      Australia
     </p>
     <img alt="sunburnedsq" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-7452" data-size-desktop-extra-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq.jpg" data-size-desktop-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq-1024x1024.jpg" data-size-desktop-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq-768x768.jpg" data-size-mobile-large="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq-680x680.jpg" data-size-mobile-medium="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq-480x480.jpg" data-size-mobile-small="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq-320x320.jpg" data-src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq.jpg" src="http://awol.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/sunburnedsq-320x320.jpg"/>
     <p>
      Many young Australians havent travelled much in their backyard  its what you do
      <em>
       after
      </em>
      youve covered the rest of the world. But if youve ever wanted to read more about Australia, or hear about it from an outsidersperspective, this is the book for you. Bryson is funny, observant and surprisingly accurate.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first part of the book covers his rail journey from Perth to Sydney via the
      <a href="http://www.railaustralia.com.au/indianPacific.php">
       Indian Pacific Rail
      </a>
      (who even knewthat was a thing?). Bryson recaps the stories of the first settlers in Australia  including a recurring joke that they drank their own urine. He also visits civilised Australia, which is the name he gives the big cities.
     </p>
     <p>
      As Bryson puts it so elegantly, This is a country that is at once staggeringly empty and yet packed with stuff. Interesting stuff, ancient stuff, stuff not readily explained. Stuff yet to be found. Trust me, this is an interesting place.
     </p>
     <p>
      The book is aneasy read that provides soundinsight into Australias place in the rest of the world. Seeing our country as a place of fascination certainly providesinspiration to explore more of this beautiful place, even for those who call Australia home.
     </p>

     </p>
