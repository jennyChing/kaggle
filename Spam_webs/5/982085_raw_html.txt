              <p>
               <span style="font-family: Theinhardt-web, 'Helvetica Neue', Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;">
                Gas stations are an intriguing building type. One could argue that they are really what make American Modernism possible, as they act as a network of fueling points for its car-based urbanism. Each station has to cover its pumps from the elements, and so very large canopies are required. Beyond shelter, however, these canopies are free to take myriad forms.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               Combine this freedom with the semiotics of highway advertising, and the stations of the mid-20th century became a
               <a href="https://www.laconservancy.org/locations/union-76-gas-station" target="_blank">
                Googie spectacular
               </a>
               of their own. Designers such as
               <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/national/archive/2011/07/frank-lloyd-wrights-forgotten-gas-station-of-the-future/242250/" target="_blank">
                Frank Lloyd Wright
               </a>
               and
               <a href="http://www.luckymojo.com/texacostar.html" target="_blank">
                Walter Dorwin Teague
               </a>
               (of Kodak Brownie fame
               <a href="">
               </a>
               ) have jumped into the mix, and even
               <a href="http://architizer.com/blog/montreal-architects-rescue-mies-van-der-rohe-gas-station-from-obscurity/" target="_blank">
                Mies van der Rohe
               </a>
               designed one.
              </p>
              <p>
               The unbridled spirit of the American car and roadside has been tampered by the now-obvious problems with a gas guzzling lifestyle, but that doesn't mean that gas stations have become completely boring  just check out these cool filling stations from the Architizer database.
              </p>
