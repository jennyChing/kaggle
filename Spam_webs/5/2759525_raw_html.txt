       <div class="body">
        <h2>
         Proving the Power of PR Through Metrics
        </h2>
        <div class="meta">
         Posted by
         <a href="http://onboardly.com/author/heather-ritchie/" title="View all posts by Heather Carson">
          Heather Carson
         </a>
         on
         <span class="entry-date">
          <a href="http://onboardly.com/startup-pr/proving-power-pr-metrics/" rel="bookmark">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2015-01-14T09:36:20-04:00">
            Jan, 14th 2015
           </time>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="byline">
         </span>
         |
         <a href="/startup-pr">
          Startup PR
         </a>
         |
         <a href="http://onboardly.com/startup-pr/proving-power-pr-metrics/#comments">
          1 Comment
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <img alt="PR Metics 0" class="aligncenter wp-image-6530" height="254" src="http://onboardly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/PR-Metics-0.png" width="846"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         We already know PR is powerful. Well-knownstatistics indicate that people trust earned media (press mentions, reviews, etc) far more than traditional advertising, and it's been proven to increase trust and inevitablyROI. But outside ofthese basic calculations - how can you best understandthe true impact of PR on your business? I'm glad you asked.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="PR Metrics 1" class="aligncenter wp-image-6531" height="503" src="http://onboardly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/PR-Metrics-1.png" width="879"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         On Friday, we published
         <a href="http://onboardly.com/startup-pr/pr-metrics-that-matter-infographic/" target="_blank" title="PR Metrics That Matter [Infographic]">
          our most recent infographic
         </a>
         dissectingwhat has long been a soft spotto many of us in the #StartupPR space:
         <strong>
          PR Metrics.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         I'll admit that getting my head around measuring the true impact of the PR opportunities we create was a little nerve wracking. I feared thatas soon as we started assigning a realistic value to each media mention, feature article, online or offline story Imightbe disappointed. Maybe I'd realize that some of our effort had been wasted over the years or that we hadn't always delivered on the expectations we'd set for our clients. You see, self-assessment is a double-edged sword. Ashumanswe
         <em>
          want
         </em>
         the feedback - as long as it's positive. But if a look under the hood shows that you'reless-than-perfect, it can be a pretty heavy blow.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="PR Metrics 2" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6533" height="614" src="http://onboardly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/PR-Metrics-2.png" width="999"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         As tempting as it would be to declare
         <em>
          "PR is a mystery! But it works!"
         </em>
         - that simply isn't the Onboardly way. Casting the fear of our possiblebruised egos aside, we beganmeasuring -recording everything. And with help from
         <a href="http://twitter.com/shanelle_mullin">
          Shanelle
         </a>
         , our Director of Marketing (she's an analytics whiz) - we've built a fairly robust PR measurement process.Allow me to explain.
        </p>
        <h2>
         What To Measure - Internal and External
        </h2>
        <p>
         For a broader
         <strong>
          internal assessment
         </strong>
         , we started keeping track of
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          hours spent on pitches
         </span>
         vs.
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          success in the media
         </span>
         (how much time/energydoes it take to get a journalist to say yes.) This helps us understand the amount of time (read: money) we're investing in each opportunity, but also demonstrates our
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          conversion rate
         </span>
         , if you will.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Externally
         </strong>
         , we needed to know what value each media mention, feature or story brings to the companieswe represent. We started by analyzing the following:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          the
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           traffic
          </span>
          these stories generate to our client sites,
         </li>
         <li>
          the probablebump in
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           SEO
          </span>
          (based on domain authority of the publication.), and
         </li>
         <li>
          general
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           awareness
          </span>
          (its reach; how many people are going to read/see the article over its lifespan.)
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           customer acquisition
          </span>
          (new customer signups, leads generatedor subscribers.)
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <img alt="PR Metrics 3" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6534" height="614" src="http://onboardly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/PR-Metrics-3.png" width="1001"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         While each of these are weighted differently - they all factor into assigning a value for our clients. Onceamalgamated, we were confident we'd begin to see trends in the types of media that perform better than others and eventually be able to predict a formula for slam-dunk success.
        </p>
        <h2>
         PR Packs a Powerful Punch
        </h2>
        <p>
         Still need more convincing? That's ok - we did too. To get a better handle on our new system, we started analyzing historical data we had on hand for several clients.
        </p>
        <p>
         One thingwe've learned is that aspirational,opinion-based content can yield pretty impressive results. One
         <a href="http://finance.yahoo.com/news/retiring-27-ambitious-lazy-crazy-140000847.html" target="_blank">
          client-penned piece
         </a>
         by Brenton Hayden(founder of Renters Warehouse) that comes to mindgenerated 82,000+ pageviews in one day. One day!
        </p>
        <p>
         An estimated 486,800 people saw the article on the first day of its publication through multiple syndications, which means approximately 16% of those readers ended up on their website that same day. The piece received 4,800+ comments in under a week and generated over 576 solid leads in under two weeks.
        </p>
        <p>
         Taking an example from the infographic,one eBook launch coordinated with a few well-timed media stories increased email leads for UK-based
         <a href="http://contactzilla.com" target="_blank">
          Contactzilla
         </a>
         by 1,227% in under two weeks. Following the launch, major content partners were lining up to work withthe client because of the sheer volume of awareness it generated in such a short period of time. A quick win, sure - but one that's had lasting impact from a business development perspective.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Short Term vs. Long Term Expectation Setting
        </h2>
        <p>
         <img alt="PR Metrics 4" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6535" height="531" src="http://onboardly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/PR-Metrics-4.png" width="1001"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Seeing both short term (huge spike in email subscribers) and long term (BD opportunities abound) successin Contactzilla's case reminds us that time needs to bea consideration. While it's tempting to want to measure the minute an article goes live, it's importantto consider the short and long term effects of media coverage. To do that, we went back and accessed additional client successes.
        </p>
        <p>
         For 15Five, aclient that we worked with for a span of 16 months through 2013/2014 - we still seethe same top two media mentions we secured for them consistentlygenerate over 22% of their referral traffic and 26% of all new visitor referrals.
        </p>
        <p>
         Through this exercise, whatwe found out was pretty impressive:
         <strong>
          PR has staying power.
         </strong>
         Unlike advertising which for the most part is here today, gone tomorrow - a great story told in a popularmedia channel can live a very long and usefullife.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="PR Metrics 5" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6536" height="274" src="http://onboardly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/PR-Metrics-5.png" width="1000"/>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         We still have a ton of work to do, but our mission is simple: To be the most transparent agency in the world as it relates to PR Metrics, to use the data we collect to help our clients be even more successful and to help make the industry a better place.
        </p>
