     <p>
      <b>
       Baumkuchen
      </b>
      is a German variety of
      <a href="/wiki/Spit_cake" title="Spit cake">
       spit cake
      </a>
      . It is a traditional pastry in many countries throughout Europe and is also a popular snack and dessert in Japan. The characteristic rings that appear when sliced resemble
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">
       tree rings
      </a>
      , and give the
      <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">
       cake
      </a>
      its
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      name,
      <i>
       Baumkuchen
      </i>
      , which translates to "tree cake".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheraton_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sheraton-1">
        <span>

        </span>
        1

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baumkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The origins of baumkuchen and where it was first baked are disputed. One theory is that baumkuchen was invented in the German town of
      <a href="/wiki/Salzwedel" title="Salzwedel">
       Salzwedel
      </a>
      , a story the town itself popularizes,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newyorker-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has been made there since at least the 19th century. Another theory suggests it began as a
      <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCrt%C5%91skal%C3%A1cs" title="Krtskalcs">
       Hungarian wedding cake
      </a>
      , which derives from the oldest Hungarian pastry
      <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCrt%C5%91skal%C3%A1cs" title="Krtskalcs">
       krtskalcs
      </a>
      (chimney cake). In
      <i>
       Ein neues Kochbuch
      </i>
      (lit. "A New Cookbook"), the first
      <a href="/wiki/Cookbook" title="Cookbook">
       cookbook
      </a>
      written for professional chefs by
      <a href="/wiki/Marx_Rumpolt" title="Marx Rumpolt">
       Marx Rumpolt
      </a>
      , there is a recipe for baumkuchen. This publication puts the origin of baumkuchen as far back as 1581, the year the cookbook was first published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oxford-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Marx Rumpolt had previously worked as a chef in Hungary and Bohemia. In 1682, a rural medic working for
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sigismund_Elsholtz" title="Johann Sigismund Elsholtz">
       Johann Sigismund Elsholtz
      </a>
      prepared a similar dish.
     </p>
     <p>
      Aside from its more recent history, baumkuchen can trace its roots back to
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       Ancient Greece
      </a>
      and then
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
       Rome
      </a>
      , with the Romans bringing the recipe for baking cakes on logs over an open fire to modern day Germany and the surrounding regions with their conquest of Northern Europe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakingsol_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bakingsol-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corsoela_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-corsoela-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      This labor-intensive specialty is made on a spit by brushing on even and thin layers of batter and then rotating the spit around a heat source, traditionally a wood fire. Each layer is allowed to brown before a new layer of batter is poured.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treecake_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-treecake-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the cake is removed and sliced, each layer is divided from the next by a golden line, resembling the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_rings" title="Tree rings">
       growth rings
      </a>
      on a crosscut tree. A typical baumkuchen is made up of 15 to 20 layers of batter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corsoela_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-corsoela-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the layering process for making baumkuchen can continue until the cakes are quite large. Skilled pastry chefs have been known to create cakes with 25 layers and weighing over 100 pounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treecake_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-treecake-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When cooked on a spit, it is not uncommon for a finished baumkuchen to be 3 to 4 feet tall.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-newyorker-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Baumkuchen ingredients are typically butter, eggs, sugar, vanilla, salt, and flour. Baking powder is not considered a traditional ingredient. The ratio of flour, butter and eggs is typically 1:1:2 respectively (i.e., 100 grams of
      <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">
       flour
      </a>
      , 100 grams of
      <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">
       butter
      </a>
      , and 200 grams of
      <a href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">
       eggs
      </a>
      ). The recipe can be varied by adding other ingredients, such as ground nuts, honey, marzipan, nougat and rum or brandy, to the batter or filling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakingsol_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-bakingsol-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, baumkuchen may be covered with sugar or chocolate
      <a href="/wiki/Glaze_(cooking_technique)" title="Glaze (cooking technique)">
       glaze
      </a>
      . With some recipes, the fully baked and cooled baumkuchen is first coated with
      <a href="/wiki/Marmalade" title="Marmalade">
       marmalade
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">
       jam
      </a>
      , and then covered with chocolate.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Baumkuchenspitzen">
       Baumkuchenspitzen
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baumkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Baumkuchenspitzen">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Baumkuchenspitzen, German for "tree cake points", are miniature versions of baumkuchen that are created from the cake when it is cut in slices and then into pieces that are referred to as "Spitzen". These pieces are typically coated in chocolate and sold individually.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Schichttorte">
       Schichttorte
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baumkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Schichttorte">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A simpler horizontally layered version of the cake called a "Schichttorte" also exists. It is baked without a spit and thus does not have circular rings but horizontal layers. The horizontally layered version results in a baumkuchen that is more like a conventional cake in shape. It can also be baked in a conventional household oven that has a
      <a href="/wiki/Grilling#Overhead_grilling" title="Grilling">
       broiler
      </a>
      inside, whereas the traditional spit version requires special equipment normally not available in an average household. However, unlike the spit variant, the Schichttorte cross section is less reminiscent of tree rings.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Baumkuchen_in_Japan">
       Baumkuchen in Japan
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baumkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Baumkuchen in Japan">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2585" data-file-width="4361" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG/190px-BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG/285px-BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG/380px-BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG 2x" width="190"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BaumkuchenKyoto.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Japanese baumkuchen.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Baumkuchen is one of the most popular pastries in Japan, where it is called
      <i>
       baumukhen
      </i>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">
         <span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">
          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </span>
      . It is a popular return present in Japan for wedding guests because of its typical
      <a href="/wiki/Wedding_ring" title="Wedding ring">
       ring
      </a>
      shape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It was first introduced to Japan by the German
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Joseph_Wilhelm_Juchheim" title="Karl Joseph Wilhelm Juchheim">
       Karl Joseph Wilhelm Juchheim
      </a>
      . Juchheim was in the Chinese city of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsingtao" title="Tsingtao">
       Tsingtao
      </a>
      during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World WarI
      </a>
      when Britain and Japan laid siege to Tsingtao. He and his wife were then interned at Okinawa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Juchheim started making and selling the traditional confection at a German exhibition in
      <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">
       Hiroshima
      </a>
      in 1919. After the war, he chose to remain in Japan. Continued success allowed him to move to
      <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">
       Yokohama
      </a>
      and open a bakery, but its destruction in the
      <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kant earthquake">
       1923 Great Kant earthquake
      </a>
      caused him to move his operations to
      <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">
       Kobe
      </a>
      , where he stayed until the end of
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World WarII
      </a>
      . Some years later, his wife returned to help a Japanese company open a chain of bakeries under the Juchheim name that further helped spread baumkuchen's popularity in Japan.
     </p>
