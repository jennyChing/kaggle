    Lucid dream
   </h1>

     <p>
      A
      <b>
       lucid dream
      </b>
      is any
      <a href="/wiki/Dream" title="Dream">
       dream
      </a>
      in which one is aware that one is dreaming. In relation to this phenomenon, Greek philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      observed: "often when one is asleep, there is something in consciousness which declares that what then presents itself is but a dream".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mavrematis1987_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mavrematis1987-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of the earliest references to personal experiences with lucid dreaming was by
      <a href="/wiki/Marie-Jean-L%C3%A9on,_Marquis_d%27Hervey_de_Saint_Denys" title="Marie-Jean-Lon, Marquis d'Hervey de Saint Denys">
       Marie-Jean-Lon, Marquis d'Hervey de Saint Denys
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bulkeley1999_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bulkeley1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The person most widely acknowledged as having coined the term is Dutch psychiatrist and writer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederik_Willem_van_Eeden" title="Frederik Willem van Eeden">
       Frederik (Willem) van Eeden
      </a>
      (18601932).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mavrematis1987_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mavrematis1987-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bulkeley1999_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bulkeley1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BayneCleeremans2009_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BayneCleeremans2009-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AngoffShapin1973_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AngoffShapin1973-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ParmeggianiVelluti2005_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ParmeggianiVelluti2005-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frederik_van_Eeden_1997_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Frederik_van_Eeden_1997-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a lucid dream, the dreamer has greater chances to exert some degree of control over their participation within the dream or be able to manipulate their imaginary experiences in the dream environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahan_1994_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kahan_1994-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor2005_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mayor2005-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpenceFodor1985_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SpenceFodor1985-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lucid dreams can be realistic and vivid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is shown that there are higher amounts of beta-1 frequency band (1319Hz) brain wave activity experienced by lucid dreamers, hence there is an increased amount of activity in the
      <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">
       parietal lobes
      </a>
      making lucid dreaming a conscious process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Skeptics of the phenomenon suggest that it is not a state of sleep, but of brief wakefulness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwartz_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-schwartz-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hartmann_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hartmann-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Others point out that there is no way to prove the truth of lucid dreaming other than to ask the dreamer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malcolm_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-malcolm-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lucid dreaming has been researched scientifically, with participants performing pre-determined physical responses while experiencing a lucid dream.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laberge-experiments1_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-laberge-experiments1-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_history">
       Scientific history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scientific history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first book to recognize the scientific potential of lucid dreams was
      <a href="/wiki/Marie-Jean-L%C3%A9on,_Marquis_d%27Hervey_de_Saint_Denys" title="Marie-Jean-Lon, Marquis d'Hervey de Saint Denys">
       Marquis d'Hervey de Saint-Denys's
      </a>
      1867
      <i>
       Les Reves et Les Moyens de Les Diriger: Observations Pratiques
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This French publication, originally published anonymously, translates as 'Dreams and the ways to direct them: practical observations'. It accounts for Saint-Denys' own experiences, but made also an extensive study of the phenomenon of lucid dreaming. Later, researcher
      <a href="/wiki/Celia_Green" title="Celia Green">
       Celia Green
      </a>
      's 1968 study
      <i>
       Lucid Dreams
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      analyzed the main characteristics of such dreams, reviewing previously published literature on the subject and incorporating new data from participants of her own. She concluded that lucid dreams were a category of experience quite distinct from ordinary dreams, and predicted that they would turn out to be associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Rapid_eye_movement_sleep" title="Rapid eye movement sleep">
       rapid eye movement sleep
      </a>
      (REM sleep). Green was also the first to link lucid dreams to the phenomenon of
      <a href="/wiki/False_awakening" title="False awakening">
       false awakenings
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Malcolm" title="Norman Malcolm">
       Norman Malcolm
      </a>
      's 1959 text
      <i>
       Dreaming
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      had argued against the possibility of checking the accuracy of dream reports. He points out "The only criterion of the truth of a statement that someone has had a certain dream is, essentially, his saying so.".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malcolm_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-malcolm-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The realization that eye movements performed in dreams may affect the dreamer's physical eyes provided a way to prove that actions agreed upon during waking life could be recalled and performed once lucid in a dream. The first evidence of this type was produced in the late 1970s by British
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parapsychologist" title="Parapsychologist">
       parapsychologist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Keith_Hearne" title="Keith Hearne">
       Keith Hearne
      </a>
      . A volunteer named Alan Worsley used eye movements to signal the onset of lucidity, which were recorded by a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysomnograph" title="Polysomnograph">
       polysomnograph
      </a>
      machine.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hearne's results were not widely distributed. The first
      <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">
       peer-reviewed
      </a>
      article was published some years later by
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_LaBerge" title="Stephen LaBerge">
       Stephen LaBerge
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
       Stanford University
      </a>
      , who had independently developed a similar technique as part of his doctoral dissertation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the 1980s, further scientific evidence of lucid dreaming was produced as lucid dreamers were able to demonstrate to researchers that they were consciously aware of being in a dream state (again, primarily using eye movement signals).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, techniques were developed that have been experimentally proven to enhance the likelihood of achieving this state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Paul Tholey, an oneirologist and Gestalt theorist laid the epistemological basis for the research of lucid dreams. His work laid the foreground for further researchers to categorize what a lucid dream is. Tholey (1980, 1981) defined seven different conditions of clarity that a dream must fulfill in order to be defined as a lucid dream:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Awareness of the dream state (orientation);
      </li>
      <li>
       Awareness of the capacity to make decisions;
      </li>
      <li>
       Awareness of memory functions;
      </li>
      <li>
       Awareness of self;
      </li>
      <li>
       Awareness of the dream environment;
      </li>
      <li>
       Awareness of the meaning of the dream;
      </li>
      <li>
       Awareness of concentration and focus (the subjective clarity of that state).
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      For a dream to be lucid as defined by Tholey, it must fulfill all 7 factors together. Tholey replaces the word Klarheit (clarity) with the word 
      <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">
       awareness
      </a>
      , which is a well known and central term in
      <a href="/wiki/Gestalt_therapy" title="Gestalt therapy">
       Gestalt therapy
      </a>
      theory and describes the subjective experience of the conscious dream state quite well (Lucid dreaming  dreams of clarity).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Initiation">
       Initiation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initiation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A lucid dream can begin in one of many ways. A dream-initiated lucid dream (D.I.L.D.) starts as a normal dream, and the dreamer eventually concludes it is a dream. A wake-initiated lucid dream (W.I.L.D.) occurs when the dreamer goes from a normal waking state directly into a dream state, with no apparent lapse in consciousness. The wake-initiated lucid dream occurs when the sleeper enters REM sleep with unbroken self-awareness directly from the waking state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      "Induction of self awareness in dreams through frontal low current stimulation of gamma activity", by Ursula Voss et al., was published online 11 May 2014 in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nature_Neuroscience" title="Nature Neuroscience">
        Nature Neuroscience
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A popular science report of this article at the
      <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">
       website
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFLS" title="IFLS">
       IFLS
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      noted that "The authors see potential for lucid dreaming to help people with post traumatic stress disorder (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PTSD" title="PTSD">
       PTSD
      </a>
      ) or otherwise prone to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nightmares" title="Nightmares">
       nightmares
      </a>
      to get their fears under control."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-lucid_dream">
       Pre-lucid dream
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-lucid dream">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Pre-lucid_dream" title="Pre-lucid dream">
       Pre-lucid dream
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A pre-lucid dream is one in which the dreamer considers the question, "Am I asleep and dreaming?" The dreamer may or may not come to the correct conclusion. Such experiences are liable to occur to people who are deliberately cultivating lucid dreams, but may also occur spontaneously to those with no prior intention to achieve lucidity in dreams.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_clinical_applications">
       Research and clinical applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research and clinical applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Neurobiological_model">
       Neurobiological model
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neurobiological model">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">
       Neuroscientist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Hobson" title="Allan Hobson">
       J. Allan Hobson
      </a>
      has hypothesized what might be occurring in the brain while lucid. The first step to lucid dreaming is recognizing one is dreaming.
      <span class="citation-needed-content" style="background-color: #fff9f9; color: DarkSlateGray; border: 1px solid #ffdcdc;">
       This recognition might occur in the
       <a href="/wiki/Dorsolateral_prefrontal_cortex" title="Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex">
        dorsolateral prefrontal cortex
       </a>
       , which is one of the few areas deactivated during REM sleep and where
       <a href="/wiki/Working_memory" title="Working memory">
        working memory
       </a>
       occurs.
      </span>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Once this area is activated and the recognition of dreaming occurs, the dreamer must be cautious to let the dream continue but be conscious enough to remember that it is a dream. While maintaining this balance, the
      <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">
       amygdala
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parahippocampal_cortex" title="Parahippocampal cortex">
       parahippocampal cortex
      </a>
      might be less intensely activated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To continue the intensity of the dream hallucinations, it is expected the
      <a href="/wiki/Pons" title="Pons">
       pons
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">
       parieto-occipital junction
      </a>
      stay active.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a study performed by Stephen LaBerge, four subjects were compared either singing while dreaming or counting while dreaming. LaBerge found that the right hemisphere was more active during singing and the left hemisphere was more active during counting. These results resemble similar findings in subjects that were awake.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This study was a precursor to the question LaBerge has always tried to answer, is there a brain state for lucid dreaming? Physiological recordings like electroencephalograms (EEG) have shown that lucid dreams begin in the Rapid Eye Movement (REM) stage of sleep.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In fact, multiple studies led by LaBerge have only shown lucid dreams to occur in the REM stage. Nonetheless it is not impossible that lucid dreams could occur in any of the other sleep stages, which is why physiological tests are highly valuable to this topic. Polysomnography is another beneficial physiological study to conduct because it collects data from a number of sources, inevitably compounding all the information to give the researcher a well-rounded understanding of the human physiology while an individual is experiencing a lucid dream. Polysomnography records your brain waves, the oxygen level in your blood, heart rate and breathing, eye movements, leg movements, and many other variables during the study.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Via polysomnographic technology LaBerge, Levitan and Dement (1986) compared eye movements, heart rate, blood pressure and skin potential in both lucid and non-lucid dreams and showed that lucid dreams occurred in the REM stage characterized by increased physiological activation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment_for_nightmares">
       Treatment for nightmares
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Treatment for nightmares">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It has been suggested that sufferers of
      <a href="/wiki/Nightmare" title="Nightmare">
       nightmares
      </a>
      could benefit from the ability to be aware they are indeed dreaming. A pilot study was performed in 2006 that showed that lucid dreaming therapy treatment was successful in reducing nightmare frequency. This treatment consisted of exposure to the idea, mastery of the technique, and lucidity exercises. It was not clear what aspects of the treatment were responsible for the success of overcoming nightmares, though the treatment as a whole was successful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Australian psychologist Milan Colic has explored the application of principles from
      <a href="/wiki/Narrative_therapy" title="Narrative therapy">
       narrative therapy
      </a>
      with clients' lucid dreams, to reduce the impact not only of nightmares during sleep, but also depression, self-mutilation, and other problems in waking life. Colic found that clients preferred direction for their lives, as identified during therapeutic conversations, could lessen the distressing content of dreams, while understandings about lifeand even charactersfrom lucid dreams could be invoked in "real" life with marked therapeutic benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Psychotherapists have applied lucid dreaming as an application for therapy. Studies have shown that by inducing a lucid dream recurrent nightmares can be alleviated. This alleviation is unclear whether it is due to lucidity or the ability to alter the dream itself. A study performed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Victor_Spoormaker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Victor Spoormaker (page does not exist)">
       Victor Spoormaker
      </a>
      <span class="noprint" style="font-size: smaller; font-style: normal;">
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="//nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Spoormaker" title="nl:Victor Spoormaker">
        nl
       </a>
       )
      </span>
      and van den Bout (2006) evaluated the validity of lucid dreaming treatment (LDT) in chronic nightmare sufferers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      LDT is composed of exposure, mastery, and lucidity exercises. Results of lucid dreaming treatment revealed that the nightmare frequency of the treatment groups had decreased. In another study, Spoormaker, van den Bout, and Meijer (2003) investigated lucid dreaming treatment for nightmares by testing 8 subjects who received a one-hour individual session, which consisted of lucid dreaming exercises.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The results of the study revealed that the nightmare frequency had decreased and the sleep quality had slightly increased. Holzinger, Klsch and Saletu managed a psychotherapy study under the working name of Cognition during dreaming  a therapeutic intervention in nightmares, which included 40 subjects, men and women, 1850 years old, whose life quality was significantly altered by nightmares.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The test subjects were administered Gestalt group therapy and 24 of them were also taught to enter the state of lucid dreaming by Holzinger. This was purposefully taught in order to change the course of their nightmares. The subjects then reported the diminishment of their nightmare prevalence from 2-3 times a week to 2-3 times per month.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Perception_of_time">
       Perception of time
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Perception of time">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1985, Stephen LaBerge performed a pilot study that showed that
      <a href="/wiki/Time_perception" title="Time perception">
       time perception
      </a>
      while counting during a lucid dream is about the same as during waking life. Lucid dreamers counted out ten seconds while dreaming, signaling the start and the end of the count with a pre-arranged eye signal measured with
      <a href="/wiki/Electrooculography" title="Electrooculography">
       electrooculogram
      </a>
      recording.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laberge-experiments2_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-laberge-experiments2-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      LaBerge's results were confirmed by German researchers in 2004. The German study, by D. Erlacher and M. Schredl, also studied motor activity and found deep knee bends took 44% longer to perform while lucid dreaming.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erlacher-experiments_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-erlacher-experiments-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Awareness_and_reasoning">
       Awareness and reasoning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awareness and reasoning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      While dream control and dream awareness are correlated, neither requires the otherLaBerge found dreams that exhibit one clearly without the capacity for the other; also, in some dreams where the dreamer is lucid and aware they could exercise control, they choose simply to observe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahan_1994_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kahan_1994-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1992, a study by
      <a href="/wiki/Deirdre_Barrett" title="Deirdre Barrett">
       Deirdre Barrett
      </a>
      examined whether lucid dreams contained four "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corollaries" title="Corollaries">
       corollaries
      </a>
      " of lucidity:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The dreamer is aware that they are dreaming
      </li>
      <li>
       Objects disappear after waking
      </li>
      <li>
       Physical laws need not apply in the dream
      </li>
      <li>
       The dreamer has a clear memory of the waking world
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Barrett found less than a quarter of lucidity accounts exhibited all four. A related and reciprocal category of dreams that are lucid in terms of some of these four corollaries, but miss the realization that "I'm dreaming," were also reported. Scores on these corollaries and correctly identifying the experience as a dream increased with lucidity experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a later study in Barrett's book,
      <i>
       The Committee of Sleep
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrett_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Barrett-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      she describes how some experienced lucid dreamers have learned to remember specific practical goals such as artists looking for inspiration seeking a show of their own work once they become lucid or computer programmers looking for a screen with their desired code. However, most of these dreamers had many experiences of failing to recall waking objectives before gaining this level of control.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrett_46-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Barrett-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, a case study published in 2012 suggests that during a lucid dream the bilateral frontopolar area of the brain (usually attributed to the evaluation of ones own thoughts and feelings etc.) undergoes an increase in activity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Skepticism">
       Skepticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Skepticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to Dr. Patrick McNamara of
      <a href="/wiki/Boston_University_School_of_Medicine" title="Boston University School of Medicine">
       Boston University
      </a>
      , there is no scientific way to know for certain that someone is dreaming other than to wake them up and ask them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Professor
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Malcolm" title="Norman Malcolm">
       Norman Malcolm
      </a>
      goes on to say that the only criterion of the truth of a statement that someone has had a certain dream is the dreamer saying so. Malcolm further describes lucid dreaming as absurd and impossible, recalling as an example, "I dreamt that I realised I was dreaming, dreamt that I was affecting the course of my dream, and then dreamt that I woke myself up by telling myself to wake up."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malcolm_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-malcolm-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other researchers have described the phenomenon of lucid dreaming not as a part of sleep, but as a brief wakeful state, or "micro-awakening".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwartz_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-schwartz-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hartmann_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hartmann-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Experiments by
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_LaBerge" title="Stephen LaBerge">
       Stephen LaBerge
      </a>
      used "perception of the outside world" as a criterion for
      <a href="/wiki/Wakefulness" title="Wakefulness">
       wakefulness
      </a>
      while studying lucid dreamers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laberge-experiments1_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-laberge-experiments1-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although their sleep state was corroborated with physiological measurements, LaBerge admits the criterion is subjective.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Physiologically, brain activity during REM sleep is similar to wakefulness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harvard-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Hobson" title="Allan Hobson">
       John Allan Hobson
      </a>
      illustrates the ambiguity of these experiments, as LaBerge's subjects always experienced their lucid dream while in a state of REM. Hobson concludes that lucid dreaming is a state of both waking and dreaming.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fellow dream researcher Michael Schredl found Hobson's conclusion to be over-simplifying, saying that the physiological state of lucid dreamers appears to be closer to other states of consciousness, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">
       meditation
      </a>
      , than to wakefulness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schredl_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-schredl-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_history">
       Cultural history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:289px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1215" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg/287px-Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg/431px-Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg/574px-Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg 2x" width="287"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Waterhouse-sleep_and_his_half-brother_death-1874.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Hypnos" title="Hypnos">
         Hypnos
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Thanatos" title="Thanatos">
         Thanatos
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         Sleep and His Half-Brother Death
        </i>
        , an 1874 painting by
        <a href="/wiki/John_William_Waterhouse" title="John William Waterhouse">
         John William Waterhouse
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Even though it has only come to the attention of the general public in the last few decades, lucid dreaming is not a modern discovery. Lucid dreaming is the Western term used to denote a practice similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Yoga_nidra" title="Yoga nidra">
       Yoga nidra
      </a>
      . The distinguishing difference is the degree to which one remains cognizant of the actual physical environment as opposed to a dream environment. In lucid dreaming, we are only (or mainly) cognizant of the dream environment, and have little or no awareness of our actual environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The concept of Yoga nidra is very ancient in Indian traditions such as Hinduism and Buddhism.
      <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">
       Krishna
      </a>
      is often associated with Yoga nidra in epic
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">
        Mahabharata
       </a>
      </i>
      . Similarly, many yogis and rishis are supposed to have experienced Yoga nidra throughout their life. In modern times, Yoga nidra was experienced by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swami_Satyananda_Saraswati" title="Swami Satyananda Saraswati">
       Swami Satyananda Saraswati
      </a>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="no reliable source (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      when he was living with his guru
      <a href="/wiki/Sivananda_Saraswati" title="Sivananda Saraswati">
       Swami Sivananda
      </a>
      in Rishikesh. He began studying the tantric scriptures and, after practice, constructed a system of relaxation, which he began popularizing in the mid 20th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.28Swami.291974_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-.28Swami.291974-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He explained yoga nidra as a state of mind between wakefulness and sleep that opened deep phases of the mind, suggesting a connection with the ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Tantra" title="Tantra">
       tantric
      </a>
      practice called nyasa, whereby
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mantras" title="Mantras">
       mantras
      </a>
      are mentally placed within specific body parts, while meditating on each part (of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bodymind" title="Bodymind">
       bodymind
      </a>
      ). The form of practice taught by Swami Satyananda includes eight stages (Internalisation,
      <a href="/wiki/Sankalpa_(Hindu_thought)" title="Sankalpa (Hindu thought)">
       Sankalpa
      </a>
      , Rotation of Consciousness, Breath Awareness, Manifestation of Opposites, Creative Visualization and Externalisation).
     </p>
     <p>
      Also, in early
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      it was a common practice among people in the monastic community. As preserved in the ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Sarvastivada" title="Sarvastivada">
       Sarvastivada
      </a>
      school's Sutra on
      <a href="/wiki/Mindfulness" title="Mindfulness">
       Mindfulness
      </a>
      of the Body in the Madhayama agama (equivalent of Pali Kayagatasati) it states that monks and nuns under practice should be 'Understanding (having awareness in) the four postures and states of being asleep or awake'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Documented since the 8th century,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhist" title="Tibetan Buddhist">
       Tibetan Buddhists
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonpo" title="Bonpo">
       Bonpo
      </a>
      were practicing a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Dream_yoga" title="Dream yoga">
       dream yoga
      </a>
      held to maintain full waking consciousness while in the dream state. One important message of the book is the distinction between the
      <a href="/wiki/Dzogchen" title="Dzogchen">
       Dzogchen
      </a>
      meditation of
      <i>
       awareness
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       dream yoga
      </i>
      . The Dzogchen
      <i>
       awareness
      </i>
      meditation has also been referred to by the terms
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rigpa" title="Rigpa">
        rigpa
       </a>
       awareness
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       contemplation
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       presence
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Awareness
      </i>
      during the sleep and dream states is associated with the Dzogchen
      <i>
       practice of natural light
      </i>
      . This practice only achieves lucid dreams as a secondary effectin contrast to dream yoga, which aims primarily at lucid dreaming. According to Buddhist teachers, the experience of lucidity helps us understand the unreality of phenomena, which would otherwise be overwhelming during dream or the death experience.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Western culture, the phenomenon had been referred to by Greek philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      who had written: "often when one is asleep, there is something in consciousness which declares that what then presents itself is but a dream".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mavrematis1987_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mavrematis1987-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The physician
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galen_of_Pergamon" title="Galen of Pergamon">
       Galen of Pergamon
      </a>
      used lucid dreams in his therapy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also in a letter written by
      <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">
       St. Augustine of Hippo
      </a>
      in 415 AD about a story of a dreamer, Doctor Gennadius, refers to lucid dreaming.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The dreamer reported that he didn't realize he was in the dream world but the man whom he met in his dream reminded him about this and pointed out that his experience was a proof of life after death.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EedenSaintDenys.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="363" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EedenSaintDenys.jpg/300px-EedenSaintDenys.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9d/EedenSaintDenys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9d/EedenSaintDenys.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EedenSaintDenys.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Frederik_van_Eeden" title="Frederik van Eeden">
         Frederik van Eeden
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Marie-Jean-L%C3%A9on,_Marquis_d%27Hervey_de_Saint_Denys" title="Marie-Jean-Lon, Marquis d'Hervey de Saint Denys">
         Marquis d'Hervey de Saint Denys
        </a>
        , pioneers of lucid dreaming.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An early recorded lucid dreamer was the philosopher and physician
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Browne" title="Thomas Browne">
       Sir Thomas Browne
      </a>
      (16051682). Browne was fascinated by the world of dreams and described his own ability to lucid dream in his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Religio_Medici" title="Religio Medici">
        Religio Medici
       </a>
      </i>
      : "...yet in one dream I can compose a whole Comedy, behold the action, apprehend the jests and laugh my self awake at the conceits thereof".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly,
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">
       Samuel Pepys
      </a>
      in his diary entry for 15 August 1665
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      records a dream "that I had my Lady Castlemayne in my arms and was admitted to use all the dalliance I desired with her, and then dreamt that this could not be awake, but that it was only a dream".
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hervey_de_Saint-Denys" title="Hervey de Saint-Denys">
       Marquis d'Hervey de Saint-Denys
      </a>
      argued that it is possible for anyone to learn to dream consciously. In 1867, he published his book
      <i>
       Les Rves et les moyens de les diriger; observations pratiques
      </i>
      ("Dreams and How to Guide them; Practical Observations"), in which he documented more than twenty years of his own research into dreams.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       lucid dreaming
      </i>
      was coined by
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Dutch
      </a>
      author and
      <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrist" title="Psychiatrist">
       psychiatrist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Frederik_van_Eeden" title="Frederik van Eeden">
       Frederik van Eeden
      </a>
      in his 1913 article
      <i>
       "A Study of Dreams"
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frederik_van_Eeden_1997_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Frederik_van_Eeden_1997-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This paper was highly anecdotal and not embraced by the scientific community. Some consider this a misnomer because it means much more than just "clear or vivid" dreaming.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The alternative term
      <i>
       conscious dreaming
      </i>
      avoids this confusion. However, the term
      <i>
       lucid
      </i>
      was used by van Eeden in its sense of "having insight", as in the phrase
      <i>
       a lucid interval
      </i>
      applied to someone in temporary remission from a
      <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">
       psychosis
      </a>
      , rather than as a reference to the perceptual quality of the experience, which may or may not be clear and vivid.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1950s, the
      <a href="/wiki/Senoi" title="Senoi">
       Senoi
      </a>
      hunter-gatherers of
      <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
       Malaysia
      </a>
      were reported to make extensive use of lucid dreaming to ensure mental health, although later studies refuted these claims.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2012 report by the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      claimed that "interest in lucid dreaming has grown in recent years", and corroborated this with examples of the many
      <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">
       smartphone apps
      </a>
      that exist to help people experience the phenomenon. One such app was downloaded half a million times in six weeks, the report says.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rapid_eye_movement_.28REM.29">
       Rapid eye movement (REM)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rapid eye movement (REM)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Rapid_eye_movement_sleep" title="Rapid eye movement sleep">
       Rapid eye movement sleep
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      When a person is dreaming, the eyes shift rapidly. Scientific research has found that these eye movements may correspond to the direction the dreamer "looks" at in the dreamscape. This has enabled trained lucid dreamers to communicate with researchers while dreaming by using eye movement signals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laberge-experiments2_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-laberge-experiments2-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="False_awakening">
       False awakening
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: False awakening">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/False_awakening" title="False awakening">
       False awakening
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In a false awakening, one dreams of having awoken. The room the dreamer falsely awakens in is often similar to the room he/she fell asleep in. If the person was lucid, they often believe that they are no longer dreaming and begin their morning routine. The dreamer remains naive to the dream either until they realize they haven't actually woken up or until they wake up again (whether false or real).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_paralysis">
       Sleep paralysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sleep paralysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Sleep_paralysis" title="Sleep paralysis">
       Sleep paralysis
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      During sleep the body paralyzes itself as a protection mechanism to prevent the movements that occur in the dream from causing the physical body to move. However, this mechanism can be triggered before, during, or after normal sleep while the brain awakens. This can lead to a state where the awakened sleeper feels paralyzed.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypnagogic" title="Hypnagogic">
       Hypnagogic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">
       hallucination
      </a>
      may occur in this state, especially auditory ones. Effects of sleep paralysis include heaviness or inability to move the muscles, rushing or pulsating noises, and brief hypnogogic or
      <a href="/wiki/Hypnopompic" title="Hypnopompic">
       hypnopompic
      </a>
      imagery.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Out-of-body_experience">
       Out-of-body experience
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Out-of-body experience">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Out-of-body_experience" title="Out-of-body experience">
       Out-of-body experience
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An out-of-body experience (OBE or sometimes OOBE) is an experience that typically involves a sensation of floating outside of one's body and, in some cases, perceiving one's physical
      <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">
       body
      </a>
      from a place outside one's body (
      <a href="/wiki/Autoscopy" title="Autoscopy">
       autoscopy
      </a>
      ). About one in ten people claim to have had an out-of-body experience at some time in their lives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scientists are learning about the phenomenon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_64-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bbc-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some work by neurologists suggests that such experiences are generated by the same brain mechanisms that cause lucid dreams.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livescience2012_65-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-livescience2012-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite some similarities in their phenomenology and induction methods, EEG studies do not suggest an equivalence between OBEs and lucid dreams. Lucidity is strongly associated with stage 1 REM sleep but OBEs are far less consistent, producing EEG traces that can variously resemble stage 3 sleep, a waking, eyes-closed state or other uncategorized states.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, while this may suggest that perceived OBEs are a type of lucid dream which takes place in a dream environment that mimics the actual environment of the dreamer, this falls short of supporting the idea that some conscious form of the dreamer actually leaves the body and perceives their external environment while still in a sleeping state.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence">
       Prevalence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lucid_dream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Prevalence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table cellspacing="4" class="toccolours" style="float: left; margin-left: 0em; margin-right: 1em; font-size: 94%; background:#white; color:black; width:30em; max-width: 30%;">
      <tr>
       <td style="text-align: left;">
        "We are asleep. Our life is a dream. But we wake up, sometimes, just enough to know that we are dreaming."
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td style="text-align: left;">
        
        <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">
         Ludwig Wittgenstein
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
         <a href="#cite_note-67">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          67
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      During most dreams, the dreamer is not aware they are dreaming, no matter how absurd or eccentric the dream is. The reason for this has not been determined, and does not have an obvious answer. There have been attempts by various fields of psychology to provide an explanation. For example, some proponents of
      <a href="/wiki/Depth_psychology" title="Depth psychology">
       depth psychology
      </a>
      suggest that mental processes inhibit the critical evaluation of reality within dreams.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Certain
      <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">
       physiological
      </a>
      studies suggest that "seeing is believing" to the brain during any mental state. If the brain perceives something with great clarity or intensity, it will believe that it is real, even when asleep.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dream consciousness is similar to that of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallucinating" title="Hallucinating">
       hallucinating
      </a>
      awake subject. Dreams or hallucinatory images triggered by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_stem" title="Brain stem">
       brain stem
      </a>
      are considered to be real, even if fantasy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The impulse to accept the experience as real is so strong the dreamer will often invent a memory or a story to cover up an incongruous or unrealistic event in the dream. For example, "That man has two heads!" is not usually followed with "I must be dreaming!" but with something like "Yes, I read in the paper about these famous conjoined twins." Other times there will be an explanation that, in the dream, makes sense and seems very logical. However, when the dreamer awakes, he/she will realize that it is rather far-fetched or even completely absurd.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
