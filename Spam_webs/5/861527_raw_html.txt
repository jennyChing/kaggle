           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             SPF Fact vs. Fiction
            </h2>
            <p>
             We lecture you a lot about the importance of wearing SPF every day  and now theres even more proof that not slathering on the sunscreen religiously can lead to skin cancer. A new study published in the Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers &amp; Prevention journal found that women who had at least five sunburns between the ages of 15 and 20 had an
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/news/five-more-bad-sunburns-while-young-linked-higher-melanoma-risk/">
              80 percent increased risk of melanoma
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             Given the
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/skin-cancer-photos/signs-of-skin-cancer.aspx">
              scary stats on skin cancer
             </a>
             , its essential that you know how to apply SPF properly to ensure youre as protected as possible from UV damage. Top dermatologists debunk common sunscreen misconceptions so that you can make smart sun decisions all year long.
            </p>
           </span>
          </span>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: The higher the SPF, the better.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             There is very little difference in an SPF 30 or 50 or 100 in the total percentage of ultraviolet light blocked, says Elizabeth Tanzi, MD, co-director of the Washington Institute of Dermatologic Laser Surgery in Washington, DC. It is far better to use an SPF 30 appropriately than an SPF 50 without reapplying. To get enough coverage, follow these rules: Apply two ounces (about a shot glass worth) of
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/news/sunscreen-application-tips/">
              sunscreen
             </a>
             30 minutes before you go outside and reapply every two hours.
            </p>
           </span>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: Makeup with SPF offers adequate sun protection.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             Using
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/skin-and-beauty/5-sunscreen-shopping-rules-for-younger-healthier-skin.aspx">
              makeup with SPF
             </a>
             doesnt offer enough protection because makeup is usually applied unevenly onto the skin, says David Bank, MD, the director of the Center for Dermatology, Cosmetic, and Laser Surgery in Mount Kisco, New York. Whats more, the SPF in your makeup only last two hours, says Macrene Alexiades-Armenakas, MD, a New York City-based dermatologist and the founder of the anti-aging skin care line 37 Extreme Actives. You can still use your favorite foundation or powder with SPF  just layer it on over your sunscreen. Doing this will double your chances of protecting your skin, says Bank.
            </p>
           </span>
          </span>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: You shouldn't wear sunscreen because the ingredients can cause cancer.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             While this is a hot topic right now, theres no strong scientific study linking the
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/beauty-pictures/the-truth-about-organic-skin-care.aspx#/slide-6">
              ingredients in sunscreen
             </a>
             to cancer, says Tanzi. However, she says, there are many studies linking excessive sun exposure to skin cancer, so the answer to this argument is simple: wear sunscreen. It's a no-brainer. Not all sunscreens have chemical ingredients, so if you are worried, choose a chemical-free, physical formula with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide instead, says Bank.
            </p>
           </span>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: You dont need to use sunscreen everywhere.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             You may be
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/beauty-pictures/surprising-spots-that-need-sunscreen.aspx">
              overlooking areas
             </a>
             and increasing your risk for skin cancer. I often find the most worrisome moles on the palms and soles, says Alexiades-Amrenakas. For starters, these areas have far less pigment  they dont tan  and second, people rarely put sunscreen on their palms and soles, so they get unprotected intense exposure. Alexiades-Amrenakas recommends saving the more expensive sunscreens for your face and using a cheaper one on your body to make sure youre actually applying enough. People tend to under-apply if they use a high-priced sunscreen, she says.
            </p>
           </span>
          </span>
         </li>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: Kids' sunscreen is gentler.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             Both kids and adult sunscreens contain the same active ingredients, they just have different formulations, explains Tanzi. Adult sunscreens tend to be more cosmetically elegant  they go on sheer and invisible  whereas kid sunscreens can be a little thicker and more opaque, since kids usually dont care, she says. Many kids' sunscreens are formulated to be tear-free so they wont sting your eyes. If you
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/allergies/are-you-allergic-to-sunscreen.aspx">
              find sunscreen irritating
             </a>
             , opt for a physical formulation with the active ingredients zinc or titanium dioxide, advises Tanzi. Physical sunscreens are always the better option for sensitive skin because there are no chemicals that can react, she says.
            </p>
           </span>
          </span>
         </li>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: Lotion sunscreen works better than the spray kind.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             There is no real difference  it depends on what you prefer in terms of how it feels on your skin, says Bank. If you find lotions to be sticky, you might be inclined to apply less than you should because you don't like the texture, says Alexiades-Amrenakas. In that case, opt for a spray. The speed of application of
             <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/skin-and-beauty/0703/fda-looks-into-safety-of-aerosol-sunscreens.aspx">
              spray-on sunscreens
             </a>
             translates into faster, more efficient coverage, which is very helpful if you are already at the beach and need to apply, she says. Just make sure youre using enough  the entire sprayed area should be wet with product, says Tanzi.
            </p>
           </span>

           <span class="slide-copy">
            <h2 itemprop="name">
             Myth: You dont have to reapply water-resistant SPF.
            </h2>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Reality:
             </strong>
             The
             <a href="http://www.fda.gov/forconsumers/consumerupdates/ucm258416.htm">
              FDAs newest guidelines
             </a>
             state that any water-resistance claims must include how much time you can expect to be protected while swimming or sweating  either 40 or 80 minutes, says Bank. Under the new guidelines, which went into effect in June 2012, sunscreen manufacturers can no longer claim their products are waterproof or sweatproof.
            </p>
