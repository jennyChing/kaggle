         <h1 class="articleHeadline" itemprop="headline">
          Scientists Link Protein Fragments to Alzheimer's
         </h1>
         <h6 class="byline">
          <span itemprop="author creator" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
           <span itemprop="name">
            By NATALIE ANGIER
           </span>
          </span>
         </h6>
         <h6 class="dateline">
          Published: May 29, 1990
         </h6>
         <div class="shareTools shareToolsThemeClassic articleShareToolsTop" data-description="LEAD: A TINY shard of a protein so biochemically tough that it can hardly be dissolved, even in a test tube, may be at the heart of the extreme neurological degeneration and related symptoms of Alzheimer's disease." data-shares="facebook,twitter,google,email,showall|Share,print,singlepage,reprints,ad" data-title="Scientists Link Protein Fragments to Alzheimer's" data-url="http://www.nytimes.com/1990/05/29/science/scientists-link-protein-fragments-to-alzheimer-s.html">
         </div>
         <meta content="oH0bwEnEmCO4bae7yt7YPg" name="emailThisHash"/>
         <div class="articleBody">
          <span itemid="http://www.nytimes.com" itemprop="copyrightHolder provider sourceOrganization" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Organization">
           <meta content="The New York Times Company" itemprop="name"/>
           <meta content="http://www.nytco.com/" itemprop="url"/>
           <meta content="NYSE NYT" itemprop="tickerSymbol"/>
          </span>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           A TINY shard of a protein so biochemically tough that it can hardly be dissolved, even in a test tube, may be at the heart of the extreme neurological degeneration and related symptoms of Alzheimer's disease.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           In a series of papers published over the last few weeks in the journal Science and The Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, and in reports that will be presented at a meeting in Toronto in July, scientists are casting important new light on the strange protein fragment, known as beta amyloid, and how a few small errors in protein metabolism may spawn a chain reaction of cell death. Although some researchers reserve judgment, others predict the insights could result in new therapies against the disease.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           The latest work strongly suggests that the abnormal accretion of the protein fragments into stiff, rope-like patches throughout the brain is either a seminal event or one of the significant steps in the development of Alzheimer's. Many of the researchers believe that these amyloid plaques destroy the surrounding brain tissue, thereby contributing to memory loss, confusion and other hallmarks of the progressive dementia.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Although researchers have known for some time that the plaques accumulate in the brains of people with Alzheimer's disease, they are now able to track the biochemical stages of their formation. They have gained new clues into the role of the normal protein from which amyloid pieces break off, and they are learning how the presence of just a few protein tatters in the brain may trigger production of beta-amyloid plaques.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Similarity to Brillo Pads
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           ''In the past year or two, I have seen a lot of my colleagues who were initially skeptical become convinced that amyloid deposits are important'' to Alzheimer's disease, said Dr. Blas Frangione, professor of pathology and an amyloid researcher at New York University Medical Center.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Researchers first were drawn to study beta amyloid about six years ago, following the discovery of microscopic patches of what looked like hemp fibers in the autopsied brains of Alzheimer's patients. When subjected to biochemical manipulations, the plaques proved surprisingly resistant to degradation. ''If you could hold them in your hand, they would feel like Brillo pads,'' said Dr. Ralph A. Nixon, associate professor of psychiatry and an amyloid expert at Harvard Medical School and McLean Hospital in Boston.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Similar plaques also were detected in the autopsied brains of elderly peo ple who had not had Alzheimer's, but they were much more prevalent in diseased brains.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Finally detangling the fibrous patches, scientists found a core of protein fragments surrounded by threadlike remnants of what looked like degraded brain cells. Several years ago investigators determined that the fragments were short segments, measuring 42 protein subunits in length, that had snapped off a larger protein. That parent molecule, called amyloid precursor protein, turned out to be about 15 times larger than the fragments in the plaques.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Scientists further discovered that the precursor protein is generated by most tissues of the body, including brain cells. Within the last year or so, they have determined that it comes in at least four forms, and they suspect they play distinct roles in different organs. But until the latest research, they had no clues about what those roles might be.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Data Are 'Preliminary'
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           New studies indicate that normal amyloid protein is vital to orchestrating the growth of cells. In a recent issue of Science, investigators at the University of California at Irvine reported evidence that platelets in the blood may release one kind of the protein at the site of a wound to help stimulate cell division and inhibit the activity of degrading enzymes as part of tissue repair. And even newer studies indicate that another type of precursor protein may be able to spur the growth of neurons or their finger-like dendrites, although the results are fiercely debated. The beta-amyloid fragments result from a miscut in the normal clipping of the bigger amyloid protein during its release from cells. In a paper published in last month's Science, Dr. Sangram S. Sisodia, a molecular biologist at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine in Baltimore and his colleagues showed that, under ordinary circumstances, cells secrete about three-quarters of the precursor protein and clip it off in the middle of the final quarter. The shorn protein eventually is freed to perform its task outside the cell.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           The researchers demonstrated that the tiny beta-amyloid fragment is formed from that hindquarter region of the protein that is supposed to be clipped right in half. ''Normally, you should never get beta amyloid,'' said Dr. Sisodia. ''That implies there is some abnormal mechanism by which the precursor protein is broken down in the brains of people with Alzheimer's.''
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Some Initiating Event
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Other researchers have begun to track the enzymes that could be behind the miscutting of amyloid protein. Scientists say that the new line of research holds the greatest promise for devising precise treatments against beta-amyloid formation. Reporting in the May issue of Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, Dr. Nixon and his colleagues have found active enzymes in the beta-amyloid deposits in Alzheimer's brains that are able to degrade proteins into the smallest possible pieces - just the size, says Dr. Nixon, of beta-amyloid fragments.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Dr. Nixon believes that large-scale plaque accretion in the brain begins with some intitiating event, perhaps a few mistakes in normal amyloid cutting, perhaps some other damage to nerve cells. ''We still don't know what those early events are,'' he says, but he adds that they then disrupt or even kill surrounding nerve cells, leading to the chaotic release of many enzymes usually confined to special compartments within the cell.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Freed of constraint, the enzymes promiscuously tear down amyloid precursors into beta-amyloid splinters, which aggregate into long strings and attract remnants of dead neurons to their periphery. The process of plaque formation and rampant enzyme release kills ever more neighboring cells. ''This creates essentially a factory for producing amyloid fragments,'' he said.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Dr. Nixon proposes that one approach to preventing plaques in the brain would be to design drugs that deactivate enzymes found to cut amyloid into very tiny pieces. In that way, he said, important normal amyloid clipping would continue inside the cells, but any stray enzymes that could mistakenly degrade amyloid outside the cells would be blocked. ''This gives us an enzyme target that we can go after to block beta-amyloid formation,'' he said.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Diagram (source: Dr. Ralph Nixon)
          </p>
