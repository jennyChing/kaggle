       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The National Instant Criminal Background Check System: What Is It and How Does It Work?
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date time published" title="2011-05-26T18:25:11+0000">
         May 26, 2011
        </span>
        By
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://www.mastersoftrivia.com/blog/author/admin/" rel="author" title="Posts by Dom">
           Dom
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_2678" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://www.mastersoftrivia.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2011/05/10yrnics200.jpg">
          <img alt="The National Instant Criminal Background Check System" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-2678" height="150" src="http://www.mastersoftrivia.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2011/05/10yrnics200-150x150.jpg" title="The National Instant Criminal Background Check System" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          The National Instant Criminal Background Check System
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         A United States law that required a five-day waiting period on handgun purchases expired on November 30, 1998, yielding to a new and faster system of checking for
         <strong>
          criminal backgrounds of handgun buyers
         </strong>
         . The new system drew criticism from both gun control advocates and organizations that oppose gun controls such as the National Rifle Association (NRA).
        </p>
        <p>
         <script type="text/javascript">
          &lt;!--

         <br/>
         <script src="http://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/show_ads.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <strong>
          five-day waiting period
         </strong>
         was mandated by the so-called
         <strong>
          Brady Law
         </strong>
         , named after former White House press secretary James Brady, who was seriously wounded in a 1981 assassination attempt on President Ronald Reagan. The law went into effect in 1994 and was intended to keep handguns from convicted felons, fugitives, and other criminals. The law is credited with blocking 250,000 handgun purchases since going into effect. But the waiting period was unpopular with gun rights activists, so lawmakers included a provision that would replace the waiting period with a faster system in 1998.
        </p>
        <p>
         Maintained by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI), the
         <strong>
          National Instant Criminal Background Check System
         </strong>
         (NCIS) replaces the five-day period with a faster, computerized system. Firearms dealers submit the names of potential buyers into the system, which determines whether the sale should be allowed to proceed. NCIS also extends the background check requirement to rifles and shotguns. Under the Brady Law only handgun buyers were subject to the background check. State-required waiting periods will remain in force under the new law.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Gun control advocates
         </strong>
         noted several potential problems with NCIS. Handgun Control, Inc., and the Center to Prevent Handgun Violence said that the five-day waiting period had served a useful purpose as a cooling-off period for individuals considering crimes of passion or impulse suicides. It also gave local authorities time to check if potential purchasers had a history of domestic violence or had restraining orders filed against them, in which case they would be denied a firearm. But NCIS does not have access to that data in all states, Handgun Control said, meaning that some previously prohibited sales could be allowed to proceed.
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <strong>
          NRA
         </strong>
         , meanwhile, objected to FBI plans to keep completed NICS background checks on file. Keeping this information constituted an invasion of privacy.
        </p>
