    <article>
     <p>
      Adjectives are used to describe people and objects. Words like fast, new and beautiful are all adjectives.   Adjectives always describe nouns. (Whereas adverbs describe verbs or actions).
     </p>
     <p>
      In the Russian language there are many different forms of each adjective.  (Relating to the 6 cases, 3 genders, plural, short and the comparative). This may sound daunting at first, but in reality, it is fairly simple once you learn the system.  The key is to just to learn the stem, or dictionary form of each adjective and then you can quickly form the rest.
     </p>
     <p>
      The dictionary form of a Russian adjective is normally the normal, nominative, masculine form.  These will almost always end in the letters - or -
     </p>
     <p>
      There are 3 main types of Russian adjectives. Normal, Short and Comparative.
     </p>
     <h3>
      Normal Adjectives
     </h3>
     <p>
      Normal adjectives are those that come before a noun. For example in a phrase like beautiful girl, or new car.
     </p>
     <p>
      Normal adjectives always agree in gender, and case with the noun that they are describing.  This means that there are several ending for each adjective.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are two systems to make the adjectives.  Use the Soft Adjectives table for those adjectives ending in -, otherwise use the Hard Adjectives
     </p>
     <h3>
      Normal Adjectives - Hard (-, -, - (but not -))

     <h3>
      Short Adjectives
     </h3>
     <p>
      The second main type of Russian adjectives are the short form.  We dont really have this form in English, but we do use adjectives the same way.
     </p>
     <p>
      The short form is generally used to make a statement about something.  In English it normally follows the word is or are.  For example, You are beautiful, He is busy.  Notice that the adjective is not followed by a noun. The use of the short form is generally limited to such simple sentences.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is important to note that not all adjectives can have a short form, (but most do).  One notable example is  (Russian).
     </p>
     <p>
      Cases are not relevant when using short adjectives, as you only need the nominative case when making such statements.  The adjective should still agree in gender with the noun.   Masculine nouns just use the stem of the adjective in the short form.  Feminine adds . Neuter adds .  Plural adds  or .
If the adjective is masculine and the stem ends in two consonants, then add a vowel (,  or ) so that the word is easier to read.
     </p>

     </h3>
     <p>
      Often you may wish to use adjectives to compare one thing to another.  To do this we normally use the comparative adjectives.
These adjectives are just adapted from normal adjectives.  However the are a couple of methods that you can use.  All of these methods are relatively easy.
     </p>
     <h4>
      Method 1 : More / Less
     </h4>
     <p>
      The lazy way to compare two things is to use the Russian words for more and less. Here are the Russian words that you need to use.
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       more
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       less
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       than
      </span>
     </p>
     <p>
      When comparing adjectives using this method, use the normal adjectives.  Here are some examples.
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       A more beautiful house.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       A less beautiful house.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
          ,  .
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       Anna is a more beautiful woman than Elena.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
          ,  .
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       Anna is a less beautiful woman than Elena.
      </span>
     </p>
     <br/>
     <h4>
      Method 2 : Comparative Adjectives
     </h4>
     <p>
      Although the above examples are acceptable, Russians will prefer to use the comparative adjectives most of the time.
These are formed by adding either  or  to the stem of the adjective. It is worth noting that these forms can also be used as comparative adverbs.
     </p>
     <p>
      1. If the  last consonant of the adjective is , , , , , ,  : Add 
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (fast) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (faster)
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (beautiful) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (more beautiful, also: more beautifully)
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (difficult) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (more difficult , also: more difficultly)
     </p>
     <p>
      2. Otherwise add  (but the stem will display typical
      <a href="/grammar/spelling_rules.php">
       consonant mutation
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (big) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (bigger)
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (easy) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (easier)
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (cheap) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (cheaper)
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (expensive) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (more expensive)
     </p>
     <p>
      3. As with English the words "good" and "bad" have irregular comparative forms.
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (good) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (better)
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (bad) -
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (worse)
     </p>
     <p>
      Here are some examples.
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
        ,  .
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       Moscow is more beautiful than London.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
        ,  .
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       Anna is more beautiful than Elena.
      </span>
     </p>
     <h4>
      3. Without 
     </h4>
     <p>
      The third way to make comparisons is almost the same as method 2, except the we omit the word  (than).  This method is popular in spoken Russian.
In order to omit  we must use the second noun in the genitive case.   When using this method the order of words in the sentence is important.
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         .
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       Moscow is more beautiful than London.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         .
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       Anna is more beautiful than Elena.
      </span>
     </p>
     <br/>
     <h3>
      Superlative Adjectives - Most
     </h3>
     <p>
      The superlative is how we indicate something is the best, or the most.  (Eg, the most beautiful, smallest, oldest).
To do this we simply use the adjective  (most) which declines like a normal adjective.
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       The most beautiful house.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       The cheapest wine.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="nobreak">
      <span class="rus" lang="ru">
         
      </span>
      -
      <span class="eng">
       The most beautiful woman
      </span>
     </p>

      Conclusion
     </h3>
     <p>

       adjectives page

      in our vocabulary section
