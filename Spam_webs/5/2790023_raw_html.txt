          <a href="http://blog.Cvent.com/blog/jenise-fryatt-bio/why-you-should-follow-some-eggs-on-twitter">
           Why You Should Follow Some Eggs on Twitter
          </a>
         </h2>
        </div>
        <div class="post-by-line">
         <font style="color: #0D91D2;">
          by
         </font>
         <span class="author-name">

         <span class="st_sharethis_hcount" st_title="Why You Should Follow Some Eggs on Twitter" st_url="http://blog.Cvent.com/blog/jenise-fryatt-bio/why-you-should-follow-some-eggs-on-twitter">

        <div class="post-body">
         <p>
          <img alt="Twitter egg" src="//cdn2.content.compendiumblog.com/uploads/user/ab04c84c-11a5-40cf-a34e-fd5aba218b07/52243708-9b44-409d-90ac-e87aa75ff8cd/Image/c6abeaab901ac3dbb4f9fb88d3ac9d99/screen_shot_2014_07_18_at_2_46_18_pm.png" style="float: right; width: 330px; height: 257px; margin-left: 10px; margin-right: 10px;"/>
          A lot of social media management professionals will tell you that you shouldn't follow Twitter accounts that have the Twitter egg as their avatar.
         </p>
         <p>
          Why? Well, the egg usually indicates that the person is new to Twitter and/or probably isn't serious enough about using it to adequately complete their profile on the platform. Such people might be seen as dead weight, not really the types of people who will engage with you or help with your content marketing goals.
         </p>
         <p>
          In many cases, I would agree that following an egg is a lost cause. On my personal Twitter account, this nearly always turns out to be the case.
         </p>
         <p>
          But I also manage a Twitter account for a large professional association with a membership median age of around 45. Most of these members are male.
         </p>
         <p>
          As our association's presence on social media grew, I noticed an interesting thing: many of the accounts that were following us belonged to the narrow niche we had identified as our target audience, but most individual accounts had very few followers. This made me begin to look at eggs in a different light.
         </p>
         <p>
          I began to realize that these folks have often been too busy dealing with the technology surrounding their jobs, to get very involved on social media. They were late to the game, but they were interested and might just need a little coaching to get going.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           How can you tell if an egg is someone with whom you may want to connect?
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          If they have followed you, that's an indication that they just might be interested in the information you have to share.
         </p>
         <p>
          Go to their full Twitter profile. Now take a good look at what they have written about who they are. And also look at their tweets. Do they mention a topic or Twitter account that is relevant to your niche? If so, that's a good indication they are part of your target audience.
         </p>
         <p>
          Twitter also has a wonderful feature that displays accounts of people who follow you and whatever account profile you are viewing. It's on the left side of each profile under the title "Followers You Know."
         </p>
         <p>
          When dealing with a target audience that includes a lot of newbies, this is a great way to find the ones who are really interested in your niche. The more familiar faces you see here, the better. But for my association account all I need to see is one familiar face and I will happily follow an egg.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Once you have followed them, ask if you can help them get started on Twitter
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          This can be the perfect opportunity to build brand ambassadors from the ground up. If they respond by asking for your help, send them a few tips and tell them to direct future questions to your Twitter handle. You can also sent them helpful links or, for more complicated questions, exchange email addresses.
         </p>
         <p>
          As a social media mentor, of sorts, you can begin to build a very strong relationship with these folks that could serve your brand well down the road.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <img alt="broken egg" src="//cdn2.content.compendiumblog.com/uploads/user/ab04c84c-11a5-40cf-a34e-fd5aba218b07/52243708-9b44-409d-90ac-e87aa75ff8cd/Image/e9bd8e8eab132b6520f9969cb9fba7f4/egg.jpg" style="float: left; margin-left: 10px; margin-right: 10px; width: 330px; height: 240px;"/>
           Why not following certain eggs back could be detrimental
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Remember when you were new to Twitter? I know I was aware of EVERY account that followed me and I got very excited about it - especially when those accounts seemed to be well connected!
         </p>
         <p>
          The newbie in your niche represents a special opportunity. You can be the one who gives the whole Twitter experience a positive start for someone who is your ideal member, attendee or client.
         </p>
         <p>
          If one of these people follows you and you decide not to follow back, you may be doing damage to your reputation among those who matter the most.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you represent an association, this is especially important. Associations must be seen as welcoming to all potential and new members.Not following people back can give them the opposite message.
         </p>
         <p>
          Remember, social media marketing is about reaching and connecting with your niche audience. Sometimes your niche audience members are new to Twitter. Don't punish them because they don't know what they're doing yet. Reward them for finding and following you by following them back EVEN if they have only tweeted a couple of times. You have nothing to lose and everything to gain.
         </p>
