        Final words of Air France passenger jet emerge: 'what's happening?'
       </h1>
       <h2 itemprop="alternativeHeadline description">
        The terrified final words of three pilots aboard an Air France passenger jet 
  which crashed into the sea with the loss of 228 lives have emerged for the 
  first time.
       </h2>
       <style media="print">

          <p class="bylineBody">
           <span itemprop="author">
            By Peter Allen in Paris
           </span>
          </p>

        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           "Damn-it! We're going to crash. It can't be true!" says one co-pilot.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           "But, what's happening?" another replies, seconds before their Airbus 330 plunged into the Atlantic, killing all on board, including five Britons and three young Irish doctors.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           The exchange is from the Cockpit Voice Recorder (CVR) of Flight 447, which went down in a tropical storm while flying from Rio de Janeiro to Paris in June 2009.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           Until now only selected exerts from the conversation between 37-year-old David Robert, Pierre-Cedric Bonin, 32, and Marc Dubois, the 58-year-old captain of the plane, have been released.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           Air accident investigators did not release the rest, saying they did not want to upset families of the pilots lost in the worst crash in the company's history.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class=" related_links_inline" id="tmg-related-links">
          <div class="headerOne styleOne">
           <h2>
            <span>

             </a>
            </p>

             01 Oct 2013
            </span>
           </li>
           <li class="bullet">

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           But Jean-Pierre Otelli, a veteran French flying instructor with contacts in the industry, has now written a book in which he lays the whole scandal bare.
          </p>
          <p>
           'Rio-Paris Crash: A Collection of Pilot Errors' describes how the men failed to deal with a loss of lift.
          </p>
          <p>
           Dubois, who had 11,000 flying hours behind him, was on a routine break when it happened, leaving his two subordinates in charges.
          </p>
          <p>
           "So is he coming?", Robert, is heard to mutter, swearing in frustration as Dubois takes a full minute to get back to the cockpit.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Hey what are you ...", Dubois is heard to say when he gets back, to which Robert replies: "What's happening? I don't know, I don't know what's happening."
          </p>
          <p>
           The plane was suffering from a loss of lift  or a 'stall'  and its airspeed sensors had malfunctioned.
          </p>
          <p>
           But instead of lowering the plane's nose to deal with the stall, as they should have done according to normal procedures, they raised it.
          </p>
          <p>
           "I've got a problem I don't have vertical speed. I don't have any indication," says Bonin, before his captain says: "I don't know, but right now we're descending."
          </p>
          <p>
           Air France argues that the pilots were confused by numerous confusing signals from the Airbus, while the plane manufacturer insists that it was responding properly.
          </p>
          <p>
           As the plane approaches the sea, the crew began conversing in short, panicked questions.
          </p>
          <p>
           "What do you think? What do you think? What should we do?" said Robert, while the Airbus rocked from side-to-side.
          </p>
          <p>
           "I don't have control of the plane, I don't have control of the plane at all," Bonin replied, as a stall alarm sounded for the sixth time in two minutes.
          </p>
          <p>
           According to an official report released earlier this year, the last words were from Captain Dubois, who said: "Ten degrees pitch".
          </p>
          <p>
           But in fact, the final exchange Robert says: "Damn-it! We're going to crash. It can't be true!"
          </p>
          <p>
           Bonin's last words are: "But what's happening?"
          </p>
          <p>
           A spokesman for France's air accident investigation unit, the BEA, said the book showed a "lack of respect to the memory of the crew who died".
          </p>
          <p>
           Air France has denied that its pilots were incompetent, but has since improved training for pilots, concentrating on how to fly a plane manually when there is a stall.
          </p>
          <p>
           Both Air France and Airbus are facing manslaughter charges, with a judicial investigation led by Paris judges under way.
          </p>
          <p>
           A judge has already ordered Air France to pay some 120,000 in compensation to the families of each victim, but this is just a provisional figure which is likely to multiply many times over.
          </p>

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
