      Why ICAC powers to investigate corruption must be restored
     </h1>

       The High Court has hamstrung efforts to stop dishonesty involving public office.
      </p>

       <img alt="Margaret Cuneen outside the Darlinghurst Courthouse in Sydney." src="/content/dam/images/1/m/m/l/b/2/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.1mty9l.png/1430123967324.jpg" title=""/>

      <p>
       The brouhaha that has arisen since the High Court's decision in the Cunneen case might cloud the fact that the decision concerns only the breadth of the Independent Commission Against Corruption's powers to investigate corrupt conduct.
      </p>
      <p>
       The decision does not validate the conduct that ICAC inferred was corruptand does not vindicate that conduct. Retrospective legislation, which would give ICAC the powers it thought it had, would allow the facts exposed by ICAC to be used by various law enforcement agencies for prosecution purposes in the way the ICAC Act contemplates.Without such legislation, damning evidence of this kind is likely to be unusable.This is one of the many reasons why such retrospective legislation should be passed as soon as possible.
      </p>
      <!-- cN-quote -->
      <blockquote class="cN-quote">
       <p>
        The people likely to be involved in large-scale corruption are usually experienced, astute and have deep pockets.
        <span>
        </span>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The High Court has found that corrupt conduct under the ICAC Act only occurs when a public official has acted dishonestly (without "probity"), and not when dishonest persons deceive innocent public officials.This means, for example, that ICAC cannot investigate allegations of serious and systemic fraud in state elections or in themaking of applications for mining and other licences.Surely this is a perversion of the community's idea of what ICAC should be doing. It is important thatthis be remedied by the government.
      </p>
      <p>
       Between 1990 and 2004, ICAC conducted numerous public investigations that concerned third parties who had deceived or tried to deceive innocent public officials. No one then thought that these investigations were outside ICAC's powers;not ICAC, nor the people being investigated, nor their lawyers, nor any of the courts before whom cases involving ICAC were brought, and not even Parliament.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 2004, Parliament caused a leading senior counsel to conduct a review into the ICAC Act.He examined ICAC's past investigations and concluded all was in order, so it was unnecessary to make substantive changes to ICAC's investigatory jurisdiction. In 2005, Parliament followed this recommendation; it was content with how ICAC was carrying out its functions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since then, ICAC has continued to investigate people who deceive innocent public officials. The notion that ICAC was thereby exceeding its powers first surfaced in the Cunneen case during argument in the Court of Appeal when it was raised and then adopted by two judges. Their reasoning, however, was not followed by the High Court. The majority in the High Court held that, under the ICAC Act, corrupt conduct could only exist if the conduct could adversely affect the probity of public officials. It is the insertion of this word that has resulted in the removal of ICAC's power to investigate where a dishonest person deceives innocent public officials. Strangely, in argument in the High Court, no one, not even Ms Cunneen's counsel, mentioned the word "probity".
      </p>
      <p>
       A curiosity is that only four of the nine judges involved in the Cunneen proceedings thought Parliament intended the act to be read in this way. The majority seem to have been motivated more by a subjective, philosophical dislike of inquisitorial powers than by the actual words used in the act.
      </p>
      <p>
       How ICAC, in these circumstances, could be criticised for not reading the word "probity" into the ICAC Actis difficult to understand.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many have argued that ICAC should not have inquisitorial powers. Janet Albrechtsen in
       <em>
        The Australian
       </em>
       asserted I "couldn't be more wrong" in saying "the High Court's decision was a terrible result because it gives dishonest people free rein to mislead innocent public officials".She said the police were there to deal with matters "ICAC has no place pursuing".
      </p>
      <p>
       Of course, the police could investigate this kind of corrupt conduct, but the fact is they seldom do.It is very rare for any person to be charged with the kind of corrupt conduct ICAC has regularly exposed.There is good reason for this.The investigation andexposure of corruption is an extremely difficult and expensive task.Secrecy is at its core.Few paper trails are left and, often, false paper trails are created. The peoplelikely to be involved in large-scale corruption are usually experienced, astute and have deep pockets.
      </p>
      <p>
       If corruption involving public office is to be fought effectively, anti-corruption agencies need to be given special powers. Without those powers, serious and debilitating corruption will go undetected. That is why ICAC was created.
      </p>
      <p>
       The truth of the aphorism that the price of freedom is eternal vigilance, is all too obvious.The point is that many basic values clash. Isaiah Berlin, the English philosopher, said "the more freedom for wolves, the more sheep will be killed, and the more sheep are protected, the more wolves will die of starvation". Security, and indeed freedoms, cannot be preserved if freedom to subvert them is permitted. Compromises have to be made if the worst is not to happen.
      </p>
      <p>
       The work of ICAC in the past has protected the democratic process, encouraged public virtue and eased community concerns. In recent years, it has brought about positive changes in the ethical behaviour of bureaucrats and politicians.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a compelling need for ICAC to be able to continue to protect the people against public corruption.It is of the greatest importance that Premier Mike Baird and the government restore ICAC's powers.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        David Ipp is a former ICAC commissioner.
       </strong>
      </p>

            A protean expression  indeed; meaning (i) adversely affect or could adversely affect the probity of the exercise of an official function by a public official or (ii) adversely affect or could adversely affect the efficacy of the exercise of an official function by a public official in the sense that the official could exercise the function in a different manner or make a different decision from that which would otherwise be the case."
            <br/>
            So
            <br/>
            To my understanding, the intention of the ICAC has always been there, and it's obvious  let's now enact the necessary changes to the legislation; close this loophole  and please make it retrospective.
            <br/>
            Return the teeth to the tiger.
           </p>
