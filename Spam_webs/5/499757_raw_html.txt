      <p style="font-family: arial; font-size: 12pt;">
       The Ural Mountains have long been considered the traditional boundary/dividing line between Europe and Asia.
      </p>
      <center>
      </center>
      <center>
       <img alt="Source is http://www.newgeology.us/presentation16.html" src="../../../Photos/Russia/Urals2.jpg" style="border: 2px solid ; width: 480px; height: 360px;"/>
       <br/>
       <span style="font-family: tahoma; font-size: 10pt;">
        Ural Mountains from space.
       </span>
      </center>
      <center>
      </center>
      <p style="font-family: arial; font-size: 12pt;">
       The Ural Mountains ( , Uralskie gory, 
the Stone Belt) as a mountain range run roughly north and south
through Russia to Kazakhstan for a little more than 1,500 mi. The
highest peak is Mount Narodnaia (about 6,200 ft.), while the average
height varies considerably but is often in the 3,000 to 4,000 ft.
range. Geographically the
Urals are often divided into five parts: Southern, Middle, Northern,
Pre-Polar (the highest) and Polar.
      </p>
      <p style="font-family: arial; font-size: 12pt;">
       The Ural
Mountains are among the world's oldest mountains, 300 to 250 million years old,
and erosion has lowered them considerably. In many places, the
mountains are little more than hills. In many respects, the Urals
remind me of the Appalachian Mountains in the eastern United States,
also old and considerably eroded. The Urals do not form a natural
barrier between Europe and Asia. For example, the mountains did
not hinder the Mongol invasion of Russia in the thirteenth century.
      </p>
      <p style="font-family: arial; font-size: 12pt;">
       The Central Urals
contain important mineral and metal deposits, such as iron,
copper, chromium, nickel, coal and oil, and it is one of the most
industrialized regions of Russia, especially around the cities of Perm, Cheliabinsk,
Ekaterinburg and Magnitogorsk.
      </p>

      <p style="font-family: arial; font-size: 12pt;">
       The most northerly
section of the Ural Mountains are known as the "Pre-Polar" and "Polar Urals." The
mountain range in the north tends to be generally higher and
subject to a more extreme
       <a href="Tundra.html">
        tundra
       </a>
       -like climate, with summer lasting only around a month. The Polar Urals are mostly un-inhabited.
      </p>
