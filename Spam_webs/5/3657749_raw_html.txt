     <p>
      <b>
       Breeching
      </b>
      was the occasion when a small boy was first dressed in
      <a href="/wiki/Breeches" title="Breeches">
       breeches
      </a>
      or trousers. From the mid-16th century
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      until the late 19th or early 20th century, young boys in the
      <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">
       Western world
      </a>
      were
      <b>
       unbreeched
      </b>
      and wore
      <a href="/wiki/Gown" title="Gown">
       gowns
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Dress" title="Dress">
       dresses
      </a>
      until an age that varied between two and eight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Breeching was an important
      <a href="/wiki/Rite_of_passage" title="Rite of passage">
       rite of passage
      </a>
      in the life of a boy, looked forward to with much excitement. It often marked the point at which the father became more involved with the raising of a boy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons">
       Reasons
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breeching_(boys)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reasons">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The main reason for keeping boys in dresses was
      <a href="/wiki/Toilet_training" title="Toilet training">
       toilet training
      </a>
      , or the lack thereof.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The change was probably made once boys had reached the age when they could easily undo the rather complicated fastenings of many Early Modern breeches and trousers. Dresses were also easier to make with room for future growth, in an age when clothes were much more expensive than now for all classes. The "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_reason_(canon_law)" title="Age of reason (canon law)">
       age of reason
      </a>
      " was generally considered to be about seven, and breeching corresponded roughly with that age for much of the period. For working-class children, about whom we know even less than their better-off contemporaries, it may well have marked the start of a working life. The debate between his parents over the breeching of the hero of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tristram_Shandy" title="Tristram Shandy">
        Tristram Shandy
       </a>
      </i>
      (1761) suggests that the timing of the event could be rather arbitrary; in this case it is his father who suggests the time has arrived.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 17th-century French cleric and memoirist
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Timol%C3%A9on_de_Choisy" title="Franois-Timolon de Choisy">
       Franois-Timolon de Choisy
      </a>
      is supposed to have been dressed in girl's clothes until he was eighteen.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrations">
       Celebrations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breeching_(boys)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Celebrations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the 19th century, photographs were often taken of the boy in his new trousers, typically with his father. He might also collect small gifts of money by going round the neighbourhood showing off his new clothes. Friends, of the mother as much as the boy, might gather to see his first appearance. A letter of 1679 from Lady Anne North to her widowed and absent son gives a lengthy account of the breeching of her grandson:"Never had any bride that was to be dressed upon her wedding-night more hands about her, some the legs and some the armes, the taylor buttn'ing and other putting on the sword, and so many lookers on that had I not a ffinger  [
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
        sic
       </a>
      </i>
      ] amongst them I could not have seen him. When he was quit drest he acted his part as well as any of them. since you could not have the first sight I resolved you should have a full relation". The dresses he wore before she calls "coats".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Unbreeched_boys">
       Unbreeched boys
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:313px;max-width:313px">

     <p>
      The first progression, for both boys and girls, was when they were
      <b>
       shortcoated
      </b>
      or taken out of the long dresses that came well below the feet that were worn by babies  and which have survived as the modern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christening_robe" title="Christening robe">
       Christening robe
      </a>
      . It was not possible to walk in these, which no doubt dictated the timing of the change. Toddlers' gowns often featured
      <a href="/wiki/Leading_strings" title="Leading strings">
       leading strings
      </a>
      , which were narrow straps of fabric or ribbon attached at the shoulder and held by an adult while the child was learning to walk.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Art_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Art-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After this stage, in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern_period" title="Early Modern period">
       Early Modern period
      </a>
      it is usually not too difficult to distinguish between small boys and girls in commissioned portraits of the wealthy, even where the precise identities are no longer known. The smaller figures of small children in genre painting have less detail, and painters often did not trouble to include distinguishing props as they did in portraits. Working-class children presumably were more likely than the rich to wear handed down clothes that were used by both sexes. In portraits the colours of clothes often keep the rough gender distinctions we see in adults  girls wear white or pale colours, and boys darker ones, including red. This may not entirely reflect reality, but the differences in hairstyles, and in the style of clothing at the chest, throat and neck, waist, and often the cuffs, presumably do.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 19th century, perhaps as childhood became sentimentalised, it becomes harder to tell the clothing apart between the sexes; the hair remains the best guide, but some mothers were evidently unable to resist keeping this long too. By this time the age of breeching was falling closer to two or three, where it would remain. Boys in most periods had shorter hair, often cut in a straight fringe, whilst girl's hair was longer, and in earlier periods sometimes worn "up" in adult styles, at least for special occasions like portraits. In the 19th century, wearing hair up itself became a significant rite of passage for girls at
      <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">
       puberty
      </a>
      , as part of their "coming out" into society. Younger girls' hair was always long, or plaited. Sometimes a quiff or large curl emerges from under a boy's cap. Boys are most likely to have side partings, and girls centre partings.
     </p>

        <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Princess_Royal_and_Princess_of_Orange" title="Mary, Princess Royal and Princess of Orange">
         Mary
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">
         James
        </a>
         unbreeched at four,
        <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">
         Charles
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Elizabeth_of_England" title="Princess Elizabeth of England">
         Elizabeth
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_England_(1637%E2%80%931640)" title="Anne of England (16371640)">
         Anne
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Girls'
      <a href="/wiki/Bodice" title="Bodice">
       bodices
      </a>
      usually reflected adult styles, in their best clothes at least, and low bodices and
      <a href="/wiki/Necklace" title="Necklace">
       necklaces
      </a>
      are common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Boys often, though not always, had dresses that were closed up to the neck-line, and often buttoned at the front  rare for girls. They frequently wear belts, and in periods when female dresses had a V at the waist, this is often seen on little girls, but not on boys.
      <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">
       Linen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">
       lace
      </a>
      at the neck and cuffs tend to follow adult styles for each gender, although again the clothes worn in portraits no doubt do not reflect everyday wear, and may not reflect even best clothes accurately.
     </p>
     <p>
      Unbreeched boys of the nobility are sometimes seen wearing swords or daggers on a belt. A speech by King
      <a href="/wiki/Leontes" title="Leontes">
       Leontes
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
       Shakespeare
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Winter%27s_Tale" title="The Winter's Tale">
        The Winter's Tale
       </a>
      </i>
      implies that, as common sense would suggest, these could not be drawn, and were purely for show:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         Looking on the lines
        </dd>
        <dd>
         Of my boy's face, methought I did recoil
        </dd>
        <dd>
         Twenty-three years, and saw myself unbreech'd
        </dd>
        <dd>
         In my green velvet coat, my dagger muzzled,
        </dd>
        <dd>
         Lest it should bite its master, and so prove
        </dd>
        <dd>
         (As ornament oft does) too dangerous.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
          <a href="#cite_note-10">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           10
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
       he also calls his dress a "coat"; "cote" was a French and English term, dating back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      , for earlier adult male gowns and seems to have been kept in use for boys clothes to preserve some gender distinction.
     </p>
     <p>
      Usually jewellery is not worn by boys, but when worn it is likely to be dark in colour, like the
      <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">
       coral
      </a>
      beads worn by the Flemish boy above. Coral was considered by medical authorities the best material to use for teething aids, and a combined rattle and whistle (in silver) and teething stick (in coral) can be seen in many portraits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In portraits even very young girls may wear
      <a href="/wiki/Necklace" title="Necklace">
       necklaces
      </a>
      , often of
      <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">
       pearls
      </a>
      . In the
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_van_Dyck" title="Anthony van Dyck">
       Van Dyck
      </a>
      portrait of the children of
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">
       Charles I
      </a>
      , only the absence of a necklace and the colour of his dress distinguish the unbreeched
      <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">
       James
      </a>
      (aged four) from his next youngest sister
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Elizabeth_of_England" title="Princess Elizabeth of England">
       Elizabeth
      </a>
      , whilst their
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">
       elder brother
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Princess_Royal_and_Princess_of_Orange" title="Mary, Princess Royal and Princess of Orange">
       sister
      </a>
      , at seven and six, have moved on to adult styles. In cases of possible doubt, painters tend to give boys masculine toys to hold like drums, whips for toy horses, or bows.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_next_step">
       The next step
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breeching_(boys)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The next step">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the late 18th century, new philosophies of child-rearing led to clothes that were thought especially suitable for children. Toddlers wore washable dresses called
      <a href="/wiki/Frock" title="Frock">
       frocks
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">
       linen
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">
       cotton
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgarten.2C_p._171_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Baumgarten.2C_p._171-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       British
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      boys after perhaps three began to wear rather short
      <a href="/wiki/Trousers" title="Trousers">
       pantaloons
      </a>
      and short jackets, and for very young boys the
      <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_suit" title="Skeleton suit">
       skeleton suit
      </a>
      was introduced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baumgarten.2C_p._171_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Baumgarten.2C_p._171-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These gave the first real alternative to dresses, and became fashionable across Europe. The skeleton suit consisted of trousers and tight-fitting jacket, buttoned together at the waist or higher up; they were not unlike the
      <a href="/wiki/Romper_suit" title="Romper suit">
       romper suit
      </a>
      introduced in the early 20th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      But dresses for boys did not disappear, and again became common from the 1820s, when they were worn at about knee-length, sometimes with visible pantaloons called
      <a href="/wiki/Pantalettes" title="Pantalettes">
       pantalettes
      </a>
      as underwear, a style also worn by little girls.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the next stage, from the mid-19th century boys usually progressed into
      <a href="/wiki/Shorts" title="Shorts">
       shorts
      </a>
      at breeching  again these are more accommodating to growth, and cheaper. The
      <a href="/wiki/Knickerbockers_(clothing)" title="Knickerbockers (clothing)">
       knickerbocker
      </a>
      suit was also popular. In England and some other countries, many
      <a href="/wiki/School_uniform" title="School uniform">
       school uniforms
      </a>
      still mandate shorts for boys until about nine or ten. The jackets of boys after breeching lacked adult tails, and this may have influenced the adult tail-less styles which developed, initially for casual wear of various sorts, like the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smoking-jacket" title="Smoking-jacket">
       smoking-jacket
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports_jacket" title="Sports jacket">
       sports jacket
      </a>
      . After the
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       First World War
      </a>
      the wearing of boy's dresses seems finally to have died out, except for babies.
     </p>
