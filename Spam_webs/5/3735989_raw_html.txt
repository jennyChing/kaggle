     <p>
      <b>
       Talal Asad
      </b>
      (born 1932) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">
       anthropologist
      </a>
      at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CUNY_Graduate_Center" title="CUNY Graduate Center">
       CUNY Graduate Center
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Asad has made important theoretical contributions to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-colonialism" title="Post-colonialism">
       post-colonialism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
       Islam
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">
       ritual
      </a>
      studies and has recently called for, and initiated, an
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropology
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">
       secularism
      </a>
      . Using a genealogical method developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">
       Friedrich Nietzsche
      </a>
      and made prominent by
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">
       Michel Foucault
      </a>
      , Asad "complicates terms of comparison that many anthropologists, theologians, philosophers, and political scientists receive as the unexamined background of thinking, judgment, and action as such. By doing so, he creates clearings, opening new possibilities for communication, connection, and creative invention where opposition or studied indifference prevailed".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His long-term research concerns the transformation of religious law (the shari'ah) in nineteenth- and twentieth-century Egypt with special reference to arguments about what constitutes secular and progressive reform.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talal_Asad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      He was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">
       Saudi Arabia
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austrian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pakistani" title="Pakistani">
       Pakistani
      </a>
      diplomat, writer and reformer
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Asad" title="Muhammad Asad">
       Muhammad Asad
      </a>
      , a Jew who converted to Islam in his mid-20s, and a Saudi Arabian Muslim mother, Munira Hussein Al Shammari (died 1978).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_thematics">
       Critical thematics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talal_Asad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Critical thematics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/William_E._Connolly" title="William E. Connolly">
       William E. Connolly
      </a>
      attempts to summarize Asad's theoretical contributions on secularism as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Secularism is not merely the division between public and private realms that allows religious diversity to flourish in the latter. It can itself be a carrier of harsh exclusions. And it secretes a new definition of "religion" that conceals some of its most problematic practices from itself.
      </li>
      <li>
       In creating its characteristic division between secular public space and religious private space, European secularism sought to shuffle ritual and discipline into the private realm. In doing so, however, it loses touch with the ways in which embodied practices of conduct help to constitute culture, including European culture.
      </li>
      <li>
       The constitution of modern Europe, as a continent and a secular civilization, makes it incumbent to treat Muslims in its midst on the one hand as abstract citizens and on the other as a distinctive minority either to be tolerated (the liberal orientation) or restricted (the national orientation), depending on the politics of the day.
      </li>
      <li>
       European, modern, secular constitutions of Islam, in cumulative effect, converge upon a series of simple contrasts between themselves and Islamic practices. These terms of contrast falsify the deep grammar of European secularism and contribute to the culture wars some bearers of these very definitions seek to ameliorate.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formations_of_The_Secular">
       <i>
        Formations of The Secular
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talal_Asad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formations of The Secular">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Formations of the Secular: Christianity, Islam, Modernity
      </i>
      is both an original work and a reworking of previous essays and papers by Asad.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <i>
       Formations of The Secular
      </i>
      , Asad examines what he views as the curious character of modern European and American societies and their notion of
      <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">
       secularism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Secularism, often viewed as a neutral or flat space that forbids religious opinion or interference in political questions, is found to be somewhat curious to Asad. Specifically, Asad's experiences with the response to the 2001
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11th_attacks" title="September 11th attacks">
       September 11th attacks
      </a>
      from the point of view of a Muslim in
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      exposed him to explosions of intolerance that seemed to him entirely compatible with secularism in a highly modern society.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :7
      </sup>
      However, rather than simply letting such a coincidence pass, Asad continue by stating that such behaviors are "intertwined" with secularism in a "modern society".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :7
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This leads Asad's deployment of the genealogical method in order to understand why a country like the United States denominates itself as secular despite the distinctly religious
      <a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">
       Manichaean
      </a>
      tones  good and evil  often found within the historical record of the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :7
      </sup>
      He further notes that despite the nominally secular character of The United States, repressive measures have been directed at real and imagined secular opponents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :7
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      These events, as well as other questions, lead Asad to what might be termed the thesis of the book:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The secular, I argue, is neither continuous with the religious that supposedly preceded it (that is, it is not the latest phase of a sacred origin) nor a simple break from it (that is, it is not the opposite, an essence that excludes the sacred). I take the secular to be a concept that brings together certain behaviors, knowledges, and sensibilities in modern life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :24
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Building on that notion, Asad is also critical of the more common concept of secularism, which he views as having no distinct features that demarcate it from other prior forms of secularism found elsewhere in the world. Instead he favors another approach to viewing modern secularism:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       In my view the secular is neither singular in origin nor stable in its historical identity, although it works through a series of particular oppositions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :24
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      With that said, Asad's goal for the book is to understand how a more general pre-secularism mutates into the more familiar "novel" form of secularism present within Euro-American societies  Asad makes clear his interest in this specific "novel" variant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asad_6-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Asad-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :12
      </sup>
     </p>
