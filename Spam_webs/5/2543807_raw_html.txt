     <strong>
      What is Freestyle BMX
     </strong>
     (
     <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_BMX" target="_blank">
      by Wikipedia
     </a>
     )
     <br/>
     <p>
      Freestyling can be traced back to 1975 when kids started riding bikes in concrete Escondido reservoir channels in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Diego,_California" target="_blank" title="San Diego, California">
       San Diego, California
      </a>
      . 
	And, bike riders were seen in 1976 riding at Carlsbad Skatepark in
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlsbad,_California" target="_blank" title="Carlsbad, California">
       Carlsbad, California
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-0">
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_BMX#cite_note-0" target="_blank">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skateboarder_Magazine" target="_blank" title="Skateboarder Magazine">
       Skateboarder Magazine
      </a>
      published photos of kids on bikes riding in empty swimming pools in 1975.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_BMX#cite_note-1" target="_blank">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Haro" target="_blank" title="Bob Haro">
       Bob Haro
      </a>
      and John Swanguen rode BMX bikes at Skateboard Heaven, 
	a concrete
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skatepark" target="_blank" title="Skatepark">
       skatepark
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Diego,_California" target="_blank" title="San Diego, California">
       San Diego, California
      </a>
      in late 1976. Later they transformed freestyle beyond skateparks by creating new bike tricks on flat streets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_BMX#cite_note-2" target="_blank">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the fall of 1977
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Haro" target="_blank" title="Bob Haro">
       Bob Haro
      </a>
      was hired as a staff artist 
	at BMX Action Magazine where he befriended R.L. Osborn, son of the magazine publisher Bob Osborn. Haro 
	and R.L. often practiced freestyle moves in their free time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_BMX#cite_note-3" target="_blank">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the summer of 1978 Paramount, Lakewood, and other Southern California skateparks began 
	reserving sessions or whole days exclusively for BMX bikes. BMX racer
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tinker_Juarez" target="_blank" title="Tinker Juarez">
       Tinker Juarez
      </a>
      was innovating freestyle moves in vert bowls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freestyle_BMX#cite_note-4" target="_blank">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
