     <p>
      <b>
       Special pleading
      </b>
      is a form of fallacious argument that involves an attempt to cite something as an exception to a generally accepted rule, principle, etc. without justifying the exception.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engel1994_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Engel1994-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The lack of criticism may be a simple oversight (e.g., a reference to
      <a href="/wiki/Common_sense" title="Common sense">
       common sense
      </a>
      ) or an application of a
      <a href="/wiki/Double_standard" title="Double standard">
       double standard
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
       Examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_pleading&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A more difficult case is when a possible criticism is made relatively immune to investigation. This immunity may take the forms of:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       unexplained claims of exemption from principles commonly thought relevant to the subject matter
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Example:
       <i>
        I'm not relying on faith in small probabilities here. These are slot machines, not roulette wheels. They are different.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       claims to data that are inherently unverifiable, perhaps because too remote or impossible to define clearly
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Example:
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">
         Cocaine
        </a>
        use should be legal. Like all drugs, it does have some adverse health effects, but cocaine is different from other drugs. Many have benefited from the effects of cocaine.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In the classic distinction among
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_fallacy" title="Material fallacy">
       informal (material)
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological_fallacy" title="Psychological fallacy">
       psychological
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Formal_fallacy" title="Formal fallacy">
       formal (logical)
      </a>
      fallacies, special pleading most likely falls within the category of psychological fallacy, as it would seem to relate to "lip service", rationalization and diversion (abandonment of discussion). Special pleading also often resembles the "
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Appeal_(disambiguation)#Argument" title="Appeal (disambiguation)">
       appeal to
      </a>
      " logical fallacies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In medieval philosophy, it was not assumed that wherever a distinction is claimed, a relevant basis for the distinction should exist and be substantiated. Special pleading subverts an assumption of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existential_import" title="Existential import">
       existential import
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical">
       Statistical
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_pleading&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Statistical">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      This variation occurs when the interpretation of the relevant statistic is "massaged" by looking for ways to reclassify or requantify data from one portion of results, but not applying the same scrutiny to other categories.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischer1970_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fischer1970-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
