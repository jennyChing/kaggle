            <p class="first-text" data-textannotation-id="2b03947b7dbca9cb35e1ace1722401d3">
             We've all hit that point where we can't figure out exactly what we really want to do with our lives. It can come when you're 18 or when you're 50, and it's always a difficult process to work through. It's not hopeless, though! Here are a few ways to help you figure it out.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="f43e12ac1c5c1309c8794a2899dd5518">
             Discovering what you really want to do with your life isn't an easy task for anyone, nor is it something that you can really create a step-by-step guide for. That said, when you're not really sure what you want to do, whether it's a career, a lifestyle, or anything else, a few different exercises might help you pinpoint what it is you truly want. Here are four ways to try to pinpoint what you want from your future.
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="2c5e506bdcf75380081da8bb87c32860">
             Think About Where You'll Be in Five Years
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-640 has-image" data-textannotation-id="4b4dd97c924e7aeb4246650baa2f57d8">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Four Ways to Figure Out What You Really Want to Do with Your Life" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--3IFBGX8t--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp6yxig7ffjjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18qp6yxig7ffjjpg" data-format="jpg" height="357" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--3IFBGX8t--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp6yxig7ffjjpg.jpg" width="636"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">

             </span>
            </p>

            <p data-textannotation-id="f3496fba4897db9ee1d1a3075d701e2e">
             The "where do you see yourself in five years?" question riddles everything from job interviews to financial plans, and while it seems clich, its common for a reason: it works. It's one of those annoyingly difficult questions to answer, and in most cases it's pretty much impossible to answer in a concrete way. Thankfully, that's okay, and sometimes just trying to answer the question is all you need.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="56c76a743f3a8058667c7775811905cb">
             Considering how common of advice it is, the idea of seeing into the future and picturing where you'll be in five years is a heck of a lot harder to do than you'd think. Plus, the chances you'll actually end up where you see yourself is pretty slim. That said, as Adam Savage
             <a href="http://www.tested.com/art/makers/452956-work-ethic-252013/" target="_blank">
              points out on the Still Untitled Podcast
             </a>
             , the exercise of talking about where you see yourself in five years is still useful. He shares this story:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="29038b551061e9bd47503b365b291684">
             The thing that you feel like you really want to do is the thing to pursue. You will not end up there, and that reminds me of one of Kurt Vonnegut's off-quoted statements is the punchline to his favorite joke: 'Keep your hat on. We may end up miles from here..."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             I remember being 19 and meeting a girlfriend's mom, and she asks me, "What are your going to be doing in five years?" And I named something I thought I'd do. She listened to me give this long explanation, and she said, "You're not going to be doing any of that. You have no idea what you'll be doing in five years. You'll be doing something really cool, but it's not something you can imagine right now." It's one of the greatest things that anyone gave me. She was totally right. Every five years it's the same. I can never imagine where I ended up from where I started.
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="c69b127248492556966753afa0cb9b6a">
             Savage's point here is pretty simple: it doesn't matter where you think you'll be in five years, but it's still important to think about because it gives you the idea you want to pursue. As far as careers are concerned,
             <a href="http://blogs.hbr.org/hmu/2011/03/where-will-you-be-in-five-year.html" target="_blank">
              Harvard Business Review takes a similar approach
             </a>
             and recommends you think about what you want to
             <em>
              learn
             </em>
             in the next five years:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="b5b66104e116e7faab0aeee145c772d4">
             What capabilities will you have wanted to build in five years? For example, "I can't say exactly what I'm going to be doing in five years, but I hope to have further developed my skills as a strategist and people manager." This is a safe way to answer regardless of your age or career stage. "You don't want to ever give the impression that you're done learning," says Weintraub.
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="d0f3fceb2db5acdc860bc8322cf9b732">
             It's a simple idea that's very similar to Savage's approach, but it does so in a way where you're directly concentrating on what you'll need to get to a place you'll be happy in five years. As we've
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5978475/how-to-pick-a-career-you-actually-like" x-inset="1">
              pointed out before
             </a>
             , picking a lifestyle to pursue instead of a job title can help you focus in what you're really interested in, and this is one way to do that.
            </p>

            <h3 data-textannotation-id="984b4d7699611d8524280c172bd2603c">
             Write Your Personal Manifesto
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-640 has-image" data-textannotation-id="ae5d7d3876f6a103c50d67bf02726e7d">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Four Ways to Figure Out What You Really Want to Do with Your Life" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--JI3ZN7-y--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp2jzkxavhfjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18qp2jzkxavhfjpg" data-format="jpg" height="312" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--JI3ZN7-y--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp2jzkxavhfjpg.jpg" width="636"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="ccc54e52510b0002f3b61d975ac76dbd">
             The idea of a personal manifesto might sound a little silly on the surface. The idea is that if you can figure out where you stand on certain ideas, you might be able to flesh out a possible career or lifestyle path. Silly or not, the idea of a personal manifesto is implemented by everyone from
             <a href="http://www.google.com/intl/en/about/company/philosophy/" target="_blank">
              Google
             </a>
             to
             <a href="http://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/the-happiness-project/201102/frank-lloyd-wrights-10-point-manifesto-his-apprentices" target="_blank">
              Frank Lloyd Wright
             </a>
             . The point, as The Art of Manliness says,
             <a href="http://www.artofmanliness.com/2012/02/13/how-and-why-to-write-your-own-personal-manifesto/" target="_blank">
              is to give yourself a call to action
             </a>
             to define how you want to do things. It's easy to write your own manifesto, and while you don't have to do it in a specific way, The Art of Manliness has a few suggestions for getting started:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li data-textannotation-id="42106837d3747d0451adfa9bcd2e2818">
              <strong>
               Pick your topics
              </strong>
              : Pick a few topics to concentrate on, and make them as specific as you can. Ideas like, "The hours I want to work," or "How I want to commute" are great for narrowing in on what kind of work you might be interested in.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="1ea2def0f7a0a5ad1398d432d15b979d">
              <strong>
               Set down your principles
              </strong>
              : Write down your beliefs and intentions. It probably sounds a little over-the-top, but if you've never really written down and thought about your morals or beliefs then this is a good time to do so.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="fdd6b3a7f67bdab88b39214613517943">
              <strong>
               Use strong, affirmative language
              </strong>
              : It's easy to write a manifesto with words like "I want" or "I should" but that's not helping you. Write it out with affirmative language like, "I will," or use the present tense with "I am."
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p data-textannotation-id="85ec1f506cdeace1c413f8eb7ce9266d">
             The main purpose of the personal manifesto is to really figure out what you care about, how you perceive yourself, and how you want to act moving forward. It's not always a key to figuring out exactly what you want to do with your life, but it's a great starting point for at least figuring out how you want to go about those goals. Grab a pen, some paper, and get to writing out what you believe.
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="10eac0d47d6e9cd186adbfc1099e81be">
             Volunteer or Shadow Someone in a Job You're Interested In
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-640 has-image" data-textannotation-id="9a09e97b0b343e52b22e8eda26614fe8">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Four Ways to Figure Out What You Really Want to Do with Your Life" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--S6ywwvDA--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp2rbz27w1yjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18qp2rbz27w1yjpg" data-format="jpg" height="314" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--S6ywwvDA--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp2rbz27w1yjpg.jpg" width="636"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="e924e0375cd510f76a3b84228239b854">
             One of the reasons that figuring out what we want to do is so terrifying is because we tend to overcommit. Want to maybe write that book? Go to grad school! Thinking about being a lawyer? Apply for law school! When you up the ante on plans, they get real serious, real fast, and it's hard to make a decision with that kind of pressure. So, don't overcommit to an idea. Instead, take a couple days to try on the lifestyle. To do this, you can try volunteering or shadowing someone at their job.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="ace6e26953c9de499a155bd1394334cd">
             We've talked before about
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5938432/how-to-find-a-volunteer-gig-youll-actually-enjoy" x-inset="1">
              finding a volunteer gig you enjoy
             </a>
             . The idea here is to put yourself in a position where you'll learn something and try on a new career. If you're struggling to really figure out what you want to do with yourself, volunteer work is a way to try out a ton of different jobs without committing to any of them. Contrary to what you might think, volunteer work can include all kinds of work, including coding or manual labor.
             <a href="http://www.volunteermatch.org/" target="_blank">
              Volunteer Match
             </a>
             is a good place to start the hunt for volunteer work.
            </p>

                </span>
               </h6>
               <p class="text small-offset-3 ">
                <span class="referenced-item-excerpt hide-for-small">
                 Volunteering is one of those quirky little things that we all know we should probably do more
                </span>
                <a class="js_readmore readmore-referenced" href="http://lifehacker.com/5938432/how-to-find-a-volunteer-gig-youll-actually-enjoy" onclick="window.ga('send', 'event', 'Permalink page click', 'Permalink page click - inset read more link');" target="_blank">
                 Read more
                 <span class="js_external-text hide">
                  Read more
                 </span>
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
            </aside>
            <p data-textannotation-id="3a1096c80a2a5bd76d314684f4906da0">
             Likewise, doing a job shadow is another way to get a closer look at an industry without committing to long hours in school or (worse) at a job you hate. Monster
             <a href="http://career-advice.monster.com/career-development/changing-careers/the-job-shadow-knows-try-on-a-career-before-you-commit-hot-jobs/article.aspx" target="_blank">
              has a few suggestions for how to go about shadowing
             </a>
             :
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="f8cca65c118b0514edf30c5af5a52a87">
             While students and recent graduates have long embraced job shadowing, Crawford, author of
             <em>
              Flying Solo: Career Transition Tips for Singles
             </em>
             , says everyone should consider job shadowing, especially in this economy. "You have to be more assertive than ever with your career, and asking for the opportunity to shadow someone is a way to not only learn what that job is like, but to expand your network as well," she says.
             <p data-textannotation-id="772b49897a978f6009a5dc6d5b5e0bee">
              <br/>
              Crawford, who is based in Atlanta, notes that certain industries lend themselves well to job shadowing, including medicine and law. However, you can usually plan a job shadow for any kind of job. Your college's career center, alumni association or your local chamber of commerce can all be instrumental in helping to arrange a job shadow if your immediate network doesn't yield any possibilities.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="93880484f3d64c8aaac1817c096cd25b">
             Obviously job shadowing is tailored to college students, but it's possible for anyone to give it a try, especially if you're willing to work a day or two for free in the industry. To actually shadow someone, you're best bet is to call up a company and set up and appointment to do so. You might need to go through a few options before you land a place that'll let you come in, but offering to work for free during your time is certainly a way to perk the company's ears up.
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="57152306970d5005b5ba1ea8bd32d42f">
             Dig Into Those Side Projects
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-640 has-image" data-textannotation-id="a32fa31c28b9283cac23062de3f75168">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Four Ways to Figure Out What You Really Want to Do with Your Life" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--vCAyFBqx--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp2x35ce39njpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18qp2x35ce39njpg" data-format="jpg" height="320" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--vCAyFBqx--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18qp2x35ce39njpg.jpg" width="636"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="f65e2dcddc8bcb823a6ec22b78bbd728">
             Sometimes, the best way to really figure out what you want to do with yourself is good old trial and error. In a lot of cases, this just means trying out various side projects you're interested in with all the free time you have.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="b72056856c52b3650c544dc3dd2c204c">
             These side projects are all kinds of things. They might be as simple as finding a hobby you enjoy, or as far-reaching as testing out different industries that you might consider looking for a career in. As writer Scott Young describes it,
             <a href="http://www.scotthyoung.com/blog/2007/07/29/what-do-you-want-to-do-with-your-life/" target="_blank">
              it's all about being curious
             </a>
             , and side projects are a great way to do that:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="5dcec40f07c91c25584bb5b0ad80a540">
             Instead of making definite decisions about a career path, I believe you should get curious. Get curious about the way the world works. Notice your own interests and find small ways you can exercise passion in something. Even if you cant find a way to make money off of it yet.
             <p data-textannotation-id="541c413b415dbed73038aba464006332">
              The bridge from passion to money-maker cant be made hastily. Interests often get discarded because they cannot be immediately relayed into a source of income. And therefore arent as important as work that does.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="60bec93bb8a10607510b3ea80cb357a6">
             The idea here is to take on a lot of side projects, learn new skills, or tackle a few passion projects to try and figure out what you want to do with your life. Once you have a few ideas,
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5590732/how-to-find-time-to-learn-something-new-or-tackle-a-passion-project" x-inset="1">
              it's time to find the time to actually work on those projects
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/how-can-i-get-into-a-new-hobby-without-breaking-the-ban-489189582" x-inset="1">
              try on new hobbies without spending too much money
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5994125/how-to-stick-with-it-when-youre-learning-something-new-on-your-own" x-inset="1">
              teach yourself to stick with it
             </a>
             , and
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5892576/getting-started-is-everything" x-inset="1">
              just get started working on them
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <aside class="referenced-wide referenced-fullwidth js_inset tmpl_referencedGroupFullWidth clearfix core-decorated-inset">
             <div class="container">
              <div class="referenced-item " data-inset-id="1558205009">

                  <img src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--NsMmFL_Z--/c_fill,fl_progressive,g_center,h_77,q_80,w_137/18ixvgdtmrbd5jpg.jpg"/>
                 </span>

                 </a>
                </span>
                <span class="medium-headline">

                 </a>
                </span>
               </h6>
               <p class="text small-offset-3 ">
                <span class="referenced-item-excerpt hide-for-small">
                 You've got a career, friends, family, and a mountain of other responsibilities that have a
                </span>
                <a class="js_readmore readmore-referenced" href="http://lifehacker.com/5590732/how-to-find-time-to-learn-something-new-or-tackle-a-passion-project" onclick="window.ga('send', 'event', 'Permalink page click', 'Permalink page click - inset read more link');" target="_blank">
                 Read more
                 <span class="js_external-text hide">
                  Read more
                 </span>

               </p>

            <p data-textannotation-id="02bcb503a1f3c4c0b6f3d9580b1e1134">
             The goal here is to give you a better idea of what you
             <em>
              want
             </em>
             to do with yourself. Like we mentioned in the first section, this might just reveal "the thing you want to pursue" and not your actual career path. That's okay, because if nothing else it sets you on the path to getting somewhere you want to end up.
            </p>
            <hr/>
            <p data-textannotation-id="447b9d1860dd90b31ba481f51e3e57d0">
             In the end, figuring out your passion, your career path, your life purposewhatever you want to call itisn't an easy process and no magic bullet exists for doing it. The fact is, you need to think about it, and to do so you might need to push yourself through some mental hurdles until you figure out exactly what is you've been looking for. The above tips are just a few of the many ways to do so.
            </p>
