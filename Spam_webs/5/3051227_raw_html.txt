     <p>
      <b>
       Subutai
      </b>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classic_Mongolian_language" title="Classic Mongolian language">
       Classical Mongolian
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Sbgti
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Sb'ti
      </i>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Khalkha_Mongolian" title="Khalkha Mongolian">
       Modern Mongolian
      </a>
      : ,
      <i>
       Sbedei
      </i>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       <span style="font-style:normal;">
        
       </span>
      </span>
      11751248) was a Mongolian general, and the primary military strategist of
      <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">
       Genghis Khan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%96gedei_Khan" title="gedei Khan">
       gedei Khan
      </a>
      . He directed more than twenty campaigns in which he conquered thirty-two nations and won sixty-five
      <a href="/wiki/Pitched_battle" title="Pitched battle">
       pitched battles
      </a>
      , during which he conquered or overran more territory than any other commander in history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He gained victory by means of imaginative and sophisticated strategies and routinely coordinated movements of armies that were hundreds of kilometers away from each other. He is also remembered for devising the campaign that destroyed the armies of Hungary and Poland within two days of each other, by forces over five hundred kilometers apart.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Historians believe Subutai was born in the year 1175,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      probably just west of the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Onon_River" title="Onon River">
       Onon River
      </a>
      in what is now
      <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">
       Mongolia
      </a>
      . He belonged to the
      <a href="/wiki/Uriankhai" title="Uriankhai">
       Uriankhai
      </a>
      clan. Subutai's family had been associated with the family of Temujin (future Genghis Khan) for many generations. Subutai's great-great grandfather, Nerbi was supposedly an ally of the Mongol Khan Tumbina Sechen. Subutai's father, Jarchigudai, supposedly supplied food to Temujin and his followers when they were in dire straits at lake Baljuna, and Subutai's elder brother
      <a href="/wiki/Jelme" title="Jelme">
       Jelme
      </a>
      also served as a general in the Mongol army and was a close companion of Temujin. Jelme rescued a severely wounded Temujin (hit by an arrow from
      <a href="/wiki/Jebe" title="Jebe">
       Jebe
      </a>
      , then an enemy) in the process of unification of the Mongolian plateau. Another brother, Chaurkhan (also romanized as Ca'urqan) is mentioned in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secret_History_of_the_Mongols" title="Secret History of the Mongols">
       Secret History of the Mongols
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite this close family association, Subutai may be considered proof that the
      <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">
       Mongol Empire
      </a>
      was a
      <a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">
       meritocracy
      </a>
      . He was a commoner by birth, the son of Jarchigudai, who was supposedly a
      <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">
       blacksmith
      </a>
      . When he was 14 years old, Subutai left his clan to join Temujin's army, following in the footsteps of his older brother Jelme who had joined when he was 17 years old. and he rose to the very highest command available to one who was not a blood relative to Genghis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Within a decade he rose to become a general, in command of one of 4
      <a href="/wiki/Tumen_(unit)" title="Tumen (unit)">
       tumens
      </a>
      operating in the vanguard. In 1212 he took Huan by storm, the first major independent exploit mentioned in the sources. Genghis Khan is reported to have called him one of his "dogs of war" in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_History_of_the_Mongols" title="The Secret History of the Mongols">
        The Secret History of the Mongols
       </a>
      </i>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       <i>
        "They are the Four Dogs of Temujin. They have foreheads of brass, their jaws are like scissors, their tongues like piercing awls, their heads are iron, their whipping tails swords . . . In the day of battle, they devour enemy flesh. Behold, they are now unleashed, and they slobber at the mouth with glee. These four dogs are
        <a href="/wiki/Jebe" title="Jebe">
         Jebe
        </a>
        , and Kublai (different than
        <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">
         Kublai Khan
        </a>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Jelme" title="Jelme">
         Jelme
        </a>
        , and Subotai."
       </i>
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       The Secret History of the Mongols
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Mongol histories say that Subutai said to
      <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">
       Genghis Khan
      </a>
      , "I will ward off your enemies as felt cloth protects one from the wind."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saunders_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Saunders-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_general">
       As a general
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As a general">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Subutai was one of the first Mongol generals, alongside Genghis Khan, who realized the value of engineers in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_warfare" title="Siege warfare">
       siege warfare
      </a>
      . Even in field battles he made use of
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_engine" title="Siege engine">
       siege engines
      </a>
      , much as Chinese armies had in their own wars. In the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mohi" title="Battle of Mohi">
       Battle of Mohi
      </a>
      , the Hungarian crossbowmen repelled a night bridge crossing by the Mongols, and inflicted considerable casualties on the Mongols fighting to cross the river the following day. Subutai ordered huge stonethrowers to clear the bank of Hungarian crossbowmen and open the way for his light cavalry to cross the river without further losses. This use of siege weapons was one of the first recorded uses in the West of artillery outside of siege warfare. While the stonethrowers were clearing the path to cross the main bridge, Subutai supervised construction of another temporary bridge downriver to outflank the Hungarians.
     </p>
     <p>
      Subutai was also well known for incorporating conquered peoples who brought specialized skills into his forces, especially engineers. He was skilled at intelligence gathering and planning his campaigns well in advance. For instance, he used
      <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">
       spies
      </a>
      to gather information on the Russian principalities, the Poles, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_people" title="Hungarian people">
       Hungarians
      </a>
      at least a year before the attacks on each. He tailored his strategy to match the enemy, adjusting his tactics according to the opponents, the terrain, and the weather as required. He emphasized the use of light cavalry in his army, maneuvering the enemy into feints and ambushes, and efficiently pursuing and defeating broken armies to destroy further resistance. Subutai kept his forces in line with the Mongol tradition of dispensing with excess baggage train and ensured his troops could efficiently live off the land and rapidly advance great distances on campaign. He preferred to maneuver the enemy into a position of weakness before committing to battle.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_campaigns_in_the_West">
       First campaigns in the West
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First campaigns in the West">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Genghis Khan sent Subutai to hunt down the
      <a href="/wiki/Merkit" title="Merkit">
       Merkits
      </a>
      . Subutai defeated them on the
      <a href="/wiki/Chu_River" title="Chu River">
       Chu River
      </a>
      in 1216 and again in 1219 in
      <a href="/wiki/Kipchaks" title="Kipchaks">
       Wild Kipchak
      </a>
      territory.
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_II_of_Khwarezm" title="Muhammad II of Khwarezm">
       Mohammad II
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khwarizm" title="Khwarizm">
       Khwarizm
      </a>
      attacked Subutai shortly afterwards along the Irghiz. Subutai held him off after a fierce battle.
     </p>
     <p>
      Genghis Khan led the Mongol army westwards in late 1219 to invade Khwarizm as retaliation for the execution of Mongol ambassadors. Subutai commanded the vanguard of the invasion force. With roughly 130,000 armed men, the Mongol army was numerically inferior to the forces of the Khwarizim Empire, but through deception and rapid maneuver, the Mongols crushed the Khwarizim in several key battles. Mohammad attempted to save himself by fleeing into central
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">
       Persia
      </a>
      , leading Genghis Khan to send Subutai and Jebe with 20,000 men to hunt him down. Mohammad eluded capture, but he fell ill and died at a fishing village on the Caspian Sea in early 1221.
     </p>
     <p>
      Subutai spent part of the winter in
      <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">
       Azerbaijan
      </a>
      . Here he conceived the idea of circling the
      <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">
       Caspian Sea
      </a>
      to fall on the rear of the Wild
      <a href="/wiki/Kipchaks" title="Kipchaks">
       Kipchaks
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cumans" title="Cumans">
       Cumans
      </a>
      . After a police action in Persia and a raid into
      <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">
       Georgia
      </a>
      , the Mongols cut across the
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Mountains" title="Caucasus Mountains">
       Caucasus Mountains
      </a>
      during the winter to get around the Derbent Pass. Using clever diplomacy, Subutai isolated and defeated the
      <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">
       Alans
      </a>
      and Don Kipchaks/Cumans in detail. He crushed a combined
      <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">
       Rus
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuman" title="Cuman">
       Cuman
      </a>
      army along the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Kalka_River" title="Battle of the Kalka River">
       Kalka
      </a>
      (31 May 1223). The campaign concluded with Subutai rejoining Genghis Khan as the Mongol army was making its way back home.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Against_Xi_Xia_and_Jin">
       Against Xi Xia and Jin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Against Xi Xia and Jin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Subutai played a key part in the campaign against the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xi_Xia" title="Xi Xia">
       Xi Xia
      </a>
      in 1226. In 1227, he conquered the
      <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(1115%E2%80%931234)" title="Jin dynasty (11151234)">
       Jin
      </a>
      districts along the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Wei_River" title="Wei River">
       Wei River
      </a>
      . The Mongol operations were interrupted by the death of Genghis Khan. Genghis Khan was succeeded by his son
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%96gedei_Khan" title="gedei Khan">
       gedei
      </a>
      . In 1230-1231, gedei personally led the main Mongol army against the Jin (in Central
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      ), but the attempt to break into the plains of
      <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">
       Henan
      </a>
      ended in failure after Subutai was defeated at Shan-che-hui. The Mongols besieged and took Fengxiang, a secondary target. In 1231-1232 the Mongols made another attempt. This time Subutai was able to outmaneuver the Jin armies.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Mongols won decisive victories at Sanfeng (9 February 1232), Yangyi (24 February 1232), and Tiehling (1 March 1232). gedei and the main Mongol army returned to Mongolia, leaving Subutai with a small force to complete the conquest of
      <a href="/wiki/Honan" title="Honan">
       Honan
      </a>
      . Subutai found it difficult to take the large cities and needed almost 2 more years to finally eliminate the Jin. He made an alliance with
      <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">
       Song
      </a>
      to get help to complete the job. But it did not take the Song long to fall out with the Mongols. Two Song armies seized
      <a href="/wiki/Kaifeng" title="Kaifeng">
       Kaifeng
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">
       Luoyang
      </a>
      during the summer of 1234. The Mongols returned and retook the cities.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_second_series_of_Western_campaigns">
       The second series of Western campaigns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The second series of Western campaigns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      gedei decided to send a major part of the army into the western regions to finally crush the
      <a href="/wiki/Kipchaks" title="Kipchaks">
       Wild Kipchaks
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bulgars" title="Bulgars">
       Bulgars
      </a>
      . Subutai was tasked to direct the operations (under the overall command of prince Batu). He defeated Kipchak leader
      <a href="/wiki/Bachman_Khan" title="Bachman Khan">
       Bachman
      </a>
      on the north side of the Caspian Sea and next conquered the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volga_Bulgars" title="Volga Bulgars">
       Volga Bulgars
      </a>
      . In late 1237, Subutai attacked
      <a href="/wiki/Ryazan" title="Ryazan">
       Ryazan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir-Suzdal" title="Vladimir-Suzdal">
       Vladimir-Suzdal
      </a>
      , operating with 3 columns (attacking as the Mongols usually did during the winter). The
      <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">
       Rus
      </a>
      forces were defeated in 3 separate engagements and their cities were taken in quick succession. The Mongols spent the summer of 1238 resting along the
      <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">
       Don River
      </a>
      . Columns were sent out to subjugate the various tribes living in the plains around the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
       Black Sea
      </a>
      . In 1239, the Rus state of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chernigov" title="Chernigov">
       Chernigov
      </a>
      was defeated and their cities were taken.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Mongols had made a treaty with Galich-Vladimir, whose prince was therefore taken by surprise when the Mongols suddenly attacked in December 1240.
      <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">
       Kiev
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir" title="Vladimir">
       Vladimir
      </a>
      , and other cities were quickly taken. The Mongols were ready to enter Central
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      . Subutai operated with several separate detachments, aiming to distract on the flanks, while he dealt with the main
      <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">
       Hungarian
      </a>
      army in the center. The Mongols defeated European armies at
      <a href="/wiki/Chmielnik" title="Chmielnik">
       Chmielnik
      </a>
      (18 March 1241),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bra%C5%9Fov" title="Braov">
       Kronstadt
      </a>
      (31 March 1241),
      <a href="/wiki/Legnica" title="Legnica">
       Liegnitz
      </a>
      (9 April 1241),
      <a href="/wiki/Muhi" title="Muhi">
       Muhi
      </a>
      (10 April 1241), and
      <a href="/wiki/Sibiu" title="Sibiu">
       Hermannstadt
      </a>
      (10 April 1241). Hungary was overrun. The Mongols set out for home in 1242, after learning that gedei had died, relieving
      <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">
       Vienna
      </a>
      and the rest of Central Europe from further assaults.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Attack_on_central_and_eastern_Europe">
       Attack on central and eastern Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Attack on central and eastern Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The attack on Europe was planned and carried out by Subutai, who achieved his lasting fame with his victories there. Having devastated the various Russian Principalities, he sent spies as far as Poland, Hungary, and Austria, in preparation for an attack into the heartland of Europe. Having a clear picture of the European kingdoms, he brilliantly prepared an attack nominally commanded by Batu Khan and two other princes of the blood. While Batu Khan, son of
      <a href="/wiki/Jochi" title="Jochi">
       Jochi
      </a>
      , was the overall leader, Subutai was the actual commander in the field, and as such was present in both the northern and southern campaigns against
      <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">
       Kievan Rus'
      </a>
      . He also commanded the central column that moved against the
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">
       Kingdom of Hungary
      </a>
      . While
      <a href="/wiki/Kadan" title="Kadan">
       Kadan
      </a>
      's northern force won the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Legnica" title="Battle of Legnica">
       Battle of Legnica
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCy%C3%BCk_Khan" title="Gyk Khan">
       Gyk
      </a>
      's army triumphed in
      <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">
       Transylvania
      </a>
      , Subutai was waiting for them on the Hungarian plain.
     </p>
     <p>
      King
      <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_IV_of_Hungary" title="Bla IV of Hungary">
       Bla IV of Hungary
      </a>
      had summoned a council of war at
      <a href="/wiki/Esztergom" title="Esztergom">
       Esztergom
      </a>
      , a large and important settlement upriver from
      <a href="/wiki/Buda" title="Buda">
       Buda
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pest,_Hungary" title="Pest, Hungary">
       Pest
      </a>
      . As Batu was advancing on Hungary from the northeast, the Hungarian leadership decided to concentrate their strength at Pest and then head north to confront the Mongol army. When news of the Hungarian battle strategy reached the Mongol commanders, they slowly withdrew to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sajo_River" title="Sajo River">
       Sajo River
      </a>
      , drawing their enemies on. This was a classic Mongol strategy, ultimately perfected by Subutai. He prepared a battlefield suitable to his tactics, and waited for his enemies to blunder in. It was a strong position, because woods prevented their ranks from being clearly scouted or seen, while across the river on the plain of Mohi, the Hungarian army was widely exposed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Only one day after the smaller Mongol army in
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      had won the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Legnica" title="Battle of Legnica">
       Battle of Legnica
      </a>
      , Subutai launched his attack, thus beginning the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mohi" title="Battle of Mohi">
       Battle of Mohi
      </a>
      during the night of April 10, 1241. At Mohi, a single division crossed the river in secret to advance on the Hungarian camp from the southern flank. The main body began to cross the Sajo by the bridge at Mohi, and continued to attack the following day. This was met with fierce resistance, so
      <a href="/wiki/Catapult" title="Catapult">
       catapults
      </a>
      were used to clear the opposite bank of crossbowmen, as was noted earlier. When the crossing was completed, the second contingent attacked from the south.
     </p>
     <p>
      The result was complete panic, and, to ensure that the Hungarians did not fight to the last man, the
      <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">
       Mongols
      </a>
      left an obvious gap in their encirclement. This was one of Subutai's classic tricks, to create a tactical situation which appeared to be favorable to the enemy, but which was anything but. The Mongols had already incurred heavier than usual casualties as the Hungarian crossbowmen had done considerable damage to the Mongol cavalry. Subutai did not want a battle where the massed crossbowmen, supported by mounted Knights, stood firm and fought to the death against his army. He far preferred to let them flee and be slaughtered individually. The gap in the Mongol lines was an invitation to retreat, which would leave the Knights and crossbowmen spread out all over the countryside, easy pickings for the disciplined Mongols. As Subutai had planned, the Hungarians poured through this apparent hole in the Mongol lines, which led to a swampy area, poor footing for horses and hard going for infantry. When the Hungarian knights split up, the Mongol archers picked them off at will. It was later noted that corpses littered the countryside over the space of a two-day journey. Two archbishops and three bishops were killed at the Sajo, plus 40,000 fighting men. At one stroke, the bulk of Hungarian fighting men were totally destroyed, with relatively minimal casualties to the Mongols, reportedly less than 1,000 men.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">
       Final years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subutai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Final years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      By late 1241, Subutai was discussing plans to invade the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      , when news came of the death of
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%96gedei_Khan" title="gedei Khan">
       gedei Khan
      </a>
      . Over the objections of Subutai, the Mongol Princes withdrew the army to Mongolia for the election of a new Great Khan. The death of gedei effectively put an end to the Mongol invasion of Europe.
     </p>
     <p>
      Subutai insisted that Batu attend the kurultai to elect the successor of Ogedei in Mongolian heartland. Batu declined to come and
      <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCy%C3%BCk_Khan" title="Gyk Khan">
       Gyk
      </a>
      was elected after 3 years. Gyk had no love for Batu and wanted this best of the Mongol generals unavailable to Batu if the feud between them came to open war. The new Khagan placed Subutai in charge at the age of 70 of the campaign against the
      <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">
       Song dynasty
      </a>
      for 12461247. The Papal envoy
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plano_Carpini" title="Plano Carpini">
       Plano Carpini
      </a>
      saw him when he was in
      <a href="/wiki/Karakorum" title="Karakorum">
       Karakorum
      </a>
      , Mongolia. He said Subutai was well respected among the Mongols and called Knight/Valiant (translation of Baghatur). Subutai returned to Mongolia from the Song campaign in 1248 and spent the rest of his life at his home in the vicinity of the
      <a href="/wiki/Tuul_River" title="Tuul River">
       Tuul River
      </a>
      (near modern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulaanbaatar" title="Ulaanbaatar">
       Ulaanbaatar
      </a>
      ), dying there at the age of 72. His descendants such as Uryankhadai and
      <a href="/wiki/Aju" title="Aju">
       Aju
      </a>
      would serve the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Khans" title="Great Khans">
       Great Khans
      </a>
      for the next three decades as commanders.
     </p>
