          <h1 class="page-title">
           10 Color Inspiration Secrets Only Designers Know About
          </h1>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="col3">

            <p class="intro-text content-element left ">
             <em>
              Color: what a deep and mysterious language!
             </em>
            </p>
            <div class="text-element content-element">
             <p>
              <a href="#_ftn1" name="_ftnref1">
               <sup>
                [1]
               </sup>
              </a>
              Specific hues can provoke different emotions, associations, and responses that affect how your brand is perceived. Put simply, color choices can make or break a design. In fact, research has shown that color can increase brand recognition (by up to 80%), memory, engagement with a design piece, text comprehension, among
              <a href="http://www.colorcom.com/research/why-color-matters">
               many others
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              Fortunately, we are far from the times when our color choices were limited to a small batch of natural pigments. Our options are no longer whatever colors minerals, animals, and plants had to offer. Synthetic pigments and the screen have made our lives increasingly easier, while making deciding infinitely more complex. With such an overwhelming amount of color options, selecting a palette for a design project has become excruciating, to say the least. The Colourlovers community has indexed nearly
              <a href="http://www.colourlovers.com/colors">
               8 million user-named colors
              </a>
              , while there are over 16 million possible hexadecimal color combinations.
             </p>
             <p>
              Overwhelmed yet? No need to worry. We asked top designers from the
              <a href="https://creativemarket.com">
               Creative Market
              </a>
              community to share their best tricks &amp; advice for creating stunning color combinations. Take note of these 10 insider secrets and bring them into your next Canva project:
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="#_ftnref1" name="_ftn1">
               <sup>
                <sup>
                 [1]
                </sup>
               </sup>
              </a>
              Words from French artist Paul Gauguin
             </p>
            </div>
            <h2 class="section-title left section-title-number ">
             <span class="title-number">
              01.
             </span>
             Capture inspiration on-the-go.
            </h2>
            <div class="text-element content-element">
             <p>
              Callie Hegstrom, the talented designer behind
              <a href="https://creativemarket.com/MakeMediaCo.">
               Make Media
              </a>
              , suggests:
              <em>
               I snap photos of gorgeous color schemes (like flowers, or sunsets), and later sample those colors directly in Photoshop or Illustrator. Its also a great way to match text or graphics with any photo youre working with to make sure your work is cohesive.
              </em>
             </p>
             <p>
              So, if you have a photograph with a colour scheme that you love, sample colours directly from itto make a quick, easy and effective palette.Tools like
              <a href="http://www.colourlovers.com/photocopa">
               Photocopa
              </a>
              make this technique even easier. Simplyupload your image, explore the different hues that make it up, and build a stunningcolor palette in no time at all.
             </p>

            <h2 class="section-title left section-title-number ">
             <span class="title-number">
              02.
             </span>
             Use your color wheel
            </h2>
            <div class="text-element content-element">
             <p>
              Artist Marc Chagall came up with a pretty amazing quote to remember what works in terms of color combinations: 
              <em>
               All colors are the friends of their neighbors and the lovers of their opposites
              </em>
              . In this case, Friends stands for analogous colors: those that are side by side on a 12-part color wheel. On the other hand, Chagall refers to complementary colors as Lovers; tones that are directly opposite each other on the wheel.
             </p>

             </span>
             Match color to your topics mood
            </h2>
