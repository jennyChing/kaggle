       <h3 class="post-title left">
        <a href="http://layersmagazine.com/create-a-talking-head-with-adobe-after-effects.html" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Create a Talking Head with Adobe After Effects">
         Create a Talking Head with Adobe After Effects
        </a>
       </h3>
       <p class="post-info right">
        <span>
         By
         <a href="http://layersmagazine.com/author/richard-harrington" rel="author" title="Posts by Richard Harrington">
          Richard Harrington
         </a>
        </span>
        in
        <a href="http://layersmagazine.com/category/tutorials/aftereffects" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in After Effects">
         After Effects
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="http://layersmagazine.com/category/tutorials/photoshop" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in Photoshop">
         Photoshop
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="http://layersmagazine.com/category/tutorials" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in Tutorial">
         Tutorial
        </a>
        <br/>
        Thursday November 2, 2006
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- End post-meta -->
      <div class="post-box">
       <div class="page-content clearfix">
        <div class="clearfix">
         <p>
          <strong>
           Can You Talk the Talk?
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           BY RICHARD HARRINGTON &amp; MARCUS GEDULD
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Recently, we needed to create a Monty Python-style talking head in Adobe After Effects and, as with most of our projects, the deadline was yesterday. So there was no time to keyframe the mouth movements. With a little know-how, we were able to craft a chatterbox faster than you can say Spam. In this tutorial, well take you step-by-step through the process, beginning with the sound recording in Adobe Audition, moving on to the image prep in Adobe Photoshop, and ending up in good old Adobe After Effects, where a little Expression magic saves the day.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Record Sound File in Audition
         </h3>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 1 Create a High-Quality Sound File
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Plug a microphone into your PC and launch Audition. Make sure youre working in Edit view, and click the Record icon. When Audition displays the New Waveform dialog, choose high-quality settings (e.g., a Sample Rate of 48000, which is DVD-quality sound). You can always export the sound at a lower quality later if necessary.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_01.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 2 Prepare File for Export
          </strong>
          <br/>
          After speaking into the mic, click Stop, then choose File&gt;Save As to save the sound. Choose the WAV format for uncompressed (highest quality) sound or MP3 for a smaller file size (but still a pretty good sound for dialog). The Options button reveals controls for fine-tuning the export. In this example, we accepted all the defaults. Once youve saved the WAV or MP3, you can close out of Audition.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_02.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <h3>
          Prepare Images in Photoshop
         </h3>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 1 Choose Your Two Images
          </strong>
          <br/>
          In Photoshop, choose File&gt;Open and navigate to your two images: one of a closed-mouth face and one of an open mouth with teeth and a tongue.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_03.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 2 Place Images into a Single File
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Choose the Move tool (V) and drag-and-drop the closed-mouth image onto the teeth and tongue image.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_04.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 3 Make a Selection
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Choose the Lasso tool (L) and make a selection of the chin and lower lip of the closed-mouth layer (Layer 1).
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_05.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 4 Cut-and-Paste Selection into New Layer
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Press Shift-Command-J (PC: Shift-Control-J) to cut-and-paste the chin selection onto a new layer. Use the Move tool to drag the chin down a little from its original position, as if the mouth is open.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_06.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 5 Rename Layers; Save File as PSD
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Now you need to move and resize the lower layer. Photoshop will only let you do this if you rename it something other than Background. In the Layers palette, double-click the text Background and rename the layer teeth. Now, using the Move tool and Free Transform command (Command-T [PC: Control-T]), adjust the lower layer (move, resize, and rotate it) until you can see the teeth and tongue showing through the open mouth on the upper layer. Rename the middle layer face and the top layer chin. Save the file as a PSD and close out of Photoshop.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_07.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <h3>
          Animate Chin in After Effects
         </h3>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 1 Import the PSD
          </strong>
          <br/>
          In After Effects, choose File&gt;Import&gt;File from the menu bar. Select the PSD and choose Composition  Cropped Layers from the Import As drop-down menu. This will import the PSD file as a layered AE Composition, keeping the layer order and layer names intact.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_08.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 2 Import the Sound File
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Find the imported comp in the Project panel and double-click it to open it in the Timeline and Comp windows. Import the sound file that you created in Audition and add it to the Comp.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_09.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 3 Convert Audio to Keyframes
          </strong>
          <br/>
          With the audio layer selected, choose Animation&gt;Keyframe Assistant&gt;Convert Audio to Keyframes. After Effects will create a new layer called Audio Amplitude and add it to the Comp. Twirl this layer open to reveal its Effects in the Timeline. Twirl open Effects and then the Both Channels effect (which AE added automatically). Youll see a property called Slider with keyframes on every frame. These keyframes contain volume information from the audio layer.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_10.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 4 Add an Expression to Chin Layer
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Select the chin layer and move it back into its original position (closed mouth). Press P to reveal its Position property, and then Option-click (PC: Alt-click) the propertys Stopwatch icon to add an Expression. Drag the Pick Whip and release it when its pointing to the Slider property on the Audio Amplitude level.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_11.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 5 The Expression Is Added
          </strong>
          <br/>
          After Effects adds an Expression to the Position property of the chin layer, as shown in the image above.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_12.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 6 Modify the Expression
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Click inside the Expression to place a text cursor in it. Highlight all of the text that appears after (Slider) and replace it with the following text:
         </p>
         <p>
          * 10;
          <br/>
          [position[0], position[1] + temp]
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Note:
          </em>
          There should be a space both before and after the asterisk (*). Also, make sure you dont accidentally erase the semi-colon (after the 10) or the square brackets on the beginning and end of the second line.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_13.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           STEP 7 Time to Play
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Play the Comp. To make the face shout, change the 10 in the Expression to a larger number. To make the face whisper, change the 10 to a smaller number.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" src="http://media.kelbymediagroup.com/layersmagazine/images/tutorials/design/premiere/2006_02/step_14.jpg"/>
