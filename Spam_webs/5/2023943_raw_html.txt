     <p>
      <b>
       Soup
      </b>
      is a primarily liquid
      <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">
       food
      </a>
      , generally served warm (but may be cool or cold), that is made by combining ingredients such as
      <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">
       meat
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">
       vegetables
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Stock_(food)" title="Stock (food)">
       stock
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">
       juice
      </a>
      , water, or another
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">
       liquid
      </a>
      . Hot soups are additionally characterized by boiling solid ingredients in liquids in a pot until the flavors are extracted, forming a
      <a href="/wiki/Broth" title="Broth">
       broth
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Traditionally, soups are classified into two main groups:
      <i>
       clear soups
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       thick soups
      </i>
      . The established
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       French
      </a>
      classifications of clear soups are
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bouillon_(broth)" title="Bouillon (broth)">
        bouillon
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Consomm%C3%A9" title="Consomm">
        consomm
       </a>
      </i>
      . Thick soups are classified depending upon the type of thickening agent used:
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pur%C3%A9e" title="Pure">
        pures
       </a>
      </i>
      are vegetable soups thickened with starch;
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bisque_(food)" title="Bisque (food)">
        bisques
       </a>
      </i>
      are made from pured
      <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">
       shellfish
      </a>
      or vegetables thickened with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">
        cream
       </a>
      </i>
      ; cream soups may be thickened with
      <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9chamel_sauce" title="Bchamel sauce">
       bchamel sauce
      </a>
      ; and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Velout%C3%A9_sauce" title="Velout sauce">
        velouts
       </a>
      </i>
      are thickened with
      <a href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">
       eggs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">
       butter
      </a>
      , and cream. Other ingredients commonly used to thicken soups and broths include
      <a href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">
       egg
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">
       rice
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lentils" title="Lentils">
       lentils
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">
       flour
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">
       grains
      </a>
      ; many popular soups also include carrots and potatoes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Soups are similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Stew" title="Stew">
       stews
      </a>
      , and in some cases there may not be a clear distinction between the two; however, soups generally have more liquid than stews.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       History

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_(1825-1905)_-_Soup_(1865).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="732" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281825-1905%29_-_Soup_%281865%29.jpg/220px-William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281825-1905%29_-_Soup_%281865%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281825-1905%29_-_Soup_%281865%29.jpg/330px-William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281825-1905%29_-_Soup_%281865%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281825-1905%29_-_Soup_%281865%29.jpg/440px-William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281825-1905%29_-_Soup_%281865%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_(1825-1905)_-_Soup_(1865).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/William-Adolphe_Bouguereau" title="William-Adolphe Bouguereau">
         William-Adolphe Bouguereau
        </a>
        <i>
         Soup
        </i>
        (1865)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Evidence of the existence of soup can be found as far back as about 20,000 BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Boiling was not a common cooking technique until the invention of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterproof" title="Waterproof">
       waterproof
      </a>
      containers (which probably came in the form of
      <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">
       clay
      </a>
      vessels). Animal hides and watertight baskets of bark or reeds were used before this. To boil the water hot rocks were used. This method was also used to cook acorns and other plants.
     </p>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       soup
      </i>
      comes from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_(language)" title="Latin (language)">
       French
      </a>
      <i>
       soupe
      </i>
      ("soup", "broth"), which comes through
      <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
       Vulgar Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       suppa
      </i>
      ("bread soaked in broth") from a
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
       Germanic
      </a>
      source, from which also comes the word "
      <a href="/wiki/Sop" title="Sop">
       sop
      </a>
      ", a piece of
      <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">
       bread
      </a>
      used to soak up soup or a thick
      <a href="/wiki/Stew" title="Stew">
       stew
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">
        restaurant
       </a>
      </i>
      (meaning "[something] restoring") was first used in
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      in the 16th century, to refer to a highly concentrated, inexpensive soup, sold by street vendors, that was advertised as an
      <a href="/wiki/Antidote" title="Antidote">
       antidote
      </a>
      to physical
      <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">
       exhaustion
      </a>
      . In 1765, a Parisian entrepreneur opened a shop specializing in such soups. This prompted the use of the modern word
      <i>
       restaurant
      </i>
      for the eating establishments.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       US
      </a>
      , the first
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/colonial" title="wiktionary:colonial">
       colonial
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Cookbook" title="Cookbook">
       cookbook
      </a>
      was published by William Parks in
      <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Virginia" title="Williamsburg, Virginia">
       Williamsburg, Virginia
      </a>
      , in 1742, based on Eliza Smith's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Compleat_Housewife" title="The Compleat Housewife">
        The Compleat Housewife
       </a>
       ; or Accomplished Gentlewoman's Companion
      </i>
      and it included several recipes for soups and bisques. A 1772 cookbook,
      <i>
       The Frugal Housewife
      </i>
      , contained an entire chapter on the topic. English cooking dominated early colonial cooking; but as new
      <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">
       immigrants
      </a>
      arrived from other countries, other national soups gained popularity. In particular,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">
       German
      </a>
      immigrants living in
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">
       Pennsylvania
      </a>
      were famous for their
      <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">
       potato
      </a>
      soups. In 1794, Jean Baptiste Gilbert Payplat dis Julien, a refugee from the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      , opened an eating establishment in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">
       Boston
      </a>
      called "
      <a href="/wiki/Julien%27s_Restorator" title="Julien's Restorator">
       The Restorator
      </a>
      ", and became known as the "Prince of Soups". The first American cooking
      <a href="/wiki/Pamphlet" title="Pamphlet">
       pamphlet
      </a>
      dedicated to soup recipes was written in 1882 by Emma Ewing:
      <i>
       Soups and Soup Making
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Portable_soup" title="Portable soup">
       Portable soup
      </a>
      was devised in the 18th century by boiling seasoned meat until a thick,
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/resin" title="wiktionary:resin">
       resinous
      </a>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/syrup" title="wiktionary:syrup">
       syrup
      </a>
      was left that could be dried and stored for months at a time.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_products">
       Commercial products
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Commercial soup became popular with the invention of
      <a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">
       canning
      </a>
      in the 19th century, and today a great variety of canned and dried soups are on the market.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canned">
       Canned
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Campbell_Soup_Company" title="Campbell Soup Company">

      </a>
      , invented condensed soup in 1897.

        </span>

       </a>
      </sup>
      Today, Campbell's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomato_Soup" title="Tomato Soup">
       Tomato
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cream_of_mushroom_soup" title="Cream of mushroom soup">
       Cream of Mushroom
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Chicken_soup" title="Chicken soup">
       Chicken Noodle Soup
      </a>
      are three of the most popular soups in America. Americans consume approximately 2.5 billion bowls of these three soups alone each year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbells_history_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Campbells_history-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Tin_can" title="Tin can">
       Canned
      </a>
      soup can be condensed, in which case it is prepared by adding
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      (or sometimes
      <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">
       milk
      </a>
      ), or it can be "ready-to-eat," meaning that no additional liquid is needed before eating. Canned soup (condensed with liquid added, or "ready-to-eat") can be prepared by heating in a
      <a href="/wiki/Cookware_and_bakeware" title="Cookware and bakeware">
       pan
      </a>
      , on the stovetop or in the
      <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">
       microwave
      </a>
      . Such soups can be used as a
      <a href="/wiki/Cooking_base" title="Cooking base">
       base
      </a>
      for homemade soups, with the consumer adding anything from a few vegetables to eggs, meat, cream or pasta.
     </p>
     <p>
      Condensing soup allows soup to be packaged into a smaller can and sold at a lower price than other canned soups. The soup is usually doubled in volume by adding a "can full" of water or milk (about 10 ounces).
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the 1990s, the canned soup market has burgeoned with soups marketed as "ready-to-eat," which require no additional liquid to prepare. Microwaveable bowls have expanded the ready-to-eat canned soup market even more, offering convenience (especially in workplaces) and are popular lunch items.
     </p>
     <p>
      In response to concern over the health effects of excessive
      <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
       salt
      </a>
      intake, some soup manufacturers have introduced reduced-salt versions of popular soups.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fruit">
       Fruit
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Fruit_soup" title="Fruit soup">
       Fruit soup
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fruit soups are prepared using fruit as a primary ingredient, and may be served warm or cold depending on the recipe. Many varieties of fruit soups exist, and they may be prepared based upon the availability of seasonal fruit.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cold">
       Cold
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cold">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Cold soups are a particular variation on the traditional soup, wherein the temperature when served is kept at or below room temperature. They may be sweet or savory. In summer, sweet cold soups can form part of a
      <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">
       dessert
      </a>
      tray. An example of a savory chilled soup is
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gazpacho" title="Gazpacho">
        gazpacho
       </a>
      </i>
      , a chilled vegetable-based soup originating from Spain. Another example is
      <i>
       mool naeng myun
      </i>
      which is a Korean cold beef broth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_figure_of_speech">
       As a figure of speech
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: As a figure of speech">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mirepoix(cuisine)-2009-04-06.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1621" data-file-width="2038" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Mirepoix%28cuisine%29-2009-04-06.jpg/220px-Mirepoix%28cuisine%29-2009-04-06.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Mirepoix%28cuisine%29-2009-04-06.jpg/330px-Mirepoix%28cuisine%29-2009-04-06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Mirepoix%28cuisine%29-2009-04-06.jpg/440px-Mirepoix%28cuisine%29-2009-04-06.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mirepoix(cuisine)-2009-04-06.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Mirepoix_(cuisine)" title="Mirepoix (cuisine)">
         Mirepoix
        </a>
        consists of carrot, onion and celery and is often used for soup stocks and soups.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the English language, the word "soup" has developed several uses in phrase.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet_soup_(linguistics)" title="Alphabet soup (linguistics)">
        Alphabet soup
       </a>
       , a large number of acronyms used by an administration. The term has its roots in a common tomato-based soup containing pasta shaped in the letters of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabet
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       "In the soup" refers to being in a bad situation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">
        Primordial soup
       </a>
       , the organic mixture leading to the development of life.
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Soup_kitchen" title="Soup kitchen">
        soup kitchen
       </a>
       , a place that serves prepared food of any kind to the homeless.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Souperism" title="Souperism">
        Souperism
       </a>
       , the practice of bible societies during the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">
        Irish Great Famine
       </a>
       to feed the hungry in exchange for religious instruction. The expression 'took the soup' is used to refer to those who converted at the behest of these organizations offers of food.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_soup" title="Stone soup">
         Stone soup
        </a>
       </i>
       , a popular children's fable about a poor man who encourages villagers to share their food with him by telling them that he can make soup with a stone.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Czernina" title="Czernina">
        Duck soup
       </a>
       , a simple soup, stands for a task that is particularly easy.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Duck_Soup_(1933_film)" title="Duck Soup (1933 film)">
        <i>
         Duck Soup
        </i>
       </a>
       , title of a Marx Brothers' film in 1933.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Tag_soup" title="Tag soup">
        Tag soup
       </a>
       , poorly coded
       <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">
        HTML
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       "From
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Soup_to_nuts" title="Soup to nuts">
        soup to nuts
       </a>
       " means from beginning to end, referring to the traditional position of soup as the first course in a multi-course meal.
      </li>
     </ul>
