      <h1>
       Does Ridesharing Economics Make Sense Now, Even Without Self-Driving Cars?
      </h1>
      <div class="metadata" role="contentinfo">
       <div class="byline">
        By Philip E. Ross
        <div>

      <div class="entry-content">
       <p>
        Autonomous cars lend themselves to lending themselves, proponents like to say: we'll rent them instead of owning them. That way, we'll make better use of a smaller number of cars and thus pay much less on average per passenger-mile.
       </p>
       <p>
        If so, then your next car may well be your lastthat is, if you can keep it going for the 3 to 50 years it may take before cars become fully, utterly robotic. Three
        <a href="http://bigstory.ap.org/article/google-driverless-cars-are-mastering-city-streets">
         if you're a techno-optimist
        </a>
        like Sergei Brin, of Google and its famous Google Car; 50 years
        <a href="http://www.economist.com/news/special-report/21576224-one-day-every-car-may-come-invisible-chauffeur-look-no-hands">
         if you're a technopessimist,
        </a>
        like Volkswagen research director Jrgen Leohold.
       </p>
       <p>
        Or maybe you've already bought your last car no matter which side is right. An entire ecosystem of car sharing is aborning right now,
        <a href="http://spectrum.ieee.org/view-from-the-valley/at-work/innovation/uber-lyft-airbnbhow-did-we-live-without-them">
         thanks to startups like Uber and Lyft
        </a>
        , and it's based on merely human drivers. True, sharing would be even more attractive with robots, which probably explains why
        <a href="http://www.wired.com/2014/01/uber-travis-kalanick/">
         Google recently invested
        </a>
        US$348 million in Uber. The startup was
        <a href="http://dealbook.nytimes.com/2014/06/09/how-uber-pulls-in-billions-all-via-iphone/?_php=true&amp;_type=blogs&amp;_r=0">
         recently valued
        </a>
        at around $18 billion
        <a href="http://www.marketwatch.com/investing/stock/fiaty">
         half again as much as Fiat
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        How much money would full-bore carsharing save? Larry Burns, the former head of research for General Motors and now the director of the
        <a href="http://sustainablemobility.ei.columbia.edu/">
         Program on Sustainable Mobility at Columbia University
        </a>
        , has estimated that we'd need just 15 percent as many cars as now. That would
        <a href="http://sustainablemobility.ei.columbia.edu/files/2012/12/Transforming-Personal-Mobility-Jan-27-20132.pdf">
         cut costs per mile by 75 percent
        </a>
        in Ann Arbor, Mich., according to his calculations.
       </p>
       <!--nextpage-->
       <p>
        Now comes a more elaborate formula from Kyle Hill, a Los Angeles-based web designer and former financial advisor.
        <a href="https://medium.com/@kaleazy/a-financial-model-comparing-car-ownership-with-uberx-los-angeles-b7becd917095">
         He figures
        </a>
        that the average car in the United States costs $12,700 a year to own, all things included (even traffic tickets). Uber would cost exactly the same, he figures, if you drove precisely 15,170 kilometers a year. Drive more, and owning is the better option; drive less, and Uber is the way to go.
       </p>
       <p>
        How about simple smartphone apps that help people join together to share a cab? According to a study in
        <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1403657111">
         <em>
          Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences
         </em>
        </a>
        , researchers at MIT, Cornell, and Italy's Istituto di Informatica e Telematica del Consiglio Nazionale delle Ricerche, people willing to put up with an average delay of just five minutes would be able to
        <a href="http://www.pnas.org/content/early/2014/08/27/1403657111.full.pdf+html?sid=cd55e636-041d-4661-9910-37f4ef62e85d">
         share a cab 95 percent of the time
        </a>
        . The scheme would cut total, cumulative trip length by at least 40 percent.
       </p>
       <p>
        And it works even if the taxicab is dull as dishwaterso long as the phone is smart.
       </p>
