  <p>
   <b>
    Explanation:
   </b>
   Cradled in cosmic dust and glowing hydrogen,
   <a href="http://archive.ncsa.uiuc.edu/Cyberia/Bima/StarForm.html">
    stellar
nurseries
   </a>
   in Orion the
   <a href="http://www.clarkfoundation.org/astro-utah/vondel/slimone.html">
    Hunter
   </a>
   <a href="http://messier.seds.org/more/oricloud.html">
    lie at
the edge
   </a>
   of giant molecular clouds some 1,500 light-years away.

Spanning about 30 degrees,
   <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/deepskycolors/16774216742">
    this breath-taking vista
   </a>
   stretches across the well-known constellation from
   <a href="ap101023.html">
    head to toe
   </a>
   (left to right) and beyond.

At 1,500
   <a href="http://www.grc.nasa.gov/WWW/k-12/Numbers/Math/Mathematical_Thinking/how_long_is_a_light_year.htm">
    light years
   </a>
   away, the
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Nebula">
    Great Orion
Nebula
   </a>
   is the closest large star forming region, here visible
just right and below center.

To its left are the
   <a href="ap100513.html">
    Horsehead Nebula
   </a>
   ,
   <a href="ap100302.html">
    M78
   </a>
   , and
   <a href="ap131212.html">
    Orion's belt
   </a>
   stars.

Sliding your cursor over the picture will also find
red giant
   <a href="ap100106.html">
    Betelgeuse
   </a>
   at the hunter's shoulder,
bright blue
   <a href="http://stars.astro.illinois.edu/sow/rigel.html">
    Rigel
   </a>
   at his foot, 
the
   <a href="ap121101.html">
    Witch Head Nebula
   </a>
   above -- and illuminated by -- Rigel, 
and the glowing Lambda Orionis
(
   <a href="http://www.gb.nrao.edu/~rmaddale/Education/OrionTourCenter/
meissa.html">
    Meissa
   </a>
   ) nebula on the left, near Orion's head.

Of course, the Orion Nebula and bright stars are
   <a href="ap030207.html">
    easy to see
   </a>
   with the
unaided eye, but dust clouds and emission from the extensive interstellar
gas in this
   <a href="ap070106.html">
    nebula-rich
   </a>
   complex,
are too faint and much harder to record.

In this mosaic of broadband telescopic images, additional image
data acquired with a narrow
   <a href="ap051223.html">
    hydrogen alpha
   </a>
   filter was used to
bring out the pervasive tendrils of energized
atomic hydrogen gas like in the arc of the giant
   <a href="ap120123.html">
    Barnard's Loop
   </a>
   .
