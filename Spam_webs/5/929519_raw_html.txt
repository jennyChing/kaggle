        <h1 class="entry-title single-title" itemprop="headline" rel="bookmark">
         8 1/2 types of content that go viral on Pinterest
        </h1>
        <p class="byline entry-meta vcard">
         posted by
         <span class="entry-author author" itemprop="author" itemptype="http://schema.org/Person" itemscope="">
          <a href="http://blog.raventools.com/author/guest-writer/" rel="author" title="Posts by Guest Writer">
           Guest Writer
          </a>
         </span>
         on
         <time class="updated entry-time" datetime="2012-05-04" itemprop="datePublished">
          May 4, 2012
         </time>
        </p>
       </header>
       <section class="entry-content cf post_typography" itemprop="articleBody">
        <div id="intro-parag">
        </div>
        <div id="post-feat-image">
         <img alt="Ryan Gosling Pinterest" class="attachment-full wp-post-image" height="330" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/ryan-630x2151.png" width="660"/>
        </div>
        <p>
         Its official: the Internet community has officially gone off the deep end over Pinterest.
        </p>
        <p>
         We tweet our pins. We Facebook our pins. We write articles about how Pinterest is the Next Big Thing and how it Sends So Much Traffic and how its basically the Greatest Thing Since Twitter. And yes, America, Pinterest may be the Greatest Thing Since Twitter (Ill let you duke that out in the comments section).
        </p>
        <p>
         Yet as the site becomes increasingly saturated, Im increasingly disappointed at all the users who get it wrong. Pinterest has a flavor and a style all on its own  and like any social media site, there are unofficial rules that savvy Pinterest users abide by.
        </p>
        <p>
         I know it seems strange to think of guidelines on a site where cat pictures, fashion magazines, cookbooks, and craft seminars meld into one big ADHD melting pot, but Pinterest is already showing signs of sagging under mass user influx.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Theres a right way and a wrong way to use Pinterest
        </h4>
        <p>
         Im sorry, folks, but you cant post random images off your blog and expect them to gain traction. You cant just post pictures of your products and wait for the referral traffic to come pouring in. And for heavens sake, Pinterest is not a Facebook photo album. You cant just pin random pictures from your life and expect Pinterest users to care.
        </p>
        <p>
         You should, however, use Pinterest as it was intended: a visual idea board, a form of visual storytelling, a way to inspire others and to be inspired. Brands that remember this gain Pinterest followers and repins by the thousands. The brands who forget it fail.
        </p>
        <p>
         We dont often talk of going viral on Pinterest, but pins regularly do go viral  this simple quote alone garnered more than 13,000 repins:
        </p>
        <img alt="quote" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22037" height="221" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/quote.png" title="quote" width="226"/>
        <p>
         The trick is to recognize which types of content will do well on the site  and then use those types to propel your brand.
        </p>
        <h4>
         8 types of content that regularly go viral on Pinterest
        </h4>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1. Tutorials
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="tutorial" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-22035" height="630" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/tutorial-167x630.png" title="tutorial" width="167"/>
        <p>
         Pinterest has a strong help-me-help-you vibe. Help your followers help themselves by creating tutorials. Image-based tutorials (such as the one above) often get more repins, but images that link to a tutorial (an image that says How to Turn a T-shirt into a Tank Top, for example) often bring more site traffic.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          2. Follower-friendly tips and tricks
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="tips-tricks" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22034" height="393" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/tips.png" title="tips-tricks" width="228"/>
        <p>
         Little tips and tricks (Catch gnats with apple cider vinegar! or Use a dryer sheet to repel mosquitos!) spread like wildfire on Pinterest. As one of the few tech brands to immediately hop on the Pinterest train, Mashable has amassed an impressive following on Pinterest thanks in part to their
         <a href="https://www.pinterest.com/mashable/?redirected=1">
          Tips &amp; Tricks pinboard
         </a>
         (and pins like this one).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          3. Inspiring and/or funny quotes
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="inspiring-quotes" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22033" height="345" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/inspiring.png" title="inspiring-quotes" width="230"/>
        <p>
         Find a quote that sum up your company mottos or beliefs? Got any brand-appropriate quotes that make you laugh? Pin them  few things spread faster on Pinterest than quotes.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          4. Deadline-oriented results
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="deadline-oriented" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22032" height="197" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/time-sensitive.png" title="deadline-oriented" width="229"/>
        <p>
         Pinterest users adore an implied promise of results within a certain timeline. A cleaner house in seven days? A safer browser in fifteen minutes? These pins sound too good for users to pass up. Heck, even I couldnt resist clicking on this pin (and Ill have you know my hands are now incredibly soft).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          5. Resource libraries
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="resource-libraries" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22031" height="288" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/resource.png" title="resource-libraries" width="241"/>
        <p>
         Collections of useful information inspire pinners to save these resources for future use  a sort of visual bookmark, if you will. Offering a link that promises quality content with a huge collection of information is a terrific way to attract tons of site traffic.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          6. Photo-heavy infographics
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="infographic" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22030" height="416" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/infographic.png" title="infographic" width="225"/>
        <p>
         Infographics are huge on Pinterest, but not the statistics-packed, long-as-your-arm infographics youre used to. Shorter, photo-heavy infographics like this one tend to attract more attention.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          7. List infographics
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="list-info" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22029" height="426" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/list-info.png" title="list-info" width="225"/>
        <p>
         This mega-list of Dr. Oz-recommended diet products (posted by none other than Dr. Oz himself) is a fantastic example of brand-targeted, Pinterest-friendly content. Users arent going to analyze each food product on the list, but the sheer length makes them feel compelled to share it with their friends. Its also a good example of a visual bookmark pin (similar to #5), since its a list each pinner can refer back to later.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          8. Must-share photos
         </strong>
        </p>
        <img alt="must-share-photos" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22028" height="356" src="http://blog.raventools.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/elephant.png" title="must-share-photos" width="224"/>
        <p>
         The entire concept of Pinterest revolves around images. Users decide which link to click, which product to buy, which pins to share purely on images. So its no surprise, then, that some of the most popular content on Pinterest are photos  but not just any photos, mind you. Users are captivated by the must-share photo: those images that cause a knee-jerk reaction within us, make us smile, make us laugh, make us sigh. These pics have the must-share factor, and they spread like wildfire on Pinterest.
        </p>
        <h4>
         The bottom line
        </h4>
        <p>
         Look, obviously theres no magical formula to predict whatll go viral on Pinterest. Just today I had a photo I pinned last summer go from 30 repins to 200 repins in an hour. Pinterest users are a fickle crowd, but theyre not hard to pleaseas long as you remember this above all else:
        </p>
        <p>
         Pinterest is about inspiration.
        </p>
        <p>
         If it doesnt inspire you, dont pin it. If it doesnt cause a reaction in you, dont pin it. If it wont be earnestly helpful to others, dont pin it.
        </p>
        <p>
         Seek to inspire your followers. Youll never disappoint.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          8.5. More Gosling
         </strong>
         <br/>
         And if all else failsjust pin something with Ryan Gosling in it. Seriously. That dudes like crack to Pinterest users.
