             <h1 class="entry-title">
              Trust Matters Issue 8: Warning Will Robinson
             </h1>
             <div class="post-info">
              <span class="date published time" title="2013-12-19T16:06:37+00:00">
               December 19, 2013
              </span>
              <span class="author vcard">
               <span class="fn">
                <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/security/author/bryankfite/" rel="author">
                 Bryan K. Fite
                </a>
               </span>
              </span>
              <span class="post-comments">
               <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/security/2013/12/trust-matters-issue-8-warning-will-robinson/#comments">
                <span class="number">
                 2
                </span>
                s
               </a>
              </span>
             </div>
             <div class="entry-content">
              <p>
               <strong>
                This is my final post for 2013, so I thought it would be appropriate to end the year by taking stock of the last twelve months, making some predications about the coming year and issuing a call to action. In addition, as a very special holiday gift, I have included
                <a href="http://day-con.org/sn2013/dc7sn2013.pdf" target="_blank">
                 a link to the Dayton Security Summit Notes
                </a>
                from this years Day-Con VII.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span id="more-4086">
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                APT, aPT or BS?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               Whether you call it an Advanced Persistent Threat, a not-so-advanced Persistent Threat, Asian Pacific Threat, cyber war, or more appropriately, Targeted Attacks, people are waking up to the fact that their computing environments are crawling with digital vermin. With very few exceptions, there is nothing novel, sophisticated or advanced about these attacks. In fact, most follow a standard operating procedure: target a human victim, get the target to click something, compromise victim, initiate lateral movement, exfiltration, objective achieved, rinse and repeat. While charlatans and outsiders profit on the hype, the reasonable minority continue to call for a back to basics approach, or even more radical thinking; disabling plug-ins, ditching Adobe and banning Java Script.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                BYO Whatever.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               The IT world has changed in some very fundamental ways in a very short period of time. Whether we are talking about the cloud, personally liable devices or the Internet of Things, enterprises are being asked to provide applications and services to any IP-enabled device, anywhere, any time with no capital investment on a subscription basis. Enterprises find themselves competing against free internet-based services with new business models. As they attempt to compete, their appetite for risk has become insatiable and unsustainable, whether they realise it or not. The practice of balancing risk and reward has never been more important, with decisions that are being made today having significant ramifications in the future. It is obvious to all involved that the current budgets must be reallocated to more effective controls and a new way of approaching security must be adopted.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                TERM matures.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/security/2012/11/trust-matters-issue-4-trust-o-meter/">
                Dr. Cofta introduced some very interesting theoretical tools
               </a>
               early in 2013 to support his continued development of Trust Enhanced Risk Management (TERM); the Trust-O-Meter, Trust Journey and other assessment approaches, which transformed into practical tools over the year. Using these tools, he convincingly identified the fundamental flaws of the insider threat with his interactive assessment of the Snowden Incident, posing the very relevant question, can we trust Trust?
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Security and privacy.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               The debate has begun in earnest; from Big Data Analytics, mega-breaches and wholesale government surveillance programs (legal or otherwise) people are starting to have a dialogue about balancing the need for security with the right to privacy. They are asking themselves what the right mix is. Some pundits posit that the genie is already out of the bottle and there is no chance for privacy or anonymity in the digital age, prompting Graeme Nielsens declaration that Encryption is the most disruptive technology! It facilitates privacy and enables anonymous currency and financial transactions.
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Looking forward
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               We will continue to see successful targeted attacks, which will be portrayed as sophisticated or advanced. However, a real dialog will emerge to address the root causes regardless of the threat actors or their capabilities.
              </p>
              <p>
               TERM will go mainstream as people see its practical application, driven by the need to trust Trust again.
              </p>
              <p>
               Privacy issues will dominate the global public psyche in the coming year. Continued visibility to big data monetization schemes and government sponsored surveillance will challenge traditional assumptions about privacy, causing new disruptive forces to emerge.
              </p>
              <p>
               Robots and zombies will start to pop up in legitimate threat catalogs and risk registers. Rogue drones, low-cost tech, Googles acquisition of Boston Dynamics and the proliferation of near real-time designer drugs will make some Hollywood-style scenarios worthy of consideration by some organisations.
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
              <p>
               I appreciate your taking the time to read and support my blogging over the last year and looking forward to discussing more Trust Matters in the coming year.
              </p>

               in March.
              </p>
