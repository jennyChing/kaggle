      <h2 class="storytitle">
       Praseodymium
      </h2>
      <div class="storycontent">
       <div class="wpInsert wpInsertInPostAd wpInsertAbove" style="margin-bottom: 10px;">
        <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
        </script>
        <!-- Dan Dumitrache - Above -->
        <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-2470767945324998" data-ad-format="auto" data-ad-slot="1350986034" style="display:block">
        </ins>
        <script>
         (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
        </script>
       </div>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Name:
        </strong>
        Praseodymium
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Symbol:
        </strong>
        Pr
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Number of Energy Levels:
        </strong>
        6
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Electrons on the outer energy level:
        </strong>
        2
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Atomic number:
        </strong>
        59
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Atomic Mass:
        </strong>
        140.90765 Amu (Atomic Mass Units)
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Protons/Electrons:
        </strong>
        59
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Neutrons:
        </strong>
        82
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Density:
        </strong>
        6.77 g/cm
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        <br/>
        <strong>
         Classification:
        </strong>
        Rare Earth
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://dandumitrache.com/praseodymium/praseodymium/" rel="attachment wp-att-3676">
         <img alt="Praseodymium" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3676" height="556" src="http://dandumitrache.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/praseodymium.png" width="862"/>
        </a>
        <span id="more-3675">
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Praseodymium
        </strong>
        was discovered in 1885 by Carl Auer von Welsbach. Its name derives from the Greek words
        <em>
         prasios
        </em>
        ,which means
        <em>
         green
        </em>
        , and
        <em>
         didymos
        </em>
        , which means
        <em>
         twin
        </em>
        . Praseodymiums melting point is at 935.0 C (1208.15 K, 1715.0 F) and its boiling point is at 3127.0 C (3400.15 K, 5660.6 F).
       </p>
       <div class="wpInsert wpInsertInPostAd wpInsertMiddle">
        <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
        </script>
        <!-- Dan Dumitrache - Middle -->
        <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-2470767945324998" data-ad-format="auto" data-ad-slot="1467869632" style="display:block">
        </ins>
        <script>
         (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
        </script>
       </div>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Praseodymium  Facts and information
        </strong>
        <br/>
        Praseodymium is soft, silvery, malleable, and ductile metal.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium is not found freely in nature due to its highly reactive properties.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium is stored under mineral oil.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium has only one stable isotope,
        <sup>
         141
        </sup>
        Pr.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium is found in small quantities in Earths crust in
        <em>
         monazite
        </em>
        and
        <em>
         bastnasite
        </em>
        minerals.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium is used in high-power magnets production, in a special alloy for high-strength metals for aircraft industry, in studio and projector lights, and in glass industry to create yellow glass.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium is not considered to be a toxic metal.
        <br/>
        Praseodymium doesnt have any biological role in living organisms.
       </p>
