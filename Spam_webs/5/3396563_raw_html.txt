         <p>
          I was walking through the interactive children's exhibit at the Anchorage Museum when I got the call. I don't remember much of the conversation after the words: They found a body. Death is not negotiable.
         </p>
         <p>
          Raucous children twirled around me, full of life. Meanwhile I wilted, like a wildflower in mid-September; and was caught by a bench nearby. The sensation of my head in my hands felt too familiar. The young museum attendees that surrounded me could cheer as loud as they wanted: the thought of six bodies--once so full of life--now frozen in the snow drowned everything out. The omnipotent, awe-inspiring mountains had claimed another group of prodigal alpinists. Death is not negotiable.
         </p>
         <p>
          Mountaineers question themselves in times like these. Is man/woman supposed to inhabit glaciated terrain, or sneak their chalked up fingers into cracked slabs of granite as they scale up big rock walls? Yet the question of should they? is an unanswerable one. Rather I wonder: is it worth it?
         </p>
         <p>
          On that day in Anchorage, Alaska I would tell you no. Two of those prodigals were my friends. I had, in some capacity, lent a hand to all six of the fallen in their alpine expedition. We could no longer wonder about their status. We could no longer anticipate their return. Death is so final. Then I wondered, how would the climbers answer? Is it worth it? Again, that is an unanswerable question.
         </p>
         <p>
          Fast forward 36 hours: It's midnight in May in the Kenai Fjords

         National Park and I've just hiked out to see a massive glacier rising up the rugged, Alaskan hillside. This time of the year in the far North lends itself to particularly long days--at this time of the night the glacier remains illuminated by the spring sunshine. Just below me, she flows freely into a river. Just above, her ice field--always active and predictably unpredictable--roars intermittently.
         <p>
          Water cascades down a boulder at my side. The glacier's grandeur leaks into all corner's of this vast wilderness. It was when I knelt down and engaged my sense of touch, allowing her waters to baptize my grief, that I could release my tears. I wept violently in the shadow of my loss, as if to match the relentless flow of snow and ice and debris that enveloped the climbers on the slopes of the mountain they sought so ambitiously to summit. I sat there for some time with my hand submerged in the pure glacial runoff, and she and I wept together.
         </p>
         <p>
          Fast forward 4 months: I am on the summit. Death, in this case, had not become any less of a tragedy. Perhaps it become more of one: life was so much better with them in it. What have we learned? I wondered. That mountains are powerful and potentially dangerous environments? I hope that not a single mountaineer has to witness loss to understand that fact. The only closure I am feeling is that I am as close to them as I can be, standing atop the mountain that they are now literally a part of.

         <em>
          It still could.

         <p>
          Fast forward 6 months: I am lighting two candles at a church in Amsterdam in memory of the fallen. It seems like an unlikely situation for someone to recall such an experience. Especially a non-catholic. Life appeared to be marching onward in their death; however their legends live on. In the last 6 months they've been teaching me; they've been making me laugh and making me cry. I've attended events they've initiated. I've written to them, spoken to them. They're even speaking to you now. Life would still be better with them here. Their death is still no less of a tragedy.
         </p>
         <p>
          But as long as I am still alive, they are too. As long as man/woman is still alive, so are all the fallen.
         </p>

	var ESCAPE_EXPRESSION_FUNCTION, LINK_DETECTION_REGEX, MARKSAFE_FUNCTION, emojify, formatTextForHTML, linkify;

	LINK_DETECTION_REGEX = /(([a-z]+:\/\/)?(([a-z0-9\-]+\.)+([a-z]{2}|aero|arpa|biz|com|coop|edu|gov|info|int|jobs|mil|museum|name|nato|net|org|pro|travel|local|internal))(:[0-9]{1,5})?(\/[a-z0-9_\-\.~]+)*(\/([a-z0-9_\-\.]*)(\?[a-z0-9+_\-\.%=&amp;]*)?)?(#[a-zA-Z0-9!$&amp;'()*+.=-_~:@/?]*)?)(\s+|$)/gi;

	linkify = function(safeContent) {
	  return safeContent.replace(LINK_DETECTION_REGEX, function(url) {
	  	brEnd = false;
	  	if(url.indexOf("\n")) {
	  		brEnd = true;
	  	}
	  	url = url.trim();
	    url = url.replace(/(?:\r\n|\r|\n)/g, '');
	    var address;
	    address = /[a-z]+:\/\//.test(url) ? url : "http://" + url;    
	    url = url.replace(/^https?:\/\//, '');
	    link = "&lt;a href='" + address + "' target='_blank' rel='nofollow'&gt;" + url + "&lt;/a&gt;";
	    if(brEnd) {
	    	return link + '&lt;br/&gt;';
	    } else {
	    	return link;
	    }
	  });
	};
