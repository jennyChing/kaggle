      <h2>
       5 Extended Jazz Guitar Arpeggio Exercises
      </h2>
      <a href="http://mattwarnockguitar.com/matt-warnock-guitar-lessons" id="postad">
       <img src="http://mattwarnockguitar.com/images/2015/06/skype.png"/>
      </a>
      <a href="http://www.jazzguitar.be/store/jazz-blues-guitar.php" id="postad">
       <img src="http://mattwarnockguitar.com/images/2015/06/jazz-blues.png"/>
      </a>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: 13px; line-height: 19px;">
        As you take lessons and continue your journey of learning
       </span>
       <a href="http://mattwarnockguitar.com/building-bebop-vocabulary-volume-1-ebook" style="font-size: 13px; line-height: 19px;" target="_blank">
        how to play jazz guitar
       </a>
       <span style="font-size: 13px; line-height: 19px;">
        , youre told about the importance of
        <strong>
         knowing your arpeggios
        </strong>
        inside and out, as well as being able to run them through changes to create improvised lines and solos.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       But what do you do when you can run all of the arpeggios up and down for any tune you know, as well as use them in an
       <strong>
        improvised context
       </strong>
       ?
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       The answer just might be to start checking out extended jazz guitar arpeggios, those that move beyond the first octave,
       <strong>
        1-3-5-7, fingerings
       </strong>
       that many of us already know.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <h3 style="text-align: center;">
       What Is An Extended Jazz Guitar Arpeggio?
      </h3>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Extended jazz guitar arpeggios are those that go beyond the
       <strong>
        typical 1-3-5-7 shapes
       </strong>
       that we normally study in the the practice room.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, a normal
       <strong>
        Cmaj7 arpeggio
       </strong>
       would be:
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       C-E-G-B or 1-3-5-7
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       The extended jazz guitar arpeggio would take you beyond the 7th, to
       <strong>
        include the 9th
       </strong>
       , 11th (in this case the #11) and 13th like so:
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       C-E-G-B-D-F#-A or 1-3-5-7-9-#11-13
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Being able to play extended jazz guitar arpeggios is a great way to outline chords while at the same time
       <strong>
        stepping beyond
       </strong>
       the first octave of the chord and out into the upper extensions, 9-11-13.
      </p>
      <p>
       You can learn more about these ideas in my in-depth article 
       <a href="http://mattwarnockguitar.com/extending-arpeggios-with-chordal-pairing" target="_blank">
        Extended Jazz Guitar Arpeggios
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       So how do we do this?
      </p>
      <p>
       Well, you could just find the notes that make up the
       <strong>
        9th, 11th and 13th
       </strong>
       for any chord youre working, such as maj7, 7, m7 or mMaj7, but that might take too much time or produce awkward fingerings.
      </p>
      <p>
       Instead, an easy way to extend arpeggios is to take any
       <strong>
        four-note shape
       </strong>
       you already know, and then pair it with a simple, three-note triad that you already know, and voila, instant extended arpeggio.
      </p>
      <p>
       You can see how this works in the following example:
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cmaj7 up to the 13th is
       <strong>
        Cmaj7+D triad
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       or
      </p>
      <p>
       C-E-G-B + D-F#-A
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       So you havent learned anything new, just took two things you already knew and
       <strong>
        combined
       </strong>
       them to form a new concept.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here is a basic chart for figuring out the extended jazz guitar arpeggios for
       <strong>
        common chords
       </strong>
       :
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Maj7 = maj7+M triad a tone higher (produces a Lydian sound)
       </li>
       <li>
        7 = 7th+M triad a tone higher (produces a 7#11 sound)
       </li>
       <li>
        m7 = m7+m triad a tone higher
       </li>
       <li>
        m7b5 = m7b5+M triad a semi-tone higher
       </li>
       <li>
        mMaj7 = mMaj7+m triad a tone higher
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Once youve checked out extending jazz guitar arpeggios for these
       <strong>
        common voicings
       </strong>
       you can come up with your own for any chord or arpeggio that you want to explore such as Maj7(#5), 7(b13), 7(b9) and many more.
      </p>
      <h3>
      </h3>
      <h3 style="text-align: center;">
       How to Practice Extended Jazz Guitar Arpeggios
      </h3>
      <p>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Pick a tune
       </li>
       <li>
        Play the ascending extended arpeggios for each chord
       </li>
       <li>
        Play the descending extended jazz guitar arpeggios for each chord
       </li>
       <li>
        Play the ascending extended arpeggio for the first chord, then descending for the second and continue
       </li>
       <li>
        Reverse the previous idea so you start on a descending extended arpeggio
       </li>
       <li>
        Improvise on the tune using only extended jazz guitar arpeggios
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Being able to solo with chord tones, either arpeggios or
       <strong>
        extended arpeggios
       </strong>
       , is an important tool in any jazz guitarists bag of tricks.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you are already familiar with the normal 1-3-5-7 shapes and how to use them, try adding a
       <strong>
        simple triad
       </strong>
       on top of these fingerings.
      </p>
      <p>
       It will allow you to keep the sound of the chord in your melodic ideas while
       <strong>
        expanding your tonal options
       </strong>
       at the same time.
      </p>
