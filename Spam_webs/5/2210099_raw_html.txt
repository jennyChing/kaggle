If such a connection were possible in humans, we might be able to solve problems far better than any individual alone.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EResearchers have known for a long time that brains have a tendency to sync up. When two people are tasked with the same activity, neurons in their brains start to fire at the same speed and in the same place, even if the people weren\u2019t instructed to cooperate. No one really knows why this happens, but it\u2019s a well-observed phenomenon\u2014our brains are naturally inclined to cooperate with one another.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe researchers behind this study knew that the same held true for monkeys, and they wanted to see how this convergence could be applied to the brain-machine interface. In the study, they sat groups of two or three monkeys in separate rooms in front of a computer screen. Each of these monkeys had electrodes implanted in the parts of their brains associated with motor skills and somatosensation, the sense of where the body is positioned in space. The monkeys all shared control of the digital arm, able to move it along various axes so that, together, the monkeys could complete a common task of moving the arm towards a circular target on the screen. Once they achieved that, the researchers gave them juice as a reward. They first learned to move the arm with a joystick, then the researchers hooked up their brain implants to control the arm with just their minds.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EOver the course of several weeks, the monkeys got much better\u2014and faster\u2014at working together.              <h1>
              Watch A Timelapse Of A Slow-Moving Landslide [Video]
             </h1>
             <div class="field-subtitle">
              <p>
               Not all landslides happen quickly
              </p>
             </div>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">
              <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
              <span class="author">
               <span class="label">
                By
               </span>
               <a datatype="" href="/popsci-authors/mary-beth-griggs" property="prefLabel" typeof="Concept">
                Mary Beth Griggs

         <article class="content-basic basic-sidebar">
          <div class="content-main basic-sidebar">
           <div class="panel-pane pane-entity-field pane-node-body">
            <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <div class="media-youtube-video full-width media-youtube-1">
               <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="media-youtube-player" frameborder="0" height="390" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/5-nyAz484WA?v=5-nyAz484WA&amp;wmode=opaque&amp;modestbranding=1&amp;rel=0" title="Val Parghera Zeitraffer Mai 2013 - Januar 2015" width="640">
                Video of Val Parghera Zeitraffer Mai 2013 - Januar 2015
               </iframe>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Usually, when people think of landslides, they think of avalanches of dirt, mud, and rock pouring down a hillside like an avalanche. While many of the
              <a href="http://landslides.usgs.gov/learn/majorls.php">
               most disastrous
              </a>
              landslides happen quickly--triggered by storms, earthquakes, or volcanoes--landslides can also move much more slowly, encouraged downslope by the pull of gravity.
             </p>
             <p>
              That's what's going on in this timelapse video, noticed by AGU's Landslide Blog
              <a href="http://blogs.agu.org/landslideblog/2015/02/11/val-parghera-1/">
               earlier today
              </a>
              . The video was compiled from pictures of a landslide in Switzerland taken over the course of about 20 months. The Val Parghera landslide is located in the Canton of Grisons, the largest canton (a division similar to a state) in Swtizerland. Grisons hired a consulting firm,
              <a href="http://www.geopraevent.ch/?lang=en">
               GEOPRAEVENT
              </a>
              to keep an eye on the landslide,
              <a href="http://www.geopraevent.ch/reference-projects/val-parghera-landslide-monitoring/?lang=en">
               located near railways and highways
              </a>
              . In addition to webcams, the team is monitoring the status of the landslide with various other instruments, including radar and laser scans, to keep track of the landslide's speed. They
              <a href="http://www.geopraevent.ch/timelapse-movie-val-parghera/?lang=en">
               note
              </a>
              that the speed of the landslide varies between as much as 3.9 inches/hour (recorded in June 2013) to less than 0.4 inches per month.
             </p>
            </div>
