     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Australoid race
      </b>
      is a broad
      <a href="/wiki/Race_(human_classification)" title="Race (human classification)">
       racial classification
      </a>
      . The concept originated with a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typology_(anthropology)" title="Typology (anthropology)">
       typological method of racial classification
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were described as having dark skin with wavy hair, in the case of the
      <b>
       Veddoid race
      </b>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
       South Asia
      </a>
      (including the eponymous
      <a href="/wiki/Vedda_people" title="Vedda people">
       Vedda people
      </a>
      indigenous to
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">
       Sri Lanka
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">
       Aboriginal Australians
      </a>
      , or hair ranging from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight_hair" title="Straight hair">
       straight
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Afro-textured_hair" title="Afro-textured hair">
       kinky
      </a>
      in the case of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melanesian" title="Melanesian">
       Melanesian
      </a>
      group.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to this model of classification, Australoid peoples ranged throughout
      <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
       Indonesia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
       Philippines
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
       Malaysia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">
       New Guinea
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">
       Melanesia
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">
       Andaman Islands
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
       Indian subcontinent
      </a>
      , as well as parts of the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
       Middle East
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Out_of_Africa_theory" title="Out of Africa theory">
       Out of Africa theory
      </a>
      , the ancestors of the Australoids, the
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Australoid" title="Proto-Australoid">
       Proto-Australoids
      </a>
      , are thought to have been the first branch off from the Proto-
      <a href="/wiki/Capoid_race" title="Capoid race">
       Capoids
      </a>
      to migrate from
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      about 60,000 BCE. This migration is hypothesized to have taken place along the now submerged
      <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">
       continental shelf
      </a>
      of the northern shore of the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
       Indian Ocean
      </a>
      , reaching
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      about 50,000 BCE. However, the suggested Proto-AustraloidProto-Capoid link has been contested.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Australoid_race&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1187" data-file-width="1500" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg/250px-Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg/375px-Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg/500px-Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meyers_b11_s0476a.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Australoid distribution according to the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Meyers_Konversations-Lexikon" title="Meyers Konversations-Lexikon">
          Meyers Konversations-Lexikon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1885-90).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the late nineteenth century,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropometric" title="Anthropometric">
       anthropometric
      </a>
      studies led to a proposition of racial groups, one of which was termed "
      <i>
       Australioid
      </i>
      " by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Huxley" title="Thomas Huxley">
       Thomas Huxley
      </a>
      in an essay 'On the Geographical Distribution of the Chief Modifications of Mankind' (1870), in which he divided humanity into four principal groups (Xanthochroic, Mongoloid, Negroid, and Australioid).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Huxley also concluded that the
      <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_race" title="Mediterranean race">
       Melanochroi
      </a>
      (Peoples of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_race" title="Mediterranean race">
       Mediterranean race
      </a>
      ) are of a mixture of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nordic_race" title="Nordic race">
       Xanthochroi
      </a>
      (northern Europeans) and Australioids.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later writers dropped the first "i" in Australioid, establishing Australoid as the standard spelling.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Peter Bellwood, "many of the present
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Mongoloid" title="Southern Mongoloid">
       Southern Mongoloid
      </a>
      populations of Indonesia and Malaysia also have a high degree of Australo-Melanesian genetic heritage."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One proponent,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R._Ruggles_Gates" title="R. Ruggles Gates">
       R. Ruggles Gates
      </a>
      , argued in 1960 that "
      <i>
       If the
       <a href="/wiki/Ainu_people" title="Ainu people">
        Ainu
       </a>
       are partly of Australoid origin it is also clear that they are even more nearly derived from archaic Caucasian ancestry
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Huxley's original model included the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">
       population of India
      </a>
      under the Australoid category. The
      <i>
       American Journal of Physical Anthropology
      </i>
      (1996, p.382) by American Association of Physical Anthropologists. L. L. (Luigi Luca) Cavalli-Sforza, Paolo Menozzi and Alberto Piazza in their text,
      <i>
       The History and Geography of Human Genes
      </i>
      (1994, P. 241) both use the term.
     </p>
     <p>
      Balgir (2004)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-balgir2004bgh_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-balgir2004bgh-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      designates tribes as Australoid or
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Australoid" title="Proto-Australoid">
       Proto-Australoid
      </a>
      according to
      <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
       language family
      </a>
      . A 2006
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Forensic_Science_Laboratory" title="Central Forensic Science Laboratory">
       CFSL
      </a>
      research article which assessed "3522 individuals belonging to 54 (23 belonging to the
      <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">
       Austroasiatic
      </a>
      , 18 to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dravidian_Languages" title="Dravidian Languages">
       Dravidian
      </a>
      , 7 to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibeto-Burman_Languages" title="Tibeto-Burman Languages">
       Tibeto-Burman
      </a>
      and 24 to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_Languages" title="Indo-European Languages">
       Indo-European
      </a>
      linguistic groups) endogamous Indian populations, representing all major ethnic, linguistic and geographic groups" for genetic variations to support such classifications found no conclusive evidence. It further summed that "the absence of genetic markers to support the general clustering of population groups based on ethnic, linguistic, geographic or socio-cultural affiliations" undermines the broad groupings based on such affiliations that exist in population genetic studies and forensic databases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kashyap2006bg_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kashyap2006bg-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_features">
       Physical features
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Australoid_race&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical features">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Forensic anthropologist Caroline Wilkenson wrote in 2004 that Australoids have the largest brow ridges "
      <i>
       with moderate to large supraorbital arches
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkenson_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkenson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Caucasoids have the second largest brow ridges with "
      <i>
       moderate supraorbital ridges
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkenson_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkenson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Negroids have the third largest brow ridges with an "
      <i>
       undulating
       <a href="/wiki/Supraorbital_ridge" title="Supraorbital ridge">
        supraorbital ridge
       </a>
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkenson_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkenson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mongoloids are "
      <i>
       absent browridges
      </i>
      ", so they have the smallest brow ridges.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkenson_10-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkenson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Huxley wrote in 1870 that Australoids are usually
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolichocephalic" title="Dolichocephalic">
       dolichocephalic
      </a>
      ;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aleph0.clarku.edu_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-aleph0.clarku.edu-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      their hair is usually silky, black and wavy;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aleph0.clarku.edu_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-aleph0.clarku.edu-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      they usually have large, heavy jaws and
      <a href="/wiki/Prognathism" title="Prognathism">
       prognathism
      </a>
      ;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aleph0.clarku.edu_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-aleph0.clarku.edu-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      their skin is the color of chocolate and the irises are dark brown or black.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aleph0.clarku.edu_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-aleph0.clarku.edu-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_Americans.3F">
       The first Americans?
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Fuegians" title="Fuegians">
       Fuegians
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Peric%C3%BAes" title="Perices">
       Perices

     </div>

     <p>
      Skulls of individuals with Australoid morphologies have been found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
       Americas
      </a>
      , leading to speculation that peoples with phenotypical similarities to modern Australoids may have been the earliest occupants of the continent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If this hypothesis is correct, it would mean that some Proto-Australoids continued the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Coastal_Migration" title="Great Coastal Migration">
       Great Coastal Migration
      </a>
      beyond
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      along the continental shelf north in
      <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">
       East Asia
      </a>
      and across the
      <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">
       Bering land bridge
      </a>
      , reaching the
      <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
       Americas
      </a>
      about 52,000 BCE.
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>
      Christy Turner notes that "cranial analyses of some South American crania have suggested that there might have been some early migration of "Australoids."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Turner argues that cranial morphology suggests
      <a href="/wiki/Sinodonty_and_Sundadonty" title="Sinodonty and Sundadonty">
       sinodonty
      </a>
      in all the populations he has studied.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the earliest skulls recovered by archaeologists is a specimen named the
      <a href="/wiki/Luzia_Woman" title="Luzia Woman">
       Luzia Woman
      </a>
      . According to
      <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">
       archaeologist
      </a>
      Walter Neves of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="University of So Paulo">
       University of So Paulo
      </a>
      , Luzia's
      <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">
       Paleo-Indian
      </a>
      predecessors lived in South East Asia for tens of thousands of years, after
      <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">
       migrating
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      , and began arriving in the
      <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
       New World
      </a>
      , as early as 15,000 years ago. Some anthropologists have hypothesized that Paleo-Indians migrated along the coast of
      <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">
       East Asia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">
       Beringia
      </a>
      in small
      <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">
       watercraft
      </a>
      , before or during the last
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
       Ice Age
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Neves' conclusions have been challenged by research done by anthropologists Rolando Gonzalez-Jose, Frank Williams and William Armelagos who have shown in their studies that the cranio-facial variability could just be due to genetic drift and other factors affecting cranio-facial plasticity in Native Americans.
