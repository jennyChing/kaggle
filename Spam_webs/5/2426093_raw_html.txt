          <img alt="How to Get Read" class="aligncenter full-media nomargin" src="https://angiesdiary.com/wp-content/themes/stylebook/timthumb.php?src=https%3A%2F%2Fangiesdiary.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2011%2F08%2FHow-to-Get-Read-01.jpg&amp;q=90&amp;w=795&amp;zc=1" title="How to Get Read"/>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <strong>
            <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/faq/how-to-post-an-article/">

          <p>
           <img alt="How to Get Read 01 How to Get Read" class="alignnone wp-image-48462 size-full" height="200" src="https://angiesdiary.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/How-to-Get-Read-01.jpg" title="How to Get Read" width="795"/>
          </p>
          <table border="0" style="width: 100%;">
           <tbody>
            <tr>
             <td>
             </td>
             <td align="center" width="50%">
              <h3>
               <strong>
                <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/profile/register/">
                 Free Sign-Up
                </a>
               </strong>
              </h3>
             </td>
             <td align="center" width="50%">
              <h3>
               <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/premium/">

          <p>
           <span style="color: #800000;">
            <strong>
             <em>
              Authors and aspiring writers oftentimes muse over how to get ones work read, reviewed, and conceivably published by a major publishing house. As many decide to follow the path of self-publishing, a great deal of debate is going on aboutwhat are acceptable ways to promote ones work.
              <br/>
             </em>
            </strong>
           </span>
          </p>
          <hr/>
          <table border="0" width="795">
           <tbody>
            <tr>
             <td align="left" scope="col" style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;" valign="top">
              <p>
               <strong>
                Traditional Publishing Pros
               </strong>
              </p>
              <ul style="list-style-type: square;">
               <li>
                Widespread distribution and exposure
               </li>
               <li>
                Most offer an advance, sometimes a substantial one
               </li>
               <li>
                They do the editing, formatting, cover art
               </li>
               <li>
                Marketing and promotion power
               </li>
              </ul>
             </td>
             <td align="left" scope="col" style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;" valign="top">
              <p>
               <strong>
                Traditional Publishing Cons
               </strong>
              </p>
              <ul style="list-style-type: square;">
               <li>
                Very hard to get published
               </li>
               <li>
                Slow processing until publication (6-18 months).
               </li>
               <li>
                High book and eBook prices
               </li>
               <li>
                They decide the title, cover art, and the final version
               </li>
               <li>
                Royalties are paid only twice a year
               </li>
               <li>
                Low royalty rates (6  25%)
               </li>
              </ul>
             </td>
            </tr>
            <tr>
             <td align="left" scope="col" style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;" valign="top">
              <p>
               <strong>
                Self-Publishing Pros
               </strong>
              </p>
              <ul style="list-style-type: square;">
               <li>
                Royalties paid once a month
               </li>
               <li>
                Complete control over price, cover, and final version
               </li>
               <li>
                Publication is virtually instant
               </li>
               <li>
                Easy to implement changes
               </li>
               <li>
                Every decision is yours
               </li>
               <li>
                High royalty rates
               </li>
               <li>
                Anyone can do it
               </li>
              </ul>
             </td>
             <td align="left" scope="col" style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;" valign="top">
              <p>
               <strong>
                Self-Publishing Cons
               </strong>
              </p>
              <ul style="list-style-type: square;">
               <li>
                No free professional editing, formatting, or cover art
               </li>
               <li>
                Hard work to market and promote
               </li>
               <li>
                Fewer sales
               </li>
               <li>
                Less than 10% of total current book sales
               </li>
               <li>
                Greater likelihood to publish substandard books
               </li>
              </ul>
             </td>
            </tr>
           </tbody>
          </table>
          <hr/>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Getting the Most out of Critique &amp; Comments
           </strong>
           <br/>
           At some point in your writing career, you should seek feedback on your work. Sure, your mom can look it over. Your best friend can read
           <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/publishing/top-promotion-tips-novelists-get-read/" title="Top Promotion Tips for Novelists to Get Read">
            your new novel
           </a>
           , but if you want honest criticism, one of the best sources for critiques is from your peers.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            A Critique Can Be Wrong
           </strong>
           <br/>
           An important aspect you must consider is that critiques, or opinions, can be wrong. Even professional editors are not always right, just be prepared to take each critique seriously and see if you can apply it to your work.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            I Like your Story.
            <br/>
           </strong>
           Someone said they like your work. Great!
           <br/>
           Then they start to get into
           <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/writing/chewing-on-a-critique/" title="Chewing on a Critique">
            a more solid critique
           </a>
           . Do not automatically tune out everything after the
           <em>
            BUT
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           If you think it is totally off the mark, move on. Ask other writers in our community about the persons comments. Do not be afraid. You are here to further your writing vocation.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            I Hate your Story.
            <br/>
           </strong>
           That is hardly feedback, and it is definitely not helpful. We frown upon this type of comment, and will not publish it. In case a subscriber persists, this can result in a ban. We want to help you build confidence in your writing. Being constructive is the key.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Positives and Negatives
           </strong>
           <br/>
           On that note, when giving feedback on anothers work, why not point out both the positives and the negatives? If the plot line is strong, say so. If the characters need work, let the writer know.
          </p>
          <p>
           This approach can work miracles for a struggling writer  no matter what their level of experience. Whether you are just starting out or have already published several novels. If you are ready to gain a new appreciation for the writing process and network with your fellow writers on Angies Diary, it can help you get a fresh perspective on your writing.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            GetBut DO Give
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Dont expect your fellow authors to overwhelm you with comments on your first post  of course it does happen, even regularly  but instead, start giving feedback on the work other writers have posted, as they will almost certainly reciprocate. That is an important aspect of this magazine.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            I Dont Know How to Give Feedback!
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Of course you do. Just as you can spot strong and weak points in your own work, you can point out the same in other peoples work.
          </p>
          <p>
           In fact, by
           <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/writing/quotes-from-writers-to-writers/" title="Quotes from Writers to Writers">
            reading work from other writers
           </a>
           , you can help develop a sense for what works and what doesnt. Developing that sense will help you to improve your writing skills along the way.
          </p>
          <hr/>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Helpful hints
            <br/>
           </strong>
           If your real goal is to get read, getting to the Top Ten and get published by Harper-Collins or similar publishing houses, here are some ideas that are NOT in violation of Angies policy:
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <em>
             Get the most out of your free Basic Account:
            </em>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Submit articles to
            <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/psychology/self-improvement/listening-the-quiet-power/" title="Listening  The Quiet Power">
             hone your writing skills
            </a>
           </li>
           <li>
            Engage other writers on a regular basis
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <em>
             Additionally for
             <a href="https://angiesdiary.com/premium/">
              Premium
             </a>
             members:
            </em>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Post book reviews
           </li>
           <li>
            Post book excerpts
           </li>
           <li>
            Keep us in the loop of your publications, so we can regularly make suggestions for promotion
           </li>
           <li>
            Mail us with the details of the book to be promoted in one of our
            <a href="http://amazon.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">

            carousels

            publications and all advertising rates
