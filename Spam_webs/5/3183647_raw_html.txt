        <div class="trb_article_page" data-content-page="1" data-role="pagination_page">
         <p>
          From what I hear and read, "50 Shades of Grey" presents a philosophy that can be summed up by this brief exchange in the movie:
         </p>
         <p>
          
          <strong>
           Anastasia Steele
          </strong>
          : "What would I get out of this?"
         </p>
         <p>
          
          <strong>
           Christian Grey
          </strong>
          : "Me."
         </p>
         <p>
          Apparently, that's good enough for Anastasia.
         </p>
         <p>
          If that image of young women bothers you, have I got a sport for you:
         </p>
         <p>
          Women's roller derby, represented locally by the Orlando Psycho City Derby Girls, who begin their sixth season Sunday at 5 p.m. with a "mashup" performance at Semoran Skateway off Highway 436 in Casselberry. The title of the event? "My Bloody Valentine." How great is that?
         </p>

         <p>
          Full disclosure: I have loved roller derby for a long, long time. When I was a kid in Miami, the team had male and female squads, but it was always the women who were the most exciting. The men were more athletically skilled, but the women were far more fierce. I met four Psycho City competitors this week and, fortunately, no violence was involved. What was impressive was the composure and confidence of the group. All have "day jobs" but when your hobby includes hitting and being hit, you are hardly the right kind of woman for a "50 Shades" lifestyle.
         </p>
         <p>
          And you have a great nickname. Can't skate without a nickname. Here are the nicknames of the competitors I met during an interview on 740theGame's The Beat of Sports:
         </p>
         <p>
          
          <strong>
           Luna Shovegood
          </strong>
          , aka
          <strong>
           Andrea Urrea
          </strong>
          , who works as an international business development project specialist at Full Sail.
         </p>
         <p>
          
          <strong>
           Shod N. Froyda
          </strong>
          , aka
          <strong>
           Shana Moshen
          </strong>
          , who works at Tijuana Flats. (By the way, she says her nickname translates as, "Joy in other people's pain and suffering.")
         </p>

          </strong>
          , who works as technical director for Pirates Dinner Theater.
         </p>
         <p>
          
          <strong>
           Missy Misdemeanor
          </strong>
          , aka
          <strong>
           Missy Corson
          </strong>
          , who works as digital producer at Purple, Rock, Scissors Creative Agency and also serves as publicist for the Psycho Girls.
         </p>
         <p>

          <strong>
           Amina Acid
          </strong>
          (aka
          <strong>
           Kiersten Kevin
          </strong>
          , a forensic scientist in her other life),
          <strong>

          (aka

          </strong>

           Monoxide,
          </strong>

          </strong>
          ,
          <strong>

          <strong>
           Tiger Beatdown
          </strong>
          .
         </p>

         <p>
          Team names are cool, too. Psycho City contains three teams: Arkham Assailants, Serial Thrillers and Sunnyland Slammers. They also combine their best to travel to other cities as the Straight Jackettes.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you don't know the sport, each team has five players on the track: Four blockers and one jammer. The jammers must break away from the pack, skate around to reach the pack again. The leading jammer earns one point for every opponent she can pass (and no problem if the opponent has been knocked down or even over the railing). The lead jammer then may make the "offsides" gesture to end the jam before the other jammer can score. One great thing is that an entire match only takes about an hour.
         </p>
         <p>
          According to Shod/Shana, a petite but clearly fearless skater, the jammer wears a "panty with a star on it" over her helmet as a means of identifying her role. I don't think that's what it really is, but the image is interesting.
         </p>
         <p>
          But why do it?
         </p>
         <p>
          "It's about empowering yourself," said Luna/Andrea. "People shouldn't be able to tell you what you can or can't do."
         </p>
         <p>
          Their website is Orlandoderbygirls.com and Bloody Valentine is a modest $10 at the door. If you are going for the first time, you might want to bring folding chairs because seating at the rink is limited.
         </p>
         <p>
          Everybody should check out the Psycho Girls once to find out if roller derby works for you. And if Christian Grey shows up, somebody may slap him silly.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Your 'signature hold'
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Like the Psycho Girls, everyone should have a nickname. Right now on The Beat of Sports, I'm being called "Mountain of Greene," which was not my choice. But it does kind of sound like a pro wrestler, which reminds me of
          <strong>
           Rikishi
          </strong>
          , who goes into the WWE Hall of Fame this year at Wrestlemania. What Rikishi had was a "signature move" with the lovely name of "Stink Face." Yes, it means exactly what you think it means. Rikishi goes into the Hall with
          <strong>
           Randy Savage
          </strong>
          , who will be remembered as "The Macho Man," and, uh,
          <strong>
           Arnold Schwarzenegger
          </strong>
          , whose "signature move" may not be listed by the WWE.
         </p>

          <strong>
           These are the notes, folks
          </strong>
