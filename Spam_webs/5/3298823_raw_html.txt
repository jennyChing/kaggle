   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

     <div class="hatnote">
      This article is about colors.  For other uses, see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_Colors_(disambiguation)" title="Primary Colors (disambiguation)">
       Primary Colors
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:377px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CRT_phosphors.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1591" data-file-width="2444" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/CRT_phosphors.png/375px-CRT_phosphors.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/CRT_phosphors.png/563px-CRT_phosphors.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/CRT_phosphors.png/750px-CRT_phosphors.png 2x" width="375"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CRT_phosphors.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_spectra" title="Emission spectra">
         emission spectra
        </a>
        of the three
        <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">
         phosphors
        </a>
        that define the
        <b>
         additive primary colors
        </b>
        of a
        <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">
         CRT
        </a>
        color video display. Unlike subtractive systems that use magenta, yellow, and cyan inks, additive systems such as computer displays mix red, green, and blue light to make all colors.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Primary colors
      </b>
      are sets of colors that can be combined to make a useful range of colors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Primary Colors: Red, yellow and blue In traditional color theory (used in paint and pigments), primary colors are the 3 pigment colors that can not be mixed or formed by any combination of other colors. All other colors are derived from these 3 hues.
     </p>
     <p>
      Secondary Colors: Green, orange and purple These are the colors formed by mixing the primary colors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Tertiary Colors: Yellow-orange, red-orange, red-purple, blue-purple, blue-green &amp; yellow-green These are the colors formed by mixing a primary and a secondary color. That's why the hue is a two word name, such as blue-green, red-violet, and yellow-orange.
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.colormatters.com/color-and-design/basic-color-theory" rel="nofollow">
       Original source
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      For human applications, three primary colors are typically used, since human
      <a href="/wiki/Color_vision" title="Color vision">
       color vision
      </a>
      is usually
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trichromatic" title="Trichromatic">
       trichromatic
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      For
      <i>
       additive
      </i>
      combination of colors, as in overlapping projected lights or in
      <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">
       CRT
      </a>
      displays, the primary colors normally used are
      <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">
       red
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">
       green
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">
       blue
      </a>
      . For a
      <i>
       subtractive
      </i>
      combination of colors, as in mixing of
      <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">
       pigments
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">
       dyes
      </a>
      , such as in printing, the primaries normally used are
      <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">
       magenta
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">
       yellow
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">
       cyan
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though the set of red, yellow, and blue is popular among artists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      See
      <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">
       RGB color model
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/CMYK_color_model" title="CMYK color model">
       CMYK color model
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/RYB_color_model" title="RYB color model">
       RYB color model
      </a>
      for more on these popular sets of primary colors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Any particular choice for a given set of primary colors is derived from the spectral sensitivity of each of the human cone photoreceptors; three colors that fall within each of the sensitivity ranges of each of the human cone cells are red, green, and blue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other sets of colors can be used, though not all will well-approximate the full range of color perception. For example, an early color photographic process,
      <a href="/wiki/Autochrome_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Autochrome Lumire">
       autochrome
      </a>
      , typically used
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_(color)" title="Orange (color)">
       orange
      </a>
      , green, and
      <a href="/wiki/Violet_(color)" title="Violet (color)">
       violet
      </a>
      primaries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, unless negative amounts of a color are allowed the
      <a href="/wiki/Gamut" title="Gamut">
       gamut
      </a>
      will be restricted by the choice of primaries.
     </p>
     <p>
      The combination of any two primary colors creates a secondary color.
     </p>
     <p>
      The most commonly used
      <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">
       additive color
      </a>
      primaries are the
      <a href="/wiki/Secondary_color" title="Secondary color">
       secondary colors
      </a>
      of the most commonly used
      <a href="/wiki/Subtractive_color" title="Subtractive color">
       subtractive color
      </a>
      primaries, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceversa" title="Viceversa">
       vice versa
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_basis">
       Biological basis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biological basis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lasers.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Lasers.jpg/220px-Lasers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Lasers.jpg/330px-Lasers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Lasers.jpg/440px-Lasers.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lasers.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RGB" title="RGB">
         RGB
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lasers" title="Lasers">
         lasers
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Primary colors are not a fundamental property of light but are related to the physiological response of the eye to light. Fundamentally, light is a continuous
      <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
       spectrum
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
       wavelengths
      </a>
      that can be detected by the human eye, an infinite-dimensional
      <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">
       stimulus
      </a>
      space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sobel_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sobel-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the human eye normally contains only three types of color receptors, called
      <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">
       cone cells
      </a>
      . Each color receptor responds to different ranges of the color spectrum. Humans and other species with three such types of color receptors are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Trichromacy" title="Trichromacy">
       trichromats
      </a>
      . These species respond to the light stimulus via a three-dimensional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensation_and_perception" title="Sensation and perception">
       sensation
      </a>
      , which generally can be modeled as a mixture of three primary colors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sobel_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sobel-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Before the nature of colorimetry and visual physiology were well understood, scientists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist)" title="Thomas Young (scientist)">
       Thomas Young
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">
       James Clerk Maxwell
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz" title="Hermann von Helmholtz">
       Hermann von Helmholtz
      </a>
      expressed various opinions about what should be the three primary colors to describe the three primary color sensations of the eye.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Young originally proposed red, green, and violet, and Maxwell changed violet to blue; Helmholtz proposed "a slightly purplish red, a vegetation-green, slightly yellowish (wavelength about 5600 tenth-metres), and an ultramarine-blue (about 4820)".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In modern understanding, human
      <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">
       cone cells
      </a>
      do not correspond precisely to a specific set of primary colors, as each cone type responds to a range of color wavelengths.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2013)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Species with different numbers of receptor cell types would have color vision requiring a different number of primaries. For example, for species known as
      <a href="/wiki/Tetrachromacy" title="Tetrachromacy">
       tetrachromats
      </a>
      , with four different color receptors, one would use four primary colors. Since humans can only see to 380
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">
       nanometers
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Violet_(color)" title="Violet (color)">
       violet
      </a>
      ), but tetrachromats can see into the
      <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
       ultraviolet
      </a>
      to about 300 nanometers, this fourth primary color for tetrachromats is located in the shorter-wavelength range.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many
      <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">
       birds
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">
       marsupials
      </a>
      are tetrachromats, and it has been suggested that some human females are tetrachromats as well,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      having an extra variant version of the long-wave (L) cone type.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The peak response of human color receptors varies, even among individuals with "normal" color vision;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in non-human species this polymorphic variation is even greater, and it may well be adaptive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placental_mammal" title="Placental mammal">
       placental mammals
      </a>
      other than primates have only two types of color receptors and are therefore
      <a href="/wiki/Dichromacy" title="Dichromacy">
       dichromats
      </a>
      ; to them, there are only two primary colors.
     </p>
     <p>
      It would be incorrect to assume that the world "looks tinted" to an animal (or human) with anything other than the human standard of three color receptors. To an animal (or human) born that way, the world would look normal to it, but the animal's ability to detect and discriminate colors would be different from that of a human with normal color vision. If a human and an animal both look at a natural color, they see it as natural; however, if both look at a color reproduced via primary colors, such as on a
      <a href="/wiki/Color_television" title="Color television">
       color television
      </a>
      screen, the human may see it as matching the natural color, while the animal does not, since the primary colors have been chosen to suit human capabilities.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Additive_primaries">
       Additive primaries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Additive primaries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main articles:
      <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">
       Additive color
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">
       RGB color model
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AdditiveColor.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/AdditiveColor.svg/220px-AdditiveColor.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/AdditiveColor.svg/330px-AdditiveColor.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/AdditiveColor.svg/440px-AdditiveColor.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AdditiveColor.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Additive color mixing
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="495" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg/220px-CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg/330px-CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg/440px-CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CIE1931xy_sRGB.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/SRGB" title="SRGB">
         sRGB
        </a>
        color triangle
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Media that combine emitted lights to create the sensation of a range of colors are using the
      <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">
       additive color
      </a>
      system. Typically, the primary colors used are red, green, and blue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
       Television
      </a>
      and other computer and video displays are a common example of the use of additive primaries and the
      <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">
       RGB color model
      </a>
      . The exact colors chosen for the primaries are a technological compromise between the available
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">
       phosphors
      </a>
      (including considerations such as cost and power usage) and the need for large
      <a href="/wiki/Color_triangle" title="Color triangle">
       color triangle
      </a>
      to allow a large gamut of colors. The
      <a href="/wiki/Rec._709" title="Rec. 709">
       ITU-R BT.709-5
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/SRGB" title="SRGB">
       sRGB
      </a>
      primaries are typical.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="495" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg/220px-CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg/330px-CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg/440px-CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CIE1931xy_CIERGB.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        CIE 1931 RGB color triangle with monochromatic primaries
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Additive mixing of red and green light produces shades of
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">
       yellow
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_(color)" title="Orange (color)">
       orange
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">
       brown
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mixing green and blue produces shades of cyan, and mixing red and blue produces shades of
      <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">
       purple
      </a>
      , including
      <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">
       magenta
      </a>
      . Mixing nominally equal proportions of the additive primaries results in shades of
      <a href="/wiki/Grey" title="Grey">
       grey
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">
       white
      </a>
      ; the
      <a href="/wiki/Color_space" title="Color space">
       color space
      </a>
      that is generated is called an
      <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_space" title="RGB color space">
       RGB color space
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/CIE_1931_color_space" title="CIE 1931 color space">
       CIE 1931 color space
      </a>
      defines
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">
        monochromatic
       </a>
      </i>
      primary colors with wavelengths of 435.8nm (violet), 546.1nm (green) and 700nm (red). The corners of the color triangle are therefore on the spectral locus, and the triangle is about as big as it can be. No real display device uses such primaries, as the extreme wavelengths used for violet and red result in a very low
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminous_efficiency" title="Luminous efficiency">
       luminous efficiency
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments_in_primary_colors">
       Recent developments in primary colors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recent developments in primary colors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Some recent TV and computer displays are starting to add a fourth "primary" of yellow, often in a four-point square pixel area, to get brighter pure yellows and larger color gamut.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even the four-primary technology does not yet reach the range of colors the human eye is theoretically capable of perceiving (as defined by the sample-based estimate called the Pointer Gamut
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), with 4-primary LED prototypes providing typically about 87% and 5-primary prototypes about 95%. Several firms, including Samsung and Mitsubishi, have demonstrated LED displays with five or six "primaries", or color LED point light sources per pixel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A recent academic literature review claims a gamut of 99% can be achieved with 5-primary LED technology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While technology for achieving a wider gamut appears to be within reach, other issues remain; for example, affordability, dynamic range, and brilliance. In addition, there exists hardly any source material recorded in this wider gamut, nor is it currently possible to recover this information from existing visual media. Regardless, industry is still exploring a wide variety of "primary" active light sources (per pixel) with the goal of matching the capability of human color perception within a broadly affordable price. One example of a potentially affordable but yet unproven active light hybrid places a LED screen over a plasma light screen, each with different "primaries". Because both LED and plasma technologies are many decades old (plasma pixels going back to the 1960s), both have become so affordable that they could be combined.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Subtractive_primaries">
       Subtractive primaries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subtractive primaries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Subtractive_color" title="Subtractive color">
       Subtractive color
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Media that use reflected light and colorants to produce colors are using the
      <a href="/wiki/Subtractive_color" title="Subtractive color">
       subtractive color
      </a>
      method of color mixing.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="CMYK_color_model.2C_or_four-color_printing">
       CMYK color model, or four-color printing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: CMYK color model, or four-color printing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/CMYK_color_model" title="CMYK color model">
       CMYK color model
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the printing industry, to produce the varying colors the
      <b>
       subtractive primaries
      </b>
      <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">
       cyan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">
       magenta
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">
       yellow
      </a>
      are applied together in varying amounts. Before the color names
      <i>
       cyan
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       magenta
      </i>
      were in common use, these primaries were often known as blue-green and purple, or in some circles as blue and red, respectively, and their exact color has changed over time with access to new pigments and technologies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SubtractiveColor.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/SubtractiveColor.svg/220px-SubtractiveColor.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/SubtractiveColor.svg/330px-SubtractiveColor.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/SubtractiveColor.svg/440px-SubtractiveColor.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SubtractiveColor.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Subtractive color mixing  the magenta and cyan primaries are sometimes called purple and blue-green, or red and blue.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mixing yellow and cyan produces green colors; mixing yellow with magenta produces reds, and mixing magenta with cyan produces blues. In theory, mixing equal amounts of all three pigments should produce grey, resulting in black when all three are applied in sufficient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_density" title="Optical density">
       density
      </a>
      , but in practice they tend to produce muddy
      <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">
       brown
      </a>
      colors. For this reason, and to save ink and decrease drying times, a fourth pigment,
      <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">
       black
      </a>
      , is often used in addition to cyan, magenta, and yellow.
     </p>
     <p>
      The resulting model is the so-called
      <a href="/wiki/CMYK_color_model" title="CMYK color model">
       CMYK color model
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">
       abbreviation
      </a>
      stands for
      <b>
       c
      </b>
      yan,
      <b>
       m
      </b>
      agenta,
      <b>
       y
      </b>
      ellow, and
      <b>
       k
      </b>
      eyblack is referred to as the
      <i>
       key
      </i>
      color, a shorthand for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Key_plate" title="Key plate">
        key printing plate
       </a>
      </i>
      that impressed the artistic detail of an image, usually in black ink.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In practice, colorant mixtures in actual materials such as
      <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">
       paint
      </a>
      tend to be more complex. Brighter or more
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_(color_theory)" title="Saturation (color theory)">
       saturated
      </a>
      colors can be created using natural pigments instead of mixing, and natural properties of pigments can interfere with the mixing. For example, mixing magenta and green in
      <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_paint" title="Acrylic paint">
       acrylic
      </a>
      creates a dark cyansomething which would not happen if the mixing process were perfectly subtractive.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the subtractive model, adding white to a color, whether by using less colorant or by mixing in a reflective white pigment such as
      <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">
       zinc oxide
      </a>
      , does not change the color's
      <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">
       hue
      </a>
      but does reduce its saturation. Subtractive color printing works best when the surface or paper is white, or close to it.
     </p>
     <p>
      A system of subtractive color does not have a simple chromaticity gamut analogous to the RGB color triangle, but a gamut that must be described in three dimensions. There are many ways to visualize such models, using various 2D chromaticity spaces or in 3D color spaces.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="RYB_color_model">
       RYB color model
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primary_color&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: RYB color model">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/RYB_color_model" title="RYB color model">
       RYB color model
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      RYB (red, yellow, and blue) is a historical set of colors. It is primarily used in art and art education, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">
       painting
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It predates modern scientific
      <a href="/wiki/Color_theory" title="Color theory">
       color theory
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BYR_color_wheel.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="382" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/BYR_color_wheel.svg/220px-BYR_color_wheel.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/BYR_color_wheel.svg/330px-BYR_color_wheel.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/BYR_color_wheel.svg/440px-BYR_color_wheel.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BYR_color_wheel.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        RYB color wheel
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      RYB make up the primary colors in a painter's
      <a href="/wiki/Color_wheel" title="Color wheel">
       color wheel
      </a>
      ; the secondary colors OGV (orange, green, and violet) make up another triad. Triads are formed by 3 equidistant colors on a particular color wheel; neither RYB nor OGV is equidistant on a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Perceptual_uniformity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Perceptual uniformity (page does not exist)">
       perceptually uniform
      </a>
      color wheel, but rather have been
      <i>
       defined
      </i>
      to be equidistant in the RYB wheel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Painters have long used more than three "primary" colors in their palettesand at one point considered red, yellow, blue, and green to be the
      <i>
       four
      </i>
      primaries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Red, yellow, blue, and green are still widely considered the
      <a href="/wiki/Opponent_process#History" title="Opponent process">
       four psychological primary colors
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though red, yellow, and blue are sometimes listed as the
      <i>
       three
      </i>
      psychological primaries,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with black and white occasionally added as a fourth and fifth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the 18th century, as theorists became aware of
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
       Isaac Newton
      </a>
      's scientific experiments with light and prisms, red, yellow, and blue became the canonical primary colorssupposedly the fundamental sensory qualities that are blended in the perception of all physical colors and equally in the physical mixture of pigments or dyes. This theory became dogma, despite abundant evidence that red, yellow, and blue primaries cannot mix all other colors, and has survived in color theory to the present day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Using red, yellow, and blue as primaries yields a relatively small
      <a href="/wiki/Gamut" title="Gamut">
       gamut
      </a>
      , in which, among other problems, colorful greens, cyans, purples, and magentas are impossible to mix, because red, yellow, and blue do not correspond to the subtractive primaries dictated by human color vision. For this reason, modern three- or four-color printing processes, as well as color photography, use cyan, yellow, and magenta as primaries instead.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since cyan pigment absorbs red light, magenta absorbs green, and yellow absorbs blue, they each allow the other two light primaries to be reflected (or transmitted) and reach the eye. Thus when two of them are mixed, they do not absorb so much of the spectrum as to produce black, which is the absence of light. Most painters include colors in their palettes which cannot be mixed from yellow, red, and blue paints, and thus do not fit within the RYB color model. Some who do use a three-color palette opt for the cyan, yellow, and magenta used by printers, and others paint with 6 or more colors to widen their gamuts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cyan, magenta, and yellow used in printing are sometimes known as "process blue," "process red," and "process yellow."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_primaries">
       Psychological primaries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Opponent_process" title="Opponent process">
       opponent process
      </a>
      is a color theory that states that the human
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">
       visual system
      </a>
      interprets information about
      <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">
       color
      </a>
      by processing signals from
      <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">
       cones
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">
       rods
      </a>
      in an antagonistic manner. The three types of cones have some overlap in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavelengths" title="Wavelengths">
       wavelengths
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
       light
      </a>
      to which they respond, so it is more efficient for the visual system to record
      <i>
       differences
      </i>
      between the responses of cones, rather than each type of cone's individual response. The opponent color theory suggests that there are three opponent channels:
      <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">
       red
      </a>
      versus
      <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">
       green
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">
       blue
      </a>
      versus
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">
       yellow
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">
       black
      </a>
      versus
      <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">
       white
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Responses to one color of an opponent channel are antagonistic to those of the other color. The theory states that the particular colors considered by an observer to be uniquely representative of the concepts red, yellow, green, blue, white, and black might be called "psychological primary colors", because any other color could be described in terms of some combination of these.
     </p>
