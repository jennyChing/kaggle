                   Thursday, July 9, 2015

                 <div class="date-posts">
                  <div class="post-outer">
                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-0iK7C4zBlC8/VZ8G1WeArQI/AAAAAAAAEGk/6SKwjj3r330/s320/image4%25282%2529.JPG" itemprop="image_url"/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://milakehomegarden.blogspot.com/2015/07/about-3-of-my-favorite-ditch-flowers.html">
                      About 3 of My Favorite "Ditch Flowers"
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-1524678975062510580" itemprop="articleBody">
                     <div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on">
                      As I was walking the dog, I realized that some of my favorite flowers are found in ditches. There is such a great variety of wildflowers: different colors, different shapes, different sizes... and all as them tough as, well, weeds.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      People often pass these flowers by without a second glance. But I think they are worth taking the time to admire, and even to consider transplanting into a garden.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Here is some information on 3 of my favorite ditch flowers. In the future, I hope to have more posts on various wildflowers and other wild plants.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-0iK7C4zBlC8/VZ8G1WeArQI/AAAAAAAAEGk/6SKwjj3r330/s1600/image4%25282%2529.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="320" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-0iK7C4zBlC8/VZ8G1WeArQI/AAAAAAAAEGk/6SKwjj3r330/s320/image4%25282%2529.JPG" width="320"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <b>
                        CHICORY
                       </b>
                      </span>
                      <i>
                       (Cichorium intybus
                      </i>
                      )
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       <span id="goog_1974872390">
                       </span>
                       <span id="goog_1974872391">
                       </span>
                       Why I love it
                      </b>
                      : It's so very BLUE! It is also airy, tough and useful. Next year, I hope to create a blue border in my yard. When I do, I am going to try to transplant one of these from the ditch to my yard. I am also going to try to collect seed from some of the plants near my home to try starting them that way as well. Wish me luck!
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Description:
                      </b>
                      Herbaceous perennial. Blue stalkless flowers 1.5 inches (3.8cm) in size. On tall stalks to 4ft. (1.2m) in height. Has a fleshy, white taproot. The leaves are spiky, and kind of look like dandelion leaves. Blooms early summer.
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-Ik9_5RlhBGY/VZ8G7oBpguI/AAAAAAAAEG0/2FNIaWhsQds/s1600/image5%25281%2529.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="320" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-Ik9_5RlhBGY/VZ8G7oBpguI/AAAAAAAAEG0/2FNIaWhsQds/s320/image5%25281%2529.JPG" width="320"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Where found:
                      </b>
                      Roadsides, and open fields.
                      <br/>
                      Grows in full sun and poor soil. The plant is native to Europe, but has become naturalized throughout the United States and Australia as well.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Uses:
                      </b>
                      The roots can be roasted and ground as a coffee substitute or filler. You can make chicory "coffee" by steeping 1.5tsp ground roasted chicory in a cup of boiled water. The leaves can be boiled and eaten if you gather them early. Older leaves can turn very bitter. It can also be used as forage for livestock.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      For more information/works cited:
                      <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicory">
                       https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicory
                      </a>
                      or
                      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Field-Guide-Edible-Wild-Plants/dp/039592622X/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1436485932&amp;sr=8-1&amp;keywords=peterson+field+guide+edible+wild+plants" target="_blank">
                       The Peterson Field Guide: Edible Wild Plants Eastern/Central North America
                      </a>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-w2ehPtdzO84/VZ8G6VVMZII/AAAAAAAAEGs/s7krfMpJEnQ/s1600/image6%25281%2529.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="297" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-w2ehPtdzO84/VZ8G6VVMZII/AAAAAAAAEGs/s7krfMpJEnQ/s320/image6%25281%2529.JPG" width="320"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <b>
                       <span style="font-size: large;">
                        Queen Anne's Lace / Bishop's Lace/ Wild Carrot
                       </span>
                      </b>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <i>
                        <span style="font-size: small;">
                         (Daucus carota)
                        </span>
                       </i>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                         Why I love it:
                        </b>
                        These flowers really do look like lace. They attract beneficial insects, and are useful. I also love the story that goes with it: Once upon a time, Queen Ann (England 1665-1714) was sewing some lace and pricked her finger. Her blood spilled onto the lace. It is said that the purple flower in the center of the lacy umbel of
                        <i>
                         Daucus carota
                        </i>
                        resembles the queen's sewing accident.
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                         Description:
                        </b>
                       </span>
                       <i>
                        <span style="font-size: small;">
                        </span>
                       </i>
                      </span>
                      Herbaceous biennial. White, lacy, flat (umbel) flower clusters, often with a single dark purple flower in the center. Hairy stem. Stiff, 3-forked bracts. The leaves are feathery and resemble carrot leaves. The tap root is white and smells like carrot. 2-3 ft (60-90cm) in height. Blooms early summer until fall.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-JEacJxgZmSM/VZ8G_zRciSI/AAAAAAAAEG8/6x3ckXL9kZ8/s1600/image7.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="320" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-JEacJxgZmSM/VZ8G_zRciSI/AAAAAAAAEG8/6x3ckXL9kZ8/s320/image7.JPG" width="240"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <b>
                       WARNING!
                      </b>
                      This plant looks a lot like poison hemlock/ fools parsley! Please be 100% certain that you have the correct plant before attempting to use or eat this plant! The main difference between hemlock and Queen Anne's lace is that hemlock stems are hairless, whereas Queen Anne's lace has hairy stems.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Where found:
                      </b>
                      Roadsides and open fields throughout North America, zones 3-9. It is indigenous to Eurasia and Northeastern Africa. It can also be found in Australia.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Uses:
                      </b>
                      Cook the roots and eat them as carrots. SEE WARNING ABOVE.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      For more information/works cited:
                      <a href="http://www.ediblewildfood.com/queen-annes-lace.aspx">
                       http://www.ediblewildfood.com/queen-annes-lace.aspx
                      </a>
                      ,
                      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Field-Guide-Edible-Wild-Plants/dp/039592622X/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1436485932&amp;sr=8-1&amp;keywords=peterson+field+guide+edible+wild+plants" target="_blank">
                       The Peterson Field Guide: Edible Wild Plants Eastern/Central North America, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daucus_carota
                      </a>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       <span style="font-size: large;">
                        Daisy Fleabane
                       </span>
                      </b>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <i>
                         (Erigeron annuus)
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-xgQ7HyBfnWY/VZ8Gv5XZOEI/AAAAAAAAEGM/D6JTIUGSL3k/s1600/image2%25282%2529.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="273" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-xgQ7HyBfnWY/VZ8Gv5XZOEI/AAAAAAAAEGM/D6JTIUGSL3k/s640/image2%25282%2529.JPG" width="640"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                         Why I love it:
                        </b>
                        The tiny daisies are so cute!! It is also a native plant that bees adore. Ancient folklore states that these plants are good for repelling fleas, however its usefulness in this regard is debatable (or unlikely).
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <i>
                        </i>
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                        </b>
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                         Description:
                        </b>
                        Herbaceous annual or biennial in the aster family. The flowers are 1/2 inch (1-2cm) across and look like daisies. The entire plant is 1-3 feet tall (0.5-1m) The stems are grooved and hairy towards the base, and branch towards the top. The leaves are thin and oblong in shape. Some of the larger leaves have coarse teeth. They bloom in late spring to early summer for 1-2 months.
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-dPBLgq7sMls/VZ8Gz-tA_9I/AAAAAAAAEGc/kcXM4tQ1Qk4/s1600/image1%25281%2529.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-dPBLgq7sMls/VZ8Gz-tA_9I/AAAAAAAAEGc/kcXM4tQ1Qk4/s320/image1%25281%2529.JPG" style="cursor: move;" width="291"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                         Where found:
                        </b>
                        Roadsides and open field throughout North America (all zones). It prefers full sun and good moisture and can tolerate any type of soil.
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        <b>
                         Uses:
                        </b>
                        Food for sheep, groundhogs, and rabbits. Bees, wasps, butterflies and beetles love their pollen.
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       <span style="font-size: small;">
                        For more information/works cited:
                        <b>
                        </b>
                        <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erigeron_annuus">
                         https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erigeron_annuus
                        </a>
                        ,
                        <a href="http://www.illinoiswildflowers.info/prairie/plantx/ds_fleabanex.htm">
                         http://www.illinoiswildflowers.info/prairie/plantx/ds_fleabanex.htm
                        </a>
                        ,
                        <a href="http://plants.usda.gov/core/profile?symbol=ERAN">
                         http://plants.usda.gov/core/profile?symbol=ERAN
                        </a>
                        <b>
                         <br/>
                        </b>
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       <span style="font-size: large;">
                       </span>
                      </b>
                      I hope that on your next walk, bike ride or drive down the road, you take the time to admire these often over-looked treasures in our landscape, and that you love them as much as I (and the bees) do!

                      <b>

                      This year, I have started helping at the
                      <a href="http://www.livingstonhunger.com/our-work/community-gardens/" target="_blank">
                       Brighton Victory Garden
                      </a>
                      . So far I have assisted with planting, weeding and hoeing rows. I hope to assist with harvest in the fall.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The victory garden is a community garden run on behalf of the
                      <a href="http://www.livingstonhunger.com/" target="_blank">
                       Livingston County Hunger Council
                      </a>
                      by Ms. Kay Simmons.
                      <a href="http://www.brightongardenclub.com/" target="_blank">
                       The Brighton Garden Club
                      </a>
                      also maintains an herb and flower garden at the front of the Victory Garden. The garden sits on a full acre of land loaned to the hunger council by
                      <a href="http://www.brightonnc.com/" target="_blank">
                       Brighton NC Manufacturing Solutions
                      </a>
                      . Kay leads a team of volunteers from local schools, businesses, organizations as well as interested individuals to plant, grow and harvest vegetables that are then donated to the local food bank. The garden provides over 10,000 lbs of free fresh produce to families in need each year.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: left; margin-right: 1em; text-align: left;">

                           <img border="0" height="476" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-oMPj8YbTFV8/VYQTS--3OJI/AAAAAAAAD5o/HI3VxK6lwT8/s640/unnamed5.jpg" width="640"/>
                          </a>

                      The Hunger Council was established in 2009 with the goal to "eradicate hunger in Livingston County." According to their website, by "December, 2010  With a year of collaborative efforts complete, the 
members of the Livingston Hunger Council could boast a total of 
3,855,423 additional meals, reducing the hunger gap by almost 77%." Because of the efforts of the Hunger Council, Livingston County is considered to be one of the few food secure counties in Michigan.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Not only does the Victory Garden provide fresh vegetables to the 
community, it also provides allotments to individuals. In exchange for 
volunteer work in the garden, individuals are given the opportunity to 
use a portion of the acre of land on which to grow their own produce.

                      Project #3 - The Herb Garden
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">

                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-3522109648100254380" itemprop="articleBody">

                      As I have mentioned in previous posts, I just moved in to my house last year (summer 2014). The house came with several garden beds, as well as some very nice hardscaping. While some of the beds around the house were already filled to bursting with daylilies, hosta, spiderwort, sundrops, irises and roses, others were virtually empty.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      So, I am attempting to plant one major portion of the garden at my home each summer. This summer the garden that I chose to work on was the area closest to the lake. I decided to designate this bed as my combination herb garden and pie garden. (Pie garden = strawberries and rhubarb.)
                      <br/>
                      <div style="text-align: left;">
                      </div>
                      <br/>
                      The garden bed, like all others has advantages and challenges.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Advantages:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      One side of the bed faces the lake, so it gets sun from that direction all day.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The soil is sandy and drains well.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The previous owners already built in a nice hardscaped path and seating area along with a retaining wall. Because of this, the space is like it's own garden "room".
                      <br/>
                      <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: left; text-align: left;">
                       <tbody>
                        <tr>
                         <td style="text-align: center;">
                          <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-7HBVPJgLvaY/VYPz8kk_SVI/AAAAAAAADz4/VYxTW5MZKGo/s1600/DSCN1797.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                           <img border="0" height="320" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-7HBVPJgLvaY/VYPz8kk_SVI/AAAAAAAADz4/VYxTW5MZKGo/s320/DSCN1797.JPG" width="240"/>
                          </a>
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                        <tr>
                         <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                          The pie garden spring 2015 - BEFORE planting.
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                       </tbody>
                      </table>
                      <br/>
                      There is a pump from the lake, so I can hook up
                      <span id="goog_1111671778">
                      </span>
                      <span id="goog_1111671779">
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      my hose. Otherwise, I can just dunk my watering can in the lake and water with that.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Disadvantages:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The bed is on the North side of the property. It is not very protected in the winter.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The soil is sandy and does not contain a high amount of nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus or potassium).
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      To each side, there are oak trees in my neighbor's garden. These do 
shade the beds a bit in the morning and evening, but also keep the sun 
from mercilessly baking the area. They are both an advantage and disadvantage.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The chipmunks have made their homes in and under this garden. They have made holes everywhere and buried several acorns that have developed into oak seedling "weeds". My dog is no help at all with regard to critter control. She just points at the chipmunks... as if I don't already see!
                      <br/>
                      <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: left; text-align: left;">
                       <tbody>
                        <tr>
                         <td style="text-align: center;">

                           <img border="0" height="320" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-sJEtQe6DlCM/VYP6ZLq-OpI/AAAAAAAAD14/SBm7Lc-u-Wo/s320/DSCN2003.JPG" style="cursor: move;" width="240"/>
                          </a>
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                        <tr>
                         <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                          The pie garden June 2015 - AFTER planting.

                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       What I Did to Prepare the Area:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Last fall, I brought in compost from
                      <a href="http://www.tuthillfarms.com/1/235/index.asp" target="_blank">
                       Tuthill Farms
                      </a>
                      .
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      I spread this over the bed to a depth of about 2 inches. This should help to improve the nutrients in the soil and help hold on to some moisture.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      This spring, I spread corn gluten mean (nitrogen), bone meal (phosphorus), and potash (potassium) over the area to improve the nutrients in the soil. These are good, slow-release, organic fertilizers.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      I mulched the area.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      I hooked up a drip hose and zig-zagged it through the garden for ease of watering.
                      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Miracle-MGSPA38050CC-Premium-Fittings-50-Feet/dp/B00E1HXYQA/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1434709527&amp;sr=8-1&amp;keywords=drip+hose" target="_blank">
                       Drip hoses
                      </a>
                      are more efficient at delivering water to the plants than spray irrigation because less is lost to evaporation. If you are interested in conserving water, but still want to provide for your garden, please consider one of these hoses. You can find them at any large hardware store (Home Depot, Lowes, Ace etc.).
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      I also purchased a
                      <a href="http://www.havahart.com/x-small-1-door-trap-3-pack" target="_blank">
                       live trap
                      </a>
                      and have been capturing the chipmunks one by one. Once I catch them, I drive them several miles away to empty woodlots and let them go. I may need to continue catching and releasing them for some time. Each time that I capture one, I see another peeking at me from under a bush or a rock!
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       What I Planted (or left planted) in the Herb Garden:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-9IeppFKL2gQ/VYQNJV7kIfI/AAAAAAAAD5Q/tBFfgWRgTfc/s1600/unnamed7.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-9IeppFKL2gQ/VYQNJV7kIfI/AAAAAAAAD5Q/tBFfgWRgTfc/s1600/unnamed7.jpg"/>
                      </a>
                      <span id="goog_1111671953">
                      </span>
                      <span id="goog_1111671954">
                      </span>
                      Sibirian Iris / Iris Siberica (purple-blue flowers) - The former owner planted this, but I like it! I
                      <br/>
                      especially like how the reedy leaves blow in the breeze off of the lake, as well as the seed pods that last through the winter.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      German Iris / Iris Germanica (yellow and white, and purple-blue flowers)
 - The former owner planted this, but I like it as well. Irises remind 
me of my grandmother.
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: left; margin-right: 1em; text-align: left;">
                       <tbody>
                        <tr>
                         <td style="text-align: center;">
                          <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-92kar0PIIvU/VYP9rxv7-8I/AAAAAAAAD20/eHnrmRfx_Jw/s1600/DSCN1911.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                           <img border="0" height="304" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-92kar0PIIvU/VYP9rxv7-8I/AAAAAAAAD20/eHnrmRfx_Jw/s320/DSCN1911.JPG" width="320"/>
                          </a>
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                        <tr>
                         <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                          German Iris
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                       </tbody>
                      </table>
                      <br/>
                      Sedum (type unknown, yellow flowers) - The former owner planted this to fill the cracks in the retaining-wall rocks. I like it!
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Tickseed / Coreopsis (yellow flowers) - The former owner planted this, too. This is a great plant for enticing beneficial insects.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Roses / Rosa (hybrid type unknown, pink flowers) - The former owner planted this. I sort of like it, but it is not highly useful, and is only doing moderately well where it is located. They are nibbled on by insects. Last winter, I protected these with rose cones. They did better this year than the year before
                      <br/>
                      because of the protection. Perhaps they will improve with time and better care.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: right; text-align: left;">
                       <tbody>
                        <tr>
                         <td style="text-align: center;">
                          <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-CyLwW0QsTJo/VYP9skxUV1I/AAAAAAAAD28/RODgKpg9-mM/s1600/DSCN1909.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                           <img border="0" height="320" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-CyLwW0QsTJo/VYP9skxUV1I/AAAAAAAAD28/RODgKpg9-mM/s320/DSCN1909.JPG" width="270"/>
                          </a>
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                        <tr>
                         <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                          German Iris &amp; Sedum in the background.
                         </td>
                        </tr>
                       </tbody>
                      </table>
                      Red Hot Poker / Kniphofia uvaria - The former owner planted this, I like it, but I *might* move it to the sunny front garden at a later time to make room for more herbs. I haven't decided for sure yet. It is lovely and doing fairly well where it is now.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Apothecary Rose / Rosa gallica (pink flowers) - This is a tough rose that smells good, makes edible flowers, makes large edible hips and was traditionally used as a medicinal plant. It is also the rose for the house of Lancaster during the British War of the Roses - for all you history buffs and Shakespeare fans.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Dwarf Bartlett Pear / Pyrus 'Bartlett' - I am hoping for fruit in the future! I did plant another pear tree elsewhere in the garden so the two can fertilize each other.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Lemon Balm / Melissa officinalis - The leaves smell like lemon and can be used to make tea, potpourri, or to flavor food.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Angelica / Angelica archangelica - This herb is a biennial. The leaves taste a bit like licorice. It can be used to flavor salad, fruit and jam. It also makes a large umbel shaped flower that beneficial insects enjoy.
                      <br/>

                      Bee Balm / Monarda didyma - This is another pollinator-friendly tea herb. The dried flowers taste a bit like earl grey tea.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Sorrel / Rumex acetosa - This is a perennial plant with sour edible leaves. It can be used as a salad green. The leaves are also food for larval butterflies and moths.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Chives &amp; Garlic Chives / Allium schoenoprasum &amp; Allium tuberosum - The narrow leaves and purple or white flowers of this plant can be used to season a variety of foods. I planted these near the roses to attempt to scare off some of the insects that might nibble the roses. So far, the roses are being nibbled anyway!

                      Egyptian Walking Onions / Allium x proliferum - This onion "walks" by making bulbs at the end of its stalks, above the ground. The bulbs become too heavy for the stalk, and cause the stalk to tip to the ground. The bulbs then grow in the dirt where they fall. This way, they "walk" across the garden, one step at a time, each summer. The bulbs above and below ground can both be eaten. They taste like scallions to me.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Honeysuckle Vine / Lonicera sempervirens - I am growing this down the 
sea wall by training it around some chicken wire. I am growing it 
primarily for the lovely bright-pink flowers and the sweet, sweet smell.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Stinging Nettles / Urtica Dioica - I know, I know it's a weed... but a useful one! It can be soaked in water to make fertilizer for tomatoes. When you cook it, the sting vanishes and it can be eaten. It is actually fairly high in protein. It is also host for red admiral butterfly larva.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Joe Pye Weed / Eutrochium purpureum
                      <i>
                       -
                      </i>
                      This is NOT edible. I grew it to make the beneficial insects happy and also to provide a screen at the end of the garden nearest to the property line. It grows into a very tall plant with a purple umbel for the flower. It blooms around mid to late August.

                      <br/>
                      Pincushion Flower / Scabosia - This is not edible. I planted it 
because it makes cute purple flowers nearly all summer long, and because
 the butterflies like it.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Salad burnet / Sanguisorba 
minor - The leaves of this herb are often used in salads (thus the 
name). They have a light, almost sweet flavor.

                      </div>
                      Lemon Grass / Cymbopogon citratus - I either have to grow this one as an annual or attempt to bring it indoors in the winter. It is a tropical plant that looks like grass but tastes like lemon. It is frequently used in Thai cooking.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Hyssop / Hyssopus officinalis - This makes narrow, feathery purple flowers. It tastes like a cross
                      <br/>
                      <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: right; text-align: right;">
                       <tbody>
                        <tr>
                         <td style="text-align: center;">
                          <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Rd-QrxUt_ro/VYP-cepD8LI/AAAAAAAAD30/FTm1WQEagas/s1600/DSCN2000.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                           <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Rd-QrxUt_ro/VYP-cepD8LI/AAAAAAAAD30/FTm1WQEagas/s320/DSCN2000.JPG" width="231"/>

                      between sage, anise and mint. The beneficial insects love it. This is another one that I prefer to dry and use as tea, but it can also be used to season salads, soup and other foods. It tends to re-seed itself easily.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Lovage / Levisticum officinale - This is another tall plant with a white umbel for a flower. The leaves taste like celery and can be used as a flavor substitute for celery in things like salad and soup.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Winter Savory / Satureja montana - The leaves of this herb have a peppery flavor and can be used to flavor a variety of foods. I hear that it also helps to decrease flatus (gas) when cooked with beans or Jerusalem artichokes.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Catnip / Nepeta cataria - This is the plant that makes the kittens crazy! I grew it to dry and gift to my in-laws' kitty. It can also be used to make tea.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Cilantro/ Coriander / Coriandrum sativum - This is an annual herb. I use it to flavor a variety of Mexican and Thai dishes. It often bolts on me early. Luckily the seeds can also be used as a flavoring.

                      Nasturtiums / Tropaeloum majus - These are annual flowers. The flower petals can be eaten. They don't taste like much, but they can improve the presentation of any dish with their bright colors. They also can act as a trap for aphids. Aphids love to nibble nasturtiums and prefer them to many vegetables. When the aphids appear, you simply allow them to swarm the nasturtium and then douse them (plant and all) in a bucket of soapy water. Sure you end up killing the nasturtium, but think of all the tomatoes you may be rescuing!
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Horehound / Marrubium vulgare - The leaves of these have an interesting flavor. It is used to flavor horehound candy. Ever tried it? No? Get ye to

                      <br/>
                      established, it will produce small, non-fuzzy, edible, kiwis. Usually, a male and female vine are both required. However, the one I purchased is grafted with both male and female branches. I hope that it will do well. I have never grown this before.

                      Dill / Anethum graveolens - This is my husband's favorite herb. It 
can be used to flavor a variety of foods including pickles, fish and 
others. My husband mixes it into his salmon dip. YUUUUUM!

                      Fennel
 / Foeniculum vulgare - The leaves of this plant taste like anise. It 
also makes a bulb that can be sliced thin and cooked into a variety of 
dishes. It is host plant to the swallow tail butterfly. It is very 
tall and makes yellow umbel flowers. It looks and smells wonderful when
 waving in the breeze!
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      California Poppies / Eschscholzia Californica - I planted this mostly 
for looks and to make the bees happy, but the seeds can also be eaten.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Sweet Majoram / Origanum majorana - This herb is commonly 
mixed with orgeano, basil, and thyme to make Italian spice mix. It is 
great for flavoring a variety of dishes. It's flavor is difficult to 
describe... you should just try tasting it sometime!

 aster / Stokesia laevis - This is NOT edible. I grew it because it

                      Chocolate cosmos / Cosmos atrosanguineus - I grew these because they 
smell like chocolate. 'Nough said. (They are annuals and NOT edible!)
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Salsify / Scorzonera - The young leaf shoots, flower petals and root of this plant can all be eaten. The root has a sweet flavor when cooked. The leaves can be used as a spinach substitute. The flower buds can be added to salad.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Good King Henry / Chenopodium bonus-henricus - The young leaves can be used in salad. They are bitter and high in iron. The flowering shoots can also be peeled and be used as an asparagus substitute.

                      Thyme &amp; Lemon Thyme/ thymus vulgaris &amp;Thymus serpyllum 
citriodora - This herb is great in cooking! It also makes lovely, small,
 purple bee-friendly flowers.

                      <br/>

                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Milkweed / Asclepias syriaca - The young shoots of this plant are edible, but I grow it for the butterflies. It is host plant to monarch butterfly larva. It also makes a beautiful ball shaped flower.

                      <b>
                       I Am Growing the Following in Pots in the Herb Garden:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Tophat Blueberry / Vaccinium 'top hat' - I love blueberries! I am growing this in a pot because it is easier to control the acidity of the soil this way. Blueberries prefer a soil pH of 5.5-6.5. My ground soil is mostly alkaline (pH 7.8), so filling a pot with more acidic soil and peat was the only way that I could grow this plant.

It makes great sachets and potpourri. I think that I will need to protect this plant in the winter as I do with the roses since the garden is on the North side of the yard. This lavender is hardy to zone 5, but this garden's exposure can cause it to seem like zone 3-4 in the winter at times.

                      We moved in to our home here at the lake last year. Because of this, it is not until now that I have been able to test my garden soil.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Testing the soil is especially important because I am planning on putting in a vegetable garden next year. In order to get a good yield of vegetables, it is critical for the soil to contain the right nutrients. As discussed in the last "

                       Pimp My Soil
                      </a>
                      " post, simply spreading a fertilizer around can be both wasteful, can pollute waterways with runoff, and could result in adding the wrong type or amount of nutrients to the soil. So, it was best for me to find out which nutrients, and in what amounts my particular patch of soil requires. I am doing the test now in preparation for next spring when I will be installing the garden and so that I will have a sort-of baseline measurement for the quality of my garden soil.

                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       WHAT I WILL DO TO FERTILIZE:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      My soon-to-be vegetable garden is about 500 square feet in size.
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       <br/>
                      </b>
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      Because of these results I plan on adding a 2" layer of compost from
                      <a href="http://www.tuthillfarms.com/1/235/index.asp" target="_blank">
                       Tuthill Farms
                      </a>
                      in order to maintain a healthy level of organic matter in the soil and to reduce the amount of fertilizer that I will need. Hopefully this will slightly acidify the soil as well since the soil's pH is quite alkaline at pH 7.8.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      I also want to use organic fertilizers because these will not cause salt build up in the soil over time the way that manufactured fertilizers can. It will also be slow-release, and less likely to cause problems if any did somehow run off into the lake. Here is what I have chosen to use:
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      4lbs Corn gluten meal (8-0-0) (Nitrogen) will be added to the garden area before planting. Three weeks after the plants emerge, I will add 2 more pounds of this to the area.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      4lbs Bone meal (0-10-0) (Phosphorus) will be added to the garden area before planting.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      2lbs Potash (0-0-60) (Potassium) will be added to the garden area before planting.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      I will work the fertilizer into the soil before I plant. I will also be keeping an eye on my vegetables in case that any need a small amount of top dressing. Hopefully this will improve my soil a great deal and make for some lovely veggies during harvest 2016! Please check back at the blog then to see the results
                      <br/>

                      If you too would like to get a soil test too, please go to
                      <a href="http://msusoiltest.com/" target="_blank">
                       http://msusoiltest.com
                      </a>
                      if you live in Michigan, or do a Google search for your local extension office's soil test web page if you live elsewhere.

                      <b>
                       What is horseradish?
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-ZU9kTQpyIC8/VXnKa-LX-WI/AAAAAAAADis/mAA-bd1_CKk/s1600/DSCN1978.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">

                      Horseradish is one of the easiest plants to grow, and is a delicious flavoring for sauces, marinades and burger patties. According to wikipedia, "
                      <b>
                       Horseradish
                      </b>
                      (
                      <i>
                       Armoracia rusticana
                      </i>
                      , syn.
                      <i>
                       Cochlearia armoracia
                      </i>
                      ) is a
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">
                       perennial plant
                      </a>
                      of the
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brassicaceae" title="Brassicaceae">
                       Brassicaceae
                      </a>
                      family (which also includes
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mustard_plant" title="Mustard plant">
                       mustard
                      </a>
                      ,
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasabi" title="Wasabi">
                       wasabi
                      </a>
                      ,
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broccoli" title="Broccoli">
                       broccoli
                      </a>
                      , and
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">
                       cabbage
                      </a>
                      ). The plant is probably native to southeastern
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
                       Europe
                      </a>
                      and western
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
                       Asia
                      </a>
                      .
 It is now popular around the world. It grows up to 1.5 meters (4.9 
feet) tall, and is cultivated primarily for its large, white, tapered
                      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root" title="Root">
                       root
                      </a>
                      ."
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-0kA5x5tuFpo/VXnKoHNumBI/AAAAAAAADjQ/WAIXB2x-I5Q/s1600/DSCN1982.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="240" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-0kA5x5tuFpo/VXnKoHNumBI/AAAAAAAADjQ/WAIXB2x-I5Q/s320/DSCN1982.JPG" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                      <b>
                       How to plant horseradish:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Horseradish is hardy in USDA zones 3-9. It is a very tough plant and spreads vigorously via its root. It is best planted in a pot in most garden situations. Simply place the root in the dirt about 1 -2 inches from the surface, laying lengthwise. Cover with dirt and water. You can purchase a horseradish root from your local garden center, or
                      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/s/?ie=UTF8&amp;keywords=horseradish+roots&amp;tag=mh0b-20&amp;index=aps&amp;hvadid=4161466693&amp;hvqmt=p&amp;hvbmt=bp&amp;hvdev=c&amp;ref=pd_sl_8oielbg8ur_p" target="_blank">
                       online garden source
                      </a>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-dBLLN4gZbNs/VXnKbGPTcNI/AAAAAAAADiw/qabRuXhLcys/s1600/DSCN1976.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="240" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-dBLLN4gZbNs/VXnKbGPTcNI/AAAAAAAADiw/qabRuXhLcys/s320/DSCN1976.JPG" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                      <b>
                       How to harvest horseradish:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      If you have grown the horseradish in a pot,
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      it is ready to harvest when the plant fills the pot or the pot is root-bound. If you have planted the horseradish in the ground (not recommended!) you can dig it up about a year after planting. Be sure to get every piece of root out of the ground, or it will regrow from the root scraps.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-QkRCrkZR7XQ/VXnKgbB5N_I/AAAAAAAADjA/k51VMNOQgok/s1600/DSCN1979.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="240" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-QkRCrkZR7XQ/VXnKgbB5N_I/AAAAAAAADjA/k51VMNOQgok/s320/DSCN1979.JPG" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                      Once you have un-potted or dug up the horseradish, you can pull it apart at the roots with a spading fork, or your hands. Then, rise off the roots with a strong blast of hose water.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      If you wish, you can save a portion of the leaves and root to re-plant in the pot. (A bit like dividing and re-planting a perennial such as daylilies.) Clean the rest of the roots thoroughly for use in the kitchen.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Cleaning the horseradish:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-bn75F66zkUY/VXnKpFb4KyI/AAAAAAAADjY/_YTQzVcMJwI/s1600/DSCN1981.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="240" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-bn75F66zkUY/VXnKpFb4KyI/AAAAAAAADjY/_YTQzVcMJwI/s320/DSCN1981.JPG" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                      After you blast the horseradish root with the hose, use a sharp knife and/or pruners to trim the thin, hair-like roots from the
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      main tap root. Next, scrub the root with a scrubbing pad. Then, peel it with a potato peeler as you would a carrot.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Preserving the horseradish:
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Unfortunately, horseradish cannot be hot water canned or pressure canned. This is because heat will destroy the allyl isothiocynate (mustard oil) chemical that gives the horseradish its flavor. Thus, canning would cause the horseradish to lose its spicy-heat and become bitter.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      CAUTION: When preparing horseradish, please do so in a well ventilated area. The root will not smell like much initially, but once it is cut, it emits a powerful mustard smell that will make your eyes burn! Onions have NOTHING on horseradish when it comes to making your cry!
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       Option 1:
                      </b>
                      Simply cut the horseradish into slices, place it in a zip bag or plastic container and place it in the freezer. It will last in the freezer for at least a year.
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                      </b>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-UOLcS1vnebY/VXnKv0RtncI/AAAAAAAADjo/T3ON8dQJNwI/s1600/DSCN1986.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="240" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-UOLcS1vnebY/VXnKv0RtncI/AAAAAAAADjo/T3ON8dQJNwI/s320/DSCN1986.JPG" width="320"/>
                       </a>
                       <b>
                       </b>
                      </div>
                      <b>
                       Option 2:
                      </b>
                      Make pickled horseradish "sauce".
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <u>
                       Ingredients:
                      </u>
                      <br/>
                      1/2 lb cleaned horseradish, 2 cups vinegar, 1 teaspoon salt, 1 teaspoon sugar.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <u>
                       Directions:
                      </u>
                      <br/>
                      1. Sterilize some glass canning jars and lids by boiling them in water for 10 minutes.
                      <br/>
                      2. Cut the horseradish root into pieces.
                      <br/>
                      3.Place the horseradish into a food processor and mince it finely. (You could also
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-y1SpaG5YSeA/VXnK04hOQLI/AAAAAAAADj4/xCybeFZgimk/s1600/DSCN1987.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="240" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-y1SpaG5YSeA/VXnK04hOQLI/AAAAAAAADj4/xCybeFZgimk/s320/DSCN1987.JPG" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                      grate the horseradish, but please only do this in a well ventilated area while wearing goggles and gloves.)
                      <br/>
                      4. Mix the vinegar, salt and sugar together.
                      <br/>
                      5. Mix the vinegar solution together with the horseradish pieces.
                      <br/>
                      6. Place the mixture into the sterilized glass jars and seal the lid tightly
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-lUZZgOUBb_c/VXnLTG82BoI/AAAAAAAADkI/5RSye3gCQEY/s1600/DSCN1985.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-lUZZgOUBb_c/VXnLTG82BoI/AAAAAAAADkI/5RSye3gCQEY/s320/DSCN1985.JPG" width="240"/>
                      </a>
                      The horseradish will keep for 6 months to a year in the refrigerator with this method.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      You could also try freezing the horseradish this way, but be sure to leave enough space in the jar for the liquid to expand. Otherwise, the jar may break in your freezer. The horseradish will keep for at least a year when frozen.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      CAUTION: As with any pickled, jarred, refrigerated or fresh food (whether you make it yourself or buy it in a store): if the food appears moldy, slimy, smells bad or "off", do NOT eat it. Please use common sense and err on the side of caution.
