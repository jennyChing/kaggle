       <h1>
        Refrigerant company Actrol fined by ACCC for carbon tax rip-off
       </h1>
       <div class="byline">
        By finance reporter
        <a href="/news/simon-frazer/5639302" target="_self" title="">
         Simon Frazer
        </a>
       </div>
       <p class="published">
        Posted
        <span class="timestamp">
         April 07, 2015 15:53:51
        </span>
       </p>
       <div class="attached-content">
        <div class="inline-content photo left">
         <a href="/news/2015-04-07/australian-competition-and-consumer-commission/6375642">
          <img alt="Australian Competition and Consumer Commission sign" height="227" src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/684622-3x2-340x227.jpg" title="Australian Competition and Consumer Commission" width="340"/>
         </a>
         <a class="inline-caption" href="/news/2015-04-07/australian-competition-and-consumer-commission/6375642">
          <strong>
           Photo:
          </strong>
          The ACCC said this is likely to be the largest carbon tax related case.
          <span class="source">
           (ABC TV)
          </span>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="inline-content map left">
         <div class="story-map">
         </div>
         <a class="inline-caption" href="http://maps.google.com/?q=-26.000,134.500(Australia)&amp;z=5">
          <strong>
           Map:
          </strong>
          Australia
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        A refrigeration gas supplier has been fined more than half a million dollars for falsely blaming price rises on the carbon tax.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Federal Court has ordered Actrol Parts Pty Ltd to pay a $520,000 fine plus $50,000 towards the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission's (ACCC) costs.
       </p>
       <p>
        The ACCC says Actrol made false representations in a letter posted on its website and sent to around 8,000 customers as the carbon tax came into effect in July 2012.
       </p>
       <p>
        Actrol increased the price of several hydrofluorocarbon refrigerants by between 100 per cent and nearly 300 per cent, advising the rise was due to the carbon tax, changes in input costs and general market conditions.
       </p>
       <p>
        But ACCC chairman Rod Sims said Actrol had built a large stockpile of the gases before the tax was introduced.
       </p>
       <p>
        "At the time the carbon tax came in, having insulated itself from the effect of the carbon tax, it then increased its prices by very large amounts and suggested it was due to cost increases when in fact it was largely all about increasing their margin," Mr Sims said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "So they gave the impression to consumers that it was to cover the cost of the carbon tax when in fact it wasn't."
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Sims said it is the commission's final case related to the beginning of the tax, with the focus now on ensuring companies lowered their prices again when it was phased out.
       </p>
       <p>
        "This is the most serious matter we've had. The others we've dealt with through infringement notices or warning letters," Mr Sims said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It was I think more tempting to misrepresent on the way in, where you could represent that you'd had a cost increase and that's why you were having a price increase.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There wasn't as much to gain on the way out ... so we don't really expect there will be that much action."
       </p>
       <p>
        The gases are widely used in home and commercial refrigerators and air conditioning systems.
       </p>
       <p>
        Actrol says it is the largest wholesale parts supplier for the sector in Australia, with 60 branches around the country.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Sims says the fine is a significant deterrence, given Actrol posted revenue of around $250 million last financial year.
       </p>
