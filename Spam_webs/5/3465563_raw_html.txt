     <p>
      A
      <b>
       grape
      </b>
      is a fruiting
      <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">
       berry
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">
       deciduous
      </a>
      woody
      <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">
       vines
      </a>
      of the botanical
      <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">
       genus
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">
        Vitis
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Grapes can be eaten raw or they can be used for making
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">
       jam
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_juice" title="Grape juice">
       juice
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jelly_(fruit_preserves)" title="Jelly (fruit preserves)">
       jelly
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Grape_seed_extract" title="Grape seed extract">
       grape seed extract
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">
       raisins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">
       vinegar
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Grape_seed_oil" title="Grape seed oil">
       grape seed oil
      </a>
      . Grapes are a non-
      <a href="/wiki/Climacteric_(botany)" title="Climacteric (botany)">
       climacteric
      </a>
      type of
      <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">
       fruit
      </a>
      , generally occurring in clusters.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The cultivation of the domesticated grape began 6,0008,000 years ago in the
      <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">
       Near East
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-This_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-This-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The earliest archeological evidence for a dominant position of wine-making in human culture dates from 8,000 years ago in
      <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">
       Georgia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">
       Yeast
      </a>
      , one of the earliest domesticated
      <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">
       microorganisms
      </a>
      , occurs naturally on the skins of grapes, leading to the innovation of alcoholic drinks such as wine. The earliest known production occurred around 8,000 years ago on the territory of Georgia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During an extensive gene-mapping project, archaeologists analyzed the heritage of more than 110 modern grape cultivars, and narrowed their origin to a region in Georgia, where wine residues were also discovered on the inner surfaces of 8,000-year-old ceramic storage jars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The oldest winery was found in
      <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">
       Armenia
      </a>
      , dating to around 4000 BC. By the 9th century AD the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">
       Shiraz
      </a>
      was known to produce some of the finest wines in the Middle East. Thus it has been proposed that
      <a href="/wiki/Syrah" title="Syrah">
       Syrah
      </a>
      red wine is named after
      <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">
       Shiraz
      </a>
      , a city in Persia where the grape was used to make
      <a href="/wiki/Shirazi_wine" title="Shirazi wine">
       Shirazi wine
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">
       Ancient Egyptian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieroglyphics" title="Hieroglyphics">
       hieroglyphics
      </a>
      record the cultivation of purple grapes, and history attests to the ancient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greeks_(wine)" title="Greeks (wine)">
       Greeks
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians_(wine)" title="Phoenicians (wine)">
       Phoenicians
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome_(wine)" title="Ancient Rome (wine)">
       Romans
      </a>
      growing purple grapes for both eating and wine production. The growing of grapes would later spread to other regions in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      , as well as
      <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
       North Africa
      </a>
      , and eventually in
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In North America, native grapes belonging to various species of the
      <i>
       Vitis
      </i>
      genus proliferate in the wild across the continent, and were a part of the diet of many
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
       Native Americans
      </a>
      , but were considered by European colonists to be unsuitable for wine.
      <i>
       Vitis vinifera
      </i>
      cultivars were imported for that purpose.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Grapes are a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">
       fruit
      </a>
      that grow in clusters of 15 to 300, and can be crimson, black, dark blue, yellow, green, orange and pink. "White" grapes are actually green in color, and are evolutionarily derived from the purple grape.
      <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">
       Mutations
      </a>
      in two regulatory genes of white grapes turn off production of
      <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">
       anthocyanins
      </a>
      , which are responsible for the color of purple grapes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Anthocyanins and other
      <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">
       pigment
      </a>
      chemicals of the larger family of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">
       polyphenols
      </a>
      in purple grapes are responsible for the varying shades of purple in red wines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Grapes are typically an ellipsoid shape resembling a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">
       prolate spheroid
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grapevines">
       Grapevines
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">
       Vitis
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ConcordGrapes.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="705" data-file-width="659" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ConcordGrapes.jpg/170px-ConcordGrapes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ConcordGrapes.jpg/255px-ConcordGrapes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ConcordGrapes.jpg/340px-ConcordGrapes.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ConcordGrapes.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Concord is a variety of North American
        <i>
         labrusca
        </i>
        grape
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Most grapes come from
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_grape_varieties" title="List of grape varieties">
       cultivars
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vitis_vinifera" title="Vitis vinifera">
        Vitis vinifera
       </a>
      </i>
      , the European grapevine native to the Mediterranean and Central Asia. Minor amounts of fruit and wine come from American and Asian species such as:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vitis_labrusca" title="Vitis labrusca">
         Vitis labrusca
        </a>
       </i>
       , the North American table and grape juice grapevines (including the
       <a href="/wiki/Concord_(grape)" title="Concord (grape)">
        Concord
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">
        cultivar
       </a>
       ), sometimes used for wine, are native to the
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">
        Eastern United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vitis_riparia" title="Vitis riparia">
         Vitis riparia
        </a>
       </i>
       , a wild vine of
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       , is sometimes used for winemaking and for jam. It is native to the entire
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_U.S." title="Eastern U.S.">
        Eastern U.S.
       </a>
       and north to
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Vitis rotundifolia
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">
        muscadines
       </a>
       , used for jams and wine, are native to the
       <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">
        Southeastern United States
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">
        Delaware
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">
        Gulf of Mexico
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vitis_amurensis" title="Vitis amurensis">
         Vitis amurensis
        </a>
       </i>
       is the most important Asian species.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_production">
       Distribution and production
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">
       Food and Agriculture Organization
      </a>
      (FAO), 75,866 square kilometers of the world are dedicated to grapes. Approximately 71% of world grape production is used for wine, 27% as fresh fruit, and 2% as
      <a href="/wiki/Dried_fruit" title="Dried fruit">
       dried fruit
      </a>
      . A portion of grape production goes to producing grape juice to be reconstituted for fruits canned "with no added sugar" and "100% natural". The area dedicated to vineyards is increasing by about 2% per year.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are no reliable statistics that break down grape production by variety. It is believed that the most widely planted variety is
      <a href="/wiki/Sultana_(grape)" title="Sultana (grape)">
       Sultana
      </a>
      , also known as Thompson Seedless, with at least 3,600km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      . (880,000 acres) dedicated to it. The second most common variety is
      <a href="/wiki/Air%C3%A9n" title="Airn">
       Airn
      </a>
      . Other popular varieties include
      <a href="/wiki/Cabernet_Sauvignon" title="Cabernet Sauvignon">
       Cabernet Sauvignon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sauvignon_blanc" title="Sauvignon blanc">
       Sauvignon blanc
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cabernet_Franc" title="Cabernet Franc">
       Cabernet Franc
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Merlot" title="Merlot">
       Merlot
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Grenache" title="Grenache">
       Grenache
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tempranillo" title="Tempranillo">
       Tempranillo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Riesling" title="Riesling">
       Riesling
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chardonnay" title="Chardonnay">
       Chardonnay
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Table_and_wine_grapes">
       Table and wine grapes
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grapes03.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1060" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wine_grapes03.jpg/170px-Wine_grapes03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wine_grapes03.jpg/255px-Wine_grapes03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wine_grapes03.jpg/340px-Wine_grapes03.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grapes03.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Wine grapes on the vine
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Commercially cultivated grapes can usually be classified as either table or wine grapes, based on their intended method of consumption: eaten raw (table grapes) or used to make
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      (wine grapes). While almost all of them belong to the same species,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vitis_vinifera" title="Vitis vinifera">
        Vitis vinifera
       </a>
      </i>
      , table and wine grapes have significant differences, brought about through
      <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">
       selective breeding
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Table_grape" title="Table grape">
       Table grape
      </a>
      cultivars tend to have large, seedless fruit (see below) with relatively thin skin. Wine grapes are smaller, usually seeded, and have relatively thick skins (a desirable characteristic in winemaking, since much of the aroma in wine comes from the skin). Wine grapes also tend to be very sweet: they are harvested at the time when their juice is approximately 24% sugar by weight. By comparison, commercially produced "100% grape juice", made from table grapes is usually around 15% sugar by weight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Seedless_grapes">
       Seedless grapes
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Seedless cultivars now make up the overwhelming majority of table grape plantings. Because grapevines are
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">
       vegetatively propagated
      </a>
      by cuttings, the lack of seeds does not present a problem for reproduction. It is an issue for breeders, who must either use a seeded variety as the female parent or rescue embryos early in development using
      <a href="/wiki/Plant_tissue_culture" title="Plant tissue culture">
       tissue culture
      </a>
      techniques.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are several sources of the seedlessness trait, and essentially all commercial cultivators get it from one of three sources:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thompson_Seedless" title="Thompson Seedless">
       Thompson Seedless
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Russian_Seedless&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Russian Seedless (page does not exist)">
       Russian Seedless
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Monukka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Black Monukka (page does not exist)">
       Black Monukka
      </a>
      , all being cultivars of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vitis_vinifera" title="Vitis vinifera">
        Vitis vinifera
       </a>
      </i>
      . There are currently more than a dozen varieties of seedless grapes. Several, such as Einset Seedless, Benjamin Gunnels's Prime seedless grapes, Reliance and Venus, have been specifically cultivated for hardiness and quality in the relatively cold climates of northeastern United States and southern
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      content of grape seeds (see
      <a href="#Health_claims">

      , below).

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>

        </span>

        <span>

      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Raisins.2C_currants_and_sultanas">
       Raisins, currants and sultanas
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raisins_01.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2025" data-file-width="3223" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Raisins_01.jpg/220px-Raisins_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Raisins_01.jpg/330px-Raisins_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Raisins_01.jpg/440px-Raisins_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raisins_01.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">
         Raisins
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In most of Europe and North America, dried grapes are referred to as "raisins" or the local equivalent. In the UK, three different varieties are recognized, forcing the EU to use the term "Dried vine fruit" in official documents.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">
        raisin
       </a>
      </i>
      is any dried grape. While
      <i>
       raisin
      </i>
      is a French
      <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
       loanword
      </a>
      , the word in French refers to the fresh fruit;
      <i>
       grappe
      </i>
      (from which the English
      <i>
       grape
      </i>
      is derived) refers to the bunch (as in
      <i>
       une grappe de raisins
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zante_currant" title="Zante currant">
        currant
       </a>
      </i>
      is a dried
      <a href="/wiki/Zakynthos" title="Zakynthos">
       Zante
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Corinth" title="Black Corinth">
       Black Corinth
      </a>
      grape, the name being a corruption of the French
      <i>
       raisin de Corinthe
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Corinth" title="Corinth">
       Corinth
      </a>
      grape).
      <i>
       Currant
      </i>
      has also come to refer to the
      <a href="/wiki/Blackcurrant" title="Blackcurrant">
       blackcurrant
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Redcurrant" title="Redcurrant">
       redcurrant
      </a>
      , two berries unrelated to grapes.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <i>
       sultana
      </i>
      was originally a raisin made from
      <a href="/wiki/Sultana_(grape)" title="Sultana (grape)">
       Sultana
      </a>
      grapes of Turkish origin (known as Thompson Seedless in the United States), but the word is now applied to raisins made from either white grapes or red grapes that are bleached to resemble the traditional sultana.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Juice">
       Juice
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grape_Juice.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1334" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Grape_Juice.jpg/150px-Grape_Juice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Grape_Juice.jpg/225px-Grape_Juice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Grape_Juice.jpg/300px-Grape_Juice.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grape_Juice.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Grape juice
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Grape juice is obtained from crushing and blending grapes into a liquid. The juice is often sold in stores or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">
       fermented
      </a>
      and made into
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">
       brandy
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">
       vinegar
      </a>
      . In the
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      industry, grape juice that contains 723% of pulp, skins, stems and seeds is often referred to as "
      <a href="/wiki/Must" title="Must">
       must
      </a>
      ". In North America, the most common grape juice is purple and made from
      <a href="/wiki/Concord_(grape)" title="Concord (grape)">
       Concord grapes
      </a>
      while white grape juice is commonly made from
      <a href="/wiki/Niagara_(grape)" title="Niagara (grape)">
       Niagara grapes
      </a>
      , both of which are varieties of native American grapes, a different species from European wine grapes. In California,
      <a href="/wiki/Sultana_(grape)" title="Sultana (grape)">
       Sultana
      </a>
      (known there as Thompson Seedless) grapes are sometimes diverted from the raisin or table market to produce white juice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="French_paradox">
       French paradox
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/French_paradox" title="French paradox">
       French paradox
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Comparing diets among Western countries, researchers have discovered that although the French tend to eat higher levels of animal fat, the incidence of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">
       heart disease
      </a>
      remains low in France. This phenomenon has been termed the French paradox, and is thought to occur from protective benefits of regularly consuming red wine. Apart from potential benefits of alcohol itself, including reduced
      <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">
       platelet
      </a>
      aggregation and
      <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">
       vasodilation
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">
       polyphenols
      </a>
      (e.g.,
      <a href="/wiki/Resveratrol" title="Resveratrol">
       resveratrol
      </a>
      ) mainly in the grape skin provide other suspected health benefits, such as:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Alteration of molecular mechanisms in blood vessels, reducing susceptibility to vascular damage
      </li>
      <li>
       Decreased activity of
       <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin" title="Angiotensin">
        angiotensin
       </a>
       , a systemic hormone causing blood vessel constriction that would elevate blood pressure
      </li>
      <li>
       Increased production of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilator" title="Vasodilator">
        vasodilator
       </a>
       hormone,
       <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">
        nitric oxide
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Endothelium-derived_relaxing_factor" title="Endothelium-derived relaxing factor">
        endothelium-derived relaxing factor
       </a>
       )
      </li>
     </ul>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Armenian_dolma.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Armenian_dolma.jpeg/200px-Armenian_dolma.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Armenian_dolma.jpeg/300px-Armenian_dolma.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Armenian_dolma.jpeg/400px-Armenian_dolma.jpeg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Armenian_dolma.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Using grape leaves in cuisine (
        <a href="/wiki/Dolma" title="Dolma">
         Dolma
        </a>
        )
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although adoption of wine consumption is not recommended by some health authorities,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a significant volume of research indicates moderate consumption, such as one glass of red wine a day for women and two for men, may confer health benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Emerging evidence is that wine
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">
       polyphenols
      </a>
      like resveratrol
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      provide physiological benefit whereas alcohol itself may have protective effects on the cardiovascular system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      More may be seen in the article the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_alcohol" title="Long-term effects of alcohol">
       Long-term effects of alcohol
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Resveratrol">
       Resveratrol
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Resveratrol" title="Resveratrol">
       Resveratrol
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Resveratrol is found in widely varying amounts among grape varieties, primarily in their skins and seeds, which, in
      <a href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">
       muscadine
      </a>
      grapes, have about one hundred times higher concentration than pulp.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guhqhr_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-guhqhr-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fresh grape skin contains about 50 to 100 micrograms of resveratrol per gram.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anthocyanins_and_other_phenolics">
       Anthocyanins and other phenolics
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg">
        <img alt="Anatomical-style diagram of three grapes on their stalks. Two of the grapes are shown in cross-section with all their internal parts labeled." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="585" data-file-width="678" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg/300px-Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg/450px-Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg/600px-Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Grape cross-section
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Anthocyanins tend to be the main
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">
       polyphenolics
      </a>
      in purple grapes whereas
      <a href="/wiki/Flavan-3-ol" title="Flavan-3-ol">
       flavan-3-ols
      </a>
      (i.e. catechins) are the more abundant phenolic in white varieties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hozczp_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hozczp-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Total phenolic content, a laboratory index of antioxidant strength, is higher in purple varieties due almost entirely to anthocyanin density in purple grape skin compared to absence of anthocyanins in white grape skin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hozczp_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hozczp-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is these anthocyanins that are attracting the efforts of scientists to define their properties for human health.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jafcsymp_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jafcsymp-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Phenolic content of grape skin varies with
      <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">
       cultivar
      </a>
      , soil composition, climate, geographic origin, and cultivation practices or exposure to diseases, such as fungal infections.
     </p>
     <p>
      Red
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      may offer health benefits more so than white because potentially beneficial compounds are present in grape skin, and only red wine is fermented with skins. The amount of fermentation time a wine spends in contact with grape skins is an important determinant of its resveratrol content.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ordinary non-
      <a href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">
       muscadine
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Red_wine" title="Red wine">
       red wine
      </a>
      contains between 0.2 and 5.8mg/L,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      depending on the grape variety, because it is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">
       fermented
      </a>
      with the skins, allowing the wine to absorb the resveratrol. By contrast, a
      <a href="/wiki/White_wine" title="White wine">
       white wine
      </a>
      contains lower phenolic contents because it is fermented after removal of skins.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wines produced from
      <a href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">
       muscadine
      </a>
      grapes may contain more than 40mg/L, an exceptional phenolic content.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guhqhr_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-guhqhr-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In muscadine skins,
      <a href="/wiki/Ellagic_acid" title="Ellagic acid">
       ellagic acid
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Myricetin" title="Myricetin">
       myricetin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Quercetin" title="Quercetin">
       quercetin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kaempferol" title="Kaempferol">
       kaempferol
      </a>
      , and trans-resveratrol are major phenolics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Contrary to previous results, ellagic acid and not resveratrol is the major phenolic in muscadine grapes.
     </p>
     <p>
      The flavonols
      <a href="/wiki/Syringetin" title="Syringetin">
       syringetin
      </a>
      , syringetin 3-O-galactoside,
      <a href="/wiki/Laricitrin" title="Laricitrin">
       laricitrin
      </a>
      and laricitrin 3-O-galactoside are also found in purple grape but absent in white grape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattivi_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mattivi-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grape_and_raisin_toxicity_in_dogs">

      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Grape_and_raisin_toxicity_in_dogs" title="Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs">
       Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The consumption of grapes and raisins presents a potential health threat to dogs. Their toxicity to dogs can cause the animal to develop
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acute_renal_failure" title="Acute renal failure">
       acute renal failure
      </a>
      (the sudden development of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_failure" title="Kidney failure">
       kidney failure
      </a>
      ) with
      <a href="/wiki/Anuria" title="Anuria">
       anuria
      </a>
      (a lack of urine production) and may be fatal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grape_therapy">
       Grape therapy
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Grape_therapy" title="Grape therapy">
       Grape therapy
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Grape therapy, also known as ampelotherapy (from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
       Ancient Greek
      </a>
      
      <i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Ampelos" title="Ampelos">
        ampelos
       </a>
       )
      </i>
      , meaning "
      <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">
       vine
      </a>
      "), is a form of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturopathic" title="Naturopathic">
       naturopathic medicine
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">
       alternative medicine
      </a>
      that involves heavy consumption of grapes, including seeds, and parts of the vine, including leaves. Although there is some limited evidence of positive benefits from the consumption of grapes for health purposes, extreme claims, such as its ability to cure cancer, have been widely derided as 
      <a href="/wiki/Quackery" title="Quackery">
       quackery
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)">
         <span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (April 2015)">
          medical citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_significance">
       Religious significance
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SSACRAM_101.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1607" data-file-width="1434" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SSACRAM_101.JPG/220px-SSACRAM_101.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SSACRAM_101.JPG/330px-SSACRAM_101.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SSACRAM_101.JPG/440px-SSACRAM_101.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SSACRAM_101.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Grapes embroidered on a 17th-century
        <a href="/wiki/Chasuble" title="Chasuble">
         chasuble
        </a>
        , Antwerp
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Wine#Religious_uses" title="Wine">
       Wine  Religious_uses
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      , grapes are first mentioned when
      <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">
       Noah
      </a>
      grows them on his farm (
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Genesis&amp;verse=9:2021&amp;src=!">
       Genesis 9:2021
      </a>
      ). Instructions concerning wine are given in the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Proverbs" title="Book of Proverbs">
       book of Proverbs
      </a>
      and in the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">
       book of Isaiah
      </a>
      , such as in
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Proverbs&amp;verse=20:1&amp;src=!">
       Proverbs 20:1
      </a>
      and
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Isaiah&amp;verse=5:2025&amp;src=!">
       Isaiah 5:2025
      </a>
      .
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Deuteronomy&amp;verse=18:35,14:2227,16:1315&amp;src=!">
       Deuteronomy 18:35,14:2227,16:1315
      </a>
      tell of the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      during
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">
       Jewish
      </a>
      feasts. Grapes were also significant to both the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       Greeks
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
       Romans
      </a>
      , and their god of
      <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
       agriculture
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">
       Dionysus
      </a>
      , was linked to grapes and
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      , being frequently portrayed with grape leaves on his head.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Grapes are especially significant for
      <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
       Christians
      </a>
      , who since the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Church" title="Early Church">
       Early Church
      </a>
      have used wine in their celebration of the
      <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">
       Eucharist
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Views on the significance of the wine vary between
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">
       denominations
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_art" title="Christian art">
       Christian art
      </a>
      , grapes often represent the
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_of_Christ" title="Blood of Christ">
       blood of Christ
      </a>
      , such as the grape leaves in
      <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist_(Caravaggio)#John_the_Baptist.2C_Toledo" title="John the Baptist (Caravaggio)">
       Caravaggios
       <i>
        John the Baptist
       </i>
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_religion">
       Use in religion
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_alcohol" title="Christian views on alcohol">
       Christian views on alcohol
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Grape juice, because of its non-alcoholic content, is commonly used by those Christians who oppose the partaking of alcoholic beverages, as the "cup" or "wine" in the
      <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">
       Lord's Supper
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UMC_-_Grape_Juice_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UMC_-_Grape_Juice-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
       Catholic Church
      </a>
      uses wine in the celebration of the Eucharist because it is part of the tradition passed down through the ages starting with Jesus Christ at the Last Supper, where Catholics believe the consecrated bread and wine
      <i>
       literally
      </i>
      become the body and blood of Jesus Christ, a dogma known as
      <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">
       transubstantiation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Wine is used (not grape juice) both due to its strong Scriptural roots, and also to follow the tradition set by the early Christian Church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Code of Canon Law of the Catholic Church (1983), Canon 924 says that the wine used must be natural, made from grapes of the vine, and not corrupt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some circumstances, a priest may obtain special permission to use grape juice for the consecration, however this is extremely rare and typically requires sufficient impetus to warrant such a dispensation, such as personal health of the priest.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although alcohol is permitted in
      <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">
       Judaism
      </a>
      , grape juice is sometimes used as an alternative for
      <a href="/wiki/Kiddush" title="Kiddush">
       kiddush
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">
       Shabbat
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_holiday" title="Jewish holiday">
       Jewish holidays
      </a>
      and it has the same blessing as wine. Many authorities maintain that grape juice must be capable of turning into wine naturally in order to be used for kiddush. Common practice, however, is to use any
      <a href="/wiki/Kashrut" title="Kashrut">
       kosher
      </a>
      grape juice for kiddush.
     </p>
