     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Molon labe
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        
      </span>
      ), meaning "come and take them", is a classical expression of defiance. When the
      <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
       Persian
      </a>
      armies demanded that the Greeks surrender their weapons at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thermopylae" title="Battle of Thermopylae">
       Battle of Thermopylae
      </a>
      , King
      <a href="/wiki/Leonidas_I" title="Leonidas I">
       Leonidas I
      </a>
      responded with this phrase. It is an exemplary use of a
      <a href="/wiki/Laconic_phrase" title="Laconic phrase">
       laconic phrase
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
       Grammar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molon_labe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Grammar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      When properly transliterated with diacritics, the spelling becomes
      <i>
       moln lab.
      </i>
      The modern Greek pronunciation is somewhat different from the ancient Greek:
      <small>
       Ancient Greek:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">
        [moln lab]
       </a>
      </span>
      ;
      <small>
       Modern Greek:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">
        [molon lave]
       </a>
      </span>
      . The literal translation is
      <i>
       having come, take
      </i>
      . Whereas the word "them" is required in English to be grammatical, it is not in the original Greek.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first word,
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        moln
       </span>
      </i>
      , is the
      <a href="/wiki/Aorist" title="Aorist">
       aorist
      </a>
      active
      <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
       participle
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
       masculine
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominative" title="Nominative">
       nominative
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
       singular
      </a>
      ) of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_verbs" title="Ancient Greek verbs">
       Greek verb
      </a>
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        blsk
       </span>
      </i>
      "to come", meaning "having come".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The root is evidently , so that -- is apparently a contraction for --
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (the suffix -, being a common specialized present stem inchoative suffix).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The form
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        labe
       </span>
      </i>
      is the aorist active imperative (second person singular) of the verb
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BB%CE%B1%CE%BC%CE%B2%CE%AC%CE%BD%CF%89" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      <i>
       <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        lamban
       </span>
      </i>
      , translated as "you take" with an emphasis since it is in the
      <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
       imperative
      </a>
      form. That is to say: it is better represented in English as "Take!" (with
      <i>
       you singular
      </i>
      understood, "you take").
     </p>
     <p>
      The two words function together in a grammatical structure (not as common in English as in Greek) called the circumstantial participle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Where English would put two main verbs in two
      <a href="/wiki/Independent_clause" title="Independent clause">
       independent clauses
      </a>
      joined by a
      <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(grammar)" title="Conjunction (grammar)">
       conjunction
      </a>
      : "come and take", a strategy sometimes called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paratactic" title="Paratactic">
       paratactic
      </a>
      , Ancient Greek, which is far richer in participles,
      <a href="/wiki/Subordination_(linguistics)" title="Subordination (linguistics)">
       subordinates
      </a>
      one to the other, a strategy called
      <a href="/wiki/Hypotaxis" title="Hypotaxis">
       hypotactic
      </a>
      : "coming, take". The first action is expressed with a participle with
      <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
       adverbial
      </a>
      force. In this structure, the participle gives some circumstance (the coming) attendant on the main verb (the taking).
     </p>
     <p>
      The aorist participle may be used where the action is completed, called the
      <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">
       perfective aspect
      </a>
      . That is: the action of the participle occurs before that of the main verb. Thus the Greek provides a nuance similar to the English translation (come and [then] take it), making clear that the coming must precede the taking (i.e. "having come, take"). The latter
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      is in second person singular, and therefore is not being spoken to a large group of people, but rather to an individual. King
      <a href="/wiki/Leonidas_I" title="Leonidas I">
       Leonidas I
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
       Sparta
      </a>
      spoke directly to
      <a href="/wiki/Xerxes_I" title="Xerxes I">
       Xerxes I
      </a>
      personally, and not to the Persians en masse.
     </p>
     <p>
      While English normally requires an explicit object in a transitive imperative construction ("Take them!" or "take it!"), Ancient Greek does not; the object
      <i>
       them
      </i>
      is understood from context.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molon_labe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The phrase was reportedly the defiant response of King
      <a href="/wiki/Leonidas_I" title="Leonidas I">
       Leonidas I
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
       Sparta
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Xerxes_I" title="Xerxes I">
       Xerxes I
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
       Persia
      </a>
      when Xerxes demanded that the Greeks lay down their arms and surrender. This was at the onset of the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thermopylae" title="Battle of Thermopylae">
       Battle of Thermopylae
      </a>
      (480 BC). Instead, the Greeks held Thermopylae for three days. Although the Greek contingent was defeated, they inflicted serious damage on the Persian army. Most importantly, this delayed the Persians' progress to Athens, providing sufficient time for the city's evacuation to the island of
      <a href="/wiki/Salamis_Island" title="Salamis Island">
       Salamis
      </a>
      . Though a tactical defeat, Thermopylae served as a strategic and
      <a href="/wiki/Moral_victory" title="Moral victory">
       moral victory
      </a>
      , inspiring the Greek forces to crush the Persians at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis" title="Battle of Salamis">
       Battle of Salamis
      </a>
      later the same year and the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plataea" title="Battle of Plataea">
       Battle of Plataea
      </a>
      one year later.
     </p>
     <p>
      The source for this quotation is
      <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
       Plutarch
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Apophthegmata Laconica,
      </i>
      51.11.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This work by Plutarch is included among the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Moralia" title="Moralia">
        Moralia
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      a collection of works attributed to him but outside the collection of his most famous works, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Parallel_Lives" title="Parallel Lives">
        Parallel Lives
       </a>
       .
      </i>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">
       Modern usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molon_labe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gonzales_Flag.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1980" data-file-width="3019" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gonzales_Flag.JPG/220px-Gonzales_Flag.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gonzales_Flag.JPG/330px-Gonzales_Flag.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Gonzales_Flag.JPG/440px-Gonzales_Flag.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gonzales_Flag.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Replica of the
        <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gonzales" title="Battle of Gonzales">
         Gonzalez
        </a>
        Flag at the Texas State Capitol
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Molon labe
      </i>
      has been repeated by many later generals and politicians in order to express an army's or nation's determination not to surrender. The motto
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        
      </span>
      is on the emblem of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_First_Army_Corps" title="Greek First Army Corps">
       Greek First Army Corps
      </a>
      , and is also the motto of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA" title="USA">
       United States
      </a>
      Special Operations Command Central (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SOCCENT" title="SOCCENT">
       SOCCENT
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The expression "
      <a href="/wiki/Come_and_take_it" title="Come and take it">
       Come and take it
      </a>
      " was a slogan in the
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_Revolution" title="Texas Revolution">
       Texas Revolution
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Molon labe
      </i>
      has been used once again in Greek history, on 3 March 1957 during a battle in Cyprus between members of the
      <a href="/wiki/EOKA" title="EOKA">
       EOKA
      </a>
      organization and the British Army. After someone had betrayed his location, the British forces surrounded the secret hideout of the second-in-command of EOKA,
      <a href="/wiki/Grigoris_Afxentiou" title="Grigoris Afxentiou">
       Grigoris Afxentiou
      </a>
      , near the
      <a href="/wiki/Machairas_Monastery" title="Machairas Monastery">
       Machairas Monastery
      </a>
      . Inside the hideout were Afxentiou and four of his followers. Realizing he was outnumbered, Afxentiou ordered them to surrender themselves whilst he barricaded himself for a fight to the death. The British asked Afxentiou to come out and surrender. He replied with the phrase
      <i>
       Molon labe
      </i>
      , imitating the ancient Spartans. Unable to get him out, and after sustaining casualties, the British set fire to the hideout, and he was burnt alive. The British buried his body in the yard of the central jail of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lefkosia" title="Lefkosia">
       Lefkosia
      </a>
      , where it lies today.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">
       United States of America
      </a>
      , both the original
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      phrase and its
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      translation are often heard from pro Second Amendment activists as a defense of the
      <a href="/wiki/Right_to_keep_and_bear_arms" title="Right to keep and bear arms">
       right to keep and bear arms
      </a>
      . It began to appear on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WWW" title="WWW">
       web
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_site" title="Web site">
       sites
      </a>
      in the late 1990s and early 2000s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the Second Amendment or firearms freedom context, the phrase expresses the notion that the person uttering the phrase is a strong believer in these ideals and will not surrender their firearms to anyone, especially to governmental authority.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Challenge_coin" title="Challenge coin">
       Challenge coins
      </a>
      similar to those used by military service members have been created with the Molon Labe text and firearm images.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">
       college football
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_Spartans_football" title="Michigan State Spartans football">
       Michigan State Spartans football
      </a>
      team wore alternate jerseys featuring the phrase in their 2011
      <a href="/wiki/Michigan%E2%80%93Michigan_State_football_rivalry" title="MichiganMichigan State football rivalry">
       rivalry
      </a>
      game with the
      <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Wolverines_football" title="Michigan Wolverines football">
       Michigan Wolverines
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
