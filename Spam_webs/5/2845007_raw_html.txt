                 <h2>
                  <a href="http://www.faithtrustinstitute.org/blog/marie-fortune/223">
                   How Many? How Long?
                  </a>
                 </h2>
                 <p class="metadata">
                  <span>
                   July 08, 2015
                  </span>
                  <span class="category-list">
                   
                           Categories:
                   <span>
                    <a class="link-category" href="http://www.faithtrustinstitute.org/search?Subject%3Alist=Current Events" rel="tag">
                     Current Events

                   <span>

                   <span>

                    </a>
                   </span>
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  This might sound like the start of a bad joke: What do a theologian (John Howard Yoder) and a comedian (Bill Cosby) have in common?  More than you might imagineand Im not laughing.

Both were major figures in their fields. Both were widely regarded and respected, even adored by many.  Both were powerful men with a sense of entitlement. With impunity, both sexually abused scores of young women who trusted them for years.  Both were shielded by their peers and colleagues from any meaningful accountability.
                 </p>
                 <p>

                  <a href="http://www.faithtrustinstitute.org/blog/220">
                   GUEST BLOG: Ramadan Reflections: Recharging for Social Justice Work by Afeefa Syeed

                 <p class="metadata">
                  <span>
                   June 15, 2015
                  </span>
                  <span class="category-list">
                   
                           Categories:

                 <p>
                  I am an aspiring potter and love to be covered in clay. Im excited about the process of what clay does even more than what products my playing with mud might yield. More than anything else, being a potter gives me head and heart space to reflect on humility, patience, submission and being passionately hopeful that whatever comes out of the process is just what I needed to learn, experience, and live to be who I am. Sometimes theres a pretty pot I can pour tea out of.

In many ways this is how Ramadan is for me as an activist.  Essentially, fasting is denying yourself something, but it really becomes more about how that denial takes place, what process makes it be more than just not eating or drinking. Principles from Ramadan are critical then in how they reinforce my work as an advocate...
