     <p>
      <b>
       Elblg Canal
      </b>
      (
      <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap">
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:IPA_for_Polish" title="Wikipedia:IPA for Polish">
        [lblk]
       </a>
      </span>
      <small class="nowrap metadata">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/File:Pl-Elbl%C4%85g-2.ogg" title="File:Pl-Elblg-2.ogg">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/>
       </a>
       <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c1/Pl-Elbl%C4%85g-2.ogg" title="Pl-Elblg-2.ogg">
        listen
       </a>
       )
      </small>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
       Polish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">
       <i>
        Kana Elblski
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
       <i>
        Oberlndischer Kanal
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">
       canal
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Warmian-Masurian_Voivodeship" title="Warmian-Masurian Voivodeship">
       Warmian-Masurian Voivodeship
      </a>
      , 80.5km in length, which runs southward from
      <a href="/wiki/Dru%C5%BCno" title="Druno">
       Lake Druno
      </a>
      (connected by the river
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elbl%C4%85g_River" title="Elblg River">
       Elblg
      </a>
      to the
      <a href="/wiki/Vistula_Lagoon" title="Vistula Lagoon">
       Vistula Lagoon
      </a>
      ), to the river
      <a href="/wiki/Drw%C4%99ca" title="Drwca">
       Drwca
      </a>
      and lake
      <a href="/wiki/Jeziorak" title="Jeziorak">
       Jeziorak
      </a>
      . It can accommodate small vessels up to 50 tons displacement. The difference in water levels approaches 100 m, and is overcome using locks and a remarkable system of tracks between lakes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Today it is used mainly for recreational purposes. It is considered one of the most significant monuments related to the history of technology and was listed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unesco" title="Unesco">
       Unesco
      </a>
      as a memorial to world culture inheritances. In Poland it has recently been named one of
      <a href="/wiki/Seven_Wonders_of_Poland" title="Seven Wonders of Poland">
       Seven Wonders of Poland
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The canal was also named one of Poland's official national
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Historical_Monuments_(Poland)" title="List of Historical Monuments (Poland)">
       Historic Monuments
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pomnik_historii" title="Pomnik historii">
        Pomnik historii
       </a>
      </i>
      ), as designated January 28, 2011. Its listing is maintained by the
      <a href="/wiki/Narodowy_Instytut_Dziedzictwa" title="Narodowy Instytut Dziedzictwa">
       National Heritage Board of Poland
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elbl%C4%85g_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The canal was designed in 182544 by
      <a href="/wiki/Georg_Steenke" title="Georg Steenke">
       Georg Steenke
      </a>
      , carrying out the commission given by the King of
      <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">
       Prussia
      </a>
      . Construction began in 1844. The difference in height over a 9.5 kilometres or 5.9 miles section of the route between the lakes was too great for building traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" title="Lock (water transport)">
       locks
      </a>
      ; an ingenious system of
      <a href="/wiki/Canal_inclined_plane" title="Canal inclined plane">
       inclined planes
      </a>
      based on those used on the
      <a href="/wiki/Morris_Canal" title="Morris Canal">
       Morris Canal
      </a>
      was employed instead, though the canal includes a few locks as well. There were originally four inclined planes with a fifth added later, replacing five wooden locks. Built under the name
      <i>
       Oberlndischer Kanal
      </i>
      (Upper land Canal) and situated in the
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">
       Kingdom of Prussia
      </a>
      , it was opened on the 29 October 1860.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uhlemann_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Uhlemann-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since 1945 the canal is now in
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      . After
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
       wartime
      </a>
      damage was repaired, it was restored to operation in 1948 and is now used for tourism.
     </p>
     <p>
      Would-be Tourists might take note, in order to not be disappointed, the canal tours do not re-open after the winter season until May 1. [Note: As of summer 2014 the canal is closed for renovation work that is due to be completed in 2015.]
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Inclined_Planes">
       The Inclined Planes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h2>

       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

     <p>
      The four original inclined planes are in order from the summit level downwards, Buczyniec (Buchwalde) with a rise of 20.4m and a length of 224.8m, Katy (Kanten) with a rise of 18.83m and a length of 225.97m, Olesnica (Schnfeld) with a rise of 21.97m and a length of 262.63m and Jelenie (Hirschfeld) with a rise of 21.97m and a length of 263.63m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShipliftsPianc1989_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ShipliftsPianc1989-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fifth incline was Calony Nowe (Neu-Kussfeld) with a rise of 13.72m, it was built to replace five wooden locks close to Elblg.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uhlemann_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Uhlemann-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tew_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tew-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were constructed from 1860 to 1880.
     </p>
     <p>
      The canal worked independently of other waterways and as a result the boats were designed within the limits of the inclines. The boats had a maximum length of 24.48m, a maximum width of 2.98m and a maximum draught of 1.1m, they carried loads of about 50 tonnes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uhlemann_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Uhlemann-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The inclines all consist of two parallel rail tracks with a gauge of 3.27m. Boats are carried on carriages which run on these rails. The inclines rise from the lower level of the canal to a summit and then down a second shorter incline to the upper canal level. The first part of the main incline and the short upper incline were both built at a gradient of 1:24. A carriage is lowered down the incline to counterbalance an upward moving carriage. Once the downward moving carriage has reached the summit and started down the main incline its weight helps pull up the upward moving carriage. This allowed the slope of the incline for this section to be built at a higher gradient of 1:12.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uhlemann_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Uhlemann-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
