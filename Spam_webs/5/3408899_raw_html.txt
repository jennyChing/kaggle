           <div class="field-item even">
            <p>
             <img alt="All-American Muslim" height="200" src="/files/images/AllAmericanMuslim.jpg" style="margin: 5px; float: left;" title="All-American Muslim" width="160"/>
             The travel website Kayak.com and Lowes Hardware stores pulled advertising from TLC's reality show All-American Muslim after the right-wing
             <a href="http://www.sourcewatch.org/index.php/Florida_Family_Association" target="_blank">
              Florida Family Association
             </a>
             (FFA) complained that the show presented Muslim-American families as ordinary people. FFA, disturbed by the normative portrayal of American Muslims, complained
             <a href="http://floridafamily.org/full_article.php?article_no=108" target="_blank">
              on its website
             </a>
             that "The point of the show is to depict Muslims as ordinary folks just like you and me who are subjected to unjust suspicion," and saying the show is "propaganda clearly designed to counter legitimate and present-day concerns about many Muslims who are advancing Islamic fundamentalism and Sharia law." FFA maintained that the show was an attempt "to manipulate Americans into ignoring the threat of jihad." The group started pressuring advertisers to drop their sponsorship of the show. The CEO of Kayak.com, which dropped its ads, claimed that no third party had influenced their decision to discontinue advertising on the show. A Kayak.com executive
             <a href="http://latimesblogs.latimes.com/technology/2011/12/all-american-muslim-kayak-calls-show-terrible-but-apologizes-to-customers.html" target="_blank">
              claimed TLC had misled them
             </a>
             about the nature of the show, and added, "...Mostly, I just thought the show sucked." Lowes responded to FFA saying the show did not "meet Lowe's advertising guidelines." The chain issued a more ambiguous public statement saying the company had a "long-standing commitment to diversity and inclusion," and saying the show had turned into "a lightning rod for people to voice complaints from a variety of perspectives - political, social and otherwise."
            </p>
            <p>
             After Lowes made it public that it had pulled its ads,
             <a href="http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/lowes-all-american-muslim-TLC-272316" target="_blank">
              boycotts
             </a>
             have been called,
             <a href="http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/lowes-boycott-tlc-all-american-muslim-274366" target="_blank">
              protests
             </a>
             planned, 200,000 petitions have been delivered by local religious leaders, a spoof Lowes TV ad has gone
             <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qQhls5PEmeQ&amp;feature=player_embedded" target="_blank">
              viral
             </a>
             and hip-hop mogul Russell Simmons
             <a href="http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/live-feed/lowes-boycott-russell-simmons-mia-farrow-272479" target="_blank">
              offered to buy
             </a>
             the remaining advertising on the show.
            </p>

             A year-old, anti-Muslim email has resurfaced and is curculating once again, riding the latest wave of U.S. anti-Muslim bigotry. The email urges people to boycott a U.S. postage stamp  that recognizes the Islamic holiday of Eid. The stamp, which rumor-mongers mistakenly refer to as a
             <a href="http://www.truthorfiction.com/rumors/e/eid.htm" target="_blank">
              "Muslim Christmas Stamp,"
             </a>
             was first issued about ten years ago, and is one of
             <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20031216042934/http://www.usps.com/holiday/stamps.htm" target="_blank">
              six seasonal postage stamps
             </a>
             the United States Postal Service sells that commemorate Hanukkah, Christmas, Kwanzaa, Eid, snowmen and music makers. The email was
             <a href="http://www.facebook.com/note.php?note_id=156900540319" target="_blank">
              first sent around
             </a>
             a year ago by John Piper, the mayor of Clarksville, Tennessee.  In it, Piper urged "all patriotic Americans" to protest a U.S. postage stamp commemorating the Islamic holiday of Eid. Piper accused "Muslim Communist"
             <a href="http://www.sourcewatch.org/index.php/Barack_Obama" target="_blank">
              President Obama
             </a>
             of ordering the stamp's creation. The stamp was, in fact, created during the
             <a href="http://www.sourcewatch.org/index.php/George_W._Bush_administration" target="_blank">
              George W. Bush administration
             </a>
             . A similar stamp, sort of an
             <a href="http://www.snopes.com/politics/stamps/eidstamp.asp" target="_blank">
              Arabic Valentine's Day stamp
             </a>
             with hearts and a butterfly on it, is also mentioned in the email. That stamp was created on a Web site called Zazzle, that lets people create their own custom stamps, and is not issued by the USPS. Snopes.com recently updated its
             <a href="http://www.snopes.com/politics/stamps/eidstamp.asp" target="_blank">
              page
             </a>
             debunking this rumor. The USPS's holiday stamps are available
             <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20031216042934/http://www.usps.com/holiday/stamps.htm" target="_blank">
              here
             </a>
             .
            </p>
