   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    The Moon is made of green cheese
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       "The Moon is made of green cheese"
      </b>
      is a statement referring to a fanciful belief that the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      is composed of
      <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">
       cheese
      </a>
      . In its original formulation as a
      <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">
       proverb
      </a>
      and metaphor for credulity with roots in fable, this refers to the perception of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
       simpleton
      </a>
      who sees a reflection of the Moon in water and mistakes it for a round cheese wheel. It is widespread as a
      <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(folkloristics)" title="Morphology (folkloristics)">
       folkloric motif
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Meme" title="Meme">
       meme
      </a>
      among many of the world's cultures, and the notion has also found its way into both children's
      <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
       folklore
      </a>
      and modern popular culture.
     </p>
     <p>
      The phrase "
      <a href="/wiki/Green_cheese" title="Green cheese">
       green cheese
      </a>
      " in this proverb simply refers to a young cheese (sometimes "
      <a href="/wiki/Cream_cheese" title="Cream cheese">
       cream cheese
      </a>
      " is used), though modern people may interpret the color reference literally.
     </p>
     <p>
      There was never an actual historical popular belief that the Moon is made of green cheese (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cf." title="Cf.">
        cf.
       </a>
      </i>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_of_the_Flat_Earth" title="Myth of the Flat Earth">
       myth of the Flat Earth
      </a>
      ). It was typically used as an example of extreme
      <a href="/wiki/Credulity" title="Credulity">
       credulity
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The concept is so ubiquitous that it displays characteristics of
      <a href="/wiki/Meme" title="Meme">
       meme
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">
       trope
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Actual_historical_beliefs">
       Actual historical beliefs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Actual historical beliefs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In ancient times the Moon, like the rest of the celestial realm above the
      <a href="/wiki/Sublunary_sphere" title="Sublunary sphere">
       sublunary sphere
      </a>
      , was considered to be made of an otherworldly and perfect "fifth element" or
      <a href="/wiki/Aether_(classical_element)" title="Aether (classical element)">
       quintessence
      </a>
      as identified by
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotelian_physics" title="Aristotelian physics">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/On_the_Heavens" title="On the Heavens">
        On the Heavens
       </a>
      </i>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Copernican_Revolution" title="Copernican Revolution">
       Copernican Revolution
      </a>
      in time overturned this view, as
      <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
       Galileo Galilei
      </a>
      's 1610 publication
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius" title="Sidereus Nuncius">
        Sidereus Nuncius
       </a>
      </i>
      heralded his telescopic discovery of an
      <a href="/wiki/Selenography" title="Selenography">
       irregular lunar surface
      </a>
      , suggesting a
      <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_pluralism" title="Cosmic pluralism">
       plurality of worlds
      </a>
      with the Earth and Moon (and other planetary bodies) sharing the same basic physical nature. In the Space Age, lunar missions for the first time allowed direct examination of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_rocks" title="Moon rocks">
       Moon rocks
      </a>
      , suggesting the
      <a href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
       giant impact hypothesis
      </a>
      , but the overall proportional composition of lunar geology is still debated, with different implications for the Moon's origin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fable">
       Fable
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fable">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There exists a family of stories in
      <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">
       comparative mythology
      </a>
      in diverse countries that concern a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
       simpleton
      </a>
      who sees a reflection of the Moon and mistakes it for a round cheese:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       ... the
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_mythology" title="Slavic mythology">
        Servian tale
       </a>
       where the fox leads the wolf to believe the moon reflection in the water is a cheese and the wolf bursts in the attempt to drink up the water to get at the cheese; the
       <a href="/wiki/Zulu_mythology" title="Zulu mythology">
        Zulu tale
       </a>
       of the hyena that drops the bone to go after the moon reflection in the water; the
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_literature" title="Occitan literature">
        Gascon tale
       </a>
       of the peasant watering his
       <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">
        ass
       </a>
       on a moonlight night. A cloud obscures the moon, and the peasant, thinking the ass has drunk the moon, kills the beast to recover the moon; the
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_folklore" title="Turkish folklore">
        Turkish tale
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Nasreddin" title="Nasreddin">
        Khoja Nasru-'d-Din
       </a>
       who thinks the moon has fallen into the well and gets a rope and chain with which to pull it out. In his efforts the rope breaks, and he falls back, but seeing the moon in the sky, praises Allah that the moon is safe; the
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_mythology" title="Scottish mythology">
        Scotch tale
       </a>
       of the wolf fishing with his tail for the moon reflection;
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       G. H. McKnight
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKnight-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      This
      <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(folkloristics)" title="Morphology (folkloristics)">
       folkloric motif
      </a>
      is first recorded in literature during the
      <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
       High Middle Ages
      </a>
      with the French rabbi
      <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">
       Rashi
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hebrew_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hebrew-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who attributes it to the
      <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">
       Talmudic
      </a>
      era
      <a href="/wiki/Rabbi_Meir" title="Rabbi Meir">
       Rabbi Meir
      </a>
      ; this may reflect the well-known
      <a href="/wiki/Beast_fable" title="Beast fable">
       beast fable
      </a>
      tradition of
      <a href="/wiki/French_folklore" title="French folklore">
       French folklore
      </a>
      or an obscure such tradition in
      <a href="/wiki/Jewish_mythology" title="Jewish mythology">
       Jewish folklore
      </a>
      ; Rashi's version already includes the fox, the wolf, the
      <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">
       well
      </a>
      and the Moon that are seen in later versions. The Iraqi rabbi
      <a href="/wiki/Hai_Gaon" title="Hai Gaon">
       Hai Gaon
      </a>
      also attributed a tale sharing elements of Rashi's story to Rabbi Meir.
      <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Alphonsi" title="Petrus Alphonsi">
       Petrus Alphonsi
      </a>
      , a Spanish Jewish convert to Christianity, popularized this tale in Europe in his collection
      <i>
       Disciplina Clericalis
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKnight-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the facets of this morphology is grouped as "The Wolf Dives into the Water for Reflected Cheese" (Type 34) of the
      <a href="/wiki/Aarne%E2%80%93Thompson_classification_system" title="AarneThompson classification system">
       AarneThompson classification
      </a>
      of folktales, in the section devoted to tales of
      <a href="/wiki/Foxes_in_popular_culture" title="Foxes in popular culture">
       The Clever Fox
      </a>
      . It can also be grouped as "The Moon in the Well" (Type 1335A), in the section devoted to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
       Stories about a Fool
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A variation featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Reynard" title="Reynard">
       Reynard
      </a>
      the Fox appeared soon after Petrus Alphonsi in the French classic
      <i>
       Le Roman de Renart
      </i>
      (as "Renart et Ysengrin dans le puits" in Branch IV); the Moon/cheese element is absent, but such a version is alluded to in another part of the collection. This was the first Reynard tale to be adapted into English (as "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Wolf_and_the_Fox" title="The Wolf and the Fox">
       The Fox and the Wolf
      </a>
      "), preceding Chaucer's "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Nun%27s_Priest%27s_Tale" title="The Nun's Priest's Tale">
       The Nun's Priest's Tale
      </a>
      " and the much later work of
      <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">
       William Caxton
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKnight-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Proverb">
       Proverb
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proverb">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "The Moon is made of green cheese" was one of the most popular proverbs in 16th and 17th century English literature,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and it was also in use after this time. It likely originated in 1546, when
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Heywood" title="John Heywood">
        The Proverbs of John Heywood
       </a>
      </i>
      claimed "the moon is made of a greene cheese."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        A
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A common variation is "to make one believe the Moon is made of green cheese" (
      <i>
       i.e.
      </i>
      , to hoax).
     </p>
     <p>
      In French, there is the proverb "
      <i>
       Il veut prendre la lune avec les dents
      </i>
      " ("He wants to grab the moon in his teeth"), alluded to in
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="Franois Rabelais">
       Rabelais
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The characterization is also common in stories of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
       gothamites
      </a>
      , including the
      <a href="/wiki/Moonrakers" title="Moonrakers">
       Moonrakers
      </a>
      of Wiltshire.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Childlore_and_popular_culture">
       Childlore and popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Childlore and popular culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A 1902 study of
      <a href="/wiki/Childlore" title="Childlore">
       childlore
      </a>
      in the United States found that though most young children were unsure of the Moon's composition, that it was made of cheese was the single most common explanation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Before that time, and since, the idea of the Moon actually being made of cheese has appeared as a humorous conceit in much of children's popular culture, and in adult references to it:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       At the end of the 1967
       <a href="/wiki/Tom_and_Jerry" title="Tom and Jerry">
        Tom and Jerry
       </a>
       short,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/O-Solar_Meow" title="O-Solar Meow">
         O-Solar Meow
        </a>
       </i>
       , Jerry is blasted to the Moon, where he is seen stuffing himself with its large quantities of cheese.
