        <div class="fsb-clear">
        </div>
        <p>
         Brennen Byrne is the founder and CEO of Clef. He is one of a rare few entrepreneurs who found a great deal of success in their first business venture. However, there is more at play in Brennens rapid success than mere luck, he has a mindset that enables him to quickly adapt to challenges and grow from them.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          How Brennen Byrne, founder of Clef, maintains his team, grows his company and faces challenges.
          <a href="http://goo.gl/cBdFqu">
           goo.gl/cBdFqu
          </a>
          @WPCurve 
          <a href="http://ctt.ec/usmd0" target="_blank">
           CLICK TO TWEET
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Brennen-whitboard-web" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-12721" height="300" src="http://wpcurve.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Brennen-whitboard-web1.jpg" width="640"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Source:
         <a href="https://twitter.com/brennenbyrne" target="_blank">
          Twitter  @brennenbyrne
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Origins of Clef
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The original idea for Clef occurred when Brennen was an intern at Adobe; he was working on how to attract people for ads. This was when LinkedIn lost all of their passwords, which (at the time) was one of the biggest password breaches to date. Brennen was working on using phones to identify people and could be used as a better way to keep information secure than the six letter passwords that most humans use and commonly forget. So far, customers dont miss their passwords.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <img alt="" class="alignnone" height="601" src="https://sslimgs.xkcd.com/comics/password_strength.png" width="740"/>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Setting targets
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Brennen believes success is very much driven by what you want to succeed at. So its important to set the right targets.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <em>
           To set targets we start by asking: If everything went perfectly, what would things look like in three months? What do we want to be? What would it take to get there? Things are currently changing so fast for Clef that it is difficult to set realistic targets beyond that time frame
          </em>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Once they have a measurable goal, they work back and figure out what it would take to get there.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Managing unexpected challenges or problems
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Brennen has a natural sense of humility, especially when facing problems. He assumes that the challenge is better than he is, it is not something that he can overcome alone, and he does not have enough information to solve it yet.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           I have spent most of my entrepreneurial career asking people for help. When I find a problem, I think about who I know that has solved this problem before.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           If I have a challenge with hiring, I go ask people who have been working in HR or hiring for years. I started this company out of school and the idea that I was going to be able to figure out hiring on my own is pretty impossible. But if you talk to people who have made a career out of solving the problem you have, theyll have wisdom that you wont find anywhere else. They can tell you in ten minutes what you would have spent years researching
          </em>
          . 
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Brennen has the ability to approach the challenges he faces with a beginners mind and keep open to help and feedback from others where most might try to figure out the problem on their own.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Finding the right people for the team
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Clef-Team-web" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-12722" height="300" src="http://wpcurve.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Clef-Team-web.jpg" width="640"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Source:
         <a href="https://twitter.com/jennyfer_morgan/status/464193781319749633" target="_blank">
          Twitter  @jennyfer_morgan
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         As a small team, it is expensive to interview a lot of people. So we have shifted to a more methodical process where we get very specific at what we are looking for and go directly to communities of people who know the skills we want. They try and define exact skills, levels of proficiency and past experience. At first, they were concerned it would be too specific and would rule out everyone interested, but instead the very clear definitions often would cause the perfect person to appear.
        </p>
        <p>
         Another tactic they use is instead of putting a post online that they are looking for a developer; they go to meet-ups of python developers and ask for recommendations for the skills they are looking for.
        </p>
        <p>
         When Brennen finds someone that is right for the job, he becomes focused on getting that person on his team, even if they are not on the job market. He builds personal connections with his prospective team member and shows an interest in them instead of simply offering a paycheck.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           When we were looking for someone to help out with business development, I knew exactly the person I wanted for the job. I had met Darrell Jones before I started Clef and after the first meeting, I said, Darrell is going to be incredibly successful, and I want to work with him. Unfortunately, for me, Darrell was in Shanghai working on his own education company at the time. I was interviewing other candidates and none of them measured up to Darrell, so I kept looking. Then I heard that Darrell had gotten scarily sick and had come back to the states to recover, I called to check in on his good health and heard that he would make a full recovery but that he wouldnt be able to return to China. We scheduled another time to talk once he was better where I made the pitch for Clef and convinced him to join our team instead. Of course I would never wish an illness like his on anyone, but I have to admit that the timing worked out perfectly and I am so happy to have Darrell on our team.
          </em>
          
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         In the hypercompetitive tech industry, they use openness and direct communication with their employees to make them feel valued and stay committed to the team and the company.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           We make a habit of being very open with our needs with our employees and our desire to keep them around. It sounds obvious, but doing it is counterintuitive.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Simply saying things like, we want you here, and it is important for us to keep you, we want to make sure this environment is working for you, youre learning a lot, youre getting better at what you do, and that you feel valued and are contributing in meaningful ways.
          </em>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         This has created an open atmosphere where his staff feel empowered to come forward to him with ideas or projects. He is always happy to support them.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="clef-cook" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-12725" height="300" src="http://wpcurve.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/clef-cook.jpg" width="640"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Source:
         <a href="https://twitter.com/getclef/status/474414581440196608" target="_blank">
          Twitter  @getclef
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Another important part of their retention has been the culture around their
         <a href="https://getclef.com/values/">
          core values
         </a>
         . Their values are well defined, and everyone is able to comment on them. They use these values to guide them through tough decisions.
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Be better today than yesterday
         </li>
         <li>
          Treat others the way theyd like to be treated
         </li>
         <li>
          Fight the default of exclusion
         </li>
         <li>
          Build trust with truth
         </li>
         <li>
          We succeed together
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         They have been considering adding transparency to this list:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           Weve been thinking about build trust with truth a lot lately as weve added some new security features to the product. Our goal with Clef is to make security simple and easy, but we also have the duty to be really transparent with our users so that they know what is going on and how they can be protecting themselves. Youll be able to dig into a lot of information about how Clef is protecting users in an upcoming update because were building trust with truth.
          </em>
          
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Documenting values, processes, and everything that goes on in the company has been a helpful for their culture. They do a lot of collaborating through Slack and Google Docs, and document decisions to people up to date. This was originally designed to keep remote workers in the loop, but has been for the entire staff.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://twitter.com/jennyfer_morgan/status/464193781319749633" target="_blank">
          <img alt="clef-shirts" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-12726" height="300" src="http://wpcurve.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/clef-shirts.jpg" width="640"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Source:
         <a href="https://twitter.com/getclef" target="_blank">
          Twitter  @getclef
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Going from technical to post-technical
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         At the beginning, all three of Clefs founders were technical. They quickly realized that someone needs to do something other than code for a company to work. There came a point where someone needed to change their responsibilities and Brennen was chosen to handle the non-technical challenges of the business. He switched roles about two years ago, and he has found it to be among the most challenging and rewarding experiences of his career.
        </p>
        <p>
         Brennens humility and willingness to go to others for help and advice was again a key asset in this situation. Where someone with more business background may have needed or thought they needed less advice, Brennen has found it obvious to ask for help in every decision.
        </p>
        <p>
         The transition in general was more natural than expected. The most difficult challenge was the fundraising process. Which was one of his new responsibilities as the non-technical founder. This was an incredibly important decision that he had no experience in. Though he was responsible for these decisions his cofounders took it upon themselves to educate themselves and support him.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <em>
           The main challenge of fundraising is how distracting it can be. For one, it takes a lot of time to have the conversations with investors. More importantly, though, is how much attention it takes emotionally.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Many investors are thoughtful, engaged people who know a lot about our industry  but the nature of their industry means they say no to 99% of companies they talk to. Having people you respect turn down the deal is inevitable, but can be really difficult and distracting. Its important to have the right mindset and expectations as a team so this doesnt keep you from making the product and the company better.
          </em>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         There are many advantages to having a technical background when managing teams as well. Though he does not write code anymore, if his team has challenges or delays in product development, he can understand the problem, contribute to the conversation meaningfully, and make decisions that are more valuable as a result.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Learning to like sports to improve sales and marketing
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Brennens open mindedness and adaptability also extends to his personal identity. He was willing to examine aspects of himself and his upbringing and change those to fit the new challenges of being a non-technical founder and having to work with a more sales and marketing oriented crowd.
        </p>
        <p>
         Brennen was aware of the importance of being able to take advantage of a conversation, and his distaste for sports that he cultivated in his upbringing was actually a problem for him and his business.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           I grew up being a nerd, and never really liked sports. When I started getting into business and hanging around people who werent developers a lot of the conversations starting being around sports, which I saw as a weakness. At first, I thought I could get by with just a few flash cards, but quickly I learned that you cant fake it, you cant just know a few stats and get away with it. So I embraced it, and I actually found it to be enjoyable.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Were in Oakland, so I was partial to the Oakland As. The Raiders are a hard team to root for, but I still do. I also grew up in Alabama where College football is important, so I have deep roots with Alabama football. Thats been a funny and uncanny transition and I have surprised a lot of my friends when I am checking the ESPN app on my phone for the latest scores.
          </em>
          
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Clefs most profitable growth channel
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Speaking-web" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-12723" height="300" src="http://wpcurve.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Speaking-web.jpg" width="640"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Source:
         <a href="%20http://wp-photographers.com" target="_blank">
          WP Photographers
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Like many startups, Clef was built on a shoestring budget, but a channel where they have been able to invest in with great returns has been sending Brennen to conferences, wordcamps, and other meet-ups around the US to speak and network. This has by far been their main growth stream. They always look for events where the attendees are of interest to Clef. It has been very valuable for this business in particular because he is able to reach out to the right audience with authenticity and trust. With a product like Clef that is focused on security, trust and credibility are vital to reach their customers. Having face time with people makes them more interesting and easier to trust than something like a banner ad does.
        </p>
        <p>
         The team at Clef are always exploring new channels to invest in as well. They want to continue to find new ways to get in front of people who are building interesting things. They have been experimenting with sponsoring things like hackathons and other events to let the participants know that Clef is an option for them.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          What keeps you awake at night?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The year 2014 was a great year for Clef. At the beginning of the year, they had around 100 customers, and they closed the year with over 200,000. They passed all other two-factor plugins, and took over a big piece of the WordPress world. But the rapid growth itself can be a problem.
        </p>
        <p>
         On the one hand we have had an incredible year and far surpassed our expectations, but now with great success comes the demand for twice that success the next year.
        </p>
        <p>
         Every goal achieved hit raises the bar, and the successes they have create an ever-growing need to outdo themselves the next time.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Conclusion
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Brennens journey has been an interesting one to examine. His humility to seek advice in every decision has helped Clef avoid pitfalls and achieve rapid growth. He has created a team culture that attracts and retains talent. Founders and entrepreneurs can learn from his example when facing new challenges in their business.
        </p>
