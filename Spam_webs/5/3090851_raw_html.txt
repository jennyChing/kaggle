     <p>
      <b>
       Asexuality
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       nonsexuality
      </b>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asexual_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-asexual-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nonsexual_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nonsexual-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harris-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is the lack of
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_attraction" title="Sexual attraction">
       sexual attraction
      </a>
      to anyone, or low or absent interest in
      <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_activity" title="Human sexual activity">
       sexual activity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2006_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2006-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kelly-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It may be considered the lack of a
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">
       sexual orientation
      </a>
      , or one of the variations thereof, alongside
      <a href="/wiki/Heterosexuality" title="Heterosexuality">
       heterosexuality
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">
       homosexuality
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Bisexuality" title="Bisexuality">
       bisexuality
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2004_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2004-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A study in 2004 placed the prevalence of asexuality at 1% in the British population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2004_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2004-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cnn-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Asexuality is distinct from
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abstinence" title="Sexual abstinence">
       abstention from sexual activity
      </a>
      and from
      <a href="/wiki/Celibacy" title="Celibacy">
       celibacy
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halter_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Halter-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaulo_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DePaulo-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which are behavioral and generally motivated by factors such as an individual's personal or religious beliefs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sexual orientation, unlike sexual behavior, is believed to be "enduring."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apahelp_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-apahelp-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some asexual people engage in sexual activity despite lacking a desire for sex or sexual attraction, due to a variety of reasons, such as a desire to pleasure themselves or romantic partners, or a desire to have children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halter_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Halter-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Acceptance of asexuality as a sexual orientation and field of
      <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">
       scientific research
      </a>
      is still relatively new,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2006_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2006-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overview-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as a growing body of research from both sociological and psychological perspectives has begun to develop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While some researchers assert that asexuality is a sexual orientation, other researchers disagree.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Various asexual communities have started to form since the advent of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">
       World Wide Web
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">
       social media
      </a>
      . The most prolific and well-known of these communities is the Asexual Visibility and Education Network (AVEN), which was founded in 2001 by
      <a href="/wiki/David_Jay" title="David Jay">
       David Jay
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
       Definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_relationships">
       Identity and relationships
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identity and relationships">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Romantic_orientation" title="Romantic orientation">
       Romantic orientation
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Because there is significant variation among people who identify as asexual, asexuality can encompass broad definitions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Researchers generally define asexuality as the lack of sexual attraction or the lack of sexual interest,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2006_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2006-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but their definitions vary; they may use the term "to refer to individuals with low or absent sexual desire or attractions, low or absent sexual behaviors, exclusively romantic non-sexual partnerships, or a combination of both absent sexual desires and behaviors."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Asexual Visibility and Education Network (AVEN) defines an asexual as "someone who does not experience sexual attraction" and stated, "[a]nother small minority will think of themselves as asexual for a brief period of time while exploring and questioning their own sexuality" and that "[t]here is no litmus test to determine if someone is asexual. Asexuality is like any other identity at its core, it's just a word that people use to help figure themselves out. If at any point someone finds the word asexual useful to describe themselves, we encourage them to use it for as long as it makes sense to do so."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overview-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Asexual people, though lacking sexual attraction to any gender, might engage in purely
      <a href="/wiki/Romance_(love)" title="Romance (love)">
       romantic
      </a>
      relationships, while others might not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_and_Barker_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards_and_Barker-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Relationships_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Relationships-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are asexual-identified individuals who report that they feel sexual attraction but not the inclination to act on it because they have no true desire or need to engage in sexual or non-sexual activity (cuddling, hand-holding, etc.), while other asexuals engage in cuddling or other non-sexual physical activity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halter_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Halter-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaulo_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DePaulo-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some asexuals participate in sexual activity out of curiosity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some may
      <a href="/wiki/Masturbation" title="Masturbation">
       masturbate
      </a>
      as a solitary form of release, while others do not feel a need to do so.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Scientist_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-New_Scientist-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridgeman_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bridgeman-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      With regard to sexual activity in particular, the need or desire for masturbation is commonly referred to as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Libido" title="Libido">
        sex drive
       </a>
      </i>
      by asexuals and they disassociate it from sexual attraction and being sexual; asexuals who masturbate generally consider it to be a normal product of the human body and not a sign of latent sexuality, and may not even find it pleasurable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some asexual men are unable to get an erection and sexual activity by attempting penetration is impossible for them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-More_to_life_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-More_to_life-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Asexuals also differ in their feelings toward performing sex acts: some are indifferent and may have sex for the benefit of a romantic partner; others are more strongly averse to the idea, though they do not typically dislike people for having sex.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridgeman_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bridgeman-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Concerning romantic or emotional aspects of
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">
       sexual orientation
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_identity" title="Sexual identity">
       sexual identity
      </a>
      , asexuals may identify as
      <a href="/wiki/Heterosexuality" title="Heterosexuality">
       heterosexual
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">
       lesbian
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">
       gay
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bisexuality" title="Bisexuality">
       bisexual
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">
       queer
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overview-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_and_Barker_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards_and_Barker-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or by the following terms to indicate that they associate with the romantic, rather than sexual, aspects of sexual orientation:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_and_Barker_18-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards_and_Barker-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       aromantic; lack of romantic attraction towards anyone
      </li>
      <li>
       biromantic; as opposed to bisexual
      </li>
      <li>
       heteroromantic; as opposed to heterosexual
      </li>
      <li>
       homoromantic; as opposed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homosexual" title="Homosexual">
        homosexual
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       panromantic; as opposed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pansexual" title="Pansexual">
        pansexual
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      People may also identify as a
      <a href="/wiki/Gray_asexuality" title="Gray asexuality">
       gray-A
      </a>
      (such as a gray-romantic, demiromantic, demisexual or semisexual) because they feel that they are between being aromantic and non-aromantic, or between asexuality and sexual attraction. While the term
      <i>
       gray-A
      </i>
      may cover anyone who occasionally feels romantic or sexual attraction, demisexuals or semisexuals experience sexual attraction only as a secondary component, feeling sexual attraction once a reasonably stable or large emotional connection has been created.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_orientation_and_etiology">
       Sexual orientation and etiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sexual orientation and etiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There is significant debate over whether or not asexuality is a sexual orientation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been compared and equated with
      <a href="/wiki/Hypoactive_sexual_desire_disorder" title="Hypoactive sexual desire disorder">
       hypoactive sexual desire disorder
      </a>
      (HSDD), in that both imply a general lack of sexual attraction to anyone; HSDD has been used to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medicalize" title="Medicalize">
       medicalize
      </a>
      asexuality, but asexuality is generally not considered a disorder or a
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dysfunction" title="Sexual dysfunction">
       sexual dysfunction
      </a>
      (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Anorgasmia" title="Anorgasmia">
       anorgasmia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Anhedonia" title="Anhedonia">
       anhedonia
      </a>
      , etc.), because asexuality does not necessarily define someone as having a medical problem or problems relating to others socially.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaulo_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-DePaulo-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_and_Barker_18-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards_and_Barker-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reconsidering_Asexuality_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reconsidering_Asexuality-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike people with HSDD, asexual people normally do not experience "marked distress" and "interpersonal difficulty" concerning feelings about their sexuality, or generally a lack of
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_arousal" title="Sexual arousal">
       sexual arousal
      </a>
      ; asexuality is considered the lack or absence of sexual attraction as a life-enduring characteristic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2006_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2006-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_and_Barker_18-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards_and_Barker-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One study found that, compared to HSDD subjects, asexuals reported lower levels of
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_desire" title="Sexual desire">
       sexual desire
      </a>
      , sexual experience, sex-related distress and depressive symptoms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brotto2015_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-brotto2015-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Researchers Richards and Barker report that asexuals do not have disproportionate rates of
      <a href="/wiki/Alexithymia" title="Alexithymia">
       alexithymia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">
       depression
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Personality_disorder" title="Personality disorder">
       personality disorders
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_and_Barker_18-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards_and_Barker-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some people may identify as asexual, however, even if their non-sexual state is explained by one or more of the aforementioned disorders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_2_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks_2-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Asexuality may be argued as not being a meaningful category to add to the continuum of sexual orientations, and instead argued as the lack of a sexual orientation or sexuality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other arguments propose that asexuality is the denial of one's natural sexuality, and that it is a disorder caused by shame of sexuality, anxiety or
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">
       sexual abuse
      </a>
      , sometimes basing this belief on asexuals who masturbate or occasionally engage in sexual activity simply to please a romantic partner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridgeman_21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bridgeman-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-More_to_life_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-More_to_life-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The suggestion that asexuality is a sexual dysfunction is controversial among the asexual community. Those who identify as asexual usually prefer it to be recognized as a sexual orientation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biological_Markers_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Biological_Markers-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Various scholars state that asexuality is a sexual orientation, as some asexuals are unable to masturbate even though they reportedly have a normal sex drive, and that there are variations of sexual preferences, arguing that asexuality ought to be included as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sexual_orientation_8-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sexual_orientation-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halter_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Halter-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-More_to_life_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-More_to_life-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They state that asexuals do not choose to have no sexual desire, and generally start to find out their differences in sexual behaviors around adolescence. Because of these facts coming to light, it is argued that asexuality is much more than a behavioral choice, and is not something that can be cured like a disorder.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-More_to_life_22-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-More_to_life-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koukounas_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koukounas-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Etiology
      </i>
      in this context is without implication of disease, disorder, or abnormality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Research on the etiology of sexual orientation when applied to asexuality has the definitional problem of sexual orientation not consistently being defined by researchers as including asexuality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sexual orientation is defined as "enduring" and resistant to change, proving to be generally impervious to interventions intended to change it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apahelp_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-apahelp-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While heterosexuality, homosexuality and bisexuality are usually, but not always, determined during the early years of preadolescent life, it is not known when asexuality is determined. "It is unclear whether these characteristics [
      <i>
       viz.
      </i>
      , "lacking interest in or desire for sex"] are thought to be lifelong, or if they may be acquired."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some studies have suggested that asexuality is linked to biological factors that are determined
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prenatal" title="Prenatal">
       prenatally
      </a>
      or in early childhood. These studies report that asexuals are shorter on average, more likely to be
      <a href="/wiki/Handedness" title="Handedness">
       non-right-handed
      </a>
      , and have more older brothers (known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternal_birth_order_effect" title="Fraternal birth order effect">
       fraternal birth order effect
      </a>
      ). Correlations with handedness and birth order have also been reported for male homosexuality, possibly indicating that it has a similar origin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bogaert2015_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bogaert2015-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Non-measurement in some areas of sexual orientation is accepted by the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">
       American Psychological Association
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association" title="American Psychiatric Association">
       American Psychiatric Association
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Social_Workers" title="National Association of Social Workers">
       National Association of Social Workers
      </a>
      : "[S]imply to document that a phenomenon occurs, case studies and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-probability_sample" title="Non-probability sample">
       non-probability samples
      </a>
      are often adequate... Some groups are sufficiently few in number relative to the entire population that locating them with probability sampling is extremely expensive or practically impossible. In the latter cases, the use of non-probability samples is often appropriate."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amici_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-amici-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In determining etiologies, when asexuals are a small percentage of a large society, asexuals with a given etiology will compose an even smaller percentage, so that etiological information is available only from some individuals, generally not randomly selected.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
       Research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence">
       Prevalence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prevalence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Asexuality is not a new aspect of human sexuality, but it is relatively new to public discourse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sesmith_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sesmith-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      SE Smith of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">
        The Guardian
       </a>
      </i>
      is not sure asexuality has actually increased, rather leaning towards the belief that it is simply more visible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sesmith_37-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sesmith-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the mid-twentieth century,
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kinsey" title="Alfred Kinsey">
       Alfred Kinsey
      </a>
      rated individuals from 0 to 6 according to their sexual orientation from heterosexual to homosexual, known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Kinsey_scale" title="Kinsey scale">
       Kinsey scale
      </a>
      . He also included a category he called "X" for individuals with "no socio-sexual contacts or reactions";
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinsey-male_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kinsey-male-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinsey-female_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kinsey-female-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in modern times, this is categorized as representing asexuality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stange_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stange-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Kinsey labeled 1.5% of the adult male population as
      <i>
       X.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinsey-male_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kinsey-male-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinsey-female_39-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kinsey-female-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In his second book,
      <i>
       Sexual Behavior in the Human Female
      </i>
      , he reported this breakdown of individuals who are X: unmarried females = 1419%, married females = 13%, previously married females = 58%, unmarried males = 34%, married males = 0%, and previously married males = 12%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinsey-female_39-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kinsey-female-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Further empirical data about an asexual demographic appeared in 1994, when a research team in the United Kingdom carried out a comprehensive survey of 18,876 British residents, spurred by the need for sexual information in the wake of the
      <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology_of_HIV/AIDS" title="Epidemiology of HIV/AIDS">
       AIDS pandemic
      </a>
      . The survey included a question on sexual attraction, to which 1.05% of the respondents replied that they had "never felt sexually attracted to anyone at all".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The study of this phenomenon was continued by the Canadian sexuality researcher Anthony Bogaert in 2004, who explored the asexual demographic in a series of studies. Bogaert believed that the 1% figure was not an accurate reflection of the likely much larger percentage of the population that could be identified as asexual, noting that 30% of people contacted for the initial survey chose not to participate in the survey. Since less sexually experienced people are more likely to refuse to participate in studies about sexuality, and asexuals tend to be less sexually experienced than sexuals, it is likely that asexuals were under-represented in the responding participants. The same study found the number of homosexuals and bisexuals combined to be about 1.1% of the population, which is much smaller than other studies indicate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2006_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2006-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2004_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2004-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_activity_and_sexuality">
       Sexual activity and sexuality
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sexual activity and sexuality">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      While some asexuals masturbate as a solitary form of release or have sex for the benefit of a romantic partner, others do not (
      <a href="#Identity_and_relationships">
       see above
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cerankowski_and_Milks_17-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cerankowski_and_Milks-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Scientist_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-New_Scientist-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Kinsey_Institute_for_Research_in_Sex,_Gender,_and_Reproduction" title="Kinsey Institute for Research in Sex, Gender, and Reproduction">
       Kinsey Institute
      </a>
      sponsored another small survey on the topic in 2007, which found that self-identified asexuals "reported significantly less desire for sex with a partner, lower sexual arousability, and lower sexual excitation but did not differ consistently from non-asexuals in their sexual inhibition scores or their desire to masturbate".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prause_6-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-Prause-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 1977 paper titled
      <i>
       Asexual and Autoerotic Women: Two Invisible Groups
      </i>
      , by Myra T. Johnson, may be the first paper explicitly devoted to asexuality in humans. Johnson defines asexuals as those men and women "who, regardless of physical or emotional condition, actual sexual history, and marital status or ideological orientation, seem to
      <i>
       prefer
      </i>
      not to engage in sexual activity." She contrasts
      <a href="/wiki/Autoeroticism" title="Autoeroticism">
       autoerotic
      </a>
      women with asexual women: "The asexual woman... has no sexual desires at all [but] the autoerotic woman... recognizes such desires but prefers to satisfy them alone." Johnson's evidence is mostly letters to the editor found in women's magazines written by asexual/autoerotic women. She portrays them as invisible, "oppressed by a consensus that they are nonexistent," and left behind by both the sexual revolution and the feminist movement. Society either ignores or denies their existence or insists they must be ascetic for religious reasons, neurotic, or asexual for political reasons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a study published in 1979 in
      <i>
       Advances in the Study of Affect
      </i>
      , vol. 5, and in another article using the same data and published in 1980 in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Personality_and_Social_Psychology" title="Journal of Personality and Social Psychology">
        Journal of Personality and Social Psychology
       </a>
      </i>
      , Michael D. Storms of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">
       University of Kansas
      </a>
      outlined his own reimagining of the Kinsey scale. Whereas Kinsey measured sexual orientation based on a combination of actual sexual behavior and fantasizing and eroticism, Storms only used fantasizing and eroticism. Storms, however, placed hetero-eroticism and homo-eroticism on separate axes rather than at two ends of a single scale; this allows for a distinction between bisexuality (exhibiting both hetero- and homo-eroticism in degrees comparable to hetero- or homosexuals, respectively) and asexuality (exhibiting a level of homo-eroticism comparable to a heterosexual and a level of hetero-eroticism comparable to a homosexual, namely, little to none). Storms conjectured that many researchers following Kinsey's model could be mis-categorizing asexual subjects as bisexual, because both were simply defined by a lack of preference for gender in sexual partners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storms1980_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Storms1980-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storms1979_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Storms1979-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first study that gave empirical data about asexuals was published in 1983 by Paula Nurius, concerning the relationship between sexual orientation and mental health. Unlike previous studies on the subject, she used the above-mentioned two-dimensional model for sexual orientation. Six hundred eighty-nine subjectsmost of whom were students at various universities in the United States taking psychology or sociology classeswere given several surveys, including four clinical well-being scales and a survey asking how frequently they engaged in various sexual activities and how often they would like to engage in those activities. Based on the results, respondents were given a score ranging from 0 to 100 for hetero-eroticism and from 0 to 100 for homo-eroticism. Respondents who scored lower than 10 on both were labeled "asexual." This consisted of 5% of the males and 10% of the females. Results showed that asexuals were more likely to have low self-esteem and more likely to be depressed than members of other sexual orientations; 25.88% of heterosexuals, 26.54% bisexuals (called "ambisexuals"), 29.88% of homosexuals, and 33.57% of asexuals were reported to have problems with self-esteem. A similar trend existed for depression. Nurius did not believe that firm conclusions can be drawn from this for a variety of reasons. Asexuals also reported much lower frequency and desired frequency of a variety of sexual activities including having multiple partners, anal sexual activities, having sexual encounters in a variety of locations, and autoerotic activities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In some other sexually reproducing species, a portion of animals exhibit no sexual interest in either sex, and could be considered asexual.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bogaert2015_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bogaert2015-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_testing_on_rodents" title="Animal testing on rodents">
       Rodent studies
      </a>
      use the label
      <i>
       duds
      </i>
      for rodents which express no sexual interest.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bogaert2015_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-bogaert2015-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A series of studies on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ram_(sheep)" title="Ram (sheep)">
       ram
      </a>
      mating preferences found that 12.5% showed no interest in mating with either sex; the researchers termed these animals
      <i>
       asexual
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bogaert2015_34-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-bogaert2015-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The asexual rams did not differ from sexual rams in measured
      <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">
       hormone
      </a>
      levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2010 paper written by Karli June Cerankowski and Megan Milks, titled
      <i>
       New Orientations: Asexuality and Its Implications for Theory and Practice
      </i>
      , suggests that asexuality may be somewhat of a question in itself for the studies of gender and sexuality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cerankowski and Milks have suggested that asexuality raises many more questions than it resolves, such as how a person could abstain from having sex, which is generally accepted by society to be the most basic of instincts. The article also states that society has deemed "[LGBT and] female sexuality as empowered or repressed. The asexual movement challenges that assumption by challenging many of the basic tenets of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-sex_feminism" title="Pro-sex feminism">
       pro-sex feminism
      </a>
      [in which it is] already defined as repressive or anti-sex sexualities." In addition to accepting self-identification as asexual, the Asexual Visibility and Education Network has formulated asexuality as a biologically determined orientation. This formula, if dissected scientifically and proven, would support researcher
      <a href="/wiki/Simon_LeVay" title="Simon LeVay">
       Simon LeVay
      </a>
      's blind study of the hypothalamus in gay men, women, and straight men, which indicates that there is a biological difference between straight men and gay men.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      <span id="Flag">
      </span>
      A community of self-identified asexuals coalesced in the early 21st century, aided by the popularity of
      <a href="/wiki/Virtual_community" title="Virtual community">
       online communities
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Scientist_20-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-New_Scientist-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Abbott" title="Elizabeth Abbott">
       Elizabeth Abbott
      </a>
      , author of
      <i>
       A History of Celibacy
      </i>
      , acknowledges a difference between asexuality and celibacy, and posits that there has always been an asexual element in the population but that asexual people kept a low profile. While the failure to consummate marriage was seen as "an insult to the sacrament of marriage" in medieval Europe, and has sometimes been used as grounds for divorce or to rule a marriage void, asexuality, unlike homosexuality, has never been illegal, and asexual people have usually been able to "fly under the radar". However, in the 21st century, the anonymity of online communication and general popularity of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">
       social networking
      </a>
      online has facilitated the formation of a community built around a common asexual identity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duenwald_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Duenwald-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ace-logo4.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="512" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ace-logo4.svg/220px-Ace-logo4.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ace-logo4.svg/330px-Ace-logo4.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ace-logo4.svg/440px-Ace-logo4.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ace-logo4.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The AVEN triangle representing asexuality. The top line of the triangle represents the
        <a href="/wiki/Kinsey_scale" title="Kinsey scale">
         Kinsey scale
        </a>
        with the third point representing the other dimension of sexual attraction; the grey area depicting the gradient between sexual and asexual.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
         <a href="#cite_note-51">

     <p>
      The Asexual Visibility and Education Network (AVEN) is an organization founded by American asexuality
      <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">
       activist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/David_Jay" title="David Jay">
       David Jay
      </a>
      in 2001 that focuses on asexuality issues.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_society_9-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_society-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swash_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swash-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-about-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its stated goals are "creating public acceptance and discussion of asexuality and facilitating the growth of an asexual community".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_52-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-about-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Communities such as AVEN can be beneficial to those in search of answers to solve a crisis of identity with regard to their possible asexuality. Individuals go through a series of emotional processes that end with their identifying with the asexual community. They first realize that their sexual attractions differ from those of most of society. This difference leads to questioning whether the way they feel is acceptable, and possible reasons for why they feel this way.
      <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">
       Pathological
      </a>
      beliefs tend to follow, in which, in some cases, they may seek medical help because they feel they have a disease. Self-understanding is usually reached when they find a definition that matches their feelings. Asexuality communities provide support and information that allows newly identified asexuals to move from self-clarification to identifying on a communal level, which can be empowering, because they now have something to associate with, which gives normality to this overall socially-isolating situation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At this time, asexual organizations and other Internet resources play a key role in informing people about asexuality. The lack of research makes it difficult for doctors to understand the causation. Most people who say they are asexual are self-identified. This can be a problem when asexuality is mistaken for an intimacy or relationship problem or for other symptoms that do not define asexuality. There is also a significant population that either does not understand or does not believe in asexuality, which adds to the importance of these organizations to inform the general population; however, due to the lack of scientific fact on the subject, what these groups promote as information is often questioned.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, AVEN members participated in the first asexual entry into an American pride parade when they walked in the San Francisco Pride Parade.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rufus_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rufus-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In August 2010, after a period of debate over having an
      <b>
       asexual flag
      </b>
      and how to set up a system to create one, and contacting as many asexual communities as possible, a flag was announced as the
      <b>
       asexual pride flag
      </b>
      by one of the teams involved. The final flag had been a popular candidate and had previously seen use in online forums outside of AVEN. The final vote was held on a survey system outside of AVEN where the main flag creation efforts were organized. The flag colors have been used in artwork and referenced in articles about the sexuality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On June 29, 2014, AVEN organised the second International Asexuality Conference, as an affiliate WorldPride event in Toronto. The event, which was attended by around 250 people, was the largest gathering of asexuals to date.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The conference included presentations, discussions, and workshops on topics such as research on asexuality, asexual relationships, and intersecting identities.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discrimination_and_legal_protections">
       Discrimination and legal protections
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Asexuality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discrimination and legal protections">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2008" data-file-width="3008" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg/220px-WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg/330px-WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg/440px-WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WorldPride_2012_-_175.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Asexuals marching
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A 2012 study published in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Group_Processes_%26_Intergroup_Relations" title="Group Processes &amp; Intergroup Relations">
        Group Processes &amp; Intergroup Relations
       </a>
      </i>
      reports there is more
      <a href="/wiki/Prejudice" title="Prejudice">
       prejudice
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dehumanization" title="Dehumanization">
       dehumanization
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">
       discrimination
      </a>
      toward asexuals than toward other
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_minority" title="Sexual minority">
       sexual minorities
      </a>
      , such as gay men, lesbians and bisexuals. Both homosexual and heterosexual people thought of asexuals as not only cold, but also animalistic and unrestrained. The same study also found more bias towards sapiosexuals (people who find intelligence the most sexually attractive feature) than towards homosexuals or bisexuals, and that attitudes towards sapiosexuals was the strongest correlate of attitudes towards asexuals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Asexual activist, author, and blogger Julie Decker has observed that sexual harassment and violence, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Corrective_rape" title="Corrective rape">
       corrective rape
      </a>
      , commonly victimizes the asexual community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huff_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-huff-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, a different study found little evidence of serious discrimination against asexuals because of their asexuality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sociologist Mark Karrigan sees a middle ground, claiming that while asexuals do often experience discrimination, it is not of a
      <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">
       phobic
      </a>
      nature but "more about marginalization because people genuinely don't understand asexuality."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Asexuals also face prejudice from within the
      <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">
       LGBT
      </a>
      community. Upon
      <a href="/wiki/Coming_out" title="Coming out">
       coming out
      </a>
      as asexual, activist Sara Beth Brooks was told by many LGBT people that asexuals are mistaken in their self-identification and seek undeserved attention within the social justice movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huff_58-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-huff-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In some jurisdictions, asexuals have legal protections. While Brazil bans since 1999 whatever pathologization or
      <a href="/wiki/Conversion_therapy" title="Conversion therapy">
       attempted treatment of sexual orientation
      </a>
      by mental health professionals through the national ethical code,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the U.S. state of New York has labeled asexuals as a
      <a href="/wiki/Protected_class" title="Protected class">
       protected class
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, asexuality does not typically attract the attention of the public or major scrutiny; therefore, it has not been the subject of legislation as much as other sexual orientations have.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogaert2004_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bogaert2004-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
