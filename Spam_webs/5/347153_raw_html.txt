   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Goal setting
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Goal setting
      </b>
      involves the development of an action
      <a href="/wiki/Plan" title="Plan">
       plan
      </a>
      designed to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motivate" title="Motivate">
       motivate
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Guide" title="Guide">
       guide
      </a>
      a person or group toward a
      <a href="/wiki/Goal" title="Goal">
       goal
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Goal setting can be guided by goal-setting criteria (or rules) such as
      <a href="/wiki/SMART_criteria" title="SMART criteria">
       SMART criteria
      </a>
      . Goal setting is a major component of
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_development" title="Personal development">
       personal-development
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">
       management
      </a>
      literature.
     </p>
     <p>
      Studies by
      <a href="/wiki/Edwin_A._Locke" title="Edwin A. Locke">
       Edwin A. Locke
      </a>
      and his colleagues have shown that more specific and ambitious goals lead to more
      <a href="/wiki/Performance_improvement" title="Performance improvement">
       performance improvement
      </a>
      than easy or general goals. As long as the person accepts the goal, has the ability to attain it, and does not have conflicting goals, there is a positive linear relationship between goal difficulty and task performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Alec_Mace" title="Cecil Alec Mace">
       Cecil Alec Mace
      </a>
      carried out the first empirical studies in 1935.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Edwin_A._Locke" title="Edwin A. Locke">
       Edwin A. Locke
      </a>
      began to examine goal setting in the mid-1960s and continued researching goal setting for over thirty years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Locke derived the idea for goal-setting from
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Hylomorphism" title="Hylomorphism">
       form
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Four_causes#Final_cause" title="Four causes">
       final causality
      </a>
      . Aristotle speculated that purpose can cause action; thus, Locke began researching the impact goals have on human activity. Locke developed and refined his goal-setting theory in the 1960s, publishing his first article on the subject, "Toward a Theory of Task Motivation and Incentives", in 1968.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This article established the positive relationship between clearly identified goals and performance.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">
       Concept
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Concept">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Goals that are deemed difficult to achieve and specific tend to increase performance more than goals that are not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A goal can become more specific through quantification or enumeration (should be measurable), such as by demanding "...increase productivity by 50%," or by defining certain tasks that must be completed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Setting goals affects outcomes in four ways:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Choice: goals narrow attention and direct efforts to goal-relevant activities, and away from perceived undesirable and goal-irrelevant actions.
      </li>
      <li>
       Effort: goals can lead to more effort; for example, if one typically produces 4 widgets an hour, and has the goal of producing 6, one may work more intensely towards the goal than one would otherwise.
      </li>
      <li>
       Persistence: someone becomes more likely to work through setbacks if pursuing a goal.
      </li>
      <li>
       Cognition: goals can lead individuals to develop and change their behavior.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_business">
       In business
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In business">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In business, goal setting encourages participants to put in substantial effort. Also, because every member has defined expectations for their role, little room is left for inadequate, marginal effort to go unnoticed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Managers cannot constantly drive
      <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">
       motivation
      </a>
      , or keep track of an employee's work on a continuous basis. Goals are therefore an important tool for managers, since goals have the ability to function as a self-regulatory mechanism that helps employees prioritize tasks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2002_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2002-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The four mechanisms through which goal setting can affect individual performance are:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Goals focus attention toward goal-relevant activities and away from goal-irrelevant activities.
      </li>
      <li>
       Goals serve as an energizer: Higher goals induce greater effort, while low goals induce lesser effort.
      </li>
      <li>
       Goals affect persistence; constraints with regard to resources affect work pace.
      </li>
      <li>
       Goals activate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_knowledge" title="Cognitive knowledge">
        cognitive knowledge
       </a>
       and strategies that help employees cope with the situation at hand.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goal_commitment">
       Goal commitment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Goal commitment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      People perform better when they are committed to achieving certain goals. Through an understanding of the effect of goal setting on individual performance, organizations are able to use goal setting to benefit organizational performance. Locke and Latham (2002) have indicated three moderators that indicate goal setting success:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2002_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2002-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       The importance of the expected outcomes of goal attainment, and
      </li>
      <li>
       Self-efficacyone's belief that they are able to achieve the goals, and
      </li>
      <li>
       Commitment to otherspromises or engagements to others can strongly improve commitment.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Expanding the three from above, the level of commitment is influenced by external factors. Such as the person assigning the goal, setting the standard for the person to achieve/perform. This influences the level of commitment by how compliant the individual is with the one assigning the goal. An external factor can also be the role models of the individual. Say if they strive to be like their favorite athlete, the individual is more likely to put forth more effort to their own work and goals.
     </p>
     <p>
      Internal factors can derive from their participation level in the work to achieve the goal. What they expect from themselves can either flourish their success, or destroy it. Also, the individual may want to appear superior to their peers or competitors. They want to achieve the goal the best and be known for it. The self-reward of accomplishing a goal, is usually one of the main keys that keep individuals committed.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goal.E2.80.93performance_relationship">
       Goalperformance relationship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Goalperformance relationship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Locke et al. (1981) examined the behavioral effects of goal-setting, concluding that 90% of laboratory and field studies involving specific and challenging goals led to higher performance than did easy or no goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Locke and Latham (2006) argue that it is not sufficient to urge employees to "do their best". "Doing one's best" has no external referent, which makes it useless in eliciting specific behavior. To elicit some specific form of behavior from another person, it is important that this person has a clear view of what is expected from him/her. A goal is thereby of vital importance because it helps an individual to focus his or her efforts in a specified direction. In other words, goals canalize behavior.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goal_setting_and_feedback">
       Goal setting and feedback
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Goal setting and feedback">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Without proper feedback channels it is impossible for employees to adapt or adjust to the required behavior. Managers should keep track of performance to allow employees to see how effective they have been in attaining their goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Providing feedback on short-term objectives helps to sustain motivation and commitment to the goal and without it, goal setting is unlikely to be successful. Feedback should be provided on the strategies followed to achieve the goals and the final outcomes achieved, as well. Feedback on strategies used to obtain goals is very important, especially for complex work, because challenging goals put focus on outcomes rather than on performance strategies, so they impair performance. Properly delivered feedback is also very essential, and the following hints may help for providing a good feedback:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Create a positive context for feedback
      </li>
      <li>
       Use constructive and positive language
      </li>
      <li>
       Focus on behaviors and strategies
      </li>
      <li>
       Tailor feedback to the needs of the individual worker
      </li>
      <li>
       Make feedback a two-way communication process
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Advances in
      <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">
       technology
      </a>
      can facilitate providing feedback.
      <a href="/wiki/Systems_analyst" title="Systems analyst">
       Systems analysts
      </a>
      have designed computer programs that track goals for numerous members of an
      <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">
       organization
      </a>
      . Such computer systems may maintain every employee's goals, as well as their
      <a href="/wiki/Time_limit" title="Time limit">
       deadlines
      </a>
      . Separate methods may check the employee's progress on a regular basis, and other systems may require perceived
      <a href="/wiki/Slacker" title="Slacker">
       slackers
      </a>
      to explain how they intend to improve.
     </p>
     <p>
      More difficult goals require more cognitive strategies and well-developed skills. The more difficult the tasks, the smaller the group of people who possess the necessary skills and strategies. From an organizational perspective, it is thereby more difficult to successfully attain more difficult goals, since resources become more scarce.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Honing_goal_setting_using_temporal_motivation_theory">
       Honing goal setting using temporal motivation theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honing goal setting using temporal motivation theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Locke and Latham (2004) note that goal setting theory lacks "the issue of time perspective".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Taking this into consideration, Steel and Konig (2006) utilize their
      <a href="/wiki/Temporal_motivation_theory" title="Temporal motivation theory">
       temporal motivation theory
      </a>
      (TMT) to account for goal setting's effects, and suggest new hypotheses regarding a pair of its moderators: goal difficulty and proximity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steel2006_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Steel2006-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The effectiveness of goal setting can be explained by two aspects of TMT: the principle of
      <a href="/wiki/Diminishing_returns" title="Diminishing returns">
       diminishing returns
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Temporal_discounting" title="Temporal discounting">
       temporal discounting
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steel2006_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Steel2006-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similar to the expression "the sum of the parts can be greater than the whole", a division of a project into several, immediate, subgoals appears to take advantage of these two elements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steel2006_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Steel2006-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Employee_motivation">
       Employee motivation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Employee motivation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Job_satisfaction" title="Job satisfaction">
       Job satisfaction
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">
       Motivation
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The more employees are motivated, the more they are stimulated and interested in accepting goals. These success factors are interdependent. For example, the expected outcomes of goals are positively influenced when employees are involved in the goal setting process. Not only does participation increase commitment in attaining the goals that are set, participation influences self-efficacy as well. Additionally, feedback is necessary to monitor one's progress. When feedback is not present, an employee might think (s)he is not making enough progress. This can reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Self-efficacy" title="Self-efficacy">
       self-efficacy
      </a>
      and thereby harm the performance outcomes in the long run.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Goal-commitment, the most influential moderator,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       becomes especially important when dealing with difficult or complex goals. If people lack
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_commitment" title="Personal commitment">
        commitment
       </a>
       to goals, they lack motivation to reach them. To commit to a goal, one must believe in its importance or significance.
      </li>
      <li>
       Attainability: individuals must also believe that they can attainor at least partially reacha defined goal. If they think no chance exists of reaching a goal, they may not even try.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Self-efficacy" title="Self-efficacy">
        Self-efficacy
       </a>
       : the higher someone's self-efficacy regarding a certain task, the more likely they will set higher goals, and the more persistence they will show in achieving them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         16
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">
       Limitations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Limitations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Goal-setting theory has limitations. In an organization, a goal of a
      <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">
       manager
      </a>
      may not align with the goals of the organization as a whole. In such cases, the goals of an individual may come into direct conflict with the employing organization. Without aligning goals between the organization and the individual, performance may suffer.
     </p>
     <p>
      For complex tasks, goal-setting may actually impair performance. In these situations, an individual may become preoccupied with meeting the goals, rather than performing tasks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some evidence suggests that goal-setting can foster
      <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">
       unethical behavior
      </a>
      when people do not achieve specified goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Goal setting may have the drawback of inhibiting implicit learning: goal setting may encourage simple focus on an outcome without openness to exploration, understanding, or growth.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      A solution to this limitation is to set
      <i>
       learning
      </i>
      goals as well as
      <i>
       performance
      </i>
      goals, so that learning is expected as part of the process of reaching goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrantLearningGoals_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GrantLearningGoals-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeganLearningGoals_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KeganLearningGoals-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Developments_in_theory">
       Developments in theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Developments in theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goal_choice">
       Goal choice
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Goal choice">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Self-efficacy, past performance, and various other social factors influence goal setting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Failure to meet previous goals often leads to setting lower (and more likely achievable) goals. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_networking" title="Social networking">
       social networking
      </a>
      website
      <a href="/wiki/43_Things" title="43 Things">
       43 Things
      </a>
      , "the world's largest goal-setting community", seeks to take advantage of these social effects.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Learning_goals">
       Learning goals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Learning goals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are times when having specific goals is not a best option; this is the case when the goal requires new skills or knowledge.
      <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_vision" title="Tunnel vision">
       Tunnel vision
      </a>
      is a consequence of specific goals; if a person is too focused on attaining a specific goal, he or she may ignore the need to learn new skills or acquire new information. In situations like this, the best option is to set a
      <i>
       learning goal
      </i>
      . A learning goal is a generalized goal to achieve knowledge in a certain topic or field, but it can ultimately lead to better performance in specific goals related to the learning goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrantLearningGoals_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GrantLearningGoals-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeganLearningGoals_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-KeganLearningGoals-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Locke and Latham (2006) attribute this response to
      <a href="/wiki/Metacognition" title="Metacognition">
       metacognition
      </a>
      . They believe that "a learning goal facilitates or enhances metacognitionnamely, planning, monitoring, and evaluating progress toward goal attainment".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is necessary in environments with little or no guidance and structure. Although jobs typically have set goals, individual goals and achievement can benefit from metacognition.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Framing">
       Framing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Framing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)" title="Framing (social sciences)">
       Framing
      </a>
      , or how goals are viewed, influences performance. When one feels threatened and or intimidated by a high goal they perform poorer than those who view the goal as a challenge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The framing of a goal as a gain or a loss influences one's eventual performance.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Affect">
       Affect
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Affect">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Realization of goals has an effect on
      <a href="/wiki/Affect_(psychology)" title="Affect (psychology)">
       affect
      </a>
      that is, feelings of success and satisfaction. Achieving goals has a positive effect, and failing to meet goals has negative consequences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the effect of goals is not exclusive to one realm. Success in one's job can compensate for feelings of failure in one's personal life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Group_goals">
       Group goals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Group goals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The relationship between group goals and individual goals influences group performance; when goals are compatible there is a positive effect, but when goals are incompatible the effects can be detrimental to the group's performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is another factor at work in groups, and that is the sharing factor; a positive correlation exists between sharing information within the group and group performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the case of group goals, feedback needs to be related to the group, not individuals, in order for it to improve the group's performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_and_traits">
       Goals and traits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Goals and traits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On a basic level, the two types of goals are
      <i>
       learning goals
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       performance goals
      </i>
      ; each possesses different traits associated with the selected goal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrantLearningGoals_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-GrantLearningGoals-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Learning goals
      </i>
      involve tasks where skills and knowledge can be acquired, whereas
      <i>
       performance goals
      </i>
      involve easy-to-accomplish tasks that will make one appear successful (thus tasks where error and judgment are possible to be avoided).
     </p>
     <p>
      A more complex trait-mediation study is the one conducted by Lee, Sheldon, and Turban (2003),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which yielded the following results:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Amotivated orientation
       </i>
       (low confidence in one's capabilities) is associated with goal-avoidance motivation, and more generally, associated with lower goals levels and lower performance.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Control orientation
       </i>
       (extrinsic motivation) is associated with both avoidance and approach goals. Approach goals are associated with higher goal levels and higher performance.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Autonomy goals
       </i>
       (intrinsic motivation) leads to mastery goals, enhanced focus, and therefore enhanced performance.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Macro-level_goals">
       Macro-level goals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Macro-level goals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Macro-level goals
      </i>
      refer to goal setting that is applied to the company as a whole. Cooperative goals reduce the negative feelings that occur as a result of alliances and the formation of groups.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The most common parties involved are the company and its suppliers. The three motivators for macro-level goals are: self-efficacy, growth goals, and organizational vision.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_and_subconscious_priming">
       Goals and subconscious priming
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal_setting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Goals and subconscious priming">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The effects of subconscious
      <a href="/wiki/Priming_(psychology)" title="Priming (psychology)">
       priming
      </a>
      and conscious goals are independent, although a conscious goal has a larger effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The interaction effect is that priming can enhance the effects of difficult goals, but it has no effect on easy goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is also the situation in which priming and conscious goals conflict with one another, and in this situation the conscious goals have a greater effect on performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lockeandlatham2006_2-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-lockeandlatham2006-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

          <span style="font-size:85%;">

           <a href="/wiki/Current_Directions_in_Psychological_Science" title="Current Directions in Psychological Science">
            Current Directions in Psychological Science
           </a>
          </i>
          <b>
           15
          </b>
          (5): 265268.
          <a href="/wiki/Digital_object_identifier" title="Digital object identifier">
           doi
          </a>
          :
          <a class="external text" href="//dx.doi.org/10.1111%2Fj.1467-8721.2006.00449.x" rel="nofollow">
           10.1111/j.1467-8721.2006.00449.x
          </a>
          .
          <q>
           Goal-setting theory (Locke &amp; Latham, 1990, 2002) was developed inductively within industrial/organizational (I/O) psychology over a 25-year period, based on some 400 laboratory and field studies. These studies showed that specific, high (hard) goals lead to a higher level of task performance than do easy goals or vague, abstract goals such as the exhortation to 'do one's best'. So long as a person is committed to the goal, has the requisite ability to attain it, and does not have conflicting goals, there is a positive, linear relationship between goal difficulty and task performance. Because goals refer to future valued outcomes, the setting of goals is first and foremost a discrepancy-creating process. It implies discontent with one's present condition and the desire to attain an object or outcome.
          </q>

       <li id="cite_note-16">
        <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
         <b>
          <a href="#cite_ref-16">
           ^
          </a>
         </b>
        </span>
        <span class="reference-text">
         Goal-setting theory might define
         <i>
          self-efficacy
         </i>
         as an impression that one has the capability of performing in a certain manner or of attaining certain goals. Or one could define self-efficacy as a belief that one has the capabilities to execute the courses of actions required to
         <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">
          manage
         </a>
         prospective situations. Unlike
         <a href="/wiki/Efficacy" title="Efficacy">
          efficacy
         </a>
         (defined as the power to produce an effectin essence,
         <a href="/wiki/Skill" title="Skill">
          competence
         </a>
         ), self-efficacy consists of the belief (whether or not accurate) that one has the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">
          power
         </a>
         to produce that effect. For example, a person with high self-efficacy may engage in more health-related activity when an illness occurs, whereas a person with low self efficacy may succumb to feelings of hopelessness. (Compare:
         <span class="citation book">
          Sue, David; Sue, Derald Wing; Sue, Stanley; Sue, Diane (2015).
          <a class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=W6CaBAAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PT224" rel="nofollow">
           <i>
            Understanding abnormal behavior
           </i>
          </a>
          (11th ed.). Belmont, CA: Wadsworth/Cengage Learning. p.194.
          <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">
           ISBN
          </a>
          <a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9781305537606" title="Special:BookSources/9781305537606">
           9781305537606
          </a>
          .
         </span>
         <span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AGoal+setting&amp;rft.aufirst=David&amp;rft.aulast=Sue&amp;rft.au=Sue%2C+David&amp;rft.au=Sue%2C+Derald+Wing&amp;rft.au=Sue%2C+Diane&amp;rft.au=Sue%2C+Stanley&amp;rft.btitle=Understanding+abnormal+behavior&amp;rft.date=2015&amp;rft.edition=11th&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fbooks.google.com%2Fbooks%3Fid%3DW6CaBAAAQBAJ%26pg%3DPT224&amp;rft.isbn=9781305537606&amp;rft.pages=194&amp;rft.place=Belmont%2C+CA&amp;rft.pub=Wadsworth%2FCengage+Learning&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook">
          <span style="display:none;">
          </span>
         </span>
         ) Note the distinction between
         <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">
          self-esteem
         </a>
         and self-efficacy. Self-esteem in this context relates to a person's sense of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-worth" title="Self-worth">
          self-worth
         </a>
         , whereas self-efficacy relates to a person's perception of their ability to reach a goal. For example, take the case of an incompetent rock-climber. Though probably afflicted with poor self-efficacy in regard to rock climbing, this hypothetical person could retain their self-esteem unaffected.

          Grant, Anthony M (September 2012).
          <a class="external text" href="http://coachingconsultinginternational.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/An-integrated-model-of-goal-focused-coaching-Grant-ICPR-2012.pdf" rel="nofollow">

          </span>

          <i>
           International Coaching Psychology Review
          </i>
          <b>
           7
          </b>
          (2): 146165 (151).
          <q>
           Learning goals (sometimes referred to as mastery goals) focus the coachee's attention on the learning associated with task mastery, rather than on the performance of the task itself. An example of a learning goal in executive or workplace coaching might be 'learn how to be the best lawyer in my area of practice'. Learning goals tend to be associated with a range of positive cognitive and emotional processes including perception of a complex task as a positive challenge rather than a threat, greater absorption in the actual task performance (Deci &amp; Ryan, 2002), and enhanced memory and well-being (Linnenbrink, Ryan &amp; Pintrich, 1999). Furthermore, individual performance can be enhanced in highly complex or challenging situations when team goals are primarily framed as being learning goals, and the use of team-level learning goals can foster enhanced co-operation between team members (Kristof-Brown &amp; Stevens, 2001). One benefit of setting learning goals is that they tend to be associated with higher levels of intrinsic motivation which in turn is associated with performance (Sarrazin et al., 2002).
