        <p>
         <img align="left" alt="how to plant and grow gas plants" height="235" src="/images/stories/users/818/gas plant.jpg" style="float: left; margin: 6px;" width="223"/>
         Intro: The gas plant  Dictamnus  gets its name from the lemon-scented volatile gas given off when the leaves are rubbed.  You can also hold a match to the flowers, which will then emit a quick series of low-temperature sparks that will not even harm the petals of the blooms.  Gas plants produce lightly scented white or pink blooms on 24-36-inch vertical spikes and make quite a statement in any landscape; however, they do require a bit of an initial commitment. They pay off big, though, and could easily become one of your favorite plants in the garden.
        </p>
        <p>
         Step 1: Site.  Once gas plants are planted, they do not like to have their roots disturbed at all.  They do not want to be moved or divided either.  Therefore, choose a site in full sun or light shade that can serve as the gas plant's permanent home. You also want to choose a site that can accommodate the mature size of the plants.  (Gas plants grow to be 2-3 feet tall and spread 2 feet.)  Plant them near flowering shrubs or rose bushes that are not apt to be moved thereby eliminating the possibility of the roots of your gas plants being disturbed.  You can also plant gas plants en masse to form a hedge.
        </p>
        <p>
         Step 2: Soil and water.  Grow your gas plants in moderately rich soil with good drainage.  Keep evenly moist.
        </p>
        <p>
         Step 3: Planting.  As previously mentioned, gas plants do not like to be moved or disturbed, and this includes transplanting.  To successfully grow gas plants, start them from seed sown outdoors in the fall.  (Gas plant seeds need the winter cold in order to germinate.)  About one-fourth of the seeds will sprout in the following spring. Another one-fourth of the seeds will sprout the second year, and the remainder may or may not sprout within the next 2-3 years.
        </p>
        <p>
         Tips:
        </p>
        <p>
         For more information on this wonderful perennial, see my article
         <a href="http://www.associatedcontent.com/article/2472708/plant_profile_gas_plant.html?cat=32" rel="nofollow">
          Plant Profile: Gas Plant
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Gas plants are hardy perennials in Zones 3-9.
        </p>
        <p>
         Choose the gas plant variety Dictamnus albus for its stunning white blooms and dark green glossy foliage.
        </p>
        <p>
         Warnings:
        </p>
        <p>
         Gas plants usually do not flower until 4-5 years after they are planted. Patience is a virtue.
        </p>
        <p>
         The volatile oil in gas plants can cause a rash, which can be aggravated by sunlight, in some people.  Wear gloves and long sleeves when working with your gas plants.
        </p>
        <p>
