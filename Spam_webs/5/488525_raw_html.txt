      <h1>
       Faber launches Modern Classics imprint
      </h1>
      <p class="taglist">
       <a href="/home/search?subject=3&amp;search=blog">
        Books
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/home/search?subject=5&amp;search=blog">
        Graphic Design
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/home/search?subject=9&amp;search=blog">
        Photography
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/home/search?subject=10&amp;search=blog">
        Type / Typography
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       Posted by Mark Sinclair, 29 September 2014, 13:06
       <a href="http://www.creativereview.co.uk/cr-blog/2014/september/faber-modern-classics-covers">
        Permalink
       </a>
       <a href="#feedback">
        Comments (3)
       </a>
      </p>
     </div>

     <div class="articlecontent">
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="444" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/fabermodernclassicsopener_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Faber has unveiled the look of its new Modern Classics range, which is set to launch early next year. The new design uses plenty of white space with a subtle band of colour, plus a line from each of the texts...
      </p>
      <p>
       Ten titles will launch the series in April 2015 (full details below), with a further six publishing in June. The series design has been led by
       <a href="http://www.faber.co.uk/" target="_blank">
        Faber
       </a>
       's art director, Donna Payne, who headed up the publisher's rebranding in 2013 (details
       <a href="/cr-blog/2013/september/fabers-word-marque" target="_blank">
        here
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Continuing on from that work, Payne has brought in several of the colours chosen as part of the Faber palette for use on the new covers. "A translucent slab of intense colour taken fromthe Faber palette cuts across the central area of each cover to create a striking visual link when the books are spine out or displayed together on a table," she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/nightwood_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       The colour palette is derived from  research carried out at the Faber archive in Bloomsbury, Payne told CR last year: "Many of our archive covers, particularly those designed in the fifties  and sixties using bright acid shades, have aged down beautifully."
      </p>
      <p>
       The first set of covers in the new Modern Classics series favour photography instead of illustration  the two others in the launch series, both awaiting approval, feature great photographic images  and are complemented with a clean, unfussy layouy and type design. All the text is set in Faber's company face, Swiss.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hannah Griffiths, Faber's associate fiction publisher, who led the development of the series, says that the criteria for inclusion was that the tiles were originally published by Faber, and at least twenty-five years old. "This was to give us a real sense that these are books that can justify the term modern classic'," she adds.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the clever use of colour picks up on both the archive material and references the chosen image (see Nightwood above, for example), what's also encouraging to see is that the additional cover text comes from the book itself. There's no attempt to define or summarise the book in a pithy phrase, just a faith in the reputation of the work  and its new cover  to do the job.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/remains_of_the_day_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/the_buddha_of_suburbia_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/selected_tseliot_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/self_help_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="/images/uploads/2014/09/venice_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="873" src="http://www.creativereview.co.uk/images/uploads/2014/09/ariel_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="874" src="http://www.creativereview.co.uk/images/uploads/2014/09/look_back_in_anger_chosen_0.jpg" width="569"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to the publishers, some of the editions will contain readers'  notes, facsimile material from the Faber archive, and newly commissioned  material to help contextualise the work. Faber writers have been  involved in the selection process, being asked to nominate their classic  of choice.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first ten titles in the series are:
      </p>
      <p>
       Nightwood by Djuna Barnes with introductions from Jeannette Winterson and T S Eliot
      </p>
      <p>
       Ariel by Sylvia Plath (chosen by Edna O'Brien)
      </p>
      <p>
       Pincher Martin by William Golding with an afterword by Phillipa Gregory
      </p>
      <p>
       The Buddha of Suburbia by Hanif Kureishi with an introduction by Zadie Smith
      </p>
      <p>
       Self-Help by Lorrie Moore
      </p>
      <p>
       Housekeeping by Marilynne Robinson (selected by Barbara Kingsolver)
      </p>
      <p>
       Venice by Jan Morris
      </p>
      <p>
       Selected Poems by T S Eliot featuring an essay by Seamus Heaney
      </p>
      <p>
       Look Back in Anger by John Osborne with an introduction from Michael Billington and an afterword by David Hare
      </p>
      <p>
       The Remains of the Day by Kazuo Ishiguro
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        An accompanying website,
        <a href="http://www.fabermodernclassics.com" target="_blank">
         fabermodernclassics.com
        </a>
        , will launch later this year
       </em>
      </p>
     </div>
     <div id="feedback">
      <h4>
       3 Comments
      </h4>
      <p id="comment-94197">
       I'm all for fantastic grid layouts and seamless consistency. But when it comes to books, should we really create a system in which all that are published by the same publishers, look similar? I understand that these form as part of a collection of Faber's modern classics, but each piece is different, Authors, writing styles, themes, and the era in which they originally created.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       I'm not saying that these are wrong
       <br/>
       <br/>
       For me, I feel as thought the 'modern classics' branding across the top is too strong and dominant. and the typography leaves much to be desired. Yes, the bottom ~half of the design shows the character of the content, but does the use of swiss type compliment the variety of writing within each book?
       <br/>
       <span class="feedbackauthor">
        James
       </span>
       <br/>
       <span>
        2014-09-29 14:27:19
       </span>
      </p>
      <div class="hr">
       <hr/>
      </div>
      <p id="comment-94203">
       I do agree with James about the choice of typeface  Helvetica is a bit of a let down here. Making a more assertive choice would have helped establish the brand in a stronger way. I'm a bit disappointed in this respect, I must admit.
       <br/>
       <span class="feedbackauthor">
        Stphane Darricau
       </span>
       <br/>
       <span>
        2014-09-29 16:30:00
       </span>
      </p>
      <div class="hr">
       <hr/>
      </div>
      <p id="comment-94320">
       I have no argument with the grid format: it is after all clean lined and 'classic', which is appropriate.  I think there's a couple of stunning photos here that give the titles in question all the individual branding they need: the moody shot of the limousine passing through the estate gates on The Remains of the Day, and the gloriously lurid interior shot ion the Buddha of Suburbia, which looks like an early Martin Parr.  A pity they haven't used such distinctive photography in the rest of the series, which is a bit of a mixed bag.  On the whole I think the series needs more, not less branding.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       I'm inclined to agree about the typography though.  Not that it wants to be too intrusive: perhaps a nice hairline sans would have done the job.
       <br/>

        <a href="http://www.simpsonscreative.co.uk">
         Keith Tricker
