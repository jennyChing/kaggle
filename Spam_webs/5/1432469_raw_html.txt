     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">
        SS
       </a>
       <i>
        Great Eastern
       </i>
      </b>
      was an iron sailing steam ship designed by
      <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">
       Isambard Kingdom Brunel
      </a>
      , and built by
      <a href="/wiki/Millwall_Iron_Works" title="Millwall Iron Works">
       J. Scott Russell &amp; Co.
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Millwall" title="Millwall">
       Millwall
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">
       River Thames
      </a>
      , London. She was by far the largest ship ever built at the time of her 1858 launch, and had the capacity to carry 4,000 passengers from England to Australia without refuelling. Her length of 692 feet (211m) was only surpassed in 1899 by the 705-foot (215m) 17,274-gross-ton
      <a href="/wiki/RMS_Oceanic_(1899)" title="RMS Oceanic (1899)">
       RMS
       <i>
        Oceanic
       </i>
      </a>
      , and her gross tonnage of 18,915 was only surpassed in 1901 by the 701-foot (214m) 21,035-gross-ton
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Celtic" title="RMS Celtic">
       RMS
       <i>
        Celtic
       </i>
      </a>
      . With five
      <a href="/wiki/Funnel_(ship)" title="Funnel (ship)">
       funnels
      </a>
      (later reduced to four), she was one of a very few vessels to ever sport that number, sharing her number of five with the Russian cruiser
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_cruiser_Askold_(1900)" title="Russian cruiser Askold (1900)">
       <i>
        Askold
       </i>
      </a>
       though several warships, including
      <a href="/wiki/HMS_Viking_(1909)" title="HMS Viking (1909)">
       HMS
       <i>
        Viking
       </i>
      </a>
      , and several French cruisers of the pre-dreadnought era had six.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brunel knew her affectionately as the "Great Babe". He died in 1859 shortly after her ill-fated maiden voyage, during which she was damaged by an explosion. After repairs, she plied for several years as a passenger liner between Britain and North America before being converted to a cable-laying ship and laying the first lasting
      <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">
       transatlantic telegraph cable
      </a>
      in 1866.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finishing her life as a floating
      <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">
       music hall
      </a>
      and advertising hoarding (for the famous department store
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis%27s" title="Lewis's">
       Lewis's
      </a>
      ) in Liverpool, she was broken up in 1889.
     </p>

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After his success in pioneering steam travel to North America with
      <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Western" title="SS Great Western">
       <i>
        Great Western
       </i>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Britain" title="SS Great Britain">
       <i>
        Great Britain
       </i>
      </a>
      , Brunel turned his attention to longer voyages as far as Australia and realised the potential of a ship that could travel round the world without the need of refuelling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 25 March 1852, Brunel made a sketch of a steamship in his diary and wrote beneath it: "Say 600 ft x 65 ft x 30 ft" (180 m x 20 m x 9.1 m). These measurements were six times larger by volume than any ship afloat; such a large vessel would benefit from
      <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">
       economies of scale
      </a>
      and would be both fast and economical, requiring fewer crew than the equivalent tonnage made up of smaller ships. Brunel realised that the ship would need more than one propulsion system; since twin
      <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">
       screws
      </a>
      were still very much experimental, he settled on a combination of a single screw and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paddle_wheels" title="Paddle wheels">
       paddle wheels
      </a>
      , with auxiliary sail power. Using paddle wheels meant that the ship would be able to reach
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcutta" title="Calcutta">
       Calcutta
      </a>
      , where the
      <a href="/wiki/Hooghly_River" title="Hooghly River">
       Hooghly River
      </a>
      was too shallow for screws.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brunel showed his idea to
      <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Russell" title="John Scott Russell">
       John Scott Russell
      </a>
      , an experienced naval architect and ship builder whom he had first met at
      <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Exhibition" title="The Great Exhibition">
       the Great Exhibition
      </a>
      . Scott Russell examined Brunel's plan and made his own calculations as to the ship's feasibility. He calculated that it would have a displacement of 20,000 tons and would require 8,500 horsepower (6,300kW) to achieve 14 knots (26km/h), but believed it was possible. At Scott Russell's suggestion, they approached the directors of the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Steam_Navigation_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eastern Steam Navigation Company (page does not exist)">
       Eastern Steam Navigation Company
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Steam_Navigation_Company">
       Eastern Steam Navigation Company
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eastern Steam Navigation Company">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Eastern Company was formed in January 1851 with the plan of exploiting the increase in trade and emigration to India, China and Australia. To make this plan viable they needed a subsidy in the form of a mail contract from the British General Post Office, which they
      <a href="/wiki/Request_for_tender" title="Request for tender">
       tendered
      </a>
      for and Brunel started the construction of two vessels,
      <i>
       Victoria
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Adelaide
      </i>
      . However, in March 1852 the Government awarded the contracts to the
      <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_and_Oriental_Steam_Navigation_Company" title="Peninsular and Oriental Steam Navigation Company">
       Peninsular and Oriental Steam Navigation Company
      </a>
      , even though the Eastern Company's tender was lower. This left them in the position of having a company without a purpose.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brunel's large ship promised to be able to compete with the fast clippers that currently dominated the route, as she would be able to carry sufficient coal for a non-stop passage and the company invited him to present his ideas to the board. He was unable to attend due to illness and Scott Russell took his place.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Company then set up a committee to investigate the proposal, and they reported in favour and the scheme was adopted at a board meeting held in July 1852. Brunel was appointed Engineer to the project and he began to gather tenders to build the hull, paddle engines and screw engines. Brunel had a considerable stake in the company and when requested to appoint a resident engineer refused in no uncertain terms:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       I cannot act under any supervision, or form part of any system which recognises any other advisor than myself... if any doubt ever arises on these points I must cease to be responsible and cease to act.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      He was just as firm in the terms for the final contract where he insisted that nothing was to be undertaken without his express consent, and that procedures and requirements for the construction were specifically laid down.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1854.E2.80.931859:_Construction_to_maiden_voyage">
       18541859: Construction to maiden voyage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 18541859: Construction to maiden voyage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">
       Construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1009" data-file-width="1571" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg/220px-SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg/330px-SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg/440px-SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS_Great_Eastern_diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sectional plan of
        <i>
         Great Eastern
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although Brunel had estimated the cost of building the ship at 500,000, Scott Russell offered a very low tender of 377,200: 275,200 for the hull, 60,000 for the screw engines and boilers, and 42,000 for the paddle engines and boilers. Scott Russell even offered to reduce the tender to 258,000 if an order for a sister ship was placed at the same time. Brunel accepted Scott Russell's tender in May 1853, without questioning it; Scott Russell was a highly skilled shipbuilder and Brunel would accept an estimate from such an esteemed colleague without question.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the spring of 1854 work could at last begin. The first problem to arise was where the ship was to be built. Scott Russell's contract stipulated that it was to be built in a dock, but Russell quoted a price of 810,000 to build the necessary dock and so this part of the scheme was abandoned, partly due to the cost and also to the difficulty of finding a suitable site for the dock. The idea of a normal stern first launch was also rejected because of the great length of the vessel, also because to provide the right launch angle the bow of the ship would have to be raised 40 feet (12m) in the air. Eventually it was decided to build the ship sideways to the river and use a mechanical slip designed by Brunel for the launch. Later the mechanical design was dropped on the grounds of cost, although the sideways plan remained.
     </p>
     <p>
      Having decided on a sideways launch, a suitable site had to be found, as Scott Russell's Millwall, London, yard was too small. The adjacent yard belonging to
      <a href="/wiki/David_Napier_(marine_engineer)" title="David Napier (marine engineer)">
       David Napier
      </a>
      was empty, available and suitable, so it was leased and a railway line constructed between the two yards for moving materials. The site of the launch is still visible on the
      <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Dogs" title="Isle of Dogs">
       Isle of Dogs
      </a>
      . Part of the slipway has been preserved on the waterfront, while at low tide, more of the slipway can be seen on the Thames foreshore. The remains of the slipways, and other structures associated with the launch of the SS Great Eastern, have recently been surveyed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Thames_Discovery_Programme" title="Thames Discovery Programme">
       Thames Discovery Programme
      </a>
      , a community project recording the archaeology of the Thames intertidal zone in London. Brunel's achievements in London are also commemorated in Rotherhithe, at the
      <a href="/wiki/Brunel_Museum" title="Brunel Museum">
       Brunel Museum
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Great Eastern's
      </i>
      keel was laid down on 1 May 1854. The hull was an all-iron construction, a double hull of 19mm (0.75inch) wrought iron in 0.86 m (2ft 10 in) plates with ribs every 1.8m (6ft). Internally, the hull was divided by two 107m (350ft) long, 18m (60ft) high, longitudinal bulkheads and further transverse bulkheads dividing the ship into nineteen compartments.
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      was the first ship to incorporate the
      <a href="/wiki/Double_hull" title="Double hull">
       double-skinned hull
      </a>
      , a feature which would not be seen again in a ship for several decades, but which is now compulsory for reasons of safety.
     </p>
     <p>
      She had sail, paddle and screw propulsion. The paddle-wheels were 17m (56ft) in diameter and the four-bladed screw-propeller was 7.3m (24ft) across. The power came from four steam engines for the paddles and an additional engine for the
      <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">
       propeller
      </a>
      . Total power was estimated at 6 MW (8,000
      <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">
       hp
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      She also had six masts (said to be named after the days of a week  Monday being the fore mast and Saturday the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanker_(sail)" title="Spanker (sail)">
       spanker mast
      </a>
      ), providing space for 1,686 square metres (18,150sqft) of sails (7 gaff and max. 9 (usually 4) square sails), rigged similar to a topsail schooner with a main gaff sail (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fore-and-aft" title="Fore-and-aft">
       fore-and-aft
      </a>
      sail) on each mast, one "jib" on the fore mast and three square sails on masts no. 2 and no. 3 (Tuesday &amp; Wednesday); for a time mast no. 4 was also fitted with three yards. In later years, some of the yards were removed. According to some sources
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2010)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      she would have carried 5,435 square metres (58,500sqft). This amount of canvas is obviously too much for seven fore-and-aft sails and max. 9 square sails. This (larger) figure of sail area lies only a few square metres below that the famous
      <a href="/wiki/Flying_P-Liner" title="Flying P-Liner">
       Flying P-Liner
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preussen_(ship)" title="Preussen (ship)">
        Preussen
       </a>
      </i>
      carried  with her five full-rigged masts of 30 square sails and a lot of stay sails. Setting sails turned out to be unusable at the same time as the paddles and screw were under steam, because the hot exhaust from the five (later four) funnels would set them on fire. Her maximum speed was 24km/h (13
      <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">
       knots
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scott_Russell_bankruptcy">
       Scott Russell bankruptcy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scott Russell bankruptcy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      At the beginning of February 1856 Brunel advised the Eastern Company that they should take possession of the ship to avoid it being seized by Scott Russell's creditors. This caused Scott Russell's bankers to refuse to honour his cheques and foreclose on his assets and on 4 February Scott Russell suspended all payments to his creditors and dismissed all his workmen a week later.
     </p>
     <p>
      Russell's creditors met on 12 February and it was revealed that Russell had liabilities of 122,940 and assets of 100,353. It was decided that his existing contracts would be allowed to be completed and the business would be liquidated. He issued a statement to the Board of the Eastern Company in which he repudiated his contract and effectively handed the uncompleted ship back to them. When the situation was reviewed it was found that three-quarters of the work on the hull had not been completed and that there was a deficit of 1,200 tons between the amount of iron supplied and that used on the ship.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brunel meanwhile wrote to John Yates and instructed him to negotiate with Scott Russell for the lease of his yard and equipment. Yates replied that Scott Russell had mortgaged the yard to his banker and that any negotiation would have to be with the bank, who after weeks of wrangling agreed to lease the yard and equipment until 12 August 1857.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Eastern Company began the task of completing the ship. Work recommenced in May and took longer than expected to complete. Brunel reported in June 1857 that once the screw, screw shaft and sternpost had been installed the ship would be ready for launching. However, the launch ways and cradles would not be ready in time since the contract for their construction had only been placed in January 1857. Under pressure from all sides, the lease of the shipyard costing 1,000 a month, and against his better judgement, Brunel agreed to launch the ship on 3 November 1857 to catch the high tide.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">
       Launch
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Launch">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="600" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg/220px-Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg/330px-Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg/440px-Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_eastern_launch_attempt.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Great Eastern
        </i>
        before launch in 1858
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Brunel had hoped to conduct the launch with a minimum of publicity but many thousands of spectators had heard of it and occupied vantage points all round the yard. He was also dismayed to discover that the Eastern Company's directors had sold 3,000 tickets for spectators to enter the shipyard.
     </p>
     <p>
      As he was preparing for the launch some of the directors joined him on the rostrum with a list of names for the ship. On being asked which he preferred, Brunel replied "Call her Tom Thumb if you like". At 12:30 pm Henrietta (daughter of a major fundraiser for the ship,
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Thomas_Hope" title="Henry Thomas Hope">
       Henry Thomas Hope
      </a>
      ) christened the ship
      <i>
       Leviathan
      </i>
      much to everyone's surprise since she was commonly known as
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      ; her name subsequently changed back to
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      in July 1858.
     </p>
     <p>
      The launch, however, failed,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mechanics_Monthly_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mechanics_Monthly-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as the steam winches and manual capstans used to haul the ship towards the water were not up to the job. Brunel made another attempt on the 19th and again on the 28th, this time using
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_cylinder" title="Hydraulic cylinder">
       hydraulic rams
      </a>
      to move the ship, but these too proved inadequate. The ship was finally launched sideways at 1:42pm on 31 January 1858, using more powerful hydraulic rams supplied by the then-new
      <a href="/wiki/Tangye" title="Tangye">
       Tangye
      </a>
      company of Birmingham, the association with such a famous project giving a useful fillip to the fledgling company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brunel2c_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-brunel2c-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She was 211m (692ft) long, 25m (83ft) wide, with a
      <a href="/wiki/Draft_(hull)" title="Draft (hull)">
       draft
      </a>
      of 6.1m (20ft) unloaded and 9.1m (30ft) fully laden, and displaced 32,000 tons fully loaded. In comparison, SS
      <i>
       Persia
      </i>
      , launched in 1856, was 119m (390ft) long with a 14m (45ft) beam.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Suez_Canal_concerns">
       Suez Canal concerns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Suez Canal concerns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1857, during the planning of the
      <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">
       Suez Canal
      </a>
      , it was thought that
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      would not be able to traverse it, since she had a draft of 28ft (8.5m) and it was expected that the canal would be excavated to a depth of 26ft (7.9m).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In any event, when the canal was opened to shipping in 1869,
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      was no longer in passenger service.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fitting_out">
       Fitting out
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fitting out">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The launch of the ship cost 170,000, a third of Brunel's estimate for the entire vessel, and it had yet to be fitted out. It was difficult to get any more money from the Eastern Company's investors as the company was close to bankruptcy. To prevent this from happening, a new company was formed, the "Great Ship Company", with capital of 340,000. They bought the ship for 160,000, which left enough funds for fitting her out. The Eastern Company's shareholders were given the market value of their 20 shares (2 10s) towards payment for shares in the new company and the Eastern Steam Navigation Company entered liquidation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Tenders were invited for fitting the ship out, and two were received  one from Wigram and Lucas for 142,000, and the other from John Scott Russell for 125,000. Brunel had taken a long holiday on medical advice and was absent when the contract was awarded to Scott Russell. The work was begun in January 1859, and was completed by August.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Maiden_voyage">
       Maiden voyage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Maiden voyage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="393" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg/220px-Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg/330px-Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great-Eastern-At-Sea-.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Great Eastern
        </i>
        At Sea, the great ship of IK Brunel as imagined at sea by the artist at her launch in 1858
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      30 August 1859 was given as the date of the first voyage, but this was later put back to 6 September. The destination was
      <a href="/wiki/Weymouth,_Dorset" title="Weymouth, Dorset">
       Weymouth
      </a>
      , from which a trial trip into the Atlantic would be made. Following this the ship would sail to
      <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">
       Holyhead
      </a>
      , Wales, its home port for American voyages.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement">
         <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2012)">
          dubious
         </span>
        </a>
        <span class="metadata">
         
         <a href="/wiki/Talk:SS_Great_Eastern#Dubious" title="Talk:SS Great Eastern">
          discuss
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The company had made an agreement with the Canada's
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Trunk_Railway" title="Grand Trunk Railway">
       Grand Trunk Railway
      </a>
      to use
      <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">
       Portland, Maine
      </a>
      as its US destination, and the railway company had built a special jetty to accommodate the ship.
      <a href="/wiki/William_Harrison_(merchant_navy_officer)" title="William Harrison (merchant navy officer)">
       William Harrison
      </a>
      was appointed Captain in 1856; he drowned in 1860 while sailing from Hythe to Southampton in the ship's boat.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 9 September the ship had passed down the Thames, and out into the English Channel, and had just passed
      <a href="/wiki/Hastings" title="Hastings">
       Hastings
      </a>
      when there was a huge explosion, the forward deck blowing apart with enough force to throw the No. 1 funnel into the air, followed by a rush of escaping steam. Scott Russell and two engineers went below and ordered the steam to be blown off and the engine speed reduced. Five stokers died from being scalded by superheated steam, while four or five others were badly injured and one had leapt overboard and had been lost. The accident was discovered to have been caused by a feedwater heater's steam exhaust having been closed, while the explosion's power had been concentrated by the ship's extremely strong bulkheads.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1860.E2.80.9363:_Transatlantic_voyages">
       186063: Transatlantic voyages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 186063: Transatlantic voyages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although designed for the far Eastern run,
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      was only ever used in the Atlantic. A historian of technology comments: "She was designed for the Far Eastern trade, but there was never sufficient traffic to put her into this. Instead, she was used in the transatlantic business, where she could not compete in speed and performance with similar vessels already in service."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage_to_North_America">
       First voyage to North America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: First voyage to North America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2248" data-file-width="2092" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg/220px-SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg/330px-SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg/440px-SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS_Great_Eastern_in_New_York_Harbor_by_Stacy.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Berthed at New York, 1860
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Her first voyage to North America began on 17 June 1860, with 35 paying passengers, eight company "
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deadhead" title="wikt:deadhead">
       dead heads
      </a>
      " (non-paying passengers), and 418 crew. Among the passengers were two journalists, the engineers
      <a href="/wiki/Zerah_Colburn_(locomotive_designer)" title="Zerah Colburn (locomotive designer)">
       Zerah Colburn
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Lyman_Holley" title="Alexander Lyman Holley">
       Alexander Lyman Holley
      </a>
      , and three directors of the Great Ship Company.
     </p>
     <p>
      Preparations were initially made for the ship to sail on 16 June 1860 and the passengers boarded her on the 14th. After visitors had been sent ashore the Captain announced that he would not be sailing until the 17th, as the crew were drunk. Director
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gooch" title="Daniel Gooch">
       Daniel Gooch
      </a>
      , who was travelling aboard her, was not pleased. He was further displeased by the route taken by the ship which was the more southerly of the regular steamer routes as he had wanted the ship to complete the journey in nine days. In the event, the voyage took 10 days 19 hours.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1861_government_charter">
       1861 government charter
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1861 government charter">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Upon
      <i>
       Great Eastern's
      </i>
      return to England, the ship was chartered by the British Government to transport troops to
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
       Quebec
      </a>
      . 2,144 officers and men, 473 women and children, and 200 horses were embarked at Liverpool along with 40 paying passengers. The ship sailed on 25 June 1861 and went at full speed throughout most of the trip arriving at her destination 8 days and 6 hours after leaving Liverpool.
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      stayed for a month and returned to Britain at the beginning of July with 357 paying passengers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although the ship had made around 14,000 on its first Atlantic voyage, the Great Ship Company's finances were in a poor state, with their shares dropping in price. They were also threatened with a lawsuit by the
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Trunk_Railway" title="Grand Trunk Railway">
       Grand Trunk Railway
      </a>
      for not making Portland, Maine the ship's port of call as agreed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Russell" title="John Scott Russell">
       Scott Russell
      </a>
      had been awarded the sum of 18,000 for repairs following the 1859 explosion. The Company managed to appeal against this but Scott Russell applied to the courts and the Chief Justice found in his favour. The company appealed again, and due to rumours that the ship was about to leave the country, Russell's solicitors took possession of her. The Great Ship Company lost its appeal and had to pay the sum awarded; to cover this and the expenses of a second US voyage, they raised 35,000 using
      <a href="/wiki/Debenture" title="Debenture">
       debentures
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Second_voyage_to_North_America">
       Second voyage to North America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Second voyage to North America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Only 100 passengers booked for the second voyage, which was originally scheduled to depart
      <a href="/wiki/Milford_Haven" title="Milford Haven">
       Milford Haven
      </a>
      on 1 May 1861. However, the boat taking the passengers to the ship ran aground and they and their luggage had to be rescued by small boats.
     </p>
     <p>
      The voyage took 9 days 13 hours.
      <i>
       Great Eastern's
      </i>
      arrival in New York was virtually unnoticed, due to the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      and when it was opened to the public at 25 cents a time there was little interest. 194 passengers sailed on the return journey on 25 May and 5,000 tons of wheat was also carried.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Third_voyage_to_North_America">
       Third voyage to North America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Third voyage to North America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      sailed from Liverpool on Tuesday 10 September 1861, commanded by Captain James Walker. On her second day out the wind increased to
      <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">
       gale
      </a>
      force, causing the ship to roll heavily. The port paddle wheel was completely lost, and the starboard paddle wheel smashed to pieces when one of the lifeboats broke loose. At the same time it was discovered that the
      <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">
       cast iron
      </a>
      rudder post, which was 11 inches (280mm) in diameter, had sheared off 2ft (0.61m) above its collar and the rudder was swinging free and hitting the screw, which was slowly breaking it up.
     </p>
     <p>
      Captain Walker ordered his officers to say nothing to the passengers concerning the situation, then had a
      <a href="/wiki/Trysail" title="Trysail">
       trysail
      </a>
      hoisted which was immediately ripped apart by the wind. He then had a four-ton spar thrown overboard secured with a hawser to try to bring some control to the ship, but it only worked for a short while before being torn away.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the end of the second day some of the passengers had an idea as to the predicament they were in and formed a committee chaired by Liverpool shipping merchant George Oakwood. The captain agreed to meet Oakwood and allowed him to inspect the ship. What he found was far worse than had been expected: none of the cargo had been stowed properly and it was all rolling loose in the holds. Hamilton E. Towle, an American
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">
       civil engineer
      </a>
      , who was returning to the states after completing his contract working as a supervising engineer on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">
       Danube River
      </a>
      dry-docks in Austria, visited the rudder room and after inspecting the damage came up with a plan to regain control of the rudder.
     </p>
     <p>
      Towle's scheme was taken to the captain, who failed to act on it. In the evening of the third day
      <i>
       Magnet
      </i>
      , a brig from
      <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">
       Nova Scotia
      </a>
      , appeared on the scene. Captain Walker asked her Captain if he would stand by. He agreed, but it turned out there was little he could do and after several hours the brig left, later succeeding in a claim for
      <a href="/wiki/Demurrage" title="Demurrage">
       demurrage
      </a>
      from the Great Ship Company for the delay.
     </p>
     <p>
      Towle now presented his plan to the passengers' committee and in turn they pressured the captain into letting him try it. Towle had a 100ft (30m) chain composed of 60lb (27kg) links wound around the rudder post below the break, then secured the ends of the chain to the port and starboard frames of the ship using block and tackle. Two lighter chains were led down from the wheelhouse and attached to the heavy chain and also to the ship's frames. This allowed some limited movement of the rudder and the ship became steerable again.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On morning of Sunday 15 September the storm finally abated. Towle and the passengers committee insisted that the Captain try the repaired rudder and eventually the engines were started and at 5 pm that day after 75 hours of drifting out of control the ship answered the helm and was turned on to a course towards Ireland, 300mi (480km) away.
     </p>
     <p>
      On arrival at
      <a href="/wiki/Cobh" title="Cobh">
       Queenstown
      </a>
      the harbourmaster refused to let the ship enter because she was not under full control and injured passengers were taken off by boats. The ship had to stand off for three days until she was towed in by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Advice_(1837)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HMS Advice (1837) (page does not exist)">
       HMS
       <i>
        Advice
       </i>
      </a>
      . Arrangements for temporary repairs were begun and the passengers were offered free transport to the US aboard other ships. Once the repairs were completed the ship sailed to
      <a href="/wiki/Milford_Haven" title="Milford Haven">
       Milford Haven
      </a>
      where permanent repairs were to be carried out. Smaller 50ft (15m) diameter paddlewheels were fitted, and improvements were made to the steering.
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon arriving in the US, Towle filed a claim for $100,000 under the laws of salvage, claiming that his efforts had saved the ship. The case was taken to court, and he was awarded the sum of $15,000, quite a considerable sum for that period. The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">
        Scientific American
       </a>
      </i>
      published an account of the incident and a description of Towle's device.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is uncertain if Towle ever received any of the money awarded to him by the court.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1862_voyages">
       1862 voyages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 1862 voyages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      sailed from
      <a href="/wiki/Milford_Haven" title="Milford Haven">
       Milford Haven
      </a>
      on 7 May 1862 with 138 passengers, arriving in New York on 17 May. The ship was opened to visitors and around 3,000 a day took the opportunity. The return journey to Liverpool was profitable, with 389 passengers travelling along with 3,000 tons of freight. The west-to-east trip took 9 days 12 hours, a reduction of 12 hours on her previous record.
     </p>
     <p>
      The second voyage of 1862 saw the ship arriving in New York on 11 July with 376 passengers including the President of
      <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">
       Liberia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Jenkins_Roberts" title="Joseph Jenkins Roberts">
       J. J. Roberts
      </a>
      . The return journey later that month carried 500 passengers and 8,000 tons of cargo, the ship arriving at Liverpool on 7 August.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Eastern_Rock_incident">
       Great Eastern Rock incident
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Great Eastern Rock incident">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      left Liverpool on the 17th of August with 1,530 passengers on board and a substantial amount of freight which increased her draught to 30ft (9.1m). The ship encountered a
      <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">
       gale
      </a>
      but the captain maintained full speed and the ship arrived off
      <a href="/wiki/Montauk_Point_State_Park" title="Montauk Point State Park">
       Montauk Point
      </a>
      , New York at midnight on the 27th of August.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Not wishing to enter
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Bay" title="New York Bay">
       New York Bay
      </a>
      over
      <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook" title="Sandy Hook">
       Sandy Hook
      </a>
      bar with the ship's deep draught, the captain decided to steam up
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Sound" title="Long Island Sound">
       Long Island Sound
      </a>
      and moor at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flushing_Bay,_New_York" title="Flushing Bay, New York">
       Flushing Bay
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Maritime_pilot" title="Maritime pilot">
       pilot
      </a>
      came on board at 1:30 am and the ship moved slowly ahead. At about 2:00 am 1 mile (2km) east of
      <a href="/wiki/Montauk,_New_York" title="Montauk, New York">
       Montauk
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">
       Long Island
      </a>
      a rumble was heard and the ship heeled slightly. The pilot said she had probably rubbed against the "North east Ripps" (later renamed "Great Eastern Rock"). The captain sent an officer down to check for damage and he reported no leaks. The ship had a list to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_(nautical)" title="Port (nautical)">
       port
      </a>
      , but she made her way into New York the next day under her own steam. Nobody was hurt, indeed the passengers never even knew what had happened.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It was discovered that the rock had opened a gash in the ship's outer hull over 9 feet (2.7m) wide and 83 feet (25m) long, perhaps 60 times the area of the
      <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">
       RMS
       <i>
        Titanic
       </i>
      </a>
      's damage. The enormous size of
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      precluded the use of any drydock repair facility in the US, and the brothers Henry and
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_S._Renwick" title="Edward S. Renwick">
       Edward S. Renwick
      </a>
      devised a daring plan to build a watertight, 104 feet (32m) by 15 feet (4.6m)
      <a href="/wiki/Caisson_(engineering)" title="Caisson (engineering)">
       caisson
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to cover the gash, held in place by chains around the ship's hull. The brothers claimed that it would take two weeks to complete the repairs and said that they would only take payment if successful. However, the demands of the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      caused delays in getting the iron plates required, and instead of two weeks the repairs took three months at a cost to the company of 70,000. The ship finally sailed from New York for Liverpool on the 6th of January 1863.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 2007, the recovery of a 6,500-pound (2.9t) anchor in 70 feet (21m) of water about 4 miles (6.4km) from the rock has stirred speculation that it may have belonged to the
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1863_voyages">
       1863 voyages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 1863 voyages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="463" data-file-width="600" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg/220px-Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg/330px-Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg/440px-Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deck_of_the_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The deck of
        <i>
         Great Eastern
        </i>
        in 1865
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1863
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      made three voyages to New York, with 2,700 passengers being carried out and 970 back to Britain along with a large tonnage of cargo. One of her paddle wheels was damaged on the last outward trip and she completed it using her screw, while on the return journey she ran down and damaged
      <i>
       Jane
      </i>
      , a small sailing ship. The company lost nearly 20,000 on the voyages due to a price war between the
      <a href="/wiki/Cunard_Line" title="Cunard Line">
       Cunard
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Inman_Line" title="Inman Line">
       Inman
      </a>
      shipping lines, and ended up with debts of more than 142,000, which forced them to lay up
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     </div>
     <p>
      A plan was mooted to offer the ship in a lottery, which came to nothing, and the ship was finally offered for sale on 14 January 1864 at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverpool_Exchange" title="Liverpool Exchange">
       Liverpool Exchange
      </a>
      , the bidding opening at 50,000. No bids were offered and the ship was withdrawn from sale, the auctioneer declaring that it would be offered for sale with no reserve in three weeks' time.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile,
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gooch" title="Daniel Gooch">
       Daniel Gooch
      </a>
      approached
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Brassey" title="Thomas Brassey">
       Thomas Brassey
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Pender" title="John Pender">
       John Pender
      </a>
      to see if they would be willing to assist in the purchase of
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      . The opening bid at the auction was 20,000 and John Yates who was acting for Gooch secured the ship for a bid of 25,000, despite the ship being worth 100,000 in materials alone.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The three men set up a new company, the Great Eastern Steamship Company, and
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      was chartered to the newly formed Telegraph Construction and Maintenance Company for 50,000 of shares, and would be responsible for carrying out the necessary conversion work for the ship's new role, laying the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Cable" title="Atlantic Cable">
       Atlantic Cable
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cable_laying">
       Cable laying
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Cable laying">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="600" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg/220px-Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg/330px-Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg/440px-Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cable_Laying_Machinery_on_SS_%27Great_Eastern%27.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Cable laying machinery installed on
        <i>
         Great Eastern
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The conversion work for
      <i>
       Great Eastern's
      </i>
      new role consisted in the removal of funnel no. 4 and some boilers as well as great parts of the passenger rooms and saloons to give way for open top tanks for taking up the coiled cable. Under
      <a href="/wiki/Sir_James_Anderson" title="Sir James Anderson">
       Sir James Anderson
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      she laid 4,200 kilometres (2,600mi) of the 1865
      <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">
       transatlantic telegraph cable
      </a>
      . Under Captains Anderson and then
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Halpin" title="Robert Halpin">
       Robert Halpin
      </a>
      , from 1866 to 1878 the ship laid over 48,000 kilometres (30,000mi) of submarine telegraph cable including from
      <a href="/wiki/Brest,_France" title="Brest, France">
       Brest, France
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint Pierre and Miquelon">
       Saint Pierre and Miquelon
      </a>
      in 1869, and from
      <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">
       Aden
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombay" title="Bombay">
       Bombay
      </a>
      in 1869 and 1870.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Break_up">
       Break up
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Great_Eastern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Break up">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="613" data-file-width="935" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg/220px-Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg/330px-Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg/440px-Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_Eastern_SLV_AllanGreen.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Great Eastern
        </i>
        beached for breaking up
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      At the end of her cable-laying career she was refitted once again as a liner but once again efforts to make her a commercial success failed. She was used as a showboat, a floating palace/concert hall and gymnasium. She acted as an advertising hoardingsailing up and down the Mersey for
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis%27s" title="Lewis's">
       Lewis's
      </a>
      Department Store, who at this point were her owners, before being sold. The idea was to attract people to the store by using her as a floating visitor attraction. She was sold piecemeal at auction in 1888.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An early example of breaking-up a structure by use of a
      <a href="/wiki/Wrecking_ball" title="Wrecking ball">
       wrecking ball
      </a>
      , she was scrapped at
      <a href="/wiki/New_Ferry" title="New Ferry">
       New Ferry
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">
       River Mersey
      </a>
      by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Bath_%26_Son_Ltd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Bath &amp; Son Ltd (page does not exist)">
       Henry Bath &amp; Son Ltd
      </a>
      in 18891890  it took 18 months to take her apart. At the time
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverpool_Football_Club" title="Liverpool Football Club">
       Liverpool Football Club
      </a>
      were looking for a flagpole for their
      <a href="/wiki/Anfield" title="Anfield">
       Anfield
      </a>
      ground, and consequently purchased her top mast. It still stands there today, at the Kop end.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2012, the
      <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
       Channel 4
      </a>
      programme
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Team" title="Time Team">
        Time Team
       </a>
      </i>
      found
      <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_survey_(archaeology)" title="Geophysical survey (archaeology)">
       geophysical survey
      </a>
      evidence to suggest that residual ironworks from the ship's keel and lower structure still reside in the foreshore.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is rumoured that a
      <a href="/wiki/Human_skeleton" title="Human skeleton">
       human skeleton
      </a>
      was found inside
      <i>
       Great Eastern'
      </i>
      s double hull, a rumour that forms the topic of
      <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">
       Sting
      </a>
      's song "Ballad of the Great Eastern." However, the same thing has been said of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">
        RMS Titanic
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Dam" title="Hoover Dam">
       Hoover Dam
      </a>
      (among others), and inspection hatches in the inner hull would have provided an easy escape. The ship was the subject of one programme in the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      documentary series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Seven_Wonders_of_the_Industrial_World" title="Seven Wonders of the Industrial World">
        Seven Wonders of the Industrial World
       </a>
      </i>
      that repeated the tale about two dead bodies in the hull, including a child worker, although stated it as a rumour. An episode of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Haunted_History_(1998_TV_series)" title="Haunted History (1998 TV series)">
        Haunted History
       </a>
      </i>
      implied that the find of the skeleton was indeed factual. One of the narrators of the segment read an article published from the time when
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      was being dismantled. The article stated that the workers broke into a compartment in the inner shell on the port side, and did find a skeleton.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Harold_T._Wilkins" title="Harold T. Wilkins">
       Harold T. Wilkins
      </a>
      in his book
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Strange_Mysteries_of_Time_and_Space&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Strange Mysteries of Time and Space (page does not exist)">
        Strange Mysteries of Time and Space
       </a>
      </i>
      said, in chapter 9, that no skeleton was found.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During 1859, when
      <i>
       Great Eastern
      </i>
      was off Portland conducting trials, an explosion aboard blew off one of the funnels. The funnel was salvaged and subsequently purchased by the water company supplying Weymouth and Melcombe Regis in Dorset, U.K., and used as a filtering device. It was later transferred to the
      <a href="/wiki/M_Shed" title="M Shed">
       Bristol Maritime Museum
      </a>
      close to Brunel's
      <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Britain" title="SS Great Britain">
       SS
       <i>
        Great Britain
       </i>
      </a>
      but has recently been moved to the
      <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Britain" title="SS Great Britain">
       SS
       <i>
        Great Britain
       </i>
      </a>
      museum itself and is displayed there. An original piece of furniture from the ship is in the museum store.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
