     <section>
      <p>
       <a href="http://goo.gl/6j5O5g">
        Book review
       </a>
       of The Strange Case of the Rickety Cossack by Ian Tattersall, with author interview.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tattersall is curator emeritus of anthropology at the American Museum of Natural History, where he was the long-time chief curator of anthropology and lead designer of its spectacular halls of human origins and human biology.
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Tattersall recounts a lifetime of his observations regarding the development of the field of paleoanthropology. It wasnt always pretty, but it sure makes for a great book. This is a MUST-READ for students of anthropology, paleontology, and anyone with an interest in human evolution.
      </p>
      <p>
       The book was published this month by St. Martins Press, of Palgrave Macmillan, Nature Publishing Group.
      </p>

      </time>
      <h1>
       <a href="http://nathanlents.tumblr.com/post/117806722682/the-pace-of-biomedical-research" rel="bookmark">
        The pace of biomedical research
       </a>
      </h1>
     </header>
     <section>
      <p>
       True story: I am now paying a bill for some next-gen metagenomic DNA sequencing. Its about $2,000. This technique is, no exaggeration, 100-1000 TIMES more powerful than the state-of-the-art technique I used while I was a postdoc ten years ago for the same type of work. At that time, a run of this size would have cost around $20,000. Thats $20k for 1% of the data that we now get for $2k. Plus, its better data because its far less biased by what we already know. Of course, ten years ago, we thought that was an enormous bargain because it was 100x more powerful, at 1/10th cost of what was available ten years before THAT. That, my friends, is how fast things are developing in the world of biomedical research and why I remain intensely optimistic about what research will bring us in the next few decades. SCIENCE!
      </p>
     </section>
