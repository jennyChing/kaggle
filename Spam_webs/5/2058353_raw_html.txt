               Note that these studies were published as abstracts and presented at a conference. These data and conclusions should be considered to be preliminary until published in a peer-reviewed journal.
              </li>
              <li>
               Compared with other veterans with serious mental illness, patients with serious mental illness in methadone maintenance treatment nationally in the Veterans' Health Administration are more likely to have both liver disease and HIV and to fill psychiatric medications prescriptions.

           AVENTURA, Fla.  --  A tobacco treatment program in a psychiatric ward for inpatients with comorbid mental health and substance abuse disorders led to lower rates of alcohol, tobacco, and cannabis use, researchers reported at the
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/MeetingCoverage/AAAP/" target="_blank">
            American Academy of Addiction Psychiatry (AAAP) annual meeting
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Also reported at AAAP, research on veterans in methadone maintenance therapy with comorbid conditions and their use of health services, and the impact of smoking on adults with depressive disorders.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Smoking and Psych Patients
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Compared with patients in a usual care group, patients in a tobacco cessation group were twice as likely to stay off tobacco 1 year later (22% versus 11%), and have lower rates of drinking (22% versus 58%) and cannabis use (18% versus 42%), reported
           <a href="http://www.researchgate.net/profile/Smita_Das3" target="_blank">
            Smita Das, MD, PhD, MPH
           </a>
           , of Stanford University School of Medicine, and colleagues.
          </p>
          <p>
           For the randomized, controlled trial, 216 inpatients from two locked, acute psychiatry units in the San Francisco Bay area were split into a treatment group and a usual care group. The patient group was majority male (66%) and diverse racially and ethnically (36% Caucasian). They reported smoking an average of 19 cigarettes per day.
          </p>
          <p>
           Intention to quit was not a prerequisite for study entry; however,
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/PrimaryCare/Smoking/48838" target="_blank">
            both of the psychiatry units were smoke-free establishments
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           The tobacco treatment program included a computer program, counseling, and nicotine replacement therapy.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Even though the usual care group was considered the control, this group still had access to nicotine replacement therapy and was given brief advice to quit as per facility protocol.
          </p>
          <p>
           The characteristics of the members of the treatment arm and the usual care groups, including intent to quit, were identical at baseline.
          </p>
          <p>
           After 12 months, 22% of the patients in the intervention group remained tobacco abstinent compared with 11% of the usual care group (risk ratio 2.01, 95% CI 1.05-3.83,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.03).
          </p>
          <p>
           Reports of drug or alcohol use were similar between the groups after 12 months (80.6% for the intervention group versus 75.7% for the usual care group, RR 1.07, CI 95% 0.89-1.27).
          </p>
          <p>
           Analysis for secondary outcomes showed that patients who quit smoking with the intervention program were less likely to drink alcohol (22% versus 58%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.002), or use cannabis (18% versus 42%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.027) compared with the usual care group in the past 12 months.
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "The takeaway of this is that we can treat tobacco [addiction] in psychiatric inpatients who have substance use disorders. In this sample and analysis, there was no worsening of use disorders and, in fact, of those who quit smoking, they were more likely to have lower rates of alcohol and cannabis use," Das said in her presentation.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Smoking and Depression
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           In another study, Roopali Parikh, MD, of
           <a href="http://larkinhospital.com/site/" target="_blank">
            Larkin Community Hospital
           </a>
           in Miami, and colleagues presented data on the impact of smoking on adults with depressive disorders.
          </p>
          <p>
           The authors looked at 2008 data collected by the CDC in their Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance Survey and identified 4,522 patients who provided a smoking history and had been diagnosed with a depressive disorder.
          </p>
          <p>
           Of this cohort, 2,053 patients (45%) said they were smokers. Compared with the nonsmokers, smokers tended to be younger (age 48 versus 57,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.001), female (72% versus 68%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.008), Hispanic (5% versus 3%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.001), and unmarried (63% versus 50%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.001).
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The authors found that the smokers also reported lower annual incomes, were less likely to have health insurance, and reported more financial barriers to medical care compared with nonsmokers (32% versus 15%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.001).
          </p>
          <p>
           Although smokers and nonsmokers
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/Cardiology/AcuteCoronarySyndrome/48555" target="_blank">
            did not show differences in rates of prior heart attack or stroke
           </a>
           , smokers did report lower rates of diabetes (14% versus 18%,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.001).
          </p>
          <p>
           The researchers looked at the prevalence of certain mental health symptoms over the course of a 14-day period and found that smokers reported more frequent symptoms compared with nonsmokers in the following categories:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Depressed mood: 4.8 days versus 3.0 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
           <li>
            Changes in sleep patterns: 7.1 days versus 5.1 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
           <li>
            Low energy: 8.0 days versus 6.2 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
           <li>
            Appetite changes: 5.3 days versus 3.8 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
           <li>
            Hopelessness: 3.8 days versus 2.3 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
           <li>
            Trouble concentrating: 3.8 days versus 2.4 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
           <li>
            Lack of interest: 4.7 days versus 2.9 days (
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            &lt;0.001)
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           "In adults diagnosed with a depressive disorder, smoking is associated with more frequent mental health symptoms," the authors wrote. "Smoking cessation counseling and therapy may be warranted in individuals diagnosed with depressive disorders."
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Services Utilization by Veterans With Serious Mental Illness
           </strong>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           In another study presented at the meeting, researchers looked at whether veterans in certain serious health groups were taking advantage of Veterans Affairs resources.
          </p>
          <p>
           Out of more than 10,000 military veterans in methadone maintenance therapy, 33.2% also had a
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/nursing/Nursing/47918" target="_blank">
            comorbid serious mental illness and they used available services
           </a>
           , as well as filled their prescriptions on a routine basis, despite the perception that there is a general lack of compliance among these patients, said
           <a href="http://medicine.yale.edu/psychiatry/people/carla_marienfeld.profile" target="_blank">
            Carla Marienfeld, MD
           </a>
           , and
           <a href="http://medicine.yale.edu/psychiatry/people/robert_rosenheck.profile" target="_blank">
            Robert Rosenheck, MD
           </a>
           , both of Yale University.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Patients who are dually diagnosed with both serious mental illness and substance use disorders are often perceived to have greater difficulty attending appointments and taking their medication," Marienfeld said. "There's a stigma that exists around these patients that they don't actually use very much service, that they're hard to get to come in, they don't follow through with what they're supposed to do."
          </p>
          <p>
           The study results show these assumptions "may not be the case," she noted. "This could imply that the structure of methadone maintenance therapy leads to improved adherence to medications prescribed and possibly improved health outcomes."
          </p>
          <p>
           However, the results do raise concerns about drug-drug interactions among methadone and psychiatric medications. "Further study and clinical awareness of potential drug-drug interactions in this high medication and service using population are needed," the authors cautioned.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Using data from the 2012 records of the Veterans' Health Administration (VA), Marienfeld and colleagues looked at patients in methadone maintenance treatment, patients with a serious mental illness, and patients who fell into both categories.
          </p>
          <p>
           Marienfeld and colleagues defined serious mental illness as schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or major affective disorder.
          </p>
          <p>
           They found that according to the 2012 records, 510,722 veterans had bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, or a major affective disorder, and 10,001 veterans were in methadone maintenance therapy.
          </p>
          <p>
           Only 0.65% of patients with a serious mental illness were also on methadone maintenance therapy. However, 33.2% of patients in methadone maintenance therapy also had a serious mental illness.
          </p>
          <p>
           Compared with methadone maintenance therapy patients who didn't have a serious mental illness, methadone maintenance patients who did have a serious mental illness were more likely to:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Have lower incomes
           </li>
           <li>
            Have been homeless
           </li>
           <li>
            Have higher rates of comorbid psychiatric diagnoses
           </li>
           <li>
            Have had a recent psychiatric hospitalization
           </li>
           <li>
            Use more outpatient substance use services
           </li>
           <li>
            Have had more outpatient and emergency department visits
           </li>
           <li>
            Had more fills for psychotropic drugs
           </li>
          </ul>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Compared with other patients with serious mental illness, methadone maintenance therapy patients with serious mental illness were more likely to:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Be diagnosed with both HIV and liver disease (data coding did not distinguish hepatitis C)
           </li>
           <li>
            Fill psychiatric medications
           </li>
           <li>
            Use outpatient substance use services
           </li>
           <li>
            Use outpatient psychiatric services
           </li>
           <li>
            Have had a mental health inpatient admission or emergency department visit
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           According to Marienfeld, significant predictors of having a serious mental illness while in methadone maintenance therapy included social factors such as homelessness and VA service connection benefits status, psychiatric co-morbidity, VA service use such as having a mental health inpatient admission, and greater psychiatric medication usage.
          </p>
          <p>
           "However, factors associated with being in methadone maintenance therapy among patients diagnosed with serious mental illness are somewhat different, the most prominent of which, not surprisingly, is having a drug use diagnosis," she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Interestingly, having a mental health inpatient admission predicts both having serious mental illness in methadone maintenance therapy and being in methadone maintenance therapy if you have serious mental illness," Marienfeld added.
          </p>
