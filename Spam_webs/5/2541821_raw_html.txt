        <h1 class="entry-title">
         An Epic Mining Boomand We Blew it
        </h1>
       </header>
       <div class="row row-meta">
        <span class="col-md-6 author vcard">

        <p>
         Your editor had  a rough night last night. His immune system succumbed to a devilish little  kiddie bug. We were up doing the old heave ho and now feel worse for wear.
        </p>
        <p>
         We were going  to discuss Australias economic growth numbers, which came out yesterday. They  were fairly tepid at 0.6% for the September quarter and 2.3% over the year.
        </p>
        <p>
         But thats  measuring economic growth in terms of production volumes. When you add in the  effect of changing import and export prices (i.e. the change in the terms of  trade) growth fell 0.6% in the quarter and was up only 0.6% over the past 12  months.
        </p>
        <p>
         Falling  commodity prices were only a part of the reason why the terms of trade fell.  After all, the price of our most important export, iron ore, continues to defy  gravity.
        </p>
        <p>
         Import prices  rose across the board, even though volumes remained flat for the quarter. In  other words, the weaker currency looks like its leading to a rise in import  prices, which is hurting our terms of trade and national income growth.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Aussie fell  further overnight on fears of
         <a href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/category/banks-1/US-federal-reserve/" title="more on the Federal Reserve">
          Federal Reserve
         </a>
         tapering. Its trading around 90  cents. If this keeps up expect to see more weakness in the terms of trade and  contracting national income next year.
        </p>
        <p>
         In fact, its a  case our mate Dan Denning has been making for some time now. Hes expecting
         <a href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/category/australian-economy-1/" title="more on the Australian Economy">
          Australias economy
         </a>
         to contract next year. The first recession in 20 plus  years.
        </p>
        <p>
         To explain why,  well hand todays
         <em>
          Daily Reckoning
         </em>
         over to Danand go back and lie down
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h2>
         <center>
          <u>
           An Epic Boomand We Blew it
          </u>
         </center>
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         By
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/u/2/117920965127634763555/about" target="_blank">
          Dan Denning
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Australia had a  10 year
         <strong>
          mining boom
         </strong>
         and forgot to get rich. Worse still
        </p>
        <p>
         We came out of  the biggest boom in the history of the world in a WORSE financial position than  when we went into it.
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres what I  mean
        </p>
        <p>
         1.We had a historic increase in national  income thanks to a record terms-of-trade. Rivers of cash flowed into Australian  coffers.
        </p>
        <p>
         2.And yet the government and households  managed to pile on record amounts of debt
        </p>
        <p>
         In other words,  weve come out of the boom in MORE DEBT than when we went in to it.
        </p>
        <p>
         You might think  thats impossible, but thats exactly whats happened.
        </p>
        <p>
         Check out the  graph below from Melbournes Grattan Institute
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <a href="http://www.portphillippublishing.com.au/DR20131205a.jpg">
          <img alt="a" border="0" height="210" src="http://www.portphillippublishing.com.au/DR20131205a.jpg" width="425"/>
         </a>
         <br/>
         <a href="http://www.portphillippublishing.com.au/DR20131205a.jpg" target="_blank">
          <em>
           click to enlarge
          </em>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         The Institute  reckons that between 2002 and 2012, Australia earned over $190 billion in  national income, thanks to a record terms-of-trade.
        </p>
        <p>
         Put simply,  Australia got record amounts of income for selling
         <a href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/category/iron-ore-1/" title="more on iron ore">
          iron ore
         </a>
         , coal, and
         <a href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/category/precious-metals-gold/" title="more on gold">
          gold
         </a>
         overseas. Meanwhile, the price of imports  the things you pay for  went down.
        </p>
        <p>
         See how that  works?
        </p>
        <p>
         You get more for  what you sell and you pay less for what you buy. You SHOULD get rich. But  somehow, many Australians forgot to.
        </p>
        <p>
         This river of  cash lifted some prices  especially house prices.
        </p>
        <p>
         But the  government still managed to blow it.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Grattan Institute  study concludes that almost all the rise in Australias national income (thanks  to the
         <strong>
          resources boom
         </strong>
         ) was squandered with government spending and tax cuts by  the Howard, Rudd and Gillard governments.
        </p>
        <p>
         In other words,  all the money we got from selling coal and iron ore to China has already been  SPENTand all were left with is higher personal debts and bigger government  deficits.
        </p>
        <p>
         If youre still  unconvinced, you only need to take one look at whats happening on the  ground
        </p>
        <p>
         
         <strong>
          INVESTMENT  BANKS ARE GETTING OUT OF COMMODITIES
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         JP Morgan Chase  is one of the biggest traders of oil, industrial metals, and coal on Wall  Street. But in August it quit the physical commodities business altogether.
        </p>
        <p>
         Only three years  ago it bought trading house RBS Sempra Commodities for $1.6 billion. Today,  theyve written off every last cent.
        </p>
        <p>
         
         <strong>
          RESOURCE-BASED  BUSINESSES ARE GOING BUST
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Take Andrew  Howard as a typical example
        </p>
        <p>
         For the last 10  years his business was buying and selling jumbo-sized earthmoving equipment  from his base in the northeast coast city of Mackay  home to the largest
         <strong>
          mining
         </strong>
         service centre in Australias richest coal country, the Bowen Basin.
        </p>
        <p>
         At the height of  the global financial crisis, Andrew travelled to the United States to buy up  machinery cheap in a depressed economyand later sold it to a resurgent
         <strong>
          Australian mining industry
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         But after a  decade of tracking the rise and fall of Australias mineral boom, hes shut the  doors of his business AFG Equipment for good.
        </p>
        <p>
         
         <em>
          Theres just absolutely nothing happening,
         </em>
           he says. 
         <em>
          Were just treading water.
         </em>
         
        </p>
        <p>
         
         <strong>
          BILLIONS  IN INVESTMENT WRITTEN DOWN EVERY WEEK
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In July, OZ  Minerals took a $240 million write-down in its assets after gold and copper  prices plummeted in the second quarter
        </p>
        <p>
         Gold producer  Evolution Mining followed with a write-down of the value of its assets of $400  million for the fiscal year
        </p>
        <p>
         And gold miners  including Newmont, Goldcorp and Newcrest had to write-down $12.6 billion  against the value of their assets
        </p>
        <p>
         How you can you  possibly go back to normal after all this?
        </p>
        <p>
         You cant.
        </p>
        <p>
         Theres no going  back when youve blown an opportunity. All you can do is look ahead and make  the best of it.
        </p>
        <p>
         By the way, you  might be surprised to know I think the best could be very good indeed.
        </p>
        <p>
         Despite the obvious  cracks I see in the economy, I also believe its one of the most exciting times  to be an investor in Australia.
        </p>
        <p>
         Do the right  things with your money you will do really well.
        </p>
        <p>
         The truth is a  lot of miners thought commodity prices would never fall. They invested money in  projects that would only be profitable with permanently high commodity prices.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now, reality has  hit home.
        </p>
        <p>
         So whats likely  to happen?
         <a href="http://pro1.portphillippublishing.com.au/169051/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Find out  here
         </a>
         .
        </p>
