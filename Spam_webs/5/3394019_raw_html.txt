        The Barcode Turns 61
       </h1>

        <p dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-1cfe801d-cf22-dd91-3475-833fe81feb87">
         <img alt="barcode" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-13463" height="179" src="https://contentequalsmoney.com/wp-content/uploads/media/2013/12/barcode-300x179.png" width="300"/>
         The barcode has turned 61! This important and much-overlooked little aspect of business has now been around since 1952  and has evolved and changed in a lot of different ways over the years. Lets take a closer look.
        </p>
        <h3 dir="ltr">
         The Barcodes History
        </h3>
        <p dir="ltr">
         The worlds first barcode was created in 1952 by Joseph Woodland and Bernard Silver, whose aim was to help speed along the checkout process at supermarkets, an industry that was growing more quickly than business owners could inventory.
         <a href="http://www.waspbarcode.com/buzz/60th-anniversary-of-the-barcode/">
          Early barcodes were round
         </a>
         and had a bulls-eye design, while the first barcode utilizing the straight lines we know today had just four vertical bars. Woodland and Silver knew that the barcode could be used to help classify different items for sale, but had no idea that their tracking system would pay off so enormously.
        </p>
        <h3 dir="ltr">
         Contemporary Uses
        </h3>
        <p dir="ltr">
         The modern UPC code was first used in 1974 on a pack of chewing gum, and is now the standard all over the country and in many other parts of the world. As a tracking and classification system, the barcode is also used for scientific research, where researchers attach tiny barcodes on bees to track their mating habits. Additionally, the U.S. military uses two-foot-long barcodes to differentiate ships in storage, and hospital patients have barcode ID bracelets for easier, more accessible care.
        </p>
        <p dir="ltr">
         Barcodes have continued to change, however. Many companies are now using
         <a href="https://contentequalsmoney.com/vanity-barcodes-for-branding/">
          vanity barcodes
         </a>
         , creating a fun new spin on the old classification system. Other fun updates include the
         <a href="http://thebostonfoodie.blogspot.com/2011/01/first-for-boston.html">
          edible barcode
         </a>
         , which is printed on foods with squid ink. Other barcodes include the recycling barcode, which targets those who may not otherwise recycle by providing an easy way to figure out just how much their can and bottle deposits are worth.
        </p>
        <p dir="ltr">
         Of course, in todays mobile world, there is no way to discuss the barcode without mentioning the QR code. Additionally,
         <a href="http://www.waspbarcode.com/buzz/tech-tools-5-smart-phone-barcode-scanner-apps/" target="_blank">
          barcode scanner apps
         </a>
         are now everywhere, an innovative way to compare prices or check the nutrition facts of a particular food item.
        </p>
        <h3 dir="ltr">
         The Takeaway
        </h3>
        <p dir="ltr">
         Of course, even if your business is not a brick-and-mortar affair, barcodes are still an enormous part of the mobile world. Additionally, there is definitely something to be said for the level of innovation and creativity that businesses are implementing with their uses of the barcode. From the graphic design elements of the vanity barcode to the incentive for environmentalism provided by the recycling barcode, there are a number of ways to use this fun little aspect of business.
        </p>
        <p dir="ltr">
         <em>
          What do you think of the barcodes history? How might you get creative with the barcode at your business?
         </em>
        </p>
