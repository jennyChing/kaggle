              <h1 class="story-title entry-title" itemprop="name headline">
               The Job That The Middle East Offered Saved My Life From Mortgage!
              </h1>
              <div class="relative" id="right-content">
               <div class="left relative" id="content-area" itemprop="articleBody">
                <div class="hide-title">
                 <span class="entry-title" itemprop="name headline">
                  The Job That The Middle East Offered Saved My Life From Mortgage!
                 </span>
                </div>
                <div id="left-content-mobi">
                 <div class="author-img left">
                  <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-120 photo" height="120" src="//2.gravatar.com/avatar/8a399f0c9f0e27a08bd8758445b10d2e?s=120&amp;d=mm&amp;r=g" srcset="http://2.gravatar.com/avatar/8a399f0c9f0e27a08bd8758445b10d2e?s=240&amp;d=mm&amp;r=g 2x" width="120"/>
                 </div>
                 <div class="author-info-mob-wrap">
                  <span class="vcard author">
                   <span class="author-name left fn" itemprop="author">
                    <a href="http://businesswolf.org/author/maansi-sharma/" rel="author" title="Posts by Maansi Sharma">
                     Maansi Sharma
                    </a>
                   </span>
                  </span>
                  <div class="post-date-wrap left relative post-date-mob">
                   <span class="post-date">
                    <time class="post-date updated" datetime="2014-12-20" itemprop="datePublished" pubdate="">
                     December 20, 2014
                    </time>
                   </span>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <p>
                 <a href="http://businesswolf.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/i-moved-to-the-middle-east-to-pay-off-70000-of-debt.jpg" rel="nofollow">
                  <img alt="How the Middle East helped me getting out of a $70,000 debt-hole" class="aligncenter wp-image-3623 size-full" height="412" src="http://businesswolf.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/550x412xi-moved-to-the-middle-east-to-pay-off-70000-of-debt.jpg.pagespeed.ic.8vyepaBbJS.jpg" title="" width="550"/>
                 </a>
                </p>
                <p>
                </p>
                <p>
                 Ive been sitting on this particular park bench for over an hour now. My stomach is churned. Im nervous. Then I realised that, there is a call I have to make. I was about to call a credit card company, a credit card company that had been making my life a living hell nonstop for nearly a year and a half now.
                </p>
                <p>
                 I had defaulted payments on one of my cards, actually, my parents had. Now I finally felt that I was ready to put an end to all those calls being diverted to the voicemail.
                </p>
                <p>
                 It was May 2011, and now I feel a bit stupid and silly to have been so petrified. I thought about the lawsuit that was going to be slapped on me by the collection agency, but the call they chose was a civil one. I was politely explained of my options of payment. I had to transfer around $400 every month from the checking account to the cards balance for five months, just to pay off the settled amount of around $2000.
                </p>
                <p>
                 This felt good for a few days, I finally felt relieved about my financial control and that I was acting like a mature and a responsible adult. It didnt take me long to realise that, $2000 was just the tip of the iceberg and that there were debts amounting to $68,000 submerged underwater.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <strong>
                  A family (Financial) obligation
                 </strong>
                </p>
                <p>
                 This may sound as if Im a spoiled kid, but I blame my parents in a lot of ways for all the debt that weve incurred. Nothing regarding this was discussed with me officially, but we had an understanding of sorts that they will bear my living expenses while in college by hook or crook. So, on top of the student loans (which were in my name), they signed me up for many credit cards which enabled me to pay for my textbooks and other expenses. The cards were in my name and my parents paid for them.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Honestly speaking, I didnt realise how much I was spending at school. I never had a job, so translated to having no idea on drafting budgets, dollar value of a days work or significance of savings or spending wisely. The loans I took out for my private university seemed like fun coupons. The thought of me paying it back didnt hit me until I landed my first job that I was expected to pay it all back with interest.
                </p>
                <p>
                 So, as you should see, my credit card charges went beyond what they were initially meant for me. I used them to finance my wanderlust: flights expenses to study overseas, internships in Europe and then finally graduate in the Middle East for a masters degree in Islamic Studies, though I got a full scholarship for my graduate tuitions, I was expected to pay for all my living expenses.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Somewhere deep down, I know I shouldnt be resenting my parents for the debt I am in. After all they were just financing my educational ambitions. But we were all in for a rough ride.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Once while visiting me in the Middle East, the payment on the aforementioned card was defaulted by my mom and she was inundated with high penalty fees. Even though it was her first payment, the card issuing company did not waive the fees.
                </p>
                <p>
                 My mom works as a nursing assistant for a hospital and my dad is an accountant, in simple terms, we werent rich and couldnt afford to pay off the penalty, on top of paying the bills on a bunch of other cards in my name. So, with the tight money flow, she didnt make a big deal out of it and resorted to ignoring the daily collection calls.
                </p>
                <p>
                 She didnt seem concerned or aware of what this was doing to my credit score. Whenever I confronted her about it, she gave me an easy going reply that Im young and itll be wiped off my credit report in seven years. Sadly, I started following this attitude.
                </p>
                <p>
                 After completing grad school, I started hunting a job abroad for almost 4 months, so in a way I was protected from the collection calls but i had to return someday. After getting a position at a non-profit near the Eastern Coast, the collection calls resumed. The influence my mother had on me took effect and I stared ignoring the calls.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <strong>
                  The debt life
                 </strong>
                </p>
                <p>
                 I resented living my life that way. Having some inflow of money did help me in being financially responsible: I drafted a monthly budget, tried cutting down on expenses like the telephone bills and subway rides and refused night outs with my friends.
                </p>
                <p>
                 I also started paying my student loans and took up responsibility for most of my credit cards from my parents. Living a life with absolute zero concept of financial responsibility, I wasnt exactly excited about it  but I also knew that I had no other option. With my sisters booming college expenses and debt, they were relieved to share the debt in whatever way they could. The debt was mine after all.
                </p>
                <p>
                 I was making only $30,000 a year and that was a problem. It just got through my living expenses and refused to move my debt  some $20,000 in cards, and $50,000 in federal and student loans. I could only pay the minimum amount due as I could only afford $100 a month for my savings plan.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Its a good beginning, yes. But when you put the long term goals in the picture  getting married, having kids, buying a house and investing  the entire landscape changed. If that wasnt enough, the defaulted card gave me a credit score in the low 600s which I knew was another time bomb waiting to explode my future. I felt like a fraud.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <strong>
                  Overseas calling
                 </strong>
                </p>
                <p>
                 Summer of 2011 changed everything in my life. Im pondering over writing a song about it. I had been applying a lot of higher paying jobs when I accidently ran into a former colleague who had been following my career for some time now and was impressed with my work. I was offered a teaching position at a university in the Persian Gulf.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Given the situation I was in, Id call this the dream job. This job in addition to taking me to the Arab world gives me the opportunity of teaching in my field of expertise. My base which was more than double of what I was earning put a small smile to my face. And that was not even including all the additional perks Im getting  free rent, transportation, and also a free flight on the move. I was astounded.
                </p>
                <p>
                 With changing financial situations, my top priority was to get out of debt. So, the one-time relocation compensation in the five digits allowed me to pay off majority of my credit cards. I sat down to draft a tight budget that would enable me to pay the remainder of my loans and cards in a year. Dining out, international travel and shopping suffered a huge blow. My new salary coupled up with rent free existence which again was supplemented by controlled spending, I was able to put large sums of money towards my debt-hole at the end of each month  sometimes as high as whopping $4,000.
                </p>
                <p>
                 I did not achieve my one-year goal to pay off the loan. Life was obstructing me from paying it off  weddings, travel (old habits do not die) and the medical expenses. But I made sure that I did not use my previous cards and used only my debit cards for expenses. If at any instant I was forced to use my credit card, I made sure to pay it back as soon as I received my card statement.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <strong>
                  New beginning awaits
                 </strong>
                </p>
                <p>
                 Today, around 900 days after moving here to the Gulf, Im proud to announce, that Im completely debt free  and my credit score has picked up in a very significant manner. Its reached the high 700s. Although, its not the ideal score, at least I wont be running around in panic the day I apply for a mortgage.
                </p>
                <p>
                 According to the experts, in order to keep a very high credit score, you need to use your credit cards. Thats right. You need to use them. That may be true, but I never do not want to put myself in such a vulnerable situation again. I have not cancelled my cards, though, at least not yet  as I know that would reduce my credit history and have a negative impact on my score, but I use them sparingly.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Right now, my goal is to build an emergency fund. In my enthusiasm to pay off my debt- even the good ones like the federal student loan I had which did not require me to pay it off as quickly I did fell wayside. Im working on the emergency fun with about the $3000 I saved.
                </p>
                <p>
                 I feel very lucky at times with the way how things worked out and also in the way they did. I believe its not always luck that helped me, but my perseverance and sheer hard work and strict budgeting. Landing on the job I do right now, helped significantly in absolving my debt, and I sincerely encourage others to expatriate job opportunities in the Middle East. Its evident in the media that living in the region isnt perfect  inequality can be seen everywhere, but for a college-educated American like me, the job, the perks I receive are very much substantial. Not to mention about many opportunities offering tempting packages with free housing and travel allowances.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Right now I can honestly declare that I do not regret my wanderlust behaviour a bit. All those international study and internship opportunities that I paid on borrowed money. Without them, I never would have been typing this. Now, not only Im out of debt, Im also investing in an employer-matched 401(k), not to mention my contribution of $1,000 a month to a private mutual fund. So in addition to the fact that I do not carry any debt now, Ive $64,000 saved for retirement.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The new financial freedom I achieved allows me to sort out some of my parents debt, like an $8,000 parental loan that my dad took out for my college. With this track record, I hope to pay it off completely in about eight months. I, owe it to them for all the financial burden they had to take on me.
                </p>
