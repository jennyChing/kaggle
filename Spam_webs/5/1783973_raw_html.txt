     <div class="columns small-10 small-centered medium-8 bordered-container">
      <p>
       End-user computing is in a state of flux, which gives IT departments a problem when trying to source the best hardware and operating system for their businesses and employees. Many businesses want a
       <a href="http://www.dell.com/learn/uk/en/ukbsdt1/windows-8">
        Windows 7 upgrade
       </a>
       after the death of XP or they may want to transition some applications to Windows 8 for a touch-screen environment. When you couple operating system diversity with the trend of employees bringing their own devices to work, it means IT managers have a host of issues to combat.
      </p>

      <h2>
       Adapting to change and BYOD
      </h2>
      <p>
       Businesses are embracing diversity in IT and have done so for many years. Its common for different departments within businesses to require a different IT set-up.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="http://www.dell.com/learn/us/en/04/virtual-data-center" target="_blank">
        Virtualization
       </a>
       has allowed businesses to use multiple operating systems on one server to accommodate the needs of end users and ensure that the best tools for the jobs are always available. What happens when the best tool for the job is the end-user themselves? Allowing users to use their own devices both at home and at the office, ensures that they can remain available, work longer hours and increase productivity.
      </p>
      <h2>
       The good and the bad of multiple device environments
      </h2>
      <p>
       Striving to give employees high-availability is not without its pitfalls. New security measures are required in order to maintain control of sensitive data and protect your business intellectual property and customer data. Ensuring your business is not at risk from increased vulnerabilities in the name of progression and competitiveness is just good sense.
      </p>
      <p>
       So how do you tackle the problem of multiple-device and platforms for employees who are both office-based and required to be active while mobile? Paul Craddock, who is actively involved in the end-user device market for Dell, discusses this and other common questions as well as offering his views on IT setups in modern businesses.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Question: How should a small business worried about using legacy applications in a modern operating system do?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       Windows XP Support ended in April this year and now we have technically, a choice of two platforms  Windows 7 and Windows 8  and the fundamental interface is different. Windows 8 really is optimized for touch applications and Windows 7, as we know, is non-touch.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now what that means is that organizations are starting to transition some applications across to Windows 8, others are just staying with a Windows 7 solution. Where the applications are not currently available for Windows 8, not all are going to be the right type of application for Windows 8.
      </p>
      <p>
       Where they [the operating systems] are not optimized for those applications, thats where customers are going to have to look for what is available out there to assist them in that transition. As I say, not every application is going to be best utilized in a touch type of environment, but where it could be better utilized, for example, on a tablet or a touch-screen notebook PC, then it is something that needs to be considered. There are services out there which are available to enable people and companies to transition
       <a href="http://www0.cs.ucl.ac.uk/staff/v.perrone/publications/Migrating_to_the_web_a_legacy_application_the%20sinfor_project(WSE02).pdf">
        legacy applications
       </a>
       across to touch environments where needed.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Question: With the growth in tablet use, how are IT managers able to identify the best device for end-users when trying to integrate tablets to the workforce?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its a minefield, isnt it? Gone, are the days when everyone was given the same laptop device or desktop device in order to fulfill ones daily functions. These days there are so many different client devices out there, its more of a challenge for the IT manager to know what is best for his end users.
      </p>
      <p>
       So lets take an example.
      </p>
      <p>
       Youve got a choice of tablets, youve got laptops, youve got desktops, youve got work stations, mobile workstations,
       <a href="http://www.dell.com/learn/us/en/04/virtual-client" target="_blank">
        virtualized workstations
       </a>
       in the data center so which is the right one for my team of end users?
      </p>
      <p>
       Lets take a look at a typical organization. Maybe what we should look at is starting from internal personnel right the way through to the high-end execs. So youve got people with administrative desk-based functions, youve got finance, youve got internal people who are fulfilling marketing and various other roles, Youve got highly-mobile sales people, sales people who are mobile some of the time and youve got other people who fly a lot and are very busy at airports etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       So why would all of those people need to use exactly the same device? I would suggest they dont.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most people can fulfill everything they need to during the day using a desktop PC. They dont need to do anything with it at home so it stays in the office all day. Youve then got some other users who need to work sometimes in the office and sometimes on the road so a midrange notebook-type device or convertible device would be ideal for those people. Youve got some people where they could get most of their applications available on a tablet; then there are the ones  who can easily run from a tablet [during the daytime], then have integration with the rest of their applications when theyre back in the office or working in the evenings. Then youve got people who are highly mobile, who travel a lot and [are] working in airports or travelling to customers on trains. For those people they could potentially use Ultrabooks, ultra-slim devices.
      </p>
      <p>
       So we have gone away from just giving everybody the same device. You can now give people a whole different series of devices  all of which we can provide from within Dells client portfolio and all of which can be managed and secured exactly the same as any other device.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Question: What challenges do IT managers who are considering a BYOD policy face?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       BYOD. Its a popular acronym, its a popular phrase. Bring your own device, but what does it actually truly mean? If you look first of all within small business, around 40 percent of small business users are bringing their own devices into work and integrating them onto the networks. But of those 40 percent about 37 percent of those are bringing non-compliant devices and that should immediately [raise] an amber red flag.
      </p>
      <p>
       What this means is that youve got the potential for non-compliant, non-manageable, non-secure products being introduced to the network. This needs serious consideration and one of the ways around that is to take
       <a href="http://www.dell.com/learn/us/en/04/mobility-dell-mobility-solutions" target="_blank">
        BYOD
       </a>
       one step further. Lets call it CYOD or Choose Your Own Device.
      </p>
      <p>
       CYOD would mean selecting a portfolio of products from tablet, laptop, notebook, convertible, desktop workstation, portable workstation, but it needs to be all secure, all manageable, potentially one portfolio. This is the preferred solution.
      </p>
