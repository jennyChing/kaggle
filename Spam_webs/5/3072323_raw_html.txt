     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Rice Northwest Museum of Rocks and Minerals
      </b>
      is a non-profit museum in
      <a href="/wiki/Hillsboro,_Oregon" title="Hillsboro, Oregon">
       Hillsboro, Oregon
      </a>
      , United States. Located just north of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunset_Highway_(Oregon)" title="Sunset Highway (Oregon)">
       Sunset Highway
      </a>
      on the northern edge of Hillsboro, the
      <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">
       earth science
      </a>
      museum is in the
      <a href="/wiki/Portland_metropolitan_area" title="Portland metropolitan area">
       Portland metropolitan area
      </a>
      . Opened in 1997, the museums collections date to the 1930s with the museum housed in a home built to display the rock and mineral collections of the museum founders. The ranch style home is listed on the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">
       National Register of Historic Places
      </a>
      , the first of its kind listed in
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The museum sits on 23 wooded acres (9.3 ha), with the main building containing 7,500 square feet (700m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) of space. Collections include petrified wood, various fossils,
      <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">
       fluorescent
      </a>
      minerals,
      <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">
       meteorites
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">
       zeolites
      </a>
      , and a variety of other minerals. With more than 20,000 specimens, the museum is the largest of its kind in the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">
       Pacific Northwest
      </a>
      . The facility has around 25,000 visitors each year, many of whom are on school tours.
     </p>

     <p>
      Richard L. Rice married Helen Hart in 1932 and the couple began rock collecting in 1938 after finding
      <a href="/wiki/Agate" title="Agate">
       agates
      </a>
      along the
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Coast" title="Oregon Coast">
       Oregon Coast
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rock_and_minerals_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rock_and_minerals-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tour_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tour-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1952 the Rices built a new home north of Hillsboro on 30 acres (12.1 ha) that would later house the museum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Rices founded a museum in 1953 to display their collections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-family_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-family-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Tucson_Gem_%26_Mineral_Show" title="Tucson Gem &amp; Mineral Show">
       Tucson Gem &amp; Mineral Show
      </a>
      , which led to that trophy's retirement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmr1962_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tmr1962-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Helen served as president of the American Federation of Mineralogical Societies from 1959 to 1960.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-splendid_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-splendid-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rock_and_minerals_1-1">

        <span>

         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other items include

      </a>
      , Oregon
      <a href="/wiki/Sunstone" title="Sunstone">
       sunstone
      </a>
      , amber, copper crystals,
      <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">
       zeolites
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morganite" title="Morganite">
       morganite
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Agate" title="Agate">
       agate
      </a>
      among others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-family_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-family-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmr_17-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-tmr-17">
        <span>

        <span>
         ]

       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">
       Programs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rice_Northwest_Museum_of_Rocks_and_Minerals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Programs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Rice Museum offers a variety of public programs, including hands-on classes for children where participants make necklaces out of rocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fun_23-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-fun-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It hosts an annual summer festival with events such as thunderegg cutting and demonstrations of gold panning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The facility has hosted the Northwest Fossil Fest.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The museum offers tours for school groups and other youth programs, often handling multiple groups each day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribune_7-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-tribune-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Children in the school tours get to select a stone to take home from a pile outside.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribune_7-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-tribune-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The museum is open Wednesday through Sunday.
     </p>

     <p>
      The museum and grounds are located on the north side of the Sunset Highway west of
      <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">
       Portland
      </a>
      between the Helvetia Road and Jackson School Road exits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quieter_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-quieter-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Situated on 23 acres (9.3ha) of mostly forested land,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-new-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the museum is housed in the historic
      <b>
       Richard and Helen Rice House
      </b>
      , built as a single family residence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-throw_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-throw-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Completed in 1952, the home was built of
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona_flagstone" title="Arizona flagstone">
       Arizona flagstone
      </a>
      on the exterior and wood native to Oregon, including curly maple and
      <a href="/wiki/Umbellularia" title="Umbellularia">
       myrtlewood
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribune_7-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-tribune-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quieter_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-quieter-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-throw_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-throw-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      William F. Wayman designed the structure with Victor Batchelar building the home, while Charles F. Walters designed the grounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordb_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordb-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      All the wood was logged by Richard Rice, who made his living as a logging contractor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rock_and_minerals_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-rock_and_minerals-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also milled the wood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribune_7-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-tribune-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The home was designed to allow the basement to serve as a museum for the Rices' collections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The structure contains three sandstone fireplaces, and the countertops are finished with hand-painted tiles from
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Myrtlewood is used inside as trim and for doors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmr_17-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-tmr-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Bedroom closets were constructed with drawers, shelves, and ironing boards built-in.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Inside the two-level building are amenities such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dumbwaiter_(elevator)" title="Dumbwaiter (elevator)">
       dumbwaiters
      </a>
      and a sewing room.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quieter_33-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-quieter-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the outside raked cedar was used on the eaves of the low-pitched roof-line building.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The exterior sandstone is tan, rose, and blue in color.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 7,500 square feet (700m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) home with a 3,300 square feet (310m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) basement cost $185,000 to build.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tour_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-tour-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The original wool carpeting is still in use at the home, as is the original
      <a href="/wiki/Linoleum" title="Linoleum">
       linoleum
      </a>
      that features the museum's logo of a shovel and a pick.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-house_3-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-house-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Ranch-style_house" title="Ranch-style house">
       ranch style home
      </a>
      was the first ranch home listed on the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places_listings_in_Oregon" title="National Register of Historic Places listings in Oregon">
       National Register of Historic Places in Oregon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-throw_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-throw-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the house, which has a full basement, the museum uses a separate building as a gallery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmr_17-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-tmr-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      That building, the Northwest Gallery, was formerly used for storage and as a shop, but looks similar to the Rice House.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordinary_19-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordinary-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This gallery focuses on items from Oregon, Idaho, and Washington, and includes collections of agates, thundereggs,
      <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">
       zeolites
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placer_gold" title="Placer gold">
       placer gold
      </a>
      , among others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordinary_19-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordinary-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of the rocks and minerals are housed in glass cases along the walls in the basement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-new-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Before opening to the public an elevator was added to the home.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tour_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-tour-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The museum includes a lapidary and arts gallery, agate gallery, petrified wood gallery, oddities gallery, crystal gallery, Northwest gallery, and fossil gallery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordinary_19-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordinary-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is also an educational room and gift shop, while the outside grounds include a walk that features sandstone, columns of basalt, and a pile of rocks for children to climb over to find stones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tmr_17-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-tmr-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This walk includes a 1,200 pound (540kg) rock made of pumice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordinary_19-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordinary-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
