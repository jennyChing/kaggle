       <h4 id="introduction">
        Introduction
       </h4>
       <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 0em;">
        Temperature anomalies and percentiles are shown on the gridded maps below. The anomaly map on the left is a product of a merged land surface temperature (
        <a href="/ghcnm/">
         Global Historical Climatology Network, GHCN
        </a>
        ) and sea surface temperature (ERSST.v4) anomaly analysis as described in
        <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.1175/JCLI-D-14-00006.1">
         Huang et al. (2015)
        </a>
        . Temperature anomalies for land and ocean are analyzed separately and then merged to form the global analysis. For more information, please visit NCDC's
        <a href="/monitoring-references/faq/anomalies.php">
         Global Surface Temperature Anomalies page
        </a>
        . The percentile map on the right provides additional information by placing the temperature anomaly observed for a specific place and time period into historical perspective, showing how the most current month, season or year compares with the past.
       </p>
       <p style="font-size: 0.8em; text-align: center">
        The most current data may be accessed via the
        <a href="/monitoring-references/faq/anomalies.php" title="link to Global Surface Temperature Anomalies">
         Global Surface Temperature Anomalies
        </a>
        page.
       </p>

       <h4 id="temp">
        Temperatures
       </h4>
       <p style="margin-top: 1em">
        In the atmosphere, 500-millibar height pressure anomalies correlate well with temperatures at the Earth's surface. The average position of the upper-level ridges of high pressure and troughs of low pressuredepicted by positive and negative 500-millibar height anomalies on the
        <a class="rollover" href="/sotc/service/global/hgtanomaly-global/201505.gif" title="link to May 2015 500 millibar height anomalies">
         <img alt="May 2015 height and anomaly map" src="/sotc/service/global/hgtanomaly-global/201505.gif?thumb"/>
         May 2015
        </a>
        and
        <a class="rollover" href="/sotc/service/global/hgtanomaly-global/201503-201505.gif" title="link to March - May 2015 500 millibar height anomalies">
         <img alt="March - May 2015 height and anomaly map" src="/sotc/service/global/hgtanomaly-global/201503-201505.gif?thumb"/>
         MarchMay 2015
        </a>
        mapsis generally reflected by areas of positive and negative temperature anomalies at the surface, respectively.
       </p>
       <h5>
        May
       </h5>
       <div class="fl" style="width:48%;">
        <a href="/sotc/service/global/map-blended-mntp/201505.gif" title="May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius">
         <img alt="May Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius" src="/sotc/service/global/map-blended-mntp/201505.gif?thumb" width="100%"/>
         <br/>
         <span style="font-size: 0.8em">
          May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface
          <br/>
          Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="fl" style="width:48%;">
        <a href="/sotc/service/global/map-percentile-mntp/201505.gif" title="May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Percentiles">
         <img alt="May Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Percentiles" src="/sotc/service/global/map-percentile-mntp/201505.gif?thumb" width="100%"/>
         <br/>
         <span style="font-size: 0.8em">
          May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface
          <br/>
          Temperature Percentiles
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p class="clear">
       </p>
       <p>
        The combined average temperature over global land and ocean surfaces for May 2015 was the highest for May in the 136-year period of record, at  0.87C (1.57F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average of 14.8C (58.6F), surpassing the previous record set just one year ago by 0.08C (0.14F). This ties with February 1998 as the fourth highest monthly departure from average for any month on record. The two highest monthly departures from average occurred earlier this year in February and March, both at 0.89C (1.60F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average for their respective months.
       </p>
       <p>
        The average global temperature across land surfaces was 1.28C (2.30F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average of 11.1C (52.0F), tying with 2012 as the highest May temperature on record. Most of Earth's land surfaces were warmer than average or much warmer than average, according to the Land &amp; Ocean Temperature Percentiles map above, with record warmth across most of Alaska, parts of tropical South America, much of southern Africa and The Middle East, and parts of northwestern Siberia. Only part of the central United States, far west central Australia, and part of Far East Russia observed temperatures characterized as "cooler than average" for May.
       </p>
       <p>
        Select national information is highlighted below. (Please note that different countries report anomalies with respect to different base periods. The information provided here is based directly upon these data):
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Prior to the onset of the seasonal Southwest Asian Monsoon, India experienced a major heatwave during late May. As reported by the
         <a href="http://ds.data.jma.go.jp/tcc/tcc/news/press_20150602.pdf">
          Japan Meteorological Agency
         </a>
         , from the 21
         <sup>
          st
         </sup>
         to the 31
         <sup>
          st
         </sup>
         temperatures surpassed 42C (108F) across much of the country and 45C (113F) in northern and central India. In the capital city of New Delhi, temperatures for the period were 4C (7F) above the May average. More than 2200 fatalities were caused by the heat. Climatologically, May is the hottest month of the year for India.
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="/sotc/national/201505">
          Alaska
         </a>
         observed its warmest May in its 91-year period of record. The temperature was 3.1C (5.6F) higher than the
         <a href="http://www.ncdc.noaa.gov/cag/">
          19812010 average
         </a>
         . The warmth was spread across the state with both Barrow in the far north and Juneau in the far southeast having a record warm May.
        </li>
        <li>
         May 2015 was the second warmest for
         <a href="http://www.aemet.es/en/noticias/2015/06/climaticomayo2015">
          Spain
         </a>
         since national records began in 1961, at 2.4C (4.3F) higher than the 19812010 average. Only May 1964 was warmer. The heat was most notable across the southern Iberian peninsula with temperature departures surpassing +3.5C (+6.3F).
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://met.no/Klima/Varet_i_Norge/2015/filestore/2015-054.pdf">
          Norway
         </a>
         was one of the cool spots during May. The nationwide average temperature was 0.7C (1.3F) below the 19611990 average, marking the coolest May since 2005. Temperatures were not uniform across the country, however; interior regions and higher altitudes saw temperatures 23C (45F) below average, while parts of Finnmark and Troms in the north observed temperatures up to 3C (5F) above average.
        </li>
        <li>
         It was also cooler than average across the
         <a href="http://www.metoffice.gov.uk/climate/uk/summaries">
          UK
         </a>
         during May, with a monthly temperature 0.8C (1.4F) below the 19812010 average. The average maximum temperature was the coldest for May since 1996.
        </li>
        <li>
         All of
         <a href="http://www.dmi.dk/nyheder/arkiv/nyheder-2015/06/groenland-lagt-paa-is-i-maj/">
          Greenland
         </a>
         , but particularly notable in the far northeast, was colder than average for May. Danmarkshavn was 4.6C (8.3F) below its 19611990 average, the coldest May temperature since records began there in 1949.
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.vedur.is/um-vi/frettir/nr/3139">
          Iceland
         </a>
         was also colder than average during May, with the coldest departures from average at the higher altitudes, 34C (57F) below the average of the past 10 years.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        For the oceans, the May global sea surface temperature was 0.72C (1.30F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average of 16.3C (61.3F), the highest for May on record, surpassing the previous record set last year by 0.07C (0.13F). Moderate El Nio conditions were present across the central and eastern equatorial Pacific Ocean during May 2015. According to NOAA's
        <a href="http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/enso_advisory/ensodisc.html">
         Climate Prediction Center
        </a>
        , there is a
        <a href="http://iri.columbia.edu/our-expertise/climate/forecasts/enso/current/?enso_tab=enso-cpc_plume">
         greater than 90 percent chance
        </a>
        that El Nio will continue through Northern Hemisphere fall 2015, and around an 85 percent chance it will last through the 2015/16 winter. This forecast focuses on the ocean surface temperatures between 5N and 5S latitude and 170W to 120W longitude, called the Nio 3.4 region. Notably, record warmth was observed across much of the equatorial Pacific as well as parts of the equatorial and southern Indian Ocean and the Barents Sea to the north of Scandinavia. Only part of the North Atlantic between Greenland and the United Kingdom was much cooler than average, an area that had been record cold for several months in 2015.
       </p>

       <h5>
        Seasonal (MarchMay)
       </h5>
       <div class="fl" style="width:48%;">
        <a href="/sotc/service/global/map-blended-mntp/201503-201505.gif" title="March 2014May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius">
         <img alt="March 2014May Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius" src="/sotc/service/global/map-blended-mntp/201503-201505.gif?thumb" width="100%"/>
         <br/>
         <span style="font-size: 0.8em">
          March 2014May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface
          <br/>
          Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="fr" style="width:48%;">
        <a href="/sotc/service/global/map-percentile-mntp/201503-201505.gif" title="March 2014May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Percentiles">
         <img alt="March 2014May Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Percentiles" src="/sotc/service/global/map-percentile-mntp/201503-201505.gif?thumb" width="100%"/>
         <br/>
         <span style="font-size: 0.8em">
          March 2014May 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface
          <br/>
          Temperature Percentiles
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p class="clear">
       </p>
       <p>
        Together, the record warm March and May and fourth warmest April made the combined average temperature over global land and ocean surfaces for the MarchMay period (austral autumn / boreal spring) the  highest on record, at 0.85C (1.53F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average of 13.7C (56.7F), surpassing the previous record warmth of MarchMay 2010 by 0.04C (0.07F). The Northern Hemisphere had its warmest spring on record and the Southern Hemisphere had its second warmest autumn, behind 2010.
       </p>
       <p>
        The globally-averaged temperature across land surfaces was the highest on record for MarchMay, at 1.33C (2.39F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average of 8.1C (46.4F). Most of western North America, South America, Africa, and Eurasia was much warmer than average, with a few regions within these areas record warm, as shown by the Land &amp; Ocean Temperature Percentiles map above. Only small areas of the south central United States  and north central Mexico, along with northeastern Canada, western Greenland, and western Australia were cooler or much cooler than average. No land areas observed record cold during MarchMay.
       </p>
       <p>
        Select national information is highlighted below. (Please note that different countries report anomalies with respect to different base periods. The information provided here is based directly upon these data):
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://met.no/Klima/Varet_i_Norge/2015/filestore/2015-054.pdf">
          Norway
         </a>
         experienced a warm spring, at 1.6C (2.9F) above the 19611990 average. The greatest departures from average were observed in the north, with Finnmark 34C (57F) warmer than average.
        </li>
        <li>
         Spring was also warm in
         <a href="http://www.meteofrance.fr/actualites/25525587-un-printemps-doux-et-sec">
          France
         </a>
         , with an average temperature 0.8C (1.4F) higher than the 19812010 average.
        </li>
        <li>
         Autumn was warm for
         <a href="http://www.niwa.co.nz/climate/summaries/seasonal/autumn-2015">
          New Zealand
         </a>
         overall, with a nationally averaged temperature 0.8C (1.4F) above the 19812010 average. The warmest regions were in isolated parts of inland Taranaki, eastern Canterbury and the West Coast, where temperatures were more than 1.2C (2.2F) above average, according to NIWA.
        </li>
        <li>
         Autumn was record warm for
         <a href="http://www.smn.gov.ar/serviciosclimaticos/clima/archivo/informe_temperatura_otono2015.pdf">
          Argentina
         </a>
         among the country's 55-year period of record, at 1.51C (2.72F) higher than the 19611990 average. The entire country observed MarchMay temperatures above average, with much of the central to northwest regions of the country 1.53.0C (2.75.4F) above average.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        Across the world's oceans, the MarchMay sea surface temperature was 0.66C (1.19F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average of 16.1C (61.0F), also the highest for MarchMay on record, surpassing the previous record of 2010 by 0.02C (0.04F). The oceans in the Northern Hemisphere and in the Southern Hemisphere were each record warm. Record warm areas were particularly notable across much of the northeastern and equatorial Pacific Ocean, parts of the western Atlantic stretching into the Gulf of Mexico, and the Barents Sea north of Scandinavia. Much cooler than average temperatures and an area of record cold for the season was observed in the North Atlantic south of Greenland.
       </p>

       <h5>
        Year-to-date (JanuaryMay)
       </h5>
       <div class="fl" style="width:48%;">
        <a href="/sotc/service/global/map-blended-mntp/201501-201505.gif" title="JanuaryMay 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius">
         <img alt="JanuaryMay Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius" src="/sotc/service/global/map-blended-mntp/201501-201505.gif?thumb" width="100%"/>
         <br/>
         <span style="font-size: 0.8em">
          JanuaryMay 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface
          <br/>
          Temperature Anomalies in degrees Celsius
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="fr" style="width:48%;">
        <a href="/sotc/service/global/map-percentile-mntp/201501-201505.gif" title="JanuaryMay 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Percentiles">
         <img alt="JanuaryMay Blended Land and Sea Surface Temperature Percentiles" src="/sotc/service/global/map-percentile-mntp/201501-201505.gif?thumb" width="100%"/>
         <br/>
         <span style="font-size: 0.8em">
          JanuaryMay 2015 Blended Land and Sea Surface
          <br/>
          Temperature Percentiles
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p class="clear">
       </p>
       <p>
        The first five months of 2015 were the warmest such period on record across the world's land and ocean surfaces, at 0.85C (1.53F) above the 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century average, surpassing the previous record set in 2010 by 0.09C (0.16F). Consequently, 2010 was the last year with El Nio conditions; however El Nio was ending at
        <a href="http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/ensostuff/ensoyears.shtml">
         this point in 2010
        </a>
        , while it appears to be maturing at the
        <a href="http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/enso_advisory/figure6.gif">
         same point in 2015
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        The average global sea surface temperature for the year-to-date was the highest for JanuaryMay in the 136-year period of record, surpassing the previous record of 2010 by 0.01C (0.02F). while the average land surface temperature was also record high, surpassing the previous record of 2007 by 0.05C (0.09F). Record warmth was most notable over the oceans, particularly the northeastern and equatorial Pacific Ocean, parts of the western North Atlantic, and the Barents Sea north of Scandinavia.
       </p>

       <h4 id="precip">
        Precipitation
       </h4>
       <h5>
        May
       </h5>
       <p style="margin-top: 1em">
        The maps below represent precipitation percent of normal (left, using a base period of 19611990) and precipitation percentiles (right, using the period of record) based on the GHCN dataset of land surface stations. As is typical, precipitation anomalies during May 2015 varied significantly around the world.
       </p>
       <p>
        Select national information is highlighted below. (Please note that different countries report anomalies with respect to different base periods. The information provided here is based directly upon these data):
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         The heat in
         <a href="http://www.aemet.es/en/noticias/2015/06/climaticomayo2015">
          Spain
         </a>
         during May was accompanied by extreme dryness. With precipitation records dating back to 1947, May 2015 was the driest May on record for the country, with total average rainfall for the month just 25 percent of normal.
        </li>
        <li>
         On the other hand, it was wet in
         <a href="http://www.dmi.dk/nyheder/arkiv/nyheder-2015/06/vaadeste-maj-og-foraar-siden-1983/">
          Denmark
         </a>
         . The country experienced its second wettest May (tied with May 1969) since national records began in 1874, with 86 mm of precipitation. The wettest May occurred in 1983.
        </li>
       </ul>

function Initialize() {
	fillYears();
	$('#year').val(2015);
	$('#month').val(5);
}

function fillYears() {
	var report = $('#report').val();
	var year   = $('#year').val();

	$('#year').find('option').remove();

	// reverse order of years
	years = new Array();
	for (var yr in dates[report]) {years.unshift(yr);}

	$.each(years, function(key, yr) {
		$('#year').append(
			$('&lt;option&gt;&lt;/option&gt;').val(yr).html(yr)
		);
	});

	if($.inArray(year, years) &gt; -1){ $('#year').val(year); }

	fillMonths();
}

function fillMonths() {
	var monthNames = new Array ( "January", "February", "March", "April", "May", "June", "July", "August", "September", "October", "November", "December", "Annual", "Winter (DJF)", "Spring (MAM)", "Summer (JJA)", "Fall (SON)" );

	var report = $('#report').val();
	var year   = $('#year').val();
	var month  = $('#month').val();

	$('#month').find('option').remove();

	$.each(dates[report][year], function(key, mon) {
		$('#month').append(
			$('&lt;option&gt;&lt;/option&gt;').val(mon).html(monthNames[mon-1])
		);
		if (mon &gt; 13) $("#month option[value='"+mon+"']").addClass("season");
	});

	if ($.inArray(month, dates[report][year]) &gt; -1){ $('#month').val(month); }
	else if (month &gt; dates[report][year][dates[report][year].length-1]) { $('#month').val(dates[report][year][dates[report][year].length-1]); }
}
