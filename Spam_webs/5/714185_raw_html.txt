     <p>
      <b>
       Myostatin
      </b>
      (also known as
      <b>
       growth differentiation factor 8
      </b>
      , abbreviated
      <b>
       GDF-8
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Myokine" title="Myokine">
       myokine
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">
       protein
      </a>
      produced by muscle cells that acts on muscle cells (autocrine function) to inhibit
      <a href="/wiki/Myogenesis" title="Myogenesis">
       myogenesis
      </a>
      : muscle cell growth and differentiation. In humans it is encoded by the
      <i>
       MSTN
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">
       gene
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9843994_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid9843994-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Myostatin is a secreted
      <a href="/wiki/Growth_differentiation_factor" title="Growth differentiation factor">
       growth differentiation factor
      </a>
      that is a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Transforming_growth_factor_beta" title="Transforming growth factor beta">
       TGF beta
      </a>
      protein family.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16842126_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid16842126-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17374508_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid17374508-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Animals either lacking myostatin or treated with substances that block the activity of myostatin have significantly more muscle mass. Furthermore, individuals who have mutations in both copies of the myostatin gene have significantly more muscle mass and are stronger than normal. Blocking the activity of myostatin may have therapeutic application in treating muscle wasting diseases such as
      <a href="/wiki/Muscular_dystrophy" title="Muscular dystrophy">
       muscular dystrophy
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_sequencing">
       Discovery and sequencing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and sequencing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">
       gene
      </a>
      encoding myostatin was discovered in 1997 by geneticists Se-Jin Lee and Alexandra McPherron who also produced a strain of mutant
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus_musculus" title="Mus musculus">
       mice
      </a>
      that lack the gene. These myostatin "knockout" mice have approximately twice as much muscle as normal mice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McPherron_1997_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McPherron_1997-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These mice were subsequently named "mighty mice".
     </p>
     <p>
      Naturally occurring deficiencies of myostatin have been identified in cattle by Ravi Kambadur,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kambadur_1997_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kambadur_1997-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Whippet" title="Whippet">
       whippets
      </a>
      , and humans; in each case the result is a dramatic increase in muscle mass. A mutation in the
      <a href="/wiki/Three_prime_untranslated_region" title="Three prime untranslated region">
       3' UTR
      </a>
      of the myostatin gene in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texel_(sheep)" title="Texel (sheep)">
       Texel sheep
      </a>
      creates target sites for the
      <a href="/wiki/MicroRNA" title="MicroRNA">
       microRNAs
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Mir-1_microRNA_precursor_family" title="Mir-1 microRNA precursor family">
       miR-1
      </a>
      and miR-206. This is likely to cause the muscular phenotype of this breed of sheep.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16751773_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid16751773-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_mechanism_of_action">
       Structure and mechanism of action
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and mechanism of action">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Human myostatin consists of two identical subunits, each consisting of 109 (NCBI database claims human myostatin is 375 residues long)
      <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">
       amino acid
      </a>
      residues. Its total
      <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">
       molecular weight
      </a>
      is 25.0 k
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">
       Da
      </a>
      . The protein is inactive until a
      <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">
       protease
      </a>
      cleaves the NH2-terminal, or "pro-domain" portion of the molecule, resulting in the active COOH-terminal dimer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Myostatin binds to the
      <a href="/wiki/Activin_type_2_receptors" title="Activin type 2 receptors">
       activin type II receptor
      </a>
      , resulting in a recruitment of either coreceptor
      <a href="/wiki/BMPR1A" title="BMPR1A">
       Alk-3
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/ACVR1B" title="ACVR1B">
       Alk-4
      </a>
      . This coreceptor then initiates a
      <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">
       cell signaling
      </a>
      cascade in the
      <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">
       muscle
      </a>
      , which includes the activation of
      <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factor" title="Transcription factor">
       transcription factors
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/SMAD_(protein)" title="SMAD (protein)">
       SMAD
      </a>
      family -
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMAD2" title="SMAD2">
       SMAD2
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMAD3" title="SMAD3">
       SMAD3
      </a>
      . These factors then induce myostatin-specific
      <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_gene_expression" title="Regulation of gene expression">
       gene regulation
      </a>
      . When applied to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myoblasts" title="Myoblasts">
       myoblasts
      </a>
      , myostatin inhibits their
      <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">
       differentiation
      </a>
      into mature
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscle_fiber" title="Muscle fiber">
       muscle fibers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Myostatin also inhibits
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AKT" title="AKT">
       Akt
      </a>
      , a kinase that is sufficient to cause
      <a href="/wiki/Muscle_hypertrophy" title="Muscle hypertrophy">
       muscle hypertrophy
      </a>
      , in part through the activation of
      <a href="/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis" title="Protein biosynthesis">
       protein synthesis
      </a>
      . However, Akt is not responsible for all of the observed muscle hyperthrophic effects which are mediated by myostatin inhibition
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus myostatin acts in two ways: by inhibiting
      <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">
       muscle
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">
       differentiation
      </a>
      , and by inhibiting Akt-induced protein synthesis.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_in_animals">
       Effects in animals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects in animals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_muscled_cattle">
       Double muscled cattle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Double muscled cattle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After that discovery, several laboratories cloned and established the
      <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">
       nucleotide
      </a>
      sequence of a myostatin gene in two breeds of cattle
      <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Blue" title="Belgian Blue">
       Belgian Blue
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmontese_(cattle)" title="Piedmontese (cattle)">
       Piedmontese
      </a>
      , and found that these animals have mutations in that myostatin gene (various mutations in each breed) which in one way or another lead to absence of functional myostatin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McPherron_1997_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-McPherron_1997-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kambadur_1997_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kambadur_1997-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grobet_1997_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grobet_1997-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike mice with a damaged myostatin gene, in these cattle breeds the muscle cells multiply rather than enlarge. People describe these cattle breeds as "double muscled", but the total increase in all muscles is no more than 40%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kambadur_1997_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kambadur_1997-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Animals lacking myostatin or animals treated with substances such as
      <a href="/wiki/Follistatin" title="Follistatin">
       follistatin
      </a>
      that block the binding of myostatin to its receptor have significantly larger muscles. Thus, reduction of myostatin could potentially benefit the
      <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">
       livestock industry
      </a>
      , with even a 20 percent reduction in myostatin levels potentially having a large effect on the development of muscles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kota_2009_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kota_2009-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the animal breeds developed as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homozygous" title="Homozygous">
       homozygous
      </a>
      for myostatin deficiency have
      <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Blue#Breed_issues" title="Belgian Blue">
       reproduction issues
      </a>
      due to their unusually heavy and bulky offspring, and require special care and a more expensive diet to achieve a superior yield. This negatively affects economics of myostatin-deficient breeds to the point where they do not usually offer an obvious advantage. While hypertrophic meat (e.g. from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmontese_(cattle)#Piedmontese_beef" title="Piedmontese (cattle)">
       Piedmontese beef
      </a>
      ) has a place on the specialist market due to its unusual properties, at least for purebred myostatin-deficient strains the expenses and (especially in cattle) necessity of veterinary supervision place them at a disadvantage in the bulk market.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De_Smet_2004_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-De_Smet_2004-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_enhancement_in_dogs">
       Performance enhancement in dogs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Performance enhancement in dogs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MyostatinMutantWhippet.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="159" data-file-width="278" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/94/MyostatinMutantWhippet.jpg/220px-MyostatinMutantWhippet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/94/MyostatinMutantWhippet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/94/MyostatinMutantWhippet.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MyostatinMutantWhippet.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.k9community.co.uk/forums/index.php?showtopic=25995&amp;st=15" rel="nofollow">
         "bully whippet"
        </a>
        with a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homozygous" title="Homozygous">
         homozygous
        </a>
        mutation in myostatin.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosher_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Mosher-14">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          14
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A 2007 NIH study in PLOS Genetics
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosher_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mosher-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      found a significant relationship in
      <a href="/wiki/Whippet" title="Whippet">
       whippets
      </a>
      between a myostatin mutation and racing performance. Whippets that were
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterozygous" title="Heterozygous">
       heterozygous
      </a>
      for a 2 base pair deletion in the myostatin gene were significantly over-represented in the top racing classes. The mutation resulted in a truncated myostatin
      <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">
       protein
      </a>
      , likely resulting in an inactive form of myostatin.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Whippet" title="Whippet">
       Whippets
      </a>
      with a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homozygous" title="Homozygous">
       homozygous
      </a>
      deletion were apparently less able runners although their overall appearance was significantly more muscular. Whippets with the homozygous deletion also had an unusual body shape, with a broader head, pronounced overbite, shorter legs, and thicker tails. These whippets have also been called "bully whippets" by the breeding community due to their size. Despite the name "bully", these dogs tend to have a friendly and positive demeanour towards people as usual for whippets.
     </p>
     <p>
      This particular mutation was not found in other muscular dog breeds such as
      <a href="/wiki/Boxer_(dog)" title="Boxer (dog)">
       boxers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/English_Mastiff" title="English Mastiff">
       mastiffs
      </a>
      , nor was it found in other
      <a href="/wiki/Sighthound" title="Sighthound">
       sighthounds
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Greyhound" title="Greyhound">
       greyhounds
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_greyhound" title="Italian greyhound">
       Italian greyhounds
      </a>
      , or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afghan_hound" title="Afghan hound">
       Afghan hounds
      </a>
      . The authors of the study suggest that myostatin mutation may not be desirable in greyhounds, the whippets' nearest relative, because greyhound racing requires more significant endurance due to the longer races (900 meters for greyhounds vs. 300 meters for whippets).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">
       Clinical significance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mutations">
       Mutations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mutations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A technique for detecting mutations in myostatin variants has been developed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mutations that reduce the production of functional myostatin lead to an overgrowth of muscle tissue. Myostatin-related muscle hypertrophy has an incomplete autosomal
      <a href="/wiki/Dominance_(genetics)" title="Dominance (genetics)">
       dominance
      </a>
      pattern of inheritance. People with a mutation in both copies of the
      <i>
       MSTN
      </i>
      gene in each
      <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">
       cell
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homozygote" title="Homozygote">
       homozygotes
      </a>
      ) have significantly increased muscle mass and strength. People with a mutation in one copy of the
      <i>
       MSTN
      </i>
      gene in each cell (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterozygote" title="Heterozygote">
       heterozygotes
      </a>
      ) also have increased muscle bulk, but to a lesser degree.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004, a German boy was diagnosed with a mutation in both copies of the myostatin-producing gene, making him considerably stronger than his peers. His mother has a mutation in one copy of the gene.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An American boy born in 2005 was diagnosed with a clinically similar condition but with a somewhat different cause:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlCTV.ca_.7C_Rare_condition_gives_toddler_super_strength_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-urlCTV.ca_.7C_Rare_condition_gives_toddler_super_strength-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      his body produces a normal level of functional myostatin; but, because he is stronger and more muscular than most others his age, it is believed that a defect in his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activin_type_IIB_receptor" title="Activin type IIB receptor">
       myostatin receptors
      </a>
      prevents his muscle cells from responding normally to myostatin. He appeared on the television show
      <i>
       World's Strongest Toddler
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Therapeutic_potential">
       Therapeutic potential
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Therapeutic potential">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Further research into myostatin and the myostatin gene may lead to therapies for
      <a href="/wiki/Muscular_dystrophy" title="Muscular dystrophy">
       muscular dystrophy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15215484_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid15215484-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18600566_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid18600566-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The idea is to introduce substances that block myostatin. A
      <a href="/wiki/Monoclonal_antibody" title="Monoclonal antibody">
       monoclonal antibody
      </a>
      specific to myostatin increases muscle mass in mice
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12559968_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid12559968-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and monkeys.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kota_2009_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kota_2009-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A two-week treatment of normal mice with soluble
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activin_type_IIB_receptor" title="Activin type IIB receptor">
       activin type IIB receptor
      </a>
      , a molecule that is normally attached to cells and binds to myostatin, leads to a significantly increased muscle mass (up to 60%).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16330774_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid16330774-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is thought that binding of myostatin to the soluble activin receptor prevents it from interacting with the cell-bound receptors.
     </p>
     <p>
      It remains unclear as to whether long-term treatment of muscular dystrophy with myostatin inhibitors is beneficial, as the depletion of muscle
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_cell" title="Satellite cell">
       stem cells
      </a>
      could worsen the disease later on. As of 2012
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , no myostatin-inhibiting drugs for humans are on the market. An antibody genetically engineered to neutralize myostatin,
      <a href="/wiki/Stamulumab" title="Stamulumab">
       stamulumab
      </a>
      , which was under development by pharmaceutical company
      <a href="/wiki/Wyeth" title="Wyeth">
       Wyeth
      </a>
      .,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is no longer under development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some athletes, eager to get their hands on such drugs and turn to the internet where
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fake_medicine" title="Fake medicine">
       fake
      </a>
      "myostatin blockers" are being sold.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kota_2009_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kota_2009-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Myostatin levels are effectively decreased by
      <a href="/wiki/Creatine" title="Creatine">
       creatine
      </a>
      supplementation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20026378_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid20026378-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gene_doping">
       Gene doping
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gene doping">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Inhibition of myostatin leads to muscle
      <a href="/wiki/Hyperplasia" title="Hyperplasia">
       hyperplasia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophy" title="Hypertrophy">
       hypertrophy
      </a>
      . Myostatin inhibitors can improve athletic performance and therefore there is a concern these inhibitors might be abused in the field of sports.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haisma_2006_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Haisma_2006-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, studies in mice suggest that myostatin inhibition does not directly increase the strength of individual muscle fibers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21565991_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid21565991-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Myostatin_in_the_heart">
       Myostatin in the heart
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myostatin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Myostatin in the heart">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Myostatin is expressed at very low levels in cardiac myocytes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although its presence has been noted in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiomyocytes" title="Cardiomyocytes">
       cardiomyocytes
      </a>
      of both fetal and adult mice,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharma_1999_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sharma_1999-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      its physiological function remains uncertain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, it has been suggested that fetal cardiac myostatin may play a role in early heart development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharma_1999_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sharma_1999-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Myostatin is produced as promyostatin, a precursor protein kept inactive by the latent
      <a href="/wiki/LTBP3" title="LTBP3">
       TGF- binding protein 3
      </a>
      (LTBP3).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pathological cardiac stress promotes N-terminal cleavage by
      <a href="/wiki/Furin" title="Furin">
       furin
      </a>
      convertase to create a biologically active C-terminal fragment. The mature myostatin is then segregated from the latent complex via proteolytic cleavage by
      <a href="/wiki/BMP-1" title="BMP-1">
       BMP-1
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/TLL1" title="TLL1">
       tolloid metallopreoteinases
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Free myostatin is able to bind its receptor, ActRIIB, and increase
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMAD2" title="SMAD2">
       SMAD2/3
      </a>
      phosphorylation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The latter produces a heteromeric complex with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMAD4" title="SMAD4">
       SMAD4
      </a>
      , inducing myostatin translocation into the cardiomyocyte nucleus to modulate transcription factor activity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKoy_2007_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKoy_2007-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Manipulating the muscle
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creatinine_kinase_convertase" title="Creatinine kinase convertase">
       creatinine kinase
      </a>
      promoter can modulate myostatin expression, though it has only been observed in male mice thus far.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Myostatin may inhibit cardiomyocyte proliferation and
      <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">
       differentiation
      </a>
      by manipulating cell cycle progression.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharma_1999_32-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sharma_1999-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This argument is supported by the fact that myostatin mRNA is poorly expressed in proliferating fetal cardiomyocytes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKoy_2007_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKoy_2007-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       In vitro
      </i>
      studies indicate that myostatin promotes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMAD2" title="SMAD2">
       SMAD2
      </a>
      phosphorylation to inhibit cardiomyocyte proliferation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKoy_2007_33-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKoy_2007-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Furthermore, myostatin has been shown to directly prevent
      <a href="/wiki/Cell_Cycle_(journal)" title="Cell Cycle (journal)">
       cell cycle
      </a>
      G1 to S phase transition by decreasing levels of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CDK2" title="CDK2">
       cyclin-dependent kinase complex 2
      </a>
      (CDK2) and by increasing
      <a href="/wiki/P21" title="P21">
       p21
      </a>
      levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKoy_2007_33-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKoy_2007-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Growth of cardiomyocytes may also be hindered by myostatin-regulated inhibition of protein kinase
      <a href="/wiki/P38_mitogen-activated_protein_kinases" title="P38 mitogen-activated protein kinases">
       p38
      </a>
      and the serine-threonine protein kinase
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akt" title="Akt">
       Akt
      </a>
      , which typically promote cardiomyocyte
      <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophy" title="Hypertrophy">
       hypertrophy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morissette_2006_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Morissette_2006-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, increased myostatin activity only occurs in response to specific stimuli,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morissette_2006_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Morissette_2006-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      such as in pressure stress models, in which cardiac myostatin induces whole-body muscular
      <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">
       atrophy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharma_1999_32-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sharma_1999-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Physiologically, minimal amounts of cardiac myostatin are secreted from the myocardium into serum, having a limited effect on muscle growth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, increases in cardiac myostatin can increase its serum concentration, which may cause skeletal muscle atrophy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pathological states that increase cardiac stress and promote
      <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">
       heart failure
      </a>
      can induce a rise in both cardiac myostatin mRNA and protein levels within the heart.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In ischemic or
      <a href="/wiki/Dilated_cardiomyopathy" title="Dilated cardiomyopathy">
       dilated cardiomyopathy
      </a>
      , increased levels of myostatin mRNA have been detected within the left ventricle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torrado_2010_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Torrado_2010-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a member of the TGF- family, myostatin may play a role in post-infarct recovery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharma_1999_32-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sharma_1999-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been hypothesized that hypertrophy of the heart induces an increase in myostatin as a
      <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback_amplifier" title="Negative feedback amplifier">
       negative feedback
      </a>
      mechanism in an attempt to limit further myocyte growth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_2008_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wang_2008-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shyu_2005_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Shyu_2005-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This process includes mitogen-activated protein kinases and binding of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mef2" title="Mef2">
       MEF2
      </a>
      transcription factor within the promoter region of the myostatin gene.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Increases in myostatin levels during
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_heart_failure" title="Chronic heart failure">
       chronic heart failure
      </a>
      have been shown to cause cardiac
      <a href="/wiki/Cachexia" title="Cachexia">
       cachexia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anker_2003_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Anker_2003-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Systemic inhibition of cardiac myostatin with the JA-16 antibody maintains overall muscle weight in experimental models with pre-existing heart failure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heineke_2010_31-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heineke_2010-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Myostatin also alters
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EC_coupling" title="EC coupling">
       excitation-contraction (EC) coupling
      </a>
      within the heart.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers_2009_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rodgers_2009-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A reduction in cardiac myostatin induces eccentric hypertrophy of the heart, and increases its sensitivity to beta-adrenergic stimuli by enhancing Ca
      <sup>
       2+
      </sup>
      release from the SR during EC coupling. Also,
      <a href="/wiki/Phospholamban" title="Phospholamban">
       phospholamban
      </a>
      phosphorylation is increased in myostatin-knockout mice, leading to an increase in Ca
      <sup>
       2+
      </sup>
      release into the cytosol during systole.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breitbart_2011_30-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-Breitbart_2011-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, minimizing cardiac myostatin may improve cardiac output.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers_2009_39-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rodgers_2009-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
