     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Cradle Will Rock
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1937
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_theater" title="Musical theater">
       musical
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Marc_Blitzstein" title="Marc Blitzstein">
       Marc Blitzstein
      </a>
      . Originally a part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Theatre_Project" title="Federal Theatre Project">
       Federal Theatre Project
      </a>
      , it was directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">
       Orson Welles
      </a>
      , and produced by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Houseman" title="John Houseman">
       John Houseman
      </a>
      . The musical is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bertold_Brecht" title="Bertold Brecht">
       Brechtian
      </a>
      allegory of corruption and corporate greed and includes a panoply of societal figures. Set in "Steeltown, USA", it follows the efforts of Larry Foreman to
      <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">
       unionize
      </a>
      the town's workers and combat wicked, greedy businessman Mr. Mister, who controls the town's factory, press, church and social organization. The piece is almost entirely
      <a href="/wiki/Sung-through" title="Sung-through">
       sung-through
      </a>
      , giving it many
      <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">
       operatic
      </a>
      qualities, although Blitzstein included popular song styles of the time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">
       WPA
      </a>
      temporarily shut down the project a few days before it was to open on Broadway, so to avoid government and union restrictions, the show was performed with Blitzstein playing piano onstage and the cast members singing their parts from the audience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune17_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune17-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The original cast consisted of John Adair, Guido Alexander, Marc Blitzstein, Peggy Coudray,
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Da_Silva" title="Howard Da Silva">
       Howard Da Silva
      </a>
      , George Fairchild, Robert Fransworth, Edward Fuller,
      <a href="/wiki/Will_Geer" title="Will Geer">
       Will Geer
      </a>
      , Maynard Holmes, Frank Marvel, Charles Niemeyer, Le Roi Operti, Jules Schmidt, George Smithfield, Olive Stanton, and Bert Weston.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Cradle Will Rock
      </i>
      was reprised JanuaryApril 1938 as part of the first season of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Theatre" title="Mercury Theatre">
       Mercury Theatre
      </a>
      , an independent repertory company founded by Welles and Houseman. An abridged version of the production was recorded and released in 1938, the first original cast recording ever made.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
       Synopsis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Cradle_Will_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Setting: Steeltown, U.S.A.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Act I
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      Moll, a tired and hungry prostitute, is arrested and jailed for refusing her services to a police officer loyal to Mr. Mister, who owns the steel factory and everything else in town. Members of the Liberty Committee, a group of prominent citizens who oppose the union, are also arrested, because a policeman mistook them for union organizers. At night court, Moll meets Harry Druggist, who is continually arrested for vagrancy after having lost his drugstore because of Mr. Mister.
     </p>
     <p>
      Harry tells Moll that the Liberty Committee are bigger prostitutes than she is; he explains how they, and even himself, have sold out to Mr. Mister. In a series of flashbacks, we see this happen: Reverend Salvation is convinced by Mrs. Mister to make sermons on
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      that are convenient to the profits of the steel industry, Editor Daily of the
      <i>
       Steeltown News
      </i>
      is bullied into running stories against union organizer Larry Foreman and giving Mr. Mister's feckless son Junior Mister a correspondence job in Honolulu. Harry Druggist is strong-armed by one of Mr. Mister's henchmen into keeping quiet about a bomb planted in a car belonging to Gus Polack, an innocent immigrant steelworker who has joined the union. Harry's son Stevie is killed trying to save Gus and his pregnant wife from the blast.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Act II
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      More flashbacks show other Liberty Committee members selling out to Mr. Mister. The painter Dauber and the violinist Yasha work for Mrs. Mister, using their art to support her husband's ideals.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the present, Larry Foreman is beaten by the police and jailed for "inciting to riot". He explains the principle behind unions, and says that the time is coming when "the cradle will rock" and overthrow Mr. Mister and others like him.
     </p>
     <p>
      In another flashback, Mr. Mister has President Prexy and other faculty at Steeltown University get students to serve in the army. Doctor Specialist, Mr. Mister's personal doctor as well as the one that treated a worker who died in a machine accident, is threatened with the loss of his chairmanship of the Liberty Committee if he does not report that the worker was drunk. Ella Hammer, the worker's sister, knows that he was pushed, and angrily confronts the doctor.
     </p>
     <p>
      When Mr. Mister arrives at night court to release the Liberty Committee, he offers Foreman a place on the Committee and a hefty bribe if he will give up his union activities. Foreman refuses: though a common man, he stands up to the corrupt forces of Mr. Mister. Mr. Mister feels that his monopoly may be slipping away. He confronts Foreman, but the workers are rising up.
     </p>

     <p>

It was the first original cast recording ever made. In December 1964 the recording was re-released in a limited-edition LP on the American Legacy Records label (T1001).

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Original_production">
       Original production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Cradle_Will_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Original production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="660" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg/260px-Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg/390px-Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg/520px-Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg 2x" width="260"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cradle-Will-Rock-Cast.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Marc_Blitzstein" title="Marc Blitzstein">
         Marc Blitzstein
        </a>
        and the cast of
        <i>
         The Cradle Will Rock
        </i>
        (1937)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A production of the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Theatre_Project" title="Federal Theatre Project">
       Federal Theatre Project
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       The Cradle Will Rock
      </i>
      was to begin previews on Broadway June 16, 1937 at the
      <a href="/wiki/Maxine_Elliott_Theatre" title="Maxine Elliott Theatre">
       Maxine Elliott Theatre
      </a>
      with elaborate sets and a full orchestra.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The production was shut down four days before its opening by the WPA,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which stated that due to budget cuts, the WPA was reorganizing its arts projects, and all openings of WPA plays, musicals, concerts, and art galleries were to be delayed until after July 1; however, many asserted that the musical had been censored because the pro-union plot was "too radical".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune17_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune17-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-green-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The theatre was padlocked and surrounded by security to prevent anyone from stealing props or costumes, as these were considered U. S. Government property.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Welles, Houseman and Blitzstein, seeking a way to privately produce the show, rented the much larger
      <a href="/wiki/New_Century_Theatre" title="New Century Theatre">
       Venice Theatre
      </a>
      and a piano just in time for the scheduled preview on June 16, 1937.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune19_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune19-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 600 audience members, who had gathered outside the Maxine Elliot Theatre for the preview, travelled 21 blocks north to the Venice Theatre; many were on foot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune17_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune17-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The sold-out house grew even larger when the show's creators invited people off the street to attend for free. The musicians' union refused to play for the show unless Houseman could provide their full salaries, and
      <a href="/wiki/Actors%27_Equity_Association" title="Actors' Equity Association">
       Actors' Equity Association
      </a>
      stated that its members could not perform onstage at the new theatre without approval of the original producer (the federal government).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The show's creators thus planned for Blitzstein to perform the entire musical at the piano.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Just after beginning the first number, Blitzstein was joined by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Olive_Stanton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Olive Stanton (page does not exist)">
       Olive Stanton
      </a>
      , the actress playing Moll, from the audience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leiter_7-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leiter-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the rest of the performance, various actors joined in with Blitzstein and performed the entire musical from the house.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to
      <i>
       The New York Times'
      </i>
      s description of the original production, "Persons who heard the opera's score and extracts last night carried no clear impression except that its theme was that steel workers should join a union." Poet
      <a href="/wiki/Archibald_MacLeish" title="Archibald MacLeish">
       Archibald MacLeish
      </a>
      , who was in the audience, "praised the 'vitality' of the Federal Theatre Project."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune17_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune17-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Houseman determined that there were no legal restrictions on performing the musical with a new financial backer, and beginning on June 18,
      <a href="/wiki/Helen_Deutsch" title="Helen Deutsch">
       Helen Deutsch
      </a>
      , press agent for the
      <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Guild" title="Theatre Guild">
       Theatre Guild
      </a>
      , agreed to serve as the financial backer for
      <i>
       The Cradle Will Rock
      </i>
      ; the actors received a two-week leave of absence from the WPA, and, in an agreement with Actors' Equity, Deutsch paid the 19 cast members $1500 for the two weeks' performances.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune19_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune19-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two days later, Houseman announced that, should the production prove successful, the two-week run would be continued indefinitely.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune20_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune20-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Houseman also announced that the musical would continue to be performed with Blitzstein playing piano onstage and the cast members singing from the audience. He asserted that this made the audience feel like part of the show, stating, "There has always been the question of how to produce a labor show so the audience feels like it is a part of the performance. This technique seems to solve that problem and is exactly the right one for this particular piece".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTJune20_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTJune20-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The success of the performance led Welles and Houseman to form the
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Theatre" title="Mercury Theatre">
       Mercury Theatre
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Broadway_and_Off-Broadway">
       Broadway and Off-Broadway
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Cradle_Will_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Broadway and Off-Broadway">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following the impromptu opening and a brief run at the Venice Theatre (later renamed the New Century Theatre) in July 1937, the production reopened January 3, 1938, at the
      <a href="/wiki/48th_Street_Theatre" title="48th Street Theatre">
       Windsor Theatre
      </a>
      as part of the first season of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Theatre" title="Mercury Theatre">
       Mercury Theatre
      </a>
      company. It played a total of 108 performances, ending in April 1938.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-green-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The musical was revived on
      <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">
       Broadway
      </a>
      on December 26, 1947,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-green-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      at the Mansfield Theater (subsequently moving to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Broadway_Theatre" title="The Broadway Theatre">
       The Broadway Theatre
      </a>
      ) with a cast that included
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Drake" title="Alfred Drake">
       Alfred Drake
      </a>
      (Larry Foreman),
      <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Vance" title="Vivian Vance">
       Vivian Vance
      </a>
      (Mrs. Mister),
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Albertson" title="Jack Albertson">
       Jack Albertson
      </a>
      (Yasha), and original cast member
      <a href="/wiki/Will_Geer" title="Will Geer">
       Will Geer
      </a>
      (Mr. Mister). The production was directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Da_Silva" title="Howard Da Silva">
       Howard Da Silva
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and played 34 performances.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The show was revived
      <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">
       Off-Broadway
      </a>
      in 1964 in a production starring
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Orbach" title="Jerry Orbach">
       Jerry Orbach
      </a>
      (Larry Foreman),
      <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Andrews_(actress)" title="Nancy Andrews (actress)">
       Nancy Andrews
      </a>
      (Mrs. Mister), and
      <a href="/wiki/Lauri_Peters" title="Lauri Peters">
       Lauri Peters
      </a>
      (Moll), directed by Howard Da Silva.
      <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">
       Leonard Bernstein
      </a>
      acted as music supervisor. The production ran at Theatre Four for 82 performances. This production won the
      <a href="/wiki/Obie_Award" title="Obie Award">
       Obie Award
      </a>
      as Best Musical Production and Dean Dittman (who played Editor Daily) won the Obie for Distinguished Performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/The_Acting_Company" title="The Acting Company">
       The Acting Company
      </a>
      presented an Off-Broadway production at the American Place Theater from May 9, 1983 to May 29, 1983, directed by John Houseman and featuring a spoken introduction by Houseman, and starring
      <a href="/wiki/Patti_LuPone" title="Patti LuPone">
       Patti LuPone
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This production was done "on a dark stage, decorated only with chairs and Dennis Parichy's poetic lighting. At dead center is the upright piano, whose expert player, Michael Barrett, delivers the Brechtian scene-setting announcements as Blitzstein once did."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This production was continued at The Acting Company's summer home at
      <a href="/wiki/Chautauqua_Institution" title="Chautauqua Institution">
       Chautauqua Institution
      </a>
      in June of that year. During the run a man jumped onto the stage at the end of the play and screamed "Mr. Mister is still among us and the only way to defeat him is to JOIN THE COMMUNIST PARTY!"
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">

         </span>

       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_productions">
       Other productions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Opera" title="New York City Opera">
       New York City Opera
      </a>
      featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Tammy_Grimes" title="Tammy Grimes">

        <span>

     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/The_Acting_Company" title="The Acting Company">

      <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olivier_Award" title="Olivier Award">

      </a>

         ]

      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Jenkins_(actor)" title="Chris Jenkins (actor)">
       Chris Jenkins
      </a>
      and Josie Benson. It was the last show at the Arcola Street location, before the company moved to its new space, opposite the Dalston Junction station.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cradle_Will_Rock_.281999_film.29">
       <i>
        Cradle Will Rock
       </i>
       (1999 film)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Cradle_Will_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cradle Will Rock (1999 film)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Cradle_Will_Rock" title="Cradle Will Rock">
       Cradle Will Rock
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1999 writer/director
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Robbins" title="Tim Robbins">
       Tim Robbins
      </a>
      wrote a semi-fictional film recounting the original production of
      <i>
       The Cradle Will Rock
      </i>
      . The film, entitled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cradle_Will_Rock" title="Cradle Will Rock">
        Cradle Will Rock
       </a>
      </i>
      (without "The") blended the true history of Blitzstein's show with the creation (and subsequent destruction) of the original
      <a href="/wiki/Diego_Rivera" title="Diego Rivera">
       Diego Rivera
      </a>
      mural
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Man_at_the_Crossroads" title="Man at the Crossroads">
        Man at the Crossroads
       </a>
      </i>
      in the lobby of
      <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Center" title="Rockefeller Center">
       Rockefeller Center
      </a>
      (the Rivera mural was actually destroyed in 1934). Several of the original actors from the 1937 production were included as characters in the film, notably
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Olive_Stanton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Olive Stanton (page does not exist)">
       Olive Stanton
      </a>
      , John Adair, and
      <a href="/wiki/Will_Geer" title="Will Geer">
       Will Geer
      </a>
      , while others were replaced by fictional characters. Leading man
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Da_Silva" title="Howard Da Silva">
       Howard Da Silva
      </a>
      was replaced by the fictional "Aldo Silvano" (
      <a href="/wiki/John_Turturro" title="John Turturro">
       John Turturro
      </a>
      ). Although
      <a href="/wiki/Will_Geer" title="Will Geer">
       Will Geer
      </a>
      played Mr. Mister in the 1937 production, for the movie he was recast in the smaller role of the Druggist and a fictional actor named "Frank Marvel" (
      <a href="/wiki/Barnard_Hughes" title="Barnard Hughes">
       Barnard Hughes
      </a>
      ) portrayed Mr. Mister.
     </p>
     <p>
      The film's climax recreates scenes from the original, legendary performance of the show, performed by veteran Broadway performers
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Clark" title="Victoria Clark">
       Victoria Clark
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Edelman" title="Gregg Edelman">
       Gregg Edelman
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Audra_McDonald" title="Audra McDonald">
       Audra McDonald
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_Jenkins" title="Daniel Jenkins">
       Daniel Jenkins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Erin_Hill" title="Erin Hill">
       Erin Hill
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_McKinney" title="Chris McKinney">
       Chris McKinney
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Robbins wrote a book,
      <i>
       Cradle Will Rock: The Movie and the Moment
      </i>
      , as a companion to the movie; it discusses the original show, his adaptation, and the filming of the motion picture.

       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
