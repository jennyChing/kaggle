           <p>
            Brandon Fleisher is part of a new wave of teenagers braving the stock market. 
  Aged 17, he has made 24,000 so far
           </p>
           <p>
            At 17,  a 
  schoolboy  who recently made international news for amassing an 
  eight-figure fortune by trading stocks might have been one of the youngest 
  self-made multi millionaires on the planet. He would, if he hadnt made the 
  whole thing up.
           </p>
           <p>
            Presumably the imaginative teen was drunk on the fantasy of strolling around 
  in crocodile loafers and smoking cigars in a Wolf of Wall Street-style 
  existence. But this was more than just an embarrassing incident; it's a sign 
  of the times.
           </p>
           <p>
            These are tough years for young people, who are often dubbed a "doomed 
  generation". Perhaps this is why an emerging wave of them are 
  succumbing to the allure of big returns on the stock market in a bid to 
  change their fate - even if it is just on a virtual stock exchange.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some want to get rich quick while others see it as a means of building their 
  future financial security. Although most arent making millions, there are a 
  number claiming to be making real money.
           </p>
           <p>
            One of them is Brandon Fleisher, who at 17 is also too young to buy cigarettes 
  or visit a nightclub. Fleisher, who lives in Toronto, Canada, has been 
  investing real money for 14 months at the same time as building an  investment 
  website . In that time he says he has almost doubled his money from 
  27,000 to 49,000, purely by stock picking.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fleischer told the Telegraph his parents provided the initial funds after he 
  proved he could turn a profit by trading pretend money on a virtual stock 
  exchange. He invested virtual currency in companies such as Netflix   (Xetra:
            <a href="https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/q?s=NFC.DE">
             552484
            </a>

            <a href="https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/q/h?s=NFC.DE">
             news

           </p>
           <p>
            After a year his Mum, a dentist, and his Dad, who runs a metal scrap heap, 
  decided their son could be trusted with 27,000. Despite knowing very little 
  about investing themselves, they set him up a nominee account with a DIY 
  broker and left him to it.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fleisher is top in his maths class but he doesnt think this gives him the 
  edge when it comes to stock picking. He says you can be a good investor 
  without being exceptional at maths.
           </p>
           <p>
            What gives him the edge, he says, is his relentless commitment to swotting up 
  on potential stocks. Hes ferociously committed to it   typically spending 
  between one and six hours every day researching. If he cant find any stocks 
  that look attractive he simply keeps going till he finds something he likes.
           </p>
           <p>
            He is also very aware that his young age means he can afford more risk than 
  most other investors. Its all exponential, he says. You can start with 
  1000 when youre 18 and you could build up a lot over time. If you make 
  10pc a year, even, you could have a huge amount by the time youre in your 
  30s.
           </p>
           <p>
            When Fleisher gets home from school his mind is instantly drawn to investing. 
  He admits he often passes on his homework off to research companies which 
  could become his next lucrative stock.
           </p>
           <p>
            He said: When I get in I dont feel like doing my homework   especially if 
  its geography. I spend most of my time investing. I love it. The feeling 
  when your stocks are doing well is amazing.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fleisher models his own investing on the work of Peter Lynch, the American 
  fund manager who achieved the best 20-year average return on record. His 
  everyday activities include trawling through quarterly reports and annual 
  reports to look at earnings and assets. Hes mainly looking for patterns. 
  For example, if a companys earnings went up first quarter then down in 
  third   has that happened in the past? This helps him decide which companies 
  are likely to make him money.
           </p>
           <p>
            He describes himself as a growth investor, and only buys small individual 
  stocks, which he typically holds for a few months at a time. He said: If I 
  buy something I make sure Im ready to hold it for a long time if it tanks. 
  I want to invest in a company thats going to do well in the long term   
  even if Im only aiming to hold it for the short or medium term.
           </p>
           <p>
            At 17, Fleisher is more confident about his own stock picking abilities than 
  most seasoned investors. He has so much faith in himself, that he has 
  invested his entire 49,000 portfolio in one stock (which he doesnt want us 
  to name) with a market cap of around 20m.
           </p>
           <p>
            Companies he's bought and sold include media firm InterActiveCorp, Disney, TV 
  and internet firm J-com and Ryder, an American trucking company.
           </p>
           <p>
            Hes never owned a fund in his life and said he never would. This is because 
  he thinks the whole point of investing is beating the market, which hed be 
  less likely to do with a fund.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fleisher says his biggest mistake when investing has been feeling too scared 
  to buy stocks at the right time, causing him to miss out on opportunities. 
  But he says he is learning to trust himself more and more. He says his 
  biggest struggle is knowing when to sell a stock. Usually he sells when he 
  sees other companies which look so attractive that he can no longer resist 
  buying them.
           </p>
           <p>
            He advises cautious investors to learn until youre not nervous anymore.
           </p>
           <p>
            A lot of people get scared out of stocks. A lot of them jump a bit and then 
  go back up. My advice is do your do your homework and trust the numbers, he 
  says.
           </p>
           <p>
            His track record is unsurprisingly short, but Fleisher does appear to practice 
  what he preaches. The day after he bought InterActiveCorp its shares 
  plummeted by 12pc. Thats when he doubled his position. By the time the 
  shares were back up at the original price he had made 6pc, and when he sold 
  the shares two months later, he was up 30pc.
           </p>
           <p>
            He believes a lot of investors go wrong because they dont research properly. 
  If you didnt research a stock and its shares go down in value, its your 
  fault. Learn from your mistakes. People also buy stocks because they are a 
  great company. But theres a difference between a good stock and a good 
  company. For example, Netflix and Tesla were good stocks when I bought them, 
  but they are overpriced now. Netflix has a price to earnings ratio of 84 
  which is really high, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            I ask him why he thinks most teenagers his age dont invest, and he says he 
  thinks young people are in a mind set where they just want to have fun. 
  They think they can work hard later in life and thats the best thing. But 
  to me, this doesnt feel like work. This is my fun.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fleisher is currently single. He says he is looking for a girlfriend though, 
  preferably a lady who is also an avid investor herself. I ask him why he 
  wants to make money from investing and he says that while he would 
  definitely like to be a multi-millionaire in the future, what he really 
  wants is to make enough money to have children and support them financially.
           </p>
           <p>
            Fleisher claims to know quite a few other kids his age that also invest real 
  money on the stock market, although he says they dont go around shouting 
  about how much theyre making. If people ask, Ill tell them, but my 
  investing friends and I tend to keep it to ourselves. We like to discuss 
  investing ideas with each other but we dont really tell each other how much 
  money weve got.
           </p>
           <p>
            *Fleisher invests with TD Direct which has confirmed that his account is 
  live. The Telegraph has also seen a screenshot of this live portfolio.*
           </p>
