     <p>
      <b>
       Iron sights
      </b>
      are a system of shaped alignment markers (usually metal) used as a
      <a href="/wiki/Sight_(device)" title="Sight (device)">
       sighting device
      </a>
      to assist in the aiming of a device such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">
       firearm
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">
       crossbow
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
       telescope
      </a>
      , and exclude the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">
       optics
      </a>
      as in
      <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight" title="Telescopic sight">
       telescopic sights
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Reflector_sight" title="Reflector sight">
       reflector (reflex) sights
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Iron sights are typically composed of two component sights, formed by metal blades: a rear sight mounted perpendicular to the line of sight and a front sight that is a post, bead, or ring.
      <i>
       Open sights
      </i>
      use a notch of some sort as the rear sight, while
      <i>
       aperture sights
      </i>
      use some form of a circular hole. Civilian, hunting, and police firearms usually feature open sights, while many military
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_rifle" title="Battle rifle">
       battle rifles
      </a>
      employ aperture sights.
     </p>
     <p>
      The earliest and simplest iron sights are fixed and cannot be easily adjusted. Many iron sights are designed to be adjustable, so that the sights can be adjusted for windage and elevation. On many firearms the rear sight is adjustable for
      <a href="/wiki/Elevation_(ballistics)" title="Elevation (ballistics)">
       elevation
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Windage" title="Windage">
       windage
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For precision applications such as
      <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">
       hunting
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">
       sniping
      </a>
      the iron sights are usually replaced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight" title="Telescopic sight">
       telescopic sight
      </a>
      . Iron sights may still be fitted alongside other sighting devices (or in the case of some models of optics, incorporated integrally) for back-up usage.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">
       Theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuzzy_sight_picture.svg">

       </a>

     </div>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_040424-N-5319A-003_Aviation_Warfare_Systems_Operator_2nd_Class_Patrick_J.P._Neeley,_from_Plainfield,_Ind.,_fires_a_.50_cal_machine_gun_out_of_an_SH-60B_helicopter.jpg" title="Enlarge">

        </div>
        Aerial gunnery iron ring sight on a GAU-21 that allows for compensating for the roll-pitch-yaw of an aircraft.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the case of firearms, where the
      <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">
       projectile
      </a>
      follows a Newtonian
      <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">
       trajectory
      </a>
      , front and rear sights must be aligned with the line of sight of the shooter to the target, known as the 'Point of Aim' (POA), calibrated to the distance of the target and the trajectory of the bullet, so that the bullet hits the target at the 'Point of Impact' (POI). Iron sights provide horizontal and vertical reference points that allow the shooter to train the weapon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Rear sights are usually mounted in a
      <a href="/wiki/Dovetail_joint" title="Dovetail joint">
       dovetail
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">
       barrel
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Receiver_(firearms)" title="Receiver (firearms)">
       receiver
      </a>
      , closer to the eye of the shooter, allowing for easy
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">
       visual
      </a>
      pick-up of the notch. Front sights are mounted to the barrel by dovetailing, sweat soldering, screwing, or staking close to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muzzle_(firearm)" title="Muzzle (firearm)">
       muzzle
      </a>
      , frequently on a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ramp_(firearms)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ramp (firearms) (page does not exist)">
       ramp
      </a>
      . Some front sight assemblies include a detachable hood intended to reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Glare_(vision)" title="Glare (vision)">
       glare
      </a>
      and if the hood is circular, then this provides a reference where the eye will naturally align one within the other.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      With typical blade or post iron sights, the shooter would center the front post in the notch of the rear sight and the tops of both sights should be level.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since the eye is only capable of focusing on one plane, and the rear sight, front sight, and target are all in separate planes, only one of those three planes can be in focus. Which plane is in focus depends on the type of sight, and one of the challenges to a shooter is to keep the focus on the correct plane to allow for best sight alignment.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even a tiny error in the angle of sight alignment results in a trajectory that diverges from the target on a trajectory directly relative to the distance from the target, causing the bullet to miss the target; for example with a
      <a href="/wiki/10_metre_air_rifle" title="10 metre air rifle">
       10 metre air rifle
      </a>
      shooter trying to hit the 10 ring, which is merely a 0.5mm (0.020in) diameter dot on the target at 10m (33ft) and with a 4.5mm (0.18in) diameter
      <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(air_gun)" title="Pellet (air gun)">
       pellet
      </a>
      , an error of only 0.2mm (0.0079in) in sight alignment can mean a complete miss (a 3mm (0.12in) point of impact miss). At 1,000m (3,300ft), that same misalignment would be magnified 100 times, giving an error of over 300mm (12in), 1500 times the sight misalignment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        note 1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A long sighting line, called the 'sight radius', helps to reduce eventual angle errors and will, in case the sight has an incremental adjustment mechanism, adjust in smaller increments when compared to a further identical shorter sighting line.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sights for shotguns used for shooting small, moving targets (wing shooting or clay shooting) work quite differently. The rear sight is completely discarded, and the rear reference point is provided by the correct and consistent positioning of the shooter's head. A brightly colored (generally
      <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">
       brass
      </a>
      or silver colored, white, or a fluorescent shade) round bead is placed at the end of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">
       barrel
      </a>
      . Often this bead will be placed along a raised, flat
      <i>
       rib
      </i>
      , which is usually ventilated to keep it cool and reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Mirage" title="Mirage">
       mirage
      </a>
      effects from a hot barrel. Rather than being aimed like a rifle or handgun, the shotgun is pointed with the focus always on the target, and the unfocused image of the barrel and bead are placed below the target (the amount below depends on whether the target is rising or falling) and slightly ahead of the target if there is lateral movement. This method of aiming is not as precise as that of a front sight/rear sight combination, but it is much faster, and the wide spread of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shotshell" title="Shotshell">
       shot
      </a>
      allows a hit even if there is some error in aim. Some shotguns also provide a
      <i>
       mid-bead
      </i>
      , which is a smaller bead located halfway down the rib, which allows more feedback on barrel alignment.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Open_sights">
       Open sights
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Open sights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Open_sight_types.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="140" data-file-width="810" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Open_sight_types.svg/600px-Open_sight_types.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Open_sight_types.svg/900px-Open_sight_types.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Open_sight_types.svg/1200px-Open_sight_types.svg.png 2x" width="600"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Open_sight_types.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A selection of open sights, and one aperture sight suitable for use with long eye relief: A) U-notch and post, B) Patridge, C) V-notch and post, D) Express, E) U-notch and bead, F) V-notch and bead, G) trapezoid, H) ghost ring. The gray dot represents the target
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Open sights generally are used where the rear sight is at significant distance from the shooter's eye. They provide minimum occlusion of the shooter's view, but at the expense of precision. Open sights generally use either a square
      <i>
       post
      </i>
      or a bead on a post for a front sight. The post or bead is placed in the rear sight notch, and the target is placed above and centered on the aligned sights. From the shooter's point of view, there should be a noticeable space between each side of the front sight and the edges of the notch; the spaces are called
      <i>
       light bars
      </i>
      , and the brightness of the light bars provides the shooter feedback as to the alignment of the post in the notch. Vertical alignment is done by lining up the top of the front post with the top of the rear sight, or by placing the bead just above the bottom of the V or U-notch. If the post is not centered in the V or U notch, the shot will not be accurate. If the post extends over the V or U-notch it will result in a high shot. If the post does not reach the top of the V or U-notch it will result in a low shot.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Patridge
      </i>
      sights, named after inventor E. E. Patridge, a 19th-century American sportsman, consist of a square or rectangular post and a flat-bottomed square notch and are the most common form of open sights, being preferred for target shooting, as the majority of shooters find the vertical alignment is more precise than other open sights.
      <i>
       V-notch
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       U-notch
      </i>
      sights are a variant of the patridge which substitute a 'V' or 'U' shaped rear notch.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other common open sight types include the
      <i>
       buckhorn
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       semi-buckhorn
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       express
      </i>
      . Buckhorn sights have extensions protruding from either side of the rear sight forming a large ring which almost meets directly above the 'V' of the notch. The semi-buckhorn is similar but has a wider gently curving notch with the more precise 'V' at its center and is standard on classic
      <a href="/wiki/Winchester_Repeating_Arms_Company" title="Winchester Repeating Arms Company">
       Winchester
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marlin_Firearms" title="Marlin Firearms">
       Marlin
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Lever-action" title="Lever-action">
       lever-action
      </a>
      rifles. Express sights are most often used on heavy caliber rifles intended for the hunting of dangerous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Five_game" title="Big Five game">
       big game
      </a>
      , and are in the form of a wide and large 'V' with a heavy white contrast line marking its bottom and a big white or gold bead front sight. In cases where the range is close and speed far outweighs accuracy (e.g. the shooter is being charged by dangerous big-game), the front sight is used like a shotgun bead; the rear sight is ignored, and the bead is placed on the target. When more time is available, the bead is placed in the 'V' of the rear sight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Open sights have many advantages: they are very common, inexpensive to produce, uncomplicated to use, sturdy, lightweight, resistant to severe environmental conditions, and they do not require batteries. On the other hand, they are not as precise as other forms of sights, and are difficult or impossible to adjust. Open sights also take much more time to usethe buckhorn type is the slowest, patridge, 'U' and 'V' type notch sights are only a bit quicker; only the express sight is relatively fast. In addition, open sights tend to block out the lower portion of the shooter's
      <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
       field of view
      </a>
      by nature, and because of the
      <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">
       depth of field
      </a>
      limitations of the human eye, do not work as well for shooters with less than perfect vision.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aperture_sights">
       Aperture sights
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aperture sights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg/220px-Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg/330px-Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg/440px-Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caroline-mire-p1000518.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Rear, rotating diopter drum sight of a
        <a href="/wiki/SIG_SG_550" title="SIG SG 550">
         SIG SG 550
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Assault_rifle" title="Assault rifle">
         assault rifle
        </a>
        . The viewing aperture above the "3" (denoting the 300 m setting) can be seen
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="691" data-file-width="1399" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg/220px-Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg/330px-Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg/440px-Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aperture_depth_of_field.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Pictures taken under identical conditions through large (left) and small (right) diameter aperture sights, with camera focused on front sight
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Aperture sights, also known as
      <i>
       peep sights
      </i>
      , range from the
      <i>
       ghost ring
      </i>
      sight, whose thin ring blurs to near invisibility (hence
      <i>
       ghost
      </i>
      ), to target aperture sights that use large disks or other occluders with pinhole-sized apertures. In general, the thicker the ring, the more precise the sight, and the thinner the ring, the faster the sight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The theory of operation behind the aperture sight is often stated that the human eye will automatically center the front sight when looking through the rear aperture, thus ensuring accuracy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hawks1_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-hawks1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, aperture sights are accurate even if the front sight is not centered in the rear aperture due to a phenomenon called parallax suppression.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is because, when the aperture is smaller than the eye's pupil diameter, the aperture itself becomes the
      <a href="/wiki/Entrance_pupil" title="Entrance pupil">
       entrance pupil
      </a>
      for the entire optical system of target, front sight post, rear aperture, and eye. As long as the aperture's diameter is completely contained within the eye's pupil diameter, the exact visual location of the front sight post within the rear aperture ring does not affect the accuracy, and accuracy only starts to degrade slightly due to parallax shift as the aperture's diameter begins to encroach on the outside of the eye's pupil diameter. An additional benefit to aperture sights is that smaller apertures provide greater
      <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">
       depth of field
      </a>
      , making the target less blurry when focusing on the front sight.
     </p>
     <p>
      These sights are used on target
      <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">
       rifles
      </a>
      of several disciplines and on several military rifles such as the
      <a href="/wiki/M1_Garand" title="M1 Garand">
       M1 Garand
      </a>
      , the No. 4 series
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee-Enfield" title="Lee-Enfield">
       Enfields
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/M16_rifle" title="M16 rifle">
       M16
      </a>
      series of weapons along with several others. Peep sights are very effective in poor lighting, and have found favor with a few hunters who hunt in heavy cover.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ghost_rings">
       Ghost rings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ghost rings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The ghost ring sight is considered by some to be the fastest type of aperture sight.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">
         <span title="This statement is possibly biased. (January 2012)">
          neutrality
         </span>
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Talk:Iron_sights" title="Talk:Iron sights">
         disputed
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      It is fairly accurate, easy to use, and obscures the target less than nearly all other non-optical sights. Because of this, ghost ring sights are commonly installed on combat shotguns and submachine guns (and to a much lesser extent, rifles). The ghost ring is a fairly recent innovation, and differs from traditional aperture sights in the extreme thinness of the rear ring, and the slightly thicker front sight. The thin ring minimizes the occlusion of the target, while the thicker front post makes it easy to find quickly.
     </p>
     <p>
      A ghost ring can also take form of a tube ranging from 3 to 6cm long, with the post mounted at the inside end of the tube. This type of ghost ring is used when there can be no front sight at the front end of the barrel. It is slightly slower to use, because the shooter's eye has to focus close on the post, then focus on the target. However, in situations when extremely fast sighting is required, the shooter can simply look through the tube, and it is simple to see if the view is straight down the tube.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Target_aperture_sights">
       Target aperture sights
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Target aperture sights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rear_aperture_sight.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Rear_aperture_sight.jpg/220px-Rear_aperture_sight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Rear_aperture_sight.jpg/330px-Rear_aperture_sight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Rear_aperture_sight.jpg/440px-Rear_aperture_sight.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rear_aperture_sight.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Rear aperture of a BRNO target sight. Note large disk and small aperture
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Front_globe_sight.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Front_globe_sight.jpg/220px-Front_globe_sight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Front_globe_sight.jpg/330px-Front_globe_sight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Front_globe_sight.jpg/440px-Front_globe_sight.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

      </div>

        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="768" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/87/Diopter_sight_picture.jpg/220px-Diopter_sight_picture.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/87/Diopter_sight_picture.jpg/330px-Diopter_sight_picture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/87/Diopter_sight_picture.jpg/440px-Diopter_sight_picture.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

        <a href="/wiki/10_metre_air_rifle" title="10 metre air rifle">
         10 metre air rifle
        </a>
        target diopter and globe aperture sight picture
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Target aperture sights are designed for maximum precision. The rear sight element (often called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Diopter_sight" title="Diopter sight">
        diopter
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is usually a large disk (up to 1inch or 2.5cm in diameter) with a small hole in the middle, and is placed close to the shooter's eye. High end target diopters normally accept accessories like adjustable diopter aperture and optical filter systems to ensure optimal sighting conditions for match shooters. Typical modern target shooting diopters offer windage and elevation corrections in 2mm (0.079in) to 4mm (0.157in) increments at 100m (109.4yd). Some
      <a href="/wiki/International_Shooting_Sport_Federation" title="International Shooting Sport Federation">
       International Shooting Sport Federation
      </a>
      (ISSF) (Olympic) shooting events require this precision level for sighting lines, since the score of the top competitors last 10 shots is expressed in tenths of scoring ring points.
     </p>
     <p>
      The complementing front sight element may be a simple bead or post, but is more often a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Globe_sight" title="Globe sight">
        globe
       </a>
      </i>
      type sight, which consists of a
      <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)" title="Cylinder (geometry)">
       cylinder
      </a>
      with a threaded cap, which allows differently shaped removable front sight elements to be used. Most common are posts of varying widths and heights or rings of varying diameterthese can be chosen by the shooter for the best fit to the target being used. Tinted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)">
       transparent
      </a>
      plastic insert elements may also be used, with a hole in the middle; these work the same way as an opaque ring, but provide a less obstructed view of the target. High end target front sight tunnels normally also accept accessories like adjustable aperture and optical systems to ensure optimal sighting conditions for match shooters. Some high end target sight line manufacturers also offer front sights with integrated aperture mechanisms.
     </p>
     <p>
      The use of round rear and front sighting elements for aiming at round targets, like used in ISSF match shooting, takes advantage of the natural ability of the eye and brain to easily align
      <a href="/wiki/Concentric" title="Concentric">
       concentric
      </a>
      circles. Even for the maximum precision, there should still be a significant area of white visible around the bullseye and between the front and rear sight ring (if a front ring is being used). Since the best key to determining center is the amount of light passing through the apertures, a narrow, dim ring of light can actually be more difficult to work with than a larger, brighter ring. The precise sizes are quite subjective, and depend on both shooter preference and ambient lighting, which is why target rifles come with easily replaceable front sight inserts, and adjustable aperture mechanisms.
     </p>
     <p>
      Rifles from the late 19th century often featured one of two types of aperture sight called a
      <i>
       tang sight
      </i>
      or a
      <i>
       ladder sight
      </i>
      . Since the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">
       black powder
      </a>
      used in
      <a href="/wiki/Muzzleloader" title="Muzzleloader">
       muzzleloaders
      </a>
      and early cartridges was not capable of propelling a bullet at high velocity, these sights had very large ranges of vertical adjustments, often on the order of several degrees, allowing very long shots to be made accurately. The
      <a href="/wiki/.45-70" title=".45-70">
       .45-70
      </a>
      cartridge, for example, was tested by the military for accuracy at ranges of up to 1500 yards, which required 3
      <span class="frac nowrap">
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
      </span>
      degrees of elevation. Both ladder and tang sights folded down when not in use to reduce the chance of damage to the sights. Ladder sights were mounted on the barrel, and could be used as sights in both the folded and unfolded states. Tang sights were mounted behind the action of the rifle, and provided a very long sight radius, and had to be unfolded for use, though rifles with tang sights often had open sights as well for close range use. Tang sights often had
      <a href="/wiki/Vernier_scale" title="Vernier scale">
       vernier scales
      </a>
      , allowing adjustment down to a single minute of arc over the full range of the sight.
     </p>
     <p>
      Aperture sights on military rifles use a larger aperture with a thinner ring, and generally a simple post front sight. The extreme case of this is the ghost ring sight, a relatively recent innovation that may be the fastest type of iron sight to use, while still providing a degree of precision comparable to or better than most open sights. Ghost ring sights are commonly found on
      <a href="/wiki/Riot_shotgun" title="Riot shotgun">
       riot
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Combat_shotgun" title="Combat shotgun">
       combat shotguns
      </a>
      and customized
      <a href="/wiki/Handgun" title="Handgun">
       handguns
      </a>
      , and they are also gaining ground as a backup sighting system on
      <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">
       rifles
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shotgun_beads">
       Shotgun beads
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shotgun beads">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Since shotgun beads are only used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_vision" title="Peripheral vision">
       peripheral vision
      </a>
      , generally a larger, brighter bead works best.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" title="Fiber optic">
       Fiber optic
      </a>
      sights are becoming popular for shotguns, as they greatly increase the brightness of the bead by collecting light and directing it to the shooter's eye. Since the "rear sight" in the case of a shotgun is the shooter's eye position, adjusting the "sights" on a shotgun consists primarily of adjusting the stock to fit the shooter as well as possible.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bead sights are inferior in practical accuracy compared to rifle and ghost-ring sights, as they provide no rear sight to verify correct alignment with the front sight. While bead sights may be acceptable on sporting shotguns, they are best avoided for use on combat shotguns.
     </p>
     <p>
      The primary advantage of bead sights is that they do not interfere with the shooter's field of vision, allowing extremely rapid target acquisition, which is essential for fast moving targets such as clays and game. They are less expensive than rifle and ghost-ring sights.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adjustment">
       Adjustment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Adjustment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="259" data-file-width="480" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg/220px-Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg/330px-Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg/440px-Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cz452_tangent_sight.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A tangent sight on a
        <a href="/wiki/CZ_452" title="CZ 452">
         CZ 452
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Rimfire_ammunition" title="Rimfire ammunition">
         rimfire
        </a>
        rifle, with calibrated markings for ranges out to 300 meters
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fixed sights are sights that are not adjustable. For instance, on many
      <a href="/wiki/Revolver" title="Revolver">
       revolvers
      </a>
      , the rear sight consists of a fixed sight that is a groove milled into the top of the gun's receiver. Adjustable sights are designed to be adjustable for different ranges, for the effect of
      <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">
       wind
      </a>
      , or to compensate for varying cartridge
      <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">
       bullet
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">
       weights
      </a>
      or propellant
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_ballistics#Propellant_charge" title="Internal ballistics">
       loadings
      </a>
      , which alter the round's
      <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
       velocity
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/External_ballistics" title="External ballistics">
       external ballistics
      </a>
      and thus its trajectory and point of impact. Sight adjustments are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal" title="Orthogonal">
       orthogonal
      </a>
      , so the windage can be adjusted without impacting the elevation, and vice versa. If the firearm is held canted instead of level when fired, the adjustments are no longer orthogonal, so it is essential to keep the firearm level for best accuracy.
     </p>
     <p>
      The downside to adjustable sights is the inherent fragility of the moving parts. A fixed sight is a solid piece of metal, usually steel, and if firmly attached to the gun, little is going to be able to damage it beyond usefulness. Adjustable sights, on the other hand, are bulkier, and have parts that must move relative to the gun. Solid impact on an adjustable sight will usually knock it out of adjustment, if not knock it right off the gun. Because of this, guns for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_defense" title="Self defense">
       self defense
      </a>
      or military use either have fixed sights, or sights with "wings" on the sides for protection (such as those on the M4 carbine).
     </p>
     <p>
      Iron sights used for hunting guns tend to be a compromise. They will be adjustable, but only with toolsgenerally either a small
      <a href="/wiki/Screwdriver" title="Screwdriver">
       screwdriver
      </a>
      or an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_wrench" title="Allen wrench">
       allen wrench
      </a>
      . They will be compact and heavily built, and designed to lock securely into position. Target sights, on the other hand, are much bulkier and easier to adjust. They generally have large knobs to control horizontal and vertical movement without tools, and often they are designed to be quickly and easily detachable from the gun so they can be stored separately in their own protective case.
     </p>
     <p>
      The most common is a rear sight that adjusts in both directions, though military rifles often have a
      <i>
       tangent sight
      </i>
      in the rear, which a slider on the rear sight has pre-calibrated elevation adjustments for different ranges. With tangent sights, the rear sight is often used to adjust the elevation, and the front the windage. The
      <a href="/wiki/M16_rifle#M16A2" title="M16 rifle">
       M16A2
      </a>
      later M16 series rifles have a dial adjustable range calibrated rear sight, and use an elevation adjustable front sight to "zero" the rifle at a given range. The rear sight is used for windage adjustment and to change the zero range.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Enhancements">
       Enhancements
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Enhancements">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While iron sights are basically very simple, that simplicity also leads to a staggering variety of different implementations. In addition to the purely geometric considerations of the front blade and rear notch, there are some factors that need to be considered when choosing a set of iron sights for a particular purpose.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Glare_reduction">
       Glare reduction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_sights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Glare reduction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Glare, particularly from the front sight, can be a significant problem with iron sights. The glare from the front sight can increase the apparent brightness of the light bar on one side of the sight, causing windage errors in aiming, or lower the apparent height of the front sight, causing elevation errors in aiming. Since the direction of the ambient light is rarely constant for a shooter, the resulting changing glare can significantly affect the point of aim.
     </p>
     <p>
      The most common solution to the problem of glare is a matte finish on the sights. Serrating or bead blasting the sight is a common solution for brightly finished sights, such as blued steel or stainless steel. Matte finishes such as
      <a href="/wiki/Parkerizing" title="Parkerizing">
       parkerizing
      </a>
      or matte black paint can also help. "Smoking" a sight by holding a match or cigarette lighter under the sight to deposit a fine layer of soot is a common technique used by many shooters, and in fact special soot producing cigarette type lighters are sold for use by competition shooters. Even a thin layer of mud or dirt applied to the sight will help kill the glare, as long as the coating is thin and consistent enough not to change the shape of the sights.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many target sights are designed with vertical or even undercut front sight blades, which reduces the angles at which light will produce glare off the sightthe downside of these sights is that they tend to snag on clothing, branches, and other materials, so they are only common on target guns. Sight hoods reduce the chances of snagging an undercut sight and are common on some types of rifles, particularly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lever_action" title="Lever action">
       lever action
      </a>
      rifles, but they are prohibited in some shooting disciplines such as some classes of handgun
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_silhouette" title="Metallic silhouette">
       metallic silhouette
      </a>
      shooting.
     </p>

     </div>
     <p>
      While target shooters generally prefer a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloss_(material_appearance)" title="Gloss (material appearance)">
       matte
      </a>
      black finish to their sights, to reduce the chance of glare and increase the contrast between the sights and the light bars, black sights don't offer good visibility with dark targets or in low light conditions, such as those often encountered in hunting, military, or self-defense situations. A variety of different contrast enhancements to the basic Patridge type sight and others have been developed to address this deficiency. It should be noted that the contrast enhancement of the front sight has to be somewhat larger compared to the contrast enhancement(s) used for the rear sight if all contrast enhancements should appear about equally large from the shooters perspective.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Three-dot
      </dt>
      <dd>
       On semi-automatic handguns, the most common type of enhancement is a bright white dot painted on the front sight near the top of the blade, and a dot on each side of the rear sight notch. In low lighting conditions the front sight dot is centered horizontally between the rear sight dots, with the target placed above the middle (front) dot. Some sight vendors offer differently colored dots for the front and rear sights.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novak_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-novak-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       White outline rear
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A contrast variation which uses a dot front sight with a thick and bright white outline around the rear sight notch.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Straight Eight
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Heinie Specialty Products produces a variant of high visibility sights in which a single dot front sight and a rear notch with a dot below can be lined up vertically to form a figure "eight".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Sight inserts
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Popular on revolvers, this enhancement consists of a colored plastic insert in the front sight blade, usually red or orange in color.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Bar / dot or express sight
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Similar to the Straight Eight type, this type of sight is traditional on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Express_rifle" title="Express rifle">
        express rifles
       </a>
       and is also found on some handguns. The open, V-shaped rear allows for faster acquisition and wider field of view, though less accurate for longer range precision type shooting. The dot on the front sight is aligned or set directly above the vertical bar on the rear sight, commonly referred to as "dotting the 'I'".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novak_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-novak-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Gold bead
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Preferred by many competitors in IPSC and IDPA shooting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novak_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-novak-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Night sights
      </dt>
      <dd>
       On tactical firearms, the contrast enhancements can consist of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traser" title="Traser">
        trasers
       </a>
       containing
       <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">
        tritium
       </a>
       gas whose
       <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
        radioactive decay
       </a>
       causes a
       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">
        fluorescent
       </a>
       material to glow. Self-luminous tritium sights provide vital visibility in extremely low light situations where normal sights would be degraded or even useless.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The tritium glow is not noticeable in bright conditions such as during daylight however. As a result, some manufacturers have started to integrate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" title="Fiber optic">
        fiber optic
       </a>
       sights with tritium vials to provide bright, high-contrast firearms sights in both bright and dim conditions.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Fiber optic
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A growing trend, started on air rifles and muzzleloaders, is the use of short pieces of
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">
        optical fiber
       </a>
       for the dots, made in such a way that ambient light falling on the length of the fiber is concentrated at the tip, making the dots slightly brighter than the surroundings.This method is most commonly used in front sights, but many makers offer sights that use fiber optics on front and rear sights. Fiber optic sights can now be found on handguns, rifles, and shotguns, both as aftermarket accessories and a growing number of factory guns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
