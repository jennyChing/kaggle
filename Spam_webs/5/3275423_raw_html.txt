   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Sociomapping
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Sociomapping
      </b>
      is a method developed for
      <a href="/wiki/Data_analysis" title="Data analysis">
       processing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Information_visualization" title="Information visualization">
       visualization
      </a>
      of relational data (e.g. social network data). It is most commonly used for mapping the social structure within small teams (10-25 people). Sociomapping uses the landscape metaphor to display complex multi-dimensional data in a 3D
      <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">
       map
      </a>
      , where individual objects are localized in such way that their distance on the map corresponds to their distance in the underlying data.
     </p>
     <p>
      Thanks to its visual coding Sociomapping engages our evolved skills for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spatial_orientation" title="Spatial orientation">
       spatial orientation
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Movement_detection&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Movement detection (page does not exist)">
       movement detection
      </a>
      , thus making the interpretation of complex data easy and accessible for everyone.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The sociomapping method was developed in 1993-1994 by R. Bahbouh as a tool that would facilitate understanding of data about social relations and help preventing conflicts within teams of military professionals. The first major application of sociomapping took place in 1994-1995 during the HUBES experiment (Human Behavior in Extended Spaceflight)  a 135-day-long simulation of a spaceflight with three crew members organized by
      <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
       European Space Agency
      </a>
      . Sociomapping was then regularly used in other spaceflight simulations (1995-1996: EKOPSY, 1999: Mars105, 2010-2012:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars500" title="Mars500">
       Mars500
      </a>
      ). Since 2005, sociomapping has been extensively used in business environment to analyze relationships within senior management teams. In 2012, C. Hschl jr. developed Real Time Sociomapping software that enables customers to do team sociomapping on the spot and is used for commercial purposes.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_principle">
       Basic principle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basic principle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The basic principle of Sociomapping is transforming original data concerning a set of objects in such a way that the distance of each pair of objects on the map corresponds to the distance between the two objects in the underlying data. Transformation of the data is a matter of 1) choosing some metric that could be reasonably interpreted as distance, and 2) translating the multi-dimensional distance matrix into 2D coordinate system so that the correlation between map-distances and data-distances is maximized. The algorithm for data-transformation, developed by C. Hschl jr., is thus a
      <a href="/wiki/Dimensionality_reduction" title="Dimensionality reduction">
       dimensionality-reduction
      </a>
      technique, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Principal_component_analysis" title="Principal component analysis">
       PCA
      </a>
      , and its goodness of fit can be measured by
      <a href="/wiki/Spearman%27s_rank_correlation_coefficient" title="Spearman's rank correlation coefficient">
       Spearman correlation
      </a>
      between the map-distances and data-distances. Sociomapping takes into account that, particularly in case of social relations, relational data may be asymmetrical (e.g. John like Mary more than she like him) and preserves this information by mapping the objects in such a way that the for each object the closest other object is the one closest to it according to the metric of choice in the underlying data, and so on for other objects ordered by distance.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Application">
       Application
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Application">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are two main areas of application for Sociomapping - groups (small systems) and populations (large systems). For each area a different method of visualization and data transformation is used in order to facilitate peoples ability to understand and interpret the analyzed data.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Groups_and_small_systems_Sociomapping">
       Groups and small systems Sociomapping
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Groups and small systems Sociomapping">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Sociomapping for small systems produces Sociomaps of subjects. These subjects (in most cases people) are placed on the Sociomap reflecting their distance measured in various ways:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Social_distance" title="Social distance">
        social distance
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       psychological distance (psychological similarity)
      </li>
      <li>
       communication distance
      </li>
      <li>
       other relation measures
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Besides the distances between the group members, Sociomap shows additional variable coded in the height (or color) of the subject. Typical variables used for the height are: social status,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Key_performance_indicators" title="Key performance indicators">
       performance indicators
      </a>
      of the subjects, average communication frequency, etc.
     </p>
     <p>
      Understanding the relative distances between the people helps to understand the structure of the group, find subgroups formed by groups members and discover functions of the group members. In connection to the height Sociomap enables complex and comprehensive insight into the groups and small systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sociomapping of small systems produces similar results to
      <a href="/wiki/Social_network_analysis" title="Social network analysis">
       Social network analysis
      </a>
      with additional visualization features.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Profile_analysis">
       Profile analysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Profile analysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Besides the small systems analysis based on various relational data, Sociomapping can be used to visualize the profiles of unrelated subjects. This is done by transformation of profiles of the subjects, computing the distances between the profiles and visualizing them in a Sociomap. There is a software to compute Profile analysis (see section Sociomapping software)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Populations_and_Large_systems_Sociomapping">
       Populations and Large systems Sociomapping
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Populations and Large systems Sociomapping">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For large systems and populations, different type of Sociomaps is used. Data used for these type of maps are rectangular
      <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">
       matrices
      </a>
      , where for each subject there is a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Preference_vector&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Preference vector (page does not exist)">
       preference vector
      </a>
      of selected objects (such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_parties" title="Political parties">
       political parties
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brands" title="Brands">
       brands
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">
       products
      </a>
      , and so on). In order to create a Sociomap, for each subject a position in the map is determined, and a small piece of mass representing this subject is placed on the map according to its vector of preferences to an object. As a result, there are places on the Sociomap where more subject are placed (hills) and where there are no subject (valleys). Therefore, hills are formed on the places representing typical preference configurations and this allows for visual cluster analysis, or segmentation. In this sense, Large systems Sociomapping is a
      <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">
       data mining
      </a>
      approach based on visual
      <a href="/wiki/Pattern_recognition" title="Pattern recognition">
       pattern recognition
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Typical uses for Large systems Sociomapping are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">
        political research
       </a>
       (preferences and polls)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Marketing_research" title="Marketing research">
        marketing research
       </a>
       (attitudes and product preferences)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Customer_segmentation" title="Customer segmentation">
        customer segmentation
       </a>
       (behavioral patterns)
      </li>
      <li>
       and other research concerned with preferences and/or attitudes.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Broader_scope_of_application">
       Broader scope of application
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Broader scope of application">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sociomapping has broader scope of application, including the following fields:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Historical events analysis (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">
        RMS Titanic
       </a>
       survival rate)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_community" title="Internet community">
        Internet community
       </a>
       tools (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Last.fm" title="Last.fm">
        Last.fm
       </a>
       <a class="external autonumber" href="http://qed-portal.com/en/last_fm/tool/" rel="nofollow">
        [1]
       </a>
       profile similarity)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        Literature
       </a>
       analysis (e.g. analysis of communication in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare_plays" title="Shakespeare plays">
        Shakespeare plays
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">
        Military
       </a>
       units and
       <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">
        human spaceflight
       </a>
       crews analysis
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">
        Sport
       </a>
       analysis and forecast (e.g. cooperation of sport teams)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">
        Politics
       </a>
       analysis and forecast
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Team_building" title="Team building">
        Team building
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Team analysis (communication, cooperation, decision-making)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Software">
       Software
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociomapping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Software">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      So far only one software tool based on Sociomapping was released.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Team profile analyzer
      </b>
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.teamprofileanalyzer.com/" rel="nofollow">
       [2]
      </a>
      is a tool for psychologists, consultants, managers and HR specialists. It enables integration of various sources of information about team from personality, performance or knowledge
      <a href="/wiki/Psychological_testing" title="Psychological testing">
       tests
      </a>
      and biographical data. It can be used for team analysis and development: team coaching,
      <a href="/wiki/Team_building" title="Team building">
       team building
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Recruitment" title="Recruitment">
       recruitment
      </a>
      etc.
     </p>
