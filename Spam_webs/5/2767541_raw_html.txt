       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        Chronic bronchitis is a common
        <a href="http://www.successandyouth.com/" target="_blank" title="health complication">
         health complication
        </a>
        among patients with chronic obstructive disease (COPD) and it may highlight a subtype of the disease that places patients at risk for increased severity and exacerbations, say researchers.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: center;">
        <a href="http://www.successandyouth.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/COPD-Subtype-May-Be-Indicated-By-Chronic-Bronchitis.jpg" target="_blank">
         <img alt="COPD Subtype May Be Indicated By Chronic Bronchitis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1949" height="290" src="http://www.successandyouth.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/COPD-Subtype-May-Be-Indicated-By-Chronic-Bronchitis.jpg" title="COPD Subtype May Be Indicated By Chronic Bronchitis" width="504"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        Jean Corhay (CHU Sart-Tilman, Lige, Belgium) and colleagues studied 974 patients with Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease (GOLD) stage 24 disease and found that 64 percent had
        <a href="http://www.successandyouth.com/" target="_blank" title="chronic bronchitis">
         chronic bronchitis
        </a>
        . These affected patients had significantly more chronic respiratory failure, cachexia, and skeletal muscle wasting than other patients, and a greater number of packyears smoking, poorer spirometry results, and were more likely to have emphysema.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        Corhay and colleagues, writing in the
        <em>
         International Journal of Clinical Practice
        </em>
        , say that their results help clarify the prevalence of chronic bronchitis among COPD patients, as well as suggest that it could represent a subtype of COPD that is easy to identify in clinical practice and appears to be associated with increased disease severity and with a high risk of exacerbations.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        However, Pierre-Regis Burgel (Paris Descartes University, France) writing in an accompanying editorial disagrees.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        [A]ssessment of individual risk cannot rely on this single disease attribute, he comments, noting that in a previous study the risk for COPD-related death was moderately increased in those with chronic bronchitis and mild airflow limitation but markedly increased in those with chronic bronchitis and severe airflow limitation.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        In conclusion, cough and sputum production may be markers of an active disease process in COPD patients, and may be used, in association with other [patient] characteristics, to identify patients at high risk of outcomes (e.g., exacerbations, death).
       </p>
