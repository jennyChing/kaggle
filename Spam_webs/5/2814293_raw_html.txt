   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Computer Online Forensic Evidence Extractor
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Computer Online Forensic Evidence Extractor
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       COFEE
      </b>
      ) is a tool kit, developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">
       Microsoft
      </a>
      , to help
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_forensics" title="Computer forensics">
       computer forensic investigators
      </a>
      extract evidence from a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows" title="Windows">
       Windows
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">
       computer
      </a>
      . Installed on a
      <a href="/wiki/USB_flash_drive" title="USB flash drive">
       USB flash drive
      </a>
      or other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/External_hard_disk_drive" title="External hard disk drive">
       external disk drive
      </a>
      , it acts as an automated forensic tool during a
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_forensics#Live_analysis" title="Computer forensics">
       live analysis
      </a>
      . Microsoft provides COFEE devices and online technical support free to law enforcement agencies.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_distribution">
       Development and distribution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Online_Forensic_Evidence_Extractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development and distribution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      COFEE was developed by Anthony Fung, a former
      <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
       Hong Kong
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Police_officer" title="Police officer">
       police officer
      </a>
      who now works as a senior investigator on Microsoft's Internet Safety Enforcement Team.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithLETC08_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmithLETC08-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fung conceived the device following discussions he had at a 2006 law enforcement technology conference sponsored by Microsoft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeaTimes_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SeaTimes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The device is used by more than 2,000 officers in at least 15 countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ms1_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ms1-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A case cited by Microsoft in April 2008 credits COFEE as being crucial in a
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      investigation into the trafficking of
      <a href="/wiki/Child_pornography" title="Child pornography">
       child pornography
      </a>
      , producing evidence that led to an arrest.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithLETC08_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmithLETC08-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2009 Microsoft and
      <a href="/wiki/Interpol" title="Interpol">
       Interpol
      </a>
      signed an agreement under which INTERPOL would serve as principal international distributor of COFEE.
      <a href="/wiki/University_College_Dublin" title="University College Dublin">
       University College Dublin
      </a>
      's Center for Cyber Crime Investigations in conjunction with Interpol develops programs for training forensic experts in using COFEE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interpol_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-interpol-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/National_White_Collar_Crime_Center" title="National White Collar Crime Center">
       National White Collar Crime Center
      </a>
      has been licensed by Microsoft to be the sole US domestic distributor of COFEE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_leak">
       Public leak
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Online_Forensic_Evidence_Extractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Public leak">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On November 6, 2009, copies of Microsoft COFEE were leaked onto various torrent websites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crunchgear_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-crunchgear-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Analysis of the leaked tool indicates that it is largely a wrapper around other utilities previously available to investigators.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prae_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-prae-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Microsoft confirmed the leak; however a spokesperson for the firm said "We do not anticipate the possible availability of COFEE for cybercriminals to download and find ways to build around' to be a significant concern".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inq_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-inq-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use">
       Use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Online_Forensic_Evidence_Extractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The device is activated by being plugged into a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus">
       USB
      </a>
      port. It contains 150 tools and a graphical user interface to help investigators collect data.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithLETC08_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmithLETC08-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The software is reported to be made up of three sections. First COFEE is configured in advance with an investigator selecting the data they wish to export, this is then saved to a USB device for plugging into the target computer. A further interface generates reports from the collected data.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prae_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-prae-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Estimates cited by Microsoft state jobs that previously took 34 hours can be done with COFEE in as little as 20 minutes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithLETC08_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmithLETC08-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tgdaily_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tgdaily-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      COFEE includes tools for
      <a href="/wiki/Password_cracking" title="Password cracking">
       password decryption
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
       Internet
      </a>
      history recovery and other data extraction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SeaTimes_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SeaTimes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It also recovers data stored in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAM" title="RAM">
       volatile memory
      </a>
      which could be lost if the computer were shut down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.com_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-news.com-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="DECAF">
       DECAF
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Online_Forensic_Evidence_Extractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: DECAF">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In mid to late 2009 a tool named Detect and Eliminate Computer Acquired Forensics (DECAF) was announced by an uninvolved group of programmers. The tool would would reportedly protect computers against COFEE and render the tool ineffective.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It alleged that it would provide real-time monitoring of COFEE signatures on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_serial_bus" title="Universal serial bus">
       USB
      </a>
      devices and in running applications and when a COFEE signature is detected, DECAF performs numerous user-defined processes. These included COFEE log clearing, ejecting USB devices, and contamination or spoofing of
      <a href="/wiki/MAC_address" title="MAC address">
       MAC addresses
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_12-14_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Register_12-14-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On December 18, 2009 the DECAF creators announced that the tool was a hoax and part of "a stunt to raise awareness for security and the need for better forensic tools".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wired-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
