         <p>
          The United States released five more Yemeni detainees from the prison camps in Guantnamo Bay, Cuba, on Wednesday to far-flung locations  four to the Arabian Sea nation of Oman, and a fifth to Estonia in Northern Europe  in continuing transfers that have stirred protest from Congress.
         </p>
         <p>
          A day earlier leading Republicans, notified in advance of the transfers, called a Capitol Hill news conference to
          <a href="http://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/guantanamo/article6287031.html" target="_blank" title="">
           seek more restrictions
          </a>
          on the release of detainees at the U.S. Navy base in Cuba, which as of Wednesday numbered
          <a href="http://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/guantanamo/article2203501.html" target="_blank" title="">
           122 prisoners
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The decision to transfer a detainee is made only after detailed, specific conversations with the receiving country about the potential threat a detainee may pose after transfer, said Paul Lewis, the Pentagons special envoy for Guantnamo prison closure, in announcing the latest release.
         </p>
         <p>
          All five freed detainees got to Guantnamo in the prison camps early days. None was ever charged with a crime and all had been cleared for transfer for years. But, as Yemenis, they could not go home because U.S. officials feared they would be lured to al-Qaida in the Arabian Peninsula, the terror group that Wednesday
          <a href="http://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article6448512.html" target="_blank" title="">
           claimed responsibility
          </a>
          for last weeks attack on the French satire newspaper Charlie Hebdo.
         </p>
         <p>
          Estonia had
          <a href="http://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/guantanamo/article2634879.html" target="_blank" title="">
           publicly said
          </a>
          it would take one prisoner, and it did 
          <a href="http://media.miamiherald.com/static/images/escenic-images/gitmopdfs/us9ym-000690dp.pdf" target="_blank" title="">
           Ahmed Abdul Qader
          </a>
          , 31, among the youngest of Guantnamos detainees.
         </p>
         <p>
          But the announcement that four other long-held Yemeni captives in their 30s and 40s had gone to Oman came as a surprise. The nation shares a border with both Yemen and Saudi Arabia.
         </p>
         <p>
          But its a stable, steadfast U.S. ally that has been so far spared militant Muslim violence, and lately emerged as a pivotal partner in U.S. diplomacy with Iran. It hosted high-level nuclear talks between the U.S. and Iran late last year.
         </p>
         <p>
          Just Saturday, Secretary of State John
          <a href="http://www.state.gov/r/pa/prs/ps/2015/01/235624.htm" target="_blank" title="">
           Kerry met the ailing 74-year-old leader
          </a>
          , Sultan Qaboos bin Said al Said, in Munich where he was receiving medical care. Kerry met with Omani Foreign Minister Yussef bin Alawi in Omans capital, Muscat, on Nov. 9.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.cipe.org/content/abdulwahab-alkebsi" target="_blank" title="">
           Abdulwahab Alkebsi
          </a>
          , an expert on Yemen at the Center for International Private Enterprise in Washington, D.C., described Oman as one of the more stable countries in the Arab World with a vast desert between it and neighboring Yemen.
         </p>
         <p>
          Socially, he said, Oman will be a better place to reintegrate into life than Latin America or Europe, with a common language, stable economy, educational and business opportunities that provide a better quality of life than impoverished Yemen.
         </p>
         <p>
          From a security standpoint, he said, it has highly trained and intrusive security systems like any other Arab country that could keep tabs on the men.
         </p>
         <p>
          Not one of the 780 detainees held at Guantnamo during the detention centers 13-year existence was an Omani. They came from 48 other nations. This is the first time Oman and Estonia have agreed to take in cleared Guantnamo detainees.
         </p>
         <p>
          This weeks transfer left
          <a href="http://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/guantanamo/article2163210.html" target="_blank" title="">
           54 cleared captives
          </a>
          among the prisons 122 detainees, 47 them Yemeni. The other remaining captives include 10 men with war crimes cases, one of them already convicted; 35 so-called forever prisoners deemed too dangerous to release; and 23 men once considered candidates for trial who are on a list to plead their case for release before a U.S. national security parole board.
         </p>
         <p>
          While the transfer missions were underway, but not publicly disclosed, GOP Sen. Lindsey Graham of South Carolina described President Barack Obamas release policy as a result of delusional thinking.
         </p>
         <p>
          The best I can say about him is, hes unfocused, Graham said on the FOX News Channels s
          <em>
           <a href="http://www.foxnews.com/on-air/on-the-record/index.html" target="_blank" title="">
            On the Record
           </a>
          </em>
          program Wednesday night. The war on terror has reached a lethal phase, and it is insane to be letting these people out of Gitmo to go back to the fight.
         </p>
         <p>
          Those released Wednesday were identified as:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://media.miamiherald.com/static/images/escenic-images/gitmopdfs/us9ym-000034dp.pdf" target="_blank" title="">
           <span class="ng_z_sym_square_bullet">
            
           </span>
           Khadr al Yafi
          </a>
          , 44, held as detainee 034. He got to Guantnamo on Jan. 16, 2002 and, like many Yemenis there was considered at one point to be a possible Osama bin Laden bodyguard. But by April 2007, a prison camp assessment approved his release.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://media.miamiherald.com/static/images/escenic-images/gitmopdfs/us9ym-000224dp.pdf" target="_blank" title="">
           <span class="ng_z_sym_square_bullet">
            
           </span>
           Abd al Rahman Abdullah Abu Shabati
          </a>
          , 32, held as detainee 224. He got to Guantanamo Feb. 9, 2002. The detention center recommended the Defense Department release him by January 2007
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://media.miamiherald.com/static/images/escenic-images/gitmopdfs/us9ym-000259dp.pdf" target="_blank" title="">
           &lt;bullet&gt; Fadil Husayn Salih Hintif
          </a>
          , in his 30s or 40s, was held as detainee 259. He was approved for release by January 2007. He got to Guantnamo on April 26, 2002 where, according to prison records, military intelligence considered him a potential threat because the model of Casio watch he wore was used in improvised explosive devices.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://media.miamiherald.com/static/images/escenic-images/gitmopdfs/us9ym-000689dp.pdf" target="_blank" title="">
           <span class="ng_z_sym_square_bullet">
            
           </span>
           Mohammed al Khatib
          </a>
          , 34, held as detainee 689, was approved for release in 2009 by an Obama administration national security task force. He got to Guantnamo June 19, 2002 and also wore a Casio watch. Pakistani security forces captured him in Faisalabad, Pakistan, according to his prisoner profile, in a raid that swept up a prized CIA captive called Abu Zubaydah. Guantnamo intelligence analysts at one point considered capture that day a threat indicator, too, although many of the men sent to Guantnamo from the Faisalabad raid have since been freed.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://media.miamiherald.com/static/images/escenic-images/gitmopdfs/us9ym-000690dp.pdf" target="_blank" title="">
           <span class="ng_z_sym_square_bullet">
            
           </span>
           Qader
          </a>
          , 31, held as prisoner 690 went to Estonia. Among the youngest of Guantnamos detainees, he arrived at age 18 on June 19, 2002 and was likewise captured in the Faisalabad raids, according to his prison profile provided to McClatchy newspapers from the anti-secrecy group WikiLeaks.
         </p>
         <p>
          Wednesdays transfers were the first since the U.S. sent five captives to Kazakhstan for resettlement on Dec. 30, a trip that was delayed by a day because a U.S. Air Force C-17s mechanical problems.
         </p>
         <p>
          That trip cost $1,646,500, said Air Force Col. Linda Pepin, chief of public affairs at the U.S. Transportation Command. The figure included $110,500 to scramble a second C-17 from an air base in Charleston, S.C., to complete the mission, aerial fueling, contractor support and maintenance, she added.
         </p>
