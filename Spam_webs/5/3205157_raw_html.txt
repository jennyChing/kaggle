     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Telemachus' name in
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      means "far from battle", perhaps reflecting his absence from the
      <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">
       Trojan War
      </a>
      . Homer also calls Telemachus by the patronymic epithet "Odysseus' son".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Odyssey">
       <i>
        Odyssey
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>
      In the
      <i>
       Odyssey
      </i>
      by Homer, under the instructions of
      <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">
       Athena
      </a>
      , Telemachus spends the first four books trying to gain knowledge of his father, Odysseus, who left for Troy when Telemachus was still an infant. At the outset of Telemachus' journey, Odysseus has been absent from his home at
      <a href="/wiki/Ithaca" title="Ithaca">
       Ithaca
      </a>
      for twenty years due to the Trojan War and the intervention of
      <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">
       Poseidon
      </a>
      . During his absence, Odysseus' house has been occupied by hordes of suitors seeking the hand of Penelope.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Telemachus first visits
      <a href="/wiki/Nestor_(mythology)" title="Nestor (mythology)">
       Nestor
      </a>
      and is well received by the old man who regales him with stories of his father's glory. Telemachus then departs with Nestor's son
      <a href="/wiki/Peisistratus_(Odyssey)" title="Peisistratus (Odyssey)">
       Peisistratus
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who accompanies him to the halls of
      <a href="/wiki/Menelaus" title="Menelaus">
       Menelaus
      </a>
      and his wife
      <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">
       Helen
      </a>
      . Whilst there, Telemachus is again treated as an honored guest as Menelaus and Helen tell complementary, yet contradictory stories of his father's exploits at Troy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Odyssey
      </i>
      returns focus to Telemachus upon his father's return to Ithaca in Book XV. He visits
      <a href="/wiki/Eumaeus" title="Eumaeus">
       Eumaeus
      </a>
      , the swineherd, who happens to be hosting a disguised Odysseus. After Odysseus reveals himself to Telemachus due to Athena's advice, the two men plan the downfall of the suitors. Telemachus then returns to the palace to keep an eye on the suitors and to await his father as the beggar.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When Penelope challenges the suitors to string Odysseus' bow and shoot an arrow through the handle-holes of twelve axeheads, Telemachus is the first to attempt the task. He would have completed the task, nearly stringing the bow on his fourth attempt; however, Odysseus subtly stops him before he can finish his attempt. Following the failure of the suitors at this task, Odysseus reveals himself, and he and Telemachus bring swift and bloody death to the suitors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Telegony">
       <i>
        Telegony
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Telemachus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Telegony">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Telegony" title="Telegony">
        Telegony
       </a>
      </i>
      was a short 2-book epic poem recounting the life and death of Odysseus after the events of the
      <i>
       Odyssey
      </i>
      . In this mythological postscript, Odysseus is accidentally killed by
      <a href="/wiki/Telegonus" title="Telegonus">
       Telegonus
      </a>
      , his unknown son by the goddess
      <a href="/wiki/Circe" title="Circe">
       Circe
      </a>
      . After Odysseus' death Telemachus returns to
      <a href="/wiki/Aeaea" title="Aeaea">
       Aeaea
      </a>
      with Telegonus and Penelope, and there marries Circe.
     </p>
     <p>
      From the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_Greek_and_Roman_Biography_and_Mythology" title="Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology">
        Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology
       </a>
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The son of Odysseus and Penelope (Hom. Od. i. 216). He was still an infant at the time when his father went to Troy, and in his absence of nearly twenty years he grew up to manhood. After the gods in council had determined that Odysseus should return home from the island of Ogygia, Athena, assuming the appearance of Mentes, king of the Taphians, went to Ithaca, and advised Telemachus to eject the troublesome suitors of his mother from his house, and to go to Pylos and Sparta, to gather information concerning his father. Telemachus followed the advice, but the suitors refused to quit his house; and Athena, in the form of Mentes, accompanied Telemachus to Pylos. There they were hospitably received by Nestor, who also sent his own son to conduct Telemachus to Sparta. Menelaus again kindly received him, and communicated to him the prophecy of Proteus concerning Odysseus (Hom. Od. i.iv.). From Sparta Telemachus returned home; and on his arrival there, he found his father, with the swineherd Eumaeus. But as Athena had metamorphosed him into a beggar, Telemachus did not recognise his father until the latter disclosed to him who he was. Father and son now agreed to punish the suitors; and when they were slain or dispersed, Telemachus accompanied his father to the aged Laertes. (Hom. Od. xv.xxiv.; comp. Odysseus.) In the post-Homeric traditions, we read that Palamedes, when endeavouring to persuade Odysseus to join the Greeks against Troy, and the latter feigned idiocy, placed the infant Telemachus before the plough with which Odysseus was ploughing (Hygin. Fab. 95; Serv. ad Aen. ii. 81; Tzetz. ad Lycoph. 384; Aelian, V. H. xiii. 12.). According to some accounts, Telemachus became the father of Perseptolis either by
       <a href="/wiki/Polycaste" title="Polycaste">
        Polycaste
       </a>
       , the daughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Nestor_(mythology)" title="Nestor (mythology)">
        Nestor
       </a>
       , or by
       <a href="/wiki/Nausicaa" title="Nausicaa">
        Nausicaa
       </a>
       , the daughter of Alcinous (Eustath. ad Hom. p. 1796; Dict. Cret. vi. 6.). Others relate that he was induced by Athena to marry Circe, and became by her the father of
       <a href="/wiki/Latinus" title="Latinus">
        Latinus
       </a>
       (Hygin. Fab. 127; comp. Telegonus), or that he married Cassiphone, a daughter of Circe, but in a quarrel with his mother-in-law he slew her, for which in his turn he was killed by Cassiphone (Tzetz. ad Lycoph. 808.). He is also said to have had a daughter called Roma, who married
       <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">
        Aeneas
       </a>
       (Serv. ad Aen. i. 273.). One account states that Odysseus, in consequence of a prophecy that his son was dangerous to him, sent him away from Ithaca. Servius (ad Aen. x. 167) makes Telemachus the founder of the town of Clusium in Etruria.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_classical_authors">
       Later classical authors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Telemachus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later classical authors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Contest_of_Homer_and_Hesiod" title="Contest of Homer and Hesiod">
        Contest of Homer and Hesiod
       </a>
      </i>
      , it is alleged that the Roman Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">
       Hadrian
      </a>
      asked the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delphic_Oracle" title="Delphic Oracle">
       Delphic Oracle
      </a>
      about Homer's birthplace and parentage. The Oracle replied that Homer came from Ithaca and that Telemachus was his father by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicasta" title="Epicasta">
       Epicasta
      </a>
      , daughter of Nestor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dictys_of_Crete" title="Dictys of Crete">
       Dictys of Crete
      </a>
      , Telemachus married
      <a href="/wiki/Nausicaa" title="Nausicaa">
       Nausicaa
      </a>
      , King Alcinous' daughter, and fathered a son named Perseptolis or Ptoliporthus.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_appearances">
       Other appearances
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Telemachus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other appearances">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Telemachus is the subject of
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_F%C3%A9nelon" title="Franois Fnelon">
       Franois Fnelon
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Les_Aventures_de_T%C3%A9l%C3%A9maque" title="Les Aventures de Tlmaque">
        The Adventures of Telemachus, Son of Ulysses
       </a>
      </i>
      (1699), a scathing attack on the
      <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">
       monarchy
      </a>
      of France.
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus is the subject of
      <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Scarlatti" title="Alessandro Scarlatti">
       Alessandro Scarlatti
      </a>
      's 1718 opera
      <i>
       Telemaco
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus and Circe are the subjects of Gluck's 1765 opera
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Telemaco" title="Telemaco">
        Telemaco, ossia L'isola di Circe
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus is featured in the 1833 poem (published in 1842) "
      <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(poem)" title="Ulysses (poem)">
       Ulysses
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">
       Alfred, Lord Tennyson
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1922 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">
        Ulysses
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">
       James Joyce
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Dedalus" title="Stephen Dedalus">
       Stephen Dedalus
      </a>
      is generally regarded as corresponding to Telemachus.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Telemachus" is the title of Book Three of
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolfe" title="Thomas Wolfe">
       Thomas Wolfe
      </a>
      's autobiographical novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Of_Time_and_the_River" title="Of Time and the River">
        Of Time and the River
       </a>
      </i>
      (1935).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Brodsky" title="Joseph Brodsky">
       Joseph Brodsky
      </a>
      published the poem "Odysseus to Telemachus" in 1972.
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus is a frequent character in the poetry of
      <a href="/wiki/Louise_Gl%C3%BCck" title="Louise Glck">
       Louise Glck
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus was the name of Carole King's cat and is pictured on the cover of her album Tapestry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus is one of the main characters in "Ulysses", a Play by
      <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Rowe_(writer)" title="Nicholas Rowe (writer)">
       Nicholas Rowe
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      Telemachus appears as the son of Ulysses in the 1981 French-Japanese animated television series
      <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_31" title="Ulysses 31">
       Ulysses 31
      </a>
     </p>
