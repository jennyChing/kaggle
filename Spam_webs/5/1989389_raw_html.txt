    Embedded journalism

     <p>
      <b>
       Embedded journalism
      </b>
      refers to
      <a href="/wiki/News" title="News">
       news
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">
       reporters
      </a>
      being attached to
      <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">
       military
      </a>
      units involved in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_conflicts" title="Armed conflicts">
       armed conflicts
      </a>
      . While the term could be applied to many historical interactions between journalists and military personnel, it first came to be used in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq_media_coverage" title="2003 invasion of Iraq media coverage">
       media coverage of the 2003 invasion of Iraq
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_military" title="United States military">
       United States military
      </a>
      responded to pressure from the country's news media who were disappointed by the level of access granted during the 1991
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">
       Gulf War
      </a>
      and the 2001
      <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001present)">
       U.S. invasion of Afghanistan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      At the start of the war in March 2003, as many as 775 reporters and photographers were traveling as embedded journalists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These reporters signed contracts with the military promising not to report information that could compromise unit position, future missions, classified weapons, and information they might find.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2013)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Joint training for war correspondents started in November 2002 in advance of start of the war.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When asked why the military decided to embed journalists with the troops, Lt. Col. Rick Long of the U.S. Marine Corps replied, "Frankly, our job is to win the war. Part of that is
      <a href="/wiki/Information_warfare" title="Information warfare">
       information warfare
      </a>
      . So we are going to attempt to dominate the information environment."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Gina_Cavallaro" title="Gina Cavallaro">
       Gina Cavallaro
      </a>
      , a reporter for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Army_Times" title="Army Times">
        Army Times
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      said, "Theyre [the journalists] relying more on the military to get them where they want to go, and as a result, the military is getting smarter about getting its own story told." But, she added, "I don't necessarily consider that a bad thing."
     </p>
     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Military_Control_Exerted">
       Military Control Exerted
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Embedded_journalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Military Control Exerted">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first journalist to run afoul of U.S. military rules in Iraq was freelancer Philip Smucker, travelling on assignment for the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Science_Monitor" title="Christian Science Monitor">
        Christian Science Monitor
       </a>
      </i>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Marine Division (United States)">
       1st Marine Division
      </a>
      . Smucker was not officially embedded, but all reporters in the theater of war were deemed subject to Pentagon oversight. On March 26, 2003, during an interview with
      <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">
       CNN
      </a>
      , Smucker disclosed the location of a Marine unit, as he'd also done during an interview with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">
       NPR
      </a>
      . He was thereafter expelled.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Just four days later,
      <a href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" title="Fox News Channel">
       Fox News Channel
      </a>
      correspondent
      <a href="/wiki/Geraldo_Rivera" title="Geraldo Rivera">
       Geraldo Rivera
      </a>
      similarly broadcast details from Iraq of the position and plans of U.S. troops. "Let me draw a few lines here for you," he said, making on-camera marks in the sand. "First, I want to make some emphasis here that these hash marks here, this is us. We own that territory. It's 40%, maybe even a little more than that." At another point, complained a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Central_Command" title="United States Central Command">
       CENTCOM
      </a>
      spokesman, Rivera "actually revealed the time of an attack prior to its occurrence." Although Riveralike Philip Smuckerwas not officially embedded, he was swiftly escorted back to Kuwait.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A week later, Rivera apologized. "I'm sorry that it happened," he said on Fox News Channel, "and I assure you that it was inadvertent. Nobody was hurt by what I said. No mission was compromised." However, a network review, he admitted, "showed that I did indeed break one of the rules related to embedment."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2005 the U.S. Coalition Forces Land Component Command in Kuwait pulled the credentials of two embedded journalists on a two-week assignment for the Virginian-Pilot newspaper in Norfolk, Virginia, claiming they violated the prohibition against photographing damaged vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Embedded_journalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Objectivity_(journalism)" title="Objectivity (journalism)">
       Objectivity (journalism)
      </a>
     </div>
     <table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
      <tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
       <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="padding:0 10px;">
        We were a propaganda arm of our governments. At the start the censors enforced that, but by the end we were our own censors. We were cheerleaders.
       </td>
       <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px;">
        <p style="font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right">
         
         <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lynch_(journalist)" title="Charles Lynch (journalist)">
          Charles Lynch
         </a>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
          <a href="#cite_note-10">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           10
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">
       ethics
      </a>
      of embedded journalism are considered controversial.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The practice has been criticized as being part of a
      <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">
       propaganda
      </a>
      campaign and an effort to keep reporters away from civilian populations and sympathetic to invading forces; for example by the
      <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">
       documentary films
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/War_Made_Easy:_How_Presidents_%26_Pundits_Keep_Spinning_Us_to_Death" title="War Made Easy: How Presidents &amp; Pundits Keep Spinning Us to Death">
        War Made Easy: How Presidents &amp; Pundits Keep Spinning Us to Death
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_War_You_Don%27t_See" title="The War You Don't See">
        The War You Don't See
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Embed critics objected that the level of military oversight was too strict and that embedded journalists would make reports that were too sympathetic to the American side of the war, leading to use of the alternate term "inbedded journalist" or "inbeds". "Those correspondents who drive around in tanks and armored personnel carriers," said journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Gay_Talese" title="Gay Talese">
       Gay Talese
      </a>
      in an interview, "who are spoon-fed what the military gives them and they become mascots for the military, these journalists. I wouldn't have journalists embedded if I had any power!... There are stories you can do that aren't done. I've said that many times."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On June 14, 2014,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      published an opinion piece critical of embedded journalism during both the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Iraq_(2003%E2%80%9311)" title="History of Iraq (200311)">
       military occupation of Iraq
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001present)">
       war in Afghanistan
      </a>
      . It was written by PVT
      <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Manning" title="Chelsea Manning">
       Chelsea Manning
      </a>
      , the former U.S. Army intelligence analyst now serving a 35-year prison sentence for leaking the largest set of classified documents in American history. At no point during her 200910 deployment in Iraq, Manning wrote, were there more than a dozen American journalists covering military operationsin a country of 31 million people and 117,000 U.S. troops. Manning charged that vetting of reporters by military public affairs officials was used "to screen out those judged likely to produce critical coverage," and that once embedded, journalists tended "to avoid controversial reporting that could raise red flags" out of fear having their access terminated. "A result," wrote Manning, "is that the American public's access to the facts is gutted, which leaves them with no way to evaluate the conduct of American officials." Manning noted, "This program of limiting press access was challenged in court in 2013 by a freelance reporter, Wayne Anderson, who claimed to have followed his agreement but to have been terminated after publishing adverse reports about the conflict in Afghanistan. The ruling on his case upheld the military's position that there was no constitutionally protected right to be an embedded journalist."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">
       Dangers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Embedded_journalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dangers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      During both the
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">
       Iraq War
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001present)">
       War in Afghanistan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Improvised_explosive_device" title="Improvised explosive device">
       improvised explosive devices
      </a>
      (IEDs) were used extensively against U.S.-led Coalition forces, and accounted for the majority of Coalition casualties. Journalists travelling with ground forces were at the same risk.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On January 29, 2006, while embedded with the U.S. Army's
      <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Division (United States)">
       4th Infantry Division
      </a>
      , ABC's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_World_News" title="ABC World News">
       <i>
        World News Tonight
       </i>
      </a>
      co-anchor
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Woodruff" title="Bob Woodruff">
       Bob Woodruff
      </a>
      and cameraman
      <a href="/wiki/Doug_Vogt" title="Doug Vogt">
       Doug Vogt
      </a>
      were, together with an Iraqi soldier, seriously injured when their convoy was ambushed near
      <a href="/wiki/Taji,_Iraq" title="Taji, Iraq">
       Taji, Iraq
      </a>
      and an IED detonated beneath them. At the time of the attack, Woodruff and Vogt were exposed, standing in the back hatch of their Iraqi mechanized vehicle taping a video log of the patrol.
