     <p>
      <b>
       Louis Thomas Hardin
      </b>
      (May 26, 1916 September 8, 1999), better known as
      <b>
       Moondog
      </b>
      , was an American composer, musician, poet and inventor of several musical instruments. He was blind from the age of 16. In
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York
      </a>
      from the late 1940s until he left in 1972, he could often be found on 6th Avenue between 52nd and 55th Street wearing a cloak and Viking-style helmet, sometimes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busking" title="Busking">
       busking
      </a>
      or selling music, but often just standing silent and still. He was widely recognized as "the Viking of 6th Avenue" by thousands of passersby and residents who had no idea that this seemingly homeless eccentric standing on "Moondog's corner" was a respected and recorded composer and musician.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moondog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Born to an
      <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">
       Episcopalian
      </a>
      family in
      <a href="/wiki/Marysville,_Kansas" title="Marysville, Kansas">
       Marysville
      </a>
      , Kansas, Hardin started playing a set of drums that he made from a cardboard box at the age of five. His family relocated to Wyoming and his father opened a trading post at
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bridger" title="Fort Bridger">
       Fort Bridger
      </a>
      . Hardin attended school in a couple of small towns. At one point, his father took him to an
      <a href="/wiki/Arapaho" title="Arapaho">
       Arapaho
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_Dance" title="Sun Dance">
       Sun Dance
      </a>
      where he sat on the lap of Chief Yellow Calf and played a
      <a href="/wiki/Tom-tom_drum" title="Tom-tom drum">
       tom-tom
      </a>
      made from buffalo skin.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hardin played drums for the high school band in
      <a href="/wiki/Hurley,_Missouri" title="Hurley, Missouri">
       Hurley, Missouri
      </a>
      before losing his sight in a farm accident involving a dynamite cap at the age of 16.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After learning the principles of music in several schools for blind young men across middle America, he taught himself the skills of ear training and composition. He studied with Burnet Tuthill and at the Iowa School for the Blind.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hardin moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Batesville,_Arkansas" title="Batesville, Arkansas">
       Batesville, Arkansas
      </a>
      where he lived until 1942, when he got a scholarship to study in
      <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">
       Memphis, Tennessee
      </a>
      . Although the majority of his musical training was self-taught by ear, he learned some music theory from books in
      <a href="/wiki/Braille" title="Braille">
       braille
      </a>
      there.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hardin moved to New York in 1943, where he met noted
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">
       classical music
      </a>
      luminaries such as
      <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">
       Leonard Bernstein
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">
       Arturo Toscanini
      </a>
      , as well as legendary jazz performer-composers such as
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">
       Charlie Parker
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Benny_Goodman" title="Benny Goodman">
       Benny Goodman
      </a>
      , whose upbeat tempos and often humorous compositions would influence Hardin's later work. One of his early street posts was near the famed
      <a href="/wiki/52nd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="52nd Street (Manhattan)">
       52nd Street
      </a>
      nightclub strip, and he was well-known to many jazz musicians and fans.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="New_York">
       New York
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moondog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New York">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      From the late 1940s until 1972, Moondog lived as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busking" title="Busking">
       street musician
      </a>
      and poet in New York City, busking in midtown Manhattan, eventually settling on the corner of 53rd or 54th Street and 6th Avenue in
      <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">
       Manhattan
      </a>
      . He was not homeless however, or at least not often; he maintained an apartment in upper Manhattan and had a country retreat in Candor, NY, to which he moved in 1972.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotto.2C_Robert_2007_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Scotto.2C_Robert_2007-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He partially supported himself by selling copies of his poetry and his musical philosophy. In addition to his music and poetry, he was also known for the distinctive fanciful "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">
       Viking
      </a>
      " cloak that he wore. Already bearded and long-haired, he added a Viking-style horned helmet to avoid the occasional comparisons of his appearance with that of Christ or a monk,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-furious.com_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-furious.com-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as he had rejected Christianity in his late teens. He developed a lifelong interest in
      <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
       Nordic mythology
      </a>
      , and maintained an altar to
      <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">
       Thor
      </a>
      in his country home in Candor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotto.2C_Robert_2007_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Scotto.2C_Robert_2007-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1947 Hardin had adopted the
      <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">
       pen name
      </a>
      "Moondog" in honor of a dog "who used to howl at the moon more than any dog I knew of." In 1949 he traveled to a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackfoot" title="Blackfoot">
       Blackfoot
      </a>
      Sun Dance in
      <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">
       Idaho
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where he performed on percussion and flute, returning to the Native American music he first came in contact with as a child. It was this Native music, along with contemporary jazz and classical, mixed with the
      <a href="/wiki/Ambient_noise_level" title="Ambient noise level">
       ambient
      </a>
      sounds from his environment (city
      <a href="/wiki/Traffic" title="Traffic">
       traffic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">
       ocean waves
      </a>
      , babies crying, etc.) that created the foundation of Moondog's music.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1954, he won a case in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State_Supreme_Court" title="New York State Supreme Court">
       New York State Supreme Court
      </a>
      against disc jockey
      <a href="/wiki/Alan_Freed" title="Alan Freed">
       Alan Freed
      </a>
      , who had branded his radio show, "The Moondog
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll" title="Rock and Roll">
       Rock and Roll
      </a>
      Matinee", around the name "Moondog", using "Moondog's Symphony" (the first record that Moondog ever cut) as his "calling card". Moondog believed he would not have won the case had it not been for the help of musicians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Benny_Goodman" title="Benny Goodman">
       Benny Goodman
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">
       Arturo Toscanini
      </a>
      , who testified that he was a serious composer. Freed had to apologize and stop using the nickname "Moondog" on air, on the basis that Hardin was known by the name long before Freed began using it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttbook_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ttbook-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">
       Germany
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moondog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Germany">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Along with his passion for Nordic culture, Moondog had an idealised view of Germany ("The Holy Land with the Holy River"the
      <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">
       Rhine
      </a>
      ), where he settled in 1974.
     </p>
     <p>
      Eventually, a young German student
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      named Ilona Sommer (birth name: Goebel) helped Moondog set up the primary holding company for his artistic endeavors
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and hosted him, first in
      <a href="/wiki/Oer-Erkenschwick" title="Oer-Erkenschwick">
       Oer-Erkenschwick
      </a>
      , and later on in
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Mnster">
       Mnster
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">
       Westphalia
      </a>
      , Germany. Moondog lived with the family of Ilona Sommer and they spent time together in Mnster. During that period Moondog created hundreds of compositions which were transferred from Braille to sheet music by Ilona Sommer. Moondog spent the remainder of his life in Germany where he died in 1999.
     </p>
     <p>
      Moondog revisited America briefly in 1989, for a tribute in which
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">
       Philip Glass
      </a>
      asked him to conduct the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Philharmonic_Chamber_Orchestra" title="Brooklyn Philharmonic Chamber Orchestra">
       Brooklyn Philharmonic Chamber Orchestra
      </a>
      , at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Music_America_Festival" title="New Music America Festival">
       New Music America Festival
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">
       Brooklyn
      </a>
      , stimulating a renewed interest in his music.
     </p>
     <p>
      He recorded many albums, and toured both in the U.S. and in EuropeFrance, Germany and Sweden.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
       Music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moondog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Moondog's music took inspiration from street sounds, such as the subway or a
      <a href="/wiki/Foghorn" title="Foghorn">
       foghorn
      </a>
      . It was characterized by what he called "snaketime" and described as "a slithery rhythm, in times that are not ordinary [...] I'm not gonna die in 4/4 time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ttbook_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ttbook-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many of his works were highly contrapuntal, and he worked hard on perfecting his
      <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">
       counterpoint
      </a>
      , criticizing Bach for his many "mistakes".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-furious.com_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-furious.com-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Moondog's work was early championed by
      <a href="/wiki/Artur_Rodzi%C5%84ski" title="Artur Rodziski">
       Artur Rodziski
      </a>
      , the conductor of
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Philharmonic" title="New York Philharmonic">
       New York Philharmonic
      </a>
      in the 1940s. He released a number of 78"s, 45"s and
      <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">
       EPs
      </a>
      of his music in the 1950s, as well as several
      <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">
       LPs
      </a>
      on a number of notable
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
       jazz
      </a>
      labels, including an unusual record of stories and songs for children with
      <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">
       Julie Andrews
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Martyn_Green" title="Martyn Green">
       Martyn Green
      </a>
      , in 1957, called
      <i>
       Songs of Sense and Nonsense - Tell it Again
      </i>
      . For ten years no new recordings were heard from Moondog until producer
      <a href="/wiki/James_William_Guercio" title="James William Guercio">
       James William Guercio
      </a>
      took him into the studio to record an album for
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">
       Columbia Records
      </a>
      in 1969.
     </p>
     <p>
      A second album produced with Guercio featured one of Moondog's daughters as a vocalist and contained song compositions in
      <a href="/wiki/Canon_(music)" title="Canon (music)">
       canons
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Round_(music)" title="Round (music)">
       rounds
      </a>
      . The album did not make as large an impression in popular music as the first had. The two CBS albums were re-released as a single CD in 1989.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of Moondog's works are published by Managarm Musikverlag in Germany. By his last will the heritage of Moondog was administered and owned by Ilona Sommer, who died in September 2011. In her will she appointed the German lawyer Alexander Duve (Berlin, Germany) as the executor of her estate including the copyrights in Moondog's works, so he now administers Moondog's heritage.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inventions">
       Inventions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moondog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Inventions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trimba1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="243" data-file-width="386" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Trimba1.jpg/240px-Trimba1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Trimba1.jpg/360px-Trimba1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/93/Trimba1.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trimba1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The trimba, Moondog percussion instrument
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Moondog also invented several musical instruments, including a small triangular-shaped harp known as the "oo", another which he named the "ooo-ya-tsu", and a triangular stringed instrument played with a bow that he called the "hs" (after the Norwegian, "hus", meaning "house"). Perhaps his best known creation is the "trimba", a triangular percussion instrument that the composer invented in the late 40s. The original Trimba is still played today by Moondog's friend Stefan Lakatos, a Swedish percussionist, to whom Moondog also explained the methods for building such an instrument.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
       Influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moondog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The music of Moondog of the 1940s and 50s is said to have been a strong influence on many early minimalist composers.
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">
       Philip Glass
      </a>
      has written that he and
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Reich" title="Steve Reich">
       Steve Reich
      </a>
      took Moondog's work "very seriously and understood and appreciated it much more than what we were exposed to at Juilliard."'
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In July 1956 the British jazz composer and musician
      <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Graham_(musician)" title="Kenny Graham (musician)">
       Kenny Graham
      </a>
      recorded the album 'Moondog and Suncat Suites' with a thirteen-piece band featuring such notable performers as
      <a href="/wiki/Stan_Tracey" title="Stan Tracey">
       Stan Tracey
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Phil_Seaman" title="Phil Seaman">
       Phil Seaman
      </a>
      . 'Moondog' featured Graham's arrangements of ten Moondog compositions, whereas 'Suncat Suite' consisted of a sequence of six of Graham's own compositions inspired by Moondog.
      <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">
       HMV
      </a>
      issued the original vinyl album in 1957, and Trunk Records reissued it on CD in 2010.
     </p>
     <p>
      Moondog inspired other musicians with several songs dedicated to him. These include "Moondog" on
      <a href="/wiki/Pentangle_(band)" title="Pentangle (band)">
       Pentangle
      </a>
      's 1968 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Child" title="Sweet Child">
        Sweet Child
       </a>
      </i>
      and "Spear for Moondog" (parts I and II) by jazz organist
      <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McGriff" title="Jimmy McGriff">
       Jimmy McGriff
      </a>
      on his 1968
      <i>
       Electric Funk
      </i>
      album. Glam rock icon Marc Bolan and T.Rex made reference to him in the song "Rabbit Fighter" with the line, "Moondog's just a prophet to the end.....". The English pop group
      <a href="/wiki/Prefab_Sprout" title="Prefab Sprout">
       Prefab Sprout
      </a>
      included the song "Moondog" on their album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jordan:_The_Comeback" title="Jordan: The Comeback">
        Jordan: The Comeback
       </a>
      </i>
      released in 1990.
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_and_the_Holding_Company" title="Big Brother and the Holding Company">
       Big Brother and the Holding Company
      </a>
      featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">
       Janis Joplin
      </a>
      covered the song "All Is Loneliness" on their 1967 self-titled album. The song was also covered by
      <a href="/wiki/Antony_and_the_Johnsons" title="Antony and the Johnsons">
       Antony and the Johnsons
      </a>
      during their 2005 tour.
      <a href="/wiki/Mr._Scruff" title="Mr. Scruff">
       Mr. Scruff
      </a>
      's single "Get a Move On," from his album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Keep_It_Unreal" title="Keep It Unreal">
        Keep It Unreal
       </a>
      </i>
      , is structured around samples from "Bird's Lament." New York band
      <a href="/wiki/The_Insect_Trust" title="The Insect Trust">
       The Insect Trust
      </a>
      play a cover of Moondog's song "Be a Hobo" on their album
      <i>
       Hoboken Saturday Night
      </i>
      . The track "Stamping Ground", with its odd preamble of Moondog reciting one of his
      <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">
       epigrams
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was featured on the sampler double album
      <i>
       Fill Your Head with Rock
      </i>
      (CBS, 1970). Canadian composer and producer
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Lanois" title="Daniel Lanois">
       Daniel Lanois
      </a>
      included a track called "Moondog" on his album/video-documentary
      <i>
       Here Is What Is
      </i>
      . The psychedelic band The Liquid Scene included a song called "Hey Moondog (See The God of Odin Come)" on their debut album "Revolutions" released in December 2014.
     </p>
     <p>
      Between 1970 and 1980 a blind bearded mystic called "Moondog" appeared as the title character in a four issue series of
      <a href="/wiki/Underground_comix" title="Underground comix">
       Underground comix
      </a>
      written and illustrated by
      <a href="/wiki/George_Metzger_(artist)" title="George Metzger (artist)">
       George Metzger
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A documentary about his life, "The Viking of 6th Avenue", is in production for release in 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
