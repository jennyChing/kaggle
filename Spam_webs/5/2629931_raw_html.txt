         <div class="section-inner layoutSingleColumn">
          <h2 class="graf--h2" id="d9f6" name="d9f6">
           Twice, Then Quit: How to Train for Resistance to Change
          </h2>
          <p class="graf--p graf--empty" id="dbe4" name="dbe4">
           <br/>
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="f793" name="f793">
           One of the most important skills regarding long-term habit formation is learning how to be comfortable with discomfort.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="0a42" name="0a42">
           If you can master comfort in discomfort, you can master any obstacle you encounter.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="7138" name="7138">
           Discomfort, often in the guise of resistance, is one of the most common causes of procrastination.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c154" name="c154">
           Procrastination causes us to offset positive habits in favour of immediate gratification and put off changing negative behaviours. The temptation to succumb to binge eating, cigarettes, negative thinking and television to name a few, is inevitable  but its also surmountable, once you learn how to overcome resistance.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="dec6" name="dec6">
           Resistance is uncomfortable and often avoided at all costs. Desensitizing ourselves to the discomfort of resistance and pursuing positive behaviours, regardless, is the path to personal growth, but one thats seldom travelled.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="edd1" name="edd1">
           Its a given: If theres an easier choice, chances are were going to choose it. Purchasing high-carb, microwavable meals is easier than learning how steam vegetables. Relaxing in bed is easier than waking up earlier and exercising. Surfing Netflix before bed is easier than reading a book that challenges you.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="793d" name="793d">
           This is why bad habits are hard to break: If theres a more familiar (and easier) option, were going to settle on it (and we cant be blamed for doing so).
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="47d8" name="47d8">
           However, if resistance is perceived, not as an obstacle, but as an opportunity to maximise your willpower and strengthen your resolve, it can often be the difference that makes the difference in long-term behavioural change.
          </p>
          <h3 class="graf--h3" id="bfd2" name="bfd2">
           Leaping Vs. Leaning
          </h3>
          <p class="graf--p" id="9a2a" name="9a2a">
           This new perception of resistance is achieved when we
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            lean
           </em>
           into our resistance. People tend to err on the side of
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            leaping
           </em>
           rather than
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            leaning
           </em>
           when trying to overcome their resistance, but this can be problematic.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="1461" name="1461">
           Firstly, its important to understand: Resistance isnt to be overcome, its to be reduced enough so we can
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            begin.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="39c0" name="39c0">
           Secondly, those who leap often
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            desensitise
           </em>
           themselves to discomfort. This can be read as a desirable trait: Who wouldnt want to be numb to resistance?
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="82d7" name="82d7">
           But when resistance is non-existent, you lose your edge. If youre not afraid to click the publish button, approach a beautiful stranger or run an extra one kilometre when youre about to collapse, in other words, feel an emotion, then youre not motivated. Youre not at your edge. Youre not human.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="dc9b" name="dc9b">
           Heres what youre never told: Youre supposed to feel afraid.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="edf6" name="edf6">
           Its ironic: In hindsight, overcoming that obstacle you resisted will often mean more to you than the reward you sought after at the beginning of your journey.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="8b0a" name="8b0a">
           The solution is not to overcome resistance, indefinitely, (thats impossible), but to learn how to become comfortable with it, as and when it arises. In other words, to lean into it. To push yourself a little more than youd normally do.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="4282" name="4282">
           And one of the most effective strategies Ive learnt to do that, is the Twice, Then Quit strategy.
          </p>
          <h3 class="graf--h3" id="f150" name="f150">
           Twice, Then Quit
          </h3>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c127" name="c127">
           Leo Babauta, author of
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://amzn.to/1u4RED2" href="http://amzn.to/1u4RED2" rel="nofollow">
            The Power of Less
           </a>
           , describes Twice, Then Quit using meditation as an example:
          </p>
          <blockquote class="graf--blockquote" id="6240" name="6240">
           When youre meditating and you feel like getting up, dont; then when you feel the urge to get up a second time, dont; and when you feel the urge to get up a third time, then get up. So you sit through the urge, the discomfort, twice before finally giving in the third time. This is a nice balance, so that youre pushing your comfort zone a little. You can do this in exercise and many other activitiespush a little. [1]
          </blockquote>
          <p class="graf--p" id="5319" name="5319">
           Often, when obstacles are encountered, we mislead ourselves to believe we only have one of two choices: Fight or flight. Either we do it, or we dont. But theres a beautiful shade of grey we seldom see; an in-between we dont consider: We can lean into it.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="25f3" name="25f3">
           To lean is to eat an extra two forkfuls of vegetables when youve already decided you dont like them.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="afdc" name="afdc">
           In social skills, its to remain in an awkward conversation for an additional one minute, even when your minds drawn a blank; or striking up a conversation when youve decided youre not in the mood, even if its turning to the person next to you on the commute home and asking: Hey, how was your day today?.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="9be5" name="9be5">
           Its promising to run that extra 0.5 kilometres in that eight kilometre run when your negative self-talk is unrelenting and youre about to keel over.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="cef6" name="cef6">
           Resistance will
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            always
           </em>
           be a result of how you perceive an event in your life. Its the self-talk that argues the obstacle is insurmountable, you dont deserve to overcome it and theres an easier, (albeit unfulfilling) option: To quit.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="dfe4" name="dfe4">
           Heres the rub: Be present with it, but dont argue with it. It will quieten down. And more importantly, do more than youd
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            normally
           </em>
           do, even if its ridiculous; make the self-compliance hoop so small, its laughable. Just
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.samuelthomasdavies.com/the-zeigarnik-effect/" href="http://www.samuelthomasdavies.com/the-zeigarnik-effect/" rel="nofollow">
            Start
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="aff8" name="aff8">
           You will encounter resistance with all habits  even ones that are automatised. Thats a given, so anticipate it. But dont cater to it as soon as it arises. Its understandable to want to remove yourself from it, but surfing the urge twice before quitting is what increases our threshold to endure it, especially when were experiencing
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.samuelthomasdavies.com/decision-fatigue/" href="http://www.samuelthomasdavies.com/decision-fatigue/" rel="nofollow">
            decision fatigue
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <h3 class="graf--h3" id="bf74" name="bf74">
           A Final Caveat
          </h3>
          <p class="graf--p" id="a366" name="a366">
           The Twice, Then Quit strategy is used by Zen practitioners to help minimise resistance with intensive meditation practices, and is applicable to all habits.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c5ee" name="c5ee">
           However, its important not to misuse it: Going through the motions of Twice, Then Quit will devalue its effectiveness. In other words, dont use it as an excuse to quit when you dont
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            really
           </em>
           want to.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="31f9" name="31f9">
           Lets suppose youre writing a book, but youre struggling with the paragraph youre writing. You want to watch a television show thats about to air, but havent met your daily quota of 500 words. It would be easier to convince yourself youre spent for the day than to slug it out and hit your daily quota.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="3126" name="3126">
           To minimise this problem, be brutally honest with yourself. Are you
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            really
           </em>
           feeling resistant to the task at hand, or is the path of least resistance more appealing?
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="fe14" name="fe14">
           In those instances, that differentiation becomes apparent. Dont feel bad about it, it happens. Now its been identified, continue as per usual and bear it in mind if and when it happens again.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="9af3" name="9af3">
           I like to ask myself: If I removed the easier option, what would I do instead?
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="81d6" name="81d6">
           Learn when to quit and bridge the distance between the person you are and the person you know you want to be. You owe it your future self.
          </p>
          <h3 class="graf--h3" id="ce9f" name="ce9f">

          </h3>

            Discomfort Zone: How to Master the Universe
           </a>
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">

           </em>
           Accessed: 15th October 2014).
