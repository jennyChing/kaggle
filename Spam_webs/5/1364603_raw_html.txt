      </p>

      <p>
       <span style="line-height: 1.6;">
        The ad binge may have failed to make the impact the Coalition hoped for because, the report notes, while transport was a hot-button issue, it was not considered as important as unemployment, health, inflation and education.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       The report is to be raised in Parliament by Labor's Special Minister of State, Gavin Jennings, on Wednesday.
      </p>
      <p>
       The campaign had strong echoes of a $6.6 million transport "information" campaign run by Labor in the lead-up to John Brumby's 2010 state election loss.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <em>
        Moving Victoria Campaign Evaluation
       </em>
       report was written by Hall and Partners Open Mind for the Transport Department.
      </p>
      <p>
       It revealed that the ad campaign aimed to "create positive awareness" of the Napthine government's transport projects.
      </p>
      <p>
       These included the East West Link, an airport rail link, a new underground train line and the removal of level crossings.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the campaign evaluation,3200 Victorians were polled from November 2013 to just before the November 2014 state poll.
      </p>
      <p>
       The report noted that one of the key hindrances to the campaign working was "a pre-existing public sensitivity to 'spin'".
      </p>
      <p>
       It also found that transport was "spontaneously the most nominated top priority" by those surveyed, and that it rose "significantly" after the ad campaign started.
      </p>
      <p>
       However,other issues were, ultimately, more important to Victorians.
      </p>
      <p>
       "When considered with other areas such as health, unemployment and cost of living, transport infrastructure was of lesser and decreasing relative importance," the research said.
      </p>
      <p>
       It also found that many people "exposed to the ads didn't remember seeing or hearing them".
      </p>
      <p>
       And people saw the campaign in the context of "political spin" and as advertising projects "which weren't expected to be delivered in the near future (particularly the airport rail link signs at Southern Cross Station)".
      </p>
      <p>
       The research proposed the campaign could be done better next time around by considering "a more staged creative rollout", and simplifying ads so that they concentrated on one transport project at a time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mr Jennings said the
       <em>
        Moving Victoria
       </em>
       campaign was a failed attempt to "fool the Victorian community"and pledged that Labor would not repeat the practice of publicly funding ad campaigns of its type.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
