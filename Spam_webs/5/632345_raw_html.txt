      <p>
       The reason - excuses, excuses - for my inactivity lately has been on account of a major move, a long-awaited return to my home in New York City. The whirlwind experience began with a job interview in early September, followed by a move four days later, and my first day at work one day after that.

        </a>

       </strong>

      <p>
       The space operates as a museum and fine arts gallery, a center for the arts, a performance venue, and beyond. Its held performances by Philip Glass and Lady Gaga, as well as Tyne Daly, Mandy Patinkin, and workshops by Arthur Miller.

       <a href="http://angelorensanz.com/" title="Angel Orensanz website">
        <strong>
         Angel Orensanz
        </strong>
       </a>

      </h1>

      </p>

       <strong>

       </strong>

         cornerstones of the LES culture

    <div class="post">
     <h3>
      <a href="http://zoespeas.tumblr.com/post/61511828826/btgo-part-one-the-marriage-plot">
       BTGO: Part One. The Marriage Plot.
      </a>
     </h3>
     <div class="bodytype">
      <p>
       <strong>
        Adventures in the Buy 2 Get 1 free Section of B&amp;N
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <em>
         PART ONE.
        </em>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       BOGO, or even BTGO (buy two get one? is it a thing? should it be? okay). Its a magical thing. Its responsible for my wardrobe being twice the size that it should be. Its why I currently have two massive tankards of Nutella in the kitchen - like I said, magic.
      </p>
      <p>
       But BOGO/BTGO involving books? As frequently seen in your friendly, neighborhood bookstore chain?
      </p>
      <p>
       Holy mother of manuscripts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Try as I might to stay
       <strong>
        out
       </strong>
       of B&amp;N to keep myself
       <strong>
        inside
       </strong>
       a library, I collect books like Carrie Bradshaw collected shoes so eventually, there I am: at the wall of BTGO.
      </p>
      <p>
       Which means -three new books for me!
       <br/>
       Which means - new blog post for you!
      </p>
      <p>
       Number One: Presenting -
       <em>
        THE MARRIAGE PLOT
       </em>
       by Jeffrey Eugenides.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="The Marriage Plot" height="900" src="http://nyoobserver.files.wordpress.com/2011/09/marriageplot.jpg" width="900"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        The BASICS
       </strong>
       .
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Author
        </strong>
        : Jeffrey Eugenides.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Published:
        </strong>
        2011.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Length
        </strong>
        :416pp.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Synopsis
        </strong>
        : Basically - 
        <em>
         What happens after you graduate, when the whole scaffolding of classes and the college social scene youve been training your personality around is suddenly taken away, and you have to grope for a new way to be in the world
        </em>
        . - William Deresiewicz, NYTimes Book Review, 10/2011
       </li>
       <li>
        Favorite Line:
       </li>
      </ol>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         She was a large, disordered woman, like a childs drawing that didnt stay within the lines."
        </em>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       But lets back up to the beginning.
      </p>
      <p>
       The book caught my attention the way most novels do - by having a funky, smart-looking cover that Ill feel good about holding between my fingers. Ill admit it; the inky, italic script and slightly parchment-esque paperback cover appealed to me as I imagined reading it with my free hand as the other gripped the handrail on the subway. I flipped through the pages.
       <em>
        Excellent
       </em>
       . The font was small and compact, printed on newsprint-thin paper that reminded me of the copy of a Margaret Atwood novel I read in a "Feminism and the Environment seminar for my English major.
      </p>
      <p>
       So far, so good. This book is lightweight, mid-length, and makes me look like I enjoy challenging, relevant literature. Hyper self-awareness: satisfied.
      </p>
      <p>
       Winner of the Pulitzer Prize  hmm. For what?
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Jeffery Eugenides, American. Author of
        <em>
         The Virgin Suicides
        </em>
        (1993) and
        <em>
         Middlesex
        </em>
        (2002), for which he won the 2003 Pulitzer Prize for Fiction
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Ahh, yes.
       <em>
        Middlesex
       </em>
       sounds vaguely familiar. Unfortunately its not in the BTGO section and Im looking for something a little lighter-hearted than the life and self-discovery of Calliope Stephanides, an intersex person raised a girl, but hormonally a boy. I make a note of that title, though, and return to
       <em>
        The Marriage Plot
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       I check out the back of the book.
      </p>
      <p>
       The synopsis of Eugenides novel advertises the story of three recently graduated students of Brown University, experiencing the drama of their coming of age in 1982. I learn that this coming of age topic is one that the author frequently explores in his work, which hooks me. As much as literature, art, music, and theatre are escapes from reality (as we need them to be), in fiction I tend to search for protagonists whose worlds are those with which I can find parallels to my own. Madeline, Leonard, and Mitchell are each in their early twenties, highly educated, and irrevocably lost. Madeline is even a slightly obsessed English major - hitting pretty close to home.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sounds familiar. Youre on, Mr. Eugenides.
      </p>
      <p>
       I purchased it as one of my three from B&amp;N and took it home to begin reading it that night. Two weeks later, heres where I am on the other side.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        I LIKED IT. IT WAS GOOD."
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       -said no English major ever who got an A on their term paper.
      </p>
      <p>
       But its true!
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        ZOES ONE-WORD REACTION
       </strong>
       :Satisfied.
       <img align="right" alt="satisfied" height="400" src="http://stockfresh.com/files/i/iqoncept/m/14/1981914_stock-photo-satisfied-word-on-blue-thumb-pleased-thumbs-up.jpg" width="397"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       If thats enough for you, Im happy to give my stamp of approval and hope that you check out this book if you have the opportunity. Go forth and prosper under the happy stars of BTGO night sky.
      </p>
      <p>
       More specifically, however:
      </p>
      <p>
       Now that my fingers have brushed against every one of those 416 pages, Im
       <em>
        satisfied
       </em>
       with the fact that the novel delivered exactly what the producing editorial team led me to believe it would. Eugenides captures pretty damn accurately the state of the twenty-something kid just out of college. Even more helpfully, he tells his story through three characters with very different backgrounds. Madeline has money and a supportive family. Leonards got nothing but the clothes on his back and the brains in his head. And Mitchell, while he has the support of a vaguely disapproving family, throws caution to the wind and lives out of a suitcase across Europe and ultimately lands in India for the first year of his post-collegiate life.
      </p>
      <p>
       Their situations are dramatic and more extreme than anything I can relate to in my own struggles to exist outside of the academic world, and yet still theres comfort to be drawn from their stories. The accuracy of Eugenides account I attribute to the bluntness of his character sketches. Madeline, Leonard, and Mitchell are neither likable nor repulsive. They display moments of impressive maturity and courage just as frequently as they behave in ways that leave me wishing their parents spanked them more as children.By the end of the novel each of them finally take steps down the path to becoming the sort of adult theyre meant to be (for now, at least). Theyre not awe-inspiring steps, and some of them are even steps
       <em>
        backward
       </em>
       . But I come away from the text with this advice: TAKE a step. Even if it is a backward one. We might have graduated college, but were never going to leave it until we start walking.
      </p>
      <p>
       I dont know. Its simpler than it seems. Its harder than it sounds. For me, moving in any direction means taking a risk - which is far scarier than standing still and simply passing the time. Following the narratives of three people for whom risk-taking is embraced, on a variety of levels, encouraged me in the way that it is encouraging to hear stories about former classmates who fell on their faces and survived. Self-comparison and doubt. Two things Eugenides seems to strongly encourage moving past in our own coming of age novels. Not get rid of, not ignore- but embrace and move past.
       <img align="left" height="250" src="http://media2.onsugar.com/files/2010/10/40/5/192/1922283/31f8402ec634900a_twilight-simpsons/i/Picture-Robert-Pattinson-Twilight-Character-Simpsons-Treehouse-Horror-Special.jpg" width="250"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Good read. Definitely not a beach read, but not
       <em>
        War and Peace
       </em>
       , either. As a milennial, I strongly suggest it to my fellow milennials.
      </p>
      <p>
       End of BTGO: Part One.
      </p>
      <p>
       Next time up. BTGO: Part Two -
       <em>
        Twilight,
       </em>
       for Grown-Ups.
      </p>
