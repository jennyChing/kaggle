         <p>
          <img alt="Local SEO reports" class="aligncenter wp-image-202780 size-full" height="450" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/reporting-charts-ss-800.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          If you work in Local SEO, youre already familiar with
          <a href="http://searchengineland.com/google-makes-significant-changes-local-search-ranking-algorithm-197778" title="Google Pigeon Updates Local Search Algorithm With Stronger Ties To Web Search Signal">
           the Pigeon update
          </a>
          and the crazy effects its had on local search results. Its painfully obvious that the rankings are still fluctuating but maybe thats a good thing.
         </p>
         <p>
          Many SEOs have been calling for an end to monthly ranking reports for quite a while now. Unfortunately, clients still see ranking reports as the best indicator of SEO success.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now that Pigeon has caused havoc with local rankings, weve got a huge opportunity to take advantage of the flux and educate our clients that rankings dont equal success. All of the Local SEOs can band together, rise up, and show our clients better reports with metrics that really matter to the bottom line.
         </p>
         <h2>
          5 Local SEOMetrics You Should Focus OnInstead Of Rankings
         </h2>
         <p>
          Contrary to popular belief, your monthly reports dont have to be complicated and show tons of data. In many cases, you could ditch your complicated, 10-page report and instead provide twosimple pages of actionable data.
         </p>
         <p>
          Here are 5 metrics that you should be tracking and including in your report to show the success of your Local SEO campaigns:
         </p>
         <h2>
          1. Organic Traffic
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Google Analytics - organic search traffic" class="has-border aligncenter wp-image-202794 size-full" height="341" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/google-analytics-organic-traffic-800.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Youd be amazed at how many SEO reports are out there that dont show organic search traffic! Hopefully youre already including this in your reports, but if youre not, stop reading and go add it in immediately.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are two ways to get to this data. The first is to go to
          <strong>
           Acquisition
          </strong>
          &gt;
          <strong>
           Channels
          </strong>
          ,andthen look at Organic Search. This is the easiest method, but not necessarily the most accurate by default smaller search engines are sometimes not included within Organic Search (theyll instead be counted as referrals), or you might have Google referrals that youd like to include within your organic search traffic. (Note: if youre usingUniversal Analytics, you can now identify any referrers as search engines, but you have to
          <a href="https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/2795821">
           set your analytics up in that manner
          </a>
          .)
         </p>
         <p>
          If youre not using Universal Analytics butknow there are sources youd like to count that wouldnt show up under the standard Organic Search report, you can check your numbers manually. Navigate to
          <strong>
           Acquisition
          </strong>
          &gt;
          <strong>
           All Traffic
          </strong>
          and scan the Source/Medium columnfor organic search sources.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Pro Tip 1:
          </strong>
          If youre going to use the second method, you can build a simple spreadsheet that counts the results for you. Create a range that lists all of the possible organic traffic sources that might be listed, then set up a column that will use VLOOKUP to check your traffic sources against the list. Export the list of traffic sources, then paste them into the spreadsheet, and the totals will be automatically calculated.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Pro Tip 2:
          </strong>
          Instead of simply showing monthly search traffic in a graph or chart, compare your organic traffic to last years organic traffic. Comparing month-to-month isnt accurate in many verticals due to seasonality, but when you compare year-over-year, you get a better view of the traffic trends.
         </p>
         <h2>
          2. Organic Landing Pages
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Google Analytics - organic landing pages" class="aligncenter wp-image-202798 size-full" height="345" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/google-analytics-organic-landing-pages-8001.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          You know which pages are optimized for which keywords. Instead of providing a report that shows how your pages are ranking, you should report on which pages are actually receiving organic search traffic.
         </p>
         <p>
          Go to
          <strong>
           Acquisition
          </strong>
          &gt;
          <strong>
           All Traffic
          </strong>
          ,then select google/organic to isolate the visits from Google (or whichever search engine youre analyzing). Use the secondary dimension drop down to show Landing Page (under Behavior) and youll have a nifty little report that shows which pages get actual visits from Google.
         </p>
         <p>
          Instead of showing what keywords youre ranking for, youre showing which pages are actually getting search engine traffic. Since you know which keywords each page is optimized for, you know which keywords are bringing you the most traffic. If your local targeting is working, you should see increased visits to SEOlanding pages over time.
         </p>
         <h2>
          3. Organic Conversion Sources
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Google Analytics - organic conversion sources" class="aligncenter wp-image-202799 size-full" height="377" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/google-analytics-conversion-sources-800.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          This is another one that youd think would be in every SEO report  yet its missing from far too many. When your client wants to know about the ROI of SEO, theyre asking you to show that the money theyre spending on your services is making a difference to their bottom line. Nothing speaks to the bottom line more than hard numbers on how many leads your client gets.
         </p>
         <p>
          Navigate to
          <strong>
           Conversions
          </strong>
          &gt;
          <strong>
           Goals
          </strong>
          &gt;
          <strong>
           Overview
          </strong>
          . If you scroll down, the default view shows the Goal Completion Locations, but if you click the Source/Medium option just underneath, youll see a list of the top 10 sources for goal conversions. Track your organic sources and make this one of the key metrics in your monthly report.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Pro Tip:
          </strong>
          Create a custom report in Google Analytics to show only organic search sources for goal completions. Again, you can export the results and drop them into an Excel sheet that automatically totals the numbers.
         </p>
         <h2>
          4. Google My Business Impressions
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Google My Business Insights - monthly impressions" class="aligncenter wp-image-202800 size-full" height="336" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/google-my-business-impressions-800.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Google Webmaster Tools will give you impression data across keywords, but if you log into your Google My Business (GMB) dashboard, the Insights data will show impressions for just your GMB listing. Keep in mindthatthe numbers shown are approximate  just like Webmaster Tools, the data is filtered and meant more for a big picture view than exact numbers. Even so, youre still able to see trends over time.
         </p>
         <p>
          Since map pack or Carousel views dont necessarily lead to website visits, its important to show your client how their GMB listing is working for them. They might be getting phone calls or physical visits from map pack views, which you cant track in your reports so showing an increase in GMB impressions over time helps to prove ROI.
         </p>
         <h2>
          5. Clicks For Driving Directions
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Google My Business Insights - driving directions" class="aligncenter wp-image-202801 size-full" height="365" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/google-my-business-driving-directions-800.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Again, values are approximate here, but its definitely a metric that your clients will love. If a user is clicking over to driving directions, you know that theyre planning on a physical visit  andthis is huge for local business owners. Track the number of driving directions clicks over time to show an increase as your Local SEO efforts give your client more visibility in more searches.
         </p>
         <p>
          You can even look at some (extremely general) numbers of where the clicks for directions came from:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Google My Business Insights - driving directions locations" class="aligncenter wp-image-202802 size-full" height="447" src="http://searchengineland.com/figz/wp-content/seloads/2014/09/google-my-business-driving-directions-areas-800.jpg" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Now that Google is moving in a hyper-localized direction, this can help to show where your customers are coming from. If youre targeting specific neighborhoods or suburbs of a metro area, you should be able to see an increase from those zip codes when you track these numbers over time.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Bonus Metric: Manual Phone Tracking
         </h2>
         <p>
          We all know that call tracking numbers are awful for local search, but weve worked with several clients whove opted for manual tracking and had great success. Theyve trained their staff to ask a simple question at the end of every call: If you dont mind, how did you find our number today?
         </p>
         <p>
          Almost 100% of the time, the caller is happy to tell them where the call came from. The numbers are collected and relayed at the end of the month, and were able to include inbound call data along with form conversions. Again, if your Local SEO efforts are successful, youll see an increase in calls from the Internet over time.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Deliver Reports With Metrics That Matter
         </h2>
         <p>
          Again, your reports should deliver metrics that matter to your clients bottom line not pages and pages of data that they dont care about. We should use the Pigeon-induced insanity of local ranking fluctuations to educate our clients and prove to them that they dont need ranking reports to prove ROI. Better reports with actionable data will result in happier clients, which means job security for all of us.
         </p>
