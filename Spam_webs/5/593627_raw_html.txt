     <p>
      <b>
       Zhang Sanfeng
      </b>
      was a legendary Chinese
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoist
      </a>
      who is believed to have achieved
      <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">
       immortality
      </a>
      . According to various accounts, he was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Shaowu" title="Shaowu">
       Shaowu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nanping" title="Nanping">
       Nanping
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">
       Fujian
      </a>
      during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Song_dynasty" title="Southern Song dynasty">
       Southern Song dynasty
      </a>
      and lived for over 200 years until the mid-
      <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">
       Ming dynasty
      </a>
      . His given name was
      <b>
       Tong
      </b>
      () and his
      <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">
       courtesy name
      </a>
      was
      <b>
       Junbao
      </b>
      (). He specialised in
      <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">
       Confucian
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoist
      </a>
      studies, scholarly and literary arts. During the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">
       Emperor Shizu
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">
       Yuan dynasty
      </a>
      , he was nominated as a candidate to join the civil service and held office as the Magistrate of Boling County (; around present-day
      <a href="/wiki/Dingzhou" title="Dingzhou">
       Dingzhou
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Baoding" title="Baoding">
       Baoding
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">
       Hebei
      </a>
      ). While touring around the mountainous regions near present-day
      <a href="/wiki/Baoji" title="Baoji">
       Baoji
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">
       Shaanxi
      </a>
      , he saw the summits of three mountains and decided to give himself the Taoist name "Sanfengzi" (), hence he also became known as "Zhang Sanfeng".
     </p>
     <p>
      Zhang Sanfeng's life is that of indifference to fame and wealth. After declining to serve the government and giving away his property to his clan, he travelled around China and lived as an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascetic" title="Ascetic">
       ascetic
      </a>
      . He spent several years on
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hua" title="Mount Hua">
       Mount Hua
      </a>
      before settling in the
      <a href="/wiki/Wudang_Mountains" title="Wudang Mountains">
       Wudang Mountains
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Sanfeng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Zhang Sanfeng is credited for creating the concept of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Neijia" title="Neijia">
        neijia
       </a>
      </i>
      () in
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_martial_arts" title="Chinese martial arts">
       Chinese martial arts
      </a>
      , specifically
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%27ai_chi_ch%27uan" title="T'ai chi ch'uan">
        taijiquan
       </a>
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">
       Neo-Confucian
      </a>
      syncretism of
      <a href="/wiki/Shaolin_Kung_Fu" title="Shaolin Kung Fu">
       Shaolin martial arts
      </a>
      with his mastery of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tao_yin" title="Tao yin">
        daoyin
       </a>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Neigong" title="Neigong">
        neigong
       </a>
      </i>
      ) principles. On one occasion, he observed a bird attacking a snake and was greatly inspired by the snake's defensive tactics. It remained still and alert in the face of the bird's onslaught until it made a lunge and fatally bit its attacker. This incident inspired him to create a set of 72
      <i>
       taijiquan
      </i>
      movements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leung_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-leung-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is also associated with the Taoist monasteries in the
      <a href="/wiki/Wudang_Mountains" title="Wudang Mountains">
       Wudang Mountains
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Huang_Zongxi" title="Huang Zongxi">
       Huang Zongxi
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Epitaph for Wang Zhengnan
      </i>
      (1669) gave Zhang Sanfeng credit for the development of a Taoist "internal martial arts" style, as opposed to the "external" style of the Shaolin martial arts tradition. Stanley Henning's article,
      <i>
       Ignorance, Legend and Taijiquan
      </i>
      , criticised the myth that Zhang Sanfeng created
      <i>
       taijiquan
      </i>
      and cast doubt on whether Zhang really existed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henning_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-henning-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Zhang Sanfeng was also an expert in the
      <a href="/wiki/Fujian_White_Crane" title="Fujian White Crane">
       White Crane
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Snake_Kung_Fu" title="Snake Kung Fu">
       Snake
      </a>
      styles of Chinese martial arts, and in the use of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jian" title="Jian">
        jian
       </a>
      </i>
      (double-edged Chinese sword).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      According to 19th century documents preserved in the archives of the
      <a href="/wiki/Yang-style_t%27ai_chi_ch%27uan" title="Yang-style t'ai chi ch'uan">
       Yang
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Wu-style_t%27ai_chi_ch%27uan" title="Wu-style t'ai chi ch'uan">
       Wu
      </a>
      -styles
      <i>
       taijiquan
      </i>
      families,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Zhang Sanfeng's master was
      <a href="/wiki/Xu_Xuanping" title="Xu Xuanping">
       Xu Xuanping
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">
       Tang dynasty
      </a>
      Taoist poet and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tao_yin" title="Tao yin">
        daoyin
       </a>
      </i>
      expert. The
      <i>
       taijiquan
      </i>
      families who honour Zhang Sanfeng as the creator of
      <i>
       taijiquan
      </i>
      traditionally celebrate his birthday on the ninth day of the third month in the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
       Chinese calendar
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">
       Writings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Sanfeng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Writings attributed to Zhang Sanfeng include the
      <i>
       Da Dao Lun
      </i>
      (),
      <i>
       Xuanji Zhi Jiang
      </i>
      (),
      <i>
       Xuan Tan Quanji
      </i>
      (),
      <i>
       Xuan Yao Pian
      </i>
      (),
      <i>
       Wu Gen Shu Ci
      </i>
      () and others. These were compiled into a collection known as
      <i>
       The Complete Collection of Mr Zhang Sanfeng
      </i>
      (), which is found in
      <i>
       Dao Zang Ji Yao
      </i>
      (), a series of Taoist texts compiled by Peng Dingqiu () in the early
      <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">
       Qing dynasty
      </a>
      . It also contained introductory notes on Taoist martial arts and music.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_folktales.2C_fiction_and_media">
       In folktales, fiction and media
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Sanfeng&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In folktales, fiction and media">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Much of the written material about Zhang Sanfeng is mythical, contradictory, or otherwise suspect. For instance, he is reported in various accounts
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2014)">
          which?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      to have been born in either 960, 1247 or 1279. He was described to be seven feet tall, with bones like a crane's, a posture like a pine tree, whiskers shaped like spear blades, and capable of travelling a thousand
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Li_(unit)" title="Li (unit)">
        li
       </a>
      </i>
      (roughly 580km or 350 miles) in a day. He was also depicted with a straw hat slung on his back. The 19th-century text
      <i>
       Wu-yang County Gazetteer
      </i>
      mentioned that his hat was actually a cymbal, which only residents in two villages famous for manufacturing cymbals had permission to sound upon meeting him. Some sources
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2014)">
          which?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      recorded two Chinese emperors sending emissaries to search for Zhang Sanfeng and consult him on governance, but they never found him.
     </p>
     <p>
      Owing to his legendary status, Zhang Sanfeng's name appears in Chinese
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wuxia" title="Wuxia">
        wuxia
       </a>
      </i>
      novels, films and television series as a spiritual teacher and martial arts master. Zhang Sanfeng's popularity among the Chinese is also attributed to his personality and association with Confucianism and Taoism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The best known depiction of Zhang Sanfeng in fiction is probably in
      <a href="/wiki/Jin_Yong" title="Jin Yong">
       Jin Yong
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       wuxia
      </i>
      novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Heaven_Sword_and_Dragon_Saber" title="The Heaven Sword and Dragon Saber">
        The Heaven Sword and Dragon Saber
       </a>
      </i>
      , which is primarily set in the final years of the
      <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">
       Yuan dynasty
      </a>
      . In the novel, Zhang Sanfeng is a former
      <a href="/wiki/Shaolin_Sect" title="Shaolin Sect">
       Shaolin
      </a>
      monk who founded the
      <a href="/wiki/Wudang_Sect" title="Wudang Sect">
       Wudang Sect
      </a>
      based in the
      <a href="/wiki/Wudang_Mountains" title="Wudang Mountains">
       Wudang Mountains
      </a>
      . He has seven apprentices, the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Heaven_Sword_and_Dragon_Saber_characters#Wudang_Sect" title="List of The Heaven Sword and Dragon Saber characters">
       "Seven Heroes of Wudang"
      </a>
      , one of whom is the father of the novel's protagonist,
      <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Wuji" title="Zhang Wuji">
       Zhang Wuji
      </a>
      . According to many regional gazettes, Zhang Sanfeng was still alive in the reign of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tianshun_Emperor" title="Tianshun Emperor">
       Tianshun Emperor
      </a>
      (r. 14571464) in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">
       Ming dynasty
      </a>
      , having lived for more than 200 years.
     </p>
     <p>
      Notable actors have portrayed Zhang Sanfeng in film and television.
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_Li" title="Jet Li">
       Jet Li
      </a>
      starred as a young Zhang Sanfeng in the 1993 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tai_Chi_Master_(film)" title="Tai Chi Master (film)">
        Tai Chi Master
       </a>
      </i>
      (1993).
      <a href="/wiki/Sammo_Hung" title="Sammo Hung">
       Sammo Hung
      </a>
      appeared as Zhang Sanfeng in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kung_Fu_Cult_Master" title="Kung Fu Cult Master">
        Kung Fu Cult Master
       </a>
      </i>
      , a 1993 film loosely adapted from
      <i>
       The Heaven Sword and Dragon Saber

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="T.27ai_chi_ch.27uan_lineage_tree">
       T'ai chi ch'uan lineage tree
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Note:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       This lineage tree is not comprehensive, but depicts those considered the "gate-keepers" and most recognised individuals in each generation of the respective styles.
      </li>
      <li>
       Although many styles were passed down to respective descendants of the same family, the lineage focused on is that of the martial art and its main styles, not necessarily that of the families.
      </li>
      <li>
       Each (coloured) style depicted below has a lineage tree on its respective article page that is focused on that specific style, showing a greater insight into the highly significant individuals in its lineage.
      </li>
      <li>
       Names denoted by an asterisk are
       <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">
        legendary
       </a>
       or semi-legendary figures in the lineage; while their involvement in the lineage is accepted by most of the major schools, it is not independently verifiable from known historical records.
      </li>
     </ul>
