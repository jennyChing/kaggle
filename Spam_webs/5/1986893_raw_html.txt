         My father-in-law gave us some cucumbers from his garden, so I thought I would try my hand at pickling for the first time.
        </p>

        <div id="nrelate_flyout_placeholder">
         <p>
          I have always loved sweet pickles, so naturally I thought this would be a great first attempt. This recipe is adapted from a
          <a href="http://www.cooks.com/recipe/d07z0fg/sweet-pickle-chips.html%20">
           Sweet Pickle Chips recipe
          </a>
          on Cooks.com, and when I say adapted all I did was replace the spices with a general pickling spice. Yes, sometimes I can get a little lazy in the kitchen. Regardless, the pickles turned out very tasty, and I even got my husband to eat them; which is impressive, because he loathes pickles.
         </p>
         <p>
          This recipe involves canning, so I strongly urge those who havent canned before to take a look at the
          <a href="http://nchfp.uga.edu/publications/publications_usda.html">
           USDA Complete Guide To Home Canning
          </a>
          before jumping in. I poured over canning websites for two weeks before starting my first canning project. There is a high risk for food-borne illness with canning, so I find it very important to learn the science, and besides, science is awesome!
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://i2.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles5.png" rel="prettyPhoto">
           <img alt="pickles5" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-379" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles5.png?resize=300%2C215"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Sweet Pickle Chips
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          (Yeild 6 Pint Jars)
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span style="color: #232323;">
            Ingredients:
           </span>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          4lbs. pickling cucumbers  washed, blemishes removed, stems and blossom ends removed
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          <strong>
           Brining solution:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          1 quart distilled white vinegar
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          3 Tbsp. salt
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          1 Tbsp. pickling spice
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          1/2 cup sugar
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          <strong>
           Canning Syrup:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          1 2/3 cup of distilled white vinegar
         </p>
         <p style="color: #232323;">
          3 cups sugar
         </p>
         <p>
          2 Tbs pickling spice
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Supplies:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Water Bath Canner
         </p>
         <p>
          Canning Rack
         </p>
         <p>
          6 Pint Jars, Lids and Rings
         </p>
         <p>
          Small Rubber Spatula (Helpful, but not necessary)
         </p>
         <p>
          Jar Grabber (Optional, Tongs will work fine as well.)
         </p>
         <p>
          Lid Magnet (Optional)
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Directions:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Note:
          </strong>
          This Recipe uses a water bath canning method,
          <a href="http://nchfp.uga.edu/publications/uga/using_bw_canners.html">
           click here
          </a>
          for a good resource onthe canning process.
         </p>
         <p style="color: #021eaa;">
          <strong>
           <a href="%20http://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_01/sterile_jars.html">
            First, Sterilize your jars.
           </a>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Wash your cucumbers, remove any blemishes and slice off ends.
          </li>
          <li>
           Cut the cucumbers into 1/4 inch chips.
          </li>
          <li>
           In a large pot, mix the ingredients for the brining solution, then add the cucumbers.
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_380" style="width: 310px">
            <a href="http://i0.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles3.png" rel="prettyPhoto">
             <img alt="Cucumbers in the Brine" class="size-medium wp-image-380" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles3.png?resize=300%2C202"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             Cucumbers in the Brine
            </p>
           </div>
          </li>
          <li>
           Cover and simmer your cucumbers in the brining solution until the skins turn from a bright to a dull green. This should take about 5-7 minutes.
          </li>
          <li>
           As your cucumbers are simmering, heat the canning syrup ingredients to a boil in a separate pot. The syrup needs to be hot when added to the jars.
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_381" style="width: 310px">
            <a href="http://i1.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles2.png" rel="prettyPhoto">
             <img alt="Canning Syrup" class="wp-image-381 size-medium" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles2.png?resize=300%2C197"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             Canning Syrup
            </p>
           </div>
          </li>
          <li>
           Drain the pickles. Pack, still hot, into your hot sterilized pint jars.
          </li>
          <li>
           Cover the pickles with the hot syrup, leaving a 1/2 of head room.
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_382" style="width: 310px">
            <a href="http://i1.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles1.png" rel="prettyPhoto">
             <img alt="Pickles Packed in Syrup and Ready to Can. " class="size-medium wp-image-382" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/backroadsrebellion.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/pickles1.png?resize=300%2C186"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             Pickles Packed in Syrup and Ready to Can.
            </p>
           </div>
          </li>
          <li>
           Remove air bubbles, wipe jar mouths and adjust lids.
          </li>
          <li>
           Process in a hot water bath for 10 minutes.
          </li>
          <li>
           Let cool overnight. Check your jar seals and store in a dry place.
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          I let my pickles sit for two weeks before I popped them open,because Iread somewhere that it helps with the pickling process. I dont know if that is fact, or just an old wives tale, but the pickles were well worth the wait and anticipation. As for most canned goods, the processed pickles have a recommended shelf life of a year, so dont forget to date your jars!
         </p>
