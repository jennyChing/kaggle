      <div class="story-intro">
       <p>
        <strong>
         <!-- google_ad_section_start(name=story_introduction, weight=high) -->
         AIR travel has come a long way since the 1950s, this we know. Were deep into our bizarre affection for SkyMall, personal TV screens and in-flight Wi-Fi (not to mention Wi-Fi in general).
         <!-- google_ad_section_end(name=story_introduction) -->
        </strong>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- // .story-intro -->
      <!-- google_ad_section_start(name=story_body, weight=high) -->
      <p>
       But in the 50s, flying was something different. It was something magical and marvellous.
      </p>
      <p>
       Air travel exploded into its Golden Age, and aeroplane trips werent just a means of getting to your vacation  they were a holiday in themselves. Passengers dressed in their finest to fly. They lined up for group photos before boarding. Riding an aeroplane made them feel like a movie star because it pretty much took the salary of a movie star to do so.
      </p>
      <p>
       But not everything was so rosy. If you took a flight in the 1950s ...
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Turbulence could snap your neck
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Early commercial planes were powered with pistons, not jet engines. As a result, they were loud, vibrated fiercely, bumped like crazy in turbulence and were grounded often due to weather (things got smoother after the first commercial jet debuted in 1952). In the 50s, pressurized aeroplane cabins were relatively new to the scene. And a non-stop flight? Not likely  getting across the country could require multiple layovers.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Your flight attendant wore a girdle and had a weight limit
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Flying was an over-the-top luxury experience, and leggy, chatty hostesses were part of the show. One stewardess recalls her airlines rule that she wear high heels at all times  only after takeoff could she switch to flatter shoes. Hair had to be short enough so as not to touch her collar. A flight attendant manual mandated that stewardesses be single, stay under 125 pounds, and maintain high moral standards during employment.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w1000h id1226956851386">
       <div class="module-content">
        <div class="image-block image-650w1000h">

         </div>
         <!--  // .image-frame -->
         <p class="caption">
          <span class="caption-text">
           circa 1972: Stewardesses working for Southwest Airlines of Texas must be able to wear hot pants and kinky leather boots or they don't get the job. In accordance with the airline's motto, 'sex sells seats' interviewees are selected on the strength of their legs and their face. Drinks served during flights have names such as 'Passion Punch' and 'Love Potion'. (Photo by Alan Band/Keystone/Getty Images)
          </span>
          <span class="image-source">
           <em>
            Source:
           </em>
           Getty Images
          </span>
         </p>
         <!-- // .caption -->
        </div>
        <!-- // .image-block -->
       </div>
       <!-- // .module-content -->
      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       <b>
        You paid up to five per cent of your salary for a ticket
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 50s, a flight from Chicago to Phoenix cost $138 round-trip  thats $1,168 when adjusted for todays inflation. A one-way to Rome would set you back more than $3,000 in todays dollars.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Lobster counted as aeroplane food
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       With commercial plane travel a new market, airlines struggled to one-up each other by offering the fanciest meals. One vintage ad lists TWAs full meal to be served in-flight: soup, meat, salad, vegetables and dessert. Real glassware and roast beef were typical sights.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Smoking was totally acceptable ... and for much longer than youd think
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 1950s, smoking (of cigarettes, pipes and cigars) was totally acceptable in the air, but strangely not in the terminal (they were afraid cigarettes might ignite the fuel fumes). Confusion and resentment ran rampant when a law prohibited smoking on short domestic flights decades later, in 1988. It wasnt until 2000 that a law mandated all flights to and from the U.S. be smoke-free.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Baggage claim was even more excruciating than it is now
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the early 50s, youd wait for a skycap to organise everyones luggage on a counter. One by one, passengers pointed to their suitcases, paid him a tip and collected their bags. Thank heavens for the first conveyor belts!
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1226956851414">
       <div class="module-content">
        <div class="image-block image-650w488h">

         </div>
         <!--  // .image-frame -->
         <p class="caption">
          <span class="caption-text">
           Passengers collect their luggage from a carousel at London Airport (now Heathrow), 1962.
          </span>
          <span class="image-source">

         </p>
         <!-- // .caption -->
        </div>
        <!-- // .image-block -->

       <!-- // .module-content -->
      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       <b>
        You were handed a postcard as you boarded
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Flying was so utterly rare that passengers felt compelled to document every moment on postcards with pictures of the plane or in-flight meal, to show their less lucky loved ones what the newfangled experience was like.
      </p>
      <p>
       The tradition at the time was that you would use your in-flight time to write people you knew on the ground, describing your flight, historian Guillaume de Syon explained.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        You drank (LOTS) for free
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Alcohol was another popular form of in-flight entertainment: passengers were served as much free alcohol as they could drink, and it was not uncommon to come off a flight totally hammered.
      </p>
      <p>
       Of course, the free boozing tapered off as air travel became less of a luxury industry and more of a commercial one. But in those early Golden Days, people just poured themselves scotch after scotch.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        You had insane amounts of legroom
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Coach seats had three to six inches more legroom than they do today  1950s economy class looked more like business class does now. And first class was clearly about as spacious as a modern hotel room.
      </p>

      <p>
       <b>
        You didnt show ID
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Even as late as 1970, passengers made it onto planes without ID of any sort  a quick look-over from security did the trick. Showing up at the airport 30 minutes before your flight was totally fine, and wellwishers could walk right up to your gate where you boarded via stairs, not jet bridge. Passenger screenings wouldnt become mandatory until 1973.
      </p>
