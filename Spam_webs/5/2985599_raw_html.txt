        <div class="headline_area">
         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Organic Chemistry Study Tips: Reaction Maps
         </h1>
         <p class="headline_meta">
          by
          <span class="author vcard">
           <span class="fn">
            James
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p class="headline_meta">
          in
          <span>
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/alcohols-2/" rel="category tag">
            Alcohols
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/aldehydes-2/" rel="category tag">
            Aldehydes
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/functional-groups/" rel="category tag">
            Functional Groups
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/organic-chem-study-tips/" rel="category tag">
            Organic Chem Study Tips
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/organic-chemistry-2/" rel="category tag">
            Organic Chemistry 2
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/category/synthesis-2/" rel="category tag">
            Synthesis
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="format_text entry-content">
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/header-00.png">
           <br/>
          </a>
          This time of year one of the most common problems students come to me with is how to do synthesis problems.
         </p>
         <p>
          Like this:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/00-synth.png">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5040" height="108" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/00-synth.png" title=" " width="343"/>
          </a>
          Theres a lot of different ways to go about learning how to do synthesis  and Ill have a lot more to say about that in future posts  but todays post addresses one of the simplest and most effective ways of learning how to do it.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its all about
          <strong>
           maps
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Maps are easy to understand  they make distances and relationships concrete.
         </p>
         <p>
          Take a map of Texas. Imagine youre in Laredo, and you want to go to Dallas. How can you get there? You have choices. A map helps you see the possibilities.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/3-texas-road-map.gif">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5042" height="587" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/3-texas-road-map.gif" title="3-texas-road-map" width="600"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          At one level of organic synthesis you can think of
          <strong>
           functional groups
          </strong>
          as being like
          <strong>
           cities on a map
          </strong>
          , and reactions that link them are like roads.
         </p>
         <p>
          Lets look at a reaction map for ketones (not comprehensive)
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map1.png">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5043" height="468" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map1.png" title=" " width="447"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          If you look at all these reactions  forward and backward  you can link functional groups to each other through these types of reactions.
         </p>
         <p>
          You can build reaction maps for other functional groups. Heres one for secondary alcohols.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map2.png">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5054" height="467" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map2.png" title="" width="435"/>
          </a>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map3.png">
           <br/>
          </a>
          The more reactions you learn, youll see that some types of functional groups (like ketones) are very busy  like central hubs, theres lots of reactions that link to them (and link from them). On the other hand, other functional groups are a little like Laredo: dusty, one-horse towns in the middle of nowhere. (Of all the functional groups you learn about, ethers fall into this category the best).
         </p>
         <p>
          Lets go back to our problem. If you identify the functional groups involved, it can help you to identify what types of reactions are possible for getting you from your starting point to the destination.
         </p>
         <p>
          Were asked to go from an aldehyde to a tertiary alcohol.
         </p>
         <p>
          Go back to the reaction map. Look for the tertiary alcohol as a product. Then work backwards. How do you get there? One way is from a ketone, in a Grignard reaction. Then think backwards from there  how to get there? One way is to oxidize a secondary alcohol. And if you trace back secondary alcohols, you can get there from the aldehyde and a Grignard reaction.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once you know what reactions to use, its much easier to design your synthesis. Here, the problem is identifying what alkyl groups to use in each of the two Grignard reactions.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map31.png">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5045" height="284" src="http://masterorganicchemistry.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/reaction-map31.png" title=" " width="503"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Heres one key way in which roadmaps and reaction maps are different, however. In real life, you
          <strong>
           <em>
            want
           </em>
          </strong>
          to pick the shortest route from, say, Laredo to Dallas, especially if your cars A/C is on the fritz in the Texas heat. And yes, in the lab, chemists will choose the shortest, most efficient route from one starting material to the final product. Thankfully, for our purposes  which is ultimately just an intellectual exercise just done on paper  the key lesson is
          <strong>
           just to get there.
          </strong>
          If your synthesis is longer than it needs to be  even if you end up driving through Moose Jaw, Saskatchewan on your way to Dallas  dont worry about it too much at this point. Efficiency is a goal for later.
         </p>

           <p>
            Having finished my regular studies (Im a chemistry grad student) I have to say that I always hated learning all these pesky name reactions. Organic chemistry has never been my favourite and fortunately Im only using it as a tool now, as Im an analytic chemist.
            <br/>
            From that point of view Id like to encourage all students not to be disheartened by the immense amount of stuff you have to learn for your organic chemistry exams. Once youve done that you can take a much more relaxed approach. Of course its important to know your basic reaction principles, but it really is of no importance whatsoever which famous chemists name someone attached to that specific reaction.
           </p>
           <p>
            When I saw the above reaction it was immediately clear, that it was some kind of carbonyl reaction. Thats the really important part as I see it. You have to know functional groups and how they react with each other. If you then sort all the reactions youver ever seen into a few simple categories like carbonyl reactions, (cross)couplings, substitution/elimination-reactions and probably Diels-Alder like reactions you may soon realise that this already covers 90% or more of all known chemical reactions.
            <br/>
            So ultimately you may say something like Well ok, this can be done by cross coupling. But If it is a Hiyama- or Suzuki-type coupling  really isnt that imortant. Thats just a matter of reading it up in some research paper and optimising the prcedure for your personal requirements like available solvents etc.
           </p>
           <p>
            To cut a long story short: Youll have to a lot of memorizing (otherwise what would your teachers ask in their exams) but in the end you may well forget most of that stuff again, as long as you understand the underlying principles.
            <br/>
            Unfortunately that insight came a bit late for me (after lots of swearing etc.) and also some of my older professors might disagree with this view. But a lot of their younger colleagues seem to have a much more liberal approach especially in their teaching. So dont lose hope the next time youre poring over a long  list of reactions you have to learn by heart. There is ligth at the end of the tunnel.
           </p>

            Sorry, by the way, for any misspellings or strange expressions. Im not a native speaker
