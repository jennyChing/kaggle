         <h2>
          Abstract
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract2.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract2.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          The malaria parasite
          <em>
           Plasmodium falciparum
          </em>
          exports several hundred proteins into the infected erythrocyte that are involved in cellular remodeling and severe virulence. The export mechanism involves the
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          export element (PEXEL), which is a cleavage site for the parasite protease, Plasmepsin V (PMV). The
          <em>
           PMV
          </em>
          gene is refractory to deletion, suggesting it is essential, but definitive proof is lacking. Here, we generated a PEXEL-mimetic inhibitor that potently blocks the activity of PMV isolated from
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Plasmodium vivax
          </em>
          . Assessment of PMV activity in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          revealed PEXEL cleavage occurs cotranslationaly, similar to signal peptidase. Treatment of
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          infected erythrocytes with the inhibitor caused dose-dependent inhibition of PEXEL processing as well as protein export, including impaired display of the major virulence adhesin, PfEMP1, on the erythrocyte surface, and cytoadherence. The inhibitor killed parasites at the trophozoite stage and knockdown of PMV enhanced sensitivity to the inhibitor, while overexpression of PMV increased resistance. This provides the first direct evidence that PMV activity is essential for protein export in
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          spp. and for parasite survival in human erythrocytes and validates PMV as an antimalarial drug target.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="abstract toc-section">
         <a class="link-target" data-toc="abstract2" id="abstract2" name="abstract2" title="Author Summary">
         </a>
         <h2>
          Author Summary
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract3.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract3.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          To survive within human red blood cells, malaria parasites must export a catalog of proteins that remodel the host cell and its surface. This enables parasites to acquire nutrients from outside the cell and to modify the cell surface in order to evade host defenses. Protein export involves proteolytic cleavage of the
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          Export Element (PEXEL) by the aspartyl protease Plasmepsin V. We report here the development of a small molecule inhibitor that closely mimics the natural PEXEL substrate and blocks the activity of Plasmepsin V from the malarial parasites
          <em>
           Plasmodium falciparum
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Plasmodium vivax
          </em>
          . The inhibitor impairs export and cellular remodeling and kills
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          at the ring-trophozoite transition, providing direct evidence that Plasmepsin V activity is essential for export of PEXEL proteins and parasite survival within the host. These findings validate Plasmepsin V as a highly conserved antimalarial drug target.
         </p>

         <a class="link-target" data-toc="s1" id="s1" name="s1" title="Introduction">
         </a>
         <h2>
          Introduction
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Each year malaria parasites cause several hundred million infections and over 650,000 deaths
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-World1">
           [1]
          </a>
          .
          <em>
           Plasmodium falciparum
          </em>
          causes the most lethal malaria and is endemic in Africa
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Snow1">
           [2]
          </a>
          .
          <em>
           Plasmodium vivax
          </em>
          causes most malarial deaths outside Africa and is associated with liver-stage hypnozoites
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Mendis1">
           [3]
          </a>
          . Although chloroquine and artemisinin have been effective antimalarials, their decreasing efficacy
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Dondorp1">
           [4]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Eastman1">
           [5]
          </a>
          emphasizes the need for therapies against novel targets shared by both
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          spp.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p2" name="article1.body1.sec1.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          Malaria parasites develop in erythrocytes within a parasitophorous vacuole and export over 450 proteins to the cell (reviewed in
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Deponte1">
           [6]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey1">
           [7]
          </a>
          ). Export utilizes an N-terminal motif called the
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          export element (PEXEL; RxLxE/Q/D)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Marti1">
           [8]
          </a>
          or Vacuolar transport signal (VTS)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Hiller1">
           [9]
          </a>
          . Exported proteins are cleaved in the PEXEL after Leu (RxL) in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Chang1">
           [10]
          </a>
          , which requires the conserved Arg and Leu residues
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey2">
           [11]
          </a>
          . PEXEL cleavage is performed by the aspartyl protease Plasmepsin V (PMV)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Russo1">
           [12]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          . PEXEL-containing proteins and PMV are conserved in all
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          spp.
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Marti1">
           [8]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Sargeant1">
           [14]
          </a>
          
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Klemba1">
           [16]
          </a>
          . Repeated attempts to disrupt the
          <em>
           PMV
          </em>
          gene have failed, suggesting it is essential
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Russo1">
           [12]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Klemba1">
           [16]
          </a>
          , but direct and decisive proof is still lacking. A functional survey of
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          exported proteins indicated that 25% or more are essential for parasite survival in human erythrocytes
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Maier1">
           [17]
          </a>
          . The current
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          PEXEL exportome is predicted to be 463 proteins
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey4">
           [18]
          </a>
          ; thus, possibly 100 or more exported parasite proteins are required for development in erythrocytes.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p3" name="article1.body1.sec1.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          Some exported proteins lack a PEXEL, for example, skeleton binding protein 1 (SBP1) and the major virulence adhesin family known as
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          erythrocyte membrane protein 1 (PfEMP1). PfEMP1 is expressed on the erythrocyte surface and mediates cytoadherence to microvascular endothelia, causing severe malaria
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Marti2">
           [19]
          </a>
          . PfEMP1 is thought not to be cleaved by PMV
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey4">
           [18]
          </a>
          , but its transport to, and expression on, the erythrocyte surface requires exported PEXEL and PEXEL-negative proteins (reviewed in
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey1">
           [7]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Maier2">
           [20]
          </a>
          ).
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p4" name="article1.body1.sec1.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          Aspartyl proteases can be inhibited by transition-state isosteres in which the scissile bond is replaced by a noncleavable moiety. Examples include statine (Sta)-containing inhibitors and several are now in clinical use
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Drag1">
           [21]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p5" name="article1.body1.sec1.p5">
         </a>
         <p>
          Here, we developed a transition-state inhibitor that potently blocks PMV from
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           P. vivax
          </em>
          . The inhibitor demonstrates that PMV activity is essential for protein export, PfEMP1 surface display, cytoadherence, and parasite survival in human erythrocytes.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section toc-section" id="section2">
         <a class="link-target" data-toc="s2" id="s2" name="s2" title="Results">
         </a>
         <h2>
          Results
         </h2>
         <h3>
          PMV Is Highly Conserved in
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          The
          <em>
           PMV
          </em>
          gene is present in all
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          spp.; however, only the
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          enzyme (PfPMV; Pf3D7_1323500) has been characterized. A multiple alignment of PfPMV with putative
          <em>
           P. vivax
          </em>
          PMV (PvPMV; PVX_116695) indicated that they share 82.2% similarity, 54.7% identity (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s001">
           Figure S1
          </a>
          ). Both proteins are predicted to contain a signal peptide, an aspartyl protease domain with DTG and DSG residues defining the catalytic dyad, and a C-terminal transmembrane domain (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1A
          </a>
          ). Due to four insertions, PfPMVHA is predicted to be approximately 7.5 kDa larger than PvPMVHA (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1A
          </a>
          ); however, following signal peptide removal, PfPMVHA is predicted to be 8 kDa larger than PvPMVHA.
         </p>

          , compare lanes 1 and 2) and colocalizes with the ER signal from the mouse PfPMV antibody (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1D
          </a>
          , top) but does not cross-react with PvPMVHA (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s002">
           Figure S2A
          </a>
          ). Using anti-HA antibodies, a strong perinuclear signal was observed in parasites expressing PvPMVHA or PfPMVHA (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1D
          </a>
          , middle panels, red). Both proteins colocalized with rabbit anti-PfPMV antibodies, indicating the location was the ER (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1D
          </a>
          ). PvPMVHA also colocalized with ERC (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1D
          </a>
          , bottom), as shown previously for PfPMVHA
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p4" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          To investigate PvPMVHA activity, we affinity purified it, as well as the PfPMVHA control, using anti-HA agarose, as previously described
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey4">
           [18]
          </a>
          . Immunoblot with anti-HA and anti-PfPMV antibodies showed that the purified proteins were species-specific (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s002">
           Figure S2A
          </a>
          ). The proteins were incubated with a fluorogenic peptide of nine amino acids that contained the PEXEL sequence from knob-associated histidine-rich protein (KAHRP), and efficient processing was observed by both enzymes (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1E
          </a>
          ). Cleavage of KAHRP by PfPMVHA was previously shown to occur after Leu (RTL) by mass spectrometry
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          , and this position was also confirmed for PvPMVHA (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s002">
           Figure S2B
          </a>
          E).
          <em>
           Km
          </em>
          values of 9.7 (3.0) and 11.7 (1.8) M were calculated for PvPMVHA and PfPMVHA, respectively (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s002">
           Figure S2F
          </a>
          ). In contrast, no processing was observed when the PEXEL Arg and Leu residues were mutated to Ala (
          <span class="underline">
           A
          </span>
          T
          <span class="underline">
           A
          </span>
          AQ), consistent with the substrate specificity of PfPMV (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1E
          </a>
          ). To verify that processing was due to HA-tagged PMV rather than other co-precipitated proteases, we expressed in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          a mutant of PvPMVHA or PfPMVHA
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          , where one or both catalytic Asp residues were mutated to Ala (see
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1A,B
          </a>
          ). Following affinity purification, the mutant enzymes were incubated with KAHRP PEXEL peptides, but no processing was observed (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1E
          </a>
          ; blue, white), confirming that the PEXEL-dependent cleavage activity observed for each protease was attributable only to HA-tagged PMV.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p5" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p5">
         </a>
         <p>
          The substrate specificity of PfPMV is restricted, such that even minor changes to the PEXEL sequence markedly reduces cleavage efficiencythat is, Arg to Lys (R&gt;K) or Leu to Ile (L&gt;I)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey4">
           [18]
          </a>
          . We assessed whether PvPMVHA shares this feature. Indeed, PvPMVHA poorly cleaved peptides possessing R&gt;K or L&gt;I mutations (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1E
          </a>
          ;
          <span class="underline">
           K
          </span>
          TLAQ, RT
          <span class="underline">
           I
          </span>
          AQ). Collectively, these data show that PvPMV localizes to the ER and cleaves the PEXEL with the same restricted specificity as PfPMV.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p6" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p6">
         </a>
         <p>
          While maintaining
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          cultures overexpressing PfPMVmutHA, we noticed a delay in growth, suggesting a possible dominant negative phenotype, which has been reported previously with a different PfPMV catalytic mutant
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Russo1">
           [12]
          </a>
          . A flow cytometry-based growth assay revealed that parasites expressing PfPMVmutHA with WR99210 selection grew to a parasitemia 2.6-fold less than parasites expressing a similar episomal construct, encoding a mini PfEMP1 reporter fused to 3 HA tags (miniVarHA) with WR99210 selection (
          <em>
           p
          </em>
          &lt;.0001;
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1F
          </a>
          ). This demonstrated that overexpression of inactive enzyme conveyed a growth disadvantage, providing evidence that PMV is important for parasite survival. Analysis of PMV protein levels in parasites overexpressing the PMVHA transgenes indicated that PvPMVHA and PvPMVmutHA had no effect on endogenous PfPMV levels; however, overexpression of inactive PfPMVmutHA caused a clear decrease in expression of the endogenous enzyme (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g001">
           Figure 1C
          </a>
          , compare lanes 1 and 4, and Aldolase loading controls), indicating a negative feedback mechanism occurs in these parasites.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Rational Design of a PMV Inhibitor
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          The conserved P
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          Arg and P
          <sub>
           1
          </sub>
          Leu residues in the PEXEL (see
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g002">
           Figure 2A
          </a>
          for a description of nomenclature) are crucial for PMV activity
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Russo1">
           [12]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          . We developed a homology model and designed compounds with a transition-state isostere that mimics the natural PEXEL substrate with the aim of inhibiting PMV. One mimetic, WEHI-916 (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g002">
           Figure 2B
          </a>
          ), consisted of Arg that could bind in the S
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          pocket of PfPMV, Val that would position in the S
          <sub>
           2
          </sub>
          pocket, and Leu-Statine (Leu-Sta), to engage the S
          <sub>
           1
          </sub>
          pocket and inhibit both catalytic Asp residues of PMV (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g002">
           Figure 2C
          </a>
          ).
         </p>

         </p>
         <h3>
          WEHI-916 Inhibits PMV in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          Infected Erythrocytes
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          To assess whether 916 could inhibit PMV in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          infected erythrocytes, parasites expressing the PEXEL protein
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          erythrocyte membrane protein 3 (PfEMP3) fused to green fluorescent protein (GFP)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          were treated with increasing concentrations of inhibitor, and PEXEL processing was evaluated by immunoblot. A dose-dependent increase in unprocessed PfEMP3-GFP was observed (black arrow,
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3A
          </a>
          ), which was the same size as uncleaved PEXEL R&gt;A mutant PfEMP3-GFP (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3A
          </a>
          )
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          . The level of PEXEL-cleaved protein (blue arrow,
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3A
          </a>
          ) did not quantitatively reflect the degree of PMV inhibition, as inhibitor was added well after PEXEL processing and export of PfEMP3-GFP had initiated. A GFP-only band, representing degraded chimera in the food vacuole, was also observed at ~26 kDa (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3A
          </a>
          ). Together, this demonstrated that PEXEL processing was impaired by 916 treatment and that engagement of PMV occurred in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          infected erythrocytes.
         </p>

          To understand the timing required for PMV inhibition in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          , parasites were treated with 916 for 15 h, and cleavage was evaluated by immunoblot. No effect was seen after 1 h; however, uncleaved PfEMP3-GFP increased between 2 and 5 h (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">

          </a>

          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s003">

          </a>

          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          , had an effect on PEXEL cleavage in parasites. Although a dose-dependent effect was again observed with 916, analogs 024 and 025 had no effect on PEXEL processing of PfEMP3-GFP or KAHRP-GFP, even at 50 M (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3C
          </a>
          ). In the case of KAHRP-GFP, 916 treatment caused accumulation of both uncleaved (black arrow) and signal peptide-cleaved (red arrow) protein, which were the same size as bands observed for PEXEL R&gt;A mutant KAHRP-GFP (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3C
          </a>
          , right). These bands were shown previously to be uncleaved and signal peptide-cleaved KAHRP-GFP, respectively, by mass spectrometry
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey2">
           [11]
          </a>
          .
         </p>

          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s003">
           Figure S3B
          </a>
          ). 916 treatment did not impair processing of the signal peptide from SERA5s-GFP (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3D
          </a>
          , see position of black arrow), indicating it cannot inhibit signal peptidase. Taken together, this shows that 916 can effectively inhibit PMV, but not signal peptidase, in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          infected erythrocytes and that 024 and 025 have no affect on PMV or signal peptidase activity at concentrations up to 50 M.
         </p>
         <h3>
          PMV Acts Cotranslationaly at the ER in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          The rate of PEXEL protein synthesis, ER import, and processing by PMV in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          is unknown. We evaluated these processes by radiolabeling parasite proteins in culture for 0.515 min before immunoprecipitating PfEMP3-GFP with anti-GFP agarose, visualizing bands by autoradiography and quantifying them by densitometry. Labeled PfEMP3-GFP became evident 1 min after addition of label to the culture medium and increased exponentially throughout the experiment (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figures 3E
          </a>
          and
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s003">
           S3D
          </a>
          ). Uncleaved PfEMP3-GFP (black arrow) was faint and the major species was a doublet of approximately 33 kDa (red arrow; signal peptide-cleaved) and 29 kDa (blue arrow; PEXEL-cleaved;
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3E
          </a>
          ). This showed that signal peptidase cleaves PfEMP3 within seconds (&lt;1 min) of protein synthesis (i.e., cotranslationaly), but this molecular species may be transient, as it was not detected by immunoblot of 916-treated parasites, or in PEXEL R&gt;A mutant protein (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3AC
          </a>
          ). The radiolabeled bands on the
          <sup>
           35
          </sup>
          S-membrane were confirmed to be GFP-specific by immunoblot (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s003">
           Figure S3C
          </a>
          ). PEXEL-cleaved PfEMP3-GFP (blue arrow) was also evident 1 min after addition of label to the culture medium and increased exponentially, indicating PMV cleavage was also rapid and likely cotranslational (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figures 3E
          </a>
          and
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s003">
           S3D
          </a>
          ). The proportion of PEXEL-cleaved protein increased slightly as signal peptide-cleaved protein decreased (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3E
          </a>
          ), suggesting signal peptidase cleaves before PMV and that PMV may cleave after signal peptidase.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p2" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          Addition of 916 to parasites for 5 h prior to radiolabeling caused accumulation of uncleaved PfEMP3-GFP in parasites (black arrow), which was evident 12 min postlabeling (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3F
          </a>
          ). After a total of 15 min, the degree of PMV inhibition in parasites was quantified by densitometry, and a 13-fold decrease in PEXEL cleavage was observed compared to labeling without 916 (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3G
          </a>
          ), indicating PMV was inhibited. The signal peptide-cleaved and PEXEL-cleaved species were only weakly detectable throughout the experiment and were not visible until 35 min postlabeling (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3F
          </a>
          ), compared to a stronger signal within 12 min of labeling in the absence of inhibitor when the same quantity of parasites was used (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3E
          </a>
          ). This indicated that 916 significantly blocked PMV in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          and caused a delay in protein synthesis, ER import, or N-terminal processing of PfEMP3-GFP.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p3" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          Processing of uncleaved PfEMP3-GFP within the PEXEL was rescued after 15 min of culture in inhibitor-free medium (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s003">
           Figure S3E
          </a>
          ,F), indicating that PMV is able to process full-length PfEMP3-GFP.
         </p>
         <h3>
          WEHI-916 Kills
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          at the Ring-Trophozoite Transition
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Having demonstrated that 916 can directly engage PMV in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          infected erythrocytes, the effect of the inhibitor on parasite viability was examined by treating early ring parasites for 72 h and assessing parasitemia by flow cytometry. 916 killed parasites with half maximal effective concentration (EC
          <sub>
           50
          </sub>
          ) of 2.55 M (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4A
          </a>
          ). Analogs 024 and 025 had negligible effect on parasite viability at concentrations up to 20 M, where 916 completely killed parasites; however, they had EC
          <sub>
           50
          </sub>
          values of 66 M and 30 M, respectively, indicating they adversely affected parasite growth at high concentrations. Because PEXEL processing in parasites was unaffected by 024 and 025 treatment, even at 50 M (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3C
          </a>
          ), we conclude that the analogs impart toxicity at high concentrations independent of PMV.
         </p>

         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p2" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          To determine the stage of the parasite lifecycle that 916 exerted its toxic effects, ring parasites were treated with 15 M 916 for increasing times through the 48 h cycle and then cultured in inhibitor-free medium to a total of 72 h to see if parasites could recover. Parasites grown in 916 for 120 h completely recovered and grew like DMSO-treated controls; however, treatment for &gt;23 h adversely affected growth (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4B
          </a>
          ), indicating the timing of killing began after 20 h of age, at the ring-trophozoite transition. Analogs 024 and 025 did not affect growth at any parasite stage at the concentration used (15 M), whereas chloroquine and artemisinin killed parasites when added to rings for only 30 min before addition of inhibitor-free medium (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4B
          </a>
          , see 0 h). This indicated the controls either killed rapidly (artemisinin) or were retained inside parasites and killed later, as chloroquine is reported to kill trophozoites
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-terKuile1">
           [22]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Fidock1">
           [23]
          </a>
          . Both profiles were clearly different than that observed for 916.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p3" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          Toxicity by 916 after the ring-trophozoite transition was then investigated by adding compound to parasites at different time points through the 48 h cycle. At 48 h, inhibitor-free medium was added and parasitemia for all conditions was determined at 72 h. Toxicity decreased when 916 was added to parasites aged beyond 24 h and schizonts were resistant, indicating 916 did not affect merozoite egress or reinvasion (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4C
          </a>
          ). As expected, the addition of 916 to rings or early trophozoites was lethal (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4C
          </a>
          ). 024 and 025 did not have any effect on parasite growth at the concentration used, whereas all parasite stages were sensitive to chloroquine and artemisinin (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4C
          </a>
          ).
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p4" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          Light microscopy of parasites following treatment of early ring stages with 916 revealed a normal ring-stage morphology after 16 h; however, treatment for 32 h revealed a blockage in the ring-trophozoite transition and the majority of parasites appeared pyknotic and abnormal (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4D
          </a>
          ). As greater than 50% of parasites could not recover from this treatment condition (refer to
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4B
          </a>
          ), the majority of parasites with this appearance were dying or dead. Treatment with DMSO, 024, or 025 had no effect on development by 32 h at 15 M (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4D
          </a>
          ). The morphology of parasites treated with 916, 024, 025, and DMSO was distinctly different from that observed for E-64treated parasites, which contained swollen food vacuoles from inhibition of haemoglobin breakdown
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Rosenthal1">
           [24]
          </a>
          (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4D
          </a>
          , arrow). This indicated that parasite toxicity to 916 was unlikely due to off-target inhibition of those food vacuole proteases. Collectively, the toxicity profile seen in the above experiments defines the window of parasite death as between 20 and 30 h, consistent with perturbed protein export and erythrocyte remodeling
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey1">
           [7]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p5" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec5.p5">
         </a>
         <p>
          To gain further insight into the effects of 916, 024, and 025 on parasites, we assessed global protein synthesis following drug treatment by radiolabeling parasite proteins. Treatment of trophozoites with inhibitor for 5 h prior to radiolabeling had no detectable effect on translation, even at 50 M concentrations (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s004">
           Figure S4A
          </a>
          ), indicating the compounds are not direct inhibitors of the translation machinery. We next assessed protein synthesis following 23 h of drug treatment of ring stage parasites (aged 13 h old at the initiation of treatment). A minor reduction in translation was observed following 916 treatment, but not 024 or 025 treatment, even at 50 M (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s004">
           Figure S4B
          </a>
          ). A small but concomitant decrease in the cytosolic protein, Aldolase, was also evident by immunoblot following 916 treatment but not 024 or 025 treatment (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s004">
           Figure S4B
          </a>
          ), suggesting that parasites were beginning to die from the 916 treatment (parasites ranged from 2426 h old at this time point). Indeed, Giemsa smears following treatment identified a small proportion of pyknotic parasites in the population (
          <a href="#s2">
           results
          </a>
          not shown). Treatment with Brefeldin A, which prevents retrograde trafficking and ER exit, for 23 h severely impaired translation and parasites appeared as dying rings (i.e., had not progressed to trophozoites). It is possible that PMV inhibition by 916 treatment has a similar but weaker effect to BFA, in that it causes the accumulation of uncleaved PEXEL precursors in the ER, perturbing ER transport, and that this negatively affects translation by an ER stress response
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Hetz1">
           [25]
          </a>
          . An alternative possibility is that translation was decreased slightly as a result of parasites dying from 916-mediated impairment of erythrocyte remodeling. Either way, the profile for 916 was different to that seen for 024 and 025, even at 50 M, indicating the latter analogs likely kill parasites via a different mechanism to 916.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Knockdown or Overexpression of PMV Modulates Parasite Sensitivity to WEHI-916
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec6.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Treatment of
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          with 916 impaired PEXEL cleavage and killed parasites, strongly suggesting that PMV is essential. To investigate this phenotype further, conditional protein knockdown was attempted in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          using the RNA-degrading
          <em>
           glmS
          </em>
          ribozyme, which utilizes glucosamine (GlcN) as a cofactor
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Prommana1">
           [26]
          </a>
          . DNA encoding 3 HA epitopes, a stop codon, and
          <em>
           glmS
          </em>
          was incorporated in frame at the 3 of the
          <em>
           PMV
          </em>
          locus by homologous recombination (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s005">
           Figure S5A
          </a>
          ). Correct genomic integration was confirmed by immunoblot with anti-HA and anti-PfPMV antibodies (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g005">
           Figure 5A
          </a>
          ). To activate
          <em>
           glmS
          </em>
          , GlcN was titrated into the culture medium of trophozoites. From 75% to 90% PMV knockdown was achieved after 48 h using 46 mM GlcN, but higher concentrations adversely affected parasite HSP70 levels and were subsequently avoided (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s005">
           Figure S5B
          </a>
          ). Addition of 5 mM GlcN to trophozoites for 24 h reduced PMV levels in subsequent rings by approximately 80% and trophozoites by ~90% but caused little knockdown in parasites expressing inactive
          <em>
           glmS
          </em>
          (M9) (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g005">
           Figure 5B
          </a>
          )
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Prommana1">
           [26]
          </a>
          . Protein export predominates in rings, when knockdown reached ~80%; surprisingly, this substantial degree of knockdown did not significantly affect PEXEL processing or parasite growth rate (
          <em>
           p
          </em>
          = .6250;
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g005">
           Figure 5C
          </a>
          ), indicating that the remaining PMV levels were sufficient to enable export and sustain parasite development. This demonstrates that PMV activity is potent in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          and that knockdown to approximately 20% of wild-type levels could not facilitate the characterization of PMV essentiality.
         </p>

         </p>

          <sub>
           50
          </sub>
          of 916 was 1.9-fold greater for parasites overexpressing PvPMVHA compared to parasites overexpressing the control construct, and 1.4-fold greater than wild-type 3D7 parasites without WR99210 selection, indicating that PMV overexpression had increased parasite resistance to 916 (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g005">
           Figure 5F
          </a>
          ).
         </p>
         <h3>
          PMV Inhibition Impairs Protein Export, PfEMP1 Display, and Cytoadherence
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec7.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Localization of parasite proteins in Maurer's clefts (MCs), which are parasite-induced membranous structures in the erythrocyte that facilitate protein trafficking, enables accurate quantification of export by immunofluorescence microscopy as the signal is concentrated in puncta
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Tilley1">
           [27]
          </a>
          . To study export in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          , we investigated a novel PEXEL-containing protein with two transmembrane domains and unknown function, called Hyp8 (MAL13P1.61/PF3D7_1301700)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Sargeant1">
           [14]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Silvestrini1">
           [28]
          </a>
          , that we hypothesized may localize to MCs. Transgenic parasites expressing Hyp8-GFP or Hyp8-HA were generated (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s006">
           Figure S6A
          </a>
          ). Immunoblotting revealed that Hyp8 is expressed in rings (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s006">
           Figure S6B
          </a>
          ), and immunofluorescence microscopy showed it is exported (
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s006">
           Figure S6C
          </a>
          ) and colocalizes with SBP1 in MCs (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g006">
           Figure 6A
          </a>
          ). Immunoelectron microscopy confirmed that Hyp8 localizes in MCs (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g006">
           Figure 6A
          </a>
          , right). Three independent attempts to delete the
          <em>
           hyp8
          </em>
          gene were unsuccessful in this study, in addition to earlier reported attempts
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Maier1">
           [17]
          </a>
          , suggesting that Hyp8 may be an essential exported protein.
         </p>

        <div class="section toc-section" id="section3">
         <a class="link-target" data-toc="s3" id="s3" name="s3" title="Discussion">
         </a>
         <h2>
          Discussion
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Protein export allows malaria parasites to remodel their cellular niche, and the protein machineries involved are obvious targets for the development of inhibitors. PMV acts by cleaving the PEXEL in the parasite ER and represents one such target. We developed a PEXEL-mimetic compound that potently inhibits the activity of PMV and, combined with protein knockdown or overexpression, used it to demonstrate the essentiality of PMV for parasite survival and its function for export.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          The PMV inhibitor 916 mimics the transition-state of amide bond proteolysis for PEXEL substrates using statine. Our PfPMV structural model in complex with 916 outlines key interactions that are likely necessary for inhibitor binding: the guanidine side chain of Arg forms salt bridges with the acid of Glu179 and 215, and a -stacking interaction with Tyr177 in the S
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          pocket of PMV. This in part explains the necessity for Arg at P
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          for PEXEL processing. The P
          <sub>
           1
          </sub>
          Leu side chain is encased by hydrophobic residues in the S
          <sub>
           1
          </sub>
          pocket formed in part by Ile116, Tyr177, and Val227, explaining the importance of Leu in PMV binding.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p3" name="article1.body1.sec3.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          916 potently blocked PMV activity
          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          and reduced PEXEL cleavage, by up to 13-fold (
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g003">
           Figure 3G
          </a>
          ), in cultured parasites, demonstrating the inhibitor directly engaged PMV in the ER. However, inhibition was time-dependent and incomplete at even 50 M, indicating the inhibitor has suboptimal qualities. This may be due to a combination of poor diffusion across membranes, suboptimal final concentration in the ER, and the potent activity of PMV in parasites, revealed in this study by knockdown of PMV protein levels. Although further work is required to develop an inhibitor with enhanced properties, 916 has proven sufficiently active in parasites to examine PMV function and essentiality.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p4" name="article1.body1.sec3.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          Previously, it has been shown that overexpression of a PfPMV D118A mutant produced a dominant-negative effect on parasite growth rate and protein export
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Russo1">
           [12]
          </a>
          . When we overexpressed an alternate PfPMV mutant (D118A, D365A, F370A) on episomes in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          , we observed a similar defect in parasite growth rate and subsequent down-regulation of endogenous PfPMV expression levels, suggesting a negative feedback effect. A similar negative feedback effect has been described for
          <em>
           Toxoplasma gondii
          </em>
          myosin A
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Meissner1">
           [34]
          </a>
          . Collectively, these PMV dominant-negative mutants provide evidence that the enzyme is important for parasite survival.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p5" name="article1.body1.sec3.p5">
         </a>
         <p>
          The effects of 916 were amplified when PMV was knocked down and decreased when PMV was overexpressed, demonstrating that PMV is a target and that its inhibition is toxic to parasites. Addition of 916 to ring stages arrested their transition to trophozoites, between 20 and 30 h postinvasion, and parasites could not recover. Although this phenotype is consistent with death from impaired export and cellular remodeling, it is possible that ER stress due to accumulation of uncleaved PEXEL proteins in the organelle, and a decrease in translation, contributed
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Hetz1">
           [25]
          </a>
          . The morphology of parasites treated with 916 was distinctly different to E-64treated parasites, which contained swollen food vacuoles from inhibition of haemoglobin breakdown
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Rosenthal1">
           [24]
          </a>
          . This indicates that parasite toxicity to 916 was unlikely due to off-target effects on food vacuole proteases. Although aspartyl protease inhibitors are known to kill
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          , it is not entirely clear which aspartyl proteases (Plasmepsins) are essential (reviewed in
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Coombs1">
           [35]
          </a>
          ). In
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          , there are 10 Plasmepsins; IIV are important enzymes for haemoglobin degradation by
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          , but the genes encoding each enzyme can be deleted from the genome, indicating they are not essential
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Bonilla1">
           [36]
          </a>
          . The death of
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          following 916 treatment is therefore unlikely to be due to inhibition of these Plasmepsins. A survey of
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          transcriptomes
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-LopezBarragan1">
           [37]
          </a>
          suggests that, of the remaining five Plasmepsins (VIX), only VII, IX, and X are expressed by asexual blood-stage parasites; however, VII and X are expressed at very low levels. Thus, Plasmepsin IX (PMIX) is considered the primary possible off-target in our study. However, we have shown previously that HA-tagged PMIX does not cleave the PEXEL motif
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          , and although the enzyme itself possesses a PEXEL motif, its function and essentiality is currently unknown.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p6" name="article1.body1.sec3.p6">
         </a>
         <p>
          Analogs of 916 that mimic noncleavable PEXEL mutant sequences (R&gt;K, L&gt;I) were ineffective inhibitors of PMV
          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          and had no discernable effect on parasites at concentrations that 916 inhibited PEXEL cleavage in parasites and was lethal (i.e., &lt;20 M). However, at concentrations above 20 M they were toxic to parasites and possessed EC
          <sub>
           50
          </sub>
          values between 6- and 26-fold less potent than 916. At 50 M, we saw no evidence of PMV or signal peptidase inhibition, export or secretion defects, or global effects on translation caused by 024 or 025. This suggests that they hit a target(s) distinct from 916.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p7" name="article1.body1.sec3.p7">
         </a>
         <p>
          The rapid rate of protein synthesis, ER import, signal peptide processing, and PEXEL cleavage in
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          was determined for the first time. The rate was within immeasurable seconds after translation, consistent with both signal peptidase and PMV activity occurring cotranslationaly. The full-length PEXEL is thus only present in the proprotein very transiently, making its function in export even more remarkable. This underscores the importance of the remaining PEXEL residues (xE/Q/D) in export following processing
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey2">
           [11]
          </a>
          . The PEXEL has been suggested to function independent of PMV by binding PI3P in the ER, and ER-derived transport vesicles, via the PEXEL Arg
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Bhattacharjee1">
           [38]
          </a>
          . The rate at which PEXEL processing occurred in our experiments is inconsistent with this hypothesis, as the PEXEL Arg is cleaved off during, or soon after, ER entry. It is also challenging to envisage how the PEXEL Arg could dock within the S
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          pocket of PMV, where it is required for proteolytic cleavage, if it is bound to the ER membrane via an interaction with PI3P.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p8" name="article1.body1.sec3.p8">
         </a>
         <p>
          This work has characterized a novel PEXEL protein, Hyp8, which is exported in the early ring-stage to MCs. The function of Hyp8 is unknown, but the
          <em>
           hyp8
          </em>
          gene was refractory to deletion and may be essential. 916 treatment dramatically impaired Hyp8 export, resulting in some accumulation in the parasite and possibly degradation. It is unknown whether the reduced export of Hyp8 directly contributed to parasite death; however, the phenotype demonstrates the importance of PMV in export of PEXEL-containing cargo. This is further supported by the decrease in MC numbers observed following 916 treatment; MC formation is known to require exported proteins (reviewed in
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Maier2">
           [20]
          </a>
          ). The secretion of EXP2 to the parasitophorous vacuole membrane was unaltered by 916 treatment, demonstrating that the effects of 916 were specific to export. A clear defect in PfEMP1 surface exposure and cytoadherence was also observed following 916 treatment. PfEMP1 is unlikely to be a PMV substrate
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey4">
           [18]
          </a>
          , but its trafficking through the erythrocyte and onto the surface requires at least six PEXEL-containing proteins
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Maier1">
           [17]
          </a>
          ; thus, the demonstration that PMV activity is essential for PfEMP1 surface expression and cytoadherence is consistent with the current literature and validates the specificity of the inhibitor. Further, it directly demonstrates the importance of PMV at the first step in the export pathway for cellular remodeling that leads to virulence.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p9" name="article1.body1.sec3.p9">
         </a>
         <p>
          <em>
           P. vivax
          </em>
          is an important global pathogen that cannot be cultured in the laboratory, and novel therapeutic targets for this enigmatic parasite are urgently needed. This work has characterized PMV from
          <em>
           P. vivax
          </em>
          for the first time. PvPMVHA possesses the trafficking information to localize to the ER and has similar PEXEL cleavage activity and specificity to PfPMV. This indicates that PMV function is to cleave the PEXEL motif of exported proteins across
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          spp., and future compounds that block PMV are likely to affect multiple
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          spp. Protein export also occurs in gametocytes
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Silvestrini1">
           [28]
          </a>
          and liver stages
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Singh1">
           [39]
          </a>
          , and 916 may aid the characterization of PMV in these stages.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.p10" name="article1.body1.sec3.p10">
         </a>
         <p>
          A putative PMV homolog, ASP5, is present in
          <em>
           Toxoplasma
          </em>
          and localizes to the Golgi
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Shea1">
           [40]
          </a>
          . Recent evidence suggests that some exported
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          proteins contain a PEXEL
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Bougdour1">
           [41]
          </a>
          , and some are cleaved in a manner that requires the conserved PEXEL residues
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Hsiao1">
           [42]
          </a>
          . The PEXEL protease may therefore be conserved beyond the
          <em>
           Plasmodium
          </em>
          genus, and PMV and its homologs may therefore represent multistage, multispecies antiparasitic targets of the future.
         </p>
        </div>

         <h2>
          Materials and Methods
         </h2>
         <h3>
          Plasmids, Parasites, and Antibody Production
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec4.sec1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          3D7 parasites expressing PfPMVHA, PfPMVmutHA, and PfEMP3-GFP were generated previously
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          , as was KAHRP-GFP
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [18]
          </a>
          and CS2-GFP
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Wilson1">
           [29]
          </a>
          . DNA encoding PvPMV or PvPMVmut fused to 3 HA tags was synthesized (Epoch Biosciences) and cloned into pGlux.1

         <h3>
          PMV-Agarose, PEXEL Cleavage Assays, Mass Spectrometry, and Parasite Growth Assays
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec4.sec2.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          PMV-agarose was prepared by adding -HA-agarose (Sapphire Bioscience) to parasite lysates, prepared by sonication in 1% Triton X-100/PBS, for 1 h before extensive washing in same
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Russo1">
           [12]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boddey3">
           [13]
          </a>
          . PEXEL cleavage assays (20 l total volume) consisted of 0.2 l PMV-agarose in digest buffer (25 mM Tris, 25 mM MES, pH 6.4) with 1.5 M FRET peptide substrate (DABCYL-RNKRTLAQKQ-E-EDANS, DABCYL-RNKATAAQKQ-E-EDANS, LifeTein; DABCYL-RNKKTLAQKQ-E-EDANS, DABCYL-RNKRTIAQKQ-E-EDANS; Mimotopes)  inhibitor. Samples were excited at 340 nm and fluorescence emission measured at 492 nm using an Envision fluorescence plate reader (Perkin-Elmer) heated to 37C for 150 min. Samples were shaken between measurements. For determination of the peptide cleavage position by PvPMVHA, the fluorogenic peptide (DABCYL-RNKRTLAQKQ-E-EDANS), representing the wild-type KAHRP PEXEL sequence, was incubated with and without PvPMVHA at 37C for 48 h. Products of the incubation were detected by a molecular formula algorithm using an Agilent 6200 TOF/6500 series mass spectrometer.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.sec2.p2" name="article1.body1.sec4.sec2.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          Parasite growth assays were performed in 96-well plates by incubating highly synchronous ring-stage
          <em>
           P. falciparum
          </em>
          3D7 or NF54 parasites with compounds solubilized in DMSO at the indicated concentrations for the indicated times. In the case of dose-response curves, medium was kept for the entire experiment; in the case of curves in
          <a href="#pbio-1001897-g004">
           Figure 4B,C
          </a>
          , medium was replaced with inhibitor-free medium at 48 h postinfection. Parasitaemia was always determined at 72 h by flow cytometry. To knock down PMV, GlcN (Sigma) was added to trophozoites and drug curves initiated by adding compound at subsequent rings (24 h) for 2472 h.
         </p>

         <h3>
          916 Dosage Regime and PfEMP1 Surface Display
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.sec6.p1" name="article1.body1.sec4.sec6.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          As 916 treatment has adverse affects on parasites after 24 h, a sublethal dosing regime was developed (see
          <a href="#pbio.1001897.s007">
           Figure S7
          </a>
          ) to maximize the inhibitor effect while ensuring parasites remained viable 24 h postinvasion when surface-exposed PfEMP1 was measured. Treatment with &gt;15 M for 23 h or with 50 M for &gt;12 h postinvasion prior to decreasing to 15 M adversely affected parasite growth and was avoided.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.sec6.p2" name="article1.body1.sec4.sec6.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          To measure PfEMP1 display, highly synchronous ring-stage CS2-GFP parasites preferentially expressing VAR2CSA (see Plasmids, Parasites, and Antibody Production) were obtained by incubation of erythrocytes with viable merozoites for 15 min
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Boyle1">
           [47]
          </a>
          , and parasites were treated with 916, 025, or DMSO using the dosage regime above. At 24 h postinvasion (presence of inhibitor for no more than 23 h), erythrocytes were incubated with human monoclonal PAM1.4 serum
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pbio.1001897-Barfod1">
           [30]
          </a>
          (1:200) to label VAR2CSA followed by goat anti-human IgG Biotin-conjugated secondary antibodies (Invitrogen) (1:200) and Alexa Fluor-633 Streptavidin-conjugated tertiary antibodies (Invitrogen) (1:500) for 30 min each. Labeled cells were washed with 0.1% casein/PBS and analyzed on a FACSCalibur cytometer (Becton-Dickinson, USA). Fluorescence in channel FL1 was used to measure parasite-infected erythrocytes (GFP), and fluorescence in channel FL4 was used to measure bound PAM1.4 IgG antibodies (Alexa 633) for each sample. The geometric mean fluorescence of uninfected erythrocytes (treated with secondary and tertiary but not primary antibodies) was deducted from the geometric mean fluorescence of infected erythrocytes using &gt;100,000 cells per condition. Experiments were conducted in duplicate. Analyses were performed using FlowJo 8.8.7 (Tree Star, USA).
         </p>
