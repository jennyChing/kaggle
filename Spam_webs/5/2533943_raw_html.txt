     <p>
      One major challenge within happiness is loneliness.The more Ive learned about happiness, the more Ive come to believe that loneliness is a terrible, common, and important obstacle to consider.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Elizabeth Bernsteins recent
      <em>
       Wall Street Journal
      </em>
      piece, 
      <a href="http://online.wsj.com/news/articles/SB10001424052702303936904579177700699367092" target="_blank">
       Alone or Lonely
      </a>
      , the rate of loneliness in the U.S. has doubled over the past thirty years. About 40% of Americans report being lonely; in the 1980s, it was 20%. One reason: more people live alone (27% in 2012; 17% in 1970). But being alone and being lonely arent the same.
     </p>
     <p>
      A while back, after reading John Cacioppos fascinating book
      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/0393335283/ref=as_li_tf_til?tag=positipositi-20&amp;camp=0&amp;creative=0&amp;linkCode=as1&amp;creativeASIN=0393335283&amp;adid=0Y1E6WZ4N69AKKP5E5EP" target="_blank">
       <em>
        Loneliness
       </em>
      </a>
      , I posted
      <a href="http://www.gretchenrubin.com/2009/11/some-counterintuitive-facts-about-loneliness" target="_blank">
       some counter-intuitive facts about loneliness
      </a>
      , and several people responded by asking, Okay, but what do I
      <em>
       do
      </em>
      about it? What steps can I take to feel less lonely?
     </p>
     <p>
      I then read another fascinating book,
      <em>
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/dp/B0057DCGSO/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_til?tag=positipositi-20&amp;camp=0&amp;creative=0&amp;linkCode=as1&amp;creativeASIN=B0057DCGSO&amp;adid=1A4P4016PV59F583F74W" target="_blank">
        Lonely
       </a>
      </em>
      a memoir by Emily White about her own experiences and research into loneliness. White doesnt attempt to give specific advice about how to combat loneliness, and I dont want to put words in her mouth, but from her book, I gleaned these strategies:
     </p>
     <p>
      1. Remember that although the distinction can be difficult to draw,
      <strong>
       loneliness and solitude are different
      </strong>
      . White observes, Its entirely reasonable to feel lonely yet still feel as though you need some time to yourself. Loneliness feels draining, distracting, and upsetting; desired solitude feels peaceful, creative, restorative.
     </p>
     <p>
      2.
      <strong>
       Nurturing others
      </strong>
      raising children, teaching, caring for animalshelps to alleviate loneliness.
     </p>
     <p>
      3. Keep in mind that to avoid loneliness,
      <strong>
       many people need
       <em>
        both
       </em>
       a social circle
      </strong>
      <strong>
       and an intimate attachment
      </strong>
      . Having just one of two may still leave you feeling lonely.
     </p>
     <p>
      4.
      <strong>
       Work hard to get your sleep.
      </strong>
      One of the most common indicators of loneliness is broken sleeptaking a long time to fall asleep, waking frequently, and feeling sleepy during the day. Sleep deprivation, under any circumstance, brings down peoples moods, makes them more likely to get sick, and dampens their energy, so its important to tackle this issue. (Here are some tips on
      <a href="http://www.gretchenrubin.com/2010/01/fourteen-tips-for-getting-more-sleep-and-why-it-matters" target="_blank">
       getting good sleep
      </a>
      .)
     </p>
     <p>
      5.
      <strong>
       Try to figure out whats missing from your life.
      </strong>
      White observes that making lots of plans with friends didnt alleviate her loneliness. What I wanted, she writes, was the quiet presence of another person. She longed to have someone else just hanging around the house with her. The more clearly you see whats lacking, the more clearly youll see possible solutions.
     </p>
     <p>
      6.
      <strong>
       Take steps to connect with other people
      </strong>
      (to state the obvious). Show up, make plans, sign up for a class, take a minute to chat.
     </p>
     <p>
      7.
      <strong>
       Stay open
      </strong>
      . Negative emotions like loneliness, envy, and guilt have an important role to play in a happy life; theyre big, flashing signs that something needs to change. The pain of loneliness can prod you to connect with other people. Unfortunatelyand this may seem counter-intuitive
      <strong>
       loneliness itself can make people feel more
       <a href="http://www.gretchenrubin.com/2009/11/some-counterintuitive-facts-about-loneliness" target="_blank">
        negative, critical, and judgmental
       </a>
      </strong>
      . If you recognize that your loneliness may be affecting you in that way, you can take steps to counter it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most people have suffered from loneliness at some point. Have you found any good strategies for making yourself less lonely? What worked or didnt work?
     </p>

            I moved from Los Angeles to Argentina (long story) and dealt with loneliness. I was far away from my friends and family and really felt it.
           </p>
           <p>
            Aside from learning the language so that I can communicate
            <img alt=":)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://cdn.positivelypositive.com/wp/wp-includes/images/smilies/icon_smile.gif"/>
            the single thing that helped the most was joining an exercise group. It combines your suggestion of taking steps to connect with other people with the mental benefits of exercise. The camaraderie and the endorphin rush combine to leave me feeling happy and optimistic about whatever challenges Im facing. An extra bonus, that I am only now aware of its implications: I sleep like a rock.
           </p>
