           <blockquote>
            <p>
             "
             <em>
              Whiskey Tango Foxtrot
             </em>
             is one of those cautionary novels about the near future that we've seen so much of in recent years. ... It's a first novel written by a young writer who actually lives in Portland, Ore. And it's often funny and it's what I would call scarily realistic. It's a techno thriller. And it takes place, as I said, in a future that is within our sight. ... It's a future in which a cabal of very dastardly people ... have decided that what they are going to do is privatize information. So everything that we so trustingly hand over via our credit card, via the Google searches that we do, for example, what we put on Facebook, what we tweet, every online move we make is going to be privatized and it's a way of making more money for those who have it. ... But three 30-somethings rather reluctantly come together in order to try to foil the plans of these people. ...
            </p>
            <p>
             "This is a book that I would give to people who loved Dave Eggers' novel from last year,
             <a href="http://www.npr.org/books/titles/236152590/the-circle" target="_blank">
              The Circle
             </a>
             ; Don DeLillo's older novel, wonderful novel,
             <em>
              White Noise
             </em>
             ; anything by William Gibson, including
             <a href="http://www.npr.org/books/titles/360208554/the-peripheral" target="_blank">
              his newest book
             </a>
             which is just recently out; and of course Neal Stephenson's
             <a href="http://www.npr.org/books/titles/138274686/cryptonomicon" target="_blank">
              Cryptonomicon
             </a>
             <em>
              .
             </em>
             The book has a very different plot, but the feelings that you get, the experience of reading this book, is similar to the experience that one has reading those four books."
            </p>

          <div class="capsulereview">
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             "1914 was the start of World War I [and] this is the 100th anniversary, so this year of course we saw a lot of books about World War I. And Samuel Hynes'
             <em>
              The Unsubstantial Air
             </em>
             I think is one of the best that we have.
            </p>
            <p>
             "Samuel Hynes taught English at Princeton for many years. He is in his 90s now, I believe. This is his most recent book and it's a book that talks about the war in terms of those young men who came from American colleges to fly and to fight in World Word I. ... Because that was a period when people wrote letters and journals ... Samuel Hynes was able to access just a treasure trove of journals and of letters from these young men, many of whom had never been to Europe before. And Samuel Hynes himself was a pilot in World War II. He's someone who has written about his experiences flying in other places, so he understands the excitement that these boys  and they were boys, they were 18 and 19 years old, young men  that they felt flying. He understands what it's like to have death be with you all the time.
            </p>
            <p>
             "And he writes in such a beautiful way. ... It is very original. I mean, I've never read a book quite like this and certainly not about World War I, when we're either concerned with the details of a particular battle or the kind of overarching themes of the war or what happened at the end of the war. So the experiences of these young men is just so moving and it's so  they were so brave. And he does a wonderful job honoring them."
            </p>
           </blockquote>
