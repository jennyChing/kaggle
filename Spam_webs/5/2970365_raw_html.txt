     <p>
      <b>
       Operation Highjump
      </b>
      , officially titled
      <i>
       The United States Navy Antarctic Developments Program, 19461947
      </i>
      , was a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      operation organized by
      <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral_(United_States)#Rear_admiral" title="Rear admiral (United States)">
       Rear Admiral
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_E._Byrd" title="Richard E. Byrd">
       Richard E. Byrd
      </a>
      Jr., USN (Ret), Officer in Charge, Task Force 68, and led by Rear Admiral Richard H. Cruzen, USN, Commanding Officer, Task Force 68. Operation Highjump commenced 26 August 1946 and ended in late February 1947. Task Force 68 included 4,700 men, 13 ships, and 33 aircraft. Operation Highjump's primary mission was to establish the
      <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
       Antarctic
      </a>
      research base
      <a href="/wiki/Little_America_(exploration_base)" title="Little America (exploration base)">
       Little America
      </a>
      IV.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polar_Record_43_224_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Polar_Record_43_224-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <br/>
      Highjumps objectives, according to the U.S. Navy report of the operation, were:
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Training personnel and testing equipment in frigid conditions;
      </li>
      <li>
       Consolidating and extending the United States' sovereignty over the largest practicable area of the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       continent (This was publicly denied as a goal even before the expedition ended
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       Determining the feasibility of establishing, maintaining, and utilizing bases in the Antarctic and investigating possible base sites;
      </li>
      <li>
       Developing techniques for establishing, maintaining, and utilizing air bases on ice, with particular attention to later applicability of such techniques to operations in interior
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       , where conditions are comparable to those in the Antarctic;
      </li>
      <li>
       Amplifying existing stores of knowledge of
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">
        electromagnetic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geological
       </a>
       , geographic,
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">
        hydrographic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">
        meteorological
       </a>
       propagation conditions in the area;
      </li>
      <li>
       Supplementary objectives of the
       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Nanook_(1946)" title="Operation Nanook (1946)">
        Nanook
       </a>
       expedition (a smaller equivalent conducted off eastern Greenland).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>

     </p>

     <p>
      The Western Group of ships reached the
      <a href="/wiki/Marquesas_Islands" title="Marquesas Islands">
       Marquesas Islands
      </a>
      on December 12, 1946, whereupon the
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Henderson_(DD-785)" title="USS Henderson (DD-785)">
       <i>
        Henderson
       </i>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Cacapon_(AO-52)" title="USS Cacapon (AO-52)">
       <i>
        Cacapon
       </i>
      </a>
      set up weather monitoring stations. By December 24, the
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Currituck_(AV-7)" title="USS Currituck (AV-7)">
       <i>
        Currituck
       </i>
      </a>
      had begun launching aircraft on reconnaissance missions.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Eastern Group of ships reached
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_Island" title="Peter I Island">
       Peter I Island
      </a>
      in late December 1946.
     </p>
     <p>
      On January 1, 1947, LCDR Thompson and Chief Petty Officer Dixon utilized "Jack Browne"
      <a href="/wiki/Diving_mask" title="Diving mask">
       masks
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/DESCO" title="DESCO">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">
       rebreathers
      </a>
      to log the first
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_diving" title="Ice diving">
       dive
      </a>
      by Americans under the Antarctic.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_losses">
       Human losses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Highjump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Human losses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On December 30, 1946, aviation radiomen Wendell K. Hendersin, Fredrick W. Williams, and Ensign Maxwell A. Lopez were killed when their
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PBM_Mariner" title="PBM Mariner">
       PBM Mariner
      </a>
      <i>
       George 1
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/1946_Antarctica_PBM_Mariner_crash" title="1946 Antarctica PBM Mariner crash">
       crashed
      </a>
      during a blizzard. The surviving six crew members, including aviation radioman James H. Robbins and co-pilot William Kearns, were rescued 13days later. A plaque was later erected at the
      <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Station" title="McMurdo Station">
       McMurdo Station
      </a>
      research base, honoring the three killed crewmen,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Lopez" title="Mount Lopez">
       Mount Lopez
      </a>
      , on
      <a href="/wiki/Thurston_Island" title="Thurston Island">
       Thurston Island
      </a>
      , was named in honor of killed airman Maxwell A. Lopez.
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2004, an attempt was made to locate the remains of the plane.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are ongoing efforts to repatriate the bodies of the three men killed in the crash.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometime after December 30, 1946, Vance N. Woodall died during a "ship unloading accident". In a crew profile, deckman Edward Beardsley described his worst memory as "when Seaman Vance Woodall died on the Ross Ice Shelf under a piece of roller equipment designed to 'pave' the ice to build an airstrip."
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Afterwards">
       Afterwards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Highjump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Afterwards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/William_Menster" title="William Menster">
       Father William Menster
      </a>
      served as
      <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">
       chaplain
      </a>
      during the expedition, and in a service in 1947 he consecrated Antarctica.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Central Group of ships reached the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Whales" title="Bay of Whales">
       Bay of Whales
      </a>
      on January 15, 1947, where they constructed temporary
      <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">
       runways
      </a>
      along the glaciers, in a base dubbed
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Little_America_(exploration_base)" title="Little America (exploration base)">
        Little America IV
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Naval ships and personnel were withdrawn back to the United States in late February 1947, and the expedition was terminated due to the early approach of winter and worsening weather conditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polar_Record_43_224_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Polar_Record_43_224-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Admiral Byrd, in an interview with Lee van Atta of
      <a href="/wiki/International_News_Service" title="International News Service">
       International News Service
      </a>
      aboard the expeditions command ship, the
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Mount_Olympus_(AGC-8)" title="USS Mount Olympus (AGC-8)">
       USS
       <i>
        Mount Olympus
       </i>
      </a>
      , discussed the lessons learned from the operation. The interview appeared in the Wednesday, March 5, 1947 edition of the Chilean newspaper
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/El_Mercurio" title="El Mercurio">
        El Mercurio
       </a>
      </i>
      and read in part as follows:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Admiral Richard E. Byrd warned today that the United States should adopt measures of protection against the possibility of an invasion of the country by hostile planes coming from the polar regions. The admiral explained that he was not trying to scare anyone, but the cruel reality is that in case of a new war, the United States could be attacked by planes flying over one or both poles. This statement was made as part of a recapitulation of his own polar experience, in an exclusive interview with International News Service. Talking about the recently completed expedition, Byrd said that the most important result of his observations and discoveries is the potential effect that they have in relation to the security of the United States. The fantastic speed with which the world is shrinking  recalled the admiral  is one of the most important lessons learned during his recent Antarctic exploration. I have to warn my compatriots that the time has ended when we were able to take refuge in our isolation and rely on the certainty that the distances, the oceans, and the poles were a guarantee of safety.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         10
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      After the operation ended, a follow-up
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Windmill" title="Operation Windmill">
       Operation Windmill
      </a>
      returned to the area, in order to provide ground-truthing to the aerial photography of Highjump, from 1947-1948.
      <a href="/wiki/Finn_Ronne" title="Finn Ronne">
       Finn Ronne
      </a>
      also financed a private operation to the same territory, until 1948.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As with other U.S. Antarctic expeditions, interested persons were allowed to send letters with enclosed envelopes to the base, where commemorative
      <a href="/wiki/Cachet" title="Cachet">
       cachets
      </a>
      were added to their enclosures, which were then returned to the senders. These souvenir
      <a href="/wiki/Philatelic_cover" title="Philatelic cover">
       philatelic covers
      </a>
      are readily available at low cost.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Books">
       Books
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Highjump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Books">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Doherty_(writer)" title="Robert Doherty (writer)">
        Robert Doherty
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Area_51" title="Area 51">
        Area 51
       </a>
       novel series Operation Highjump is said to have been a cover for an expedition to excavate
       <a href="/wiki/Flying_saucer" title="Flying saucer">
        flying saucers
       </a>
       buried under Antarctica's ice shelf by long-ago extraterrestrial visitors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Much of the action in
       <a href="/wiki/James_Rollins" title="James Rollins">
        James Rollins
       </a>
       ' novels
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/James_Rollins#Subterranean_.281999.29" title="James Rollins">
         Subterranean
        </a>
       </i>
       (1999) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/James_Rollins#SIGMA_Force_Book_10:_The_6th_Extinction_.282014.29" title="James Rollins">
         The 6th Extinction: Sigma Force Book #10
        </a>
       </i>
       (2014) takes place at the American base in the Antarctic and liberally reference Colonel Byrd and other nation's bases, expeditions, equipment, and artifacts. For example, in
       <i>
        The 6th Extinction
       </i>
       , the characters investigate the remains of a Nazi U-boat, discuss cryptic statements by
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_D%C3%B6nitz" title="Karl Dnitz">
        Admiral Karl Dnitz
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">
        Nuremberg trials
       </a>
       and his light prison sentence, and some escape using Colonel Byrd's snow cruiser.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Adherents to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hollow_Earth" title="Hollow Earth">
        Hollow Earth
       </a>
       hypothesis believe that Admiral Byrd flew over the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       and into the hollow earth in February 1947, and that he kept a secret diary of the incident. This belief was first published in F. Amadeo Giannini's book
       <i>
        The Worlds Beyond the Poles: Physical Continuity of the Universe
       </i>
       (1959). Giannini writes that Byrd encountered a humanoid being from another "world" who warned humanity to pursue peace and not war; he also reports that Byrd spotted a living
       <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">
        woolly mammoth
       </a>
       near the North Pole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       One major problem with Giannini's account is that in February 1947, Byrd was leading Operation Highjump in Antarctica and was, consequently, nowhere near the North Pole. Another problem is that in February the Arctic is in winter, and sunlight does not reach the North Pole, so it would have been impossible for Byrd to see something, like a woolly mammoth, from the air. Furthermore, Giannini quotes directly from the movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lost_Horizon_(1937_film)" title="Lost Horizon (1937 film)">
         Lost Horizon
        </a>
       </i>
       (1937) [29] in the dialog of the humanoid Byrd allegedly encountered. Some Hollow Earth theorists believe that
       <i>
        The Worlds Beyond the Poles
       </i>
       was published by "controllers" in order to discredit the Hollow Earth theory.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      A film of Operation Highjump, entitled "Highjump" by Christopher Brand, is currently in development.
