       <p>
        The promise oftheInformation Age is based ona unique partnershipbetween humans andmachines. Machines did the heavy lifting of transforming datainto information, which allowedhumans to then transforminformation into knowledge.Some humans still do routine data entry and management, but mostof thosejobs are now doneby machines. As thathappened,knowledge worker jobs emerged to handlethe resulting explosion of information.
       </p>
       <p>
        Joseph Schumpeterdescribed shifts like thisasa kind of 
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_destruction" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_destruction', 'creative destruction');" rel="nofollow">
         creative destruction
        </a>
        unleashed byinnovationcyclesof economicdestruction and creation, catalyzed by waves of new technology.We are now about to enter a new phase of creative destruction asmachines begin taking overthe work of transforming information into
        <em>
         knowledge
        </em>
        .
       </p>
       <h2>
        A Knowledge Hierarchy
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Wisdom-Pyramid.jpg">
         <img alt="Wisdom-Pyramid" class=" wp-image-6669 alignright" height="247" src="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Wisdom-Pyramid-300x209.jpg" width="354"/>
        </a>
        If youre ever boredand looking for some fun, try tellingaknowledge management expert that youve figured out the exact meaning of thisdiagram to the right.
       </p>
       <p>
        Its aknowledge hierarchy or
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DIKW_Pyramid" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DIKW_Pyramid', 'DIKW Pyramid');">
         DIKW Pyramid
        </a>
         (DIKW = Data, Information, Knowledge and Wisdom) and lots of people have lots of opinions about what it means.
       </p>
       <p>
        So, heres my takeon the D, I, K and W:
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Data:
         </strong>
         Internal or environmental signals
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Information:
         </strong>
         Data, filtered byimplicit or explicit intention
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Knowledge:
         </strong>
         Information, transformed into meaning
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Wisdom:
         </strong>
         Knowledge that has been experienced
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        The story I hope to paint here is ourrace with machines, a kind of competitive partnership as we scale this pyramid together. The real question is what emerges at the end.
       </p>
       <h2>
        EmbeddedKnowledge
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Embedded-Knowledge.jpg">
         <img alt="Embedded Knowledge" class="wp-image-6621 alignright" height="234" src="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Embedded-Knowledge.jpg" width="366"/>
        </a>
        Through most of our history, weve
        <em>
         embedded
        </em>
        our knowledgein our minds and bodies through various
        <a href="http://www.human-memory.net/types.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.human-memory.net/types.html', 'types of human memory');">
         types of human memory
        </a>
        . The invention of symbolsandwriting eventually gave us a way to embed that knowledge into something else. In a way, our history isa history of that 
        <em>
         something else
        </em>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Knowledge management experts like to divide knowledge into two categories: tacit knowledge and explicit knowledge.
        <em>
         Tacit
        </em>
        knowledge is experience-based knowledge  things we know, but dont really know how we know  like riding a bike, speaking a language or playing the guitar.
        <em>
         Explicit
        </em>
        knowledge is knowledge that is articulated. We embed explicit knowledge in written rules, procedures  and increasingly, in software.We embed tacit knowledge in memoryand explicit knowledge in information systems.
       </p>
       <pre style="color: #444444;">
        <div class="tm-tweet-clear">
        </div>
        <div class="tm-click-to-tweet">
         <div class="tm-ctt-text">

           We embed tacit knowledge in memory andexplicit knowledge in information systems.
          </a>
         </div>
         <a class="tm-ctt-btn" href="https://twitter.com/share?text=We+embed+tacit+knowledge+in+memory+and%C2%A0explicit+knowledge+in+information+systems.&amp;url=http://www.the-vital-edge.com/knowledge-and-artificial-intelligence/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://twitter.com/share?text=We+embed+tacit+knowledge+in+memory+and%C2%A0explicit+knowledge+in+information+systems.&amp;url=http://www.the-vital-edge.com/knowledge-and-artificial-intelligence/', 'Click To Tweet');" target="_blank">
          Click To Tweet
         </a>
         <div class="tm-ctt-tip">

       <p>
        Though the relationship between these two forms of knowledge is complex, the story of human civilization is an accelerating conversion ofimplicit knowledge into explicit knowledge.
       </p>
       <p>
        And were just getting started.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Artificial IntelligenceInformation Filtering
       </h2>
       <p>
        When it comes to developing knowledge, the first step is determining which signals have value and which are just noise. If wecan offloadthat pattern-recognizing work to machines, we take a big step towardsautomating knowledge creation.
       </p>
       <p>
        Until recently, our information systems for doing that kind of filteringhave been
        <em>
         deterministic,
        </em>
        which is to say, you takeaninput, applypre-determined rules coded by asoftware developer, and itspits out a pre-determined output.The system never knows more than the coders who coded it.
       </p>
       <p>
        Thats now changing. 
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_learning" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_learning', 'Deep Learning');" rel="nofollow">
         Deep Learning
        </a>
         is a branch of artificial intelligencethat tacklescomplex pattern recognition problems by breaking them into layers of
        <em>
         simpler
        </em>
        pattern recognition problems. Whatsstriking about recent Deep Learning breakthroughs is that theyre ableto learn on their own. Google recently used Deep Learning algorithmsto achieve an
        <a href="http://research.google.com/archive/unsupervised_icml2012.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://research.google.com/archive/unsupervised_icml2012.html', '82% accuracy in detecting faces among a set ofsome 37,000 images');">
         82% accuracy in detecting faces among a set ofsome 37,000 images
        </a>
        . That may not sound impressive until you realize that these techniques required notraining of the algorithm in advance. By simply exposing the Deep Learning softwareto enough data, an efficient face-recognizing neuron emerged without humans having to code it. Google ran similar tests forrecognizing human bodiesand even
        <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2012/06/26/technology/in-a-big-network-of-computers-evidence-of-machine-learning.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.nytimes.com/2012/06/26/technology/in-a-big-network-of-computers-evidence-of-machine-learning.html', 'cats on YouTube');">
         cats on YouTube
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://static.googleusercontent.com/media/research.google.com/en/us/archive/unsupervised_icml2012.pdf" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'download', 'http://static.googleusercontent.com/media/research.google.com/en/us/archive/unsupervised_icml2012.pdf');" rel="nofollow">
         <img alt="Cat in the Machine" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6638" height="250" src="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Cat-in-the-Machine.jpg" width="500"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        With Deep Learning,software developers dont have to know the answers before they code. The software trains itself. It
        <em>
         learns
        </em>
        .Andwhat its learning is how to automatically turn data into information withminimalhuman intervention.
       </p>
       <p>
        Ilike
        <a href="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/about-the-vital-edge/">
         digging beneath the surface of technology
        </a>
        , andfrom that place,you could say that the digital neurons oftodays Deep Learning software areroughly analogous to the
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensory_neuron" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensory_neuron', 'sensory neurons');">
         sensory neurons
        </a>
        that emerged in biology hundreds of millionsof years ago. I think theres no coincidence that todays sensor technologies arecreating an explosion of precisely the kind of data that Deep Learning is so well-suited to process, or that the first real practical applications of Deep Learning are inimage processingand speech recognition  digitalextensions of biologyseyes and ears.
       </p>
       <p>
        We are nowembedding our knowledge into a new digital container, and these automated information processing techniques are a vital step in that direction.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Automating Meaning Extraction
       </h2>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Thealgorithm didnt know the word cat  Ng had to supply that  but over time, it learned to identify the furry creatures we know as cats, all on its own.This approach is inspired by how scientists believe that humans learn. As babies, we watch our environments and start to understand the structure of objects we encounter, but until a parent tells us what it is, we cant put a name to it. 
         <a href="http://www.wired.com/2013/05/neuro-artificial-intelligence/all/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.wired.com/2013/05/neuro-artificial-intelligence/all/', 'Wired');">
          Wired
         </a>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Meaning-Making.jpg">
         <img alt="Meaning Making" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-6692" height="245" src="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Meaning-Making-300x245.jpg" width="300"/>
        </a>
        Googles algorithm could find cats, but couldntconnect the cat pattern to the word cat.
       </p>
       <p>
        Knowledgeis only part pattern recognition. We can learn to correctly spota smoke signal in the distance, but knowing what it
        <em>
         means
        </em>
        is what makes that pattern useful.
       </p>
       <p>
        When it comes totransforming information into knowledge, meaning-making is a critical function  and it just so happens that humans are really good at it.
       </p>
       <p>
        One way of thinking about meaning-making is connecting ideas to oneanother. I think of a camera and it kicks off a cascade of related ideas from shutter speed, to Android phones, and the Kodak Corporation.Thetechnology for making these associative connections has been around for a while. In 2001, web inventor, Tim Berners-Lee painted a compelling visionfor a newweb connectedthroughmeaning,which he called the 
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_Web" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_Web', 'semantic web');">
         semantic web
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        One of the legitimate criticismsof the semantic web is that implementing it requires too much expertise and work on the part of website publishers. Better, the thinking goes, to push this work off to machines.
       </p>
       <p>
        There are many approaches to automating meaning extractionon the web.Themost intriguing to me, because of itsaudacious scale and the fact that Google is behind it, is the
        <a href="http://www.google.com/insidesearch/features/search/knowledge.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.google.com/insidesearch/features/search/knowledge.html', 'Knowledge Graph');" rel="nofollow">
         Knowledge Graph
        </a>
        . Google originally seeded itsKnowledge Graph with data from a number of sources, including
        <a href="https://www.freebase.com/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.freebase.com/', 'Freebase');" rel="nofollow">
         Freebase
        </a>
        ,which itacquired in 2010, and which usedan interesting combination of automation and crowd-sourced human editing to build ahuge, and highly structured, database of knowledge. As of this writing, Freebase includes some 45 million topics.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="Jefferson Mapped" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-4830" height="165" src="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Jefferson-300x165.jpg" width="300"/>
        Overtime, Google has expanded the automation techniques used by Freebase in some interesting ways. Its beenexceptionally smart in
        <a href="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/shared-interest-graph-in-work/" title="The Interest Graph">
         usingpeoples interactions with Search and other services to augmentthe Knowledge Graph
        </a>
        . For example, when I search for Thomas Paine just after searching for Thomas Jefferson, and lots of others do the same, Google can infer a relationship between the two men, which isthen added to the Knowledge Graph. They also surface that useful information to users as other people searched for listings on the right-hand side of the search results.
       </p>
       <p>
        Now Google istaking this automationto a newlevel by automating the extraction of meaning, much the way their search botscrawl and extract data from websites today bycombining that information with
        <em>
         prior
        </em>
        knowledge stored in the Knowledge Graph. One of Googlesinternal projects for doing this is loosely termedthe Knowledge Vault (
        <a href="http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~nlao/publication/2014.kdd.pdf" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'download', 'http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~nlao/publication/2014.kdd.pdf');">
         detailed PDF research paper
        </a>
        ), and I believe it, or something like it, has the potential to be quite important.
       </p>
       <p>
        Google is now using these techniques to embed human meaning into agrowingweb of words and the meaning behind those words. Its important to bear in mind that the seeds of all of this knowledge (the entries in Freebase and the websites that Google crawls) were all human in origin. Google is just using some really smart techniques for extracting and processing this information in order to synthesize meaning out ofit.
       </p>
       <p>
        From the bigger perspective, youcould say that Google is using thesetechniquesto extract tacit knowledge from us and embed it as explicit knowledge into machines. Yes, machinescan find pictures of cats without our help, but when it comes to knowing that these cat patterns are actually cats andknowing what cats
        <em>
         mean
        </em>
        , well, they still need somehelp from humans.
       </p>
       <p>
        At least for now.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Creating a New Container: The Fusion of Knowledge and Artificial Intelligence
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Destructive-Creation.jpg">
         <img alt="Destructive Creation" class="size-medium wp-image-6684 alignright" height="184" src="http://www.the-vital-edge.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Destructive-Creation-300x184.jpg" width="300"/>
        </a>
        We are making good progress in automatingfilters to transform data into informationand inautomating the way we attach meaning to that information. These are important stepsto automating the transformation of humanitys
        <em>
         tacit
        </em>
        knowledge into
        <em>
         explicit
        </em>
        knowledge stored in machines. Soon we will be doingiton a staggering scale.
       </p>
       <p>
        There are many questions with regardto where this is all headed. Theres little question that thesenew tools will bring economic destruction for many lower-end knowledge workers. Still, where there is destruction, there is also creation. Just look at what IBM is doing with Watson Discovery Advisor, a service designed to help humans use the power of machine learning to uncover creativeinsights in science, healthcare, law and even gourmet cooking:
       </p>
       <p>
        <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/qry_zGZFjOc?rel=0" width="560">
        </iframe>
       </p>
       <p>
        I believe weare on a learning curve that is
        <em>
         of
        </em>
        our own creation but that is not exactly ours. What we are seeing today is the earths most intelligent species extracting knowledge from thebiology in which it is currently embedded and moving it into something else.
       </p>
       <div class="tm-tweet-clear">
       </div>
       <div class="tm-click-to-tweet">

          We are on a learning curve that is of our creation but that is not exactly ours.
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <a class="tm-ctt-btn" href="https://twitter.com/share?text=We+are+on+a+learning+curve+that+is+of+our+creation+but+that+is+not+exactly+ours.&amp;url=http://www.the-vital-edge.com/knowledge-and-artificial-intelligence/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://twitter.com/share?text=We+are+on+a+learning+curve+that+is+of+our+creation+but+that+is+not+exactly+ours.&amp;url=http://www.the-vital-edge.com/knowledge-and-artificial-intelligence/', 'Click To Tweet');" target="_blank">
          Click To Tweet

        </p>
        <div class="tm-ctt-tip">
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        That something else will eventually become the newest, most intelligent entities on the planet. We have no idea what theseentities will eventually be. We do not know how much humanity they will contain or whether they willeventually form a type of consciousness, let alone, what the
        <em>
         experience
        </em>
        of that consciousness might actually be.
       </p>
       <p>
        If my take on the knowledge pyramid is correct, and knowledge
        <em>
         does
        </em>
        have to be experienced in order to become wisdom, then let us hope that whatever arises next will be more than just data, information or knowledge. Let us hope that it will be capable of a conscious experience that leads to true wisdom.
       </p>
