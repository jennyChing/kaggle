            <h2 datatype="" property="dc:title">
             The no shadow Skyscraper
            </h2>
           </header>
           <div class="content">
            <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
             <div class="field-items">
              <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
               <p>
                Ever tried to get planning permission for a building project? Or perhaps your neighbour has gone through the process? Either way, youll probably be familiar with the concept of right to light. Basically, this means that as a homeowner, you can claim a legal right to a minimum amount of daylight entering your house. So if a new structure  a kitchen extension, say, or even a new, higher garden wall  casts a shadow over your property, this can have a negative impact on the light levels in your house.
               </p>
               <p>
                Considering that even a garden shed can cause shadow-related issues, imagine the planning permission nightmares caused by building a skyscraper. Unsurprisingly, super-tall buildings often face problems securing planning permission because of the impact they can have on the surrounding environment  think shadows that can stretch for half a mile or more at sunset.
               </p>
               <p>
                But thanks to the ingenuity of a London-based architecture firm, there may be a clever solution. NBBJ, a worldwide architectural practice which has designed buildings for Cambridge University, Microsoft, Google and Amazon, has come up with the designs for a pair of aligned towers with curved and angled facades which reflect light on to the ground below. The idea is that one of the towers would reflect sunlight into the shadow of its neighbouring tower, thereby reducing the total area of shade caused by both towers as a whole.
               </p>
               <p>
                NBBJ claim that the No Shadow Tower would redirect sunlight to visibly reduce shadows at the base of the towers by 60 per cent compared to typical buildings, providing more sunlight for pedestrians and local residents.
               </p>
               <p>
                The designs were produced for a site on the Greenwich Peninsula in south east London, near the O2 dome. In devising the buildings, NBBJ were careful to avoid repeating the mistakes that have been made in the past, which have seen skyscrapers impact on their surrounding environments.
               </p>
               <p>
                NBBJ design director Christian Coop said the project was about finding a way in which we can have the tall buildings we need without losing natural light on the areas below. The design ensures that the area between the towers is bright and pleasant, so is more likely to be used as a public space.
               </p>
               <p>
                So when it comes to building your own slightly-less-grand design, you might want to consider getting creative with the curvature, to ensure your neighbours see the bright side
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
