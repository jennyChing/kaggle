       <h3 class="cufon">
        How to Grow Violas
       </h3>

       <h4 align="justify">
        <b>
         An Interview with Laura and Jack Wildgoss of Wildegoose Nurseries
        </b>
       </h4>
       <p align="justify">
        <b>
         <font size="4">
          Andy
         </font>
         :
        </b>
        Laura and Jack Wildgoss grow a lot of violas at
        <b>
        </b>
        Wildegoose Nurseries in South Shropshire, UK. 180 different varieties in fact. They are all perennial types and range from some very old and precious heritage varieties to the latest, most exciting introductions. I asked Laura to tell us how to grow and enjoy them in our gardens.
        <b>
        </b>
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <b>
         <font size="4">
          Laura:
         </font>
        </b>
        Violas are plants with personality, which is what attracted us to them in the first place; their smiling faces and sunny disposition. The violas we grow can be loosely grouped into Viola cornutas and Viola hybrids.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <i>
         Viola cornuta
        </i>
        is a species that originates in the meadows of the Pyrenees. It has long stems to hold the purple, honey scented flowers high enough above the surrounding grasses to attract the attention of passing insects.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <a href="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/The-Bouts-Viola-Collection-1280x883.jpg">
         <img alt="Viola, cornuta, Grow, How to, Violas, Wildegoose Nursery, Jack Wildgoss,  Laura Wildgoss, Viola Roscastle Black , Viola Eastgrove Blue , " border="0" height="383" src="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/The-Bouts-Viola-Collection-1280x883_thumb.jpg" style="border-top: 0px; border-right: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom: 0px; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin: 0px auto; border-left: 0px; display: block; padding-right: 0px" title="Viola, cornuta, Grow, How to, Violas, Wildegoose Nursery, Jack Wildgoss,  Laura Wildgoss, Viola Roscastle Black , Viola Eastgrove Blue , " width="554"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        The flowers of
        <i>
         Viola cornuta
        </i>
        have characteristically narrow elongated petals that are so delicate, yet these are remarkably hardy robust little plants.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        They are often recommended for ground cover, as they happily spread to make large flowering clumps under shrubs, notably roses, with which they combine extremely well.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        Viola hybrids, as the name suggests, is something of a mixed bag and is the category into which the rest of our large collection is grouped.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <a href="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Viola-Fiona-Lawrenson-1280x1280.jpg">
         <img alt="Viola Fiona Lawrenson, a perennial viola." border="0" height="504" src="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Viola-Fiona-Lawrenson-1280x1280_thumb.jpg" style="border-top: 0px; border-right: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom: 0px; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin: 0px auto; border-left: 0px; display: block; padding-right: 0px" title="Viola Fiona Lawrenson, a perennial viola." width="504"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        Their parentage is somewhat diverse and they can be striped and splashed, bicoloured or the image of simplicity itself in pure hues of white, yellow, pink, mauve, purple and black.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        Many are scented too. The most common scent is of honey, but some varieties are different, such as
        <i>
         Viola
        </i>
        Alice Kate with sherbet yellow flowers and a scent reminiscent of citrus and cloves, another lovely variety is
        <i>
         Viola
        </i>
        Eris with frilly creamy white flowers and she has the scent of delicious vanilla custard!
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        There can be few plants that can match the length of the flowering season of violas. Many of the viola hybrids start flowering in April (May for the cornutas) and combine beautifully with spring bulbs such as
        <i>
         Muscari
        </i>
        Valerie Finnis and
        <i>
         Narcissus triandus
        </i>
        Hawera.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <a href="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/mix-of-violas-planted-in-an-old-wire-basket-1280x960.jpg">
         <img alt="mix of violas planted in an old wire basket (1280x960)" border="0" height="416" src="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/mix-of-violas-planted-in-an-old-wire-basket-1280x960_thumb.jpg" style="border-top: 0px; border-right: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom: 0px; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin: 0px auto; border-left: 0px; display: block; padding-right: 0px" title="mix of violas planted in an old wire basket (1280x960)" width="554"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        They then continue to flower throughout the summer and well into September. Violas are remarkably versatile and suit a range of garden situations.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        They look lovely in pots, whether potted as a collection of individual varieties (as you might do with auriculas), or mixed together. You can also combine them with other plants in mixed containers.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        We like planting them in an old vintage container with culinary herbs or salads, as viola flowers are not only ornamental but edible too and can be used to decorate salads and cakes.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <a href="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Viola-Jennifer-Andrews-960x1280.jpg">
         <img alt="Viola 'Jennifer Andrews' (960x1280)" border="0" height="671" src="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Viola-Jennifer-Andrews-960x1280_thumb.jpg" style="border-top: 0px; border-right: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom: 0px; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin: 0px auto; border-left: 0px; display: block; padding-right: 0px" title="Viola 'Jennifer Andrews' (960x1280)" width="504"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        In the garden the more vigorous varieties, notably many of the cornutas and some of the hybrids such as
        <i>
         Viola
        </i>
        Ivory Queen ,
        <i>
         Viola
        </i>
        Roscastle Black and
        <i>
         Viola
        </i>
        Eastgrove Blue are good for under planting shrubs, but they are also lovely subjects for the front of the border, scrambling through their taller flowering neighbours.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        They combine well with many traditional cottage garden favourites such as geraniums, potentillas and astrantias.
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        We have also found them to suit more contemporary naturalistic styles of planting, particularly the cornutas, with their more delicate flowers, combining well with shorter grasses such as
        <i>
         Stipa tennuissima
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         Hordeum jubatum
        </i>
        .
       </p>
       <p align="justify">
        <a href="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Auricula-Style-display-of-Violas-1280x960.jpg">
         <img alt="Auricula Style display of Violas (1280x960)" border="0" height="416" src="http://www.my-garden-school.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Auricula-Style-display-of-Violas-1280x960_thumb.jpg" style="border-top: 0px; border-right: 0px; background-image: none; border-bottom: 0px; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin: 0px auto; border-left: 0px; display: block; padding-right: 0px" title="Auricula Style display of Violas (1280x960)" width="554"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <font color="#249fea">
            <font size="3">
             Violas are remarkably generous plants and easy perennials to grow, if you follow a few golden rules:
            </font>
           </font>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
           They enjoy part shade through to full sun, but will not tolerate deep shade.
         </p>
         <p>
           They like good, humus-rich garden soil. They are happy on clay, if garden compost has been incorporated to break it up, and on sandy soil if garden compost has been incorporated to improve moisture retention and nutrients.
         </p>
         <p>
           If growing in pots use a good quality general purpose growing medium and incorporate grit or perlite to aid drainage through winter. You can also add slow release fertiliser to the compost, or give them a liquid feed every two to three weeks to give them a boost. Feed first with a balanced feed for healthy plant growth and once established switch to a tomato feed to encourage more flowers.
         </p>
         <p>
           If growing in pots make sure they are deep pots. All our stock pots are made up of 3 plants potted up into one 10 litre pot; that is approximately 30cm (1ft) wide and deep. We find the plants knit together well and make a large flowering clump. You can grow them individually in smaller pots for the summer if you want to create an auricula theatre type of effect, but pot your violas on into something deeper by late summer or early autumn, as their vigorous root systems will appreciate more space and a larger pot will help insulate them from winter cold.
         </p>
         <p>
           Deadhead and keep deadheading. Some people find deadheading very therapeutic. I cant say I do, but then there are 180 varieties of viola to get through in our collection. The rewards are however great and your violas will keep compact for longer and keep flowering all summer long.
         </p>
         <p>
           If your violas become leggy, and you dont want to do all that deadheading, you can undertake a mid-season chop. Usually in mid-July we take the garden shears to our violas and simply cut straight across the top leaving 5cm (2) of growth. Give them a balanced liquid feed and in 4 weeks they should begin to shoot again and come into flower.
         </p>
         <p>
           Check for slugs and aphids hiding deep in the crowns of your viola plants. A drench of nematodes can control the slug damage; a spray with a soap based organic pesticide should keep the sap suckers at bay, otherwise there are non-organic chemical alternatives to these.
         </p>
         <p>
           Winter maintenance. After a season of flowering their socks off put your violas to bed for the winter by once again shearing across the top in late September and leaving a couple of inches growth as protection. Next years young shoots will emerge from the base of the plant. Check for pests and diseases from time to time and treat accordingly.
         </p>
         <p>
          Following this advice you should have a glorious show of flowers and scent from mid spring to late summer, for your pots, or your borders, or even decorating your salad bowl. What garden can afford to be without them?
         </p>
         <p>
          For more information check out Jack and Lauras website
          <a href="http://www.boutsviolas.co.uk/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           http://www.boutsviolas.co.uk/
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Follow Wildegoose Nurseries on twitter: @wgviolas
         </p>
        </li>
