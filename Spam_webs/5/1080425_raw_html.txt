           <p>
            June 14 (Bloomberg) -- The wealthiest Democrat in U.S.
House leadership held her title while the top Republican leader,
Eric Cantor, will lose his at the end of July.
           </p>
           <p>
            House Democratic leader Nancy Pelosi and her husband, Paul,
have $42.4 million to $199.5 million in assets. That includes
commercial property in San Francisco, stock in Apple Inc. and
Visa Inc., an interest in the Auberge du Soleil resort in
Rutherford, California, and an ownership stake in the defunct
United Football League.
           </p>
           <p>
            That fortune makes her once again the wealthiest member of
the leadership in the U.S. House of Representatives, according
to financial-disclosure reports released yesterday. The Pelosis
disclosed $6.75 million to $31.5 million in liabilities against
those assets.
           </p>
           <div data-view-uid="1|0_5_1_5">
            <div class="ad-v2 on-mobile" data-position="box1" id="1|0_5_1_5">
             <script type="text/javascript">

           <p>
            Lawmakers are required to disclose most assets and
liabilities annually and make periodic disclosures after
purchasing or selling stocks. The reports cover 2013.
           </p>
           <p>
            House Speaker John Boehner, an Ohio Republican, reported
assets valued between $2.3 million and $6.7 million, an increase
from 2012 when he reported holdings between $1.8 million and
$5.4 million.
           </p>
           <p>
            Most of Boehners holdings are in widely held funds and
individual retirement accounts. He holds a pension from the
state of Ohio valued at between $50,001 and $100,000.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its difficult to tell exactly how wealthy individual
lawmakers are, and the variation increases as members gain more
wealth. Financial-disclosure forms filed by members of Congress
require lawmakers to state the value of holdings in broad
ranges. Precise figures arent made public.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Republican Leaders
           </h2>
           <p>
            House Republicans soon will lose the wealthiest member of
their leadership team.
           </p>
           <p>
            Majority Leader Cantor, who lost his primary June 10 in
Virginia, has a net worth of $6.2 million to $20 million. He
will resign from his leadership position July 31, he said this
week.
           </p>
           <p>
            The largest assets held by Cantor and his wife, Diana,
include more than $1 million in stock in Dominos Pizza Inc. and
more than $500,000 in deferred stock of Media General Inc.,
which owns two Virginia television stations. Diana Cantor is a
director of both companies.
           </p>
           <h2>
            McCarthys Assets
           </h2>
           <p>
            California Republican Kevin McCarthy, the leading candidate
to take over the partys No. 2 leadership spot in the House,
reported between $76,000 and $266,000 in assets, mostly in
mutual funds managed by California-based Capital Research &amp;
Management. The single liability for the former deli owner is a
mortgage valued between $100,000 and $250,000 on his home in
Bakersfield, California.
           </p>
           <p>
            The median value of McCarthys assets -- $171,000 -- has
dropped from $299,500 since 2007, his first year in office, when
he held a mix of mutual funds and stocks.
           </p>
           <p>
            Idaho Republican Raul Labrador announced yesterday that
hell challenge McCarthy for majority leader.
           </p>
           <p>
            Outside of his home, Labrador has few assets. He has $5,000
to $75,000 in retirement accounts and up to $15,000 in a bank
account.
           </p>
           <p>
            Labrador owes $45,000 to $150,000 in student loans and
lines of credit. He also owes $265,000 to $550,000 on his home
in Eagle, Idaho.
           </p>
           <p>
            If McCarthy wins, three Republicans will vie to replace him
as majority whip, the third-ranking leadership job, which
requires persuading party members to back legislation.
           </p>
           <p>
            Peter Roskam of Illinois, the chief deputy whip, listed
assets valued at almost $350,000 to $1.5 million, mostly from
bonds and exchange-traded funds.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Study Committee
           </h2>
           <p>
            Steve Scalise of Louisiana, currently chairman of the
Republican Study Committee, reported a negative net worth. He
holds assets between $4,000 and $60,000, offset by mortgage
debt. Scalise also reported a personal loan of $15,000 to
$50,000 and a revolving charge account of $10,000 to $15,000.
           </p>
           <p>
            Assets for the third majority whip contender, Marlin
Stutzman, werent immediately available. The Indiana Republican
requested an extension to file by Aug. 13.
           </p>
           <p>
            At a time when Democrats are trying to force a debate over
income inequality, the leaders wealth means they are rarely
personally affected by paycheck concerns, such as the need to
pay off student loans.
           </p>
           <p>
            While student loan debt surpassed $1.2 trillion, only one
current top House leader, Cathy McMorris Rodgers, reported she
had any. Labrador, if elected majority leader, would be the
second.
           </p>
           <p>
            Senate lawmakers this week failed to advance legislation
that would have allowed students to refinance student loan debt.
Someone who took out an undergraduate Stafford loan in the
2011-2012 year at a 6.8 percent interest rate could have
refinanced at the 2013-2014 rate of 3.86 percent.
           </p>
           <p>
            The measure drew Republican opposition in part because it
would be paid for with new taxes on wealthy individuals. The
Republican-led House hasnt taken up similar legislation.
           </p>
