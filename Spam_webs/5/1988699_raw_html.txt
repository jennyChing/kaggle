        <p>
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/solr_logo.png">
          <img alt="solr_logo" class="wp-image-14230 alignright" height="283" src="http://blog.smartbear.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/solr_logo.png" width="288"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          There are many open source programs that get a lot of press. And then there a few, like Solr, that are important to businesses but are also virtually unknown, even in geek circles.
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         When I did a story recently about
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/open-source/yes-open-source-jobs-are-hot-and-we-have-stats-to-prove-it/" target="_blank" title="Yes, Open Source Jobs are Hot and We Have the Stats to Prove it">
          in-demand open source jobs
         </a>
         , I wasnt surprised to hear from
         <a href="http://www.dice.com/" target="_blank" title="Dice">
          Dice
         </a>
         that the job market was hot, hot, hot for OpenStack (for
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/open-source/drilling-into-big-data-with-apache-drill/" target="_blank" title="Drilling Into Big Data with Apache Drill">
          Big Data
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://hadoop.apache.org/" target="_blank" title="Hadoop">
          Hadoop
         </a>
         in particular) and for the LAMP stack (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP/Perl/Python). What did surprise meindeed, shocked mewas that another red-hot tech jobs area was
         <a href="http://lucene.apache.org/solr/" target="_blank" title="Apache Solr">
          Solr
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Solr? I wondered. What the heck is Solr? That was also the reaction of all of my developer friends. And, since my buddies and I, among the lot of us, have centuries in the tech business, weve seen a lot of programs. That Oh, did you say
         <i>
          money
         </i>
         ? item set me to do my research. Now Ill let you into the secrets of Solr.
        </p>
        <p>
         One reason why Solr may not have gained the attention it deserves is its actually apart of another larger and much better known open source project, Apache Lucene. This, as Im sure you know, is a Java-based text search engine library.
        </p>
        <p>
         Lucene is used by many companies and groups as
         <a href="http://wiki.apache.org/lucene-java/PoweredBy" target="_blank" title="Lucene Java wiki">
          the foundation for their search engines
         </a>
         . These organizations include AOL, Disney, and
         <a href="http://www.eclipse.org/" target="_blank" title="Eclipse">
          Eclipse
         </a>
         . Lucenes chief selling point is that the indexing engine, with a footprint of a mere megabyte of RAM, can
         <a href="http://people.apache.org/~mikemccand/lucenebench/indexing.html" target="_blank" title="Indexing Throughput">
          index up to 150GBs per hour of text
         </a>
         on commercial off-the-shelf hardware. Thats darn good!
        </p>
        <p>
         Solr comes into the picture as the search platform front-end for Lucene. It provides full-text search, including the ability to handle such formats as Microsoft Word and PDF with Apache
         <a href="http://tika.apache.org/" target="_blank" title="Apache Tika">
          Tika
         </a>
         ; hit test highlighting; and
         <a href="http://www.uie.com/articles/faceted_search/" target="_blank" title="Designing for Faceted Search">
          faceted search
         </a>
         , which incorporates free text searching with topic taxonomy indexing.
        </p>
        <p>
         Like Lucene, Solr is very popular (even if I didnt know about it before now). Its used by sites such as Reddit, Netflix, and Instagram. These are all websites whose users wont stand for slow response time. Solr can deliver the kind of performance that cranky users demand.
        </p>
        <p>
         Under the hood, Solr is written in Java and it relies on Lucene for its core functionality. It usually runs within a servlet container such as the
         <a href="http://www.eclipse.org/jetty/" target="_blank" title="Eclipse - Jetty">
          Jetty
         </a>
         HTTP server and
         <a href="http://docs.oracle.com/javaee/5/api/javax/servlet/package-summary.html" target="_blank" title="Package javax.servlet">
          Javax.servlet
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Solr has REST-like HTTP/XML and JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) APIs for ease of programming from almost any language. So, while you can work with Solr using its native Java, you also can use your language of choice. For example, query results can be returned in XML/XSLT, JSON, Python, Ruby, PHP, Velocity, CSV, or binary formats. You can use this data with whatever package strikes your fancy.
        </p>
        <p>
         While Solr is built on Lucene, it also expands upon it. For instance, it supports
         <a href="http://www.codefutures.com/database-sharding/" target="_blank" title="codeFutures database sharing">
          sharded data
         </a>
         , geospatial search, and user extensible caching. The end-result is a very fast and flexible back-end DBMS for almost any Web search engine job.
        </p>
        <p>
         With its
         <a href="http://wiki.apache.org/solr/" target="_blank" title="Apache Solr Wiki">
          exhaustive documentation
         </a>
         , the program promises to make it easy to get up to speed. As for administrators, with an AJAX-based administration interface and
         <a href="http://wiki.apache.org/solr/SolrLogging" target="_blank" title="Solr's Logging Mechanism">
          comprehensive logging facilities
         </a>
         , Solr is simple to manage.
        </p>
        <p>
         While Solr is clearly useful and easy, clearly not enough Solr experts are out there. According to Dice on September 11th, 2013, there were no fewer than 318 Solr jobs listed. Many of these job listings had a phrase like, Solr experience is a must. If youre interested in pursuing this in-demand open source job skill, big data experience is a real plus. In particular,
         <a href="http://hadoop.apache.org/" target="_blank" title="Hadoop Apache">
          Hadoop
         </a>
         and its close relative,
         <a href="http://hbase.apache.org/" target="_blank" title="Hbase Apache">
          Hbase
         </a>
         , were frequently mentioned. And, of course, if you can do all this on a cloud architecture, thats a real plus.
        </p>
        <p>
         So, in short, if youd like a programming job sooner rather than later, dont be like me and my buddies. Learn about Solr today so
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/software-quality/bid/221181/Leveraging-Open-Source-Experience-in-Your-Job-Hunt" target="_blank" title="Leveraging Open Source Experience in Your Job Hunt">
          your resume will look better tomorrow
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          How about you? Have you heard good things about Solr, or just nothing at all? Do you have Solr experience, and does it help you in your career? Let us know in the comments.
         </em>
        </p>
