             <h1 class="entry-title">
              Quantum Keys (to the Future of the Internet)  Part 1
             </h1>
             <div class="post-info">
              <span class="date published time" title="2014-05-01T09:30:33+00:00">
               May 1, 2014
              </span>
              <span class="author vcard">
               <span class="fn">
                <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/security/author/konstantinoskaragianni/" rel="author">
                 Konstantinos Karagiannis
                </a>
               </span>
              </span>
              <span class="post-comments">
               <a href="https://letstalk.globalservices.bt.com/en/security/2014/05/quantum-keys-future-internet-part-1/#comments">
                <span class="number">
                 2
                </span>
                s
               </a>
              </span>
             </div>
             <div class="entry-content">
              <p>
               <strong>
                <span style="line-height: 1.5em">
                 by Konstantinos Karagiannis,
                </span>
                BT Director, Ethical Hacking.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Think back to any major breach or security bug in the past decade. All began with a flaw in a companys systems or in a product that went undetectedall ended with users freaking out en masse to change passwords or get new credit cards. No matter the scope of these incidents, the panic only seemed global. It was never really everyone affected.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               But what if suddenly all of the worlds data communications, even encrypted ones, were at risk? The arrival of Quantum Computing will, for a while, make this a reality.
               <span id="more-4262">
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               Despite the best efforts of those of us researching this field, Quantum Computing is still misunderstood by the masses. Most think QCs will be basically one new type of machine that can do the work of a thousand classical ones. Actually, QCs will make possible what couldnt be done by classical computers given millions of years. Yes, millions. As long as the computing feat is one for which a quantum algorithm exists.
              </p>
              <p>
               Shors Algorithm is the most notable here, as it will allow a QC with enough quantum bits (qubits) to factor large prime numbers in seconds. Even 4096-bit PK keys will be crackable in minutes or hours, tops. This is clearly going to be the first use of QCs, much like the first supercomputer Colossus was built to crack encryption in WWII. Forget about worrying if you need to change a password at a particular group of web sites. Everything you send over the wire will be readable by the entity that gets a quantum computer first.
              </p>
              <p>
               Governments around the world are already gathering encrypted streams in the hopes that the information may still be useful if cracked in the future. The Washington Post only reported a few months ago that the NSA is not exactly hiding their budget for building a Quantum Computer to crack encryption.
              </p>
              <p>
               Is the Internet doomed? No, yet it will have to evolve. We will need to take advantage of quantum weirdness and build machines that exploit it to protect future sensitive data that goes out into the world. May 24
               <sup>
                th
               </sup>
               may be one step in this evolution.
               <a href="http://www.btplc.com/News/Articles/ShowArticle.cfm?ArticleID=05C86FD3-A148-443C-AF60-00F3C6E60B53" target="_blank">
                BT announced
               </a>
               that it worked with Toshiba, ADVA, and the NPL to do something quite amazing.
              </p>
              <p>
               Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) allows us to send a new type of security key with a quantum state over a network. Any attempt to intercept these photons will destroy the quantum state, thereby alerting machines on both ends that a new key is required. BTs Adastral Park facility was able to transmit this type of photon packet over a live, noisy fiber network. Unlike past lab experiments, this real-world test allowed teams to communicate quantum information amidst a sea of other types of data. Were at the brink of sending unlimited amounts of quantum information around the world within existing fiber networks.
              </p>
              <p>
               Next time well look at what this and other quantum techniques mean for the future of encryption.
              </p>
