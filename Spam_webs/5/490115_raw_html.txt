     <p>
      <b>
       Fred Holland Day
      </b>
      (Boston July 8, 1864 - November 12, 1933) was an American photographer and publisher. He was the first in the U.S.A. to advocate that photography should be considered a fine art.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=F._Holland_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life">
       Life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=F._Holland_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Day was the son of a Boston merchant, and was a man of independent means for all his life.
     </p>
     <p>
      Day's life and works had long been controversial, since his photographic subjects were often nude male youths.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pam_Roberts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pam Roberts (page does not exist)">
       Pam Roberts
      </a>
      , in
      <i>
       F. Holland Day
      </i>
      (Waanders Pub, 2001; catalog of a Day exhibition at the
      <a href="/wiki/Van_Gogh_Museum" title="Van Gogh Museum">
       Van Gogh Museum
      </a>
      ) writes: "Day never married and his sexual orientation, whilst it is widely assumed that he was homosexual, because of his interests, his photographic subject matter, his general flamboyant demeanor, was, like much else about him, a very private matter."
     </p>
     <p>
      Day spent much time among poor immigrant children in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">
       Boston
      </a>
      , tutoring them in reading and mentoring them. One in particular, the 13-year-old Lebanese immigrant
      <a href="/wiki/Kahlil_Gibran" title="Kahlil Gibran">
       Kahlil Gibran
      </a>
      , went on to fame as the author of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Prophet_(book)" title="The Prophet (book)">
        The Prophet
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Day co-founded and self-financed the publishing firm of Copeland and Day, which from 1893 through 1899 published about a hundred titles. The firm was influenced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">
       Arts and Crafts movement
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">
       William Morris
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelmscott_Press" title="Kelmscott Press">
       Kelmscott Press
      </a>
      . The firm was the American publisher of
      <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">
       Oscar Wilde
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salom%C3%A9_(play)" title="Salom (play)">
        Salom
       </a>
      </i>
      , illustrated by
      <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Beardsley" title="Aubrey Beardsley">
       Aubrey Beardsley
      </a>
      ;
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Yellow_Book" title="The Yellow Book">
        The Yellow Book
       </a>
      </i>
      periodical, also illustrated by Beardsley; and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Riders_and_Other_Lines" title="The Black Riders and Other Lines">
        The Black Riders and Other Lines
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Crane" title="Stephen Crane">
       Stephen Crane
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      He is known to have traveled. Beaumont Newhall states that he visited
      <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">
       Algiers
      </a>
      , possibly as a result of reading Wilde and Gide. There is a photo "Portrait of F. Holland Day in Arab Costume, 1901" by
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_H._Evans" title="Frederick H. Evans">
       Frederick H. Evans
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      He was a friend of
      <a href="/wiki/Louise_Imogen_Guiney" title="Louise Imogen Guiney">
       Louise Imogen Guiney
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Adams_Cram" title="Ralph Adams Cram">
       Ralph Adams Cram
      </a>
      . He was a major patron of
      <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Beardsley" title="Aubrey Beardsley">
       Aubrey Beardsley
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      He was also a lifelong bibliophile and collector. Most notable among his collections was his world-class collection on the poet
      <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">
       John Keats
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Work">
       Work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=F._Holland_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      At the turn of the century, his influence and reputation as a photographer rivaled that of
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz" title="Alfred Stieglitz">
       Alfred Stieglitz
      </a>
      , who later eclipsed him. The high point of Day's photographic career was probably his organization of an exhibition of photographs at the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Photographic_Society" title="Royal Photographic Society">
       Royal Photographic Society
      </a>
      in 1900.
      <i>
       New School of American Photography
      </i>
      presented 375 photographs by 42 photographers, 103 of them by Day, and evoked both high praise and vitriolic scorn from critics. The popularist "Photographic News" saw it as the result... "of a diseased imagination, of which much has been fostered by the ravings of a few lunatics... unacademic ...and eccentric".
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Day,_Fred_Holland_(1864-1933)_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_(detail)_-_Selfportrait.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="350" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Day%2C_Fred_Holland_%281864-1933%29_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_%28detail%29_-_Selfportrait.jpg/200px-Day%2C_Fred_Holland_%281864-1933%29_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_%28detail%29_-_Selfportrait.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Day%2C_Fred_Holland_%281864-1933%29_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_%28detail%29_-_Selfportrait.jpg/300px-Day%2C_Fred_Holland_%281864-1933%29_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_%28detail%29_-_Selfportrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/Day%2C_Fred_Holland_%281864-1933%29_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_%28detail%29_-_Selfportrait.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Day,_Fred_Holland_(1864-1933)_-_Seven_last_words_of_Christ_(detail)_-_Selfportrait.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         The Seven Last Words,
        </i>
        by F. Holland Day
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Day belonged to the
      <a href="/wiki/Pictorialism" title="Pictorialism">
       pictorialist
      </a>
      movement which regarded photography as a fine art and which often included
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolist" title="Symbolist">
       symbolist
      </a>
      imagery. The
      <a href="/wiki/Photo-Secession" title="Photo-Secession">
       Photo-Secessionists
      </a>
      invited him to join, but he declined the offer. As was common at the time, his photographs allude to
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">
       classical antiquity
      </a>
      in manner, composition and often in theme. From 1896 through 1898 Day experimented with Christian themes, using himself as a model for Jesus. Neighbors in
      <a href="/wiki/Norwood,_Massachusetts" title="Norwood, Massachusetts">
       Norwood, Massachusetts
      </a>
      assisted him in an outdoor photographic
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Staged_photography&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Staged photography (page does not exist)">
       staged photography
      </a>
      re-enactment of the crucifixion of Jesus. This culminated in his series of self-photographs,
      <i>
       The Seven Last Words,
      </i>
      depicting the seven last words of Christ.
     </p>
     <p>
      He often made only a single print from a negative. He used only the
      <a href="/wiki/Platinum_print" title="Platinum print">
       platinum process
      </a>
      , being unsatisfied with any other, and lost interest in photography when platinum became unobtainable following the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">
       Russian Revolution
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=F._Holland_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Day became all but forgotten for a number of reasons. He was eclipsed by his rival, Stieglitz. The pictorial and symbolist photographic style went out of fashion in the face of the radical shift towards early
      <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">
       modernism
      </a>
      in the art world. Two thousand of his prints and negatives were lost in a 1904 fire. The few hundred that survived were sent to the Royal Photographic Society in the 1930s.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the 1990s Day's works have been included in major exhibitions by museum curators, notably in the solo Day retrospective at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Museum_of_Fine_Arts" title="Boston Museum of Fine Arts">
       Boston Museum of Fine Arts
      </a>
      in 2000/2001 and similar shows at the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Photographic_Society" title="Royal Photographic Society">
       Royal Photographic Society
      </a>
      in England and the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fuller_Museum_of_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fuller Museum of Art (page does not exist)">
       Fuller Museum of Art
      </a>
      . Art historians are once again taking an interest in Day, and there are now significant academic texts on Day's homoerotic portraiture, and its similarities to the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Pater" title="Walter Pater">
       Walter Pater
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Eakins" title="Thomas Eakins">
       Thomas Eakins
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Day's house at 93 Day Street, Norwood, Massachusetts is now a museum (The F. Holland Day House &amp; Norwood History Museum), and the headquarters of the Norwood Historical Society.
     </p>
