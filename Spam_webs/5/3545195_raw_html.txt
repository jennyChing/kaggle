         <p>
          "It's not the kind of book you read before bed," said the Montgomery man. "It's very detailed, very graphic."
         </p>
         <p>
          Recently released to coincide with Mother's Day and the 70
          <sup>
           t
          </sup>
          <sup>
           h
          </sup>
          anniversary of the liberation of Mauthausen, this book is about three mothers who gave birth in a Nazi concentration camp. It's official launch in North America will take place May 19 at the Illinois Holocaust Museum and Education Center in Skokie, where those "Miracle Babies"  now retired with children and grandkids  will be special guests.
         </p>

         <p>
          But Petersohn already had received an advanced copy because his father was such an integral part of this remarkable tale, and is even credited with directly saving one of the infants.
         </p>
         <p>
          LeRoy "Pete" Petersohn, a medic with Patton's Third Army, was among the first of the liberators inside Mauthausen, one of the Nazis' most brutal and deadly concentration camps near Linz, Austria.
         </p>
         <p>
          It was there that the 22-year-old soldier from Aurora witnessed decaying and skeletal bodies stacked against walls; watched some emaciated prisoners drop to the ground and die before his eyes; and felt for himself the searing heat from the recently used crematorium furnaces.
         </p>

         </div>
         <p>
          It was also where he helped deliver a few of the seven babies born during that liberation. And it's where he came upon a 3-week-old infant whose mother had been among the women who'd hid their pregnancies from "Angel of Death" Dr. Josef Mengele in Auschwitz, then were starved and forced to give birth inside or en route to slave labor camps.
         </p>
         <p>
          The baby girl, her badly infected body covered by seeping boils, appeared too far gone to save when the liberators arrived. Petersohn, however, convinced his commander to let him treat her anyway, and he spent an entire day carefully lancing and cleaning her wounds.
         </p>
         <p>
          In 2005, that baby  Hana Berger Moran, now a retired research scientist  and Pete Petersohn were reunited at Mauthausen during the 60th anniversary of its liberation.
         </p>
         <div class="trb_gptAd trb_articleAd_bottomBanner" data-adloader-adtype="cube" data-adloader-label="" data-adloader-position="pageBottomCube" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" id="">
         </div>
         <p>
          Petersohn's remarkable story has been well chronicled, not only in this newspaper but in other books, including Michael Hirsh's "The Liberators: America's Witness to the Holocaust;" and Tricia Goyer's "Remembering You," a fictional novel loosely-based on the Aurora medic's war experience.
         </p>
         <p>
          He's not around to read this newest book, written by internationally acclaimed author Wendy Holden. Pete died from brain cancer at age 87 in June of 2010  but not before he and Moran met a couple more times, including a visit she made to his Montgomery home a few months before he passed.
         </p>
         <p>
          Another baby featured in "Born Survivors," Mark Olsky, an emergency room doctor from Madison, Wisconsin, who was born in an open coal wagon on the way to Mauthausen, also came to see Pete a couple weeks before his death. There, he too spent hours looking through an elaborate scrapbook the old soldier had put together of his war experiences that included photos the medic snapped of the horrors he encountered inside the death camp.
         </p>
         <p>
          His family, Brian added, was interviewed extensively for this latest book and have been invited to attend the Skokie event, where the author and all three "Miracle Babies" will be guests.
         </p>
         <p>
          It's no surprise "Born Survivors" is gathering excellent reviews: I'm anxious to get my hands on a copy, myself. But it won't be the last time Pete's story will be told. Julie Rosenberg, Hana's daughter in-law, left her job as a BBC journalist in New York to write another account, this one, centering on how fate brought the American soldier and dying baby together at that remarkable time in history.
         </p>
         <p>
          That book will be titled, "What Happened to that Baby," a question Brian Petersohn said his father would always ask after sharing his story with others.
         </p>
         <p>
          Recalling the horrific sights he encountered upon entering Mauthausen was not easy for Pete, who retired after 44 years working as a printer with the Beacon-News. In my several interviews with him over the years, this quiet, gentle man would break down sobbing when recounting those long-ago scenes, and he admitted he struggled emotionally when the war was over.
         </p>
         <p>
          Brian noted that his dad, who was also injured soon after arriving in Europe at the Battle of the Bulge, sought counseling upon his return to the United States at Hines Veteran's Hospital for what we all know now as post traumatic stress disorder. His father opened up somewhat when Brian got into middle school "and I began asking Dad questions." Then later, when Pete's grandsons were in high school, he'd share more of the horrors he witnessed when invited to speak to classes.
         </p>
         <p>
          In addition to the Purple Heart and other war-time distinctions, Pete received the Golden Badge of Honor from the Austrian government and the prestigious Shofar of Freedom award from Temple Israel of Albany, New York. While he appreciated those honors, it was apparent nothing meant more than knowing those babies not only survived their horrendous starts but went on to lead happy and productive lives.
         </p>
         <p>
          And what would he say about his pivotal role in a critically acclaimed book being released worldwide?
         </p>
         <p>
          "Dad was extremely proud that Hana feels part of our family and still refers to him as "Daddy Pete," said Brian Petersohn. "He would be very emotional yet honored ..."
         </p>
