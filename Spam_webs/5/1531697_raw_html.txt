       <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
        Weigh the return': Publishers chime in on controversial Facebook ploy
       </h1>
       <p class="entry-meta">
        <time class="entry-time" datetime="2015-03-26T12:28:51+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
         March 26, 2015
        </time>
        By
        <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
         <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://webnesday.com/author/digiday/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
          <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
           digiday
          </span>
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="entry-comments-link">
         <a href="http://webnesday.com/weigh-the-return-publishers-chime-in-on-controversial-facebook-ploy/#respond">
          Leave a Comment
         </a>
        </span>
       </p>
      </header>
      <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
       <p>
        The
        <a class=" autoblog-load-by-popup thickbox" href="http://digiday.com/publishers/case-publishing-directly-facebook/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://digiday.com/publishers/case-publishing-directly-facebook/', 'controversial news');">
         controversial news
        </a>
        that publishers including The New York Times and BuzzFeed may publish straight into Facebooks ecosystem was one of several hot topics at
        <a class=" autoblog-load-by-popup thickbox" href="http://digiday.com/publishers/watch-digiday-publishing-summit-live/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://digiday.com/publishers/watch-digiday-publishing-summit-live/', 'Digidays Publishing Summit');">
         Digidays Publishing Summit
        </a>
        in Vail, Colorado, where hundreds of digital publishing executives are gathering this week to hash out the issues facing their industrys future.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Facebook scheme raises some uncomfortable questions about what publishers are ready to give up in revenue and reader data to get Facebooks referral traffic. Everyone has an opinion about the subject, so Digiday rounded up some publishing execs at the summit and asked them a simple question: Should publishers posttheir content directly to Facebook? Heres what they told us:
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Paul Marcum, global head of digital video, Bloomberg
         <br/>
        </strong>
        Until theres monetization, its a marketing tool, and like any marketing tool, you have to weigh the return. Future returns at this point are questionable. It doesnt mean you shouldnt experiment in the meantime. You should, but do it in balance with more profitable ventures and other marketing opportunities.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Shay Borg, senior director, head of partner strategy, AOL
         <br/>
        </strong>
        The risk is similar to Demand Media, which lost traffic when Google changed its algorithm, and its the same problem all MCNs have on YouTube. Facebook is just another walled garden, and its happy to extract your content and make money on it. Maybe thats just how it is. Are publishers willing to give up the battle being a destination site? If publishers audience matters, they should fight against it.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Mike Dodge, COO, Evolve Media
         <br/>
        </strong>
        We will skeptically explore it. What are the revenue splits? Or, once were hooked on it, will it change, without a viable competitor there? I worry it will become just like a YouTube  its not where we publish to monetize our content.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Sarah Frank, executive producer, NowThis
        </strong>
        <br/>
        That Facebook engagement is really exciting. What would it take to get 65 million fans across platforms without Facebook? Its also very natural to our audience.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Chris Pirrone, GM of sports digital media, USA Today
        </strong>
        <br/>
        If Facebook sells advertising around the content, you might not have gotten those views if you sold them on your own. But it depends on your goal as a publisher. Is Facebook going to favor publishers that publish directly to them?
       </p>
       <p>
        The post
        <a class=" autoblog-load-by-popup thickbox" href="http://digiday.com/publishers/publishers-weigh-controversial-facebook-scheme/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://digiday.com/publishers/publishers-weigh-controversial-facebook-scheme/', 'Weigh the return': Publishers chime in on controversial Facebook ploy');" rel="nofollow">
