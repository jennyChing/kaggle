      <u>
       ducation and Psychology
       <o:p>

      </u>
     </b>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:9.4pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Marvin Minsky
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:9.4pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     22 January 2009
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     Whatgoals do we want our schools to achieve? Most parents agree that their childrenshould learn about History, Language, Science and Math, and get someinstruction in Health, Sports, and Art.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Most parents also want their children taught to behave in what theyregard to be civilized ways.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     <span class="EBTimes14">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       And
      </span>
     </span>
     surely, most parents would also agree that schools should help children learn
     <i>
      goodways to think.
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     However, while schools have good ways to teach factsabout subjects, many pupils still fail to build adequate skills for applyingthat knowledge. [
     <a href="#_edn1" name="_ednref1" style="mso-endnote-id:edn1" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [1]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span class="EBTimes14">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span class="EBTimes14">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      But if good thinking is one of our principal goals,then why dont schools try to explicitly teach about how human Learning andReasoning work?
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
      I
     </span>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     nstead we tacitly
     <span class="EBTimes14">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       a
      </span>
     </span>
     ssume that if we simply provide enoughknowledge, then each childs brain will self-organize appropriate ways toapply those facts.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Then would itmake sense for us to include a subject called Human Psychology as part of thegrade-school curriculum?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     I dontthink that we can do this yet, because,
     <a name="OLE_LINK5">
     </a>
     <a name="OLE_LINK6">
      <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK5">
       few present-day teacherswould agree about which Theories of Thinking to teach.
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </span>
     </a>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK6">
     <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK5">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </span>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK6">
     <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK5">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       Soinstead, well propose a different approach:
       <i>
        to provide our children withideas they could use to invent their own theories about themselves!
       </i>
      </span>
     </span>
    </span>
    <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK6">
     <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK5">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
       </span>
       The restof this essay will suggest some benefits that could come from this, and somepractical ways to accomplish itby engaging children in various kinds ofconstructive, computer-related projects.
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </span>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK6">
     <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK5">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </span>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK5">
   </span>
   <span style="mso-bookmark:OLE_LINK6">
   </span>
   <h1 align="center" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     1. Why we cant yet include
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Psychology in the Primary SchoolCurriculum.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </h1>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Todays most popular theories of learning are based on acentury of experiments in which we place a pigeon or rat in a certain situationand reward it with food if it responds with a certain action
     <i>
      .
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Then later, that animal will moreoften behave in that same way.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
       Of several responses made to the same situation, thosewhich are closely followed by satisfaction to the animal will, other thingsbeing equal, be more firmly connected with the situation, so that, when thatsituation recurs, those responses will be more likely to recurwhile responsesfollowed by discomfort will become less likely to occur. Edward L. Thorndike1911
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Those experiments also showed that a quicker reward has alarger effect, so educators often use problem-sets in which each new task isalmost the same as the last. This causes most answers to be correct, whichtherefore results in more frequent rewardsand this helps to make classroomsmore pleasant. And indeed, those carefully graded assignments work well toprepare for short-answer testsbut they dont help prepare us for real-worldlife, where problems dont come in neat sequences. [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn2" name="_ednref2" style="mso-endnote-id:edn2" title="">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [2]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     However, this type of learning only works well when theanimal already can recognize each such situation or stimulus and the requiredreaction or
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     response already isin that animals repertoire.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Toensure these conditions, most classic experiments constrained each animalsimply to choose between only two different buttons to press. Then thatreward-based theory of learning worked remarkably well to predict how sub-humananimals react to those simplified situations. But that theory only describedthose animals external behaviors their motor responses to sensory inputsandnever progressed to shed more light on how people learn to
     <i>
      react in theirminds
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     , to morecomplex problems and situations. [
     <a href="#_edn3" name="_ednref3" style="mso-endnote-id:edn3" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [3]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;mso-pagination:none;mso-layout-grid-align:none;text-autospace:none">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Inany case, children are different from pigeons and rats, and those classicexperiments dont help much to explain how people learn to represent knowledgein their brains and, later, retrieve the information that might be mostrelevantso that they can reason, plan, and construct new ideas.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     So when we try to extend those oldtheories from pigeons to people, we find that they tell us little about thehigher-levels of minds that distinguish us from those animals.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     More advanced ideas about psychology began to emerge in the1940s from the new field called Cyberneticswhich then evolved into CognitiveScience and Artificial Intelligence.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     These new sciences are constantly producing new ideas about mindsbutthose concepts are still changing so fast that they arent yet stable enough toteach.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     So by default, the ideasfrom those early years of research on the external behavior of animals stilldominate the context in which most present-day teachers are taught toteach.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <h1 align="center" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     2. Some deficiencies ofbehavior-based theories.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </h1>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <b>
      <i>
       Humanslearn Negative Expertise
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     . We tendto think of knowledge in positive termsand of experts as people who knowwhat to dobut much of an experts ability comes from knowing about the mostcommon mistakesand thus, knowing which things one should not do.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Thus, much of a persons competence isbased on learning to detectand suppressunproductive ways to think. Also, anadequate theory of learning should also cover the reflective skills thatpeople use to recognize exceptions to generalizations, to eliminate tacticsthat waste too much time, and more generally, to make longer-range planes andform broader perspectives.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     In anycase, the early behaviorists rarely recognized the importance of NegativeExpertise because they cannot directly observe how this affects our ways tobehave. In particular, when people deal with difficult problems, we often learnmore from our failures than from our successes. [
     <a href="#_edn4" name="_ednref4" style="mso-endnote-id:edn4" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [4]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Human Minds make high-level Credit-Assignments.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     New situations are seldom the same as previous ones, soafter you solve a difficult problem, it wont help much to just remember theactions that solved itbecause you probably had to make many attempts beforeyou found a successful oneso it would
     <span class="EBTimes14">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       nt make sense to reward all those prior attempts.
      </span>
     </span>
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     It sometimes helps to reward your mostrecent reactionsbut generally, its more important to recognize which earlierstrategic decisions should get credit for ones latest success.
     <i>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     We briefly discussed this in Memo 2, but also see Section 8-5of
     <i>
      The Emotion Machine
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     .
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;mso-pagination:none;mso-layout-grid-align:none;text-autospace:none">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;mso-pagination:none;mso-layout-grid-align:none;text-autospace:none">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       HumanMinds think about what theyre thinking about.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     After youve wasted a lot of time by using a process thatfinally failed, you usually try to figure out some cause or reason for
     <i>
      why
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     it failed.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     However, those older theories never tried to describe themethods that people use for
     <i>
      thinking about what weve been thinkingabout.
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     But Im convinced that these self-reflectiveprocesses are the principal ones that people use for
     <i>
      developing new ways tothink.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     I doubt that simply rewarding successhelps much to promote ones mental development.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;mso-pagination:none;mso-layout-grid-align:none;text-autospace:none">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:9.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;mso-pagination:none;mso-layout-grid-align:none;text-autospace:none">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     PsychologyStudent:
     <i>
      But isnt it an established fact that every child is born with acertain IQthat is, an unchangeable quantity of innate mental ability?
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     There is a popular belief that each persons amount ofintelligence is fixed, because those
     <i>
      IQ
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     numbers dont usually change very much after earlychildhood.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, the evidencefor this may be biased because of ignoring other important causes for this:
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:9.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      " Biographical information on a sample of twentymen of genius suggests that the typical developmental pattern includes asimportant aspects: (1) a high degree of attention focused upon the child byparents and other adults, expressed in intensive educational measures and,usually, abundant love; (2) isolation from other children, especially outsidethe family; and (3) a rich efflorescence of fantasy as a reaction to thepreceding conditions.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     "
     <i>
      [If so, then] the mass education of our public school system is, in itsway, a vast experiment on the effect of reducing all three factors to aminimum: accordingly, it should tend to suppress the occurrence ofgenius."
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     HaroldMcCurdy, 1960 [
     <a href="#_edn5" name="_ednref5" style="mso-endnote-id:edn5" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [5]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     This also could be related to the process we mentioned inMemo 2:
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.05in;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:9.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      A new class of 6-year-old children will soon begin toshare similar ways to think and behave.
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
      Then, next year, when they are 7 years old, most of those pupils will stillremain in that groupand thus will tend to perpetuate those same patterns ofactivity. The next year, they will be 8-year-olds, but will continue to sharemany attitudes, values, and cognitive strategies.
      <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
      </span>
      So as those children proceed through their K-12 grades,large portions of their ways to think will remain much like those of6-year-olds!
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Human Thinking involves Prediction, Comparing, andPlanning.
       <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
       </span>
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     The standard reward-based learningschemes envision a pigeon or rat as simply containing a database of two-partrules like
     <i>
      
      <b>
       If
      </b>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      the situation is S,
      <b>
       Do
      </b>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      action R.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     [
     <a href="#_edn6" name="_ednref6" style="mso-endnote-id:edn6" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [6]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <i>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     But while suchrules can describe much of our external behavior, this ignores the fact thatwhen you consider (in your mind) two actions that you might possibly do, then(before you perform any actions at all) you can try to predict the result ofeachand then compare those imagined results!
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     This means that to extend our theory from pigeons to people,well need to include some
     <i>
      ways to predict the effects of possible actions.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     However,
     <b>
      <i>
       If
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      -
      <i>
       Do
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     rules dont predict the results of their actionsso wellalso need 3-part
     <i>
      
      <b>
       If
      </b>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      -
      <b>
       Do
      </b>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     -
     <b>
      <i>
       Then
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     like, If the situation is
     <i>
      S
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     , and you do
     <i>
      R
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     , Then situation
     <i>
      T
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     will result.
     <i>
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Chapter 5 of
     <i>
      TheEmotion Machine
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     explainshow to link sets of these 3-part rules into networks of knowledge that we canuse to simulate virtual worlds' inside our minds, so that we can imagine thesequences of possible actions that we call our Future Plans.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     But before the dawn of computers andprograms, it was hard to envision such simulationsso those early Behavioristpsychologists were forced to ignore the fact that people make longer-rangeplans and strategies. [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn7" name="_ednref7" style="mso-endnote-id:edn7" title="">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [7]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Human Thinking is largely directed by Goals.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     You dont open every door you pass,or pick up every object you see.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     You dont often use an If-&gt;Do rule unless it serves some currentmotive or goal. However before the era of Cybernetics, few psychologists triedto make theories about
     <i>
      what goals are and how they work.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     Instead, they simply assumed that each animal brain containsa separate set of rules to engage one for each of that animals goals,intentions, motives or drivesone set of rules to use when hungry and anotherset to use when angry, etc.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Butfinally in 1957, Allen Newell and Herbert Simon proposed a more constructivetheory: a
     <i>
      purposeful
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     or
     <i>
      goal-based
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     action is one that results from using a special kind of process (calledGPS) that operates on two different descriptions:
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-13.5pt;tab-stops:.25in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      S
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     is a description of the situationthat the machine is currently in.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-13.5pt;tab-stops:.25in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      G
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     is a description of a
     <i>
      future
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     state that the machine wants tobe in.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-13.5pt;tab-stops:.25in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      GPS
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     is a process that repeatedly looksfor a difference between
     <b>
      S
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     and
     <b>
      G
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     , and then performs some action on
     <b>
      S
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     that is likely to lessen thatdifference.
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-13.5pt;tab-stops:.25in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Such a process will result inbehavior that appear to do just what we mean by pursuing a goal,  because itwill persist at removing the differences it perceives between what it has andwhat it wantsthat is, unless some other process intervenes to change orremove that G. [
     <a href="#_edn8" name="_ednref8" style="mso-endnote-id:edn8" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [8]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent3" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-indent:0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;color:blue">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent3" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-indent:0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <b>
      <i>
       However, human learning doesnt always dependonly on getting pleasant rewards.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     Wecan adapt to small changes in familiar conditions by making small changes toskills that we already know.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Butwhen thrust into strange environments, we may need to abandon our oldertechniquesand this can lead to unpleasant feelings like pain and grief.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Chapter 9 of
     <span class="EBTimes14">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       <i>
        The Emotion Machine
       </i>
      </span>
     </span>
     <span class="EBTimes14">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       discusses some ways to prevent
      </span>
     </span>
     such discomforts from keeping us from learning new thingswhereas the oldreward based theories ignored what one could call the
     <i>
      Pleasure ofExploration-Pain
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     .
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent2" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
      </span>
      Pleasure pursues objects that are beautiful, melodious,fragrant, savory, soft. But curiosity, seeking new experiences, will even seekout the contrary of these, not to experience the discomfort that may come withthem, but from a passion for experimenting and knowledge.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Augustine,
     <i>
      Confessions
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     , Book 10.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent2" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span class="EBTimes14">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
      <i>
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </i>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.7pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     <i>
      Why do children enjoy the rides in amusement parks,knowing that they will be scared, even sick?
      <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
      </span>
      Why do explorers endure discomfort and painknowing thattheir very purpose will disperse once they arrive?
      <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
      </span>
      And what makes people work for years at jobs they hate, sothat someday they will be able tothey seem to have forgotten what! 
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
      It is the same for solving difficultproblems, or climbing freezing mountain peaks, or playing pipe organs withone's feet: some parts of the mind find these horrible, while other parts enjoyforcing those first parts to work for them.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     
     <i>
      The Society of Mind,
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Chap. 9.04.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span class="EBTimes14">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
      In otherwords, adventurousness can overcome unpleasantness: when "pleasant" orpositive rewards fail to help us learn more difficult subjects, we can makeourselves enjoy discomforts and painsby acquiring what Augustine called apassion for experimenting and knowledge.
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.7pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.7pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Citizen:
     <i>
      How can you speak ofenjoying discomfort? Isnt that a self-contradiction?
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoPlainText" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;tab-stops:1.5in 508.5pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     This only seems paradoxical when youthink of yourself as an entity that can feel only one feeling at a time. But ifyou imagine your mind as a system that runs a host of concurrent processes,then you can see how some parts of your mind might enjoy making other partssufferas when it feels good to have just discovered a new kind of blunderor mistake.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     In fact, thatsexactly what sport coaches teach!
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Of course, those athletes still feel physical pains, just as artists andscientists feel mental pains. But somehow, they manage to train themselves tokeep those pains from spiraling into the awful cascades we call
     <i>
      suffering
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     . [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn9" name="_ednref9" style="mso-endnote-id:edn9" title="">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [9]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.7pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-4.7pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      Scientist: Few things bring morepleasure to me than replacing my old ideasand then showing that my newtheories are better than those of my competitors.
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.7pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-4.7pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      Artist: It hurts to discard ahard-earned technique, but nothing surpasses the thrill of conceiving new waysto represent and to think about things.
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent3" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-indent:0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent3" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-indent:0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     One needs to learn not only what works, but also whatto do when failure looms. I dont like that tale of
     <i>
      The Little Engine thatCould
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     with its helpless injunctionto simply repeat 
     <i>
      I think I can, I think I can.
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      A better motto would be to think
     <i>
      perhaps itstime to try something else
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     because everysetback can offer a chance for a new phase of mental development.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent3" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-indent:0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       The traditional theories do not explain Pleasure.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     From before the dawn of psychology, everyone has alwaysagreed that pleasant rewards help us to learn, but Ive never seen any plausibleexplanations for this. Here is what may be a new way to explain how pleasureworks
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times;color:blue">
     :
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      Pleasure is a word we use to describe a process thatsused to keep
      <span class="EBTimes120">
       <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
        a mind fromchanging the subject of the persons most recent concern.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        One might need such a function topreserve the current contents of memory for long enough for Credit-Assignmentto be accomplished!
       </span>
      </span>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span class="EBTimes120">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       <i>
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </i>
      </span>
     </span>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:13.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     So,while we usually see Pleasure as positive, we also should see it asnegativebecause, during the time in which
     <span class="EBTimes120">
      <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
       Credit Assignment
      </span>
     </span>
     proceeds, itsuppresses competing activities.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Similarly, we often need to suppress other interests to keep ourselvesworking on difficult problems.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     Butif pleasure has negative aspects, then why does it feel good to us?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     People often answer this by saying thatfeelings are so basic, simple, and direct that theres nothing much to sayabout them! However, in Psychology, its often the case that the things thatseem simplest turn out to be the ones that are the most complex!
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     In this case, I feel good might mean,Right now, Ive suppressed all my other concerns, so all of my problems seemto be gone.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <u>
       3. Teaching Cyberneticsinstead of Psychology
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </u>
     </b>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     All this suggests that our ideas about psychology are stilldeveloping so rapidly that it wouldnt make sense for us to select any currenttheory of thinking to teach.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Soinstead, well propose a different approach:
     <i>
      to provide our children withideas they could use to invent their own theories about themselves!
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     The rest of this essay will suggest that such ideas couldcome from engaging children in projects that involve making machines that havelifelike behaviors. Such projects would engage and integrate many conceptsthat we separately treat today, in Physics, Biology, and Mathematicsand inSocial Studies, Psychology, and Economicsalong with other important principlesthat dont fit into any of those traditional subjects.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     A flood of new concepts about what machines could do beganto emerge in the 1940s, from research in the field called Cyberneticswhich soonthen led to other fields called Control Theory, Computer Science, ArtificialIntelligence, and Cognitive Psychology.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Each of those new sciences brought hosts of new ideas about how to buildsystems that actually do some of the things we use thinking to do. So now, inthe spirit of Seymour Paperts constructionism, we can enable our children toexperiment with networks composed of collections of parts that support manysorts of knowledge-based processes. [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn10" name="_ednref10" style="mso-endnote-id:edn10" title="">
     <span style="font-size:12.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [10]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ] This is important because
     <i>
      thatswhat we are
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     :
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Consequently, it makes sense to expect children to use suchideas to make better ways to think about themselves.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     We could start by encouraging them to build simplifiedmodels of the sensor and motor systems of animalsand put these creatures intoenvironments to experiment, first with each ones separate behavior, and thenwith the social relations in groups of them.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Do we have enough evidence that such experiments willimprove those childrens self-images?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Our answer must be ambiguous: in the past weve seen many cases in whichsuch experiments appeared to work well, but those LOGO-based projects werenever extended to large enough scales. But such projects are far more feasibletoday, because equipment is now so much cheaper.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Some critics might complain that subjects like Cyberneticsand Computer Science do not deserve a special place in the elementary schoolcurriculum, because they are too specialized.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     However, Computer Science is not only about computersthemselves; more generally, it provides us with a whole new world of ways tounderstand complex processesincluding the ones that go on in own mind.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     For until those new techniques arrived(such as
     <i>
      programming languages
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     for describing processes, and
     <i>
      data structures
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     for representing knowledge), we hadno expressions that people could use to articulateand then communicategoodnew ideas about such things. [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn11" name="_ednref11" style="mso-endnote-id:edn11" title="">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [11]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      Understanding Systems with Feedback Loops.
      <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
      </span>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     While rule-based systems work well to explain much of howother species behave, human beings do much more than directly react toSituations or Stimuli. For example (as in the GPS systems we mentionabove), we often make mental comparisons between (1) the real-world situationthat we are actually in with (2) some goal-situation wed prefer to bein.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     <i>
      Then we can use ourdescriptions of those differences to decide which of the actions that we couldwould most likely to help us to achieve that goal.
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Now, this might seem so obvious as to make you wonder whyIm repeating it.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     But myself, Iveoften wondered why this idea wasnt clear to the early Behaviorists!
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     For if we adopt this process-basedconcept of what goals are, this changes our view of our own behaviors:
     <i>
      wereact less to Situations themselves, than to their Differences to our goals!
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     In other words, if we think in terms of goal-basedprocesses, we can see our actions as mainly resulting from feedback loopsthat work to change differences rather than things. Of course it is perfectlyobvious that some such loops increase differences, while others tend to reducethem.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     It also is not very hard tosee that when feedback
     <i>
      increases
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     differences, this can lead to exponential growth (which cannever be sustained for long, because it will soon exhaust some resource)andits only a little harder to see why, when feedback
     <i>
      reduces
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     differences, this can make aprocess more stable and reliable.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, it also turns out that there is also an astonishing range ofphenomena that come from systems that lie between those extremesand the studyof such processes was what led to the new science called Cybernetics.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     For example, when feedback-loops havetime-delays, this can lead to repetitive oscillations, or even to the complexphenomena that mathematicians describe as chaotic.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:31.9pt;margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:9.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:-9.0pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     In any case, virtually every real-world system includes someof those kinds of feedback loops, so it seems to me that everyone should knowthese ideasbut unless we teach people names for them, theyll find it hard tothink about such things, or even to learn to recognize them. An easy way tolearn about feedback loops is to make (or better, to simulate) a turtle-likerobot that follows a line on the floor, by detecting when the line is to theright or left, and using that information for steering. [
     <a href="#_edn12" name="_ednref12" style="mso-endnote-id:edn12" title="">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [12]
      </span>
     </a>
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     That basic idea is simple enough, but soon the child willfind that the system is likely to get off the track
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     (because of excessive overshoot, or by encountering anobstacle).
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Then the child caninvent various ways to prevent or recover from this (say, by discovering waysto search for it).
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Also, in thecase of objects that move, one can invent many different ways to try to trackand predict their future paths. But this opens the door to another whole world:our child might now want to go further, to make this animal try to predict andpursue another one that also movesor even one that is trying to escape!
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Then, wow, weve suddenly entered thesocial realm!
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>

   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <b>
      <i>
       RoboticProjects.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     Most present-dayrobotic projects use wheeled vehicles for mobility, because these are so easyto build.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, Id like to seemore projects that try to design robots that walk, because they would be more versatile.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Its not very difficult to make 4-legwalking machines, but 2-legged ones are much harder to stabilizebut once youcan keep them from falling down, you can go on to make them jump and run!
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     And of course, theres a whole newworld of ideas that will come from building micro-worlds in which several suchcreatures interact; you could either control them all from a central place, orjust give them some ways to communicate. For discussions about making robotscooperate, see examples at
     <a href="http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~robosoccer/main/">
      http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~robosoccer/main/
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flocking_(behavior)">
      http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flocking_(behavior)
     </a>
     ,and
     <a href="http://www.lalena.com/AI/Flock/">
      http://www.lalena.com/AI/Flock/
     </a>
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <b>
      Simulatedvs. Physical Robots.
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     It is remarkable how engaged peoplebecome when they first see mechanical, physical robots.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     But eventually, our students learn moreby working with computer programs that simulate robots, because then one canbetter design and control ones experiments.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     One trouble with physical robots is that too much of thestudents time is consumed with trivial bugs that are hard to fix; also, it ishard to control enough of the robots environment to make the resultsreproducible.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, to makegood use of our young peoples time, well need to provide them with virtualphysical worlds that work quickly and realistically.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     However,we also should note that in recent years, increasingly fewer children havedeveloped skills for making and fixing mechanical things, because so fewproducts today are repairable.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Sobuilding physical robots can be an excellent way to remedy this growinghelplessnessand one can find many robot construction kits with a Google searchfor robot kits.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, thereare advantages to learning to build machines using general-purpose constructionsets, such as Erector, TinkerToy, and Fischertechnikbut these are becominghard to get.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     LEGO is more widelyavailable, but is generally less versatile.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     The next few years will bring new sorts of fabricationtechniques, e.g., 3-D printers, which today are still too expensive forschools. But still, there's nothing wrong with learning some old-fashionedcarpentry!
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <b>
      <i>
       Balancingand manipulation.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Build a moving hand that can balance astickfirst in 1 and then in 2 dimensions.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     Try to discover the limitations of such processes. Try tobuild a juggling machine. I dont know of any projects that have made machinesthat can tie a knot or button a shirt.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Optimization and problem solving.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     To recognize a particular objectfrom a collection of several things, one will need some way to determine whichof them best fits a certain description.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     A powerful method for solving such problems is to describe things interms of coordinates, parameters, or variablesand then to find the path ofsteepest ascent.  Such hill-climbing programs can often provide us withnumerical ways to optimize things when our more clever symbolic methods fail(as they most often will, in everyday life).
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Visual recognition projects.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Recognizing printed and handwrittencharacters and words.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Why is iteasier to recognize an entire word than to recognize the letters that makeit?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Can you make a programthat will distinguish pictures of cats from pictures of dogs?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     (I dont know of any successfulsuch project.) There do exist programs that do well at recognizing faces, butIve not yet heard of any program that can look at a picture of a typical roomand identify the most common types of objects in view.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     One problem is that the appearance ofan object will depend on ones point of view, so the programmer will need toinvent some concepts to deal with this.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     So these kinds of projects can demonstrate the importance of makingappropriate kinds of abstractions.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      Processing Voices and other Sounds.
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     One can also learn a lot about perceptual processes by makingprograms that classify features of everyday sounds.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     Can you design a program to distinguish the tones of aviolin, trumpet, and clarinet?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Howfar can you get toward recognizing such everyday sounds as footsteps, voices,the clinks of cups and plates, coughs, crumpling paper, etc?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Could children invent better tricks forthis than the ones that most engineers would use?
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     Experiment with grammars and parsing procedures. Then takeany other project weve mentioned here and add an interface to it so that youcan control it by using verbal commands.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      Performance systems.
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Some children might like to make a program that canread a Music scoreand then actually perform the music. Or try to experimentwith phonetics to make a program that reads a story aloud; this would lead toall sorts of ideas about pronunciation, accent, and stressand up to thethreshold of rhetoric.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Puzzles and Game-Playing programs:
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Write a program to do your mathhomework (and see if your teacher will accept the results) or write programs tosolve such spatial puzzles as Tangrams, Pentominos, Magic Squares, orCrossword-like puzzles, etc.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Many children become so addicted to playing particular games that thiscan become a serious problem.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, we might be able to exploit this by encouraging children to tryto write front end programs to play existing gamesor to design and programtheir own new games.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Cognitive projects:
       <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
       </span>
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     How difficult would it be to make a system that does some humanlikecommonsense reasoning?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Answer:this would be very hard, indeed: in fact, it is at the forefront of currentresearch in Artificial Intelligence.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Nevertheless, it would be interesting for children to see how much theycan do, using Logic alone, and then to add other forms of reasoningforexample, by using analogies.
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times;color:red">
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     And very such project could beimproved by enabling it to improve itself, by adding abilities to learn.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     For example, each part of a systemmight be improved by adding a reinforcement-based neural network to it.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Experimenting with Rule-Based Systems.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Theories about Finite-State Machines.
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     Modern Computer Science includes a great collection of usefulideas about different kinds of processesmany of which can be built intosystems that even young children could make and then play with.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     These could include simple logicalreasoning systems, or machines that do arithmetic, or ones that play some populargames.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     One productive way to beginwould be to experiment with networks of logical
     <i>
      AND
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     s,
     <i>
      OR
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     s, and
     <i>
      NOT
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     s.
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     Indeed, a good way to learn about computers might be tobegin with such logical networks instead of conventional programminglanguagesbecause this yields different insights into what computers are andhow they work. [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn13" name="_ednref13" style="mso-endnote-id:edn13" title="">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [13]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ] In any case, if our goal is toattract children to technical concepts, this finite state approach might turnout to be more productive (and more enjoyable) than making them learnArithmetic. [
    </span>
    <a href="#_edn14" name="_ednref14" style="mso-endnote-id:edn14" title="">
     <span style="font-size:20.0pt">
      <span style="mso-special-character:footnote">
       [14]
      </span>
     </span>
    </a>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     ]
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <b>
      <i>
       Learning Critical Thinking
      </i>
     </b>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     .
     <span style="mso-spacerun:yes">
     </span>
     This may seem somewhat out of place, but I want to recommendencouraging every child to learn a number of magic tricksbecause (1) this is agood introduction to the psychology of perception and (2) it also leads toimportant insights about how what we perceive is affected by context,expectation and deception. For children who cant get lessons from localmagicians, it would be good to have some online magic-teaching programs.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <h1 align="center" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     4. How it can help to think ofoneself as a Machine
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </h1>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Many people are firmly convinced that to have a mechanicalimage of oneself must lead to a depressing sense of helplessnessbecause itmeans that youre doomed to remain what you are, and theres nothing that youcan do about this.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, Illargue exactly the opposite: seeing yourself as a kind of machine can be aliberating ideabecause
     <i>
      whatever you might dislike about yourself, thatmight be caused by a bug that you could fix!
     </i>
    </span>
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     For example, contrast these pairs of self-images:
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center;tab-stops:297.0pt 6.0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Imnot good at math. ---There are some bugs in my symbolic processes.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center;tab-stops:297.0pt 6.0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Imjust not very smart ------ Some of my programs need improvements.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center;tab-stops:297.0pt 6.0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Idont like this subject. ---------- My current goals need better priorities.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center;tab-stops:297.0pt 6.0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     I amconfused. -----------Some of my processes may conflict with others.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center;tab-stops:297.0pt 6.0in">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     If you think of yourself in terms of I, then youll seeyourself as a single thing, that has no parts to change or rearrange.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     But using My can help you to envisionyourself as composed of parts, which could enable you to imagine specificchanges that might improve your ways to think. In other words, if you canrepresent your mind as made of potentially repairable machinery, then you canthink about remedies. For example, you might be able to diagnose some bugs ordeficiencies in the apparatus that you use for everyday functions likethese:
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <i>
      <o:p>
      </o:p>
     </i>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Time-management. ----- OrganizingSearches. -------Splitting problems into parts.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Selecting good ways to representthings. -----Making appropriate cognitive maps.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     Allocating short-term memory.---------- Making appropriate Credit Assignments.
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt;text-align:center">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
   <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-right:31.9pt">
    <span style="font-size:20.0pt;font-family:Times">
     It seems clear that some children are better than others areat doing this kind of self reflection. Could this be a skill that we couldteach?
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     Perhaps, but this might notyet be practical because we dont yet know enough about our human mentalmachinery.
     <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
     </span>
     However, the types ofprojects this essay recommends could help us to promote that goal, by givingour children more tools to use for constructing better views of themselves!
     <o:p>
     </o:p>
    </span>
   </p>
