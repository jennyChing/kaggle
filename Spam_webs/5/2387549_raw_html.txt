     Eo ipso

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Eo ipso
       </b>
      </i>
      means "by (or from) the thing itself" in
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      and is similar to the sense expressed by the English idioms, "by the same token," "of itself" or "on its own account". It is often used in various schools of
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">
       philosophy
      </a>
      to demonstrate the possibility/impossibility of propositions from their nature. For example, "That I am does not
      <i>
       eo ipso
      </i>
      mean that I think." The term is also used in
      <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">
       law
      </a>
      , and it is through law that it was brought into English from Latin.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Crisis_and_a_Crisis_in_the_Life_of_an_Actress" title="The Crisis and a Crisis in the Life of an Actress">
        The Crisis and a Crisis in the Life of an Actress
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard" title="Sren Kierkegaard">
       Sren Kierkegaard
      </a>
      , the philosopher describes the quality of
      <i>
       Eo ipso
      </i>
      in the following excerpt:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       But to be gallant towards an artist is precisely the highest degree of insolence, a maudlin impertinence and a disgusting kind of intrusiveness. Anyone who is something, and is something essentially, possesses "eo ipso," the claim to be recognized for
       <i>
        exactly this special thing
       </i>
       , and for nothing more or less.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Also see the following aphorism from
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">
       Nietzsche's
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Beyond_Good_and_Evil" title="Beyond Good and Evil">
        Beyond Good and Evil
       </a>
      </i>
      (73), as translated by Walter Kaufmann (1966):
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Whoever reaches his ideal transcends it
       <i>
        eo ipso
       </i>
       .
      </p>
     </blockquote>
