             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://sahwira.blogspot.com/2008/08/not-anymore.html">
               Not Anymore
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               Everyday I wake up to no electricity and no water. I take my bucket, which is now turning white inside from being consistently used as my 'bath tub'. Obviously this also reflects on how often I wash it, but that is a subject for another day. The house a couple of blocks down has got a borehole. In fact, thinking about it now, it's actually that borehole water that's making my bucket white, but like I said, we can talk about this more some other day.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I make my way down the couple of blocks to fill my bucket, and two 5 litre containers with water for bathing and the necessary breakfast. This has become normal. I don't complain anymore because I figured the plan works, and that's just how things are.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               In some hidden place in my room, is a stash of bank notes, US dollars, South African Rands, a little bit of British Pounds (my friend sent me these, you know)  and some Botswana Pula. This stash is my bank, my own FCA (Foreign Currency Account). Everyday I have 100 Rands on me, and of course our own Zim Dollar for transport and the necessary lunch. The 100 Rands is never changed to Zim Dollar except where necessary, it protects me from the unexpected (or rather expected) price increases that happen daily. You will never see me stranded with no money for transport. I don't complain anymore because I figured this plan works, this is how things are.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Every time I'm not at work I'm in the queue at my local bank, but these days I've been spending some bit of time at Pizza Inn, offering those buying with cash, a discount if they give me the cash and I use my debit card to pay for their Pizzas. This is how I get all my money out of the bank, and you wont hear me complain. The excess cash ends up as forex in my FCA, that way I preserve value for my money, and this is quite normal, and this plan works, the way things are.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I know a lady who is always selling foodstuffs she brings every month from South Africa. I buy my cooking oil, bath soap, toothpaste and what have you from her. She charges in Rands, but I'm not complaining, her stuff is good and she even gives good payment terms. Last week she brought a bread maker for this other guy I know, and I'm making plans to get mine, that's how the plan works, its just how things are.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               A lot of people have packed and left, been considering it myself. Everyday something happens in Zimbabwe, and everyday if not careful, you lose hope. When you are left with not enough hope, you just leave. But that's not how it works. That's not the plan. Zimbabwe has survived the worst mismanagement, human rights abuse, corruption, political violence, and put simply, it's the most dysfunctional country I have ever seen (OK, so maybe I have never been outside Zimbabwe before, but...).
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The plan is to remain resilient. To be able to continually complain about how bad things have become, and manage to find solutions to the current challenges. To give up some of your basic rights and liberties and focus on survival. To realize that this government failed years ago, and their continued stay in power is for their own personal benefit, so relying on them spells doom on you. To accept that Zimbabwe of yesterday is gone. It was invaded during the farm invasions, starved during the years that followed the invasions, destroyed during
               <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Murambatsvina">
                Murambatsvina
               </a>
               ,  looted during the price freeze, slashed during Sunrise, and killed during the run-off campaign.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               What is left is not Zimbabwe. This is 'Survivor Zimbabwe' where you need to bribe one or two officials, con a couple of (not so) innocent citizens, break a bunch of laws, with the usual cursing and a bit of nostalgia, your typical '
               <span style="font-style: italic;">
                Akoma Zimbabwe yaoma
               </span>
               ' (Guys Zimbabwean life is now tough) and then you top it off with the absence of any sense of guilt, because hey, what else were you supposed to do. That is the plan. And it works. And its just the way it is.
               <br/>
              </p>

               I Thirst
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               I was born in 1980, that makes me as old as Zimbabwe is, meaning that I am one those "born frees". In some circles we are seen as the most troublesome lot, because we do not appreciate how much our fathers,fore-fathers, mothers and fore-mothers went through in trying to liberate this country. Maybe it's true we don't get it, but what I don't get even more is why after all that suffering they went through, I suffer even more in peace-times.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               So many times the radio says something about protecting the "gains of the liberation war", and "gains of the land redistribution" and that Zimbabwe shall never be a colony again. I'm so tired of all these empty and quite patronizing statements of propaganda, when everyday I carry a bucket of water for bathing. Each morning I bring an extra sum of money because you just never know when bus fare goes up. I'm paying 1 billion dollars for a one way trip to work, maybe 1.5 billion in the evening.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Everyone everywhere talks about the situation in Zimbabwe, when Mbeki came to Zimbabwe during the state of limbo we were in soon after elections and said "Crisis, what crisis?" (ok, maybe not in those exact words but...) he was right, election results were not the crisis, Zimbabwe itself is a crisis. I guess the biggest problem is that we don't have oil, and Bush can't really make up some more WMDs for us so that the world can really start noticing and take firm action.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Voices of thousands of innocent youths and young adults, whose prime years have been robbed by a government that is so determined that they alone are the only legitimate leaders of Zimbabwe, by virtue of them having held a gun and shot a few white men and quite a number of black folks in the name of Chimurenga, are now like echoes of a desperate stranger lost in some isolated cave in the jungles of an undiscovered forest.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Its like the world has watched us on our way to Golgotha and nobody offered to carry the cross for us, and now here we are at the end of all things, with all dreams shattered, most hope gone, and promises destroyed the final nails being hammered. Its as if the only thing left to say before its all over is "I thirst" and maybe a little sponge of sour vinegar is all that's left before we roll credits.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </p>

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://sahwira.blogspot.com/2008/03/sharing-excitement.html">
               Sharing the Excitement
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               The last time our country held elections, not even one person asked me whether I was a registered voter. This year, before Simba announced no one asked. In the last six days alone, almost everyone is interested in whether I'm going to vote this year! I don't see why anyone would not want to go and vote. It's free, in a country infamously known for its extremely high inflation rates, anything that goes for free I'm getting. I saw condoms in some shop the other day, with a big sticker that said FREE but no one was taking any, so I walk to the assistant and I ask are they really free or do I have to buy something first? She says they are free totally, and so everytime I walk into that shop, I really have to resist the urge to keep getting more and more!
               <br/>
               <br/>
               But anyway, back to elections. I'm really hoping that this year we will have a bigger turnout at the polls, like I was saying, I don't see why people shouldn't. In a country where queues are everywhere, I'm not going to mind queuing for a while to cast my vote. I can even see big business at the polls, vendors now target queues like you've never seen before.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               My worry however is the rural folks. And the media. And our Presidential Candidates. I was kumusha last week and I was very disappointed to see that people were not even aware our darling Simba is in the race. With such few days to go, this is bad news. Rural folks are good at going to the poll, they almost always exercise that right in large numbers (or so we've been told), and if one candidate is not known to them, I don't see how that candidate can win, seriously. And since our media tend to have a bias towards the usual candidate, the others just have to do way much more. Really if they could go around the country in 20 days they should, and visit every homestead, in every village of every province. The media is really being a condom to progress, I wish I could reach every corner of this country with this blog.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               But still, Zimbabwe, lets go and vote.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </p>

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://sahwira.blogspot.com/2008/02/morgan-robert-simba.html">
               Morgan, Robert, Simba
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               I'm really excited about this year's elections. OK, so what the excitement started when Simba Makoni announced he wanted us to vote for him, I'm all excited and can't wait for March. It doesn't matter this is the same excitement I had when Morgan, ages ago decided to ride on the popularity of ZCTU back then and took his chances on real politics.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The timing then was almost perfect. ZANU(PF) at the time was a bit lax, if only that guy from NCA hadn't pushed too hard on that referendum and constitution thing, MDC did have a chance. But when everyone went and voted 'NO' it was a wake up call for Robert, and that the story of how he never sleeps since then. But people were sympathetic of Tsvangirai, they loved him, but personally I didn't get him. At all. He appeared on SABC some time asking for sanctions and stuff, I think he also asked for drought and everything. But who can blame him, everyone wanted change, and he thought if Zimbabwe suffers more, it would translate to more votes for him. Rigging or no rigging, today he is not our president. Sad ha?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The thing is Robert is old and all that but he really isn't stupid. He knows what he is doing, and he seems to have a bunch of heavyweight supporters locally and internationally. I think he was a good president at some point, and very important International figure as well. Didn't the Queen knight him at some point? But I wasn't going to vote for him back then, because his portfolio had grown to include a long list of damaging blemishes. But Tsvangirai was going to get my vote not because i liked him but because, well, there was noone else!
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Now Makoni is something else. I really don't know this guy, but he's likeable. He was my most favourate finance minister back in the day (not really, but if i had a favourate,he'd be the one), I mean I like the guy. I think he's more composed than Morgan, more likely to say (or do) something more sensible than Morgan. Because Morgan is spent, hasn't he served enough terms as president already?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Unfortunately I'm told my vote is my secret, I'm not going to tell you who I'll vote for, but its really tempting.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               And again Zimbabwe, pick your candidate, then please go and vote. Please.
               <br/>
               <br/>

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://sahwira.blogspot.com/2008/02/zimbabwe-just-doesn-make-sense.html">
               Zimbabwe Just Doesn't Make Sense
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               Enough everyone! Zimbabwe this, Zimbabwe that, stop it already! I remember "pakadonha dhora" (when our Dollar fell), way back when, to something like 9:1 to the USD, that was my first time to hear about exchange rates and stuff. The term "dhora rakadonha" (the dollar fell) was the new way of saying things are tight. But we got over that. Because a few weeks/months later, we were 18:1 to the USD. At that point life as we knew it was finished! But we survived.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The exchange rate then continued to go on to embarassing rates, and we simply stopped following. What mattered to the ordinary guy on the street was the price of bread, money for bus fare e.t.c. That too, sadly was closely following, I was young but bread used to cost less than 20c, the time it went to 75c from 50c (I think) caused serious uproars, Our Edwin Hama (singer, where is he now anyway?) sang
               <span style="font-style: italic;">
                'I've been counting the change from a dollar, after buying bread ... we've got a problem..'
               </span>
               Yeah, you think! but we survived!
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The price of bread then continued to rise to embarassing prices, we didn't stop following. What mattered to the same ordinary guy on the street, was that he could still buy bread, and go to work and get more money to buy more bread. Sadly, going to work, or anywhere for that matter, was also following the trends. Noczim, i didn't know them until the fuel stations started accumulating unholy queues of cars wanting to fuel up, had its own issues getting fuel for the masses. I also learnt that all these years, can you believe it, all these years, we had the cheapest fuel in the region, and now we had none of it. The shortage of fuel fueled fuel prices to amounts I'm not too proud to blog, which in turn fueled the price of everything else to the same unproudly prices. Zimbabwe was burning, still is, but we survived.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Then drought, then the chaotic farm invasions, then &lt;insert anything that can go wrong here&gt;. As I speak, we've had no water for 5 days in a row. Electricity, comes and goes at will. Price hikes are getting much crazier, shortages of everything, deteriorating service standards everywhere, it just doesn't make sense that we are still here.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I could detail everything that has gone wrong with this country, but how will I explain that the country still stands? Now its February 2008; on March 29 Zimbabwe votes. We embrace ourselves for yet another maybe-this-time-it-will-happen moment, are we still sure that this country will remain standing after that? That whatever happens, rigging, intimidation, apathy, &lt;insert fancy politicized term here&gt;, we will wake up after elections and we will continue.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               We have become so used to complaining about something that I'm worried we will not survive a stable economy. The reason we remain standing is simply that we've adapted to this way of life. If i have money, I wont bank it, I convert it to forex and keep it under the matress. (Refer to Christmas 2007, if you think this is ridiculous. We had cash shortages since november.).
               <br/>
               Can you then picture me in a new Zimbabwe where the banks are actually functional? Been bitten once bruru, forget it.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Seriously do you really think it can get worse than this? Seriously?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               P.S. If you are Zimbabwean, please go and vote this year for a change. Please.
              </p>

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://sahwira.blogspot.com/2007/08/yet-another-day-in-life-of-zimbabwean.html">
               Yet another day in the life of a Zimbabwean
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               Recalling
               <a href="http://manulite.blogspot.com/2005/07/new-day-in-life-of-zimbabwean.html">
                <span class="blsp-spelling-error" id="SPELLING_ERROR_0">
                 manulite's
                </span>
                post
               </a>
               2 years ago, I wondered if he'd blogged today how his day would have been like?
               <br/>
               Would he still complain about power cuts now that it has become a way of life?
               <br/>
               Would he tell you that he wakes up well before 6am just to have a chance to prepare breakfast and boil some water for bathing?
               <br/>
               Would he mention that electricity is never there between 6am and some
               <span class="blsp-spelling-corrected" id="SPELLING_ERROR_1">
                arbitrary
               </span>
               hour well after bedtime?
               <br/>
               Would he tell about the price cuts and how shops have become empty?
               <br/>
               Would he tell you that unless if you spend the whole day in some queue at some shop where rumors are saying that there's going to be chicken delivered, the only meat you can ever get, IF you get it at all, is pork sausages?
               <br/>
               Would he tell you that since the price freeze, bread, his
               <a href="http://manulite.blogspot.com/2006/04/hundrend-thousand-dollar-bread.html">
                hundred thousand dollar bread
               </a>
               , now costs around $25,000 (= $25,000,000 in his blogging days value.)  and has suddenly became scarce, and when available, the quality is atrocious?
               <br/>
               Would he mention that shops have become so empty you go in wanting to buy something, anything, and you come out with nothing?
               <br/>
               Would he mention the panic buying, people standing in queues to buy whatever it is that everyone is queuing for, not because they need it, but because "you never know"?
               <br/>
               Would he mention how unavailable our mobile networks have become since a call now costs around $500 a minute?
               <br/>
               Would he talk about fuel, or would there be no point going there?
               <br/>
               Would he tell you where he's sleeping now and how much he's paying for it?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               At the end of the day, when he'd finally look back on those days and say those were the dark days, what would he have said about these days we are now living? Can it ever get darker than dark?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Everyday, everyone says "it can't get any worse than this", but everyday it does. We are a nation in crisis. We have stretched ourselves beyond any limits, and its still not enough. Everyday we get more hungry, more poorer. We have tried to keep our hopes burning, but as it continues to get darker, the darkness now clouds the small flames of hope we still hold. And this is just one of the many cries from our beloved country. And all I'm asking is a warm hug, and an assuring whisper that says "Everything is going to be
               <span class="blsp-spelling-corrected" id="SPELLING_ERROR_2">
                OK
               </span>
               !", because right now that phrase is the most difficult of all phrases to believe.
              </p>

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://sahwira.blogspot.com/2006/09/broken-dreams.html">
               Broken Dreams
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              <p>
               <style type="text/css">
                !--   @page { size: 21.59cm 27.94cm; margin: 2cm }   P { margin-bottom: 0.21cm }  --
               </style>
               My daughter Chido isn't born yet. Well, I'm kinda of still sorting out the small issue of finding the right mother for her. But here I am already worried. I'm not sure I'll afford her even the most basic of needs that she'll grow up to require. When one is told that dreams come true, that all you need is to believe, one finds oneself believing that believing in dreams, works. But when one's dreams fail to come through, one finds oneself in confused state of mind, especially so when one had made serious effort to
               <i>
                work
               </i>
               <span style="font-style: normal;">
                towards one's dreams. And when one then looks in the world around him and see other people's dreams coming true, and seeing those who wished for less getting more, one gets a bit more depressed.
               </span>
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I could say close to half of my friends have left the country, if not more. Each time any of them calls, one the question they almost never miss to ask is what I'm still doing here. I've almost always told them that I'm holding on, that I'm hopeful things will get better. Things must get better, mustn't they? But each day erodes away part of that hope, with each slice of bread I can no longer afford, and each drop of milk I can no longer drink, I'm getting more and more worried. I will get married (hopefully), and I will have kids (God willing). I could swear that when I was growing up, I saw things turning out a little bit differently. Hopes, dreams, aspirations all told me a different story from what I ate for lunch this afternoon. I'm not sure my daughter will go to that school that she really likes. I'm not even sure she'll get a proper education at all. I have friends who are teachers, I know how they cry everyday, I just don't know how much they will be able to teach my lovely daughter.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Will she have the toys I could never have? Will she have a childhood she'll be proud of? Or will she wish her dad had left the country? By staying, am I making a bad decision for my kids? Have I already failed her before she's even born? I love Zimbabwe. Who doesn't love their own country? Its just that I get really frustrated because I now have this long list of dreams that have misfired. I'm starting to have blisters on my hands for holding onto this thin rope for too long. I cannot let go, I don't want to crash and burn. I could never rise from the ashes. I feel that if things continue the way they are, then my whole life, and that of my kids will have been robbed.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I did not have a childhood that I'd have wanted because my parents were poor. I vowed I'd make up for it as soon as I could take care of myself. I'd spoil me with plenty. I'd have my kids have that childhood I never had. I'm all grown up now, everyone's now passing all sorts of hints and comments to say that I should really now start thinking about settling down. And yet here I am, not very pleased with the life I'm living. Why did I grow up at a time when my country would break inflation records by such embarrassing margins? Why did I grow up to witness our country's banks running out cash, and people having to literally buy money? And these days, everyone's got at least one electrical appliance that is no longer working because of the new-way-of-life power cuts. We've got to a point where the water bills should really come with a fine print saying "Please note that Harare water is not safe for human consumption".
               <br/>
               <br/>
               All this has robbed me of my youth, and as a young adult, a part of my adulthood. What will I tell Chido when gets to that age where they ask all sorts of questions? Or will she be born when everything is so substandard that she will not be able to imagine that things could be better? Definitely everyone right now have lowered their standards, which is how we can still survive. If your bread weighs funny, you don't complain to the bakery, you just say "
               <span style="font-style: italic;">
                NdoZimbabwe yacho
               </span>
               " with resigned sigh that says, the state of things as they are right now, is exceptionally, painfully bad, and you have just given up. This isn't exactly a good feeling, by the way, the $250 (Revalued) to buy that bread didn't come that easily. But then what can one do?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               All I'm hoping for now, is that there exists a limit x, and that things cannot go bad beyond x, and that when x is reached, things will immediately get better, and that right now we are so close to x, so close that we can say, with the bits of hope we miraculously still have, its only a matter of time. There is no night so long that it will not end with dawn.
              </p>
