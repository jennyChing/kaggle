     <p>
      <b>
       Moorland
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       moor
      </b>
      is a type of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">
       habitat
      </a>
      found in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upland_(geology)" title="Upland (geology)">
       upland
      </a>
      areas in
      <a href="/wiki/Temperate_grasslands,_savannas,_and_shrublands" title="Temperate grasslands, savannas, and shrublands">
       temperate grasslands, savannas, and shrublands
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Montane_grasslands_and_shrublands" title="Montane grasslands and shrublands">
       montane grasslands and shrublands
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">
       biomes
      </a>
      , characterised by low-growing vegetation on
      <a href="/wiki/Soil_pH" title="Soil pH">
       acidic
      </a>
      soils. Moorland nowadays generally means uncultivated
      <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">
       hill
      </a>
      land (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">
       Dartmoor
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">
       South West England
      </a>
      ), but the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
       Old English
      </a>
      <i>
       mr
      </i>
      also refers to low-lying
      <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">
       wetlands
      </a>
      (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sedgemoor" title="Sedgemoor">
       Sedgemoor
      </a>
      , also SW England). It is closely related to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" title="Heath (habitat)">
       heath
      </a>
      although experts disagree on precisely what distinguishes the types of vegetation. Generally, moor refers to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_(geography)" title="Highland (geography)">
       highland
      </a>
      , high rainfall zones, whereas heath refers to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowland" title="Lowland">
       lowland
      </a>
      zones which are more likely to be the result of human activity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_1986_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rackham_1986-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Moorland habitats are most extensive in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropics" title="Neotropics">
       neotropics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
       tropical
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      but also occur in
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">
       northern
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">
       western Europe
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Australia" title="Northern Australia">
       Northern Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">
       Central Asia
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
       Indian subcontinent
      </a>
      . Most of the world's moorlands are very diverse
      <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">
       ecosystems
      </a>
      . In the extensive moorlands of the tropics
      <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">
       biodiversity
      </a>
      can be extremely high. Moorland also bears a relationship to
      <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">
       tundra
      </a>
      (where the subsoil is
      <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">
       permafrost
      </a>
      or permanently frozen soil),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_1986_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rackham_1986-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      appearing as the tundra retreats and inhabiting the area between the permafrost and the natural
      <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">
       tree
      </a>
      zone. The boundary between tundra and moorland constantly shifts with
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heather_moorland">
       Heather moorland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moorland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Heather moorland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heather_moorland.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="640" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fe/Heather_moorland.jpg/220px-Heather_moorland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fe/Heather_moorland.jpg/330px-Heather_moorland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fe/Heather_moorland.jpg/440px-Heather_moorland.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heather_moorland.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Heather moorland on the
        <a href="/wiki/North_York_Moors" title="North York Moors">
         North York Moors
        </a>
        mainly consisting of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calluna_vulgaris" title="Calluna vulgaris">
         Calluna vulgaris
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heathland" title="Heathland">
       Heathland
      </a>
      and moorland are the most extensive areas of semi-natural vegetation in the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
       British Isles
      </a>
      . The eastern British moorlands are similar to heaths but are differentiated by having a covering of
      <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">
       peat
      </a>
      . On western moors the peat layer may be several metres thick. Scottish muirs are generally heather moors, but will also have extensive covering of
      <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">
       grass
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotton-grass" title="Cotton-grass">
       cotton-grass
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">
       mosses
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">
       bracken
      </a>
      and under-shrubs such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crowberry" title="Crowberry">
       crowberry
      </a>
      , with the wetter moorland having
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphagnum_moss" title="Sphagnum moss">
       sphagnum moss
      </a>
      merging into
      <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">
       bog-land
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_1986_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rackham_1986-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is uncertainty about how many moors were created by human activity. Rackham writes that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollen_analysis" title="Pollen analysis">
       pollen analysis
      </a>
      shows that some moorland, such as in the islands and extreme north of Scotland, are clearly natural, never having had trees,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      whereas much of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennine" title="Pennine">
       Pennine
      </a>
      moorland area was forested in
      <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">
       Mesolithic
      </a>
      times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      How much the destruction of this forest was caused by climatic changes and how much by human activity is uncertain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_1986_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rackham_1986-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_ecology_of_moorland">
       The ecology of moorland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moorland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The ecology of moorland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A variety of distinct
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">
       habitat
      </a>
      types are found in different world regions of moorland. The wildlife and vegetation forms often lead to high
      <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">
       endemism
      </a>
      because of the severe soil and
      <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">
       microclimate
      </a>
      characteristics. For example, in England's
      <a href="/wiki/Exmoor" title="Exmoor">
       Exmoor
      </a>
      is found the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rare_horse_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rare horse breed (page does not exist)">
       rare horse breed
      </a>
      the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exmoor_Pony" title="Exmoor Pony">
       Exmoor Pony
      </a>
      , which has adapted to the harsh conditions of that environment.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Europe, the associated
      <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">
       fauna
      </a>
      consists of bird species such as
      <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">
       red grouse
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hen_harrier" title="Hen harrier">
       hen harrier
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">
       merlin
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasian_golden_plover" title="Eurasian golden plover">
       golden plover
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_curlew" title="Eurasian curlew">
       curlew
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skylark" title="Skylark">
       skylark
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Meadow_pipit" title="Meadow pipit">
       meadow pipit
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Whinchat" title="Whinchat">
       whinchat
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">
       ring ouzel
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Twite" title="Twite">
       twite
      </a>
      . Other species dominate in moorlands elsewhere. Reptiles are few due to the cooler conditions. In Europe, only the
      <a href="/wiki/Vipera_berus" title="Vipera berus">
       common viper
      </a>
      is frequent, though in other regions moorlands are commonly home to dozens of
      <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">
       reptile
      </a>
      species.
      <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">
       Amphibians
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">
       frogs
      </a>
      are well represented in moorlands. When moorland is
      <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">
       overgrazed
      </a>
      , woody vegetation is often lost, being replaced by coarse, unpalatable
      <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">
       grasses
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">
       bracken
      </a>
      , with a greatly reduced fauna.
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </a>

     <p>
      Some hill
      <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">
       sheep
      </a>
      breeds, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Blackface" title="Scottish Blackface">
       Scottish Blackface
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lonk_(sheep)" title="Lonk (sheep)">
       Lonk
      </a>
      , thrive on the austere conditions of
      <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">
       heather
      </a>
      moors.

       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Management">
       Management
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moorland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Management">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Burning of moorland has been practiced for a number of reasons, for example when
      <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">
       grazing
      </a>
      is insufficient to control growth. This is recorded in Britain in the fourteenth century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Uncontrolled burning frequently caused (and causes) problems, and was forbidden by
      <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">
       statute
      </a>
      in 1607. With the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">
       sheep
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">
       grouse
      </a>
      management in the nineteenth century it again became common practice. Heather is burnt at about 10 or 12 years old when it will regenerate easily. Left longer, the woodier stems will burn more aggressively and will hinder regrowth. Burning of moorland vegetation needs to be very carefully controlled as the peat itself can catch fire, and this can be difficult if not impossible to extinguish. In addition, uncontrolled burning of heather can promote alternative
      <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">
       bracken
      </a>
      and rough grass growth which ultimately produces poorer grazing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result burning is now a controversial practice; Rackham calls it second-best land management.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_1986_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rackham_1986-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Mechanical cutting of the heather has been used in Europe, but it is important for the material to be removed to avoid smothering regrowth. If heather and other vegetation is left for too long, a large volume of dry and combustible material builds up. This may result in a
      <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">
       wildfire
      </a>
      burning out a large area, although it has been found that heather seeds germinate better if subject to the brief heat of controlled burning.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Moorland_in_literature">
       Moorland in literature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moorland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Moorland in literature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; width:99%; max-width:908px;">
      <div class="thumbinner">
       <div class="overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg" title="Dartmoor in winter covered in snow. Several tors top the sparsely vegetated hills.">
         <img alt="" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="7500" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg/900px-Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg/1350px-Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg/1800px-Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg 2x" width="900"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Saddle_tor_to_Hey_tor_pano.jpg" title="File:Saddle tor to Hey tor pano.jpg">
         </a>
        </div>
        Dartmoor in winter covered in snow. Several
        <a href="/wiki/Tor_(rock_formation)" title="Tor (rock formation)">
         tors
        </a>
        top the sparsely vegetated hills.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The development of a sensitivity to nature and one's physical surroundings grew with the rise of interest in landscape painting, and particularly the works of artists that favoured wide and deep prospects, and rugged scenery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To the English Romantic imagination, moorlands fitted this image perfectly, enhancing the emotional impact of the story by placing it within a heightened and evocative landscape. Moorland forms the setting of various works of late Romantic English literature, ranging from the
      <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">
       Yorkshire
      </a>
      moorland in
      <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Bront">
       Emily Bront
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights" title="Wuthering Heights">
        Wuthering Heights
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Garden" title="The Secret Garden">
        The Secret Garden
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Frances_Hodgson_Burnett" title="Frances Hodgson Burnett">
       Frances Hodgson Burnett
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">
       Dartmoor
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">
       Arthur Conan Doyle
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">
       Holmesian
      </a>
      mystery
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Hound_of_the_Baskervilles" title="The Hound of the Baskervilles">
        The Hound of the Baskervilles
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Enid_Blyton" title="Enid Blyton">
       Enid Blyton's
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Famous_Five_series" title="Famous Five series">
       Famous Five series
      </a>
      featured the young protagonists adventuring across various moorlands where they confronted criminals or other individuals of interest. Such a setting enhanced the plot as the drama unfolded away from the functioning world where the children could solve their own problems and face greater danger. Moorland in the
      <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">
       Forest of Bowland
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">
       Lancashire
      </a>
      is the setting for Walter Bennett's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pendle_Witches" title="The Pendle Witches">
        The Pendle Witches
       </a>
      </i>
      , the true story of some of England's most infamous witch trials. In
      <a href="/wiki/Erin_Hunter" title="Erin Hunter">
       Erin Hunter
      </a>
      's famous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warriors_(book_series)" title="Warriors (book series)">
       Warriors
      </a>
      series, one of the four Clans, WindClan, lives in the moorland alone.
     </p>

      </a>

       Scottish Highlands

      <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">

        <span class="reference-text">
         <span class="citation web">
          <a class="external text" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/nature/habitats/Moorland" rel="nofollow">
           "Moorland"
          </a>
          .
          <i>
           BBC Nature
          </i>
          . BBC. October 2014
          <span class="reference-accessdate">
           . Retrieved
           <span class="nowrap">
            3 April
           </span>
           2015
          </span>
          .
          <q>
           Moorlands are upland areas with acidic, low-nutrient and often water-logged soils. In their cold, windy and wet conditions colourful heather plants dominate, growing on the deep peaty layers. These seas of pinks and purples are a haven for many small mammals and insects, but fewer reptiles than on lowland heaths. British moorland may look like wilderness, but it is actually a man-made and highly managed landscape, with regular burning to allow new heather growth. Some 10-15% of the world's moorland is found in the UK, mainly in the north and west of the country.
