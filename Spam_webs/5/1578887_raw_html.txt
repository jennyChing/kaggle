     <p>
      <b>
       Neal Leon Cassady
      </b>
      (February 8, 1926 February 4, 1968) was a major figure of the
      <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">
       Beat Generation
      </a>
      of the 1950s and the
      <a href="/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">
       psychedelic
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">
       counterculture
      </a>
      movements of the 1960s. He was prominently featured as himself in the original "scroll" (first draft) version of
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">
       Jack Kerouac
      </a>
      's novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">
        On the Road
       </a>
      </i>
      . He also served as the model for the character Dean Moriarty in the 1957 version of the novel. In many of Kerouac's later books, Cassady is represented by the character Cody Pomeray.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">
       Early years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Cassady was born to Maude Jean (Scheuer) and Neal Marshall Cassady in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_Utah" title="Salt Lake City, Utah">
       Salt Lake City, Utah
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His mother died when he was ten, and he was raised by his
      <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">
       alcoholic
      </a>
      father in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">
       Denver, Colorado
      </a>
      . Cassady spent much of his youth either living on the streets of
      <a href="/wiki/Skid_row" title="Skid row">
       skid row
      </a>
      with his father or in
      <a href="/wiki/Reform_school" title="Reform school">
       reform school
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As a youth, Cassady was repeatedly involved in petty crime. He was arrested for car theft when he was 14, for shoplifting and car theft when he was 15, and for car theft and
      <a href="/wiki/Fence_(criminal)" title="Fence (criminal)">
       fencing
      </a>
      when he was 16.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1941, the 15-year-old Cassady met
      <a href="/wiki/Justin_W._Brierly" title="Justin W. Brierly">
       Justin W. Brierly
      </a>
      , a prominent Denver educator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brierly was well known as a mentor of promising young men and was impressed by Cassady's intelligence. Over the next few years, Brierly took an active role in Cassady's life. Brierly helped admit Cassady to
      <a href="/wiki/East_High_School_(Denver)" title="East High School (Denver)">
       East High School
      </a>
      where he taught Cassady as a student, encouraged and supervised his reading, and found employment for him. Cassady continued his criminal activities, however, and was repeatedly arrested from 1942 to 1944; on at least one of these occasions, he was released by law enforcement into Brierly's safekeeping. In June 1944, Cassady was arrested for
      <a href="/wiki/Possession_of_stolen_goods" title="Possession of stolen goods">
       possession of stolen goods
      </a>
      , and served eleven months of a one-year prison sentence. He and Brierly actively exchanged letters during this period, even through Cassady's intermittent incarcerations; this correspondence represents Cassady's earliest surviving letters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brierly, a closeted homosexual, is also believed to have been responsible for Cassady's first homosexual experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In October 1945, after being released from prison, Cassady married the sixteen-year-old LuAnne Henderson.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1946, Cassady and his wife traveled to
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      to visit their friend, Hal Chase, another protege of Brierly's. It was while visiting Chase at
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia University
      </a>
      that Cassady met Jack Kerouac and
      <a href="/wiki/Allen_Ginsberg" title="Allen Ginsberg">
       Allen Ginsberg
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although Cassady did not attend Columbia, he soon became friends with them and their acquaintances, some of whom later became members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">
       Beat Generation
      </a>
      . While in New York, Cassady persuaded Kerouac to teach him to write fiction. Carolyn Cassady has stated that, "Neal, having been raised in the slums of Denver amongst the world's lost men, [was] determined to make more of himself, to become somebody, to be worthy and respected. His genius mind absorbed every book he could find, whether literature, philosophy or science. Jack had had a formal education, which Neal envied, but intellectually he was more than a match for Jack, and they enjoyed long discussions on every subject."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Cassady" title="Carolyn Cassady">
       Carolyn Robinson
      </a>
      met Cassady in 1946 while she worked in Denver, Colorado as a teaching assistant. Carolyn would leave the Beat group shortly after walking in on Neal, Allen Ginsberg, and Neal's wife, LuAnne, in bed together. Five weeks after her departure, Neal got an annulment from LuAnne and married Carolyn on April 1, 1948. Carolyn Cassady's book,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Off_the_Road" title="Off the Road">
        Off the Road
       </a>
      </i>
      , details her marriage to Cassady and recalls him as, "the archetype of the American Man."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Academic_Search_Premier_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Academic_Search_Premier-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During this period, Cassady worked for the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Transportation_Company" title="Southern Pacific Transportation Company">
       Southern Pacific Railroad
      </a>
      and kept in touch with his "beat" acquaintances, even as they became increasingly different philosophically.
     </p>
     <p>
      The couple eventually had three children and settled down in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranch_house" title="Ranch house">
       ranch house
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Monte_Sereno,_California" title="Monte Sereno, California">
       Monte Sereno, California
      </a>
      , 50 miles south of
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      , where Kerouac and Ginsberg sometimes visited.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OffTR_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OffTR-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This home, built in 1954 with money from a settlement from Southern Pacific Railroad for a train-related accident, was demolished in August 1997.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1950, Cassady entered into a
      <a href="/wiki/Bigamy" title="Bigamy">
       bigamous
      </a>
      marriage with Diane Hansen, a young model who was pregnant with his child, and he fathered one son, Curtis Hansen.
     </p>
     <p>
      It has also been reported that Cassady had a sexual relationship with Ginsberg, which lasted off and on for the next twenty years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cassady traveled cross-country with both Kerouac and Ginsberg on multiple occasions, including the trip that is documented in Kerouac's
      <i>
       On the Road
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_drugs">
       Role of drugs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role of drugs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following an arrest in 1958 for offering to share a small amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">
       marijuana
      </a>
      with an
      <a href="/wiki/Undercover_operation" title="Undercover operation">
       undercover
      </a>
      agent at a San Francisco
      <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">
       nightclub
      </a>
      , Cassady served a two-year sentence at California's
      <a href="/wiki/San_Quentin_State_Prison" title="San Quentin State Prison">
       San Quentin State Prison
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marin_County" title="Marin County">
       Marin County
      </a>
      . After his release in June 1960, he struggled to meet family obligations, and Carolyn divorced him when his parole period expired in 1963. Carolyn stated that she was looking to remove the burden of supporting a family from Cassady, but "this was a mistake and removed the last pillar of his self-esteem."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the divorce, Cassady shared an apartment with Allen Ginsberg and
      <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">
       Beat
      </a>
      poet
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Plymell" title="Charles Plymell">
       Charles Plymell
      </a>
      in 1963 at 1403 Gough Street, San Francisco.
     </p>
     <p>
      Cassady first met author
      <a href="/wiki/Ken_Kesey" title="Ken Kesey">
       Ken Kesey
      </a>
      during the summer of 1962; he eventually became one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Merry_Pranksters" title="Merry Pranksters">
       Merry Pranksters
      </a>
      , a group who formed around Kesey in 1964 who were vocal proponents of the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">
       psychedelic drugs
      </a>
      . During 1964, Cassady served as the main driver of the bus named "
      <a href="/wiki/Further_(bus)" title="Further (bus)">
       Furthur
      </a>
      " on the iconic first half of the journey from San Francisco to New York, which was immortalized by
      <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">
       Tom Wolfe
      </a>
      's book,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Kool-Aid_Acid_Test" title="The Electric Kool-Aid Acid Test">
        The Electric Kool-Aid Acid Test
       </a>
      </i>
      . Cassady appears at length in a
      <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">
       documentary film
      </a>
      about the Merry Pranksters and their cross-country trip,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Magic_Trip" title="Magic Trip">
        Magic Trip
       </a>
      </i>
      , directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Alex_Gibney" title="Alex Gibney">
       Alex Gibney
      </a>
      , released on 5 August 2011.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Travels_and_death">
       Travels and death
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Travels and death">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In January 1967, Cassady traveled to
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      with fellow prankster George "Barely Visible" Walker and Cassady's longtime girlfriend Anne Murphy. In a beachside house just south of
      <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Vallarta" title="Puerto Vallarta">
       Puerto Vallarta
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">
       Jalisco
      </a>
      , they were joined by Barbara Wilson and Walter Cox. All-night storytelling, speed drives in Walker's
      <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Elan" title="Lotus Elan">
       Lotus Elan
      </a>
      , and the use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">
       LSD
      </a>
      made for a classic Cassady performance "like a trained bear," Carolyn Cassady once said. Cassady was beloved for his ability to inspire others to love life.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the next year, Cassady's life became less stable and the pace of his travels became more frenetic. He left Mexico in May, traveling to San Francisco, Denver, New York City, and points in-between. Cassady then returned to Mexico in September and October (stopping in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">
       San Antonio
      </a>
      , on the way to visit his oldest daughter who had just given birth to his first grandchild), visited Ken Kesey's
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      farm in December, and spent the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Year" title="New Year">
       New Year
      </a>
      with Carolyn at a friend's house near San Francisco. Finally, in late January 1968, Cassady returned to Mexico once again.
     </p>
     <p>
      On February 3, 1968, Cassady attended a wedding party in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Allende" title="San Miguel de Allende">
       San Miguel de Allende
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Guanajuato" title="Guanajuato">
       Guanajuato
      </a>
      , Mexico. After the party,
      <span id="occasion_of_death">
      </span>
      he went walking along a railroad track to reach the next town, but passed out in the cold and rainy night wearing nothing but a T-shirt and jeans. In the morning, he was found in a
      <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">
       coma
      </a>
      by the tracks, reportedly by Dr.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
         <span title="You can help  Scholar, or M.D.? Also provide source -- if only to clarify whether he had that degree when found him, rather than having it now (December 2014)">
          vague
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Anton Black, later a professor at
      <a href="/wiki/El_Paso_Community_College" title="El Paso Community College">
       El Paso Community College
      </a>
      , who carried Cassady over his shoulders to the local post office building. Cassady was then transported to the closest hospital where he died a few hours later on February 4, four days short of his forty-second birthday.
     </p>
     <p>
      The exact cause of Cassady's death remains uncertain. Those who attended the wedding party confirm that he took an unknown quantity of
      <a href="/wiki/Secobarbital" title="Secobarbital">
       secobarbital
      </a>
      , a powerful
      <a href="/wiki/Barbiturate" title="Barbiturate">
       barbiturate
      </a>
      sold under the brand name of Seconal. The physician who performed the
      <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">
       autopsy
      </a>
      wrote simply, "general congestion in all systems." When interviewed later, the physician stated that he was unable to give an accurate report because Cassady was a foreigner and there were drugs involved. "Exposure" is commonly cited as his cause of death, although his widow believes he may have died of
      <a href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">
       renal failure
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">
       In literature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In literature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The late
      <a href="/wiki/Ken_Kesey" title="Ken Kesey">
       Ken Kesey
      </a>
      wrote a fictional account of Cassady's death in a
      <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">
       short story
      </a>
      named "The Day After Superman Died", where Cassady is portrayed as mumbling about the number of
      <a href="#occasion_of_death">
       railroad ties
      </a>
      he had counted on the line (64,928) as his last words before dying. It was published as a part of Kesey's 1986 collection
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Demon_Box_(book)" title="Demon Box (book)">
        Demon Box
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Cassady's autobiographical novel
      <i>
       The First Third
      </i>
      , was published in 1971, three years after his death. Most of his complete surviving letters were published in
      <i>
       Grace Beats Karma: Letters from Prison
      </i>
      (Blast, 1993) and
      <i>
       Neal Cassady: Collected Letters, 1944-1967
      </i>
      (Penguin, 2007).
      <span id="Joan_Anderson">
      </span>
      The legendary
      <i>
       Joan Anderson letter
      </i>
      has been reported to have been recently found, according to an AP story dated Nov 24, 2014. The 18-page letter, which is said to have substantially inspired Kerouac's subsequent writing style, was to be auctioned on Dec 17, 2014, however a legal dispute over ownership prevented the auction going ahead. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">
       National Public Radio
      </a>
      website story includes an
      <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">
       Associated Press
      </a>
      photograph of 30 lines of the similar "stream of consciousness," annotated first draft of Kerouac's
      <i>
       On the Road
      </i>
      for comparison.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cassady was the model for the character Dean Moriarty in Kerouac's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">
        On the Road
       </a>
      </i>

      <i>
       On the Road
      </i>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      <i>
       On The Road
      </i>

      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">

       Howl

     <p>
      <span id="Writing_Style_and_Influence">
      </span>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_style_and_influence">
       Writing style and influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neal_Cassady&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Writing style and influence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Cassady is credited with helping Kerouac break with his
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolfe" title="Thomas Wolfe">
       Thomas Wolfe
      </a>
      -influenced sentimental style, as seen in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Town_and_the_City" title="The Town and the City">
        The Town and the City
       </a>
      </i>
      . After reading Cassady's letters, Kerouac was inspired to write his story in the style that Cassady spoke, "in a rush of mad ecstasy, without self-consciousness or mental hesitation."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This fluid writing style, reading more like a stream of consciousness or hypermanic rapid-fire conversation than written prose, is best demonstrated within Cassady's letters to family and friends. In a letter to Keroauc from 1953, Cassady begins with the following fervent sentence;
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "Well its about time you wrote, I was fearing you farted out on top that mean mountain or slid under while pissing in Pismo, beach of flowers, food and foolishness, but I knew the fear was ill-founded for balancing it in my thoughts of you, much stronger and valid if you werent dead, was a realization of the experiences you would be having down there, rail, home, and the most important, climate, by a remembrance of my own feelings and thoughts (former low, or more exactly, nostalgic and unreal; latter hi) as, for example, I too seemed to spend time looking out upper floor windows at sparse, especially night times, traffic in femalesold or young."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-22">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         22
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      "On the Road" became a sensation by capturing Cassady's voice and Kerouac discovered a unique style of his own that he called "spontaneous prose", a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_writing" title="Stream of consciousness writing">
       stream of consciousness
      </a>
      prose form.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Unfortunately, Cassadys own written work was never formally published in his lifetime, and he left behind only a half written manuscript and a number of personal letters. Cassady admitted to Kerouac in a letter from 1948, My prose has no individual style as such, but is rather an unspoken and still unexpressed groping toward the personal. There is something there that wants to come out; something of my own that must be said. Yet, perhaps, words are not the way for me.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
