       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The Colors of Thought
       </h1>
       <div class="entry-meta">
        <span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">
         Posted on
        </span>
        <a href="https://www.thunderbolts.info/wp/2015/01/28/the-colors-of-thought/" rel="bookmark" title="00:09">
         <span class="entry-date">
          January 28, 2015
         </span>
        </a>
        <span class="meta-sep">
         by
        </span>
        <span class="author vcard">
         <a class="url fn n" href="https://www.thunderbolts.info/wp/author/mel-acheson/" title="View all posts by Mel Acheson">
          Mel Acheson
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <!-- .entry-meta -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="pf-content">
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_17004" style="width: 560px">
          <a href="https://www.thunderbolts.info/wp/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/B5J38jYCEAAczL0.jpg">
           <img alt="B5J38jYCEAAczL0" class="wp-image-17004 size-large" height="531" src="https://www.thunderbolts.info/wp/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/B5J38jYCEAAczL0-550x531.jpg" width="550"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Credit: ESO
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Jan 28, 2015
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           M47 is an open cluster of fewer-than-usual stars
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The contrasting colors of blue and red have an aesthetic appeal. One can even detect a few orange and yellow stars. A person may glimpse an occasional green one, especially if that person fiddles with the RGB settings in Photoshop.
         </p>
         <p>
          Astronomers talk about the
          <a href="http://www.eso.org/public/news/eso1441/">
           colors
          </a>
          , too, but what they have in mind is not RGB settings; its max, the wavelength of maximum luminosity in a stars spectrum. They plug that number into an equation, such as Plancks, to get another number for temperature. This ties the color to the temperature, which we understand from experience: a yellow flame is usually hotter than a red one, and a blue one is hotter than both. The temperature, in turn, is tied to the theory of stellar evolution.
         </p>
         <p>
          Alternatively, astronomers can measure the total luminosity of a star and use Stefans Law to get a temperature. Plancks equation (which gave rise to quantum physics), as well as Wiens Law and Stefans Law, are all derived from Kirchhoffs Law, which Kirchhoff proclaimed in the 19th century after conducting many experiments with the light emitted from cavities.
         </p>
         <p>
          To a man (and the few women involved), they ignore the assumptions underneath it all. As it turns out, this is fatal. If they were doctors, all their patients would have died. Lets turn over Stefans Law and see whats underneath it. The law states:
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <span style="font-size: 12pt;">
            = T
           <sup>
            4
           </sup>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          where  is the total emissivity, T is the temperature, and  is a cocktail of constants. We can see the how much light the star is emittingand we can feel the Tor we could if we could get close enough to stick our finger into the star. But what about that cocktail of constants?
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <span style="font-size: 12pt;">
            = (2
           <sup>
            5
           </sup>
           k
           <sup>
            4
           </sup>
           )/(15
           <sup>
            3
           </sup>
           c
           <sup>
            2
           </sup>
           )
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          where  is the ratio of the circumference to the diameter of an Euclidian circle, k is the Boltzmann constant, is Plancks constant, and c is the speed of light.
         </p>
         <p>
          Theyre empirical. They were measured. In the real world. The one that goes bump in the night.
         </p>
         <p>
          So one might ask, how did they come to cross over into the conceptual world? Two of them came from Kirchhoffs experiments in the 19th centuryor rather from his assumptions about his experiments. He measured the spectral distribution of emission from cavities. He found that it varied with the temperature. To clarify the relationship, he sprinkled soot into the cavities. He concluded that the radiation was independent of the form and nature of the bodies, being determined by T alone.
         </p>
         <p>
          Later, Stefan and Wien and Planck refined the relationship, and the refinements produced the k and  that appear in all from experiments with the emission from cavities, all of which were sprinkled with soot. Without the soot, the relationship wasnt as clear. It was actually far from clear. In fact, what was clear was that every material had a unique relationship. Kirchhoffs Law was not universal, it was merely the law for soot. It would be better named Kirchhoffs Error.
         </p>
         <p>
          This could make one suspicious of . What about the other constants? c, it turns out, seems to have been decreasing since it was first measured, and by an amount greater than the precision of the measurements: it cant be dismissed as experimental error.  is surely sacrosanct. But if you draw a circle around an egg and measure the diameter over the top, the ratio is closer to 1 or 2 than 3.14. You say: I must measure across the flat plane of the circle. I say: in the real world, the one that goes bump in the night, show me a flat egg.
         </p>
         <p>
          The interesting question at the bottom of the hole weve dug under Stefans Law is just this: What is the relationship between the ideal and the real? How often do we mistake the ideal for the realfor example, with
          <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3Hstum3U2zw&amp;list=PLwOAYhBuU3UfklHQ8Rb1sOzkSAw50fCfc&amp;index=2">
           Kirchhoffs Error
          </a>
          , and black holes, and dark matter? How are we to understand this tension between the ideal and the real?
         </p>
         <p>
          The ideal is a world we can be sure of! k! ! c!known to a dozen or more decimal places. But its a ghost world, insubstantial (meaning, not composed of substance, immaterial). And the world we live in, that we bump into, that knocks against our shins as we stumble around in the dark, that one tells us only that some mysterious thing hurts. Between the pain and the ghost, there is only thought.
         </p>
         <p>
          So what shall we think? Theres nothing of confidence left of Stefans Law except 2/15and now Im suspicious of that.
         </p>
         <p>
          Mel Acheson
         </p>
