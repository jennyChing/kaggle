        <h1 class="entry-title">
         The Scrappy Gnome Hat Pattern
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         Clearly my presence here has been kind of spotty lately. I thought about doing a where Ive been type post, but then I figured Id never get around to it, so hows about a cute free knitting pattern instead?
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <img alt="1" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3264" height="643" src="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/1-795x1023.jpg" title="1" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         These two are just silly!
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <img alt="2" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3265" height="338" src="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/2-1024x694.jpg" title="2" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         Though the silly hats arent actually for them, they are for these little guys
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <img alt="3" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3266" height="704" src="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/3-727x1024.jpg" title="3" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="4" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3267" height="362" src="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/4-1024x743.jpg" title="4" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         This pattern is pretty much as easy as knitting gets. Its the perfect keeping-your-hands-busy-while-doing-something-else kind of knitting, in two sizes, that as it turns out, fit just about everyone. It also happens to be the ideal stash busting project. In fact, thats the whole idea behind it! It also knits up extremely quick. Like, need a gift for tomorrow? This is the way to go, quick.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         So this is what youre going to need:
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         A set of size 13 double point needles
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         and
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         A bag, basket, pile, whatever (!) of scrap yarns, both worsted and bulky weight, in colors that complement each other.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         thats it.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="6" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3269" height="569" src="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/6-898x1023.jpg" title="6" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <strong>
          Scrappy Gnome Hat
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          For the pattern in an easy to print PDF version, click
          <a href="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/scrappy-gnome-hat.pdf" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/scrappy-gnome-hat.pdf']);">
           here
          </a>
          .
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         The hat comes in two sizes. The baby/toddler size is first, fitting around 6 months-2.5 years (Its shown here on both a one year old and a two year old), with directions for the kid/adult version following in parenthesis (shown here on a 3 year old, a 5 year old and a 31 year old). The entire hat is worked in the round while holding both a strand of worsted and a strand of bulky yarn. As you run out of one yarn, replace it with another like weight yarn. The two different colored yarns are what give the hat its heathered appearance. Switching one yarn at a time means that one section kind of blends and fades into the next.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         CO 42 (52) sts, pm, join in the round
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 3 rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         Round 4: K2tog, K 20 (25), K2tog, K to end
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         Continue even until hat measures 4 3/4 (5 1/2)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K8, K2tog* 4 times (*K8, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 3 (4) rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K7, K2tog* 4 times (*K7, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 3 (4) rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K6, K2tog* 4 times (*K6, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K3 (4) rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K5, K2tog* 4 times (*K5, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 3 (4) rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K4, K2tog* 4 times (*K4, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 3 (4) rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K3, K2tog* 4 times (*K3, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 3 (4) rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         On the next round: *K2, K2tog* 4 times (*K2, K2tog* 5 times)
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 1 round
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K1, K2tog around
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          Larger size only:
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          (K 1 round
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          K2tog around)
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         Both sizes:
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K 5 rounds
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         K2tog around
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         switch to knitting a i-cord for around 6 repeats
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         bind off and work in ends
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         If you knit it, let me know how it turns out! And, as always, Id love to see pictures!
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         You can check out other peoples gnome hats and post your own
         <a href="http://www.ravelry.com/patterns/library/the-scrappy-gnome-hat" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.ravelry.com/patterns/library/the-scrappy-gnome-hat']);">
          here
         </a>
         on Ravelry.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="7" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3268" height="380" src="http://02f0d6b.netsolhost.com/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/7-1023x779.jpg" title="7" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          Hee-hee! He looks like a little garden gnome, doesnt he?
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          And who knows? Maybe someday Ill even get around to posting about that sweater.
         </em>
        </p>

            When a pattern like this says to continue even or work even, it means to keep working in the set pattern without any increases or decreases until the point stated.  So, in this case it means that you would continue working in stockinette stitch (knitting every round) until the hat measures 4 3/4 (for the small version) or 5 1/2 (for the larger version).  I hope this helps!  If you have any further questions, feel free to ask away!

          <section class="comment-content comment">
           <p>
            Hi Hannah,
            <br/>
            The only dumb knitting questions are the ones that dont get asked and result in a huge waste of time!  You will need what are called double point needles for this pattern.  You will cast on to several of these (probably 4 will be most comfortable for you) and then you will bring all of the needles in to a circle (sort of, it will have sides obviously because the needles are straight, so really a triangle for 3 needles a square for 4, etc) and start knitting with the first stitch that you cast so that everything is now connected.  Then you just keep working your way around the circle.  Stockinette Stitch, which is used for this pattern is just knitting every stitch.  This can be a bit confusing because when you knit flat, as you have been, you knit one row and then purl a row to create the same pattern.  If you are able to, I suggest checking out a couple of youtube videos on knitting in the round.  I think its much easier to understand if you can actually see someone doing it.  And feel free to write again if you have any questions.  Im happy to help.
            <br/>
            ~Melody
           </p>
