          9 Habits Of Bosses Who Build Exceptional Relationships
         </li>
        </ul>
       </div>
       <div class="postentry" id="InterviewCol">
        <div id="post_date">
         February 18, 2014
        </div>
        <div id="post_title">
         9 Habits Of Bosses Who Build Exceptional Relationships
        </div>
        <div id="socialshares">
         <div class="socialsharetotal">
         </div>
         <div class="subsmall shares">
         </div>
         <div class="social-share-links2" data-share-networks="facebook twitter googleplus linkedin">
         </div>
        </div>
        <div id="content_area">
         <img class="topimg" src="http://www.ceo.com.s3.amazonaws.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/gratitude-426x242.png"/>
         <p>
          Professional success is important to everyone, but still, success in business and in life means different things to different people  as well it should.
         </p>
         <p>
          But one fact is universal. Real success, the kind that exists on multiple levels, is impossible without building great relationships. Real success is impossible unless you treat other people with kindness, regard and respect.
         </p>
         <p>
          (After all, you can be a rich jerk but you will also be a lonely jerk.)
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats why leaders who build extraordinary business relationships do the following:
         </p>
         <h2>
          1. They take the hit.
         </h2>
         <p>
          A customer gets mad. A vendor complains about poor service. A mutual friend feels slighted.
         </p>
         <p>
          Sometimes, whatever the issue and regardless of who is actually at fault, some bosses step in and take the hit. Theyre willing to accept the criticism or abuse because they know they can handle it  and they know that maybe the person actually at fault cannot.
         </p>
         <p>
          Few acts are more selfless than taking the undeserved hit. And few acts better cement a relationship, professional or personal.
         </p>
         <h2>
          2. They step in without being asked.
         </h2>
         <p>
          Its easy to help when youre asked. Most people will.
         </p>
         <p>
          Very few people offer help before they have been asked, even though most of the time that is when a little help makes the greatest impact.
         </p>
         <p>
          Bosses who build extraordinary relationships pay close attention so they can tell when their employees are struggling. Then they offer to help, but not in a general, Is there something I can do to help you? way.
         </p>
         <p>
          Instead they come up with specific ways to help. That way they can push past the reflexive, No, Im OK objections. And they can roll up their sleeves and make a difference.
         </p>
         <p>
          Not just because they want to build a better relationship  although that is certainly a result  but simply because they care.
         </p>
         <h2>
          3. They answer the unasked question.
         </h2>
         <p>
          Where relationships are concerned, face value is usually without value. Often people will ask a different question than the one they really want answered.
         </p>
         <p>
          An employee might ask whether he should teach a class at a local college; what he really wants to talk about is how you view his career potential. A partner might ask how you felt about the idea he presented during the last board meeting; what he really wants to talk about is his diminished role in running the company. An employee might ask how you built a successful business; instead of kissing up he might be looking for some advice  and encouragement  to help him follow his own dreams.
         </p>
         <p>
          Behind many simple questions is often a larger question that goes unasked. Bosses who build great relationships think about what lies underneath so they can answer that question, too.
         </p>
         <h2>
          4. They know when to dial it back.
         </h2>
         <p>
          Outgoing and charismatic people are a lot of fun until they arent. When a major challenge pops up or a situation gets stressful, still, some people cant stop expressing their individuality. (Admit it: You know at least one person so in love with his personality he can
          <em>
           never
          </em>
          dial it back.)
         </p>
         <p>
          Bosses who build great relationships know when to have fun and when to be serious, when to be over the top and when to be invisible, and when to take charge and when to follow.
         </p>
         <p>
          Great relationships are multifaceted and therefore require multifaceted people willing to adapt to the situation  and to the people in that situation.
         </p>
         <h2>
          5. They show they think of others.
         </h2>
         <p>
          Bosses who build great relationships dont just
          <em>
           think
          </em>
          about other people. They
          <em>
           act
          </em>
          on those thoughts.
         </p>
         <p>
          An easy way is to give unexpected praise. Everyone loves unexpected praise  its like getting flowers not because its Valentines Day, but just because. Praise helps other people feel better about themselves and lets them know youre thinking about them (which is flattering in itself.)
         </p>
         <p>
          Take a little time every day to do something nice for someone you know, not because youre expected to but simply because you can. When you do, your relationships improve dramatically.
         </p>
         <h2>
          6. They realize when they have acted poorly.
         </h2>
         <p>
          Most people apologize when their actions or words are called into question. Fewer apologize before they are asked  or even before anyone notices they should.
         </p>
         <p>
          Responsibility is a key building block of a great relationship. Bosses who take the blame, who say they are sorry and explain why they are sorry, who dont try to push any of the blame back on the other person those are the bosses everyone wants to work for because they instantly turn mistakes into bumps in the road rather than permanent roadblocks.
         </p>
         <h2>
          7. They give consistently and receive occasionally.
         </h2>
         <p>
          A great relationship is mutually beneficial. In business terms that means connecting with people who can be mentors, who can share information, who can help create other connections; in short, that means going into a relationship wanting
          <em>
           something
          </em>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The boss who builds great relationships doesnt think about what she wants; she starts by thinking about what she can give. She sees giving as the best way to establish a real relationship and a lasting connection. She approaches building relationships as if its all about the other person and not about her, and in the process builds relationships with people who follow the same approach.
         </p>
         <p>
          In time she, and they, make real connections.
         </p>
         <h2>
          8. They value the message by always valuing the messenger.
         </h2>
         <p>
          When people speak from a position of position of power or authority or fame, its tempting to place greater emphasis on their input, advice and ideas.
         </p>
         <p>
          We listen to Tony Hsieh. We listen to Norm Brodsky. We listen to Seth Godin.
         </p>
         <p>
          The guy who mows our lawn? Probably not so much.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats too bad. Smart people strip away the framing that comes with the source  whether positive or negative  and consider the information, advice or idea based solely on its merits.
         </p>
         <p>
          Bosses who build great relationships never automatically discount the message simply because they discount the messenger. They know good advice is good advice, regardless of where it comes from.
         </p>
         <p>
          And they know good people are good people, regardless of their perceived status.
         </p>
         <h2>
          9. They start small and they know sometimes its OK to stay small.
         </h2>
         <p>
          I sometimes wear a Reading Football Club sweatshirt. The checkout clerk at the grocery store noticed it one day and said, Oh, youre a Reading supporter? My team is Manchester United.
         </p>
         <p>
          Normally, since Im pretty shy, I would have just nodded and said something innocuous, but for some reason I said, You think Man U can beat Real Madrid next week?
         </p>
         <p>
          He gave me a huge smile and said, Oh yeah. Well crush them! (Too bad he was wrong.)
         </p>
         <p>
          Now whenever I see him he waves, often from across the store. I almost always walk over, say hi, and talk briefly about soccer.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats as far as our relationship is likely to go, and thats OK. For a couple of minutes we transcend the customer/employee relationship and become two people brightening each others day.
         </p>
         <p>
          And thats enough, because every relationship, however minor and possibly fleeting, has value.
         </p>
         <p>
          Bosses who build great relationships treat every one of their relationships that way. (And thats a lesson I definitely need to remember more often.)
         </p>
