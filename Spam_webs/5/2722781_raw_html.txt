                var crossdomain = (urls.badge_url.substr(0,4) == "http") ? true : false;

        <div class="entry-title">
         <h1>
          Mixed Mutts and Designer Crosses: Healthier Than Purebred Pets?
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="byline">
         By
         <a href="/our-pet-experts/dr-mary-fuller-bio">
          Dr. Mary Fuller
         </a>
         | Fri May 03 05:04:00 MST 2013
        </div>
        <div id="main-share">
         <ul class="clearfix" id="controls">
          <li class="first">
           <div id="fb-root">
           </div>
           <script src="http://connect.facebook.net/en_US/all.js#xfbml=1">
           </script>
           <fb:like action="like" font="" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/our-pet-experts/mixed-mutts-and-designer-crosses-healthier-than-purebred-pets" layout="button_count" send="false" show_faces="true" width="40">
           </fb:like>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a class="twitter" href="javascript:;" title="Share on Twitter">
            Twitter
           </a>
          </li>
          <li class="pin-it">
           <a class="pin-it-button" count-layout="none" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/" target="_new">
            <img border="0" src="//assets.pinterest.com/images/PinExt.png" title="Pin It"/>
           </a>
          </li>
          <li class="google">
           <g:plusone count="false" size="medium">
           </g:plusone>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a class="print" href="javascript:;" title="View Print Preview">
            Print
           </a>
          </li>
          <li class="last">
           <a class="email" href="javascript:;" title="Send to a friend">
            Email
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
        <div class="entry-content">
         <ins class="richtext">
          <style type="text/css">
           .cms-textAlign-left{text-align:left;}.cms-textAlign-center{text-align:center;}.cms-textAlign-right{text-align:right;}.cms-customHeadings-h2{font-size: 17px;
color: #AB483A;
margin-top: 15px;
padding: 0px;
line-height: 1.5;}.cms-customHeadings-h2a{font-size: 17px;
color: #000000;
margin-top: 15px;
padding: 0px;
line-height: 1.5;}
          </style>
          <div class="left-photo" style="width: 335px;">
           <img alt="Purebred and mixed breed dogs" height="285" src="http://s3.amazonaws.com/assets.prod.vetstreet.com/76/49/4f52b29049cbb5468e7bcc305740/purebred-v-mixed-breed.jpg" width="335"/>
           <div class="photo-credit">
            <div>
             Thinkstock
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           According to conventional wisdom, mixed-breed dogs and
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/cats/">
            cats
           </a>
           are healthier than their purebred counterparts. On the surface, it makes sense: Dip too often into a shallow gene pool and the pet is more likely to inherit health problems. So mixing things up between breeds should create healthier genetic hybrids, right?
          </p>
          <p>
           This genetic hodgepodge and its perceived health advantages has been a selling point for designer dogs, such as Labradoodles,
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/yorkipoo">
            Yorkipoos
           </a>
           and
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/puggle">
            Puggles
           </a>
           . And its one reason why breeders have been able to ask exorbitant prices.
          </p>
          <p>
           But its simply not what veterinarians are encountering in practice, according to a recent presentation at the
           <a href="http://www.wvc.org/" target="_blank">
            Western Veterinary Conference
           </a>
           by
           <a href="http://vet.tufts.edu/facpages/bell_j.html" target="_blank">
            Dr. Jerold Bell
           </a>
           , clinical associate professor at
           <a href="http://vet.tufts.edu/" target="_blank">
            Tufts Cummings School of Veterinary Medicine
           </a>
           and author of
           <em>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Veterinary-Medical-Guide-Dog-Breeds/dp/1591610028" target="_blank">
             Veterinary Medical Guide to Dog and Cat Breeds
            </a>
            .
           </em>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Mixed-Breed Dogs Arent Immune to Genetic Disorders
          </h2>
          <p>
           In a five-year study of veterinary cases at the
           <a href="http://www.vetmed.ucdavis.edu/index.cfm" target="_blank">
            University of California-Davis
           </a>
           , there was no difference between mixed-breed and purebred
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/">
            dogs
           </a>
           in the prevalence of common inherited disorders. Even designer-bred dogs were being seen with hereditary conditions that it was assumed crossbreeding would eliminate:
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/care/hip-dysplasia-in-pets" target="_self">
            hip dysplasia
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/care/epilepsy-and-seizures-in-pets" target="_self">
            epilepsy
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/category/sitecontent/Cancer-Care" target="_self">
            cancer
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/care/hypothyroidism-in-dogs" target="_blank">
            hypothyroidism
           </a>
           , eye disorders and more.
          </p>
          <p>
           Although mixed-breed
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/">
            dogs
           </a>
           were just as likely to inherit health problems, there were a few exceptions, according to the study, which will be published in a veterinary journal later this year. Some genetic disorders remained isolated to specific breeds.
          </p>
          <p>
           Still, the findings make sense: If you cross a
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/labrador-retriever" target="_self">
            Labrador Retriever
           </a>
           that has
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/care/hip-dysplasia-in-pets">
            hip dysplasia
           </a>
           with a
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/poodle" target="_self">
            Poodle
           </a>
           that has hip dysplasia, what do you get? Chances are, a
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/labradoodle">
            Labradoodle
           </a>
           with hip dysplasia. In addition to hip problems,
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/labradoodle">
            Labradoodles
           </a>
           are now being diagnosed with
           <a href="http://www.vetstreet.com/canine-hypoadrenocorticism-addisons-disease-in-dogs" target="_self">
            Addisons disease
           </a>
           (a deficiency in adrenal hormones) and elbow dysplasiatwo genetic disorders that are common to purebred Labradors and
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/dogs/poodle">
            Poodles
           </a>
           , according to Dr. Bell.
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, theres no way to control genetic disorders passed on by the accidental backyard mating or a tryst with the neighborhood
           <b>
           </b>
           Romeo. But it's possible to prevent many inherited conditions in pure- and cross-bred pets that are mated on purpose, says Dr. Bell.
          </p>
          <h2>
           How Can Breeders Help?
          </h2>
          <p>
           Before breeding, Dr. Bell recommends that all animals be tested for heritable conditions such as
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/care/hip-dysplasia-in-pets">
            hip dysplasia
           </a>
           , deafness and eye disorders, as determined by the national parent club for each breed. Breeders are encouraged to register the test results on a national health database, such as the
           <a href="http://www.caninehealthinfo.org/" target="_blank">
            Canine Health Information Center
           </a>
           (CHIC), sponsored by the
           <a href="http://www.akcchf.org/" target="_blank">
            American Kennel Club/Canine Health Foundation
           </a>
           and the
           <a href="http://www.offa.org/" target="_blank">
            Orthopedic Foundation for Animals
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Although a breeder can choose to keep test results private, open disclosures of results for breeding dogs and their close relatives can help identify potential risks. Breeders can search the database for a healthy mate that will produce the best offspring.
          </p>
          <h2>
           What Potential Pet Owners Can Do
          </h2>
          <p>
           Thinking about buying a puppy from a breeder? Then its a good idea to search the CHIC database at
           <a href="http://www.caninehealthinfo.org" target="_blank">
            caninehealthinfo.org
           </a>
           . The website provides a list of recommended tests for each breed. Potential owners can use this information to learn about inheritable conditions associated with each breed, so they can ask breeders for verification of health testing before they purchase a pet.
          </p>
          <p>
           In addition, each breeding dog that completes the tests is assigned a CHIC number, whether or not it passes all the tests. If the breeder has chosen to release the information, potential owners can view results for the parent dogs and relatives.
          </p>
          <p>
           Although there currently isnt a similar website for
           <a class="crosslinked cl-mapping-vetstreet cl-dictionary-vetstreet" href="http://www.vetstreet.com/cats/">
            cat
           </a>
           information, potential owners can view breed-related diseases on the Feline Advisory Board website at
           <a href="http://www.fabcats.org/breeders/inherited_disorders" target="_blank">
            fabcats.org
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, as more tests become available for inheritable conditions, few dogs and cats will have normal results for every test. But, according to Dr. Bell, prebreeding testing isnt as much about creating perfection as it is about increasing awareness of health risks and finding good breeding mates that will produce healthier offspring.
          </p>
