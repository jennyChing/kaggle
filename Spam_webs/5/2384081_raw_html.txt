     <div class="articleBody" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="An Asian arowana fish." src="/content/dam/images/3/h/r/b/d/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.114hj0.png/1412949600000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        An Asian arowana fish.
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       A woman's three-year battle with a government department has ended with a conviction, a fine and the death of a "lucky" $5000 fish.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Asian arowana, considered in some Asian cultures to bring good luck, was seized by federal environment department officers from a Mount Gravatt pet shop in November 2011.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was then euthanised after a veterinary officer using a scanner failed to detect a microchip, which is usually present in Asian arowanas farmed overseas.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was only after the fish was dead that the microchip was found.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       Authorities suspected that the native South-East Asian specimen was brought in from overseas because they were not aware of Asian arowanas being bred in captivity in Australia.
      </p>
      <p>
       Asian arowanas are considered threatened and are listed on the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species. Trading the animal is banned unless there are exceptional circumstances.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Australia, it is illegal to own an Asian arowana unless it is legally imported.
      </p>
      <p>
       When officers grilled then Pet City Mount Gravatt aquarium manager Kelly Hsu, she said the fish was purchased for $5000 from a local supplier named "Justin Smith" who bred the animals in Australia.
      </p>
      <p>
       However it later emerged that Mr Smith did not exist.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ms Hsu, a US citizen in her mid-30s who holds science and arts degrees, was charged with breaches of the Environmental Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act.
      </p>
      <p>
       She pleaded guilty to possessing an endangered animal in the Brisbane Magistrates Court in May this year. But the federal environment department, not wanting to let her off the hook, appealed
      </p>
      <p>
       Representatives argued the $500 fine and the lack of conviction was "manifestly inadequate".
      </p>
      <p>
       In his findings handed down on September 29, District Court Judge William Everson agreed, fining Ms Hsu $800 and recording a conviction.
      </p>
      <p>
       "The offence, whilst not overly serious, is far from trivial," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Judge Everson said the penalty needed to deter people "committing offences involving the commercial trade in endangered wildlife".
      </p>
