&lt;p&gt;These queries represent diverse fields of human activities, spanning &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=mathematics"&gt;mathematics&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=physics"&gt;physics&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=engineering"&gt;engineering&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=chemistry"&gt;chemistry&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=biology"&gt;biology&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=geography"&gt;geography&lt;/a&gt;, and some just-geek-fun questions. And despite their diversity, they all have something in common; they contain numbers: integers and reals. Some of the numbers are pure numbers in mathematics, some are counts, and many are quantifying the size, mass, age, et cetera of an object. Some numbers are pretty small, some are pretty large,and some are just one-digit integers.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;If one looks at many of the numbers that occur in the queries answered by Wolfram|Alpha, what kind of distribution and what regularities would these numbers have? One regularity is the distribution of the first digit of all numbers, the so-called &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Benford%27s+law"&gt;Benford&#8217;s law&lt;/a&gt;; an &lt;a href="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/2010/12/13/the-curious-case-of-benfords-law/" target="_self"&gt;earlier blog post&lt;/a&gt; discussed these.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;While in principle numbers of any scale exist, in daily life people only use a certain fraction of numbers in size, often between 10&lt;sup&gt;-10&lt;/sup&gt; and 10&lt;sup&gt;10&lt;/sup&gt; (from the &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=diameter+of+an+atom"&gt;diameter of an atom&lt;/a&gt; measured in meters to, say, the &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Apple+profit+2011"&gt;profit of Apple in 2011&lt;/a&gt; measured in US dollars). Occasionally one needs larger numbers, especially for scientific calculations. The &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=SI+prefixes"&gt;SI prefixes&lt;/a&gt; cover a range from 10&lt;sup&gt;-24&lt;/sup&gt; and 10&lt;sup&gt;24&lt;/sup&gt;, spanning 48 orders of magnitude (&lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=yotta%2Fyocto"&gt;yotta/yocto&lt;/a&gt;). Let&#8217;s have a look at the sizes of numbers that users actually used in Wolfram|Alpha queries. We start by looking at integers. As the sample set, I will use a list of the &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=2.5+billion"&gt;2.5 billion&lt;/a&gt; integers from the recent queries that Wolfram|Alpha did answer.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;&#8220;God made the integers; all else is the work of man&#8221; (according to &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Kronecker"&gt;Kronecker&lt;/a&gt;), so let&#8217;s see how man uses the integers. We intuitively expect there to be more integers as real numbers in user queries, as they more frequently occur in daily life situations, especially in the form of counting (e.g. &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=12+eggs"&gt;12 eggs&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=120+words&amp;a=*C.words-_*Unit-"&gt;120 words&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=2000+people"&gt;2,000 people&lt;/a&gt;). Here is a plot of how many times a number between &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0+to+120"&gt;0 and 120&lt;/a&gt; occurred. We use a logarithmic plot, as the first few integers are substantially more common than even two-digit integers. &lt;p&gt;The last image shows at least three characteristic features:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ol&gt;
&lt;li&gt;A power-law-like-decrease in the probability &lt;em&gt;p&lt;/em&gt; to find an integer &lt;em&gt;n&lt;/em&gt;: &lt;em&gt;p&lt;/em&gt;(&lt;em&gt;n&lt;/em&gt;)~(&lt;em&gt;n&lt;/em&gt;&lt;em&gt;&lt;sup&gt;&#945;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/em&gt;) where in average &lt;em&gt;&#945;&lt;/em&gt; &#8776; -1.6. (Some care has to be taken to extract the exponent from noisy data that are conjectured to obey a power law; see &lt;a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/0706.1062" target="_blank"&gt;Clauset et al&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1208.3524" target="_blank"&gt;Virkar/Clauset&lt;/a&gt;, and &lt;a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1209.1270" target="_blank"&gt;Corral et al&lt;/a&gt; for details.) The appearance of a &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Zipf+distribution"&gt;Zipf law&lt;/a&gt; is not unexpected, as it often appears in many settings where data span many orders of magnitude. (The strictly decreasing lower envelope of the occurrences has a slope of &lt;em&gt;&#945;&lt;/em&gt; = -1.75).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;A quite fractal distribution of the occurrences. Especially for larger numbers, neighboring integers often have a substantially different number of occurrences (sometimes up to six orders of magnitude).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The peak-like structure near &lt;i&gt;n&lt;/i&gt; &#8776; 2000&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ol&gt;

Compared with the use of, say, 2012 in a mathematical calculation, its use in a date is much more common. Zooming in shows this clearly. Recent years (&lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=2010+to+2012&amp;a=*MC.~-_*PseudoTokenYear-"&gt;2010 to 2012&lt;/a&gt;) are, not unexpectedly, most common (the red line indicates the &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=number+2012"&gt;number 2012&lt;/a&gt;). (For the curious: the peaks near 2050 arise from the queries &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=2%5E2014"&gt;2&lt;sup&gt;2014&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Moore%27s+law+2050"&gt;Moore&#8217;s law 2050&lt;/a&gt;, and &lt;a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=30th+Tuesday+in+2051"&gt;30th Tuesday in 2051&lt;/a&gt;.)&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;This ends our little study of the size distribution of integers and real numbers in Wolfram|Alpha queries. We leave the study of Heap&#8217;s law (see &lt;a href="http://ptps.oxfordjournals.org/content/194/202.full.pdf+html" target="_blank"&gt;Sano et al&lt;/a&gt;), meaning how fast new numbers come in, for another time. A study of numbers occurring in web pages was carried out by &lt;a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/physics/0504185" target="_blank"&gt;Dorogovtsev et al&lt;/a&gt;. a few years ago; various aspects of the distribution of integers in web pages agree with the above-found distributions in Wolfram|Alpha queries. The importance of special and round numbers was studied by &lt;a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0271530910000534" target="_blank"&gt;Coupland&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.jstor.org/stable/4167925" target="_blank"&gt;Sigurd&lt;/a&gt;, and &lt;a href="http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1076/jqul.8.3.187.4095" target="_blank"&gt;Jansen and Pollmann&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;

        <p>
         While many integers occur in a list of 2.5 billion integers, not every integer can occur. So, a naturally occurring question is, What is the first integer that nobody used? For the sample set of 2.5 billion integers used, this number turns out to be
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=69926&amp;a=*C.69926-_*NonNegativeDecimalInteger-">
          69,926
         </a>
         (which is not a valid US
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=ZIP+code">
          ZIP Code
         </a>
         and also not a mathematically interesting number, either). Here are the first ten integers that did not occur within the sample (not unexpectedly, none of these are valid US ZIP Codes; US users asked about population and location of about every valid US ZIP Code).
        </p>
        <p>
         69926 | 70246 | 70635 | 70908 | 70982 | 71501 | 72781 | 72942 | 73519 | 75909
        </p>
        <p>
         Here is a graphic showing how many integers less than or equal to
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         did not occur.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Integers less than or equal to n did that not occur" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19514" height="223" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/Integers-Less-Equal.png" title="Integers less than or equal to n did that not occur" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Over the much larger interval [1, 10
         <sup>
          120
         </sup>
         ], the probability of an integer occurring drops quickly with the size of the integer, but less quickly than the above power law for numbers smaller than a million suggested. And the relation between the frequency of an integer and the integer itself is no longer a power law.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Relation between the frequency of an integer and the integer itself" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19515" height="201" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/LargerInterval.png" title="Relation between the frequency of an integer and the integer itself" width="395"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Over this large interval [1, 10
         <sup>
          100
         </sup>
         ], the probability of the occurrence of an integer
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         scales approximately as
         <em>
          p
         </em>
         (
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         ) ~ ln(
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         )
         <em>
          <sup>
           
          </sup>
         </em>
         with
         <em>
          
         </em>
          -2.8. (The gray line indicates the power law.)
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Probability of the occurrence from 1-10^100" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19516" height="221" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/Large-1-100.png" title="Probability of the occurrence from 1-10^100" width="403"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         The last graphic shows the number of occurrences of the integers. A complementary view is the cumulative one: if one takes all integers
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         up to a certain size
         <em>
          N
         </em>
         into account, how many integers has one not taken into account? The following graphic shows this curve.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Number of occurrences of the integers" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19517" height="189" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/NumberOfOccurrences.png" title="Number of occurrences of the integers" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Not all integers that occur in Wolfram|Alpha queries are given in fully written out form; often they are written in the form
         <em>
          b
         </em>
         <em>
          <sup>
           e
          </sup>
         </em>
         with base
         <em>
          b
         </em>
         and exponent
         <em>
          e
         </em>
         being an integer. The next graphic shows the relative distribution of such powers. One sees the Zero Barrier (0
         <em>
          <sup>
           e
          </sup>
         </em>
         ) in the East, the Decadrian Wall (10
         <em>
          <sup>
           e
          </sup>
         </em>
         ) in the left half divides the Times Table Valley from the Exponentially Decaying (in popularity) Power Plane, the Cubic Wall (
         <em>
          b
         </em>
         <sup>
          3
         </sup>
         ) is in the South, and the Diagonal Towers (
         <em>
          b
         </em>
         <em>
          <sup>
           b
          </sup>
         </em>
         ) are along the middle.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Relative distribution of integers written in the form b^e" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19518" height="333" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/RelativeDistribution.png" title="Relative distribution of integers written in the form b^e" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         In a similar spirit, one could more generally ask which integers
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         <sub>
          1
         </sub>
         and
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         <sub>
          2
         </sub>
         occur frequently together with each other in a query. We display again the frequency of co-occurrence, and we observe that many small integers like to pair with 10 or 100.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Frequency of co-occurrence" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19519" height="335" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/co-occurrence.png" title="Frequency of co-occurrence" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Now let us look at some real numbers. Typically there are fewer real numbers than integers occurring in queries, but they come in a larger variety. Using the same query set that contained the above-analyzed 2.5 billion integers yields about 180 million real numbers. The absolute value of real numbers can be greater or less than 1. Here is a distribution of the real numbers by their binned exponent. The pronounced spikes are the real numbers
         <em>
          x
         </em>
         = 0.5,
         <em>
          x
         </em>
         = 0.1, and in general at multiples of 0.1.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Distribution of the occurring real numbers by their binned exponent" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19521" height="219" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/RealNumbers.png" title="Distribution of the occurring real numbers by their binned exponent" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         The most common real numbers that do not have simple equivalent fractions are 9.81 (from the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=acceleration+due+to+gravity">
          acceleration due to gravity
         </a>
         ), 3.14 (from pi), 6.6710
         <sup>
          -11
         </sup>
         (from the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Newtonian+gravitational+constant">
          Newtonian gravitational constant
         </a>
         ), 1.60210
         <sup>
          -19
         </sup>
         (from the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=electron+charge">
          electron charge
         </a>
         ), 6.62610
         <sup>
          -34
         </sup>
         (from the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Planck+constant">
          Planck constant
         </a>
         ), 1.3810
         <sup>
          -23
         </sup>
         (from the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Boltzmann+constant">
          Boltzmann constant
         </a>
         ), and 8.85410
         <sup>
          -12
         </sup>
         (from the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=permittivity+of+free+space">
          permittivity of free space
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <p>
         0.5 | 0.1 | 0.2 | 1.5 | 0.9 | 0.01 | 0.25 | 0.3 | 0.4 | 0.05 | 2.5 | 0.8 | 0.6 | 9.8 | 0.7 | 1.2 | 0.75 | 1.1 |
         <br/>
         4.5 | 9.81 | 0.02 | 1. | 0.15 | 3.5 | 0.001 | 1.4 | 0.04 | 3.14 | 1.3 | 0.03 | 1.6 | 1.8 | 0.06 | 0.08 |
         <br/>
         1.25 | 2.2 | 2.1 | 4.9 | 2. | 2.4 | 7.5 | 0.12 | 1.6 x 10
         <sup>
          -19
         </sup>
         | 0.005 | 0.95 | 6.5 | 8.314 | 5.5 | 1.7 | 0.35 |
         <br/>
         0.007 | 0.002 | 1.02 | 2.8 | 3.7 | 0.09 | 0.0001 | 4.2 | 0.025 | 3.2 | 0.45 | 0.5 | 2.3 | 1.9 | 3.6 |
         <br/>
         0.125 | 4.8 | 0.99 | 2.7 | 3. | 0.16 | 0.85 | 3.141 | 1.5 | 12.5 | 2.6 | 3.3 | 0.18 | 0.65 | 6.67 x 10
         <sup>
          -11
         </sup>
         |
         <br/>
         8.5 | 1.602 x 10
         <sup>
          -19
         </sup>
         | 2.25 | 1.38 x 10
         <sup>
          -23
         </sup>
         | 6.626 x 10
         <sup>
          -34
         </sup>
         | 6. | 1.75 | 1.01 | 3.1 | 5. | 3.4 |
         <br/>
         0.55 | 0.14 | 0.11 | 5.8 | 8.854 x 10
         <sup>
          -12
         </sup>
         | 1000. | 0.015 | 2.9
        </p>
        <p>
         Here is a plot of the distribution over a larger range of reals, ranging over the interval [10
         <sup>
          -100
         </sup>
         , 10
         <sup>
          100
         </sup>
         ], now with a logarithmic vertical scale.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Distribution over a larger range of reals" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19529" height="230" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/LargeRangeReals.png" title="Distribution over a larger range of reals" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         And similar to the integers, over the larger interval, the probability of the occurrence of a real number
         <em>
          x
         </em>
         scales approximately as
         <em>
          p
         </em>
         (
         <em>
          x
         </em>
         ) ~ |ln(
         <em>
          x
         </em>
         )|
         <em>
          <sup>
           
          </sup>
         </em>
         with
         <em>
          
         </em>
          -2.8. The first graphic shows numbers greater than 1 and the second graphic less than 1 (the gray line indicates again the power law in the exponent).
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Numbers greater than 1 and less than 1" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19530" height="162" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/OccurrenceRealNumbers.png" title="Numbers greater than 1 and less than 1" width="505"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         The real numbers with just one digit followed by (implied or explicit) zeros (e.g.
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.2">
          0.2
         </a>
         , or
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.5">
          0.5
         </a>
         ) are in general much more common than real numbers with two nonzero digits (e.g.
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.36">
          0.36
         </a>
         or
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.89">
          0.89
         </a>
         ). The real numbers with just two digits followed by (implied or explicit) zeros (e.g.
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.58">
          0.58
         </a>
         or
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.89">
          0.89
         </a>
         ) are in general much more common than real numbers with three non-zero digits (e.g.
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.582">
          0.582
         </a>
         or
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=0.880">
          0.880
         </a>
         ). And so on with four, five, et cetera nonzero digits. There are some exceptions from this rule, most notably real numbers starting with the digit sequences 314 (leading digits of
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=%5C%5BPi%5D">
          <em>
           
          </em>
         </a>
         ) and 667 (three-digit rounded result for
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=2%2F3+to+three+digits">
          2/3
         </a>
         and the above mentioned
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Newtonian+gravitational+constant">
          Newtonian gravitational constant
         </a>
         ), and the physical constants mentioned above.
        </p>
        <p>
         The following graphic shows the frequency of occurrence for four groups of real numbers: in red, the real numbers that have just one nonzero digit; in blue, the real numbers with just two nonzero digits; in green, the real numbers with just three nonzero digits; and in yellow, the real numbers with at least four leading digits. The outliers in green are the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=digits+of+%5C%5BPi%5D">
          digits of
          <em>
           
          </em>
         </a>
         , the first few digits of the
         <a href="http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=acceleration+due+to+gravity+on+Earth">
          acceleration due to gravity on Earth
         </a>
         , and multiples of one-eighths.
         <a href="http://blog.wolfram.com/data/uploads/2012/10/NumbersOfWolframAlpha.cdf">
          Download the image as a CDF
         </a>
         and mouse over the points to see the leading digits. As one would intuitively expect, for every digit added, the number of found real numbers decreases by an order of magnitude. The three thin purple lines are the probabilities for two, three, and four consecutive integers according to the generalized Benford law.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Frequency of occurrence for four groups of real numbers" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19531" height="261" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/NumbersOfWolframAlpha.png" title="Frequency of occurrence for four groups of real numbers" width="505"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         The last plot that uses the first four digits of all real numbers shows a lot of details. If we only take the first two digits into account, we have 100 bins, and one clearly sees the integer multiples of 0.1 as well as some of the physical constants emerge.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Plot that uses the first four digits of all real number" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-19532" height="163" src="http://blog.wolframalpha.com/data/uploads/2012/10/FourDigits.png" title="Plot that uses the first four digits of all real number" width="360"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         This ends our little study of the size distribution of integers and real numbers in Wolfram|Alpha queries. We leave the study of Heaps law (see
         <a href="http://ptps.oxfordjournals.org/content/194/202.full.pdf+html" target="_blank">
          Sano et al
         </a>
         ), meaning how fast new numbers come in, for another time. A study of numbers occurring in web pages was carried out by
         <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/physics/0504185" target="_blank">
          Dorogovtsev et al
         </a>
         . a few years ago; various aspects of the distribution of integers in web pages agree with the above-found distributions in Wolfram|Alpha queries. The importance of special and round numbers was studied by
         <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0271530910000534" target="_blank">
          Coupland
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://www.jstor.org/stable/4167925" target="_blank">
          Sigurd
         </a>
         , and
         <a href="http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1076/jqul.8.3.187.4095" target="_blank">
          Jansen and Pollmann
         </a>
         .
        </p>

                function validEmail(email)
                {
                    signPos = email.indexOf("@");
                    dotPos = email.lastIndexOf(".");

                    if (email.length &lt; 6 || signPos == -1 || dotPos == -1 || (dotPos &lt; signPos) || (dotPos - signPos == 1) || dotPos == email.length-1 )
                    {
                        return false;
                    }

                    return true;
                }

    function slideSwitch() {
        old = current;
        current = getNext(current);
        if(old != current &amp;&amp; !hovering) {
            $('#rotator'+old).fadeOut(600,function() {
                $('#rotator'+current).fadeIn(600);
            });
        }
    }

    function getNext(current) {
        var next = current + 1;

        while(next != current) {
            if(next &gt; 10) {
                next = 1;
            }
            if($('#rotator'+next).length) {
                return next;
            }
            next++;
        }
        return current;
