       <h1 id="main-content" itemprop="headline">
        <span class="title">
         Contractor Mortgages Assessor 
         <em>
          How much can I borrow?
         </em>
        </span>
       </h1>
       <div class="kindleWidget kindleLight guide" id="nsAxmYJPBTxaGXk2i6S3BK9W8O">
        <img src="http://www.freelancerfinancials.uk.com/wp-content/plugins/send-to-kindle/media/black-15.png"/>
        <span>
         Send to Kindle
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="guidePrint">
        <div class="printfriendly pf-alignright">
         <a class="noslimstat" href="#" onclick="window.print(); return false;" rel="nofollow">
          <img alt="Print Friendly" src="http://cdn.printfriendly.com/pf-button-both.gif" style="border:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none; box-shadow:none;"/>

       <div class="mnbox" itemprop="mainContentOfPage" itemtype="http://schema.org/WebPageElement">
        <div class="pf-content">
         <p>
          The first question we get asked when contractors contact us for a mortgage is, Can I secure a mortgage if Im new to contracting?
         </p>
         <p>
          The second most popular question is How much can I borrow?. Which, in turn, prompts How do lenders work
          <strong>
           that
          </strong>
          out?
         </p>
         <p>
          If these sound like questions you have, a
          <a href="http://www.freelancerfinancials.uk.com/mortgages-for-contractors/" itemprop="significantLink">
           contractor mortgage
          </a>
          may be the solution for you.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span id="more-3797">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          The answer to the first question is
          <strong>
           YES
          </strong>
          . Thats irrespective of whether youre a veteran or a newbie on your first contract, we can help you secure a mortgage.
         </p>
         <h2>
          How much can I borrow and how will I be assessed?
         </h2>
         <p>
          The other two questions needs further explanation. Both can depend on a contractors individual circumstances and status.
         </p>
         <p>
          First of all, we need to define what we mean by a
          <strong>
           contractor.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          A typical contractor will work for one client at a time through an agency. The agency expect the contractor to keep certain office hours.
         </p>
         <p>
          Theyll also work on projects/assignments that last between three to twelve months. They work to an hourly or daily rate and will raise a timesheet at the end of the week.
         </p>
         <p>
          These contractors can apply for a contractor mortgage, based on their rate. Or, if theyve been trading long enough, apply as a self-employed entity, using their accounts.
         </p>
         <p>
          The key benefit of the former is that contractor friendly banks will annualise their rate to work out how much they can afford.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Self Employed Basis
         </h3>
         <p>
          Contractors who are company directors usually prefer to draw a low salary and minimise dividends. This is by far and away the most tax efficient way of working.
         </p>
         <p>
          However, this has a knock-on effect of limiting how much they can borrow. The majority of banks will also demand to see three years trading history via this route. That excludes any contractors just starting out.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Contractor Mortgage
         </h3>
         <p>
          As a rule of thumb, you can calculate the typical borrowing threshold as follows:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           multiply your daily contract rate by the number of days you contract for per week and then again by 48 weeks.
          </li>
          <li>
           this determines your annualised income. Contractor-friendly lenders will use this as the basis of their calculation;
          </li>
          <li>
           as a guide line, you have the potential to secure a mortgage of up to 5 times this total sum.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          In real life, what does that mean? A contractor earning 425 per day and working 5 days a week could potentially borrow 102,000 x 5 =
          <strong>
           510,000
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <h3>
          Joint Application (contractor applying with a Permie)
         </h3>
         <p>
          If you are applying on a joint basis with your partner who is a permie, then use the following calculation:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           multiply your daily contract rate by the number of days you contract for per week and then by 48 weeks. Just the same as you would do as if applying on your own;
          </li>
          <li>
           then add your income to the permies gross annual earnings. Again as a guide, your joint potential is a mortgage of up to 5 times this total sum;
          </li>
          <li>
           so, the same contractor applying with a permie earning 35,000 a year could borrow (102,000 + 35,000) x 5 = 685,000.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <h3>
          What Paperwork will the lender ask for?
         </h3>
         <p>
          Your mortgage lender or broker will expect you to provide a copy of the contract outlining the terms of your assignment. Theyll also need bank statements to prove your income is as per the contract.
         </p>
         <p>
          Contractor mortgages are only available to contractors who:
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           work through their own limited company, or
          </li>
          <li>
           through a UK payroll umbrella company.
          </li>
         </ol>
         <h3>
          Where Can I get a Contractor Mortgage?
         </h3>
         <p>
          The place you
          <strong>
           dont
          </strong>
          want to start looking for a contractor mortgage is on the High Street. Use a specialist mortgage broker who understands how contracting works.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you dont, you may get offered a mortgage based on your take home pay. If youre a tax-efficient contractor, this wont amount to a fat lot.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are many websites purporting to offer contractor mortgages. When you apply, ensure they understand what contract-based underwriting means. If you dont have sufficient accounts, dont let them bully you into a self-employed mortgage. Theyre
          <em>
           NOT
          </em>
          the same.
         </p>
         <p>
          Also, ensure that the mortgage they offer you is from a
          <span itemprop="significantLink">
           <a href="http://www.freelancerfinancials.uk.com/guides/contractor-mortgage-lenders/" title="Genuine Contractor Mortgage Lender Guides">
            genuine contractor-friendly lender
           </a>
          </span>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          So back to the original question, How much can you borrow based on your contract rate?.
         </p>
         <p>
          For a guide, enter your contract rate into a
          <a href="http://www.freelancerfinancials.uk.com">
           contractor mortgage calculator
          </a>
          . For a more specific enquiry, you are always best to discuss your unique circumstances with a qualified
          <acronym title="Independent Financial Adviser">
           IFA
          </acronym>
          .
         </p>
