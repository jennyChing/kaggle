   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Special functions
      </b>
      are particular
      <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">
       mathematical functions
      </a>
      which have more or less established names and notations due to their importance in
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">
       mathematical analysis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Functional_analysis" title="Functional analysis">
       functional analysis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
       physics
      </a>
      , or other applications.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is no general formal definition, but the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_mathematical_functions" title="List of mathematical functions">
       list of mathematical functions
      </a>
      contains functions which are commonly accepted as special.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tables_of_special_functions">
       Tables of special functions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tables of special functions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many special functions appear as solutions of
      <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">
       differential equations
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">
       integrals
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elementary_functions" title="Elementary functions">
       elementary functions
      </a>
      . Therefore, tables of integrals
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GR-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      usually include descriptions of special functions, and tables of special functions
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRENE_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IRENE-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      include most important integrals; at least, the integral representation of special functions. Because symmetries of differential equations are essential to both physics and mathematics, the theory of special functions is closely related to the theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">
       Lie groups
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">
       Lie algebras
      </a>
      , as well as certain topics in
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_physics" title="Mathematical physics">
       mathematical physics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_computation" title="Symbolic computation">
       Symbolic computation
      </a>
      engines usually recognize the majority of special functions. Not all such systems have efficient algorithms for the evaluation, especially in the
      <a href="/wiki/Complex_plane" title="Complex plane">
       complex plane
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notations_used_for_special_functions">
       Notations used for special functions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notations used for special functions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Functions with established international notations are
      <a href="/wiki/Sine" title="Sine">
       sin
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosine" title="Cosine">
       cos
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Exponential_function" title="Exponential function">
       exp
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Error_function" title="Error function">
       erf
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erfc" title="Erfc">
       erfc
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Some special functions have several notations:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_logarithm" title="Natural logarithm">
        natural logarithm
       </a>
       can be written Log, log, log
       <sub>
        e
       </sub>
       or ln, depending on the context.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Trigonometric_functions#tangent" title="Trigonometric functions">
        tangent
       </a>
       function may be denoted Tan, tan or tg (especially in Russian and Bulgarian literature).
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctangent" title="Arctangent">
        Arctangent
       </a>
       may be called arctan, atan, arctg, or tan
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Bessel_function" title="Bessel function">
        Bessel functions
       </a>
       may be written
       <ul>
        <li>
         <img alt="~J_n(x),~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/e/04eed3445844309aac13d8e2ddf30b86.png"/>
        </li>
        <li>
         <img alt="~{\rm besselj}(n,x), ~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/7/c/97c50f177769356c35e9b9ed39c7e821.png"/>
        </li>
        <li>
         <img alt="~ {\rm BesselJ}[n,x].~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/1/3/2137f554bf780f90c782699e5c452f87.png"/>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Subscripts are often used to indicate arguments, typically integers. In a few cases, the semicolon (;) or even backslash (\) is used as a separator. In this case, the translation to algorithmic languages admits
      <a href="/wiki/Ambiguity#Ambiguity_in_names_of_functions" title="Ambiguity">
       ambiguity
      </a>
      and may lead to confusion.
     </p>
     <p>
      Superscripts may indicate not only exponentiation, but modification of a function. Examples include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <img alt="~\cos^3(x)~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/5/d/55d02bb52ece8d8ecc6866efc4ef0f35.png"/>
       usually indicates
       <img alt="~(\cos(x))^3~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/c/09cc1469c402be804b51331089bb4a13.png"/>
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="~\cos^2(x)~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/0/d/90daf960ee46ccfa1e1246bd842e01b8.png"/>
       is typically
       <img alt="~(\cos(x))^2~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/f/6/2f66f602d3e60314f6da6861a9424aef.png"/>
       , but never
       <img alt="~\cos(\cos(x))~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/4/5/945b8fe1dd020784f8541957e3fe9222.png"/>
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="~\cos^{-1}(x)~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/5/d/85d2381e037dad0986e175b83c4978a5.png"/>
       usually means
       <img alt="~\arccos(x)~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/e/deef9a4de1a1de0a1f0c2ad60e394c3a.png"/>
       , and not
       <img alt="~(\cos(x))^{-1}~" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/9/a/19a7a32375766872193e22fca010365c.png"/>
       ; this one typically causes the most confusion, as it is inconsistent with the others.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation_of_special_functions">
       Evaluation of special functions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evaluation of special functions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Most special functions are considered as a function of a complex variable. They are
      <a href="/wiki/Analytic_function" title="Analytic function">
       analytic
      </a>
      ; the singularities and cuts are described; the differential and integral representations are known and the expansion to the
      <a href="/wiki/Taylor_series" title="Taylor series">
       Taylor series
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymptotic_series" title="Asymptotic series">
       asymptotic series
      </a>
      are available. In addition, sometimes there exist relations with other special functions; a complicated special function can be expressed in terms of simpler functions. Various representations can be used for the evaluation; the simplest way to evaluate a function is to expand it into a Taylor series. However, such representation may converge slowly if at all. In algorithmic languages,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pade_approximation" title="Pade approximation">
       rational approximations
      </a>
      are typically used, although they may behave badly in the case of complex argument(s).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_special_functions">
       History of special functions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of special functions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_theory">
       Classical theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classical theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      While
      <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">
       trigonometry
      </a>
      can be codified  as was clear already to expert mathematicians of the eighteenth century (if not before)  the search for a complete and unified theory of special functions has continued since the nineteenth century. The high point of special function theory in the period 1800-1900 was the theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_function" title="Elliptic function">
       elliptic functions
      </a>
      ; treatises that were essentially complete, such as that of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tannery_and_Molk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tannery and Molk (page does not exist)">
       Tannery and Molk
      </a>
      , could be written as handbooks to all the basic identities of the theory. They were based on techniques from
      <a href="/wiki/Complex_analysis" title="Complex analysis">
       complex analysis
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      From that time onwards it would be assumed that
      <a href="/wiki/Analytic_function" title="Analytic function">
       analytic function
      </a>
      theory, which had already unified the trigonometric and
      <a href="/wiki/Exponential_function" title="Exponential function">
       exponential functions
      </a>
      , was a fundamental tool. The end of the century also saw a very detailed discussion of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical_harmonic" title="Spherical harmonic">
       spherical harmonics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_and_fixed_motivations">
       Changing and fixed motivations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changing and fixed motivations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Of course the wish for a broad theory including as many as possible of the known special functions has its intellectual appeal, but it is worth noting other motivations. For a long time, the special functions were in the particular province of
      <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">
       applied mathematics
      </a>
      ; applications to the physical sciences and engineering determined the relative importance of functions. In the days before the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_computer" title="Electronic computer">
       electronic computer
      </a>
      , the ultimate compliment to a special function was the computation, by hand, of extended
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_table" title="Mathematical table">
       tables of its values
      </a>
      . This was a capital-intensive process, intended to make the function available by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Look-up_table" title="Look-up table">
       look-up
      </a>
      , as for the familiar
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logarithm_tables" title="Logarithm tables">
       logarithm tables
      </a>
      . The aspects of the theory that then mattered might then be two:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">
        numerical analysis
       </a>
       , discovery of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infinite_series" title="Infinite series">
        infinite series
       </a>
       or other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytical_expression" title="Analytical expression">
        analytical expression
       </a>
       allowing rapid calculation; and
      </li>
      <li>
       reduction of as many functions as possible to the given function.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In contrast, one might say, there are approaches typical of the interests of
      <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">
       pure mathematics
      </a>
      :
      <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_analysis" title="Asymptotic analysis">
       asymptotic analysis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Analytic_continuation" title="Analytic continuation">
       analytic continuation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Monodromy" title="Monodromy">
       monodromy
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Complex_plane" title="Complex plane">
       complex plane
      </a>
      , and the discovery of
      <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">
       symmetry
      </a>
      principles and other structure behind the faade of endless formulae in rows. There is not a real conflict between these approaches, in fact.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">
       Twentieth century
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The twentieth century saw several waves of interest in special function theory. The classic
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Whittaker_and_Watson" title="Whittaker and Watson">
        Whittaker and Watson
       </a>
      </i>
      (1902) textbook sought to unify the theory by using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_variable" title="Complex variable">
       complex variables
      </a>
      ; the
      <a href="/wiki/G._N._Watson" title="G. N. Watson">
       G. N. Watson
      </a>
      tome
      <i>
       A Treatise on the Theory of Bessel Functions
      </i>
      pushed the techniques as far as possible for one important type that particularly admitted asymptotics to be studied.
     </p>
     <p>
      The later
      <a href="/wiki/Bateman_Manuscript_Project" title="Bateman Manuscript Project">
       Bateman Manuscript Project
      </a>
      , under the editorship of
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Erd%C3%A9lyi" title="Arthur Erdlyi">
       Arthur Erdlyi
      </a>
      , attempted to be encyclopedic, and came around the time when electronic computation was coming to the fore and tabulation ceased to be the main issue.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_theories">
       Contemporary theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Contemporary theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The modern theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomials" title="Orthogonal polynomials">
       orthogonal polynomials
      </a>
      is of a definite but limited scope.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergeometric_series" title="Hypergeometric series">
       Hypergeometric series
      </a>
      became an intricate theory, in need of later conceptual arrangement.
      <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">
       Lie groups
      </a>
      , and in particular their
      <a href="/wiki/Representation_theory" title="Representation theory">
       representation theory
      </a>
      , explain what a
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Spherical_function" title="Spherical function">
       spherical function
      </a>
      can be in general; from 1950 onwards substantial parts of classical theory could be recast in terms of Lie groups. Further, work on
      <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics" title="Algebraic combinatorics">
       algebraic combinatorics
      </a>
      also revived interest in older parts of the theory. Conjectures of
      <a href="/wiki/Ian_G._Macdonald" title="Ian G. Macdonald">
       Ian G. Macdonald
      </a>
      helped to open up large and active new fields with the typical special function flavour.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Difference_equation" title="Difference equation">
       Difference equations
      </a>
      have begun to take their place besides
      <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">
       differential equations
      </a>
      as a source for special functions.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Special_functions_in_number_theory">
       Special functions in number theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_functions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Special functions in number theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">
       number theory
      </a>
      , certain special functions have traditionally been studied, such as particular
      <a href="/wiki/Dirichlet_series" title="Dirichlet series">
       Dirichlet series
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Modular_form" title="Modular form">
       modular forms
      </a>
      . Almost all aspects of special function theory are reflected there, as well as some new ones, such as came out of the
      <a href="/wiki/Monstrous_moonshine" title="Monstrous moonshine">
       monstrous moonshine
      </a>
      theory.
     </p>
