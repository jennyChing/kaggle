     <p>
      <b>
       Bibendum
      </b>
      , commonly referred to as the
      <b>
       Michelin Man
      </b>
      , is the symbol of the
      <a href="/wiki/Michelin" title="Michelin">
       Michelin
      </a>
      tire company. Introduced at the
      <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">
       Lyon
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Colonial_exhibition" title="Colonial exhibition">
       Exhibition of 1894
      </a>
      where the Michelin brothers had a stand,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motor196908_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Motor196908-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Bibendum is one of the world's oldest
      <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">
       trademarks
      </a>
      . The slogan
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nunc_est_bibendum" title="Nunc est bibendum">
        Nunc est bibendum
       </a>
      </i>
      (Now is the time to drink) is taken from
      <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">
       Horace
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Odes_(Horace)" title="Odes (Horace)">
        Odes
       </a>
      </i>
      (book I, ode xxxvii, line 1). He is also referred to as
      <b>
       Bib
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Bibelobis
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bibendum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While attending the Universal and Colonial Exposition in
      <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">
       Lyon
      </a>
      in 1894,
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Michelin_(born_1859)" title="douard Michelin (born 1859)">
       douard
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Michelin" title="Andr Michelin">
       Andr Michelin
      </a>
      noticed a stack of
      <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">
       tires
      </a>
      that suggested to douard the figure of a man without arms. Four years later, Andr met French cartoonist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marius_Rossillon" title="Marius Rossillon">
       Marius Rossillon
      </a>
      , popularly known as O'Galop, who showed him a rejected image he had created for a
      <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">
       Munich
      </a>
      brewerya large, regal figure holding a huge glass of beer and quoting Horace's phrase "Nunc est bibendum". Andr immediately suggested replacing the man with a figure made from tires. Thus O'Galop transformed the earlier image into Michelin's symbol. Today, Bibendum is one of the world's most recognised trademarks, representing Michelin in over 150 countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 1898 poster showed him offering the toast
      <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
       <i>
        Nunc est bibendum
       </i>
      </span>
      to his scrawny competitors with a glass full of road hazards, with the title and the tag
      <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
       <i>
        C'est  dire:  votre sant. Le pneu Michelin boit l'obstacle
       </i>
      </span>
      ("That is to say, to your health. The Michelin tire drinks up obstacles"). The implication is that Michelin tires will easily take on road hazards. The company used this basic poster format for fifteen years, adding its latest products to the table in front of the figure. It is unclear when the word "Bibendum" came to be the name of the character himself. At the latest, it was in 1908, when Michelin commissioned
      <a href="/wiki/Curnonsky" title="Curnonsky">
       Curnonsky
      </a>
      to write a newspaper column signed "Bibendum".
     </p>
     <p>
      From 1912, tires took on a black appearance because carbon was added as a preservative and strengthener to the base rubber material. Before then, they were a gray-white or light, translucent beige colour. Bibendum's appearance also changed. Though briefly featured in several print ads, Michelin quickly changed back his appearance, citing printing and aesthetic issues for the change, and not racial concerns as commonly believed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1398" data-file-width="1000" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg/220px-Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg/330px-Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg/440px-Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Michelin_Poster_1898.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A poster by "
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marius_Rossillon" title="Marius Rossillon">
         O'Galop
        </a>
        " of Bibendum, the
        <a href="/wiki/Michelin" title="Michelin">
         Michelin
        </a>
        Man, 1898
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The name of the plump tire-man has entered the language to describe someone obese or wearing comically bulky clothing. (e.g. "How can I wrap up warm without looking like the Michelin Man?")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bibendum's shape has changed over the years. O'Galop's logo was based on
      <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">
       bicycle
      </a>
      tires, wore
      <a href="/wiki/Pince-nez" title="Pince-nez">
       pince-nez
      </a>
      glasses with lanyard, and smoked a
      <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">
       cigar
      </a>
      . By the 1980s, Bibendum was being shown running, and in 1998, his 100th
      <a href="/wiki/Anniversary" title="Anniversary">
       anniversary
      </a>
      , a slimmed-down version became the company's new logo. He had long since given up the cigar and pince-nez. The slimming of the logo reflected lower-profile, smaller tires of modern cars. Bib even had a similar-looking puppy as a companion when the duo were
      <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">
       CGI
      </a>
      animated for recent American television advertisements.
     </p>
     <p>
      A history of the emblem was written by Olivier Darmon and published in 1997:
      <i>
       Le Grand Sicle de Bibendum
      </i>
      ; Paris: Hobeke.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The "Bibendum chair" was designed by
      <a href="/wiki/Eileen_Gray" title="Eileen Gray">
       Eileen Gray
      </a>
      in 1925.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cayce_Pollard" title="Cayce Pollard">
       Cayce Pollard
      </a>
      , the main character of
      <a href="/wiki/William_Gibson" title="William Gibson">
       William Gibson
      </a>
      's novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pattern_Recognition_(novel)" title="Pattern Recognition (novel)">
        Pattern Recognition
       </a>
      </i>
      , has a strong aversion to corporate brands and logos. The sight of Bibendum in particular gives her panic attacks.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the village of
      <a href="/wiki/La_Roque-Gageac" title="La Roque-Gageac">
       La Roque-Gageac
      </a>
      on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dordogne_river" title="Dordogne river">
       Dordogne river
      </a>
      , the doorway of an ancient house was refurbished, with Bibendum carved into the replacement stone surround.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bibendum made a brief guest appearance in the
      <a href="/wiki/Asterix" title="Asterix">
       Asterix
      </a>
      series, as the
      <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">
       chariot
      </a>
      -wheel dealer in certain translations, including the English one, of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Asterix_in_Switzerland" title="Asterix in Switzerland">
        Asterix in Switzerland
       </a>
      </i>
      . (The original French version used the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">
       Gaulish
      </a>
      warrior mascot of French service-station company
      <a href="/wiki/Antar_(company)" title="Antar (company)">
       Antar
      </a>
      .)
     </p>
     <p>
      Michelin sued the
      <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">
       performance artist
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Momus_(artist)" title="Momus (artist)">
       Momus
      </a>
      for releasing a song about the trademarked Michelin Man.
     </p>
     <p>
      French
      <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">
       reggae
      </a>
      band
      <a href="/wiki/Tryo" title="Tryo">
       Tryo
      </a>
      sang about Bibendum on their album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grain_de_Sable" title="Grain de Sable">
        Grain de Sable
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
       'Monsieur Bibendum, il est vraiment norme / Monsieur Bibendum, le bonheur en personne'
      </span>
      ('Mr Bibendum, he is truly enormous, Mr Bibendum; happiness in person').
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 2009 animated,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">
       Academy Award
      </a>
      -winning satire
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Logorama" title="Logorama">
        Logorama
       </a>
      </i>
      , a series of Bibenda play police detectives, a sheriff, and a squad of SWAT personnel who all work together to try to bring down a psychotic, ultraviolent criminal played by
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_McDonald" title="Ronald McDonald">
       Ronald McDonald
      </a>
      .
     </p>
