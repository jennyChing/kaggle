   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Quadroon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">

      )
     </span>
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Quadroon
      </b>
      , and the associated words
      <b>
       quarteron
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       octoroon
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       quintroon
      </b>
      are terms that, historically, were applied to define the ancestry of people of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-race" title="Mixed-race">
       mixed-race
      </a>
      , generally of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African" title="African">
       African
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_peoples" title="European peoples">
       European
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancestry" title="Ancestry">
       ancestry
      </a>
      in the slave societies of the Americas, but also, within
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      , to those of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal" title="Australian Aboriginal">
       Aboriginal
      </a>
      and European ancestry.
     </p>
     <p>
      Governments incorporated the terms in law defining rights and restrictions. The use of such terminology is a characteristic of
      <a href="/wiki/Hypodescent" title="Hypodescent">
       hypodescent
      </a>
      , which is the practice within a society of assigning children of mixed unions to the ethnic group which is perceived by the dominant group as being subordinate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The racial designations refer specifically to the number of full-blooded African
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancestors" title="Ancestors">
       ancestors
      </a>
      or equivalent, emphasizing the quantitative least, with quadroon signifying that a person has one-quarter black ancestry, etc.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The word "quadroon" was borrowed from the French
      <i>
       quarteron
      </i>
      , and Spanish
      <i>
       cuartern,
      </i>
      which have their roots in the Latin
      <i>
       quartus
      </i>
      , meaning fourth. The word octoroon is derived from quadroon, and rooted in the Latin
      <i>
       octo
      </i>
      , which means eight.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quarter_White_or_European">
       Quarter
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=White_or_European&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="White or European (page does not exist)">
        White or European
       </a>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quarter White or European">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Latin America, the terms
      <b>
       griffe
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Sambo_(racial_term)" title="Sambo (racial term)">
        sambo
       </a>
      </b>
      were sometimes used for an individual of three-quarters African heritage, or the child of a biracial parent and a fully black parent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoodWes_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WoodWes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Half_and_half">
       Half and half
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Half and half">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Mulatto" title="Mulatto">
        mulatto
       </a>
      </b>
      was used to designate a person who was biracial, with one pure black parent and one pure white parent, or a person whose parents are both Mulatto.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoodWes_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WoodWes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some cases, it became a general term to refer to all persons of mixed race.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quarter_Black_or_African_descent">
       Quarter
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Black_or_African_descent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Black or African descent (page does not exist)">
        Black or African descent
       </a>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quarter Black or African descent">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <b>
       Quadroon
      </b>
      was used to designate a person of one-quarter African/Aboriginal ancestry, that is one
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biracial" title="Biracial">
       biracial
      </a>
      parent (African/Aboriginal and Caucasian) and one White or European parent; in other words, one African/Aboriginal
      <a href="/wiki/Grandparent" title="Grandparent">
       grandparent
      </a>
      and three White or European grandparents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoodWes_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-WoodWes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Casta" title="Casta">
       Latin America
      </a>
      , which had a variety of terms for racial groups, some terms for quadroons were
      <i>
       morisco
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       chino.
      </i>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eighth_Black_or_African_descent">
       Eighth Black or African descent
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eighth Black or African descent">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       octoroon
      </b>
      referred to a person with one-eighth African/Aboriginal ancestry;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that is, someone with family heritage of one biracial grandparent; in other words, one African
      <a href="/wiki/Grandparent#Great-grandparents" title="Grandparent">
       great-grandparent
      </a>
      and seven European great-grandparents. As with the use of
      <i>
       quadroon
      </i>
      , this word was applied to a limited extent in Australia for those of one-eighth Aboriginal ancestry, as the government implemented
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stolen_generation" title="Stolen generation">
       assimilation policies
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Casta" title="Casta">
       Latin America
      </a>
      , a term for octoroon is
      <i>
       albino
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Terceron
      </b>
      was a term synonymous with
      <i>
       octoroon
      </i>
      , derived from being three generations of descent from an African ancestor (great-grandparent).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mustee" title="Mustee">
        mustee
       </a>
      </b>
      was also used to refer to a person with one-eighth African ancestry.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sixteenth_Black_or_African_descent">
       Sixteenth Black or African descent
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sixteenth Black or African descent">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       mustefino
      </b>
      refers to a person with one-sixteenth African ancestry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoodWes_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-WoodWes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The terms "quintroon" or "hexadecaroon" were also used.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Favoritism">
       Favoritism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Favoritism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the antebellum period, abolitionists featured mulattoes and other light-skinned former slaves in public lectures in the North, to arouse public sentiments against slavery by showing Northerners slaves who were visually indistinguishable from them, preventing them from putting the people in the category of "
      <a href="/wiki/Other" title="Other">
       other
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature_and_pop_culture">
       In literature and pop culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In literature and pop culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The figure of the "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragic_octoroon" title="Tragic octoroon">
       tragic octoroon
      </a>
      " or "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragic_mulatta" title="Tragic mulatta">
       tragic mulatta
      </a>
      " became a
      <a href="/wiki/Stock_character" title="Stock character">
       stock character
      </a>
      of abolitionist literature in the antebellum era, beginning in 1836 with the first American anti-slavery novel,
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hildreth" title="Richard Hildreth">
       Richard Hildreth
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       The Slave
      </i>
      and including
      <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">
       Harriet Beecher Stowe
      </a>
      's bestselling 1852 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">
        Uncle Tom's Cabin
       </a>
      </i>
      and the extremely popular 1859 play by
      <a href="/wiki/Dion_Boucicault" title="Dion Boucicault">
       Dion Boucicault
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Octoroon" title="The Octoroon">
        The Octoroon
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zanger_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zanger-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Stereotypically, a light-skinned woman is raised as a white woman in her father's household, until his bankruptcy or death leaves her reduced to a menial position or even sold into slavery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some cases, she may be unaware of her full ancestry before being reduced to victimization, as in
      <a href="/wiki/Lydia_Maria_Child" title="Lydia Maria Child">
       Lydia Maria Child
      </a>
      's short story,
      <i>
       The Quadrons
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This stock character allowed abolitionists to reveal the sexual exploitation prevalent in
      <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">
       slavery
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the period after the American Civil War, Southerners also began writing about people of mixed race, as a way to explore the many contradictions in a postwar society based on a binary division of race. The topic continues to be a means to explore race in society. Authors in the early 20th century were writing against a background of legal racial segregation and disfranchisement in the South; the successes of the civil rights movement have not solved all problems. Authors in the 21st century are writing historical novels set in the 19th century that explore racial permutations.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hildreth" title="Richard Hildreth">
        Richard Hildreth
       </a>
       's 1836 novel
       <i>
        The Slave; or, Memoirs of Archy Moore
       </i>
       ("almost certainly the first anti-slavery novel published in America") is narrated by slave Archy Moore (mulatto son of his owner, Colonel Moore) who marries a light-skinned slave, Cassy, sexually pursued by slave owners
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Railton_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Railton-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Lydia_Maria_Child" title="Lydia Maria Child">
        Lydia Maria Child
       </a>
       's 1842 short story
       <i>
        The Quadroons
       </i>
       was printed in the abolitionist anthology
       <i>
        The Liberty Bell
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">
        Henry Wadsworth Longfellow
       </a>
       's 1842 poem,
       <i>
        The Quadroon Girl
       </i>
       , has a planter knowingly selling his quadroon daughter to a slaver to be his sexual slave.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">
         Uncle Tom's Cabin
        </a>
       </i>
       (1852), a novel by
       <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">
        Harriet Beecher Stowe
       </a>
       , described Eliza and her son Harry as quadroons. As Harry's father was described as a
       <a href="/wiki/Mulatto" title="Mulatto">
        mulatto
       </a>
       , Harry would have less than a quarter African ancestry.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">
        Walt Whitman
       </a>
       's 1855 poem
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Song_of_Myself" title="Song of Myself">
         Song of Myself
        </a>
       </i>
       refers to a "quadroon girl" who is sold at a slave auction.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The Quadroon
       </i>
       (1856) is a novel by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mayne_Reid" title="Thomas Mayne Reid">
        Thomas Mayne Reid
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Octoroon" title="The Octoroon">
         The Octoroon
        </a>
       </i>
       , a play by
       <a href="/wiki/Dion_Boucicault" title="Dion Boucicault">
        Dion Boucicault
       </a>
       adapted from Reid's
       <i>
        The Quadroon
       </i>
       , was first performed at New York Citys Winter Garden on December 12, 1859. The play describes the suffering by Zoe, an octoroon who learns about her African ancestry, which disrupts her life plans.
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Little_Women" title="Little Women">
         Little Women
        </a>
       </i>
       (1868-1869), a novel by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisa_M._Alcott" title="Louisa M. Alcott">
        Louisa M. Alcott
       </a>
       , Mr and Mrs Bhaer accept a quadroon boy to their school.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Cable" title="George Washington Cable">
        George Washington Cable
       </a>
       wrote about the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creoles" title="Louisiana Creoles">
        Louisiana Creoles
       </a>
       of mixed-race heritage; he explored issues for mixed-race mothers who had daughters with white men, and denied their parenthood to enable their light-skinned daughters to pass into the dominant white world in the post-war South. His most important works are
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Creole_Days" title="Old Creole Days">
         Old Creole Days
        </a>
       </i>
       (1879),
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Grandissimes" title="The Grandissimes">
         The Grandissimes
        </a>
       </i>
       (1880), and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madame_Delphine" title="Madame Delphine">
         Madame Delphine
        </a>
       </i>
       (1881).
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Awakening_(Chopin_novel)" title="The Awakening (Chopin novel)">
         The Awakening
        </a>
       </i>
       (1899) by
       <a href="/wiki/Kate_Chopin" title="Kate Chopin">
        Kate Chopin
       </a>
       , Pontellier's nurse is described as a quadroon.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9sir%C3%A9e%E2%80%99s_Baby" title="Dsires Baby">
         Dsires Baby
        </a>
       </i>
       (1893), a short story by Kate Chopin, features an apparently quadroon child born to two white parents; as the mother was adopted, the father assumes she has African ancestry and rejects her. He later burns a letter from his own mother; the reader learns that she was telling of her mixed-race ancestry.
      </li>
     </ul>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="20th-century_to_present">
       20th-century to present
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quadroon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 20th-century to present">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The titular character of
       <a href="/wiki/James_Weldon_Johnson" title="James Weldon Johnson">
        James Weldon Johnson
       </a>
       's book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Autobiography_of_an_Ex-Colored_Man" title="The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man">
         The Autobiography of an Ex-Colored Man
        </a>
       </i>
       (1912/1927) is a man of mixed race who can (and does) decide to
       <a href="/wiki/Passing_(racial_identity)" title="Passing (racial identity)">
        pass
       </a>
       for white.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">
        Aldous Huxley
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Brave_New_World" title="Brave New World">
         Brave New World
        </a>
       </i>
       (1931) included some lower-caste social members described as octoroons.
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">
        William Faulkner
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Absalom,_Absalom!" title="Absalom, Absalom!">
         Absalom, Absalom!
        </a>
       </i>
       (1936), Charles Bon had a son by his octoroon mistress in the 19th century.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Anne_Rice" title="Anne Rice">
        Anne Rice
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Feast_of_All_Saints" title="The Feast of All Saints">
         The Feast of All Saints
        </a>
       </i>
       (1979) features the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gens_de_couleur_libres" title="Gens de couleur libres">
         gens de couleur libres
        </a>
       </i>
       , "free people of color" in New Orleans before the Civil War. Her three novels in the series,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Mayfair_Witches" title="Lives of the Mayfair Witches">
         Lives of the Mayfair Witches
        </a>
       </i>
       (1990 to 1994), also refer to mulatto and quadroon people in late 19th and early 20th-century New Orleans. The city had a high proportion of mixed-race people.
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Love_in_the_Time_of_Cholera" title="Love in the Time of Cholera">
         Love in the Time of Cholera
        </a>
       </i>
       (1985/1988 in English), by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabriel_Garcia_Marquez" title="Gabriel Garcia Marquez">
        Gabriel Garcia Marquez
       </a>
       and set during slavery time in Colombia, Florentino Ariza's mother Transito is described as a "freed quadroon".
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_January_Mysteries" title="Benjamin January Mysteries">
        Benjamin January
       </a>
       /Janvier is a free man of color and the main character in
       <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hambly" title="Barbara Hambly">
        Barbara Hambly
       </a>
       's historical mystery series set in and around 1830s
       <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">
        New Orleans
       </a>
       ; he is described as a griffe. Hambly's novels, published from 1997 to 2013, explores the city's complex caste system and the severe legal and social restrictions placed on people of color.
      </li>
      <li>
       A scene in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rabbit-Proof_Fence_(film)" title="Rabbit-Proof Fence (film)">
         Rabbit-Proof Fence
        </a>
       </i>
       features an
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australian
       </a>
       government official explaining the government's plan to breed Aboriginal children with white people to erase the Aboriginal ancestry. He uses the words quadroon and octoroon.
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Island_Beneath_the_Sea" title="Island Beneath the Sea">
         Island Beneath the Sea
        </a>
       </i>
       (2009), a novel set in late 18th-early 19th century
       <a href="/wiki/Saint-Domingue" title="Saint-Domingue">
        Saint-Domingue
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
        Haiti
       </a>
       ) by
       <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Allende" title="Isabel Allende">
        Isabel Allende
       </a>
       , the main character's daughter Rosette, is a quadroon. Her mother is a free woman of color and former slave, and her father is a white Frenchman.
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Archer_(TV_series)" title="Archer (TV series)">
         Archer
        </a>
       </i>
       , an animated comedy series on FX created by
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_Reed" title="Adam Reed">
        Adam Reed
       </a>
       , the main character, Archer, refers to mentioning the word "quadroon" to his co-worker,
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Archer_characters#Lana_Kane" title="List of Archer characters">
        Lana
       </a>
       , in the past, and her "freaking out". The term is also used by a female character in the show, Pam, when talking about Lana's daughter, who is of mixed race.
      </li>
     </ul>
