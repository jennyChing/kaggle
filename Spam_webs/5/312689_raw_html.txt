        <div class="post-column pull-left">
         <article class="entry post-42144 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-movies-tv tag-entertainment tag-feature-article last_archivepost">
          <img alt="Film Festivals: Who Needs Them?" class="aligncenter full-media nomargin" src="https://angiesdiary.com/wp-content/themes/stylebook/timthumb.php?src=https%3A%2F%2Fangiesdiary.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2013%2F12%2Ffilmfestival1.jpg&amp;q=90&amp;w=795&amp;zc=1" title="Film Festivals: Who Needs Them?"/>
          <span class="entry-content">
           <p>
            <span style="color: #000080;">
             <strong>
              <i>
               Last week
              </i>
             </strong>
             <b>
              <i>
              </i>
             </b>
             <strong>
              <i>
               I wrote about
              </i>
             </strong>
             <b>
              <i>
              </i>
             </b>
             <span style="color: #800000;">
              <strong>
               <i>
                <span style="color: #800000;">
                 <a href="http://angiesdiary.com/lifestyle/movies/basking-sundance/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                  Sundance
                 </a>
                </span>
               </i>
              </strong>
             </span>
             <strong>
              <i>
               and how it impacts the American film industry. But whats the point of film festivals in the first place? What industry professionals wont tell you, is that theyre not meant for the general public at all!
              </i>
             </strong>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Just how many?
             <br/>
            </b>
            How many film festivals do you think exist worldwide? Lets try to name a few:
            <br/>
            <a href="http://www.festival-cannes.com/en.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Cannes
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.labiennale.org/en/cinema/71st-festival/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Venice
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.berlinale.de/en/HomePage.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Berlin
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://tiff.net/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Toronto
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.sundance.org/festival/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Sundance
            </a>
            , anymore? These are indeed the biggest, most prestigious film festivals out there. Most of you have probably heard of at least 3 of them. This is also why they are referred to as the Majors. The Majors will generally only accept films that would be premiering there, to help maintain their exclusivity.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="filmfestival1 Film Festivals: Who Needs Them?" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42195" height="284" src="https://angiesdiary.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/filmfestival1.jpg" title="Film Festivals: Who Needs Them?" width="504"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            Other film festivals youve probably heard of are
            <a href="http://tribecafilm.com/festival" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Tribeca
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.edfilmfest.org.uk/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Edinburgh
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.sansebastianfestival.com/in/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             San Sebastian
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.pardolive.ch/en/Pardo-Live/today-at-the-festival;jsessionid=F47FE12E9A3BC5683DFEBAB96B52DE36" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Locarno
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://hamptonsfilmfest.org/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Hamptons
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.raindance.org/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Raindance
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.filmfestivalrotterdam.com/en/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Rotterdam
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.idfa.nl/nl.aspx" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             IDFA
            </a>
            ? These and about 15 more are called the Mini-majors and, as their nickname suggests, follow right on the Majors in industry importance and size. Some of these are the most important film festivals in their niche, like IDFA is for documentary films.
           </p>
           <p>
            The
            <a href="http://oscar.go.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Oscars
            </a>
            and
            <a href="http://www.bafta.org/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             BAFTA
            </a>
            s are really more of a prestigious award ceremony, and boost PR and marketing, but are not considered festivals per-se, mainly because they only accept award winners from other film festivals, so the discovery factor is not there. Then there are the local film festivals, per country, which mostly serve as a springboard for the bigger ones. So, how many in total? There are more than 6,000 film festivals worldwide.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             What is the point of film festivals?
             <br/>
            </b>
            Out of the 6,000 film festivals out there, it is the Majors, Mini-Majors and local film festivals that serve as the film industrys main quality control mechanism. Taking into account that some of the bigger film festivals receive up to 4,000 submissions each year, the programmers indeed have their work cut out for them. Other than the purely commercial ventures (mostly coming from the US major producers), most films go through the highly critical evaluation machine of the film festivals. At the head of each film festivals evaluation committee reside the Head Programmers, and they will decide on the fate of the filmmaker and his creation.
           </p>
           <p>
            The question remains, how do you get your film seen by the programmer? Thats a tough ask. Most people in the industry advise for the filmmaker to recruit their local fund or association to advocate them with the festivals. A phone call or email from someone more established in the industry, local or otherwise, can get your film to the top of that huge pile. Recently, a sales agent tried to assure me that even in the Majors, you always have a chance of being discovered from the pile if your film is good enough: every programmers dream is to the discover the next Tarantino; thats why they do what they do. That might well be true, but in my humble opinion, theres simply too much white noise to be discovered like that. Besides, even if your film is amazing, would you take that chance, or would you go look for a champion to bring your film to the programmer? I know what I would do
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Who needs them?
             <br/>
            </b>
            If we are to separate
            <em>
             first-time filmmakers
            </em>
            from the rest of the industry, they are the ones in most need of film festivals. As the sales and distribution side of the industry is very lean on people, theres very little chance that you know someone in the right place that can help you get your film sold and distributed, even if you didnt need financing to make it. As such, the only way to get your film treated with any respect is to run it through the festival circuit. A successful festival career for a film, although it doesnt guarantee a distribution deal, it raises the odds infinitely. Hit it big with screenings in the Majors, and youre home free. No festival laurels on your film whatsoever? Youre probably dead in the water.
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             The producers
            </em>
            need them to sell their stuff, whether it is a finished film, or early stages; the buyers are there.Directorsneed film festivals to gain critical acclaim, enabling them to raise finance for their next project more easily. Of course, its more important to the financiers that your film does well in the box offices, but critical acclaim goes a long way, especially in Europe where most films are made on public funding.
            <em>
             The sales agents
            </em>
            need festivals to help them sieve through the unbelievably high volume of films and help them separate the pros from the amateurs. As camera equipment is getting cheaper every day, and editing can be done on the same laptop your 10 year old nephew uses to play Minecraft, some say over 50,000 unique films are made and submitted to festivals worldwide every year.
           </p>
           <p>
            The bigger festivals also serve as a type of trade show, where huge markets take place, and films are bought and sold in the millions. The Cannes film festival is the largest of these events thatin 2012 have
            <a href="http://www.marchedufilm.com/uploads/albums/STATISTIQUES_2012.pdf" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             reportedly
            </a>
            seen over 2,700 titles presented in the market section, with 3,300 sales agents and distributors and more than 3,400 producers in attendance. As a side note, the circus that is Cannes film festival attracts annually over 200,000 film lovers for two weeks in the sunny South of France. This is where most of the
            <em>
             film business
            </em>
            takes place.
           </p>
           <p>
            Who else?
            <em>
             Local governments
            </em>
            use them to enrich the culture and attract tourism. And last but not least,
            <em>
             film festival organizers
            </em>
            need them. What can I say? With over 6,000 festivals worldwide, most of them are just, lets face it, moneymaking schemes. Submission fees are climbing as high as $100 per film, times it by say 1,000 submissions and you got some serious cash flow. And, lest we forget that most festivals are either subsidized or looking for subsidies from local art-supporting foundations and local government. Sounds to me like a well-established business model.
           </p>
           <p>
            So be careful out there when you set out to make your film. Its a complicated, interconnected and somewhat incestuous industry that revolves not only around making great movies, but also around being business savvy, knowing the right people, and playing your cards right.
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             Has any of you been to any film festivals before? Maybe you were presenting a film? What was your impression? Id love to hear some of your experiences!
            </em>
           </p>
          </span>

           <p class="first_p">
            About Roi Frey
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-125 photo" height="125" src="https://2.gravatar.com/avatar/b8cfb616e40b074c5e4e88deafbdea8b?s=125&amp;d=wavatar&amp;r=pg" srcset="https://2.gravatar.com/avatar/b8cfb616e40b074c5e4e88deafbdea8b?s=250&amp;d=wavatar&amp;r=pg 2x" width="125"/>
            Roi is a sucker for entertainment. He grew up in Israel watching funny American and British films and series, and at the age of 10 started making his own ridiculous news shows and videos on his dads VHS camera. In 2003 Rois name went down in the history books as the first model ever to ask their producer for a job as a production runner. These days Roi is an indie producer based in the Netherlands, distributing niche films, and developing a sit-com web-series as well as a number of European co-productions. He uses crowdfunding but hates it with a passion, and wishes to explore other ways of using the internet for financing and distribution of content.  

Before becoming a producer Roi was an award winning DoP, and a project manager on commercial construction projects. He also worked on a sheep station in Australia, chasing sheep across the outback on a dirtbike. He plays Lacrosse, Ultimate Frisbee and Football, and spends his free time as a board member of a charity.
