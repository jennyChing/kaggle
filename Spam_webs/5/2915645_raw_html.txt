         7 Etiquette Essentials for Afternoon Tea
        </h1>

        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/etiquette.jpg" style="display: none;"/>
        <p>
         Afternoon tea is more popular than ever, with some leading hotels holding as many as six sittings per day to meet demand. For those who want to brush up on the traditions before joining this great British ritual, we have canvassed the experts from Londons top hotels for their tips:
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea1b.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           1. Spoon
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The teaspoon should never be left in the teacup. When not in use, the small teaspoon is placed on the right side of the tea saucer.
         <strong>
          Matti Wente, afternoon tea ambassador at the
          <a href="http://www.parklanehilton.com/restaurants" target="_blank">
           London Hilton on Park Lane
          </a>
         </strong>
         , adds: As a gesture of respect to other guests you should pay attention to not touch the cup with your tea spoon, in order to avoid noise from the silver banging against the china.
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea2b.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           2. Teacup
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Mr Michael Kotb, Palm Court Manager at
          <a href="http://www.bookatable.com/uk/108845/tea-at-the-ritz">
           The Ritz
          </a>
         </strong>
         , says: Never wave or hold your tea cup in the air. When not in use, the teacup is placed back on the saucer, rather then held. The saucer will be on the table, or in ones lap if there is no table.
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea3.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <span style="font-size: medium;">
          <strong>
           3. The posh stir
          </strong>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         Do not stir your tea in sweeping circular motions. Rather, place your tea spoon at the six oclock position and softly fold the liquid towards the twelve oclock position two or three times.
         <strong>
          Michael Kotb
         </strong>
         adds another point of courtesy: When stirring your tea, do it gently and refrain from clinking the sides of your teacup.
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea4c.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           4. Milk
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         If having milk in ones tea, pouring the milk into the cup first constitutes good manners.
         <strong>
          Matti Wente
         </strong>
         , however, says there is some leeway here: In fact many tea drinkers prefer to see the colour of the tea, before they decide on the right amount of milk to get the taste of their cup to their liking.
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea5.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           5. Sugar
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Sugar cubes may be added to tea with or without milk.
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea6.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           6. Cutlery
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Forks should never be set back down on the table after eating. Instead, they are set on the side of the plate.
         <strong>
          Matti Wente
         </strong>
         adds:
        </p>
        <p>
         Even though it is nice to exchange information during tea time, you should avoid gesturing while having cutlery in your hand. It also shows respect to others to not make any scraping noise with your cutlery.
        </p>
        <img alt="" src="/blog/wp-content/uploads/axd/2011/08/tea7.png" style="float: left;"/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           7. Napkin
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         When seated at the table, place the napkin on your lap. When excusing yourself from the table, gently place the napkin to the left side of your place setting. Avoid placing it on your seat, because if the napkin is soiled the seat covering may be damaged.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.tea.co.uk/top-tea-places" target="_blank">
           The Tea Guild
          </a>
         </strong>
         recently gave its top award to
         <a href="http://www.claridges.co.uk/page.aspx?id=1792" target="_blank">
          <strong>
           afternoon tea at Claridges
          </strong>
         </a>
         , but the most commonly booked by diners on Bookatable.com, is
         <strong>
          Tea at the Ritz
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
