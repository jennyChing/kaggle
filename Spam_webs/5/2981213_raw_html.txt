      <div class="mt-story-text-share article-txt-top no-flow entry-txt">
       <p>
        <style>
         .embed-container { position: relative; padding-bottom: 56.25%; padding-top: 30px; overflow: hidden; max-width: 100%; height: auto; } .embed-container iframe, .embed-container object, .embed-container embed { position: absolute; top: 0; left: 0; width: 100%; height: 100%; }
        </style>
       </p>
       <div class="embed-container embed-responsively">
        <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/ObC0lcC-hLA">
        </iframe>
       </div>
       <p>
        One-time Boston College baseball player
        <strong>
         Pete Frates
        </strong>
        , the real-life inspiration who fueled the ALS
        <a href="http://www.alsa.org/fight-als/ice-bucket-challenge.html" target="new">
         "Ice-Bucket Challenge
        </a>
        ," somehow missed the cut when Sports Illustrated named its
        <a href="http://www.si.com/sportsman" target="new">
         20 candidates for Sportsman of the Year
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        There are the usual suspects on SI's list's of nine finalists
        <a href="http://www.si.com/sportsman/2014/12/02/vote-si-sportsman-poll?noroute=true">
         in its fan poll
        </a>
        . Among them: all-world World Series MVP
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/bumgama01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Madison  Bumgarner
          </a>
         </strong>
        </strong>
        , the MVP of the rest of the season
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/k/kershcl01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Clayton  Kershaw
          </a>
         </strong>
        </strong>
        , US soccer goalie
        <strong>
         Tim Howard
        </strong>
        , Super Bowl champ
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.pro-football-reference.com/players/W/WilsRu00.htm?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Russell  Wilson
          </a>
         </strong>
        </strong>
        , tennis star
        <strong>
         Serena Williams
        </strong>
        , golfer extraordinaire
        <strong>
         Rory McIlroy
        </strong>
        and the Stanley Cup champion LA
        <strong>
         <a href="http://www.pro-football-reference.com/players/K/Kingxx20.htm?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Kings
         </a>
        </strong>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        All worthy sportsmen or women. Football player
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.pro-football-reference.com/players/S/SamxMi00.htm?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Michael  Sam
          </a>
         </strong>
        </strong>
        also made the final cut on SI's list as well, something he didn't do in St. Louis or Dallas.
       </p>
       <p>
        There's no reason to denigrate any of the nine finalists or 20 nominees. They've all accomplished exceptional things on the field, court, ice, pitch, diamond, track or other playing surface.
       </p>
       <p>
        We'll be taking a few shots at the 2013 winner in just a bit. Stay tuned.
       </p>
       <p>
        Perhaps Frates was No. 21 on SI's list. Perhaps the people the magazine contacted to write nominations missed that whole "Ice-Bucket Challenge" thing. Or maybe they weren't aware that
        <strong>
         Pat Quinn
        </strong>
        , Frates and their friends began a social media phenomenon that raised more than $120 million for ALS research.
       </p>
       <p>
        Frates' story goes far beyond a hundred million or so that has already helped
        <a href="http://www.als.net/ALS-Research/" target="new">
         ALS researchers study new protocols
        </a>
        . He remains ever optimistic and hopeful while wrestling with a disease that has no cure and never produces a joyous ending. ALS awareness was all the rage this past summer, and the money raised because of it and people willing and not willing to get soaked should pay dividends for years. The money is there even after #IceBucketChallenge is no longer a trending topic on Twitter or polluting your in-box with videos.
       </p>
       <p>
        Frates and his wife welcomed a daughter, Lucy, on Sept. 1. He's now a dad, just like a few billion of us. Fellow BC alum
        <strong>
         Mike Lupica
        </strong>
        offered a compelling case for Frates and Quinn to share in SI's honor, or even Time's "Man of the Year" in the
        <a href="http://www.nydailynews.com/sports/more-sports/lupica-year-mvps-pat-quinn-pete-frates-article-1.2028015" target="new">
         New York Daily News
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Frates "does not quit. He keeps on coming," Lupica wrote in describing his fellow former Eagle.
       </p>
       <p>
        When Lupica asked Frates what he was feeling on Thanksgiving, Frates answered via e-mail and eye-blink technology:
       </p>
       <p>
        "Hungry. I will not be satisfied until we see this thing through. Incredibly thankful. Restored faith in God. I know why I am here."
       </p>
       <p>
        Humor, resilience, and everything else that makes the human spirit unique and special.
       </p>
       <p>
        Enough for a Tweet with eight characters to spare.
       </p>
       <p>
        Brevity is the soul of brilliance.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Phil Stacey
        </strong>
        of the Salem Evening News - which has the coolest newspaper logo ever -
        <a href="http://www.salemnews.com/sports/phil-stacey-column-pete-frates-should-be-si-s-sportsman/article_f877e8c6-126c-5150-a0e7-e23e312eb83e.html" target="new">
         began the public/print push
        </a>
        to get Frates SI's coveted award. Last month, he wrote:
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <blockquote>
          Frates, a chiseled 6-foot-2 and 220 pounds with wrists like coils of rope during his playing days, [is] now confined to a wheelchair, but stronger now that he probably even realizes.
         </blockquote>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Frankly, Frates is way too good for SI's "Sportsman of the Year" award. He's far better for not being nominated. "Sportsman of the Year" is too limiting and constrictive for Frates and what he's helped to accomplish.
       </p>
       <p>
        If you think I'm off here, let's take a quick look at SI's 2013 "Sportsman of the Year" via a Tweet from a recent Patriots game.
       </p>
       <p>
        That would be this guy:
       </p>
       <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
        <p>
         3rd and 6, 10, 11, 12 and 20
Nope, nope, nope, nope and nope. 
4th and 6, OUCH.
         <a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/NEvsDEN?src=hash">
          #NEvsDEN
         </a>
         <a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/FritosOnAPizza?src=hash">
          #FritosOnAPizza
         </a>
         <a href="http://t.co/joAgg7864N">
          pic.twitter.com/joAgg7864N
         </a>
        </p>
         Obnoxious Boston Fan (@realOBF)
        <a href="https://twitter.com/realOBF/status/529042156640800769">
         November 2, 2014
        </a>
       </blockquote>
       <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
       </script>
       <p>
        Ouch indeed. You see, putting Frates in the same category of
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.pro-football-reference.com/players/M/MannEl00.htm?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Eli  Manning
          </a>
         </strong>
         's
        </strong>
        brother would be sort of be embarrassing for all of us. What Frates has set in motion, and helped to continue with his
        <a href="https://www.facebook.com/TeamFratetrain" target="new">
         Team Frate Train,
        </a>
        cannot be measured in the same galaxy with a spokesman for Papa John's Pizza with a crushing inability to beat
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.pro-football-reference.com/players/B/BradTo00.htm?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Tom  Brady
          </a>
         </strong>
        </strong>
        when the temperature is below 40 degrees.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Sportsmanship" on the playing field and life-and-death courage in the real world share only a small portion of the same DNA. Another initial SI nominee, Marine and double-amputee
        <strong>
         Rob Jones
        </strong>
        , helped raise more than $1 million by taking an excruciating
        <a href="http://www.si.com/sportsman/2014/10/20/michael-rosenberg-sportsman-rob-jones-marine" target="new">
         5,181 bicycle ride across the United States
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Compare what Frates and Jones accomplished, and continue to accomplish, to winning the NL
        <strong>
         <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/y/youngcy01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Cy  Young
         </a>
        </strong>
        and MVP award for a team that got washed out of the playoffs in the NLDS with a $235 million payroll. It is a
        <em>
         real-world
        </em>
        non-starter.
       </p>
       <p>
        Save the trivial awards like "Sportsman of the Year" for simple sportsmen, sportswomen, or even the LA  Kings.
       </p>
       <p>
        Frates was a baseball player for BC, and three-sport star at St. John's Prep. His "Ice Bucket Challenge" didn't really take off until it was met by members of the
        <a href="http://www.cbssports.com/nhl/eye-on-hockey/24648980/watch-ice-bucket-challenge-sweeps-through-bruins-for-als-awareness" target="new">
         Bruins
        </a>
        , Patriots and
        <a href="http://youtu.be/jnugGWZd49k?t=1m8s" target="new">
         Red Sox
        </a>
        . His story was shared by millions of sports fans before it was shared by millions of humanity's fans.
       </p>
       <p>
        But he's no longer a simple athlete, or former athlete, fighting a gruesome disease. He's not someone whose success and story should be limited to the wide world of sports. Some people are far too worthy of awards because any recognition they receive pales in contrast to what they've actually accomplished.
       </p>
       <p>
        Medal of Honor recipients demonstrate this truism better than anyone.
       </p>
       <p>
        In reality, our culture is awash in awards. The entertainment industry's "award season" started with the Golden Globes in January and ended with the American Music Awards last week. Media types strain their shoulders in self-congratulation at the state, regional and nation level. We have players of the week, rookies of the month and mid-season honors. Closer to home? Just take a glance at all those "Effort" awards and "Participation" trophies on little Jimmy or Tammy Lynn's mantle for multiple examples of frivolity.
       </p>
       <p>
        Perhaps Frates has earned the ultimate participation trophy. He helped to get more than 700,000 people to donate to ALS research. He put the heartless disease front-and-center in the hearts and minds of millions more.
       </p>
       <p>
        ALS has never been the sole property of
        <strong>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/g/gehrilo01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-blogcabin.boston.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Lou  Gehrig
          </a>
         </strong>
        </strong>
        . Nor does it remain the domain of countless families who deal with its unrelenting effects every day. It has become part of all of our lives.
       </p>
       <p>
        If Frates, hopefully with a nod to Quinn, does pull off an upset of sorts and is named "Sportsman of the Year," it would be well-deserved. No doubt it would accomplish more wonder for his now life-long effort to raise money to fight ALS and increase awareness of it. There's nothing wrong with that.
       </p>
       <p>
        Of course, donations never hurt. And you can do that right here
        <a href="http://www.petefrates.com/contribute.html" target="new">
         on the Pete Frates #3 Fund Page
        </a>
        or bounce some coin directly to the
        <a href="https://secure2.convio.net/alsa/site/Donation2?df_id=27420&amp;27420.donation=form1" target="new">
         ALS Association here
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        No awards necessary.
       </p>
