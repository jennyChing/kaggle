     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Junction Boys
      </b>
      is the name given to the survivors of
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M_Aggies_football" title="Texas A&amp;M Aggies football">
       Texas A&amp;M Aggies football
      </a>
      coach
      <a href="/wiki/Bear_Bryant" title="Bear Bryant">
       Paul Bear Bryants
      </a>
      10 day summer camp in
      <a href="/wiki/Junction,_Texas" title="Junction, Texas">
       Junction, Texas
      </a>
      beginning September 1, 1954. The ordeal has achieved legendary status and has become the subject of a 2001 book
      <i>
       The Junction Boys
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MightyOrphansGoogle_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MightyOrphansGoogle-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Dent_(author)" title="Jim Dent (author)">
       Jim Dent
      </a>
      and a television movie produced by
      <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">
       ESPN
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_camp">
       The camp
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Junction_Boys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The camp">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M_University" title="Texas A&amp;M University">
       Texas A&amp;M University
      </a>
      hired
      <a href="/wiki/Bear_Bryant" title="Bear Bryant">
       Bear Bryant
      </a>
      as head football coach in 1954, replacing former coach
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_George" title="Raymond George">
       Ray George
      </a>
      . Bryant arrived in
      <a href="/wiki/College_Station,_Texas" title="College Station, Texas">
       College Station
      </a>
      on February 8, 1954 and began cleaning house. He felt that many of the players on the team were weak and not properly trained or coached. He decided that his players needed a camp away from the distractions on campus; thus, he arranged for the camp to be held in the small
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_Hill_Country" title="Texas Hill Country">
       Hill Country
      </a>
      town of Junction, where Texas A&amp;M had a 411-acre (1.7km) adjunct campus (now the
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_Tech_University_Center_at_Junction" title="Texas Tech University Center at Junction">
       Texas Tech University Center at Junction
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      At the time of the camp, the Hill Country was experiencing an epic
      <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">
       drought
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">
       heat wave
      </a>
      . The drought, the worst in the recorded history of the region, had lasted four years and would last another two after the camp was over. According to the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Climatic_Data_Center" title="National Climatic Data Center">
       National Climatic Data Center
      </a>
      , all 10 days of the camp saw hot temperatures with a couple of days topping 100F (38C).
     </p>
     <p>
      Practices began before dawn and usually lasted all day with meetings in the evening until 11:00 PM. The oppressive heat combined with the brutal practice schedule was too much for many of the players. Each day, there would be fewer and fewer players at practice, as many players quit the team from illness or disgust. The situation was compounded by Bryant's refusal to allow
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_break" title="Water break">
       water breaks
      </a>
      . This practice, which is now widely recognized as dangerous, was at the time commonly employed by athletic coaches in an attempt to "toughen up" their players. The only relief provided the players were two towels soaked in cold water; one towel was to be shared by the offensive players, and one by the defense. One of the Junction Boys, future
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NFL" title="NFL">
       NFL
      </a>
      coach
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Pardee" title="Jack Pardee">
       Jack Pardee
      </a>
      , would later say in an interview that it was not unheard of for players to sweat away 10% of their body weight.
     </p>

     <p>
      It has often been portrayed that over 100 players made the trip to Junction. In fact, a smaller number actually went to the camp. Although Bryant started out with over 100 players on the roster, many had already quit or been cut by the time of the Junction camp. Accounts of the exact number that left for Junction vary, but all the survivors insist that it was less than 100.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1954, Richard Vick had his photograph taken with Bear Bryant. The photo was featured on the cover of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Houston_Chronicle" title="Houston Chronicle">
        Houston Chronicle
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine. Today, the photo of Richard Vick and Bear Bryant, as well as Vick's uniform, can be seen at the Texas A&amp;M Sports Museum at
      <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Field" title="Kyle Field">
       Kyle Field
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, nineteen of the Junction Boys had a 54th anniversary reunion at a ranch in
      <a href="/wiki/Brenham,_Texas" title="Brenham, Texas">
       Brenham, Texas
      </a>
      . The remaining members have agreed to have a reunion every 5 years.
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2010, the surviving Junction Boys were by The Texas Children's Cancer Center at "An Evening with Texas Legends" in Houston, Texas at the Hilton Americas Hotel. They were interviewed by legendary sportswriter Mickey Herskowitz at this special event.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">
       Impact
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Junction_Boys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although the "survivors", as they came to be called, were mentally tougher after the experience, this new strength did not translate into immediate success on the field. In 1954, Texas A&amp;M won only one game against nine losses, the only losing season in Bryant's 38 years as a head coach.
     </p>

       at SMU lost 6-3
      </li>
      <li>
       vs. Rice lost 29-19
      </li>
      <li>
       at Texas lost 22-13
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In 1955 Texas A&amp;M went 7-2-1, and in 1956, the team went 9-0-1 and won the
      <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Conference" title="Southwest Conference">
       Southwest Conference
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Many of the Junction Boys went on to great success in various fields after college.
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Pardee" title="Jack Pardee">
       Jack Pardee
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gene_Stallings" title="Gene Stallings">
       Gene Stallings
      </a>
      would go on to become head coaches in the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">
       NFL
      </a>
      . Pardee was a two-time All-Pro with the
      <a href="/wiki/1971_Los_Angeles_Rams_season" title="1971 Los Angeles Rams season">
       Los Angeles Rams
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/1963_Los_Angeles_Rams_season" title="1963 Los Angeles Rams season">
       (1963)
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/1971_Washington_Redskins_season" title="1971 Washington Redskins season">
       Washington Redskins
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/1971_Washington_Redskins_season" title="1971 Washington Redskins season">
       (1971)
      </a>
      . Stallings would also become the head coach of Texas A&amp;M, where his Aggie team beat Bryant's Alabama team in the 1968
      <a href="/wiki/Cotton_Bowl_Classic" title="Cotton Bowl Classic">
       Cotton Bowl
      </a>
      , and later took over Bryant's
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama" title="University of Alabama">
       Alabama
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Crimson_Tide_football" title="Alabama Crimson Tide football">
       Crimson Tide
      </a>
      and won a national championship in
      <a href="/wiki/1992_Alabama_Crimson_Tide_football_team" title="1992 Alabama Crimson Tide football team">
       1992
      </a>
      .
     </p>
