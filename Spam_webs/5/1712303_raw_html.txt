                <p class="mce">
                 As the issue of the quality of democracy is the subject of an intense debate, any public institution should open up governmental processes to the public scrutiny. In this regard, Catalonia has just passed the test with flying colours. The Catalan governments gets full marks (100 out of 100) in the survey presented yesterday by the Spanish chapter of Transparency International. In 2013 it received 82,5 points, up from 78,8 in 2012. After measuring 80 indicators, Catalonia is number one among Spain's regions, in a tie with the Basque Country and Castile. Valencia and the Balearics also get an excellent score, with 93 points each.
                </p>
                <p class="mce">
                 A year ago the Catalan government set up a dedicated portal to collect and disclose all the information. The data available includes the remuneration of high-ranking officials and staff on government contracts. It was an important step which the authors of the survey have certainly taken into consideration. "They publish everything we ask for", admitted Jess Lizcano, president of the NGO. The index that helps to assess the various administrations on the issue of transparency is based on several parameters: from the liaison with the public to the clarity of the information provided on finances, contracts, tenders and services. According to the people behind the survey, the fact that the three Spanish regions with the highest score are ruled by different parties -CiU, the PNB and the PP- proves that this democratic duty is more about a certain attitude than a particular political affiliation.
                </p>
                <p class="mce">
                 The region with the lowest score is Madrid, with only 65 points. This is, in fact, a step backwards from previous years. Mr Lizcano admitted they were "surprised" and urged Ignacio Gonzlez's government to set up a portal similar to Catalonia's.
                </p>
                <p class="mce">
                 <strong class="mce">
                  Spain's average score is up
                 </strong>
                </p>
                <p class="mce">
                 This year's score shows that Madrid's rating has deteriorated over the years. The overall Spanish trend is, in fact, the opposite, as Spain's average has got better with time: from 71.5 points in 2010 to 86.6 this year. The main reason for the improvement, they said, lies with the new transparency law passed by Spain's parliament in 2013. The law meant that the institutions had no choice but to get down to work in order to improve the way they prepare and present the information. Because of this legislation, as a matter of fact, transparency will be one of the main priorities for local and regional authorities after the May elections next year.
                </p>
                <p class="mce">
                 Information on finances is the one area that still leaves a lot to desire. The average score is 76 out of 100. However, Transparency International sees a slight improvement which could be either an attempt to bring things up to speed or perhaps the result of the drop in overall activity. Madrid, Andalusia, Murcia and Extremadura are at the bottom of the list in this particular regard. While these figures are no indication of the possible level of corruption, transparency is a good antidote, as the authors of the study pointed out yesterday.
                </p>
