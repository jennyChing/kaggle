              Crisis at Home  How to Cope at Work
             </h1>
            </div>

          <div class="at-above-post addthis-toolbox at-wordpress-hide" data-title="Crisis at Home  How to Cope at Work" data-url="http://www.realbusiness.com/2014/10/human-resources/crisis-at-home-how-do-you-cope-at-work/">
          </div>
          <div class="at-above-post-recommended addthis-toolbox at-wordpress-hide">
          </div>
          <p class="sub-header prominent h5">
           <time class="updated" datetime="2014-10-20" pubdate="">
            October 20, 2014
           </time>
           By Sachin Shenolikar
          </p>
          <p>
           We try to keep our work lives and personal lives separate, but that can be tough to maintain when a stressful situation develops outside the office.
          </p>
          <p>
           Whether its a serious illness, a breakup with a significant other, or an argument with a friend, there are times when outside distractions will eat up your energy, and attention. Of course, you still have a job to do, and as a manager, theres the added responsibility of being there for your team.
          </p>
          <p>
           Real Business asked Jodi Glickman, founder and president of
           <a href="http://greatonthejob.com">
            Great on the Job
           </a>
           , for advice on how managers should handle work when theres a crisis at home. Here are 4 things you need to know:
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Be Transparent.
           </b>
           Meet with your direct reports to tell them that about your situation. If its going to impact your performance, let colleagues know that youre going through difficult times, without going into the grisly details, says Glickman.
          </p>
          <p>
           Part of the reason for transparency is to contain the rumor mill. Say, youre having a health issue and will need to be out for medical appointments or treatments. You dont want people wondering: Are you interviewing for another job? Are you checked out? says Glickman. The danger of not sharing information is people automatically will draw their own conclusions that A) are going to be wrong and B) are typically not favorable.
          </p>
          <p>
           Making staffers feel like they are in the loop provides comfort that you are still involved and engaged.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            There is Such a Thing as TMI.
           </b>
           Be mindful of what you share. The gist of your message should be: I have an issue and may need to be offline at various times, but Im doing okay and taking care of it.
          </p>
          <p>
           Full details are not necessary, especially if its something that people may judge, like a divorce. If your husband or wife was having an affair, thats too much information for your employees, says Glickman. The rule is if something is going to make someone else uncomfortable, you never want to share it.
          </p>
          <p>
           If youre caring for a sick parent or child, let employees know that you have to help take care of them. Its good to show that you have things out of the office that are important and that you place health as a priority, says Glickman.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Never Melt Down at Work.
           </b>
           If you are feeling upset in the office, tell colleagues you arent 100 percent.
           <b>
           </b>
           However, dont have a breakdown in front of your staff. If you feel you cant keep it together, get out of the office, go take a walk, get some air, schedule a meeting on your calendar thats not real, says Glickman. You dont want people to see you crying in your office or having an argument on the phone. Thats very undermining.
          </p>
          <p>
           If youre unable to leave the office, turn to compartmentalization. Think, okay, this is awful, but for two hours I need to focus on work, says Glickman. Break your day into things that are manageable, such as getting through a presentation or meeting, and then take a break.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Be There for Your Employees.
           </b>
           What if its your employee who is going through the tough situation? As a manager, dont ask for details, but do let the employee know you are there for them and there are resources for them, says Glickman. Say, take the time you need and let us know what we can do.
          </p>
          <p>
           Figure out ways for the employee to handle their situation and then work on their own time. If the issue severely impacts their productivity, short-term leave or vacation might be necessary.
          </p>
