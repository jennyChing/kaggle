   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog
   </h1>

     <p>
      "
      <b>
       The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog
      </b>
      " is an
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English-language
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Pangram" title="Pangram">
       pangram
      </a>
      a
      <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
       phrase
      </a>
      that contains all of the letters of the
      <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
       alphabet
      </a>
      . It is commonly used for touch-typing practice. It is also used to test
      <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">
       typewriters
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_keyboard" title="Computer keyboard">
       computer keyboards
      </a>
      , show
      <a href="/wiki/Font" title="Font">
       fonts
      </a>
      , and other applications involving all of the letters in the English alphabet. Owing to its brevity and coherence, it has become widely known.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_quick_brown_fox_jumps_over_the_lazy_dog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The earliest known appearance of the phrase is from
      <i>
       The Michigan School Moderator
      </i>
      , a journal that provided many teachers with education-related news and suggestions for lessons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an article titled "Interesting Notes" in the March 14, 1885 issue, the phrase is given as a suggestion for writing practice: "The following sentence makes a good copy for practice, as it contains every letter of the alphabet: 'A quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Note that the phrase in this case begins with the word "A" rather than "The". Several other early sources also use this variation.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the use of typewriters grew in the late 19th century, the phrase began appearing in typing and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stenography" title="Stenography">
       stenography
      </a>
      lesson books as a practice
      <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">
       sentence
      </a>
      . Early examples of publications which used the phrase include
      <i>
       Illustrative Shorthand
      </i>
      by Linda Bronson (1888),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       How to Become Expert in Typewriting: A Complete Instructor Designed Especially for the Remington Typewriter
      </i>
      (1890),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <i>
       Typewriting Instructor and Stenographer's Hand-book
      </i>
      (1892). By the turn of the 20th century, the phrase had become widely known. In the January 10, 1903, issue of
      <i>
       Pitman's Phonetic Journal
      </i>
      , it is referred to as "the well known memorized typing line embracing all the letters of the alphabet".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Baden-Powell,_1st_Baron_Baden-Powell" title="Robert Baden-Powell, 1st Baron Baden-Powell">
       Robert Baden-Powell
      </a>
      's book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scouting_for_Boys" title="Scouting for Boys">
        Scouting for Boys
       </a>
      </i>
      (1908) uses the phrase as a practice sentence for signaling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfb_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sfb-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first message sent on the
      <a href="/wiki/Moscow%E2%80%93Washington_hotline" title="MoscowWashington hotline">
       MoscowWashington hotline
      </a>
      was the test phrase "THE QUICK BROWN FOX JUMPED OVER THE LAZY DOG'S BACK 1234567890".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, during testing, the Russian translators sent a message asking their American counterparts "What does it mean when your people say 'The quick brown fox jumped over the lazy dog?'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the 20th century, technicians tested typewriters and
      <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">
       teleprinters
      </a>
      with repeated lines of "THE QUICK BROWN FOX..." sentence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_usage">
       Computer usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_quick_brown_fox_jumps_over_the_lazy_dog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Computer usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the age of computers, this pangram is commonly used to display
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sample_font_displays_in_other_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sample font displays in other languages (page does not exist)">
       font samples
      </a>
      and for testing
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_keyboard" title="Computer keyboard">
       computer keyboards
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">
       cryptography
      </a>
      , it is commonly used as a test vector for hash and encryption algorithms to verify their implementation, as well as to ensure alphabetic character set compatibility.
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">
       Microsoft Word
      </a>
      has a command to auto-type the sentence, in versions up to Word 2003, using the command
      <i>
       =rand()
      </i>
      , and in Microsoft Office Word 2007 and later using the command
      <i>
       =rand.old()
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">
       Cultural references
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_quick_brown_fox_jumps_over_the_lazy_dog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural references">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Numerous passing references to the phrase have occurred in movies, television, books, video games, advertising, websites, and graphic arts.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Lipogram" title="Lipogram">
       lipogrammatic
      </a>
      novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ella_Minnow_Pea" title="Ella Minnow Pea">
        Ella Minnow Pea
       </a>
      </i>
      by Mark Dunn is built entirely around the "Quick Brown Fox" pangram and its inventor. It depicts a fictional country off the
      <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">
       South Carolina
      </a>
      coast that idealizes the pangram, chronicling the effects on literature and social structure as various letters are banned from daily use by government dictum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
