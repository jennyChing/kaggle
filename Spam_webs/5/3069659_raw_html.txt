   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Barabbas
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      This article is about the biblical character Barabbas.  For other uses, see
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Barabbas_(disambiguation)" title="Barabbas (disambiguation)">
       Barabbas (disambiguation)
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GiveUsBarabbas.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1382" data-file-width="2022" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/GiveUsBarabbas.png/360px-GiveUsBarabbas.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/GiveUsBarabbas.png/540px-GiveUsBarabbas.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/GiveUsBarabbas.png/720px-GiveUsBarabbas.png 2x" width="360"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GiveUsBarabbas.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        "Give us Barabbas!", from
        <i>
         The Bible and its Story Taught by One Thousand Picture Lessons
        </i>
        , 1910
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Barabbas
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Jesus Barabbas
      </b>
      (a Hellenization of the Aramaic
      <i>
       bar abba
      </i>
       , literally "son of the father" or "Jesus, son of the Father" respectively) is a figure in the accounts of the
      <a href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" title="Passion (Christianity)">
       Passion of Christ
      </a>
      , in which he is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insurrection" title="Insurrection">
       insurrectionary
      </a>
      whom
      <a href="/wiki/Pontius_Pilate" title="Pontius Pilate">
       Pontius Pilate
      </a>
      freed at the
      <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">
       Passover
      </a>
      feast in
      <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">
       Jerusalem
      </a>
      , instead of
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels#Canonical_gospels" title="Gospels">
       canonical gospels
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-canonical" title="Non-canonical">
       non-canonical
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Peter" title="Gospel of Peter">
       Gospel of Peter
      </a>
      there was a prevailing Passover custom in Jerusalem that allowed or required Pilate, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prefect#Ancient_Rome" title="Prefect">
        praefectus
       </a>
      </i>
      or governor of
      <a href="/wiki/Judea_(Roman_province)" title="Judea (Roman province)">
       Judea
      </a>
      , to commute one prisoner's death sentence by popular acclaim, and the "crowd" (
      <i>
       ochlos
      </i>
      ), "the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">
       Jews
      </a>
      " and "the multitude" in some sources, were offered a choice of whether to have either Barabbas or Jesus released from Roman custody. According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_Gospels" title="Synoptic Gospels">
       Synoptic Gospels
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">
       Matthew
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans2012_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Evans2012-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">
       Mark
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">
       Luke
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the accounts in
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">
       John
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Peter" title="Gospel of Peter">
       Gospel of Peter
      </a>
      , the crowd chose Barabbas to be released and Jesus of Nazareth to be crucified. Pilate is portrayed as reluctantly yielding to the insistence of the crowd. A passage found only in the Gospel of Matthew has the crowd saying,
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_curse" title="Blood curse">
       "Let his blood be upon us and upon our children."
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The story of Barabbas has special social significance because it has historically been used to lay the blame for the
      <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">
       crucifixion of Jesus
      </a>
      on the Jews, and to justify
      <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">
       antisemitism
      </a>
      an interpretation, known as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_deicide" title="Jewish deicide">
        Jewish deicide
       </a>
      </i>
      , dismissed by Pope
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedict_XVI" title="Benedict XVI">
       Benedict XVI
      </a>
      in his 2011 book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth_(book)" title="Jesus of Nazareth (book)">
        Jesus of Nazareth
       </a>
      </i>
      , in which he changes the translation of "
      <i>
       ochlos
      </i>
      " in Matthew to mean the "crowd", rather than to mean the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_people" title="Jewish people">
       Jewish people
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JRPB16_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JRPB16-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_account">
       Biblical account
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barabbas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biblical account">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Matthew refers to Barabbas only as a "notorious prisoner".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mark and Luke further refer to Barabbas as one involved in a
      <i>
       stasis
      </i>
      , a riot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Robert Eisenman states that John 18:40 refers to Barabbas as a
      <i>
       lsts
      </i>
      ("bandit"), "the word
      <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">
       Josephus
      </a>
      always employs when talking about Revolutionaries".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenman_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eisenman-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">
       gospels
      </a>
      state that there was a custom at
      <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">
       Passover
      </a>
      during which the Roman governor would release a prisoner of the crowd's choice;
      <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap">
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+15%3A6&amp;version=ESV" rel="nofollow">
        Mark 15:6
       </a>
      </span>
      ,
      <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap">
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+27%3A15&amp;version=ESV" rel="nofollow">
        Matthew 27:15
       </a>
      </span>
      , and
      <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap">
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+18%3A39&amp;version=ESV" rel="nofollow">
        John 18:39
       </a>
      </span>
      . Later copies of Luke contain a corresponding verse (
      <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap">
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Luke+23%3A17&amp;version=ESV" rel="nofollow">
        Luke 23:17
       </a>
      </span>
      ), although this is not present in the earliest manuscripts, and may be a later
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_gloss#Glosses_as_textual_additions" title="Biblical gloss">
       gloss
      </a>
      to bring Luke into conformity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The custom of releasing prisoners in Jerusalem at Passover is known as the
      <i>
       Paschal Pardon
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but this custom (whether at Passover or any other time) is not recorded in any historical document other than the gospels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
       Name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barabbas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barabbas_(James_Tissot).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="659" data-file-width="670" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Barabbas_%28James_Tissot%29.jpg/220px-Barabbas_%28James_Tissot%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Barabbas_%28James_Tissot%29.jpg/330px-Barabbas_%28James_Tissot%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Barabbas_%28James_Tissot%29.jpg/440px-Barabbas_%28James_Tissot%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barabbas_(James_Tissot).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Portrait of Barabbas by
        <a href="/wiki/James_Tissot" title="James Tissot">
         James Tissot
        </a>
        (18361902)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Barabbas's name appears as
      <i>
       bar-Abbas
      </i>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      texts of the gospels. It is derived ultimately from the
      <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">
       Aramaic
      </a>
      -,
      <i>
       Bar-abb
      </i>
      , "son of the father". Some ancient manuscripts of Matthew 27:1617 have the full name of Barabbas as "Jesus Barabbas" and this was probably the name as originally written in the text.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Evans-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Early
      <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">
       church father
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">
       Origen
      </a>
      was troubled by the fact that his copies of the gospels gave Barabbas' name as "Jesus Barabbas" and declared that since it was impossible he could have had such a holy name, "Jesus" must have been added to Barabbas' name by a heretic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dimont_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dimont-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is highly likely that later scribes, copying the passage, removed the name "Jesus" from "Jesus Barabbas" to avoid dishonour to the name of Jesus the Messiah.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Abba
      </i>
      has been found as a personal name in a 1st-century burial at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giv%27at_ha-Mivtar" title="Giv'at ha-Mivtar">
       Giv'at ha-Mivtar
      </a>
      , and
      <i>
       Abba
      </i>
      also appears as a personal name frequently in the
      <a href="/wiki/Gemara" title="Gemara">
       Gemara
      </a>
      section of the
      <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">
       Talmud
      </a>
      , dating from AD 200400.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It could be argued that these findings support "Barabbas" being used to indicate the son of a person named Abba or
      <a href="/wiki/Abbas_(name)" title="Abbas (name)">
       Abbas
      </a>
      (a
      <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">
       patronymic
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (March 2015)">
          original research?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_interpretations">
       Other interpretations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barabbas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other interpretations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to historian
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Dimont" title="Max Dimont">
       Max Dimont
      </a>
      , the story of Barabbas as related in the gospels lacks credibility from the Roman standpoint, as it presents the Roman authority, Pontius Pilate, backed by overwhelming military might, being cowed by a small crowd of unarmed civilians into releasing a prisoner condemned to death for insurrection against the Roman empire. Any Roman governor who had done that would have swiftly faced execution himself, according to Dimont.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dimont_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dimont-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Urrutia" title="Benjamin Urrutia">
       Benjamin Urrutia
      </a>
      , co-author of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Logia_of_Yeshua" title="The Logia of Yeshua">
        The Logia of Yeshua
       </a>
       : The Sayings of Jesus
      </i>
      , agrees with a well known theory in biblical scholarship
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dimont_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dimont-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as presented for instance by
      <a href="/wiki/Hyam_Maccoby" title="Hyam Maccoby">
       Hyam Maccoby
      </a>
      , which says that Yeshua Bar Abba or Jesus Barabbas must be none other than Jesus of Nazareth, and that the choice between two prisoners is a fiction. However, Urrutia opposes the notion that Jesus may have either led or planned a violent insurrection. Jesus was a strong advocate of "turning the other cheek". Jesus, in this view, must have been the planner and leader of the Jewish nonviolent resistance to Pilate's plan to set up Roman Eagle standards on
      <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">
       Jerusalem's
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Temple_Mount" title="Temple Mount">
       Temple Mount
      </a>
      . The story of this successful resistance is told by
      <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">
       Josephus
      </a>
      who does not say who the leader was, but
      <a href="/wiki/Josephus_on_Jesus" title="Josephus on Jesus">
       does tell of Pilate's crucifixion of Jesus
      </a>
      just two paragraphs later in a passage whose authenticity is heavily disputed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Story_as_a_possible_parable">
       Story as a possible parable
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barabbas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Story as a possible parable">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This practice of releasing a prisoner is said by Magee
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and others to be an element in a literary creation of Mark, who needed to have a contrast to the true "son of the father" in order to set up an edifying contest, in a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Parable" title="Parable">
       parable
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Dennis_MacDonald" title="Dennis MacDonald">
       Dennis R. MacDonald
      </a>
      , in
      <i>
       The Homeric Epics and the Gospel of Mark
      </i>
      , notes that a similar episode to the one that occurs in Markof a crowd picking one figure over another figure similar to the otheroccurred in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Odyssey" title="The Odyssey">
        The Odyssey
       </a>
      </i>
      , where
      <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">
       Odysseus
      </a>
      entered the palace disguised as a beggar and defeated his wife's suitors to reclaim his throne.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jesus_and_Barabbas_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jesus_and_Barabbas-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      MacDonald suggests Mark borrowed from this section of
      <i>
       The Odyssey
      </i>
      and used it to pen the Barabbas tale, only this time Jesus  the protagonist  loses to highlight the cruelty of Jesus' persecutors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jesus_and_Barabbas_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jesus_and_Barabbas-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, this theory is rejected by other scholars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
