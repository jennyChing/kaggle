     <p>
      <b>
       News design
      </b>
      is the process of arranging material on a
      <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">
       newspaper
      </a>
      page, according to editorial and graphical guidelines and goals. Main editorial goals include the ordering of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/News_story" title="News story">
       news stories
      </a>
      by order of importance, while graphical considerations include
      <a href="/wiki/Readability" title="Readability">
       readability
      </a>
      and balanced, unobtrusive incorporation of
      <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">
       advertising
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      News design incorporates principles of
      <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">
       graphic design
      </a>
      and is taught as part of
      <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">
       journalism
      </a>
      training in schools and colleges. Overlapping and related terms include
      <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">
       layout
      </a>
      , makeup (formerly
      <a href="/wiki/Paste_up" title="Paste up">
       paste up
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Pagination" title="Pagination">
       pagination
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The era of modern newspapers begins in the mid-nineteenth century, with the
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">
       Industrial Revolution
      </a>
      , and increased capacities for
      <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
       printing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(business)" title="Distribution (business)">
       distribution
      </a>
      . Over time, improvements in printing technology, graphical design, and editorial standards have led to changes and improvements in the look and readability of newspapers. Nineteenth-century newspapers were often densely packed with type, often arranged vertically, with multiple headlines for each article. A number of the same technological limitations persisted until the advent of digital
      <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">
       typesetting
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pagination" title="Pagination">
       pagination
      </a>
      in late 20th century.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Process">
       Process
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=News_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Process">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Designers typically use
      <a href="/wiki/Desktop_publishing" title="Desktop publishing">
       desktop publishing
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">
       software
      </a>
      to arrange the elements on the pages directly. In the past, before digital pre-press pagination, designers used precise "lay out dummies" to direct the exact layout of elements for each page.
     </p>
     <p>
      A complete layout dummy was required for designating proper column widths by which a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typesetter" title="Typesetter">
       typesetter
      </a>
      would set type, and arrange columns of text. Layout also required the calculation of lengths of copy (text in "
      <a href="/wiki/Column_inch" title="Column inch">
       column inches
      </a>
      "), for any chosen width.
     </p>
     <p>
      Much of the variance and incoherence of early newspapers was due to the fact that last minute corrections were exclusively handled by typesetters. With photographic printing process, typesetting gave way to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paste-up" title="Paste-up">
       paste-up
      </a>
      , whereby columns of type were printed by machines (
      <a href="/wiki/Phototypesetting" title="Phototypesetting">
       phototypesetters
      </a>
      ) on high-resolution film for paste-up on photographed final prints. These prints in turn were "shot to negative" with a large format production camera directly to steel-
      <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">
       emulsion
      </a>
      photographic plates.
     </p>
     <p>
      Though paste-up put an end to cumbersome typesetting, this still required planned layouts and set column widths. Photographic plates are (still) wrapped on printing drums to directly apply ink to
      <a href="/wiki/Newsprint" title="Newsprint">
       newsprint
      </a>
      (paper). In the mid-1990s, the paste-up process gave way to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_to_plate" title="Direct to plate">
       direct to plate
      </a>
      process, where computer-paginated files were optically transmitted directly to the photographic plate. Replacing several in-between steps in newspaper production, direct to plate pagination allowed for much more flexibility and precision than before. Designers today still used column grid layouts only with layout software, such as Adobe InDesign or Quark.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_options">
       Design options
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=News_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design options">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Designers choose photo sizes and headline sizes (both the size of the letters and how much space the headline will take). They may decide what articles will go on which pages, and where on the page, alone or in consultation with editors. They may choose
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typefaces" title="Typefaces">
       typefaces
      </a>
      for special pages, but newspapers usually have a design style that determines most routine uses.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_news_designers">
       Notable news designers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=News_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable news designers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      John E. Allen in
      <i>
       Linotype News
      </i>
      of the 1930s was the first to write extensively about the design of the U.S. press, followed at mid century by Syracuse journalism professor
      <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Arnold" title="Edmund Arnold">
       Edmund Arnold
      </a>
      , sometimes identified as the father of "modern" newspaper design, and journalist Harold Evans played a key role in British news design later in the century.
