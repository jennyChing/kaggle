     <p>
      <b>
       Guy Murchie
      </b>
      (Jr.) (25 January 1907 - 8 July 1997), the son of Ethel A. and Guy Murchie Sr.,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairways_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fairways-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BG1955_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BG1955-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was a writer,
      <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">
       Chicago Tribune
      </a>
      photographer, staff artist and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reporter" title="Reporter">
       reporter
      </a>
      , who had served as a
      <a href="/wiki/War_correspondent" title="War correspondent">
       war correspondent
      </a>
      . He was briefly married to
      <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Cooney" title="Barbara Cooney">
       Barbara Cooney
      </a>
      (19441947), with whom he shared two children (Gretel and Barnaby).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skidompha_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Skidompha-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was a
      <a href="/wiki/Flight_instructor" title="Flight instructor">
       flight instructor
      </a>
      and a practising member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bah' Faith">
       Bah' Faith
      </a>
      . His books included
      <i>
       Men on the Horizon
      </i>
      (1932), and
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Month_Club" title="Book of the Month Club">
       Book of the Month Club
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CT-Obit_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CT-Obit-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      members
      <i>
       Song of the Sky
      </i>
      (1954),
      <i>
       Music of the Spheres
      </i>
      (1961), and
      <i>
       The Seven Mysteries of Life
      </i>
      (1978). Murchie also illustrated his books with
      <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">
       etchings
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">
       woodcuts
      </a>
      of his own design.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Up_to_his_first_book_and_marriage">
       Up to his first book and marriage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Murchie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Up to his first book and marriage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Guy Sr was a Harvard graduate,
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">
       Harvard Law School
      </a>
      graduate, in Theordore Roosevelt's
      <a href="/wiki/Rough_Riders" title="Rough Riders">
       Rough Riders
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marshall" title="US Marshall">
       US Marshall
      </a>
      , an prominent attourney in Boston
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuySr_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GuySr-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was an attourney serving
      <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">
       Winston Churchill
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His mother was the first woman designer for equipping a seaplane for
      <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_Aircraft" title="Sikorsky Aircraft">
       Sikorsky Aircraft
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sitting President Theodore Roosevelt and his wife attended Guy Jr.'s christening.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His parents set a high bar of activity in life.
     </p>
     <p>
      Guy Jr was raised as an Episcopalian,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IamaBahai_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IamaBahai-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      attended
      <a href="/wiki/Kent_School" title="Kent School">
       Kent School
      </a>
      , which at the time was just "for Boys", graduated from Harvard in 1929
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Parker-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as an impressive 6' 6" and 225lbs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BG1955_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BG1955-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      But from this heritage of privilege and physical capacity he instead turned to traveling and making his own way - neither did he return to the arena of Bostonian privilege. Instead he left before receiving his credentials on a trip headed to the far-east. Murchie returned to the States in 1930. From the experience he gained a deep seated appreciation for the basic common humanity across any divide of culture,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IamaBahai_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IamaBahai-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in 1932 published his first book (and his own illustrations) -
      <i>
       Men on the Horizon
      </i>
      in Boston
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOTH_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MOTH-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and London,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      dedicated it to his mother, and married Eleanor Forrester Parker Cushman.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BG1955_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BG1955-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Parker-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cushman was some 26 years his senior. Though the marriage eventually failed Murchie dedicated a book of his in later years to her after she died in 1960.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Men_on_the_Horizon">
       Men on the Horizon
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Murchie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Men on the Horizon">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_E._Byrd" title="Richard E. Byrd">
       Richard E. Byrd
      </a>
      wrote the forward on the heels of his first Antarctic expedition - and wrote of first being interested in the "clean man" of Murchie, and did not take sides in Murchie's views on the governments of the day but was very struck by the encounter with people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOTH_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MOTH-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Murchie traveled as a "common man" from Boston to Panama, San Francisco, Alaska, Hawaii, and then down the orient side of the Pacific to Japan, the Philippines, and China working as an able seaman.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From China he traveled inland into Russia where he passed as a peasant and back to Korea and then all the way to Moscow and then into Poland as a laborer. He was struck by the generosity of the poor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IamaBahai_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-IamaBahai-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While in the Orient he studied Buddhism and found it generally spiritual.
     </p>
     <p>
      The book was generally well received in America,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      United Kingdom,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Australia,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and New Zealand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Journalist">
       Journalist
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Murchie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Journalist">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A newspaper story of his was published in the "Winnipeg Tribune" in 1934,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a piece in New Zealand in 1935,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but his steady job was at the
      <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">
       Chicago Tribune
      </a>
      . Some 37 news stories were then printed in the Chicago Tribune under his name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CT37_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CT37-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first story under his name at the Chicago Tribune was in 1934, followed by three in 1935, 1 in 1936, (the same year he earned his pilot's license,)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BG1955_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-BG1955-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      none in 1937, and two in 1938. Some stories of his were noted in other newspapers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Seven_Mysteries_of_Life">
       Seven Mysteries of Life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guy_Murchie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Seven Mysteries of Life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1978 he published
      <i>
       The Seven Mysteries of Life
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it was reprinted in 1981
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 1999.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Parts of the book appeared in
      <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_News" title="Bah' News">
       Bah' News
      </a>
      as well as other periodicals like the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Farmer%27s_Almanac" title="Old Farmer's Almanac">
       Old Farmer's Almanac
      </a>
      . The "Seven Mysteries" are: abstraction, interraliation, omnipresence, polarity, transcendence, germination and divinity. It discusses senses, super-organism, language, properties of the mind, and individual consciousness. His review of literature identified some 32 senses which has been noted online.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In it he quoted a Serbian proverb which is seen as part of the heritage of the idea put forth as
      <i>
       We are made of star stuff
      </i>
      : "Be humble for you are made of dung. Be noble for you are made of stars."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">

      published a string of articles by Murchie - a couple new
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and eight reprints from 1978.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">

      <a href="/wiki/Fullerton,_California" title="Fullerton, California">
       Fullerton, California
      </a>
