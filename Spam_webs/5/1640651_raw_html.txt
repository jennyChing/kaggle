       <h2>
        2004-2008 Renewed Volcanic Activity
       </h2>
       <div class="image_mngr" flags="tl" image_id="781" iw="350" style="float:left;margin:1em;">
       </div>
       <p>
        The sudden reawakening of Mount St. Helens in late September 2004 was surprising 
because the 
preceding four years had seen the fewest
        <a class="glossary" gid="85" href="/vsc/glossary/seismicity.html" title="View glossary.">
         earthquakes
        </a>
        since the 1980-86 eruption 
ended.  In the early 
hours of 23 September 2004, a
        <a class="glossary" gid="107" href="/vsc/glossary/seismic_swarm.html" title="View glossary.">
         swarm
        </a>
        of small-magnitude (earthquakes (lava
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         dome
        </a>
        . Over the next 
seven days,
        <a class="glossary" gid="85" href="/vsc/glossary/seismicity.html" title="View glossary.">
         earthquake
        </a>
        frequency and size increased and a growing welt formed on the SE 
margin of the 1980-86 
lava dome and nearby portions of Crater
        <a class="glossary" gid="113" href="/vsc/glossary/glacier.html" title="View glossary.">
         Glacier
        </a>
        . By September 26, 2004, the rise 
in activity led 
scientists to warn of an increased chance of explosions from the lava dome.
       </p>
       <br style="clear:both"/>
       <video controls="" style="max-width:400px;">
        <source src="/vsc/movies/movie_99/2004_Oct_1_720.mp4">
         <div style="float:left;margin:.5em;width:400px;">
          <div class="image_mngr" image_id="788" iw="400">
          </div>
          <a href="/vsc/movies/movie_99/2004_Oct_1_720.mp4">
           View Movie
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Your browser does not support HTML 5 video.
         </i>
        </source>
       </video>
       <div class="image_mngr" flags="tl" image_id="764" iw="300" style="float:right;margin:0.2em;">
       </div>
       <br style="clear:both"/>
       <p>
        On October 1, 2004, the first of several explosions shot a plume of volcanic
        <a class="glossary" gid="3" href="/vsc/glossary/ash_volcanic.html" title="View glossary.">
         ash
        </a>
        and gases into the 
atmosphere from a
        <a class="glossary" gid="56" href="/vsc/glossary/vent.html" title="View glossary.">
         vent
        </a>
        on the southwest margin of the growing welt.  Four 
additional steam and ash 
explosions occurred through October 5th, and three produced noticeable
        <a class="glossary" gid="25" href="/vsc/glossary/fallout.html" title="View glossary.">
         fallout
        </a>
        of fine ash downwind.
       </p>
       <div class="image_mngr" flags="tl" image_id="855" iw="350" style="float:right;margin:1em;">
       </div>
       <div class="image_mngr" flags="tl" image_id="865" iw="350" style="float:right;margin:1em;">
       </div>
       <h4>
        Lava Dome Growth
       </h4>
       <p>
        Following the brief series of small steam-and-
        <a class="glossary" gid="3" href="/vsc/glossary/ash_volcanic.html" title="View glossary.">
         ash
        </a>
        explosions, the volcano 
settled into a quieter phase 
of activity. On October 11, 2004, spines of solid, but still hot,
        <a class="glossary" gid="32" href="/vsc/glossary/lava.html" title="View glossary.">
         lava
        </a>
        punctured 
the surface of the 
deformed
        <a class="glossary" gid="113" href="/vsc/glossary/glacier.html" title="View glossary.">
         glacier
        </a>
        , initiating a new
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         dome
        </a>
        -building phase of activity.
       </p>
       <p>
        By late October, a larger whaleback-shaped extrusion of solid
        <a class="glossary" gid="32" href="/vsc/glossary/lava.html" title="View glossary.">
         lava
        </a>
        (called a 
spine) had emerged from 
the crater floor. During the 3+ years of the eruption a series of hot, solid, 
smooth-sided lava spines 
rose from the
        <a class="glossary" gid="56" href="/vsc/glossary/vent.html" title="View glossary.">
         vent
        </a>
        , bulldozed their way across the crater floor, and piled up to 
form a new
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         dome
        </a>
        460 m 
(1,500 ft) high.
       </p>
       <p>
        In contrast to the 1980s, when the
        <a class="glossary" gid="32" href="/vsc/glossary/lava.html" title="View glossary.">
         lava
        </a>
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         dome
        </a>
        grew by
        <a class="glossary" gid="19" href="/vsc/glossary/effusive_eruption.html" title="View glossary.">
         effusion
        </a>
        of thick lava 
flows, the 20042008 dome 
grew by continuous extrusion of degassed lava spines that had mostly solidified 
at less than 1 km (0.62 
mi) beneath the surface. From October 2004 to late January 2008, about 92 
million cubic meters (121 
million cubic yards, or 36,800 Olympic swimming pools) of lava had erupted onto 
the crater 
floor This would be enough to pave seven highway lanes three feet thick 
from New York City to 
Portland, Oregon and is roughly equivalent to the volume of the 198086 
lava dome. By 2013, 
the two
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         domes
        </a>
        refilled about 7% of the Mount St. Helens crater.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="/volcanoes/st_helens/st_helens_geo_hist_104.html">
         Crater Glacier
        </a>
        , 
which formed after 
1986 between the south crater wall and the 1980-86
        <a class="glossary" gid="32" href="/vsc/glossary/lava.html" title="View glossary.">
         lava
        </a>
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         dome
        </a>
        , had only a small 
influence on growing 
lava spines, but was dramatically affected by the new dome growth.  The lava 
spines divided the
        <a class="glossary" gid="113" href="/vsc/glossary/glacier.html" title="View glossary.">
         glacier
        </a>
        into two arms piling it upon itself pushing it to flow rapidly northward toward 
the front of the 1980 
breach (or amphitheater).
       </p>
       <h4>
        Explosive Eruptions
       </h4>
       <p>
       </p>
       <div class="image_mngr" flags="tl" image_id="964" iw="400" style="float:right;margin:1em;">
       </div>
       The 2004-2008 eruption was notable in its lack of explosive events.  The first 
of only two significant 
explosions occurred on January 16, 2005, at the start of a winter storm.  There 
were no recognizable 
precursors. Monitoring instruments located in the crater were destroyed, and a 
few others stopped for 
hours- or seconds-long intervals, probably as a result of
       <a class="glossary" gid="3" href="/vsc/glossary/ash_volcanic.html" title="View glossary.">
        ash
       </a>
       in the air and on 
instruments, blocking 
radio signals.  Ejected
       <a class="glossary" gid="119" href="/vsc/glossary/bomb.html" title="View glossary.">
        ballistic
       </a>
       blocks landed hundreds of meters away, and ash 
fell on the volcano's 
flanks.
       <p>
        The second explosion occurred under good viewing conditions late on the 
afternoon of March 8, 2005.  
Again, there were no recognizable precursors, but there was a very subtle 
increase in
        <a class="glossary" gid="85" href="/vsc/glossary/seismicity.html" title="View glossary.">
         seismicity
        </a>
        a few 
hours before the event.  A dense
        <a class="glossary" gid="3" href="/vsc/glossary/ash_volcanic.html" title="View glossary.">
         ash
        </a>
        cloud rose from the crater and
        <a class="glossary" gid="119" href="/vsc/glossary/bomb.html" title="View glossary.">
         ballistic
        </a>
        -
impact craters were 
observed in the snow on the north flank of the old
        <a class="glossary" gid="17" href="/vsc/glossary/dome.html" title="View glossary.">
         dome
        </a>
        . A dominantly white 
vapor cloud drifted toward 
the east-northeast, reaching an altitude of 11 km (6.8 mi).  Minor amounts of 
ash were erupted, and 
dustings were reported in Ellensburg, Yakima, and Toppenish, Washington as far 
as 150 km (93 mi) 
from the volcano.
       </p>
