               <p dir="ltr">
                Trike meets fattie in the 55.8-pound aluminum
                <a href="http://riderungu.com/juggernaut/" target="_blank">
                 Rungu Juggernaut
                </a>
                , a bike designed to take you up and over pretty much anything.
               </p>
               <p dir="ltr">
                The bike comes with two forks, 26-inch wheels, and 4.7-inch tires. According to Rungu, the added third wheel improves float in the snow up to 50 percent and the bike can "overcome obstacles up to six-inches tall at crawl speed." Let's just say were excited to test those claims this winter.
               </p>
               <p dir="ltr">
                Need even more power to conquer the trails? The Juggernaut is fixed with mounting points for electric-bike kits.
               </p>

                      &lt;p&gt;If I&rsquo;m backpacking to my surf spot,&nbsp;I want&nbsp;the lightest, most buoyant board possible. It also needs to be tough,&nbsp;because if (really when) I knock a hole in it, I&rsquo;m not going to be able to do an elaborate backcountry repair. &lt;a href="http://varialsurfboards.com/" target="_blank"&gt;Varial Surfboards&lt;/a&gt; hopes to check all those boxes with its&nbsp;innovative&nbsp;new board material.&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;Varial doesn&#39;t use&nbsp;your standard polyurethane or epoxy. It uses&nbsp;something else entirely&mdash;although, and here&#39;s the rub, the boardmakers won&rsquo;t say what, just that&nbsp;it&#39;s&nbsp;from the aerospace industry. From testing, I&#39;ve found that&nbsp;it&rsquo;s strong as hell. According to the company, boards made with this material don&rsquo;t need a wooden stringer down the middle, making them 25 percent lighter but still&nbsp;20 percent stronger than a normal board. The material&nbsp;is&nbsp;closed-cell foam, so even if you end up with a sizeable ding, you can keep surfing because it&rsquo;s not going to soak through. And it&rsquo;s just as easy to shape as regular foam, so there&rsquo;s no learning curve.&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;I used a 5&rsquo; 8&rdquo; Rusty Dwart made with Varial foam, and found it to be incredibly responsive and light. I now want all my boards to be made of this stuff.&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;$TBD (Ask your shaper), &lt;a href="http://varialsurfboards.com/" target="_blank"&gt;varialsurfboards.com&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

              &lt;div class="ad-block-inner"&gt;&lt;/div&gt; &lt;!-- /.ad-block-inner --&gt;
            &lt;/div&gt; &lt;!-- /.ad-block --&gt;
          
                      &lt;p&gt;Designed by a Slovakian architecture firm, the Ecocapsule is the ultimate in small, (nearly) self-contained living. The egg-shaped cabin, which comfortably houses two adults, features a kitchenette and bathroom with running water, a foldout bed, and a small living and working area. Storage is accessible from the exterior.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Because the Ecocapsule was designed for the backcountry, there&rsquo;s no need to worry about utilities. The exterior is covered with solar cells and has a telescoping wind turbine, providing up to 600 watts of solar and 750 watts of wind power. The unit&rsquo;s unique shape collects rainwater, which is stored in a tank underneath the main living area.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;No details on pricing yet, but the cost should be determined by the end of 2015. A camper version of the Ecocapsule could be available as soon as late 2016.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;$TBD, &lt;a href="http://www.ecocapsule.sk/" target="_blank"&gt;ecocapsule.sk&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
