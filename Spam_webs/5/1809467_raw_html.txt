        <div class="blog-content blog-content-mce message-body">
         <p>
          We all have read or been told how important a strong password is to maintain your online safety. There are numerous rules of thumb to follow such as mixing upper and lower case letters, including numbers and special characters, and not using the same password on everything. Of course, the problem is that it can be difficult to remember all of your passwords. Software, hardware, and other methods of storing and/or remembering your password look to solve the problem but they usually just end up becoming cumbersome or difficult to remember.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          Im going to share a simple and easy way to remember your password and create a unique password for every site you visit.
         </p>
         <p>
          The key to this method is a single number. It can be any number you like as long as you can remember what it is. Lets begin with the number 20.
         </p>
         <p>
          The basic idea is that there are many ways to get the number 20 which becomes the basis for the password. Since most websites allow up to three attempts to log on, we will start with three passwords. Here are some examples
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          ONE+19=twenty
         </p>
         <p>
          TENandTEN=20
         </p>
         <p>
          Twenty+Zero=20
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          Now that we have 3 easy to remember passwords. All we have to do now is make them unique for each website. This is easily done simply by including a portion of the websites address in the password. The first three letters of the domain name work well. For google services like Gmail, my password would simply start with Goo.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          GooONE+1=twenty
         </p>
         <p>
          GooTENandTEN=20
         </p>
         <p>
          GooTwenty+Zero=20
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          Or, for Digg.com, we would use Dig
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          DigONE+19=twenty
         </p>
         <p>
          DigTENandTEN=20
         </p>
         <p>
          DigTwenty+Zero=20
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          And thats it, we now have strong passwords that are easily remembered. All you really need to remember is a single number and three ways to arrive at that number.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          Give it a try.
         </p>

             <br/>
             <br/>
             What I suggest sometimes are:
             <br/>
             <br/>
             1) Pick a word that doesn't exist. An example would be "timelord" (Dr Who fans recognize that). Then replace o/i/e with 0/1/3. Or misspell it.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             2) Think of a phrase that you won't forget, like "my job sucks". remove the spaces and replace as in #1 and/or change some to caps (MyJ0bSucks).

             <br/>
