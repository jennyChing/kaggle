   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Rubber-hose cryptanalysis
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      For the encrypted filesystem, see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubberhose" title="Rubberhose">

     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">
       cryptography
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       rubber-hose cryptanalysis
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">
       euphemism
      </a>
      for the extraction of cryptographic secrets (e.g. the password to an encrypted file) from a person by
      <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">
       coercion
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">
       torture
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNET_20081024_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CNET_20081024-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      such as beating that person with a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hose_(tubing)" title="Hose (tubing)">
       rubber hose
      </a>
      , thus the namein contrast to a mathematical or technical
      <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis" title="Cryptanalysis">
       cryptanalytic attack
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Details">
       Details
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-hose_cryptanalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">
       Amnesty International
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
       UN
      </a>
      , many countries in the world routinely torture people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_2004_10_27_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN_2004_10_27-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is therefore logical to assume that at least some of those countries use (or would be willing to use) some form of rubber-hose cryptanalysis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNET_20081024_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CNET_20081024-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In practice,
      <a href="/wiki/Coercion#Psychological" title="Coercion">
       psychological coercion
      </a>
      can prove as effective as physical
      <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">
       torture
      </a>
      . Not physically violent but highly intimidating methods include such tactics as the threat of harsh legal penalties. The incentive to cooperate may be some form of
      <a href="/wiki/Plea_bargain" title="Plea bargain">
       plea bargain
      </a>
      , such as an offer to drop or reduce criminal charges against a suspect in return for full co-operation with investigators. Alternatively, in some countries threats may be made to prosecute as co-conspirators (or inflict violence upon) close relatives (e.g. spouse, children, or parents) of the person being questioned unless they co-operate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_2004_10_27_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN_2004_10_27-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In some contexts, rubber-hose cryptanalysis may not be a viable attack because of a need to decrypt data covertly; information such as a password may lose its value if it is known to have been compromised. It has been argued that one of the purposes of strong cryptography is to force adversaries to resort to less covert attacks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The earliest known use of the term was on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sci.crypt" title="Sci.crypt">
       sci.crypt
      </a>
      newsgroup,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="No evidence cited that this use was the first known use of the term (February 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      in a message posted 16 October 1990 by
      <a href="/wiki/Marcus_J._Ranum" title="Marcus J. Ranum">
       Marcus J. Ranum
      </a>
      , alluding to
      <a href="/wiki/Foot_whipping" title="Foot whipping">
       corporal punishment
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       ...the rubber-hose technique of cryptanalysis. (in which a rubber hose is applied forcefully and frequently to the soles of the feet until the key to the cryptosystem is discovered, a process that can take a surprisingly short time and is quite computationally inexpensive).
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <i>
        
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          9
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </i>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Although the term is used
      <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">
       tongue-in-cheek
      </a>
      , its implications are serious: in modern cryptosystems, the weakest link is often the human user.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brute_force_attack" title="Brute force attack">
       direct attack
      </a>
      on a cipher algorithm, or the
      <a href="/wiki/Cryptographic_protocol" title="Cryptographic protocol">
       cryptographic protocols
      </a>
      used, is likely to be much more expensive and difficult than targeting the people who use or manage the system. Thus, many cryptosystems and security systems are designed with special emphasis on keeping human vulnerability to a minimum. For example, in
      <a href="/wiki/Public-key_cryptography" title="Public-key cryptography">
       public-key cryptography
      </a>
      , the defender may hold the key to encrypt the message, but not the decryption key needed to decipher it. The problem here is that the defender may be unable to convince the attacker to stop coercion. In plausibly
      <a href="/wiki/Deniable_encryption" title="Deniable encryption">
       deniable encryption
      </a>
      , a second key is created which unlocks a second convincing but relatively harmless message (for example, apparently personal writings expressing "deviant" thoughts or desires of some type that are lawful but taboo), so the defender can prove to have handed over the keys whilst the attacker remains unaware of the primary hidden message. In this case, the designer's expectation is that the attacker will not realize this, and forego threats or actual torture. Alternatively, a defender can use a proof-of-work-high-density-steganographic counterattack to incentivize the attacker to listen to what the defender has to say.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      See also
      <a href="/wiki/Steganography" title="Steganography">
       steganography
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_law">
       In law
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubber-hose_cryptanalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In law">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In some jurisdictions, statutes assume the oppositethat human operators know (or have access to) such things as session keys, an assumption which parallels that made by rubber-hose practitioners. An example is the United Kingdom's
      <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_Investigatory_Powers_Act_2000" title="Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000">
       Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Q.26As_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Q.26As-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RIP_online_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RIP_online-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which makes it a crime to not surrender
      <a href="/wiki/Key_(cryptography)" title="Key (cryptography)">
       encryption keys
      </a>
      on demand from a government official authorized by the act.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">
       Home Office
      </a>
      , the burden of proof that an accused person is in possession of a key rests on the prosecution; moreover, the act contains a defence for operators who have lost or forgotten a key, and they are not liable if they are judged to have done what they can to recover a key.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Q.26As_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Q.26As-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RIP_online_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-RIP_online-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
