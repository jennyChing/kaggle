       Monica, Shame, and Fame
      </h1>

      <div class="post-content">
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         <img alt="monica-lewinsky2-vanity-fair.jpg.CROP.rtstoryvar-large-1" class="aligncenter wp-image-84904" height="420" src="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/monica-lewinsky2-vanity-fair.jpg.CROP_.rtstoryvar-large-1.jpg" width="620"/>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         In her essay 
         <a href="http://www.vanityfair.com/online/daily/2014/05/monica-lewinsky-speaks">
          Shame and Survival
         </a>
          in the June 2014 issue of
         <em>
          Vanity Fair
         </em>
         , Monica Lewinsky calls out Bill and Hill, the Clinton machine, the media, and a catty confab of feminists for humiliating her so publicly that the resulting shame has prevented her from moving on for the past 16 years. As an example, she cites a 2002 HBO documentary in which she was asked a particularly crass question.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         One of the unintended consequences of my agreeing to put myself out there and to try to tell the truth [was] that shame would once again be hung around my neck like a scarlet-A albatross, she writes. Believe me, once its on, it is a bitch to take off.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         It doesnt have to be. Shame, embraced, is both a friend and a teacher.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Shame, the devastating feeling of being deeply flawed, is often called the secret emotionthe one were most likely to keep hidden from other people and from ourselves. It is the temporary I-wish-I-could-drop-off-the-face-of-the-earth feeling we experience when we are exposedto ourselves or othersas having fallen short of some ideal. The key word is
         <em>
          temporary.
         </em>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         If Monica feels like a victim of shame 16 years after her affair with then-President Clinton hit the headlines in 1998
         <em>
          ,
         </em>
         it seems to me that she is trying to heal her shame in all the wrong ways.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Her latest example is the decision to break her decade of self-imposed silenceThe last major interview I granted was 10 years agoby publishing this essay in the first place. Her rationale for speaking out now is that at age 40, it is time . . . I am determined to have a different ending to my story. . . .
         <br/>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         The Clinton administration, the special prosecutors minions, the political operatives on both sides of the aisle, and the media were able to brand me. And that brand stuck . . .
         <br/>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Unlike the other parties involved, I was so young that I had no established identity to which I could return. I didnt let this define meI simply hadnt had the life experience to establish my own identity in 1998. If you havent figured out who you are, its hard not to accept the horrible image of you created by others.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Well said. The question now is, Why perpetuate that image yourself? Why keep reinvigorating the opprobrium by calling attention to it?
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         There is an essential contradiction between feeling shame and drawing attention to the acts that caused it. That is, if shame is characterized by a desire to hide, to disappear, why would Monica invite readersnot once, but twice!to check out that humiliating HBO documentary?
        </span>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          Thanks to the all-encompassing nature of the Web, you can, 12 years later, watch it all day long on YouTube if you want to (but I really hope you have better things to do with your time).
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          . . . each easy click of that YouTube link reinforces the archetype, despite my efforts to parry it away: Me, Americas B.J. Queen.
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Monica, Monica, youve worked in communications! Its PR 101 that you never,
         <em>
          never
         </em>
         repeat something you dont want out there. Repeating it just reinforces it.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         And surely you, with a masters in social psychology, have heard of those experiments where students are instructed
         <em>
          not
         </em>
         to think of a pink elephant. (Guess what they cant stop thinking about?)
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         So despite your protestations to the contrary, do you think there might, just might, be a small part of you that likes being Queen of
         <em>
          something?
         </em>
         Why else would you be bringing all this up again? Why else would you write, I would give anything to go back and rewind the tape? (So that you could replay it? Or did you mean
         <em>
          erase
         </em>
         ?) Why else, according to
         <em>
          The New York Times
         </em>
         , would you have been talking on and off during 7 of your self-proclaimed 10 years of silence to your friends at
         <em>
          Vanity Fair
         </em>
         about cooperating with a profile for the magazine or writing a first-person piece? Why else, if you long to be seen as an upstanding young woman with career aspirations, would you agree to pose reclining on a sofa? Not that theres anything inherently wrong with that. But it is suggestivesuggestive that the motivation for your essay might be fame, not shame.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Same goes for your apparent habit of Googling yourself daily to see how many times youre referenced in the media: Every day someone mentions me in a tweet or a blog post, and not altogether kindly. Every day, it seems, my name shows up in an op-ed column or a press clip or two.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         <em>
          Patient: Doctor, it hurts when I go like this.
         </em>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         <em>
          Doctor: Dont go like that.
         </em>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Or there could be other motivations:
        </span>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          Do you want to cash in? [I turned down offers that would have earned me more than $10 million, because they didnt feel like the right thing to doIve managed to get by (barely, at times) with my own projects, usually with start-ups that I have participated in, or with loans from friends and family.] It would be interesting to know how much
          <em>
           Vanity Fair
          </em>
          paid you for your essay.
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          . . . exact revenge, now that Hillary is poised to run for president? (When I hear of Hillarys prospective candidacy, I cannot help but fear the next wave of paparazzi, the next wave of Where is she now? stories) Despite your claim that you wish them no ill, you clearly believe they threw you under the bus: I fully understand that what has happened to me and the issue of my future do not matter to either of them.
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          . . .extract an apology from Bill or at least invite him to take more blame for The Affair than he has in the past?
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         The problem with all of these motivations is that they entail going public and perpetuating the shame you claim you want to put behind you.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ffffff;">
         .
        </span>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Whether the shame from which youve been suffering is rooted in social opprobrium or self-disappointment, or both, there are a limited number of ways to deal with it:
        </span>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          You can stew in it, which often leads to depression, addiction (to food, drugs, shopping, fame), and a slew of unproductive defenses, including rage and blaming behaviors. It can also cause you unconsciously to seek out or remain in destructive relationships.
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          You can try to set the record straight, as youve done in your essay, and hope that you will be judged more kindly in light of todays social mores, where the sexual advances of powerful bosses are condemned. (However, with that comes the certainty of dredging up the affair and the risk of
          <em>
           too much information
          </em>
          .)
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
          Or you can accept humbly that youre a person with flaws and let the world know that you can no longer be shamed for your youthful lapse in judgment because youve forgiven yourself.
         </span>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Which bring us back to shame as friend and teacher.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         In a book I read years ago by two psychotherapists (
         <em>
          Letting Go of Shame
         </em>
         ), the authors put it this way: Healthy shame is like having a true friend, one who is not afraid to tell you that you are messing up your life. But like a good friend, healthy shame says, No, youre not perfect, but I love you anyway. It puts us in touch with our humanity. It allows us to acknowledge our flaws, learn from our mistakes, and move on. In short, to replace humiliation with humility.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="font-size: 10pt; font-family: georgia,palatino;">
         Monica, you can do it.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
