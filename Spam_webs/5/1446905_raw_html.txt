         <a class="social-icon email" data-omni-click="r'article',r' ',d,r'email',r' ',r'381111 '" href="mailto:?subject=When%20Musicians%20Surrender%20to%20Nostalgia&amp;body=Jazz%20guitarist%20Bill%20Frisell%27s%20new%20record%20Guitar%20in%20the%20Space%20Age%20isn%27t%20revolutionary%2C%20and%20that%27s%20fine.%20But%20listeners%20should%20hope%20for%20more.%0A%0ARead More:%0Ahttp%3A//www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/10/bill-frisell-guitar-in-the-space-age-review-the-jazz-guitarist-has-stopped-exploring/381111/">

        </li>

       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/david-a-graham/" title="David A. Graham">
          David A. Graham
         </a>
        </li>
        <li class="date">
         <time datetime="2014-10-07T07:45:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          Oct 7, 2014
         </time>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="article-body" itemprop="articleBody">
     <section id="article-section-1">
      <p>
       What do listeners have a right to ask of musicians? Is it enough for them to produce beautiful, carefully crafted music? Or is there an intellectual imperative, a demand that an album function as more than just a 40-minute monument of aesthetic achievement?
      </p>
      <p>
       The question seems especially relevant in improvisational music, where theres a strong impulse toward difficult listening: Artists make records that are conceptually innovative  and sometimes practically unlistenable. For many hipsters and
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2013/08/jazzbros-a-force-for-jazzgood-or-jazzevil/278696/">
        jazzbros
       </a>
       , anything with a traditional ensemble playing swinging 4/4 time is a little suspect; a standards record is highly suspect. This tension has cut through jazz at least since beboppers in Harlem began deriding moldy figs playing traditional swing in the early 1940s.
      </p>
      <p>
       The 63-year-old Bill Frisell, who is probably the most important and innovative exponent of jazz guitar in his generation, is a good case study in this tension. Deeply rooted in the avant-garde scene centered around John Zorn, he has often broken with tradition by playing a rock-style solid-body guitar and employing effects pedals and a squealing, distorted tone. Some of his records are challenging going, and some of the albums hes appeared onwork with Zorns Naked City, for exampleare
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Pxhg3INmpeo">
        good examples
       </a>
       of the practically unlistenable strain. (Defiantly so: One track is called Jazz Snob Eat Shit.)
      </p>
      <aside class="callout">
       <hr/>
       <h4>
        Related Story
       </h4>
       <div>
        <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/07/can-millennials-save-jazz/373783/">
         <img alt="" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/mt/2014/07/AP559548096037/lead.jpg?n825yb"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p>
        <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/07/can-millennials-save-jazz/373783/">
         Can Millennials Save Jazz?
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
      </aside>
      <p>
       But non-jazz obsessives might be most likely to hear Frisell in melodic, rustic-sounding interludes between NPR segments. He started dabbling in a fusion of country and folk with jazz long ago, culminating in the 1997 record
       <em>
        Nashville
       </em>
       . Since then, Frisell has often peppered his records and live sets with tunes by countrys great Williamses (Hank and Lucinda), and many of his records and touring bands feature Greg Leisz, an ace pedal- and lap-steel guitarist. It hasnt been a single-minded shift: Over the same period, hes recorded with large, horn-accoutered ensembles, string quartets, and a trio featuring the old-line giants Elvin Jones and Dave Holland.
      </p>
      <p>
       That dabbling has won Frisell fans and admirers in the pop and country worlds, and presumably, he has sold more records than he would have in the jazz ghetto. But these Americana sessions sometimes raise hackles among traditional jazz purists and avant-garde fans. For the first bunch, playing three- and four-chord Western tunes in the key of G or C represents a departure from what jazz ought to be: bluesy swing with rich harmonies. For the latter, Frisell isnt fully embracing his potential as a creative musician.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       Francis Davis
       <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/past/docs/issues/2002/07/davis.htm">
        addressed this issue head-on in
        <em>
         The Atlantic
        </em>
        in 2002
       </a>
       , concluding that what Frisell is doing fits in the jazzs melting-pot tradition. He likened Frisell to the clarinetist Jimmy Giuffre, who borrowed from folk in the late 1950s. Giuffres most radical departure from convention was the deceptive simplicity of many of his compositions for the group, which were harmonically sophisticated without being arduously complex, he wrote. Thats a good characterization of many Frisells compositions. Even when soloing over a basic, folky song, he gravitates to dissonance, builds elaborate superharmonic structures, and destabilizes the rhythm with a quirky, distinctive sense of timing and syncopation.
      </p>
      <p>
       In any case, the is it real jazz? question seems irrelevant today. The period of parsing and labeling and excommunication that surrounded Ken Burnss
       <em>
        Jazz
       </em>
       is past; the arguments have moved on. Its another line in Daviss article that sticks out now. As I get older, Im becoming more and more comfortable with the idea of playing something that was a part of my life, Frisell told him. Some naive melody or childhood memory that I once would have thought wasnt complicated enough.
      </p>
      <p>
       That 12-year-old quote certainly explains
       <span>
        Frisells new disc,
       </span>
       <em>
        Guitar in the Space Age
       </em>
       <span>
        . By almost any rubric, it is not complicated enough. Frisells fine with that, and many listeners will be too; plenty of other listeners, though, wont
       </span>
       
       <span>
        and probably shouldntbe.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       Frisell has been experimenting with not just recreating the textures of non-jazz styles but directly covering the tunes for years: Dylan, Copland, Ives, Rollins, Ciccone (er,
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)">
        Madonna
       </a>
       ). In 2011, Frisell released an honest-to-God John Lennon tribute album,
       <em>
        All We Are Saying
       </em>
       . There isnt a single impeachable note on the disc; even the incurably maudlin and simple Imagine delights. On the other hand, what purpose does a disc of relatively faithful covers of John Lennon tunes serve? Its not as if Lennon is an underappreciated figure.
      </p>
      <aside class="pullquote instapaper_ignore" data-oembed-name="www.youtube.com" data-oembed-src="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MQpERgQpwnI">
       <span>
        Frisell's new disc isn't "complicated enough." Frisells fine with that; plenty of listeners wont
       </span>
       
       <span>
        and shouldn't
       </span>
       
       <span>
        be.
       </span>
      </aside>
      <p>
       <em>
        Guitar in the Space Age
       </em>
       keeps the trend going. The pitch: Frisell and Leisz tag-team great six-string showcases of their youth. The Chantays are here (Pipeline) along with their peppy younger brothers the Beach Boys (Surfer Girl). Duane Eddy comes by to run through Rebel Rouser, then its across the ocean for the Kinks Tired of Waiting for You. As on the Lennon disc, many of the songs faithfully replicate the originalsperhaps too faithfully. Frisell picks out some sadly neglected classics, but theres not much abstraction or deconstruction here; a version of Turn, Turn, Turn slavishly replicates all the bubble-gum jangle of the Byrds version. (Incredibly, Frisells version is even shorter than McGuinn and companys.) Link Wrays Rumble is rendered with carefully reproduced distortion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thats not to say there arent great moments. When Frisell and Leisz take off on the third bridge of Turn, Turn, Turn, with drummer Kenny Wollesen and bassist Tony Scherr locking on the backbeat, its sublime. By slowing down and spacing out Pipeline, Frisell gives it an eerily open, movie western sound. There are moments like that across the disc.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector2">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=15" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=15"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-3">
      <p>
       Overall, however, its a little too perfect for the NPR crowd: middle-aged, kinda eclectic, but not very adventurous. Perhaps unavoidably for a set so heavily geared to surf tunes, it also feels awfully white. Since the Original Dixieland Jass Band cut its first side, there has been a lively debate about white appropriation of black music and the ongoing blackness (or not) of the music. As jazz becomes increasingly academicand, critics say, loses its ties to black culturetheres been an
       <a href="http://nicholaspayton.wordpress.com/2014/04/30/black-american-music-and-the-jazz-tradition/">
        especially heated round
       </a>
       of this fight. The only track here originally recorded by a black musician is Junior Wellss Messin With the Kid, and that feels a little uncomfortable. (But it is fun to hear Leisz, one of the worlds great sidemen, stepping out front with solos on the record, including a Steve Cropper-esque turn here.)
       <em>
        Guitar
       </em>
       might have benefited from the inclusion of, say, the Delfonicss La-La (Means I Love You), which Frisell is known to play live.
      </p>
      <div class="oembed" data-oembed-name="www.youtube.com" data-oembed-src="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Hbq13bLylCY">
       <div class="embed-wrapper" style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:61.4035087719%;">
        <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="embedly-embed lazyload" data-src="//cdn.embedly.com/widgets/media.html?src=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.youtube.com%2Fembed%2FHbq13bLylCY%3Ffeature%3Doembed&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.youtube.com%2Fwatch%3Fv%3DHbq13bLylCY&amp;image=http%3A%2F%2Fi.ytimg.com%2Fvi%2FHbq13bLylCY%2Fhqdefault.jpg&amp;key=8164a61223cb4585ad276041728caca9&amp;type=text%2Fhtml&amp;schema=youtube" frameborder="0" height="350" scrolling="no" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;" width="570">
        </iframe>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Looking backward for inspiration isnt always a dead endthough the standards record, where musicians just run through classic tunes, is often the dullest kind of jazz. The problem is that as with some of the more worn-out standards, the songs here dont offer much possibility; a tune like the Tornados delightful proto-synth-pop kitsch 
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u2ybCjf6ras">
        Telstar
       </a>
        simply cant bend that much before it breaks, though Frisell takes it to that limit.
       <em>
        Guitar
       </em>
       suffers by comparison with the trumpeter Ron Miless new record, out on October 14, with a trio that includes Frisell and drummer Brian Bladeand, which, perhaps not coincidentally, includes Giuffres Two Kinds of Blues. Miless
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Circuit-Rider-Ron-Miles/dp/B00MOLOJRS">
        <em>
         Circuit Rider
        </em>
       </a>
       doesnt bear much resemblance to the popular idea of What Jazz Should Sound Like, and it borrows from country textures too, but the songs offer more space for harmonic expression.
      </p>
      <p>
       Maybe all of this seems a little churlish. If a bunch of outrageously talented baby boomers get together to play old tunes, is that so terrible? Theres plenty of bad music out there; why object to the good? Why begrudge Frisell and his friends a trip down memory lane? Perhaps you cant, in good faith. Still, its fair to predict that this isnt going to go down as one of Frisells marquee records. The man can have his nostalgia, but please: Make some weird, jarring music sometime soon, too.
      </p>
     </section>

        <h3 class="hed">
         Why the Iran Deal Makes Obama's Critics So Angry
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/peter-beinart/" title="Peter Beinart">
          Peter Beinart
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         The nuclear agreement highlights the limits of American powersomething the presidents opponents wont accept.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Mankind faces a crossroads,
          <a href="http://www.skeptic.ca/Woody_Allen_Speech_to_Graduates.htm">
           declared
          </a>
          Woody Allen. One path leads to despair and utter hopelessness. The other, to total extinction. Let us pray we have the wisdom to choose correctly.
         </p>
         <p>
          The point is simple: In life, what matters most isnt how a decision compares to your ideal outcome. Its how it compares to
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/alternatives-obama-iran-nuclear-deal-israel/389751/">
           the alternative at hand
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The same is true for the Iran deal,
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-33518524">
           announced Tuesday
          </a>
          <span>
           between Iran and six world powers
          </span>
          . As Congress begins debating the agreement, its opponents have three real alternatives. The first is to kill the deal, and the
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/the-iran-nuclear-deal-by-the-numbers/389592/">
           interim agreement
          </a>
          that preceded it, and do nothing else, which means few restraints on Irans nuclear program. The second is war. But top American and Israeli officials have warned that military action against Iranian nuclear facilities could ignite a catastrophic regional conflict and would be ineffective, if not counterproductive, in delaying Irans path to the bomb. Meir Dagan, who oversaw the Iran file as head of Israels external spy agency, the Mossad, from 2002 to 2011,
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/04/world/middleeast/04mossad.html">
           has said
          </a>
          an attack would mean regional war, and in that case you would have given Iran the best possible reason to continue the nuclear program. Michael Hayden, who ran the CIA under George W. Bush from 2006 to 2009,
          <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2012/01/19/bushs-cia-director-we-determined-attacking-iran-was-a-bad-idea/">
           has warned
          </a>
          that an attack would guarantee that which we are trying to prevent: an Iran that will spare nothing to build a nuclear weapon.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
