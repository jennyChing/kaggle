          A Snapshot of Cannabis Culture in New York City
         </h1>
         <a class="color--white" href="/news/authors/sara-dilley">

        <div class="news__article-body notranslate" itemprop="articleBody">
         <p>
          This year
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.leafly.com/news/headlines/governor-of-new-york-signs-medical-marijuana-bill-into-law">
            New York joined the growing list of states to legalize medical marijuana
           </a>
          </strong>
          , at last bringing oneof Americas greatest cities to the forefront of the cannabis movement. Their somewhat fashionably late arrival tothe medical cannabis party has gotten us curious aboutNew Yorks history with cannabis; as it turns out, its a complicated one.
         </p>
         <div class="pull-right">
          <div class="ad-unit l--spacer-left l--spacer-bottom" id="azk76440">
          </div>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Who is Consuming Cannabis in New York City?
         </h2>
         <p>
          It should come as no surprise that New York, with its long tradition of arts, culture, and music, also has a history of cannabis use. While marijuana wasnt popular in the pre-Vietnam era, its resurgence among the counterculture resonated in other communities and, by the end of the 20th century, moved 
          <strong>
           <a href="http://crj.sagepub.com/content/13/1/63.full.pdf+html">
            from being a small minority to majority activity.
           </a>
          </strong>
          
         </p>
         <p>
          Consumers span all ages, genders, races, and incomes. The typical cannabis consumer could be an NYU undergrad,a Wall Street investor,a musician in Bed-Stuy, or a waiter in Mott Haven.
         </p>
         <h2>
          What are New Yorkers Consuming Cannabis (and What Does it Cost)?
         </h2>
         <p>
          New York is considered by many to be Americas trendsetter: fashion, food, art, music, business, and so much more have origins in the Empire State. Given New York'sproclivity to innovate, one might expect concentrates or vaporization to be themost popular methodof consumption given their current hype (and lets not forget
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.leafly.com/news/headlines/governor-of-new-york-signs-medical-marijuana-bill-into-law">
            New York's medical cannabis program mandates
           </a>
          </strong>
          ), but overwhelmingly, the states preferred method of consumption is the blunt.
         </p>
         <p>
          To those unfamiliar, a blunt refers to ground cannabis rolled in a cigar wrap,either hollowed out of a pre-rolled cigar or rolled with a fresh wrap.
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           A survey conducted by the
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2561263/">
            National Institute of Health
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           indicated that 37% of New Yorkconsumers preferred blunts, 22% preferred joints, and 31% preferred a mix of blunts, joints, and other consumption methods including glass pieces and edibles.
          </span>
          Some theorize that blunt culture became so prevalent because of the poor quality of black market east coast cannabis (the flavor profile and aroma border on non-existent when consumed in a cigar wrap).
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           Flower is hands down the most popular cannabis product in New York, though edibles get an honorable mention.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          Pricing is a pain point for many New Yorkers, as (illegal) delivery services typically offer flower as high as
          <strong>
           <a href="http://elitedaily.com/life/culture/the-10-reasons-why-being-a-stoner-in-nyc-sucks/">
            $30 a gram
           </a>
          </strong>
          , though prices are often closer to $40-50 an eighth -
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2014/03/06/weed-delivery-nyc-marijuana-brooklyn_n_4906701.html">
            $60 if you're gullible
           </a>
          </strong>
          . If a consumercan'tafford delivery services, he or she is often dependent on unreliable, tardy dealers with sketchy, under-weighed product sold in shady areas.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Where Do New Yorkers Consume Cannabis?
         </h2>
         <p>
          Finding a place to consume cannabis can also be a challenge. To those with kind neighbors, full-floor apartments, brownstones, penthouses, or a domestic, amplespace that hastolerant neighbors, the comfort of your own home is generally the best place to partake.
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           To the other millions of New Yorkers, however, consumptioncan be challenging. Too many people are stacked
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="http://elitedaily.com/life/culture/the-10-reasons-why-being-a-stoner-in-nyc-sucks/">
            shoulder-to-shoulder in overpriced boxes
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           that easily transfer scent. Lighting up can aggravate neighbors and lead to an unwanted visit from the NYPD.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           If a New Yorker wants to consume cannabis but his or herapartment is out of the question, another popular place issomething the cityhas a lot of: rooftops. In summer, this is glorious. Expansive views and gorgeous skylines, great weather, great company. Winter, however, is less jubilant. Snow, freezing temperatures, and have you heard of wind tunnels? Not so pleasant.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          With all the challenges mentioned above, many consumers turn to smoking in public places. Unfortunately, this is a misdemeanor in New York City, despite the fact that cannabis has been decriminalized statewide. In the late 90s, quality-of-life policing mandated police officers to aggressively pursue open cannabis consumption, resulting in a surge of marijuana-related arrests. Between 1996 and 2010,
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.drugpolicy.org/docUploads/_75_Million_A_Year.pdf">
            536,322 marijuana possession arrests were made
           </a>
          </strong>
          , over 15 times as many cannabis-related arrests as were made in the previous 15 years. That averages out to about 50,000 public possession arrests each year.
         </p>
         <p>
          Not only is this tremendously uncool (not to mention expensive), but these measures havent succeeded in reducing public consumption.
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           Whats worse, the arrests disproportionately affect blackcommunities. According to the
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/wp/2013/06/04/the-blackwhite-marijuana-arrest-gap-in-nine-charts/">
            Washington Post
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           (via a
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="https://www.aclu.org/files/assets/aclu-thewaronmarijuana-rel2.pdf">
            2013 ACLU report
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           ),
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/files/2013/06/marijuana_use_rate_by_race_year.png">
            blacks and whites consume at almost the same rate
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           , yet
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/wp/2013/06/04/the-blackwhite-marijuana-arrest-gap-in-nine-charts/">
            blacks are about 3 times more likely to be arrested
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           . These figures represent not only New York, but the entire U.S.
          </span>
         </p>
         <h2>
          What Does the Future Hold?
         </h2>
         <p>
          Given the hardships cannabis culture in NewYork has faced  bad, overpriced product, shady dealers, and overly-prominent consumption policing that disproportionately affects blackswere curiousto how the landscape will change with the launch of New York's medical cannabis program (and hopefully, eventual recreational legalization).
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           Once
          </span>
          <strong style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           <a href="http://www.leafly.com/news/headlines/governor-of-new-york-signs-medical-marijuana-bill-into-law">
            medical cannabis is fully instated
           </a>
          </strong>
          <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
           , a change in the political climate will be just around the corner. Were confident that one day, not too long from now, New Yorks administration will catch up to its savvy, sophisticatedpopulation.
          </span>
         </p>
