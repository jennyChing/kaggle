    Russian reversal
   </h1>

     <p>
      A
      <b>
       Russian reversal
      </b>
      is a type of joke, usually starting with the words "In
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic" title="Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic">
       Soviet Russia
      </a>
      ", in which the subject and objects of a statement are reversed, usually to reference the propaganda of an enemy that is the exact opposite of the interlocutor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liberman_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-liberman-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The jokes are usually told in broken English, without
      <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
       articles
      </a>
      , in the way that a native Russian speaker might, which makes such reversals easier. A Russian reversal is an example of an
      <a href="/wiki/Antimetabole" title="Antimetabole">
       antimetabole
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Transpositional_pun" title="Transpositional pun">
       transpositional pun
      </a>
      , and a
      <a href="/wiki/Chiasmus" title="Chiasmus">
       chiasmus
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
       Examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Russian_reversal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Examples include:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       In America, you watch
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(TV_series)" title="Big Brother (TV series)">
         Big Brother
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </dd>
      <dd>
       In Soviet Russia,
       <a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(Nineteen_Eighty-Four)" title="Big Brother (Nineteen Eighty-Four)">
        Big Brother
       </a>
       watches you!
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       In America, you break law.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       In Soviet Russia, law breaks you!
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This type of joke has existed since at least the 1960s. In the 196873 television show
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laugh-In" title="Laugh-In">
        Laugh-In
       </a>
      </i>
      , a recurring character, "Piotr Rosmenko the Eastern European Man" (played by
      <a href="/wiki/Arte_Johnson" title="Arte Johnson">
       Arte Johnson
      </a>
      ), delivered short jokes such as "Here in America, is very good, everyone watch television. In old country, television watches you!" This joke alludes to video screens that both
      <a href="/wiki/Telescreen" title="Telescreen">
       reproduce images and monitor the citizenry
      </a>
      , as in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four">
        Nineteen Eighty-Four
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The joke form is often credited to Ukrainian-American comedian
      <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Smirnoff" title="Yakov Smirnoff">
       Yakov Smirnoff
      </a>
      , although he only rarely used Russian reversals; one exception was a
      <a href="/wiki/Miller_Lite" title="Miller Lite">
       Miller Lite
      </a>
      commercial he appeared in in 1985, in which he states, "In America, there's plenty of light beer and you can always find a party. In Russia,
      <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">
       Party
      </a>
      always finds you."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liberman_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-liberman-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
