By the time he became president in 1969, Richard Nixon had been on the giving  and receiving end of major underworld favors for more than two decades.  Watergate was just the tip of the iceberg. by  Don Fulsom During the height of the Watergate scandal, Atty. Gen. John Mitchell's wife, Martha, sounded one of the first alarms, telling a reporter, ''Nixon is involved with the Mafia. The Mafia was involved in his election.'' White House officials privately urged other reporters to treat any anti-Nixon comments by Martha as the ravings of a drunken crackpot. Time, however, has proved Mrs. Mitchell right. Richard Nixon's earliest campaign manager and political advisor was Murray Chotiner, a chubby lawyer who specialized in defending members of the Mafia and who enjoyed dressing like them too, in a wardrobe highlighted by monogrammed white-on-white dress shirts and silk ties with jeweled stickpins. The monograms said MMC, because  perhaps to seem more impressive  he billed himself as Murray M. Chotiner, though, in reality, he lacked a middle name. In this cigar chomping, wheeler-dealer, Nixon had found what future Nixon aide Len Garment called ''his Machiavelli  a hardheaded exponent of the campaign philosophy that politics is war.'' When Nixon went on to the White House, both as vice president, and later as president, he took Chotiner with him as a key behind-the-scenes advisor  and for good reason. By the time he became president in 1969, thanks in large part to Murray Chotiner's contacts with such shady figures as Mafia-connected labor leader Jimmy Hoffa, New Orleans Mafia boss Carlos Marcello, and Los Angeles gangster Mickey Cohen, Richard Nixon had been on the giving and receiving end of major underworld favors for more than two decades." name="description"/>

          <div class="meta">
           Oct 5, 2009 - by
           <a href="/category/authors/don-fulsom">
            Don Fulsom
           </a>
           -
           <a href="/mobs-president-richard-nixons-secret-ties-mafia#comment-form">
            0 Comments
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="clear">
          </div>
          <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
           <div class="field-items">
            <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: x-small;">
               <span style="font-family: Arial;">
                February 5, 2006
               </span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <a href="http://www.crimemagazine.com/images/nixon-rebozo-hoover.jpg">
               <img alt='President Richard Nixon with Bebe Rebozo (left) and J. Edgar Hoover (center) at the "Florida White House". Credit: National Archives.' height="316" src="http://www.crimemagazine.com/images/nixon-rebozo-hoover-sm.jpg" width="438"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <span style="font-size: x-small;">
               President Richard Nixon with Bebe Rebozo (left) and J. Edgar Hoover (center)
               <br/>
               at the "Florida White House". Credit: National Archives.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <span style="font-family: Arial; font-size: small;">
               <span style="font-size: small;">
                <em>
                 By the time he became president in 1969, Richard Nixon had been on the giving     and receiving end of major underworld favors for more than two decades.     Watergate was just the tip of the iceberg.
                </em>
               </span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <span style="font-size: large;">
               <span style="font-family: Arial;">
                by
                <a href="http://www.crimemagazine.com/authors/fulsom_don_bio.htm">
                 Don Fulsom
                </a>
               </span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-family: Arial;">
               <span style="font-size: x-large;">
                D
               </span>
               uring the height of the Watergate scandal, Atty. Gen. John Mitchell's wife,  Martha, sounded one of the first alarms, telling a reporter, ''Nixon is involved  with the Mafia. The Mafia was involved in his election.''
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-family: Arial;">
               White House officials privately urged other reporters to treat any anti-Nixon  comments by Martha as the ravings of a drunken crackpot.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-family: Arial;">
               Time, however, has proved Mrs. Mitchell right.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-family: Arial;">
               Richard Nixon's earliest campaign manager and political advisor was Murray  Chotiner, a chubby lawyer who specialized in defending members of the Mafia and  who enjoyed dressing like them too, in a wardrobe highlighted by monogrammed  white-on-white dress shirts and silk ties with jeweled stickpins. The monograms  said MMC, because  perhaps to seem more impressive  he billed himself as  Murray
               <em>
                M.
               </em>
               Chotiner, though, in reality, he lacked a middle name.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-family: Arial;">
               In this cigar chomping, wheeler-dealer, Nixon had found what future Nixon  aide Len Garment called ''his Machiavelli  a hardheaded exponent of the campaign  philosophy that politics is war.''
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-family: Arial;">
               When Nixon went on to the White House, both as vice president, and later as  president, he took Chotiner with him as a key behind-the-scenes advisor  and  for good reason. By the time he became president in 1969, thanks in large part  to Murray Chotiner's contacts with such shady figures as Mafia-connected labor  leader Jimmy Hoffa, New Orleans Mafia boss Carlos Marcello, and Los Angeles  gangster Mickey Cohen, Richard Nixon had been on the giving and receiving end of  major underworld favors for more than two decades.
              </span>
             </p>
