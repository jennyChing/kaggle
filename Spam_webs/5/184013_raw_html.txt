      <!-- Begin Title -->
      <h1>
       Billiards
      </h1>
      <!-- End Title -->
      <!-- Begin Total Content -->
      <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/notebooks/PlaneGeometry/Billiards.nb" onclick="downloadNotebook('http://mathworld.wolfram.com/notebooks/PlaneGeometry/Billiards.nb');return false;">
       <img alt="DOWNLOAD Mathematica Notebook" border="0" height="26" src="/images/entries/dnld-nb.gif" width="119"/>
      </a>
      <a href="/contribute/incomplete_entry.html#Billiards">
       <img align="bottom" alt="Contribute to this entry" border="0" height="26" src="/images/entries/contribute2.gif" width="91"/>
      </a>
      <!-- Begin Content -->
      <p>
       The game of billiards is played on a
       <a href="/Rectangle.html">
        rectangular
       </a>
       table (known as a billiard table) upon which balls are placed. One ball (the "cue
 ball") is then struck with the end of a "cue" stick, causing it to
 bounce into other balls and
       <a href="/Reflection.html">
        reflect
       </a>
       off the sides
 of the table. Real billiards can involve spinning the ball so that it does not travel
 in a straight
       <a href="/Line.html">
        line
       </a>
       , but the mathematical study of billiards
 generally consists of
       <a href="/Reflection.html">
        reflections
       </a>
       in which the reflection
 and incidence angles are the same. However, strange table shapes such as
       <a href="/Circle.html">
        circles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/Ellipse.html">
        ellipses
       </a>
       are often considered. The popular 1959 animated
 short film
       <i>
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/6301017129/ref=nosim/weisstein-20">
         Donald
 in Mathmagic Land
        </a>
       </i>
       features a tutorial by Donald Duck on how to win at billiards
 using the diamonds normally inscribed around the edge of a real billiard table.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many interesting problems can arise in the detailed study of billiards trajectories. For example, any smooth plane convex set has at least two
       <a href="/DoubleNormal.html">
        double
 normals
       </a>
       , so there are always two distinct "to and fro" paths for any
 smoothly curved table. More amazingly, there are always
       <img alt="phi(k)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline1.gif" width="25"/>
       distinct
       <img alt="k" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline2.gif" width="6"/>
       -gonal periodic orbits on smooth billiard table,
 where
       <img alt="phi(k)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline3.gif" width="25"/>
       is the
       <a href="/TotientFunction.html">
        totient
 function
       </a>
       (Croft
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       1991, p.16). This gives Steinhaus's result
 that there are always two distinct periodic triangular orbits (Croft and Swinnerton
 1963) as a special case. Analysis of billiards path can involve sophisticated use
 of
       <a href="/ErgodicTheory.html">
        ergodic theory
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/DynamicalSystem.html">
        dynamical
 systems
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <img alt="BilliardsRectangular" class="center-image" height="303" src="images/eps-gif/BilliardsRectangular_850.gif" width="508"/>
      <p>
       Given a rectangular billiard table with only corner pockets and sides of
       <a href="/Integer.html">
        integer
       </a>
       lengths
       <img alt="m" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline4.gif" width="10"/>
       and
       <img alt="n" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline5.gif" width="7"/>
       (with
       <img alt="m" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline6.gif" width="10"/>
       and
       <img alt="n" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline7.gif" width="7"/>
       <a href="/RelativelyPrime.html">
        relatively
 prime
       </a>
       ), a ball sent at a
       <img alt="45 degrees" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline8.gif" width="23"/>
       angle from a corner will be
 pocketed in another corner after
       <img alt="m+n-2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline9.gif" width="54"/>
       bounces (Steinhaus
 1999, p.63; Gardner 1984, pp.211-214). Steinhaus (1999, p.64) also
 gives a method for determining how to hit a billiard ball such that it caroms off
 all four sides before hitting a second ball (Knaster and Steinhaus 1946, Steinhaus
 1948).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/AlhazensBilliardProblem.html">
        Alhazen's billiard problem
       </a>
       seeks to find the point at the edge of a circular "billiards" table at which a cue ball
 at a given point must be aimed in order to carom once off the edge of the table and
 strike another ball at a second given point. This problem is insoluble using a
       <a href="/Compass.html">
        compass
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/Ruler.html">
        ruler
       </a>
       construction
 (Elkin 1965, Riede 1989, Neumann 1998).
      </p>
      <img alt="BilliardsElliptical" class="center-image" height="315" src="images/eps-gif/BilliardsElliptical_800.gif" width="511"/>
      <p>
       On an
       <a href="/Ellipse.html">
        elliptical
       </a>
       billiard table, the
       <a href="/Envelope.html">
        envelope
       </a>
       of a trajectory is a smaller
       <a href="/Ellipse.html">
        ellipse
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/Hyperbola.html">
        hyperbola
       </a>
       ,
 a
       <a href="/Line.html">
        line
       </a>
       through the
       <a href="/Focus.html">
        foci
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/Ellipse.html">
        ellipse
       </a>
       , or a closed polygon (Steinhaus 1999, pp.239
 and 241; Wagon 1991). The closed polygon case is related to
       <a href="/PonceletsPorism.html">
        Poncelet's
 porism
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <img alt="BilliardsPolygons" class="center-image" height="173" src="images/eps-gif/BilliardsPolygons_1000.gif" width="507"/>
      <p>
       One can also consider billiard paths on polygonal billiard tables. The only closed billiard path of a single circuit in an
       <a href="/AcuteTriangle.html">
        acute triangle
       </a>
       is the
       <a href="/PedalTriangle.html">
        pedal triangle
       </a>
       . There are an infinite number
 of multiple-circuit paths, but all segments are parallel to the sides of the
       <a href="/PedalTriangle.html">
        pedal
 triangle
       </a>
       . There exists a closed billiard path inside a
       <a href="/CyclicQuadrilateral.html">
        cyclic
 quadrilateral
       </a>
       if its
       <a href="/Circumcenter.html">
        circumcenter
       </a>
       lies inside
 the quadrilateral (Wells 1991).
      </p>
      <table align="center" border="0" summary="">
       <tr style="Text">
        <td align="center">
         <img alt="BilliardsCube" class="center-image" height="317" src="images/eps-gif/BilliardsCube_1000.gif" width="315"/>
        </td>
        <td align="center">
         <img alt="BilliardsTetrahedron" class="center-image" height="315" src="images/eps-gif/BilliardsTetrahedron_1000.gif" width="314"/>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       There are four identical closed billiard paths inside and touching each face of a
       <a href="/Cube.html">
        cube
       </a>
       such that each leg on the path has the same length
 (Hayward 1962; Steinhaus 1979, 1999; Gardner 1984, pp.33-35; Wells 1991). This
 path is in the form of a chair-shaped hexagon, and each leg has length
       <img alt="sqrt(3)/3" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline10.gif" width="40"/>
       . For a
 unit cube, one such path has vertices (0, 2/3, 2/3), (1/3, 1, 1/3), (2/3, 2/3, 0),
 (1, 1/3, 1/3), (2/3, 0, 2/3), (1/3, 1/3, 1). Lewis Carroll (Charles Dodgson) also
 considered this problem (Weaver 1954).
      </p>
      <p>
       There are three identical closed billiard paths inside and touching each face of a
       <a href="/Tetrahedron.html">
        tetrahedron
       </a>
       such that each leg of the path has
 the same length (Gardner 1984, pp.35-36; Wells 1991). These were discovered
 by J.H.Conway and independently by Hayward (1962). The vertices of the
 path are appropriately chosen vertices of equilateral triangles in each facial plane
 which are scaled by a factor of 1/10. For a tetrahedron with unit side lengths, each
 leg has length
       <img alt="sqrt(10)/10" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline11.gif" width="54"/>
       . For a tetrahedron with vertices
 (0, 0, 0), (0,
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline12.gif" width="40"/>
       ,
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline13.gif" width="40"/>
       ), (
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline14.gif" width="40"/>
       , 0,
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline15.gif" width="40"/>
       ), (
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline16.gif" width="40"/>
       ,
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/2" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline17.gif" width="40"/>
       , 0), the
 vertices of one such path are (
       <img alt="3sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline18.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="7sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline19.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/5" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline20.gif" width="40"/>
       ), (
       <img alt="3sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline21.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="3sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline22.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="3sqrt(2)/10" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline23.gif" width="57"/>
       ), (
       <img alt="7sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline24.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="3sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline25.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="sqrt(2)/5" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline26.gif" width="40"/>
       ), (
       <img alt="7sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline27.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="7sqrt(2)/20" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline28.gif" width="57"/>
       ,
       <img alt="3sqrt(2)/10" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="22" src="/images/equations/Billiards/Inline29.gif" width="57"/>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Conway has shown that period orbits exist in all
       <a href="/Tetrahedron.html">
        tetrahedra
       </a>
       , but it is not known if there are periodic orbits in every
       <a href="/Polyhedron.html">
        polyhedron
       </a>
       (Croft
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       1991, p.16).
      </p>
