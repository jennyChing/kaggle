         <h1 class="post-title entry-title">
          <a href="http://www.handsoccupied.com/2013/08/how-to-upholstered-arm-rest-end-tables/">
           How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="byline">
          By
          <span class="author vcard">
           <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.handsoccupied.com/author/hjg/" rel="author" title="Heidi">
            Heidi
           </a>
          </span>
          <time class="published" datetime="2013-08-05T00:00:17-05:00" title="Monday, August 5th, 2013, 12:00 am">
           August 5, 2013
          </time>
          |
          <a class="comments-link" href="http://www.handsoccupied.com/2013/08/how-to-upholstered-arm-rest-end-tables/#comments" title="Comment on How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables">
           9 Responses
          </a>
         </div>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p>
          <span class="pibfi_pinterest ">
           <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5472" height="400" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables8.jpg" width="600"/>
           <span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://www.handsoccupied.com/2013/08/how-to-upholstered-arm-rest-end-tables/&amp;media=http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables8.jpg&amp;description=How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables')">
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          In every dorm room and small apartment, space is at a premium. My living room is definitely one Im constantly getting creative in to save space, which is where the ideas for these arm rest end tables came from. Ive been using these in my home for a few months now, and theyre super convenient for keeping drinks, phones and remotes within reach.
         </p>
         <p>
          The arm rest end tables come together in two phases: first converting a single four foot board from the hardware store into the right shape, and phase two is as simple as a making them pretty with a bit of paint, fabric and Mod Podge.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Supplies
         </h2>
         <p>
          <strong>
           for phase 1, construction
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          1 x 12 x 4
          <a href="http://www.homedepot.com/p/1-x-12-x-4-2-Whitewood-Pine-Board-458503/100322335#.Uf6vXmT71j0">
           unfinished pine shelf board
          </a>
          <br/>
          8 L-brackets
          <br/>
          16 screws (unless your L-brackets require more than two each)
          <br/>
          screw driver/drill
          <br/>
          primer
          <br/>
          paint
          <br/>
          paint brush
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           for phase 2, upholstery
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          1/2 yd upholstery fabric, dont use anything too stretchy
          <br/>
          freezer paper (optional)
          <br/>
          iron(optional)
          <br/>
          pen
          <br/>
          ruler/tape measure
          <br/>
          paper scissors
          <br/>
          fabric scissors (optional)
          <br/>
          Matte Mod Podge
          <br/>
          sponge brush
          <br/>
          brayer (optional but recommended)
          <br/>
          something to stand your arm rests on as you work
          <br/>
          Hard Coat Mod Podge
         </p>
         <h2>
          Directions
         </h2>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Phase 1  construct the arm rest end tables
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Measure the distance across the top of your couchs arm rests. Cut your pine board into six pieces (with some assistance if necessary): two pieces that are the width of your couchs arm rests, two pieces that are 4 inches long, and two pieces that are 10 inches long. You dont have to use my exact measurements for the side pieces  I ultimately chose those lengths at based on how much board I had left after cutting the top pieces of wood for the arm rests.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter wp-image-5454" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables11.jpg" width="500"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Next, using 4 L-brackets per arm rest, attach the side pieces of wood to the bottom of the top piece. See the above and below pictures for how I constructed mine. They construction is very straightforward. Just make sure to align the edges of the top and side pieces as closely as possible before securing with screws.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter wp-image-5450" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables2.jpg" width="500"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Prime your arm rests and paint them. The color theyre painted will be visible through the upholstery, so choose your paint color wisely. I went with white since theres white in my upholstery fabric. Let dry completely before phase 2.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Phase 2  upholstery
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <em>
           And by upholstery, I mean decoupaging fabric to wood.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Take two measurements: all the way around the outside of the arm rest (the black dotted line pictured below) &amp; the width of your arm rest (red dotted line below).
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter wp-image-5441" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables3.jpg" width="500"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Youve got two options for cutting out your fabric. One, you can draw your rectangle, using the measurements from above, onto the wrong side of your fabric with a water soluble marker (a.k.a. embroidery marker). Draw a second rectangle on the wrong side of your fabric and cut both out.
         </p>
         <p>
          Two, you can draw your rectangle onto the non-waxed side of some freezer paper, cut it out, iron it to the wrong side of your fabric, and cut out your fabric. I opted for this method because I had the supplies necessary on hand, and because its incredibly accurate. And since the fabric will be adhered to the hard corners of a wooden object, my priority was accuracy. Bonus: you can reuse the freezer paper stencil for the second rectangle of fabric youll need to upholster your second arm rest. Just something to keep in mind! :)
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter wp-image-5442" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables4.jpg" width="500"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          When your fabric rectangle is all cut out and the freezer paper peeled off, double check your work by lining up the edges and corners of the fabric on the arm rest to ensure cuts are straight and the fabric is the right length.
         </p>
         <p>
          Find something to rest your arm rest on while you work through the rest of the project. Since the arm rests have differently-lengthed sides, theyre not a fan of standing on their own.
         </p>
         <p>
          Paint a layer of Matte Mod Podge onto the shorter side and top of the arm rest. Carefully lay your fabric, all lined up, onto the shorter side and top of the arm rest. If you need to do any adjusting/realigning
          <em>
           do it now.
          </em>
          Last chance, guys. Luckily, its still super simple at this point and feels almost like moving a post-it note around.
         </p>
         <p>
          All lined up? Next, move your arm rest stand, tilting the arm rest so its resting with the top and long sides facing up. Holding the rest of your fabric out of the way, paint a layer of Matte Mod Podge onto the long side of the arm rest. Fold down the fabric, carefully aligning the edges. If you measured right, all should be perfectly lined up. Just like when you stuck the fabric to the first two sides of the arm rest you can peel off the fabric from the long side  post-it note style  and rearrange it if needed.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter wp-image-5443" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables5.jpg" width="550"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Now, were past point of no return.* For each side of the arm rest, starting with the shortest side, youll paint a layer of Matte Mod Podge and run the brayer over it to really work in the Podge. Same order as last time  small side, top side, long side. I find this easiest for grappling with the arm rests asymmetrical shape. Also, use your arm rest stand as necessary to keep from messing up your work.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter wp-image-5444" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables6.jpg" width="550"/>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <em>
           *Yep, that
           <strong>
            was
           </strong>
           a Phantom of the Opera reference. I admit it.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Repeat the whole upholstery process on the second arm rest and let both dry over night. In the morning, paint a layer of Mod Podge Hard Coat over the top of the whole thing with a sponge brush (no brayer required), and let dry for a few days. The instructions on the bottle for Hard Coat Mod Podge recommend letting it cure (get super duper sealed) for 4 days. So basically dont put a bunch of condensation-covered beers on it for a few days before using. The Hard Coat is great because after the pain-in-the-butt that is curing, youll have a totally awesome, space-saving surface to set drinks, remote controls, or whatever on that you can wipe clean. Nice, huh?
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <span class="pibfi_pinterest ">
           <img alt="How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables | Hands Occupied" class="aligncenter" height="400" src="http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables7.jpg" width="600"/>
           <span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://www.handsoccupied.com/2013/08/how-to-upholstered-arm-rest-end-tables/&amp;media=http://www.handsoccupied.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/armresttables7.jpg&amp;description=How-to: Upholstered Arm Rest End Tables')">
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          This project takes some doing, but it saved me so much money and a lot of space my tiny living room doesnt have. How would you make this project yours? Different fabric? Covering the whole thing in fabric instead of just the top? Using Sparkle Mod Podge instead of Matte? Let me know, ladies!
         </p>
        </div>
