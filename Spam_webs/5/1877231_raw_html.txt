       <header class="entry-header">
        <h2 class="entry-title">
         How Much Are Miles Really Worth? Valuations for Each Program
        </h2>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <span class="entry-author">
          by
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/author/viewfromthewing/">
           <span class="entry-author-name">
            Gary Leff
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-12-07T12:00:22+00:00">
          on December 7, 2014
         </time>
        </p>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         What is the value of miles and points by airline, hotel and credit card program?  Miles used to be thought to be worth 2 cents.  I think that conventional wisdom developed out of the idea that a cross country flight cost about $500 or 25,000 miles.  20 years ago that was generally true, and award availability wasnt really a problem either.
        </p>
        <p>
         That was long before:
         <br/>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Airline alliances opened up huge possibilities for miles.
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          Mileage programs increased the cost of many awards.
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          Awards became tougher to get with flights full.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Plus the 2 cent number probably wasnt ever true to begin with.
        </p>
        <p>
         Many different folks have taken a stab at how much miles are worth.  For this post Im not going to give you a single number.  Im going to share
         <i>
          my own rough and ready number for several different programs
         </i>
         .  And Im going to explain how I think about the value of miles  why they are different for different people, and for different circumstances of how you plan to use them.
        </p>
        <h2>
         The Value of Points are Not Actually Fixed.
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres how to think about the value of miles and points by airline, hotel and credit card program.
         <span id="more-28526">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          It depends on how you redeem them.
         </b>
         What value are you going to get for your points?  The important thing here is not to use the retail price of a ticket youre getting, since
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          with premium cabin rewards you might not have been willing to spend that much cash.
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          Frequent flyer tickets arent necessarily worth as much as a paid ticket.  They dont earn miles. They may not be upgradeable.  And you cant necessarily just pick whatever flight you want, you have to be flexible and worry about award availability.
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         <b>
          It depends on when youre going to redeem them.
         </b>
         You dont earn a rate of return on miles and points like you might with cash in a bank or investment account.  And you need to discount to present value if youre going to use the points later.  Plus theres substantial risk of devaluation with many points currencies.
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          It depends on how many you already have.
         </b>
         The value of points
         <i>
          at the margin
         </i>
         is different than an overall average value.  As you approach having enough points for an award, the marginal value of a few more points goes up substantially  since those extra points are what make the award possible.  On the other hand, once you have more points than youll redeem in the near-term the value of additional points falls since you may not ever use them, or may not use them under current award charts.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Nonetheless We Can Make Some Overall Comparisons
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <b>
          value of a mile is the amount at which you are indifferent to holding miles versus cash
         </b>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         If a mile is worth 2 cents you should be equally happy with a mile or two pennies, if youre offered a mile at a price of 1.9 cents you would be a buyer  youd consider yourself to be earning a 5% margin.
        </p>
        <p>
         Put another way, when we put a charge on a credit card, that doesnt earn any bonuses (it earns 1 mile per dollar spent), were effectively buying that mile for 2 cents since the opportunity cost is putting the charge on a 2% cash back card.
         <i>
          Youre revealing a preference through your behavior
         </i>
         that you believe the mile is worth two cents.
        </p>
        <h2>
         My Overall List of Values
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres
         <b>
          my overall ranking and valuation
         </b>
         of several common airline, hotel, and credit card points currencies.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <img alt="value of miles and points by airline" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/OverallPointValues.jpg"/>
        </ul>
        <h2>
         Transferrable Points Currencies Top the List
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <b>
          most valuable points, at the top of my currency list, are transferrable points
         </b>
         .  That means Starwood, American Express, and Starwood points.  You can transfer those into a variety of programs.  Earning those gives you tremendous flexibility and optionality.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <img alt="value of points" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/TransferrablePoints.jpg"/>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Starwood lets you transfer 20,000 points into 25,000 miles with the most number of airlines.  That means their points are worth
         <i>
          at least
         </i>
         25% more than the most valuable currency they transfer into.  And theyre worth
         <i>
          even more than that
         </i>
         because of their option value, the ability to put points into the account you need them in when you need them  to top off an account at the margin (remember, miles at the margin that you need for an award are worth even more).
        </p>
        <p>
         As a result, if anything, I may be undervaluing Starwood Starpoints a little bit.
        </p>
        <p>
         Meanwhile, Chase transfers to several valuable partners  and unlike Starwood in most cases the transfers happen instantly.  I like that Chase transfers to United (no fuel surcharges) and duplicates Amex with British Airways and Singapore.  They also have a more valuable transfer partner and ratio in Hyatt than Amex does.  Thats why I value their points a bit more highly than American Express Membership Rewards.
        </p>
        <p>
         Nonetheless, American Express has
         <i>
          more
         </i>
         transfer partners and many do transfer instantly.
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/10/08/top-amex-mr-redemptions-destination-now-delta-transfers-capped/" target="_blank">
          Here are the best ones
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h2>
         The Value of Miles and Points by Airline
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Here are airline programs compared.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <img alt="how much are airline miles worth how much are miles worth" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/AirlineMiles.jpg"/>
        </ul>
        <p>
         United miles get you access to the Star Alliance, which means the best availability between the US and both Europe and Asia in business class.  There are no
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2013/07/23/if-fuel-prices-fall-will-i-still-have-to-pay-fuel-surcharges-on-award-tickets-and-how-can-i-reduce-those-now/" target="_blank">
          fuel surcharges
         </a>
         .  But awards are
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2013/11/01/uniteds-frequent-flyer-award-prices-going-redeem-now/" target="_blank">
          quite expensive when traveling in premium cabins on partner airlines, impossibly so if you want international first class
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         American on the other hand has a more reasonable award chart (and an indication that we wont see wholesale changes to it in 2015).  It is best for first class awards  Cathay Pacific, Etihad, Qatar (limited routes), British Airways (fuel surcharges) as well as somewhat more difficult gets
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/10/15/reliable-trick-get-qantas-first-class-awards/" target="_blank">
          Qantas
         </a>
         and Malaysia Airlines.
        </p>
        <p>
         Alaska Airlines miles are often undervalued  they partner with many airlines in both the oneworld and Skyteam alliances, offer one-way awards, and show availability for most partners on their website.  The biggest drawback is that you cannot combine different partners on a single one-way award.  I love that they partner with Emirates, whose A380 first class awards have become
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/09/24/emirates-a380-first-class-award-space-wide-open-winter-act-fast/" target="_blanK">
          quite easy to get on several U.S. routes
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         I value Aeroplan points a little bit below United miles.  Theyre both members of the Star Alliance, and for many awards their prices in miles are cheaper but they add fuel surcharges onto many of those partners.
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/08/30/booking-star-alliance-awards-using-amex-no-fuel-surcharges/" target="_blanK">
          Heres how to book Star Alliance awards without fuel surcharges
         </a>
         .  And see also (for both Aeroplan and United)
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/11/03/star-alliance-partners-award-tickets-guide/" target="_blank">
          how to book award tickets on Star Alliance partners
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         British Airways points are worth still less to me, although I make great use of them on domestic short non-stop flights.  I can get 4-7 cent s point in value sometimes, and for trips I really do need to take.  But long distance flying, especially in premium cabins, is super expensive.  And they add fuel surcharges onto awards to boot.  First class Los Angeles  Sydney on Qantas?  Thats 300,000 points and ~ $900 roundtrip  and that wouldnt include any flights to and from Los Angeles, or beyond Sydney!
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/10/30/great-deal-british-airways-visa-drops-annual-fee-first-year-still-offers-50000-points/" target="_blank">
          Heres a primer on the overall value of the British Airways program
         </a>
         and on the British Airways Visa.
        </p>
        <p>
         Korean Air  expected to return as a Chase trasnfer partner  on the other hand has a pretty good award chart, does add fuel surcharges to awards, but offers amazing first class award availability.  The two challenges are that theyre a member of Skyteam, so their partners arent as good and dont often offer as good award availability as some of their competitors, and also that you have to deal with the idiosyncrasies of Korean  rules like only redeeming your points for family members.
        </p>
        <p>
         Still, their better award chart.. option for one-way awards.. and availability of first class means that I value their miles slightly more than Deltas.
        </p>
        <p>
         The least value points currency among the major ones often discussed by US frequent flyers has to be Virgin Atlantics  fuel surcharges, cannot mix and match partners, partner awards that cannot be booked one-way, and challenging call center agents all rolled up into one.
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/01/29/virgin-atlantic-may-worst-miles-8-great-ways-can-use/" target="_blank">
          Here are the best uses for Virgin Atlantics miles
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h2>
         Hotel Points Use a Totally Different Scale
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Here are my values for hotel program points compared:
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <img alt="value of hotel points" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/HotelPoints.jpg"/>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Hotel loyalty programs simply use a different scale than airlines for the most part.  A room with Hilton HHonors might well cost 90,000 points for a single night, and points in many programs can be transfered to airline miles at ratios like 2:1 or even 5:1.
        </p>
        <p>
         On the other hand they give out points in pretty large denominations.  Ive often assumed that was to make you feel like you were getting a lot especially if you were used to airline miles.  Earning 5000 airline miles for a roundtrip flight might make 20,000 points for your hotel stay feel quite rewarding.
        </p>
        <p>
         So its a different question entirely to ask
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/06/26/hotel-program-rewarding-spend-answer-may-shock/" target="_blanK">
          which hotel program is the most rewarding for your spending
         </a>
         in terms of free nights.  They each award and redeem points at different rates, so I
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2014/06/26/hotel-program-rewarding-spend-answer-may-shock/" target="_blank">
          created a way to compare them
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres were just looking at what 1 point is worth in each program.  And Starwood points have to be worth at least 25% more than American miles, since they transfer in 20,000 point increments at a rate of 1:1.25.  And you get optionality of where to point the points.  And you can redeem for hotels, Ill pretty much always do better than 2 cents a point in value (usually quite a bit better).
        </p>
        <p>
         At the high end Hyatt points can be worth quite a bit, especially for category 5 and 6 properties during high season and even more so when cash and points awards are available.  Still, the airline transfers arent as lucrative as Starwoods and if youre looking at an ability to trade in Hyatt points for a hotel stay where youd otherwise use cash I find that most of the time you can do a bit better than 1.5 cents.  I value the cash more than the points at the margin, though, so Im not actually a buyer at 1.5 cents.. I downgrade them slightly plus I would choose Aeroplan points over Hyatt points 1:1.
        </p>
        <p>
         Hilton points are worth a little less than a half cent apiece, the average redemption I find hovers somewhere just above that half cent mark.  And IHG Rewards points are worth only a little bit less than
         <a href="http://viewfromthewing.boardingarea.com/2012/05/18/priority-club-increases-cost-of-cash-points-awards/" target="_blank">
          what you can buy them for through the back door
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h2>
         How Do You Value Your Miles?
        </h2>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         What is
         <u>
          your
         </u>
         value of miles and points by airline, hotel and credit card program?
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           Let me know if you think Im off base on any of my valuations!
