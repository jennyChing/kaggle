       <p>
        Running late to school is a punishable offence in a growing number of schools in the United Kingdom.
        <em>

      <p>
       When my daughter started school this year I was nervous about getting her there on time every day. Punctuality has never been my strong point. I lose track of time thinking that I can finish that e-mail or get the laundry on the line, when really we should be heading out the door.
      </p>
      <p>
       We haven't been late so far, but often we've made it by the skin of our teeth, running through the gates as the bell starts to sound. It is inevitable that one day, we will be late. So when I read that some schools in the UK are fining parents the equivalent of $117 when their kids are late I was horrified.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thousands of schools across the UK are taking part in the scheme with some deploying "late gate patrols" to stop tardy parents and ask them why their children are not on time. If parents don't pay up within 21 days the fine is doubled. Some councils have even threatened prosecution and prison time for parents who refuse to pay.
      </p>
      <p>
       While there are currently no plans to implement a similar scheme in Australia, it does raise interesting questions about lateness.
      </p>

       I asked a group of Australian parents what they thought of the scheme and whether or not they would support lateness fines in Australian schools. Unsurprisingly, the overwhelming majority were against fines.
      </p>
      <p>
       Deb, a mother of two, says that fines would punish families that are already under pressure. "Never have we lived in a world so full of pressures and demands on families - especially mothers. There are many unsupported mothers, some with other younger kids that may have special needs, or the mothers may have health issues themselves."
      </p>
      <p>
       Louise, a mother of one, says that while she does not support something as extreme as fines, something does need to be done to encourage punctuality.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Of course there will be the occasional drama or an important event that causes lateness, and schools should be understanding, but on every other day I think it's important that parents get their kids there on time.
      </p>
      <p>
       "The kids learn that school is something to be valued, and the teacher can get the day's learning going without disruptions," she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Likewise, Mark, a father of one says that going down the disciplinary path could lead to more conflict between schools and families. "Where would that path lead to anyway?" he asks. "Fines for unhealthy lunches? For clothes with holes in them or food dropped down the front? Having an out of date iPad/learning device?"
      </p>
      <p>
       So would teachers support fines for lateness? Primary school teacher Daniel Mitchel says that latecomers can be disruptive to the class. And for children that are often late, he worries that there can be a cumulative effect on their education. "Over time, lateness adds up and can really impact their learning," he says.
      </p>
      <p>
       But while Mitchell thinks that being on time is important, he does not support the idea of fining parents of latecomers. "On the one hand you do need to be tough and send the message that it's not acceptable, but on the other hand by being punitive and fining parents you are just going to put them offside," he explains.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mitchell thinks that a better solution to lateness would be to foster good relationships with families and educate them about the importance of being on time.
      </p>
      <p>
       "We need better communication with families," he says. "Rather than fining them we need to support them to get their kids to school on time," he says.
