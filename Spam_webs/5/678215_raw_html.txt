     <p>
      <b>
       Khaki
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
         UK
        </a>
       </small>
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'father'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ long 'e' in 'bead'">
           i
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      and
      <span class="nowrap">
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
         US
        </a>
       </small>
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'a' in 'bad'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ long 'e' in 'bead'">
           i
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ) is a color, a light shade of yellow-brown.
      <i>
       Khaki
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
       loanword
      </a>
      incorporated from
      <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">
       Hindustani
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">
       Urdu
      </a>
      ) / (meaning "soil-colored") and is originally derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
       Persian
      </a>
      :
      <b>
       <span class="Nastaliq" style="font-size: 125%; font-family: 'Jameel Noori Nastaleeq', 'Urdu Typesetting', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu', 'Hussaini Nastaleeq', 'AlQalam Taj Nastaleeq', IranNastaliq, 'Nafees Nastaleeq', 'Nafees Nastaleeq v1.01', Nafees, 'Pak Nastaleeq', 'PDMS_Jauhar', 'Alvi Lahori Nastaleeq';" title="Urdu Nastaliq">
        
       </span>
      </b>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Persian" title="Help:IPA for Persian">
        [xk]
       </a>
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       Khk
      </i>
      , literally meaning "soil"), which came to English from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_India" title="British India">
       British India
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      via the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">
       British Indian Army
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Khaki has been used by many armies around the world for uniforms, including
      <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">
       camouflage
      </a>
      . It has been used as a color name in English since 1848 when it was first introduced as a military uniform, and was called both drab and khaki
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soldier_1859_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Soldier_1859-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      - khaki being a translation of the English drab light-brown color.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A khaki uniform is often referred to as
      <b>
       khakis
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Western fashion, it is a standard color for
      <a href="/wiki/Smart_casual" title="Smart casual">
       smart casual
      </a>
      dress trousers for civilians, which are also often called
      <i>
       khakis
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
       Origin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Khaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Khaki was first worn in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corps_of_Guides_(British_India)" title="Corps of Guides (British India)">
       Corps of Guides
      </a>
      that was raised in December 1846 as the brain-child of Sir Henry Lawrence (1806-1857) Resident at Lahore, and Agent to the Governor-General for the North-West Frontier. Lawrence chose as its commandant
      <a href="/wiki/Harry_Burnett_Lumsden" title="Harry Burnett Lumsden">
       Sir Harry Lumsden
      </a>
      supported by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Stephen_Raikes_Hodson" title="William Stephen Raikes Hodson">
       William Stephen Raikes Hodson
      </a>
      as Second-in-Command to begin the process of raising the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corps_of_Guides_(British_India)" title="Corps of Guides (British India)">
       Corps of Guides
      </a>
      for frontier service from British Indian recruits at
      <a href="/wiki/Peshawar" title="Peshawar">
       Peshawar
      </a>
      , Punjab. Initially the border troops were dressed in their native costume, which consisted of a smock and white pajama trousers made of a coarse home-spun cotton, and a cotton turban, supplemented by a leather or padded cotton jacket for cold weather. For the first year (1847) no attempt was made at uniformity. Subsequently in 1848 Lumsden and Hodson decided to introduce a drab (khaki) uniform
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which Hodson commissioned his brother in England to send them  as recorded in Hodson's book of published letters,
      <i>
       Twelve Years of a Soldier's Life in India
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soldier_1859_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Soldier_1859-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was only at a later date, when supplies of drab (khaki) material was unavailable, did they improvise by dying material locally with a dye prepared from the native
      <a href="/wiki/Nannorrhops" title="Nannorrhops">
       mazari palm
      </a>
      . Some believe the gray drab/khaki color it produced was used historically by Afghan tribals for camouflaging themselves. The mazari could not, however, dye leather jackets and an alternative was sought: Cloth was dyed in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mulberry" title="Mulberry">
       mulberry
      </a>
      juice which gave a yellowish drab shade.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Invicta_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Invicta-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :537539
      </sup>
      Subsequently all regiments, whether British or Indian, serving in the region had adopted khaki uniforms for active service and summer dress. The original khaki fabric was a closely twilled cloth of
      <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">
       linen
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">
       cotton
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Military_use">
       Military use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Khaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="648" data-file-width="667" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg/220px-Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg/330px-Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg/440px-Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chennai_City_Mounted_Police.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Policemen in India in their khaki-colored uniform
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Khaki-colored uniforms were used officially by British troops for the first time during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1868_Expedition_to_Abyssinia" title="1868 Expedition to Abyssinia">
       Abyssinian campaign
      </a>
      of 186768, when Indian troops traveled to Ethiopia (
      <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Empire" title="Ethiopian Empire">
       Abyssinia
      </a>
      ) under the command of general
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_Napier" title="Sir Robert Napier">
       Sir Robert Napier
      </a>
      to release some British captives and to "persuade the Abyssinian King
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tewodros_II_of_Ethiopia" title="Tewodros II of Ethiopia">
       Theodore
      </a>
      , forcibly if necessary, to mend his ways".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Subsequently, the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      adopted khaki for colonial campaign dress and it was used in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mahdist_War" title="Mahdist War">
       Mahdist War
      </a>
      (1884-1889) and
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">
       Second Boer War
      </a>
      (18991902).
     </p>
     <p>
      During the Second Boer War, the British forces became known as Khakis because of their uniforms. After victory in the war the government called an election, which became known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Khaki_election" title="Khaki election">
       khaki election
      </a>
      , a term used subsequently for elections called to exploit public approval of governments immediately after victories.
     </p>

     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
       United States Army
      </a>
      adopted khaki during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_American_War" title="Spanish American War">
       Spanish American War
      </a>
      (1898). The
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">
       United States Marine Corps
      </a>
      followed suit.
     </p>
     <p>
      When khaki was adopted for the continental British
      <a href="/wiki/Service_Dress_(British_Army)" title="Service Dress (British Army)">
       Service Dress
      </a>
      in 1902, the shade chosen had a clearly darker and more green hue. This color was adopted with minor variations by all the British Empire Armies and the US expeditionary force of
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      , in the latter under the name
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olive_drab" title="Olive drab">
       olive drab
      </a>
      . This shade of brown-green remained in use by many countries throughout the two World Wars. Khaki was devised to protect soldiers against the dangers of the industrialized battlefield, where the traditional bright colors and elaborate costumes made them vulnerable to attack. A response to surveillance technologies and smokeless guns, khaki could camouflage soldiers in the field of battle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_civilian_clothing">
       Use in civilian clothing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Khaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in civilian clothing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The trousers known as "khakis", which became popular following World War II, were initially military-issue khaki twill used in uniforms and were invariably khaki in color. Today, the term can refer to the fabric and style of trousers based on this older model, also called "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinos" title="Chinos">
       chinos
      </a>
      ", rather than their color.
     </p>
