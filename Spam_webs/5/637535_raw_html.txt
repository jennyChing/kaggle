     <p>
      <b>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        Tah
       </span>
      </b>
      (Pli; Sanskrit:
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        t
       </span>
      </i>
      , also
      <i>
       trishna
      </i>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhist
      </a>
      term that literally means "thirst," and is commonly translated as craving or desire. Within Buddhism,
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        tah
       </span>
      </i>
      is defined as the craving to hold on to pleasurable experiences, to be separated from painful or unpleasant experiences, and for neutral experiences or feelings not to decline. The Buddhist tradition identifies
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        tah
       </span>
      </i>
      as a self-centered type of desire that is based in
      <a href="/wiki/Avidy%C4%81_(Buddhism)" title="Avidy (Buddhism)">
       ignorance
      </a>
      . This type of desire is contrasted to wholesome types of desire such as the desire to benefit others or to follow the Buddhist path. In the first teaching of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">
       Buddha
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Four_Noble_Truths" title="Four Noble Truths">
       Four Noble Truths
      </a>
      , the Buddha identified
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       tah
      </span>
      as a principal cause in the arising of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">
        dukkha
       </a>
      </i>
      (suffering, anxiety, dissatisfaction).
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Tah
      </span>
      is also identified as the eighth link in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Nidanas" title="Twelve Nidanas">
       Twelve Links of Dependent Origination
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        Tah
       </span>
      </i>
      is the craving to hold onto pleasurable experiences, to be separated from painful or unpleasant experiences, and for neutral experiences or feelings not to decline.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpola1_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-walpola1-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hopkins1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sonam1_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sonam1-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sucitto1_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sucitto1-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leifer1_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-leifer1-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the first teaching of the Buddha on the
      <a href="/wiki/Four_Noble_Truths" title="Four Noble Truths">
       Four Noble Truths
      </a>
      , the Buddha identified
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       tah
      </span>
      as a principal cause in the arising of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">
        dukkha
       </a>
      </i>
      (suffering, anxiety, unsatisfactoriness).
      <a href="/wiki/Walpola_Rahula" title="Walpola Rahula">
       Walpola Rahula
      </a>
      states:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpola1_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-walpola1-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       It is this "thirst", desire, greed, craving, manifesting itself in various ways, that gives rise to all forms of suffering and the continuity of beings. But it should not be taken as the first cause, for there is no first cause possible as, according to Buddhism, everything is relative and inter-dependent. Even this "thirst", tah, which is considered as the cause or origin of
       <a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">
        dukkha
       </a>
       , depends for its arising (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samudaya" title="Samudaya">
        samudaya
       </a>
       ) on something else, which is sensation (
       <a href="/wiki/Vedan%C4%81" title="Vedan">
        vedan
       </a>
       ), and sensation arises depending on contact (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phassa" title="Phassa">
        phassa
       </a>
       ), and so on and so forth goes on the circle which is known as Conditioned Genesis (
       <a href="/wiki/Prat%C4%ABtyasamutp%C4%81da" title="Prattyasamutpda">
        Paicca-samuppda
       </a>
       )... So tah, "thirst", is not the first or the only cause of the arising of dukkha. But it is the most palpable and immediate cause, the "principal thing" and the "all-pervading thing". Hence in certain places of the original Pali texts themselves the definition of samudaya or the origin of dukkha includes other defilements and impurities (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilesa" title="Kilesa">
        kiles
       </a>
       , ssav dhamm), in addition to tah "thirst" which is always given the first place. Within the necessarily limited space of our discussion, it will be sufficient if we remember that this "thirst" has as its centre the false idea of self arising out of
       <a href="/wiki/Avidy%C4%81_(Buddhism)" title="Avidy (Buddhism)">
        ignorance
       </a>
       .
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Tah
      </span>
      is also identified as the eighth link in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_Nidanas" title="Twelve Nidanas">
       Twelve Links of Dependent Origination
      </a>
      . In the context of the twelve links, the emphasis is on the types of craving "that nourish the karmic potency that will produce the next lifetime."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hopkins1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Tah
      </span>
      is a type of desire that can never be satisfied.
      <a href="/wiki/Ajahn_Sucitto" title="Ajahn Sucitto">
       Ajahn Sucitto
      </a>
      states:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sucitto1_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sucitto1-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       However, tanh, meaning "thirst," is not a chosen kind of desire, it's a reflex. It's the desire to pull something in and feed on it, the desire that's never satisfied because it just shifts from one sense base to another, from one emotional need to the next, from one sense of achievement to another goal. It's the desire that comes from a black hole of need, however small and manageable that need is. The Buddha said that regardless of its specific topics, this thirst relates to three channels: sense-craving (kmatah); craving to be something, to unite with an experience (bhavatah); and craving to be nothing, or to dissociate from an experience (vibhavatah).
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
       Types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Buddha identified three types of
      <i>
       tah
      </i>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpola1_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-walpola1-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sucitto1_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sucitto1-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leifer1_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-leifer1-5">

     <p>
      Contemporary Buddhist teacher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chogyam_Trungpa" title="Chogyam Trungpa">
       Chogyam Trungpa
      </a>
      emphasizes the non-deliberate quality of tanha. He states:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">

     <blockquote class="templatequote">

       [Craving] is like someone who is extremely hungry. Such a person doesn't actually think in terms of eating the food, chewing it and swallowing it. Instead the food just goes into his stomach. It's very simple, there's no effort involved, it just goes into him... Craving in this case is not so much what the weightwatcher's club talks about, but it's genuine craving. It actually just happens. We could actually say to somebody literally, "I don't know what happened, I just did it. It just happened to me. It just happens to me constantly." ...      <h3>

     <p>
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Tah
      </span>
      encompasses both the desire to get something and its opposite, the desire to get rid of it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ron Leifer states:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>

      <p>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">

       </span>
       itself is bipolar, divided into greed and hatred, or passion and aggression. On the one hand is the desire to have something, to possess it, to experience it, to pull it in, to own it. On the other hand is the desire to avoid something, to keep it away, reject it, renounce it, destroy it, and separate it from oneself.      <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cessation_of">
       Cessation of
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cessation of">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Four_Noble_Truths" title="Four Noble Truths">
       third noble truth
      </a>
      teaches that the cessation of
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        tah
       </span>
      </i>
      is possible. For example, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dhammacakkappavattana_Sutta" title="Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta">
        Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta
       </a>
      </i>
      states:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Bhikkhus, there is a noble truth about the cessation of suffering. It is the complete fading away and cessation of this craving [
       <i>
        tanha
       </i>
       ]; its abandonment and relinquishment; getting free from and being independent of it.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      According to the four noble truths, cessation of
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       tah
      </span>
      can be obtained by following the
      <a href="/wiki/Noble_Eightfold_Path" title="Noble Eightfold Path">
       Noble Eightfold Path
      </a>
      . Within this path, contemplating the
      <a href="/wiki/Impermanence" title="Impermanence">
       impermanent nature
      </a>
      of all things is regarded as a specific antidote to
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       tah
      </span>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrast_to_wholesome_desire_.28chanda.29">
       Contrast to wholesome desire (chanda)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Contrast to wholesome desire (chanda)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Buddhist teachings contrast the reflexive, self-centered desire of
      <i>
       tah
      </i>
      with wholesome types of desire, such as the desire to benefit others or the desire to follow the Buddhist path.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        c
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Wholesome types of desire are traditionally identified as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chanda_(Buddhism)" title="Chanda (Buddhism)">
        chanda
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-succito_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-succito-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        d
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ajahn_Sucitto" title="Ajahn Sucitto">
       Ajahn Sucitto
      </a>
      states:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Sometimes tah is translated as desire, but that gives rise to some crucial misinterpretations with reference to the way of Liberation. As we shall see, some form of desire is essential in order to aspire to, and persist in, cultivating the path out of dukkha. Desire as an eagerness to offer, to commit, to apply oneself to meditation, is called
       <a href="/wiki/Chanda_(Buddhism)" title="Chanda (Buddhism)">
        chanda
       </a>
       . Its a psychological yes, a choice, not a pathology. In fact, you could summarize Dhamma training as the transformation of tah into chanda. Its a process whereby we guide volition, grab and hold on to the steering wheel, and travel with clarity toward our deeper well-being. So were not trying to get rid of desire (which would take another kind of desire, wouldnt it). Instead, we are trying to transmute it, take it out of the shadow of gratification and need, and use its aspiration and vigor to bring us into light and clarity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-succito_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-succito-23">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         20
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_the_three_poisons">
       Relation to the three poisons
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relation to the three poisons">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Tah
      </span>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avidya_(Buddhism)" title="Avidya (Buddhism)">
       avidya
      </a>
      (ignorance) can be related to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_poisons" title="Three poisons">
       three poisons
      </a>
      as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        e
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avidya_(Buddhism)" title="Avidya (Buddhism)">
         Avidya
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Moha
       </i>
       (ignorance), the root of the three poisons, is also the basis for
       <i>
        tah
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Raga_(Buddhism)" title="Raga (Buddhism)">
         Raga
        </a>
       </i>
       (attachment) is equivalent to
       <i>
        bhava-tah
       </i>
       (craving to be) and kma-tah (sense-craving).
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dvesha_(Buddhism)" title="Dvesha (Buddhism)">
         Dosa (Dvesha)
        </a>
       </i>
       (aversion) is equivalent to
       <i>
        vibhava-tah
       </i>
       (craving not to be).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      For example, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Dhammacakkappavattana_Sutta" title="Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta">
       first discourse
      </a>
      of the Buddha, the Buddha identified
      <i>
       tanha
      </i>
      as the principal cause of suffering. However, his third discourse, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fire_Sermon" title="Fire Sermon">
        Fire Sermon
       </a>
      </i>
      , and other suttas, the Buddha identifies the causes of suffering as the "fires" of
      <i>
       raga
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       dosa
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       dvesha
      </i>
      ), and
      <i>
       moha
      </i>
      ; in the
      <i>
       Fire Sermon
      </i>
      , the Buddha states that nirvana is obtained by extinguishing these fires.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarvey201373_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarvey201373-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        </dd>

      </li>
      <li>

          <a href="#cite_note-33">
           <span>

       </a>
       states:
       <dl>
        <dd>
         Attachment is in many ways comparable to addiction, a compulsive dependency on external objects or experiences to manufacture an illusion of wholeness. Unfortunately, like other addictions, attachment becomes more intense over time.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
          <a href="#cite_note-34">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           29
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
          <a href="#cite_note-35">
           <span>

           </span>
           f

       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Titmuss" title="Christopher Titmuss">

       </a>
       states:
       <dl>
        <dd>

To take whats called the three poisons of the mind: greed, its got desire in it, obviously; anger, violence, it has desire in it; fear, has desire in it; confusion, has desire in it. So, replication of the word is desire is something which is problematic, which has an impact on our own life. Stress has desire in it, worry has desire in it, anxiety, etc. And it also has its impacting consequence on others.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Translating_the_term_tanha">
       Translating the term
       <i>
        tanha
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Translating the term tanha">

       </span>
      </span>

     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       tanha
      </i>
      is sometimes translated as "craving" or "desire". However, some translators prefer to leave the term
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        tah
       </span>
      </i>
      untranslated. For example, Smith and Novak emphasize the difficulty of translating this term as follows:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The cause of lifes dislocation is
       <i>
        tanha
       </i>

There is some truth in this, but if we try to make desire tanhas equivalent, we run into difficulties. To begin with, the equivalence would make this Second Truth unhelpful, for to shut down desires, all desires, in our present state would be to die, and to die is not to solve lifes problem. But beyond being unhelpful, the claim of equivalence would be flatly wrong, for there are some desires the Buddha explicitly advocatedthe desire for liberation, for example, or for the happiness of others.

Unfulfilled craving and frustrated attachment become the conditions for aversion, anger, depression, hatred, and cruelty and violence which are in themselves quite manifestly unpleasant (dukha as pain) and in turn bring further suffering. But what about doubt, agitation, and ignorance? Developed Buddhist thought understands these states as having an important psychological relationship. Even in the absence of craving and aversion, we view the world through a mind that is often fundamentally unclear, unsettled, and confused. Not surprisingly we fail to see things as they truly are. At this point it begins to become quite apparent just how and why craving leads to suffering. There is a discrepancy between our craving and the world we live in, between our expectations and the way things are. We want the world to be other than it is. Our craving is based on a fundamental misjudgement of the situation; a judgement that assumes that when our craving gets what it wants we will be happy, that when our craving possesses the objects of its desire we will be satisfied. But such a judgement in turn assumes a world in which things are permanent, unchanging, stable, and reliable. But the world is simply not like that. In short, in craving we fail to see how things truly are, and in failing to see how things truly are we crave. In other words craving goes hand in hand with a fundamental ignorance and misapprehension of the nature of the world.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGethin199873-74_27-0">
           <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGethin199873-74-27">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            23
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
         <li>
          Ron Leifer relates
          <i>
           tanha
          </i>
          to
          <i>
           raga
          </i>
          and
          <i>
           doha
          </i>
          as follows: "
          <i>
           Tanha
          </i>
          itself is bibolar, divided into greed and hatred, or passion and aggression. On the one hand is the desire to have something, to possess it, to experience it, to pull it in, to own it. On the other hand is the desire to avoid something, to keep it away, reject it, renounce it, destroy it, and separate it from oneself. If we call these two poles desire and aversion, we can see more clearly that they represent the antithetical poles of tahthe desire to possess and the desire to get rid of.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeifer199796_28-0">
           <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeifer199796-28">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            24
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
         <li>
          Leifer relates
          <i>
           tanha
          </i>
          to
          <i>
           avidya
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           moho
          </i>
          ) as follows: "Tanha, desire, is interwoven with avidya, ignorance, by means of the mistaken presumption that the samsaric dance of opposites is ultimate reality. Ignorance is the mother of greed and hatred because it gives them life. It rationalizes and justifies them."
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeifer199797_29-0">
           <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeifer199797-29">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            25
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>

        <span class="mw-cite-backlink">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Further_reading">
       Further reading
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%E1%B9%87h%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Further reading">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Philosophy of the Buddha
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Archie_J._Bahm" title="Archie J. Bahm">
        Archie J. Bahm
       </a>
       . Asian Humanities Press. Berkeley, CA: 1993.
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0875730256">
        ISBN 0-87573-025-6
       </a>
       .
       <ul>
        <li>
         Chapter 5 is about craving, and discusses the difference between
         <i>
          <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
           tah
          </span>
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          chanda
         </i>
         .
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       "Nietzsche and Buddhism: A Study in Nihilism and Ironic Affinities" by Robert Morrison. Oxford University Press, 1998.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Chapter 10 is a comparison between Nietzsche's Will to Power and Tanha, which gives a very nuanced and positive explanation of the central role tanha plays in the Buddhist path.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
