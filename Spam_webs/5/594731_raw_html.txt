   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Mungiki
      </b>
      is a banned ethnic organization and sect
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">
       Kenya
      </a>
      . The name means "A united people" or "
      <a href="/wiki/Multitude" title="Multitude">
       multitude
      </a>
      " in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kikuyu_language" title="Kikuyu language">
       Kikuyu language
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The religion, which apparently originated in the late 1980s, is secretive and bears some similarity to
      <a href="/wiki/Western_esotericism" title="Western esotericism">
       mystery religions
      </a>
      . Specifics of their origin and doctrines are unclear. What is clear is that they favor a return to indigenous African traditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpost_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wpost-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      They reject
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westernisation" title="Westernisation">
       Westernisation
      </a>
      and all things that they believe to be trappings of
      <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">
       colonialism
      </a>
      , including
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      . The ideology of the group is characterised by revolutionary rhetoric, Kikuyu traditions, and a disdain for Kenyan
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernization" title="Modernization">
       modernization
      </a>
      , which is seen as immoral corruption.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpost_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wpost-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mungiki is often referred to as Kenyas
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosa_Nostra" title="Cosa Nostra">
       Cosa Nostra
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yakuza" title="Yakuza">
       Yakuza
      </a>
      , or Kenyan Mafia due to its organization.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      They have been newsworthy for associations with ethnic violence and anti-government resistance.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mungiki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to one of Mungiki's founders, the group began in the late 1980s as a local militia in the highlands to protect Kikuyu farmers in disputes over land with
      <a href="/wiki/Maasai_people" title="Maasai people">
       Maasai
      </a>
      and with forces loyal to the government, which was dominated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Kalenjin_people" title="Kalenjin people">
       Kalenjin
      </a>
      ribe at the time. Mungiki arguably has its roots in discontent arising from severe unemployment and landlessness arising from Kenya's rapid population growth, with many disaffected unemployed youth attracted to an organisation giving them a sense of purpose and cultural and political identity, as well as income.
     </p>
     <p>
      The founders supposedly modelled Mungiki on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mau_Mau" title="Mau Mau">
       Mau Mau
      </a>
      fighters who fought British colonial rule. During the 1990s, the group had migrated into Nairobi with the acceptance of the government under
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_arap_Moi" title="Daniel arap Moi">
       Daniel arap Moi
      </a>
      and began to dominate the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Matatu" title="Matatu">
        matatu
       </a>
      </i>
      (private minibus taxi) industry. With the move to
      <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">
       Nairobi
      </a>
      came the development of a cell structure within the group. Each cell contains 50 members and each cell is then divided into 5
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platoons" title="Platoons">
       platoons
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Using the matatus as a springboard, the group moved into other areas of commerce, such as rubbish collection, construction, and even protection
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racketeering" title="Racketeering">
       racketeering
      </a>
      . Inevitably, the group's actions led to involvement with politicians eager for more support. In 2002, Mungiki backed losing candidates in elections and felt the wrath of the government. The group's activities became less visible although it still received revenue from protection taxes, electricity taxes and water taxes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There have been unconfirmed allegations that Mungiki has links to both the old
      <a href="/wiki/Kenya_African_National_Union" title="Kenya African National Union">
       KANU
      </a>
      government and some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MPs" title="MPs">
       MPs
      </a>
      in the current government. In fact, because of the cult's extreme secrecy, little is known about its membership or
      <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">
       hierarchy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many members state that at the height of its influence, the group could claim as many as 500,000 members and received substantial sums of money. Many Kenyans debate whether the group's influence in
      <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">
       Nairobi
      </a>
      is waning or is on the rise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpost_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-wpost-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Extortion_and_ethnic_violence">
       Extortion and ethnic violence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mungiki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extortion and ethnic violence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mungiki operates most extensively in
      <a href="/wiki/Mathare" title="Mathare">
       Mathare
      </a>
      , Nairobi's second largest
      <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">
       slum
      </a>
      , where poverty and crime are pronounced, but it is also in
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kayole&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kayole (page does not exist)">
       Kayole
      </a>
      '
      <a href="/wiki/Murang%27a" title="Murang'a">
       Murang'a
      </a>
      District and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruai,_Nairobi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruai, Nairobi (page does not exist)">
       Ruai, Nairobi
      </a>
      [waithaka, dagoretti], Kinoo and Westlands. A recent
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Inter_Press_Service" title="Inter Press Service">
        Inter Press Service
       </a>
      </i>
      article vividly describes Mungiki operations in that slum as essentially constituting a "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_gang" title="Street gang">
       street gang
      </a>
      " or a criminal network that contributes to, and feeds off of, an environment plagued by a state of perpetual security crisis.
     </p>
     <p>
      Every resident of the slum pays a variable sum of money to the organization, in exchange for protection against theft and property damage. In addition, the gang "mans" public toilets, and charges a fee for use of the facilities. Such acts of
      <a href="/wiki/Extortion" title="Extortion">
       extortion
      </a>
      , along with the general lack of effective local law enforcement, have generally enraged residents of Mathare.
     </p>
     <p>
      More than 50 people died in 2002 in clashes involving the sect and owners of
      <a href="/wiki/Matatu" title="Matatu">
       <i>
        matatus
       </i>
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">
       Nairobi
      </a>
      alone. In 2002 the sect was banned and in February 2003, the sect was in the news following two days of clashes with Nairobi police which left at least two officers dead and 74 sect members in police custody.
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 2007, the Mungiki embarked upon a murderous campaign to instil fear by beheading
      <i>
       matatu
      </i>
      drivers, conductors, and Mungiki defectors,and those who refuse their recruitment, drawing an armed response from Kenyan security forces, who stormed the Mathare area. Some 100 people died in the operation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mungiki has also been linked to the murder of a family in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA" title="USA">
       USA
      </a>
      in which Mrs Jane Kurua, 47, and her two daughters were killed; the case is still under investigation by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">
       FBI
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 12 July 2007 Kenyan authorities reported that Mungiki decapitated and mutilated the body of a two-year-old boy, possibly as part of a ritual.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is alleged that Mungiki members participated in targeted violence against ethnic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luo_(family_of_ethnic_groups)" title="Luo (family of ethnic groups)">
       Luos
      </a>
      around the time of the disputed
      <a href="/wiki/Kenyan_presidential_election,_2007" title="Kenyan presidential election, 2007">
       December 2007 presidential elections
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Police_Response">
       Police Response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mungiki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Police Response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In November 2007, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-rights" title="Human-rights">
       human-rights
      </a>
      group called the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oscar_Foundation_Free_Legal_Aid_Clinic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oscar Foundation Free Legal Aid Clinic (page does not exist)">
       Oscar Foundation Free Legal Aid Clinic
      </a>
      -Kenya reported that in the five years up to August 2007, Kenyan police had killed over 8,000 people in crackdowns against the Mungiki sect, with further 4,000 people still missing. These allegations were based on interviews, autopsies, and police reports, and were widely circulated both in Kenya
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and through an appeal to the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">
       International Criminal Court
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile, the
      <a href="/wiki/Kenya_National_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="Kenya National Commission on Human Rights">
       Kenya National Commission on Human Rights
      </a>
      linked the police to the execution of 500 Mungiki over the previous five months. The police described these reports as fictitious.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 5 March 2009, Oscar Foundation Director
      <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Kamau_Kingara" title="Oscar Kamau Kingara">
       Oscar Kamau Kingara
      </a>
      and Programme Coordinator
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Paul_Oulo" title="John Paul Oulo">
       John Paul Oulo
      </a>
      were shot and killed while en route to a meeting at the offices of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kenya_National_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="Kenya National Commission on Human Rights">
       Kenya National Commission on Human Rights
      </a>
      in Nairobi.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12

     <p>
      In 2007, Mungiki was rumoured to have fractured into two groups. In spite of the peace gestures of Prime Minister Raila Odinga, the dramatic murders of the top Mungiki leaders continued, and police also denied involvement in the assassinations. The Chairman and Treasurer of the Kenya National Youth Alliance (Maina Njenga faction) were gunned down at Uplands after a car chase on the Nairobi  Naivasha highway. The Kenya National Youth Alliance (KNYA) served as Mungikis political wing.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to relatives, Wagacha and Irungu were driving to Naivasha Prison, where Mungiki leader Maina Njenga is serving a jail term, to consult him over possible talks with the government, proposed by Prime Minister Odinga. The relatives said that elements in the government are using the police to ensure negotiations fail, hence the killings. However, police spokesman Eric Kiraithe denied the claims.
     </p>
     <p>
      At least 500 bodies of suspected Mungiki members have since been discovered in thickets outside Nairobi in the past year. Police say that the recent mysterious deaths of Mungiki leaders were a result of infighting between various Mungiki factions over control of funds and differing political positions. The Mungiki leadership, however, denied the split within their ranks.
     </p>

     <p>
      In early 2003, soon after
      <a href="/wiki/Mwai_Kibaki" title="Mwai Kibaki">
       Mwai Kibaki
      </a>
      came into power, the government gave the military leadership three days to explain why ten of their
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_Rovers" title="Land Rovers">
       Land Rovers
      </a>
      were given to the outlawed Mungiki sect. In the lead up to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Election" title="General Election">
       General Election
      </a>
      , then
      <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_the_General_Staff_(Kenya)" title="Chief of the General Staff (Kenya)">
       Chief of the General Staff
      </a>
      General
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Kibwana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joseph Kibwana (page does not exist)">
       Joseph Kibwana
      </a>
      was asked to investigate the scandal in person and present his findings to the Office of the President. The report was to detail the value of the ten vehicles, who got them, and why they were disposed of.
     </p>
     <p>
      Military sources at the time said that the orders were issued by National Security minister
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Murungaru" title="Chris Murungaru">
       Chris Murungaru
      </a>
      during a meeting with General Kibwana and other top generals at the Department of Defence headquarters in Nairobi. The issue of Land Rovers cropped up when Murungaru made his first familiarisation tour of the DoD, a month after Narc came to power.
     </p>
     <p>
      Murungaru, who as security minister was responsible for the military, reportedly expressed shock that a
      <a href="/wiki/Cartel" title="Cartel">
       cartel
      </a>
      of high-ranking officers could have been involved in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subversive" title="Subversive">
       subversive
      </a>
      activities by diverting the Land Rovers to Mungiki, as detailed in a
      <a href="/wiki/Daily_Nation" title="Daily Nation">
       Daily Nation
      </a>
      report on the scandal. Senior DoD officials involved in the cartel were said to have held secret talks shortly before Dr. Murungaru arrived to plan their next course of action. The report and its findings have never been made public.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Department of Defence has since been converted into the Ministry of Defense with hon
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yusuf_Haji" title="Yusuf Haji">
       Yusuf Haji
      </a>
      at the helm.
     </p>
