     <p>
      <b>
       Lww Eaglets
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
       Polish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">
       <i>
        <b>
         Orlta Lwowskie
        </b>
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a term of affection applied to the
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Polish
      </a>
      teenagers who defended the city of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lw%C3%B3w" title="Lww">
       Lww
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
       Ukrainian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">
       L'viv
      </span>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Eastern_Europe)" title="Galicia (Eastern Europe)">
       Eastern Galicia
      </a>
      , during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Ukrainian_War" title="Polish-Ukrainian War">
       Polish-Ukrainian War
      </a>
      (19181919).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lw%C3%B3w_Eaglets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The city now known as the Ukrainian
      <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">
       Lviv
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
       Polish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">
       <i>
        Lww
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was before the
      <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary#Dissolution" title="Austria-Hungary">
       breakdown of the Austro-Hungarian empire
      </a>
      known as Lemberg, the capital of one of the Habsburg dominions, namely the
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia_and_Lodomeria" title="Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria">
       Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">
       Poles
      </a>
      were the prevailing ethnic group in the province overall, but in the eastern Galician territories
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">
       Ukrainians
      </a>
      were a majority (65%), and Poles a significant minority (22%) dominating the cities along with
      <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
       Jews
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Lemberg, according to the Austrian census of 1910, 51% of the city's population were
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">
       Roman Catholics
      </a>
      , 28% Jews, and 19%
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church">
       Ukrainian Greek Catholics
      </a>
      . 86% of the city's population spoke Polish and 11% Ukrainian.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the final days of the collapsing Habsburg empire, on November 1, 1918, Ukrainian soldiers from Austrian army units occupied Lemberg's public buildings and military depots, raised Ukrainian flags throughout the city and proclaimed the birth of a new
      <a href="/wiki/West_Ukrainian_People%27s_Republic" title="West Ukrainian People's Republic">
       Ukrainian state
      </a>
      . While the Ukrainian residents enthusiastically supported the proclamation, the city's significant Jewish minority remained mostly neutral towards it and the Polish residents, the majority of the city's inhabitants, were shocked to find themselves in a Ukrainian state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Subtelny367_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Subtelny367-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reacting to this military revolution, Poles rose up throughout the city. Polish forces, initially numbering only about 200, organized a small pocket of resistance in a school at the western outskirts, where a group of veterans of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_Military_Organization" title="Polish Military Organization">
       Polish Military Organization
      </a>
      put up a fight armed with 64 outdated rifles. After initial clashes, the defenders were joined by hundreds of volunteers, mostly boy scouts, students and youngsters. More than 1000 people joined the Polish ranks in the first day of the fighting.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lw.C3.B3w_Eaglets">
       The
       <i>
        Lww Eaglets
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lw%C3%B3w_Eaglets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Lww Eaglets">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Among these were many young volunteers (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Antoni_Petrykiewicz" title="Antoni Petrykiewicz">
       Antoni Petrykiewicz
      </a>
      ), who became known as the
      <i>
       Lww Eaglets
      </i>
      . Initially this term was confined to those who had participated in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lw%C3%B3w_(1918)" title="Battle of Lww (1918)">
       fights
      </a>
      within the city between November 1 and November 22 1918, and the following siege by the Ukrainian army between November 23 1918 and May 22 1919. With time, however, the term's application was broadened, and it is now used for all the young soldiers who fought in the area of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Galicia" title="Eastern Galicia">
       Eastern Galicia
      </a>
      for the Polish cause in the Polish-Ukrainian War and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Bolshevik_War" title="Polish-Bolshevik War">
       Polish-Bolshevik War
      </a>
      . In addition to the young Polish nationals of Lviv, those fighting in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Przemy%C5%9Bl_(1918)" title="Battle of Przemyl (1918)">
       Polish-Ukrainian battle for Przemyl
      </a>
      are also frequently referred to as
      <i>
       Przemyskie Orlta
      </i>
      ('"Przemyl Eaglets").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cemetery_of_the_Lw.C3.B3w_Eaglets">
       Cemetery of the Lww Eaglets
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lw%C3%B3w_Eaglets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cemetery of the Lww Eaglets">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG/220px-Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG/330px-Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG/440px-Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luetzenhofer_Friedhof_028.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Cemetery_of_the_Defenders_of_Lw%C3%B3w" title="Cemetery of the Defenders of Lww">
         Cemetery of the Defenders of Lww
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After the Polish-Ukrainian conflict, the
      <i>
       Lww Eaglets
      </i>
      were interred at the
      <a href="/wiki/Cemetery_of_the_Defenders_of_Lw%C3%B3w" title="Cemetery of the Defenders of Lww">
       Cemetery of the Defenders of Lww
      </a>
      (Polish popular name:
      <i>
       Cmentarz Orlt Lwowskich
      </i>
      ), part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Lychakiv_Cemetery" title="Lychakiv Cemetery">
       Lychakiv Cemetery
      </a>
      . The Cemetery of the Defenders held the remains of both teenaged and adult soldiers, including foreign volunteers from
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      . The Cemetery of the Defenders of Lww was designed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolf_Indruch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rudolf Indruch (page does not exist)">
       Rudolf Indruch
      </a>
      , a student at the Lviv Institute of Architecture, himself an Eaglet. Among the most notable Eaglets to be buried there was 14-year-old
      <a href="/wiki/Jerzy_Bitschan" title="Jerzy Bitschan">
       Jerzy Bitschan
      </a>
      , the youngest of the city's defenders, whose name became an icon of the Polish
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbellum" title="Interbellum">
       interbellum
      </a>
      . Also resting in the Eaglet's pantheon is six-year-old
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oswald_Anissimo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oswald Anissimo (page does not exist)">
       Oswald Anissimo
      </a>
      who was executed together with his father Micha by the Ukrainian soldiers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the annexation of Eastern Galicia with the city of Lww by the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      in World war II (see:
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Poland" title="Soviet invasion of Poland">
       Soviet invasion of Poland
      </a>
      ) and the following
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish_population_transfers_(1944%E2%80%931946)" title="Polish population transfers (19441946)">
       expulsion of the Polish population
      </a>
      from the city, the graves were destroyed in 1971, and the Cemetery of the Defenders of Lww was turned into a municipal waste dump and then into truck depot. After the
      <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">
       dissolution of the Soviet Union
      </a>
      and the formation of an independent
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      , work began on the restoration of the "Eaglets' Cemetery", although slowed by opposition of local nationalists. Following Polish support for Ukraine's
      <a href="/wiki/Orange_Revolution" title="Orange Revolution">
       Orange Revolution
      </a>
      (2004), the opposition declined and the Cemetery was officially reopened in a Polish-Ukrainian ceremony on June 24, 2005. The last surviving Lww Eaglet, Major
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aleksander_Sa%C5%82acki&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aleksander Saacki (page does not exist)">
       Aleksander Saacki
      </a>
      (born 12 May 1904), died in
      <a href="/wiki/Tychy" title="Tychy">
       Tychy
      </a>
      , on April 5, 2008.
     </p>
