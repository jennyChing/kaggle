                 <h1>
                  <a class="link hubspot-editable" data-hubspot-form-id="name" data-hubspot-name="Blog Title" href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/elite-data-analysis-for-security-iv-statistical-modeling" id="hubspot-name">
                   <span class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text" id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" style="">
                    Elite Data Analysis For Security IV: Statistical Modeling
                   </span>
                  </a>
                 </h1>
                 <div class="graydivider" style="margin: 10px 0 15px 0;">
                 </div>
                 <strong>
                  By Todd LaRocca
                 </strong>
                 <p>
                  <span class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text" id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" style="">
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <img align="right" alt="statistical-modeling-methods-for-security-data-analysis.jpg" src="//cdn2.hubspot.net/hub/283820/file-2615489650-jpg/statistical-modeling-methods-for-security-data-analysis.jpg.jpg?t=1436890744533&amp;width=217" style="width: 217px; float: right; margin: 0px 0px 5px 8px;" width="217"/>
                  The difference between mitigating and managing security threats at your federal agency boils down to one major factor: your ability to predict them.
                 </p>
                 <!--more-->
                 <p>
                  Whether youre monitoring potential adversaries or predicting insider threat behaviors, statistical modeling is a critical component to agency security because the more incidents youre able to predict and prevent, the fewer major security incidents youll face.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  In this fourth post of a five-part series, we take a deeper dive into the data analysis tools created from statistical modeling  and how to use those tools to proactively address security threats at your agency.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  In Part I, we discussed
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/elite-data-analysis-for-security-part-i-data-collection-tactics" target="_blank">
                   robust data collection tactics for stronger security decision-making
                  </a>
                  ;in Part II, we examined
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/elite-data-analysis-for-security-ii-data-dashboard-pitfalls" target="_blank">
                   how to avoid common pitfalls when working with data dashboards
                  </a>
                  ;and
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/elite-data-analysis-for-security-iii-emphasizing-data-quality" target="_blank">
                   in Part III, we drilled into the importance of data quality for security decisions.
                  </a>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  If you want to predict and proactively address security threats at your intelligence or defense agency, you must learn to leverage your past data for
                  <a href="https://twitter.com/microslices" target="_blank">
                   future predictive modeling
                  </a>
                  .When youre continuously learning from past security measures, youre able to more effectively prevent incidents from occurring.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Here are five statistical modeling tactics
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/federal-government" target="_blank">
                   your federal agency
                  </a>
                  needs to deploy to improve your predictive organizational security efforts:
                 </p>
                 <h3>
                  <strong>
                   1. Use Only Clean, Formatted Data
                  </strong>
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  In order to leverage the full power of statistical modeling,
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/elite-data-analysis-for-security-iii-emphasizing-data-quality" target="_blank">
                   you need to work with clean data
                  </a>
                  . It should be in a format thats easy for multiple users to work with and doesnt require too many manual processing steps. Unformatted data that requires intensive parsing or formatting is difficult to keep clean, so aim for data that is easily entered or migrated into usable data fields.
                 </p>
                 <h3>
                  <strong>
                   2. Make Your Models Reproducible
                  </strong>
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  Your statistical models should be backed by robust data science, which means any models you create (or
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/7-criteria-to-check-before-you-hire-a-consulting-services-firm" target="_blank">
                   hire others to create for you
                  </a>
                  )should be easily reproducible. A different agency executive should be able to apply your same model to a different data set with the same variables and still produce valid results. Reproducibility isnt just about academic integrity  it ensures your models dont contain any errors with the potential to produce costly false positives.
                 </p>
                 <h3>
                  <strong>
                   3. Conduct Exploratory Data Analysis
                  </strong>
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  Before you create your final statistical model, start with some exploratory data analysis. Compare high-level charts, find correlating variables and search for interesting patterns. If your federal agency is trying to model particular behaviors off of data indicators, take some time upfront to determine which variables and data points model those behaviors. This exploratory analysis helps you (or your professional services firm) create
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/skysolve-data-tools" target="_blank">
                   a more accurate and truly predictive model
                  </a>
                  .
                 </p>
                 <h3>
                  <strong>
                   4. Hire Data Science Experts
                  </strong>
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  While there are
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/bid/383809/5-tips-to-simplify-your-excel-models-for-organizational-data-analysis" target="_blank">
                   some aspects of statistical modeling you can perform yourself,
                  </a>
                  the consequences of creating a poor model for your security operation could be drastic. Robust predictive modeling involves high-level data science and should be completed by professional advisors or contractors who specialize in data analysis for agency security. In addition,
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/insider-threat-assessment" target="_blank">
                   any potential contractor should hold a
                   <span>
                    Top Secret Facility clearance
                   </span>
                  </a>
                  <span>
                   and know how to handle sensitive data.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <h3>
                  <strong>
                   5. Dont Constrict Your Model To Past Events
                  </strong>
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  When youre working to prevent rare security events (such as a security breach or insider threat incident), you might be tempted to build your model to prevent incidents that have happened similarly in the past. For example, if your predictive model is built to prevent the next
                  <a href="http://www.bigskyassociates.com/blog/bid/344664/The-Navy-Yard-Shooting-A-Silver-Lining-In-Lessons-Learned" target="_blank">
                   Navy Yard shooting
                  </a>
                  ,its predictive power for other insider threat incidents could be wildly inaccurate. Your model must take past variables into account but remain flexible enough to predict yet-unknown future circumstances.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Without tapping into the power of statistical modeling, your agencys security efforts are at the mercy of unpredictable threats. However, when you leverage the power of predictive models, you proactively deter threats before they ever become full-scale incidents.
                 </p>
