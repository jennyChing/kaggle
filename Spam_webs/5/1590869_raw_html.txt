          <h1 class="hdr-article">
           Five Factors That Judges Consider in Reviewing Photo Contest Entries
          </h1>
         </div>
         <h3>
         </h3>
         <div class="info">
          04-25-2011
        
        //
        John Nuhn
         </div>
         <div id="content_0_maincontent_0_ctlEmailSharePrint_pnlSharing">
          <div class="top-links clearfix">
           <span class="st_facebook_hcount" displaytext="Facebook" style="position:relative;top:-4px;">
           </span>
           <span class="st_twitter_hcount" displaytext="Tweet" st_via="NWF" style="position:relative;top:-4px;">
           </span>
           <input id="content_0_maincontent_0_ctlEmailSharePrint_btnEmail" name="content_0$maincontent_0$ctlEmailSharePrint$btnEmail" src="../../../../../common/images/main/common/btn-email.png" style="margin-left: 2px;" title="Email this page to your friends" type="image"/>
          </div>
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           As one of the judges
          </strong>
          of the
          <a href="http://www.nwf.org/photocontest?s_src=20110401_Web_NWM_Photo_Zone_Channel" title="National Wildlife Photo Contest">
           <em>
            National Wildlife
           </em>
           Photo Contest
          </a>
          , Im often asked what makes a winning photograph. The contest started in 1971, and from its beginning, judges have looked at each image with five criteria in mind. So whether you are deciding which images to enter or just looking at what others have entered to vote for the
          <a href="http://www.nwf.org/photocontest?s_src=20110401_Web_NWM_Photo_Zone_Channel" title="People's Choice Award">
           Peoples Choice Award
          </a>
          , you will find helpful tips in these five factors that the judges look for in evaluating entries:
         </p>
         <h4>
          1) Originality
         </h4>
         <p>
          We've seen many cedar waxwing images but this second place winning photo was very original. The photographers companion was looking in a field guide at differences between a cedar waxwing and a bohemian waxwing when the bird got curious, too. You never know when a photo opportunity may arise, so have your camera ready.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="Cedar waxwing and guide book" height="382" src="/~/media/Content/National Wildlife Magazine Layouts/2011/cedar-waxwing-guide-book.ashx" width="570"/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h4>
          2) Technical Excellence
         </h4>
         <p>
          Remember when we were told we shouldnt photograph toward the sun? But this first place winner of a mother and child dancing on the beach shows you can do this quite effectively if you select the right camera settings. That means the proper exposure, shutter speed, white balance and depth of field. And be aware of lens flare!
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="Connecting people to nature" height="374" src="/~/media/Content/National Wildlife Magazine Layouts/2011/Connecting-People-hector-astoga-570x374.ashx" width="570"/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h4>
          3) Composition
         </h4>
         <p>
          This entry has great balance, and the placement and strong color of the canoe emphasize the whiteness of the scene. Its always best to make your composition when you shoot, but sometimes you might find a better composition by cropping afterward. Dont be afraid to crop if it will help composition.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="Canoe composition" height="376" src="/~/media/Content/National Wildlife Magazine Layouts/2011/cannoe-sharnon-mamoser-570x370.ashx" width="570"/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h4>
          4) Artistic Merit
         </h4>
         <p>
          All the elements came together for this photographer?the large elk, the double rainbow, the sun highlighting the bands of rain. The photographer didnt let weather stop her from shooting and she was rewarded with this beautiful wildlife landscape. Sunny blue skies have their place, but what we might consider bad weather can add a lot of drama to a photograph.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="Rainbow and elk" height="338" src="/~/media/Content/National Wildlife Magazine Layouts/2011/rainbow-elk-lydia-dotto-570x374.ashx" width="570"/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h4>
          5) Overall Impact
         </h4>
         <p>
          This first-place winning image of an indigo bunting is sharp, well-lit, and the bird contrasts well against the palm leaf background. The wingspread also adds implied motion. Good photographs are made, not taken. Work at your subject, be aware of the background, and anticipate behavior.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="Indigo Bunting" height="374" src="/~/media/Content/National Wildlife Magazine Layouts/2011/indigo-bunting-bernard-friegel-570x374.ashx" width="570"/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          So do what the judges do and keep these factors in mind as you select what photos to enter or what photos to vote for.Strive to select winning images!
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
