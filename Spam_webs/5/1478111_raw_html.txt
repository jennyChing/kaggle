        <p>
         <strong>
          
          <a href="http://news.co.cr/costa-rica-will-stop-sending-cocaine-to-miami/24277/">
           Costa Rica Will Stop Sending Cocaine to Miami
          </a>
          (Costa Rica Star, Aug 6, 2013):
         </strong>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          According  to an official press release from the Organization of Judicial  Investigations (OIJ in Spanish), Costa Rica will no longer send cocaine  or other controlled substances to the United States, at least for the  time being. The announcement comes in the wake of news reports about  nearly
          <strong>
           <a href="http://news.co.cr/snow-job-u-s-air-force-flies-cocaine-from-costa-rica-to-miami/23936/" title="Snow Job: U.S. Air Force Flies Cocaine from Costa Rica to Miami">
            24 tons of cocaine transported by the U.S. Air Force to Miami
           </a>
          </strong>
          on Saturday, July 27
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          2013.
         </p>
         <p>
          The OIJ further explained that officials in Costa Rica had spoken to  the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) about the
          <span style="color: #ff0000;">
           <strong>
            temporary lack  of an incinerator to destroy seized powder cocaine, marijuana and other  illegal drugs.
           </strong>
          </span>
          Prior to the massive airlift of cocaine to Miami in late  July, the OIJ had managed to destroy almost 23 tons of drugs over the  years at a cement factory in Cartago, but a couple of unfortunate  incidents resulted in the stockpiling of confiscated drugs, and the OIJ  ended up with too much coke.
          <span id="more-76824">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           A Warehouse Full of Yeyo
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Before a Boeing C-17 Globemaster from the U.S. Air Force landed at  the Daniel Oduber Quiros International Airport (LIR) in late July to  transport pallets upon pallets of cocaine to Miami, the OIJ evidence  warehouse in San Joaquin de Flores, province of Heredia, was ready to  burst from all the drugs contained therein -particularly cocaine  hydrochloride in powder form. The only incinerator in Costa Rica to  handle the destruction of tons of drugs, routinely seized by the OIJ,  Fuerza Publica (the national police force), the Border Police, and the  National Coast Guard Service is currently out of commission.
         </p>
         <p>
          At one time, the OIJ entrusted a cement and concrete factory in  Cartago to handle the destruction of seized drugs in its massive  incinerators. In 2009, however, a bag holding 20 kilograms of flake was
          <a href="http://www.nacion.com/sucesos/narcotrafico/Robo-freno-destruccion-fabrica-cemento_0_1357064302.html" target="_blank" title="La Nacion on cocaine theft at incineration facility">
           stolen by employees of the cement factory
          </a>
          during the destruction of 4,300 kilos. The individuals responsible for  the theft were caught, tried and given prison sentences between 6 and 7  years, and the OIJ stopped using that factory altogether.
         </p>
         <p>
          By 2011, OIJ employees complained that they could not even walk  around the warehouse because of all the cocaine stuffed inside. The  cement factory agreed to donate an incinerator the size of a jacuzzi to  the OIJ, and agents got busy destroying up to 300 kilograms per hour. By  February of 2012, however, agents had
          <a href="http://www.nacion.com/sucesos/narcotrafico/Falla-incinerador-OIJ-EE-UU_0_1357064300.html" target="_blank" title="La Nacion on broken incinerator">
           burned so much coke
          </a>
          that the incinerator began to malfunction. Fumes were escaping the  incinerators chambers and those involved in the destruction -who  included judges in charge of supervision- were basically getting high,  and thus the OIJ went back to stockpiling seized drugs.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The U.S. Air Force to the Rescue
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          The OIJ has acknowledged that storing that much cocaine in Costa Rica  is an inherent risk, and thus the DEA was called in to help. This is  when things start to get a bit murky. Apparently the OIJ took the matter  to the Judicial Branch and the Ministry of Public Safety (MSP in  Spanish); but, according to online news daily CRHoy, which broke the  story, at least
          <a href="http://www.crhoy.com/en-poder-ejecutivo-afirman-que-decision-para-que-avion-de-armada-estadounidense-entrara-al-pais-fue-del-oij/" target="_blank" title="CRHoy on U.S. Air Force cocaine flight ">
           two magistrates at the Judicial Branch were in the dark
          </a>
          about the U.S. Air Force arriving in Costa Rica to pick up a massive amount of cocaine.
         </p>
         <p>
          One of the magistrates who did not know about the operation, which  was shrouded in secrecy until CRHoys Alvaro Sanchez investigated, is  Carlos Chinchilla, President of the Criminal Affairs Committee of the  Supreme Court in Costa Rica. Magistrate Chinchilla explained that the  OIJ and DEA were supposed to file a request for a legislative permit  that would grant permission for a foreign military aircraft to land in  Costa Rica.
         </p>
         <p>
          The OIJ assured CRHoy that the C-17 Globemaster was not armed and had  legislative permission to enter Costa Ricas airspace, land and fly  away loaded with cocaine with Miami as its ultimate destination. The  problem is that no legislator recalls seeing such request or permission  come through the docket at the National Assembly. When questioned about  the type of aircraft, the OIJ repeated that it was from the U.S. Air  Force with a legislative permit. The Minister of Public Safety, Mario  Zamora, said that he was thankful that the United States arrived to  transport cocaine away from Costa Rica, but he was quick to mention that  the OIJ was responsible for obtaining all permits.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Passing the Buck
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Even the identification of the C-17 from the U.S. Air Force remained a mystery until recently. CRHoy
          <a href="http://www.crhoy.com/aviacion-civil-otorgo-otro-permiso-para-que-aeronave-militar-ingresara-al-pais-a-pesar-de-que-sala-iv-ya-los-habia-reprendido/" target="_blank" title="CRHoy documents on Air Force cocaine flight">
           obtained a series of documents
          </a>
          related to the ongoing investigation, which apparently include a letter signed on July 10
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          by a U.S. Coast Guard Commander named B.J. Ripkey requesting permission  for a military aircraft from the U.S. to land in Costa Rica with 12  individuals aboard. According to that letter, the aircraft would not fly  directly to Miami after takeoff: it would first stop in Nicaragua and  later in Honduras while carrying all that cocaine.
         </p>
         <p>
          The letter was addressed to the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the MSP  and the Director of Air Surveillance -a special police unit in Costa  Rica that is known for their presence at our airports. According to a  2011 Coast Guard document, there is a Commander Bradley Ripkey assigned  to
          <a href="http://www.uscg.mil/announcements/ALCGPSC/014-11_psc.txt" target="_blank" title="USCG announcement of CDR Ripkey">
           DDE-COSTA RICA SEC ASST
          </a>
          .  Somehow his request ended up in the hands of Gerardo Masis Delgado, who  is apparently in charge of Aeronautical Operations of Costa Ricas  Civil Aviation Directorate. Mr. Masis issued
          <a href="http://www.crhoy.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/aviaci%C3%B3n-civil.jpg" target="_blank" title="CRHoy letter from Civil Aviation in CR">
           an interesting letter
          </a>
          that authorizes:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            aircraft type C-17 with registration 00534, or  alternatively 10186, 66165, 66167, 66168, 77169, 77170, 77173, 77174,  77175, 77176, or 7717
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          to enter Costa Ricas airspace and land between the 26
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          and 28
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          of July. Mr. Masis also ordered reminded that no airport costs should  be assessed to any of the aircraft above. It is interesting to note that  the number 77178 corresponds to the 
          <a href="http://www.512aw.afrc.af.mil/news/story.asp?id=123121400" target="_blank" title="Spirit of Delaware">
           Spirit of Delaware
          </a>
          ,  which is the C-17 Globemaster III out of Dover Air Force Base pictured  in this article, and that number happens to have been underlined with an  ink pen on the letter above.
         </p>
         <p>
          What is also interesting about the above is that Civil Aviation has  been admonished by the Constitutional Chamber Court (Sala Cuarta), the  highest court in Costa Rica, of having
          <a href="http://news.co.cr/president-obamas-helicopters-disturbed-the-peace-in-costa-rica/22816/" title="President Obamas Helicopters Disturbed The Peace in Costa Rica">
           illegally approved the arrival of UH-60 Blackhawk helicopters
          </a>
          prior to President Barack Obamas historic visit to our country earlier  this year. Civil Aviation seems to the the go-to guy, or maybe the fall  guy, in these matters; but, many legislators in Costa Rica are not  amused. The Constitution clearly requires legislative permission for  foreign military forces to enter Costa Rica, and the U.S. has a history  of
          <a href="http://news.co.cr/costa-rica-snubs-u-s-coast-guard-but-welcomes-colombian-navy-heres-why/22643/" title="Costa Rica Snubs U.S. Coast Guard But Welcomes Colombian Navy. Heres Why:">
           being snubbed in this regard
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           So What Happened to All That Cocaine?
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          The
          <a href="http://www.poder-judicial.go.cr/oij/index.php?option=com_content&amp;view=article&amp;id=1752:organismo-de-investigacion-judicial-oij-envia-droga-a-estados-unidos-de-america-para-su-destruccion&amp;catid=36:modulo-noticias&amp;Itemid=247" target="_blank" title="Official OIJ press release">
           OIJs official press release
          </a>
          assures that the controversial payload left Costa Rica on Saturday July 27
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          ,  at 2:00 in the afternoon. The C-17 presumably stopped in Nicaragua and  Honduras before landing in Miami, this is in accordance to the letter  signed by USCG Commander B.J. Ripkey. A judge from Costa Rica supposedly  flew with all the blow and met with the Consul in Miami, who was the  last link in the chain of command to certify the arrival and destruction  of the drugs.
         </p>
         <p>
          The Consulate usually answers to the Ministry of Foreign Relations,  but in this case that entity has not published anything on the matter.  The OIJ, however, assures that the destruction process started at 6:00  am on Monday, July 29
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          , and that it lasted five hours until  the last bag was incinerated. The OIJ also mentioned that U.S. and Costa  Rican officials were present during the process, although there are no  local media reports to corroborate.
         </p>
         <p>
          As to the matter of legislative permit for this operation, some  legislators interviewed by CRHoy indicated that everything was fine as  long as the military aircraft did not land with bellicose intent. The  OIJ mentioned that the C-17 was not armed, but a legal expert  interviewed by CRHoy mentioned:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            Do you really think that the U.S. Air Force would  risk picking 24 tons of cocaine in Costa Rica and then land in Nicaragua  and Honduras without some sort of armed escort or weaponry to defend  the aircraft and crew?
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The Need for Legislative Permits
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          News about this incident have prompted debate on the requirement of  legislative permits for U.S. military forces to enter Costa Rica. Many  people think that such permits add a layer of bureaucracy to worthy  causes such as drug interdiction. No one is questioning the value of the  assistance by the U.S. Air Force and other authorities from that  country in taking away a prodigious amount of drugs that our country  could not destroy; the problem is one of transparency and sovereignty.
         </p>
         <p>
          The original story by Alvaro Sanchez of CRHoy and the English  language version by The Costa Rica Star were picked up by various  international media outlets, and many of the comments left by readers  indicate a significant level of skepticism about the operation. Allowing  foreign military forces to enter a country to transport narcotics in  bulk calls for a certain amount of transparency that can be achieved  with legislative permits.
         </p>
         <p>
          History has shown that nothing good happens when you the U.S.  government interests transport controlled substances from Central  America. When such operations take place in a shadowy manner, you end up  with
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_North" target="_blank" title="Oliver North">
           Colonel Oliver North
          </a>
          of the U.S. Marine Corps, the late American pilot
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Seal" target="_blank" title="Barry Seal">
           Barry Seal
          </a>
          smuggling for the CIA, shady companies such as
          <a href="http://www.darkpolitricks.com/cia-involvement-in-drug-smuggling-part-4/" target="_blank" title="Costa Rica's involvement in CIA drug smuggling">
           DIASCA and Frigorificos de Puntarenas
          </a>
          , and other kinds of
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CIA_and_Contras_cocaine_trafficking_in_the_US" target="_blank" title="CIA Contra smuggling">
           malfeasance
          </a>
          . The people of Costa Rica have a right to know about these things; this can be accomplished with legislative permits.
         </p>
         <p>
          The short answer as to why that U.S. Air Force C-17 should have had  permission to land in Costa Rica is: Because our Constitution requires  it. The rationale is simple: As a country who abolished her military  more than six decades ago, Costa Rica cant afford to have foreign  military or forces entering the country at their pleasure or with a  permit issued by a mid-level government official. We have enough  problems with
          <a href="http://news.co.cr/a-history-of-invasions-costa-rica-and-nicaragua/423/" title="A History of Invasions: Costa Rica and Nicaragua">
           Nicaraguan Army invasions
          </a>
          and
          <a href="http://news.co.cr/mysterious-paramilitary-group-invades-costa-rica/23284/" title="Mysterious Paramilitary Group Invades Costa Rica">
           paramilitaries entering the countr
          </a>
          y. As a peace-loving representative democracy, Costa Rica must discuss and decide these matters at the legislative level.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
