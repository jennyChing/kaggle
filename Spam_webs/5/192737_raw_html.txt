           Fiberglass Animal Farm

          <h4 class="graf--h4 graf--last" data-align="center" id="2c46" name="2c46">
           A visit to the Wisconsin factory where advertising sculptures are bred and pastured

          <figure class="graf--figure" id="8c66" name="8c66">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 400px; max-height: 100px;">

            <a class="graf-imageAnchor" data-href="http://www.bmwusa.com/standard/content/vehicles/2015/4/default.aspx?source=bmw034scmedium&amp;origin=bmw034scmedium" href="http://www.bmwusa.com/standard/content/vehicles/2015/4/default.aspx?source=bmw034scmedium&amp;origin=bmw034scmedium">
             <img class="graf-image" data-height="200" data-image-id="1*GGzZc4sH8SkfnUsIKzojHw@2x.png" data-width="800" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*GGzZc4sH8SkfnUsIKzojHw@2x.png"/>

          <blockquote class="graf--pullquote pullquote" id="5fa9" name="5fa9">
           Its a crisp autumn day in the small town of Sparta, Wisconsin, and Darren Schauf is showing off the very unusual tools of his trade: about 600 large fiberglass molds strewn around a five-acre field.
          </blockquote>
          <p class="graf--p" id="a3a8" name="a3a8">
           Most of the molds are oversized versions of recognizable shapesa horse here, a beer bottle there, assorted arms, legs, and faces all over the place. Theyre primarily a drab yellowish brown, and most are lying on their sides, making them look dead or discarded. I make a note: Looks like the molds are moldering.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="31f5" name="31f5">
           But where others might see a big field of junk or scrap, Schauf sees the lifeblood of his business. This is our agriculture section, he says proudly. Pigs, horses, cows. Theres Babe the Blue Ox, who goes with Paul Bunyan. Oh, look, youre standing on Paul Bunyans ax!
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure graf--layoutOutsetLeft" id="b6be" name="b6be">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 525px; max-height: 787px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 150%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*uRf2zonD4xdYpl0jri6_uw.jpeg" data-height="1600" data-image-id="1*uRf2zonD4xdYpl0jri6_uw.jpeg" data-width="1067" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/600/1*uRf2zonD4xdYpl0jri6_uw.jpeg"/>
           </div>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c43a" name="c43a">
           This is FAST Corp.the acronym stands for Fiberglass Animals, Shapes, and Trademarksone of the countrys most intriguing and entertaining niche manufacturers. FAST uses these molds to cast large fiberglass statues that have become icons of roadside Americana. If youve ever seen a big steer
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://roadsidewonders.net/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/kellysrestaurant.jpg" href="http://roadsidewonders.net/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/kellysrestaurant.jpg" rel="nofollow">
            perched atop a steakhouse
           </a>
           , a giant soft-serve cone
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="https://c1.staticflickr.com/9/8048/8136951047_1e526d08f1_z.jpg" href="https://c1.staticflickr.com/9/8048/8136951047_1e526d08f1_z.jpg" rel="nofollow">
            in front of an ice cream stand
           </a>
           , or a Bob's Big Boy statue, FAST probably made it. The company also makes
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://farm2.staticflickr.com/1194/640538458_9a214b6e17.jpg" href="http://farm2.staticflickr.com/1194/640538458_9a214b6e17.jpg" rel="nofollow">
            slides for water parks
           </a>
           (a sub-niche that constitutes about 40 percent of FASTs business) and occasionally collaborates with artists who need a specialized vendor to execute their large-scale projects, like
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-gcAN4Lrwn3A/T88_gW69iBI/AAAAAAAAt3k/xCLstsEVlng/s1600/LI-sculp-tony-006b.jpg" href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-gcAN4Lrwn3A/T88_gW69iBI/AAAAAAAAt3k/xCLstsEVlng/s1600/LI-sculp-tony-006b.jpg" rel="nofollow">
            the 30-foot eyeball
           </a>
           FAST made in 2007 for the artist Tony Tasset.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="f641" name="f641">
           FAST, which currently has two dozen employees, produces close to 1,000 statues a year and typically has about 50 pieces in various stages of production at any given moment. Turnaround time ranges from six to ten weeks. About 10 percent of the companys projects are custom jobs; the rest are stock designs from
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.fastkorp.com/Catalog-test" href="http://www.fastkorp.com/Catalog-test" rel="nofollow">
            the FAST Corp. catalog
           </a>
           , which is why all those molds are kept out in the field. If someone orders, say, a giant ear of corn, the appropriate mold will be brought in from the field and the piece will be cast in fiberglass. The molds are too big to put in storage, so they just sit there out in the open, which works out fine. Its not as though anyones going to sneak off with a 15-foot root beer mug.

          <p class="graf--p" id="9c2d" name="9c2d">
           Weve all seen giant statues at some pointthe worlds largest this, a colossal that. What exactly is their appeal? Part of it, to be sure, is the simple truism that large things are impressive. But theres more to it than that. Its one thing to be wowed by a very large mountain; its another to encounter a very large cow, or banana, or bottle of Tabasco sauce. We cant change our own size, but we can change the size of these other things and thereby alter ourselves relative to them. Theres something in our brains that finds pop-cultural whimsy and delight in those altered spatial relationships (it works with miniatures, too). That whimsy is at the core of FASTs appeal.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="967e" name="967e">
           But while FASTs products are large and attention-getting, the company itself is one of those enterprises that hover just under the radar of peoples consciousness. You always understood on an intuitive level that
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            someone
           </em>
           was making those giant statures, but you probably never thought about who those someones might be, and you almost certainly didnt imagine their factory being in a small town in rural Wisconsin, surrounded by rolling farmland.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="6dc6" name="6dc6">
           FAST is the third incarnation of a company that began in the 1950s under the name Sculptured Advertising. A few statues from this era are still in use (including a steakhouse steer that was recently returned to FAST for repairs after having been hit by a car). The company then became known as Creative Display, one of whose employees, a designer and artist named Jerry Vettrus, took over the operation and renamed it FAST in 1983. Many of the molds out in the field are his designs.
          </p>

           Most FAST products are much simpler than the dog. Assuming the mold for a project doesnt already exist out in the field, the process begins with the creation of a plug or model. Schauf shows me one thats currently being made for a two-scoop ice cream cone. The only other double-scoop cone we have is the one we use
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://static.panoramio.com/photos/large/50127253.jpg" href="http://static.panoramio.com/photos/large/50127253.jpg" rel="nofollow">
            for a chain called Andy's Frozen Custard
           </a>
           , he explains. Andy doesnt technically have an exclusive on that design, but hes a good customer, so Im building a new double-scoop cone that we can market to other customers, and I wont sell Andys to anyone else.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="fe35" name="fe35">
           The new ice cream cone plug, like all of FASTs plugs, is made from expandable urethane foam. Its great stuff, says Schauf. You spray it on and it expands to, like, 10 times its size. Then you cut it with a filet knife. If you cut off too much, you just spray on some more and do it over. We just use a photo for reference until it looks right. Once a plug is finished, its painted to make it slick. Then its coated with fiberglass, which is removed in several pieces, creating the mold. One challenge when sculpting the plug is to minimize undercuts or recessed areas, which will make the mold harder to remove from the plug. The more undercuts there are, the more pieces the mold will end up being. In the case of the two-scoop cone, Schauf says the resulting mold will probably end up being 15 to 20 separate pieces.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c59a" name="c59a">
           Once the mold is made, the plug is discarded (theyre not very recyclable, unfortunately, so they end up in the landfill) and the statue is cast and painted. Then the mold joins the others out in the field. We dont throw any of them away, says Schauf. Even if I think well probably never use it again, we hold onto it.

FAST has also benefited from the growth of agritourism, where the rising popularity of corn mazes, pick your own pumpkin patches, and so on has led to more orders from farmers looking to spruce up their properties with a giant chicken or
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.fastkorp.com/Food/i-VHRrgLc/0/M/Pumpkin%20Hut%20-%206115-M.jpg" href="http://www.fastkorp.com/Food/i-VHRrgLc/0/M/Pumpkin%20Hut%20-%206115-M.jpg" rel="nofollow">

           Youd think other players would want to get in on the action, but FAST appears to have the category largely to itself. Were not a big company, but were the biggest in the industry, says Schauf. The other people who do what we do, working with fiberglass, are mostly onesie, twosie types in a garage. There are lots of companies now doing 3-D CAD cutting with EPS foam, which theyll hard-coat with sprayed plastic. It looks really nice, but its extremely expensive for large pieces, so they cant really compete with us for the big stuff.
