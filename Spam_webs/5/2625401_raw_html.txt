     <p>
      <b>
       Gris-gris
      </b>
      , also spelled
      <b>
       grigri
      </b>
      , and sometimes also "gregory" or "gerregery",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive.org_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-archive.org-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/West_African_Vodun" title="West African Vodun">
       voodoo
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">
       amulet
      </a>
      originating in
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      which is believed to protect the wearer from evil or brings luck,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Az_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Az-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in some
      <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">
       West African
      </a>
      countries is used as a method of
      <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">
       birth control
      </a>
      . It consists of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mojo_(African_American_culture)" title="Mojo (African American culture)">
       small cloth bag
      </a>
      , usually inscribed with verses from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">
       Qur'an
      </a>
      and containing a ritual number of small objects, worn on the person.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gris-gris_(talisman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although the exact origins of the word are unknown, some historians trace the word back to the African word
      <i>
       juju
      </i>
      meaning
      <a href="/wiki/Fetishism" title="Fetishism">
       fetish
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-book-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An alternative theory is that the word originates with the French
      <i>
       joujou
      </i>
      meaning doll or play-thing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-book-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It's otherwise been attributed in scholarly sources to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandingo" title="Mandingo">
       Mandingo
      </a>
      word meaning "magic."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive.org_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-archive.org-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gris-gris_(talisman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The gris-gris originated in
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dagombha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dagombha (page does not exist)">
       Dagombha
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">
       Ghana
      </a>
      and was associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
       Islamic
      </a>
      traditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Africa-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Originally the gris-gris was adorned with Islamic scripture and was used to ward off evil spirits (evil
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Djinn" title="Djinn">
       djinn
      </a>
      ) or bad luck.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Africa-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Historians of the time noted that they were frequently worn by non-believers and believers alike, and were also found attached to buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africa_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Africa-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The practice of using gris-gris, though originating in Africa, came to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA" title="USA">
       USA
      </a>
      with African slaves and was quickly adopted by practitioners of
      <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">
       voodoo
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slaves_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-slaves-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the practice soon changed, and the gris-gris were thought to bring black magic upon their 'victim'. Slaves would often use the gris-gris against their masters and gris-gris can still be seen adorning the tombs of some slave owners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slaves_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-slaves-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During this period, there were also reports of slaves cutting, drowning or otherwise manipulating the gris-gris of others in order to cause harm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although in
      <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
       Haiti
      </a>
      gris-gris are thought to be a good amulet and are used as part of a widely practised religion, in the Cajun communities of Louisiana, gris-gris are thought to be a symbol of black magic and ill-fortune.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In spite of the negative connotations of gris-gris, so called
      <i>
       Gris-Gris doctors
      </i>
      have operated in the Creole communities of Louisiana for some centuries and are looked upon favourably by the community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 1800s, gris-gris was used interchangeably in Louisiana to mean both
      <i>
       bewitch
      </i>
      and in reference to the traditional amulet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gris-gris are also used in Neo-
      <a href="/wiki/Hoodoo_(folk_magic)" title="Hoodoo (folk magic)">
       Hoodoo
      </a>
      which has its origins in Voodoo. In this context, a gris-gris is meant to represent the self.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_use">
       Contemporary use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gris-gris_(talisman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contemporary use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to a 1982 survey, gris-gris were one of the top three methods of contraception known to women in
      <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">
       Senegal
      </a>
      . All three were traditional methods ("abstinence, roots and herbs, and charms ('gris-gris')"). Over 60% of women reported having knowledge of such methods; modern means of contraception were not well known, with the
      <a href="/wiki/Combined_oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Combined oral contraceptive pill">
       pill
      </a>
      the best-known of those, a little over 40% of women reporting knowledge of it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gris-gris are worn by a wide strata of society by everyone "from wrestlers to soldiers to housewives, and can feature anything from monkey to snake to mouse."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
