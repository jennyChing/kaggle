     <p>
      <b>
       Babesiosis
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">
       malaria-like
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_disease" title="Parasitic disease">
       parasitic disease
      </a>
      caused by infection with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Babesia" title="Babesia">
        Babesia
       </a>
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">
       genus
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">
       protozoal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Piroplasmida" title="Piroplasmida">
       piroplasms
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Human babesiosis is an uncommon but emerging disease in the Northeastern and Midwestern United States and parts of Europe, and sporadic throughout the rest of the world. Ticks transmit the human form of babesiosis, so it often presents with other tick-borne illnesses such as
      <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">
       Lyme disease
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After
      <a href="/wiki/Trypanosoma" title="Trypanosoma">
       trypanosomes
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      is thought to be the second-most common blood
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">
       parasite
      </a>
      of mammals, and they can have a major impact on health of domestic animals in areas without severe winters. In cattle, a major host, the disease is known as
      <b>
       Texas cattle fever
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       redwater
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       piroplasmosis
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunfeld_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hunfeld-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
       Classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      species are in the
      <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">
       phylum
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Apicomplexa" title="Apicomplexa">
       Apicomplexa
      </a>
      , which also has the protozoan parasites that cause
      <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">
       malaria
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Toxoplasmosis" title="Toxoplasmosis">
       toxoplasmosis
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidiosis" title="Cryptosporidiosis">
       cryptosporidiosis
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Four clades of
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      species infect humans. The main species in each clade are:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <i>
        B. microti
       </i>
       (&lt;3m)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        B. duncani
       </i>
       , related to babesia of dogs
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        B. divergens
       </i>
       (cattle parasite seen mostly in Europe) and
       <i>
        B. venatorum
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Roe_deer" title="Roe deer">
        roe deer
       </a>
       parasite, formerly called EU1), most closely related to the large babesia clade
      </li>
      <li>
       Large
       <i>
        Babesia
       </i>
       (&gt;3m) mostly infects
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ungulates" title="Ungulates">
        ungulates
       </a>
       , but also includes K01 strain (an isolated case observed in South Korea, see
       <a href="#Isolated_Cases">
        isolated cases
       </a>
       )
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">
       Signs and symptoms
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Half of all children and a quarter of previously healthy adults are asymptomatic with
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      infection. For those who develop symptoms, they are similar to malaria, because both cause fever and
      <a href="/wiki/Hemolytic_anemia" title="Hemolytic anemia">
       hemolytic anemia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      People with symptoms usually become ill 1 to 4 weeks after the bite, or 1 to 9 weeks after transfusion of contaminated blood products. A person infected with babesiosis gradually develops
      <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">
       malaise
      </a>
      and fatigue, followed by a fever. Hemolytic anemia, in which red blood cells are destroyed and removed, also develops. Chills, sweats, and
      <a href="/wiki/Thrombocytopenia" title="Thrombocytopenia">
       thrombocytopenia
      </a>
      (a low number of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platelets" title="Platelets">
       platelets
      </a>
      in the blood) are also common symptoms.
     </p>
     <p>
      Less common symptoms and physical exam findings of mild-to-moderate babesiosis:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Headache
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Myalgia" title="Myalgia">
        Muscle pain
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_(symptom)" title="Anorexia (symptom)">
        Anorexia
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Nonproductive cough (mucus is not coughed up)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthralgias" title="Arthralgias">
        Arthralgias
       </a>
       (noninflammatory joint pain, unlike arthritis, which is inflammatory)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">
        Nausea
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">
        Vomiting
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Sore_throat" title="Sore throat">
        Sore throat
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Abdominal pain
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunctival_injection" title="Conjunctival injection">
        Pink eye
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Photophobia" title="Photophobia">
        Photophobia
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Weight loss
      </li>
      <li>
       Emotional lability
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">
        Depression
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperesthesia" title="Hyperesthesia">
        Hyperesthesia
       </a>
       (more sensitive to stimuli)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Splenomegaly" title="Splenomegaly">
        Enlarged spleen
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Pharyngeal erythema
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Hepatomegaly" title="Hepatomegaly">
        Enlarged liver
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Jaundice" title="Jaundice">
        Jaundice
       </a>
       (yellowing of the skin and of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sclera" title="Sclera">
        sclera
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       Retinopathy with splinter hemorrhages
      </li>
      <li>
       Retinal infarcts
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In more severe cases, symptoms similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">
       malaria
      </a>
      occur, with fevers up to 40.5C (105F), shaking chills, and severe anemia (hemolytic anemia). Organ failure may follow, including adult respiratory distress syndrome. Severe cases occur mostly in people who have had a
      <a href="/wiki/Splenectomy" title="Splenectomy">
       splenectomy
      </a>
      . Severe cases are also more likely to occur in the very young, very old, and persons with
      <a href="/wiki/Immunodeficiency" title="Immunodeficiency">
       immunodeficiency
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">
       HIV/AIDS
      </a>
      patients.
     </p>
     <p>
      A reported increase in human babesiosis diagnoses in the 2000s is thought to be caused by more widespread testing and higher numbers of people with immunodeficiencies coming in contact with ticks, the disease vector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunfeld_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hunfeld-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Little is known about the occurrence of
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      species in malaria-
      <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(epidemiology)" title="Endemic (epidemiology)">
       endemic
      </a>
      areas, where
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      can easily be misdiagnosed as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">
        Plasmodium
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Human patients with babesiosis may exhibit
      <a href="/wiki/Premunity" title="Premunity">
       premunity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShawDay2005_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ShawDay2005-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">
       Pathophysiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="559" data-file-width="564" height="446" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg/450px-Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg/675px-Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg/900px-Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg.png 2x" width="450"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Babesia_life_cycle_human_en.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Babesia
        </i>
        lifecycle
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      parasites reproduce in
      <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">
       red blood cells
      </a>
      , where they can be seen as cross-shaped inclusions (four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merozoite" title="Merozoite">
       merozoites
      </a>
      asexually budding, but attached together forming a structure looking like a "
      <a href="/wiki/Maltese_cross" title="Maltese cross">
       Maltese cross
      </a>
      ")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noskoviak2008_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Noskoviak2008-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and cause hemolytic anemia, quite similar to malaria.
     </p>
     <p>
      Unlike the
      <i>
       Plasmodium
      </i>
      parasites that cause malaria,
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      species lack an exoerythrocytic phase, so the liver is usually not affected.
     </p>
     <p>
      In nonhuman animals,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Babesia_canis" title="Babesia canis">
        Babesia canis
       </a>
       rossi
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Babesia_bigemina" title="Babesia bigemina">
        Babesia bigemina
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Babesia_bovis" title="Babesia bovis">
        Babesia bovis
       </a>
      </i>
      cause particularly severe forms of the disease, including a severe haemolytic anaemia, with positive erythrocyte-in-saline-agglutination test indicating an immune-mediated component to the haemolysis. Common sequelae include haemoglobinuria "red-water", disseminated intravascular coagulation, and "cerebral babesiosis" caused by sludging of erythrocytes in cerebral capillaries.
     </p>
     <p>
      In bovine species, the organism causes hemolytic anemia, so an infected animal shows pale mucous membranes initially. As the levels of
      <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">
       bilirubin
      </a>
      (a byproduct of red blood cell lysis) continue to increase, the visible mucous membranes become yellow in color (icterus) due to the failure of the liver to metabolise the excess bilirubin. Hemoglobinuria is seen due to excretion of red-blood-cell lysis byproducts via the kidneys. Fever of 40.5C (105F) develops due to release of inflammatory byproducts.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">
       Diagnosis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Only specialized laboratories can adequately diagnose
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      infection in humans, and as a result,
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      infections are considered highly under-reported. It develops in patients who live in or travel to an endemic area or receive a contaminated
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">
       blood transfusion
      </a>
      within the preceding 9 weeks, so this aspect of the medical history is vital.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idsa-guidelines_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-idsa-guidelines-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Babesiosis may be suspected when a person with such an exposure history develops persistent fevers and hemolytic anemia. The definitive diagnostic test is the identification of parasites on a
      <a href="/wiki/Giemsa_stain" title="Giemsa stain">
       Giemsa-stained
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_film" title="Blood film">
       thin blood smear
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idsa-guidelines_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-idsa-guidelines-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      So-called "Maltese cross formations" on the blood film are essentially diagnostic of babesiosis, since they are not seen in malaria, the primary differential diagnosis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noskoviak2008_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Noskoviak2008-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Careful examination of multiple
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smear_test" title="Smear test">
       smears
      </a>
      may be necessary, since
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      may infect less than 1% of circulating red blood cells, thus be easily overlooked.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krause_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-krause-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Serology" title="Serology">
       Serologic testing
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">
       antibodies
      </a>
      against
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      (both
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IgG" title="IgG">
       IgG
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IgM" title="IgM">
       IgM
      </a>
      ) can detect low-level infection in cases with a high clinical suspicion, but negative blood film examinations. Serology is also useful for differentiating babesiosis from malaria in cases where people are at risk for both infections. Since detectable antibody responses require about a week after infection to develop, serologic testing may be falsely negative early in the disease course.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">
       polymerase chain reaction
      </a>
      (PCR) test has been developed for the detection of
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      from the peripheral blood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      PCR may be at least as
      <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_and_specificity" title="Sensitivity and specificity">
       sensitive and specific
      </a>
      as blood film examination in diagnosing babesiosis, though it is also significantly more expensive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most often, PCR testing is used in conjunction with blood film examination and possibly
      <a href="/wiki/Serology" title="Serology">
       serologic testing
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idsa-guidelines_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-idsa-guidelines-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other laboratory findings include decreased numbers of red blood cells and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platelets" title="Platelets">
       platelets
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Complete_blood_count" title="Complete blood count">
       complete blood count
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In animals, babesiosis is suspected by observation of clinical signs (haemoglobinuria and anaemia) in animals in endemic areas. Diagnosis is confirmed by observation of merozoites on thin film blood smear examined at maximum magnification under oil using Romonovski stains (methylene blue and eosin). This is a routine part of the veterinary examination of dogs and ruminants in regions where babesiosis is endemic.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Babesia canis
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       B. bigemina
      </i>
      are "large
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      s" that form paired merozoites in the erythrocytes, commonly described as resembling "two pears hanging together", rather than the "Maltese cross" of the "small
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      s". Their merozoites are around twice the size of small
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      s.
     </p>
     <p>
      Cerebral babesiosis is suspected
      <i>
       in vivo
      </i>
      when neurological signs (often severe) are seen in cattle that are positive for
      <i>
       B. bovis
      </i>
      on blood smear, but this has yet to be proven scientifically. Outspoken red discolouration of the grey matter
      <i>
       post mortem
      </i>
      further strengthens suspicion of cerebral babesiosis. Diagnosis is confirmed
      <i>
       post mortem
      </i>
      by observation of
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      -infected erythrocytes sludged in the cerebral cortical capillaries in a brain smear.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
       Treatment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Treatment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In mild-to-moderate babesiosis, the treatment of choice is a combination of
      <a href="/wiki/Atovaquone" title="Atovaquone">
       atovaquone
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Azithromycin" title="Azithromycin">
       azithromycin
      </a>
      . This regimen is preferred to
      <a href="/wiki/Clindamycin" title="Clindamycin">
       clindamycin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">
       quinine
      </a>
      because side effects are fewer. The standard course is 7 to 10 days, but this is extended to at least 6 weeks in people with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relapsing" title="Relapsing">
       relapsing
      </a>
      disease. It is recommended that even mild cases are treated to decrease the chance of inadvertently transmitting the infection by donating blood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In life-threatening cases,
      <a href="/wiki/Exchange_transfusion" title="Exchange transfusion">
       exchange transfusion
      </a>
      is performed. In this procedure, the infected red blood cells are removed and replaced with uninfected ones. Imizol is a drug used for treatment of babesiosis in dogs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Extracts of the poisonous,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulbous" title="Bulbous">
       bulbous
      </a>
      plant
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Boophone_disticha" title="Boophone disticha">
        Boophone disticha
       </a>
      </i>
      are used in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_medicine" title="Folk medicine">
       folk medicine
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
       South Africa
      </a>
      to treat
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equine" title="Equine">
       equine
      </a>
      babesiosis.
      <i>
       B. disticha
      </i>
      is a member of the daffodil family
      <a href="/wiki/Amaryllidaceae" title="Amaryllidaceae">
       Amaryllidaceae
      </a>
      and has also been used in preparations employed as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrow_poisons" title="Arrow poisons">
       arrow poisons
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallucinogens" title="Hallucinogens">
       hallucinogens
      </a>
      , and in
      <a href="/wiki/Embalming" title="Embalming">
       embalming
      </a>
      . The plant is rich in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaloids" title="Alkaloids">
       alkaloids
      </a>
      , some of which display an action similar to that of
      <a href="/wiki/Scopolamine" title="Scopolamine">
       scopolamine
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jad_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jad-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">
       Epidemiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Babesiosis is a
      <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">
       vector-borne
      </a>
      illness usually transmitted by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ixodes_scapularis" title="Ixodes scapularis">
        Ixodes scapularis
       </a>
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">
       ticks
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Babesia microti
      </i>
      uses the same tick vector as
      <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">
       Lyme disease
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ehrlichiosis" title="Ehrlichiosis">
       ehrlichiosis
      </a>
      , and may occur in conjunction with these other diseases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunfeld_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hunfeld-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The organism can also be transmitted by
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">
       blood transfusion
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Ticks_of_domestic_animals" title="Ticks of domestic animals">
       Ticks of domestic animals
      </a>
      , especially
      <i>
       Rhipicephalus (Boophilus) microplus
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       R. (B.) decoloratus
      </i>
      transmit several species of
      <i>
       Babesia
      </i>
      to livestock, causing considerable economic losses to farmers in tropical and subtropical regions.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States, the majority of babesiosis cases are caused by
      <i>
       B. microti
      </i>
      , and occur in the Northeast and northern Midwest from May through October.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Areas with especially high rates include eastern
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">
       Long Island
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_Island" title="Fire Island">
       Fire Island
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nantucket_Island" title="Nantucket Island">
       Nantucket Island
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha's Vineyard">
       Martha's Vineyard
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herwaldt_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Herwaldt-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Europe,
      <i>
       B. divergens
      </i>
      is the primary cause of infectious babesiosis and is transmitted by
      <i>
       I. ricinus
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Babesiosis has emerged in Lower Hudson Valley, New York since 2001.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Australia, babesiosis of types
      <i>
       B. duncani
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       B. microti
      </i>
      has recently been found in symptomatic patients along the eastern coastline of the continent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerging_incidence_of_lyme_borreliosis_babesiosis_bartonellosis_and_granulocytic_ehrlichiosis_in_Australia_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Emerging_incidence_of_lyme_borreliosis_babesiosis_bartonellosis_and_granulocytic_ehrlichiosis_in_Australia-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A similar disease in cattle, commonly known as tick fever, is spread by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Babesia_bovis" title="Babesia bovis">
        Babesia bovis
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Babesia_bigemina" title="Babesia bigemina">
       <i>
        B. bigemina
       </i>
      </a>
      in the introduced cattle tick
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rhipicephalus_microplus" title="Rhipicephalus microplus">
        Rhipicephalus microplus
       </a>
      </i>
      . This disease is found in eastern and northern Australia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIF_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PIF-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The disease is named for the genus of the causative organism,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which was named after the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_people" title="Romanian people">
       Romanian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">
       bacteriologist
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Babe%C5%9F" title="Victor Babe">
       Victor Babe
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1888, Victor Babe identified the microorganisms in red blood cells as the cause of febrile
      <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobinuria" title="Hemoglobinuria">
       hemoglobinuria
      </a>
      in cattle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1893,
      <a href="/wiki/Theobald_Smith" title="Theobald Smith">
       Theobald Smith
      </a>
      and Frederick Kilborne discovered that a tick was the vector for transmission in Texas cattle. The agent was
      <i>
       B. bigemina
      </i>
      . This was the first demonstration that an
      <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">
       arthropod
      </a>
      could act as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disease_vector" title="Disease vector">
       disease vector
      </a>
      to transmit an infectious agent to a vertebrate host.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1957, the first human case was documented in a
      <a href="/wiki/Splenectomy" title="Splenectomy">
       splenectomized
      </a>
      Croatian herdsman.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEJM_Babesiosis_Review-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The agent was
      <i>
       B. divergens
      </i>
      . In 1969, the first case was reported in an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunocompetent" title="Immunocompetent">
       immunocompetent
      </a>
      individual on Nantucket Island. The agent was
      <i>
       B. microti
      </i>
      , and the vector was the tick
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ixodes_scapularis" title="Ixodes scapularis">
        I. scapularis
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">
       Equine
      </a>
      babesiosis is also known as piroplasmosis (from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
       Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       piro
      </i>
      , meaning
      <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">
       pear
      </a>
      +
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      <i>
       plasma
      </i>
      , a thing formed).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lexic_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lexic-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_animals">
       In other animals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Babesiosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In other animals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Veterinary treatment of babesiosis does not normally use antibiotics. In nonhuman animals,
      <a href="/wiki/Diminazen" title="Diminazen">
       diminazen
      </a>
      (Berenil),
      <a href="/wiki/Imidocarb" title="Imidocarb">
       imidocarb
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Trypan_blue" title="Trypan blue">
       trypan blue
      </a>
      would be the drugs of choice for treatment of
      <i>
       B. canis rossi
      </i>
      (dogs in Africa),
      <i>
       B. bovis
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       B. bigemina
      </i>
      (cattle in Southern Africa). In acute cases in cattle,
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">
       blood transfusion
      </a>
      may be carried out.
     </p>
     <p>
      A vaccine is effective against
      <i>
       B. canis canis
      </i>
      (dogs in the Mediterranean region), but is ineffective against
      <i>
       B. c. rossi
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       B. imitans
      </i>
      causes a mild form of the disease that frequently resolves without treatment (dogs in Southeast Asia).
     </p>
