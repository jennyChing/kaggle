          <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
           <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
            tips for growing better tomatoes from seed
           </h1>
           <p>
            <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/tomatoseedlings.jpg">
             <img alt="Cellpack of young tomato seedlings ready for transplant." class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-12812" height="430" src="http://awaytogarden.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/tomatoseedlings.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
            <span class="drop_cap">
             T
            </span>
            HAT OLD, DISCARDED ELECTRIC FAN that isnt strong enough for the hot summers of global warminghey, bring it on. Its perfect for accomplishing one of the tricks to growing better tomato seedlings, which is (after all) the only thing you probably really care about on the run-up to another spring. To hell with winter. Onward!
            <span id="more-3566">
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            Its still too early for sowing tomatoes in my Zone 5B Hudson Valley, New York, area, with April 15 my target date, but I can dream. Those of you in warmer zones can stop dreaming, and do (and if you already sowed, its not too late to start
            <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/why-vegetables-seedlings-stretch-and-get-spindly/" target="_blank">
             petting a.k.a. brushing your plants
            </a>
            to help them grow sturdierreally). My goal is not a tall seedling by transplant time but a stout and sturdy one, about 4 inches high and wide.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/heirloom-tomatoes.jpg">
             <img alt="Homegrown heirloom tomatoes" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-19322" height="446" src="http://awaytogarden.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/heirloom-tomatoes.jpg" width="640"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <h3>
            the top tomato tips:
           </h3>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Work from your final frost date
             </strong>
             to determine when to sow (mines late May-early June).
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/when-to-start-seeds-calculator/" target="_blank">
              Use my seed-starting calculator
             </a>
             to get your start date (and a link to figure out your frost date if you dont know it).
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Count back from 5 or 6 to as much as 8 weeks
             </strong>
             . Everyone has their own beliefs on this. I like a strong little plant grown in a medium-large cell, so 6ish is plenty. My seedlings would need larger quarters (like a 3- or 4-inch pot each) to thrive for 8 weeks indoors; more work, but to my mind not much extra benefit. Again: many experts prefer 8 weeks, and you may, too.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Seeds germinated on
              <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0001WV010/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B0001WV010&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=4T7MKTEUIWE2QETI" target="_blank">
               a heat mat
              </a>
              in flats will get off to a particularly fast start
             </strong>
             . A soil temperature of 70 degrees is essential; higher (like mid-80s) is better in those early days of germination. I did not use a heat mat until recently because I sowed directly in
             <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B007XIVACE/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B007XIVACE&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=KNBE6XAXTL74Q6TR" target="_blank" title="APS seed-starting systems">
              APS cells
             </a>
             (insulated, self-watering foam trays that last many years), not seed flats; lately Ive been doing some of both.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              If youre re-using seedling flats
             </strong>
             or
             <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B007XIVACE/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B007XIVACE&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=TZVKCLMEBMQTF3NR" target="_blank">
              APS
             </a>
             or
             <a class="external" href="http://www.groworganic.com/speedling-transplant-tray-32-cell.html?SID=3deo32s0hir0lmo8i07cvfel63" target="_blank">
              Speedlings
             </a>
             (another polystyrene system) or other containers, first clean everything with a 1:10 bleach:water solution.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Start with fresh seed-starting mix.
             </strong>
             A soilless mix is best. They are usually peat-based, but with consciousness about non-sustainable peat lately, coir is often used. This is not any old potting soil; its specially formulated for seedlings.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             My earliest hero, James Underwood Crockett of the original Victory Garden series, would sprinkle 6-8 seeds per 3-inch seedling pot, then after about two weeks, or when the plants were 1 inches high, transplant them into 6-packs, one plant per cell. Confession: Rather than transplant,
             <strong>
              I usually put two seeds per cell and use a nail scissors to cut out the weakling, skipping the potting up
             </strong>
             . I start with slightly larger cells than a conventional plastic 6-pack, however; others swear by transplanting each baby to its own 3- or even 4-inch pot.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Barely cover seeds
             </strong>
             after sowing with  inch more mix and tamp down, then moisten thoroughly (easiest with a spray bottle). If you are using a bottom-watering system like APS,
             <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000AQNQ4Q/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000AQNQ4Q&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=FJVO2TBWUCMIL22V" target="_blank">
              mist
             </a>
             the tops, then fill the water wells.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Keep the covered trays in a warm spot
             </strong>
             (no light needed or even desired); the heat mat will create all the warmth you need. Again, 70 degrees is the minimum requirement, and the desired temperature throughout their young lives with heat-lovers like tomatoes.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Do not let the seeds dry out
             </strong>
             before they germinate.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Germination will take place
             </strong>
             from a few days (with early varieties) to 10 days or so. A consistently moist environment is essential, but dont let them cook or drown, either; vent the lid or bag so no moisture beads are ever running down the sides.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Once up, its critical to move the babies fast
             </strong>
             off the heat mat and into a high-light situation or they will stretch out and be useless. Forget low light. Even new T-5 grow bulbs (high-output fluorescents) in a reflective hood like
             <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B002JQBQZQ/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B002JQBQZQ&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=KAVSMN3SPT7KQLUC" target="_blank">
              this
             </a>
             or
             <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0001XLSGQ/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B0001XLSGQ&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=TN4YE6KXX2GYFDOF" target="_blank">
              this
             </a>
             provide only a fraction of the light outdoors; read up on
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/why-vegetables-seedlings-stretch-and-get-spindly">
              why seedlings stretch and get spindly.
             </a>
             Set a timer for 12 hours minimum a day (some recommend 14), and keep the lights very close to the plants (a couple of inches maximum, meaning some form of adjustable setup like hooks and chains, to adjust as they grow).
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Feed with half-strength water-soluble organic fertilizer after the first true leaves appear
             </strong>
             . IMPORTANT: If the seed-starting mix you bought includes fertilizer, go easy or skip this step. Overfertilized baby plants can stress out and even die. If using unfertilized mix, plan to feed at half-strength twice during the plants indoor growing period.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             They will grow fast now, and
             <strong>
              this is when you must transplant if you sowed in community pots
             </strong>
             or theyll become overcrowded. Discard the weakest or runt-like seedlingsor snip them out with a nail scissor to prevent disturbing roots of the desired plants.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Dont water with cold water; and never douse.
             </strong>
             Mist, with tepid water (
             <a class="external" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000AQNQ4Q/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000AQNQ4Q&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=awatoga-20&amp;linkId=FJVO2TBWUCMIL22V" target="_blank">
              I use this device
             </a>
             ), or use a device whose spout delivers gently. Ive known people to use everything from a houseplant watering can with a very narrow spout to a discarded soda bottle with one of those pop-up tops. Some seedling systems have wick-like mats beneath the cells that draw up moisture.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Turn on the fan, on low,
             </strong>
             not right next to the plants but so they feel it a little bit. This helps strengthen them, like young trees whose trunks grow stronger in the wind. Air movement may also help prevent deadly fungal diseases like damping off.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Brush the tops of the seedlings daily with your hand
             </strong>
             to provide the
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/why-vegetables-seedlings-stretch-and-get-spindly">
              mechanical conditioning
             </a>
             that creates sturdy, not spindly seedlings. Not just once, but gently for a half-minute or minute. (Yes, go ahead, talk to them while you do it. I probably do without even realizing it.)
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              If the weather is somewhat settled,
             </strong>
             I
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/how-to-start-seed-indoors/">
              carry my flats outdoors
             </a>
             for at least a few hours a day, setting them in a sunny but protected spot.
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/why-vegetables-seedlings-stretch-and-get-spindly">
              The light is just so much stronger
             </a>
             .
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              If any flower buds form on your young plants, pinch them off.
             </strong>
             Dont let the baby stress itself trying to reproduce just yetnot till they are settled in the ground.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              More, more more:
             </strong>
             All my
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/tomato-growing-faqs">
              Tomato-Growing FAQs
             </a>
             are on this page, or read all my
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/tomato-troubles-faqs">
              Tomato-Troubles FAQs
             </a>
             (in case you encounter any pest or disease).
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/when-to-start-seeds-calculator">
              My vegetable-seed calculator tool
             </a>
             can tell you when to start your tomatoes and other crops.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              All sound like too much trouble?
             </strong>
             You can direct-sow tomato seed right in the garden if your season is 4 months or longer between frostsand if you are vigilant about weeding, watering and all the other steps in tomato TLC. And of course you can also just buy seedlings, locally or by mail. I like to make sure Im growing from regionally adapted seed for best results with my tomatoes (and many other crops). I think its worth it, if a great-tasting tomato is what youre after. The best-tasting tomato is
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/grow-best-tasting-tomato/" target="_blank">
              a combination of nature and nurture
             </a>
             , you see.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             One more thing:
             <a href="http://awaytogarden.com/how-to-grow-tomatoes-good-tomato-hygiene/">
              How-tos on tomato staking, pruning, and disease management
             </a>
             outdoors.
            </li>
           </ul>

               I have a small greenhouse and I sell to neighbors and friends, so I start LOTS of tomatoes and peppers. I have 28 varieties of tomatoes this year- mostly heirlooms. I have been using your germination calculator for a couple of years now, and find it spot on.
               <br/>
               I dont start the plants in the greenhouse, which is unheated and is really no more than a big cold frame. Im lucky enough to have a separate outbuilding that has been set up with racks and lights. But anyone could use their basement or unused bedroom.
               <br/>
               Because I have whole flats of seedlings to water, I find it better to dunk the tray and bottom water.  I stand there until I see the tops of the soil just beginning to get moist, pull them out and let them drain on a rack over the sink. This keeps the foliage dry and helps prevent disease. I pet them the whole time, crooning sweet nothings in their ears.
               <br/>
               I use a big plastic under-the-bed Rubbermaid storage container to do this. I water the seedlings the first week or so with CHAMOMILE TEA. It an old gardeners trick to prevent damping off. You can find directions online.
               <br/>
               I also have 2 fans blowing in the seedling room. One on each end of the room so they create turbulence. Lots of air for stimulation to help them grow strong and sturdy.  It also helps to prevents disease.
               <br/>
               My biggest rule for short, sturdy seedlings is to keep the lights tight over them. No more than 1/4 inch over the plants, and I dont raise the lights until a few are touching. Tight lights help to keep them warmer, too.
               <br/>
