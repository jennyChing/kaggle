               <span>
                Dr. Sanjay Gupta
               </span>
               <h1>
                Clinic Pushes COPD Patients to Their Physical Limit
               </h1>
              </header>
              <aside class="disclaimer">
              </aside>
              <h2>
               Exercising to the point of exhaustion will build lung capacity in healthy people, but can it work for people with advanced lung disease?
              </h2>
              <div class="bottom">
               <div class="byline">
                Video featuring Dr. Sanjay Gupta
               </div>
               <div class="reviewedby">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="clear">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div id="video-player-top">
             </div>
             <aside class="aside">
              <img alt="physical therapy for COPD" class="left" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/cs_gupta_videos_pt2_article.jpg"/>
             </aside>
             <p>
              At 80 years old, after
              <a href="/health-report/healthier-living-with-copd/sg-copd-smoking.aspx">
               decades of heavy smoking
              </a>
              , Mickey
Perlmuth has chronic pulmonary obstructive disease (COPD), heart disease, and
gastric reflux. But on the treadmill, he works as hard as an athlete one
quarter his age.
             </p>
             <p>
              His doctor has enrolled him in a physical therapy program
with a unique philosophy. Designed by physical therapist Noah Greenspan, the
              <a href="http://pulmonarywellness.com/" rel="nofollow" target="new">
               Pulmonary Wellness and Rehabilitation
              </a>
              program in New York pushes these fragile, elderly patients to their physical
limit.
             </p>
             <p>
              I believe that the way you do change lung function is by
getting people exercising very vigorously, pumping the lungs very vigorously,
Greenspan says. By pushing people to their limits our data shows that you can
improve lung function.
             </p>
             <p>
              In healthy people, vigorous exercise can expand lung
capacity, allowing a person to take in more air when at rest. Greenspan
believes that works for COPD patients as well.
             </p>
             <p>
              Most programs would say, OK, were going to exercise you at
65%. We try to get people to 100%. In his view, other programs treat COPD
patients too gently.  COPD is a big
disease, its a killer, he says. That means if you do nothing, yes, you are
going to get worse.
             </p>
             <p>
              Because these patients are being pushed hard, they are
monitored carefully. Each is under the watchful eye of an individual therapist.
The average age here is 80, and we dont like surprises, says Greenspan.
             </p>
             <p>
              Pulmonologists agree that physical exercise is essential in
halting the progress of
              <a href="/health-report/healthier-living-with-copd.aspx">
               COPD
              </a>
              . Not all agree with Greenspan, however, that
vigorous exercise can restore lost lung function.
             </p>
             <p>
              But Perlmuth says hes seen improvement. Before he started
the program, he got winded walking just a block. Now he can walk a mile.
             </p>
