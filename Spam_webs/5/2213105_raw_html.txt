     <p>
      <b>
       Marcel Duchamp
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       French:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">
        [masl dy]
       </a>
      </span>
      ; 28 July 1887  2 October 1968) was a French, naturalized American painter, sculptor, chess player and writer whose work is associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">
       Dadaism
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">
       conceptual art
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although careful about his use of the term Dada
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and direct association with Dada groups. Duchamp is commonly regarded, along with
      <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">
       Pablo Picasso
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">
       Henri Matisse
      </a>
      , as one of the three artists who helped to define the revolutionary developments in the
      <a href="/wiki/Plastic_arts" title="Plastic arts">
       plastic arts
      </a>
      in the opening decades of the twentieth century, responsible for significant developments in painting and sculpture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2010_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC_2010-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Duchamp has had an immense impact on twentieth-century and twenty first-century art. By
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      , he had rejected the work of many of his fellow artists (like Henri Matisse) as "
      <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">
       retinal
      </a>
      " art, intended only to please the eye. Instead, Duchamp wanted to put art back in the service of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">
       mind
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Marcel Duchamp was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Blainville-Crevon" title="Blainville-Crevon">
       Blainville-Crevon
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-Maritime" title="Seine-Maritime">
       Seine-Maritime
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_Normandy" title="Upper Normandy">
       Upper Normandy
      </a>
      region of France, and grew up in a family that enjoyed cultural activities. The art of painter and engraver
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89mile_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Nicolle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="mile Frdric Nicolle (page does not exist)">
       mile Frdric Nicolle
      </a>
      <span class="noprint" style="font-size: smaller; font-style: normal;">
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Nicolle" title="fr:mile Frdric Nicolle">
        fr
       </a>
       )
      </span>
      , his maternal grandfather, filled the house, and the family liked to play chess, read books, paint, and make music together.
     </p>
     <p>
      Of Eugene and Lucie Duchamp's seven children, one died as an infant and four became successful artists. Marcel Duchamp was the brother of:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Villon" title="Jacques Villon">
        Jacques Villon
       </a>
       (18751963), painter, printmaker
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Duchamp-Villon" title="Raymond Duchamp-Villon">
        Raymond Duchamp-Villon
       </a>
       (18761918), sculptor
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Duchamp" title="Suzanne Duchamp">
        Suzanne Duchamp-Crotti
       </a>
       (18891963), painter.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      As a child, with his two older brothers already away from home at school in Rouen, Duchamp was close to his sister Suzanne, who was a willing accomplice in games and activities conjured by his fertile imagination. At 8 years old, Duchamp followed in his brothers' footsteps when he left home and began schooling at the
      <a href="/wiki/Lyc%C3%A9e_Pierre-Corneille" title="Lyce Pierre-Corneille">
       Lyce Pierre-Corneille
      </a>
      , in Rouen. Two other students in his class also became well-known artists and lasting friends:
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Antoine_Pinchon" title="Robert Antoine Pinchon">
       Robert Antoine Pinchon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Dumont_(painter)" title="Pierre Dumont (painter)">
       Pierre Dumont
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For the next 8 years, he was locked into an educational regime which focused on intellectual development. Though he was not an outstanding student, his best subject was mathematics and he won two mathematics prizes at the school. He also won a prize for drawing in 1903, and at his commencement in 1904 he won a coveted first prize, validating his recent decision to become an artist.
     </p>
     <p>
      He learned academic drawing from a teacher who unsuccessfully attempted to protect his students from
      <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">
       Impressionism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">
       Post-Impressionism
      </a>
      , and other avant-garde influences. However, Duchamp's true artistic mentor at the time was his brother Jacques Villon, whose fluid and incisive style he sought to imitate. At 14, his first serious art attempts were drawings and watercolors depicting his sister Suzanne in various poses and activities. That summer he also painted landscapes in an Impressionist style using oils.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">
       Early work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Duchamp's early art works align with
      <a href="/wiki/Post-Impressionism" title="Post-Impressionism">
       Post-Impressionist
      </a>
      styles. He experimented with classical techniques and subjects. When he was later asked about what had influenced him at the time, Duchamp cited the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">
       Symbolist painter
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Odilon_Redon" title="Odilon Redon">
       Odilon Redon
      </a>
      , whose approach to art was not outwardly anti-academic, but quietly individual.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marcel_Duchamp,_1911-12,_Nude_(Study),_Sad_Young_Man_on_a_Train_(Nu_-esquisse-,_jeune_homme_triste_dans_un_train),_Peggy_Guggenheim_Collection,_Venice.jpg">

        </a>
        , Venice. This painting was identified as a self-portrait by the artist. Duchamp's primary concern in this painting is the depiction of two movements; that of the train in which there is a young man smoking, and that of the lurching figure itself.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">

          </span>

          <span>

     <p>
      He studied art at the
      <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Julian" title="Acadmie Julian">
       Acadmie Julian
      </a>
      from 1904 to 1905, but preferred playing billiards to attending classes. During this time Duchamp drew and sold cartoons which reflected his ribald humor. Many of the drawings use verbal
      <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">
       puns
      </a>
      (sometimes spanning multiple languages),
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_pun" title="Visual pun">
       visual puns
      </a>
      , or both. Such play with words and symbols engaged his imagination for the rest of his life.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1905, he began his compulsory military service with the 39th Infantry Regiment,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fran.C3.A7ois_Lespinasse_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fran.C3.A7ois_Lespinasse-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      working for a printer in Rouen. There he learned
      <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">
       typography
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
       printing
      </a>
      processesskills he would use in his later work.
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to his eldest brother
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Villon" title="Jacques Villon">
       Jacques'
      </a>
      membership in the prestigious
      <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_royale_de_peinture_et_de_sculpture" title="Acadmie royale de peinture et de sculpture">
       Acadmie royale de peinture et de sculpture
      </a>
      Duchamp's work was exhibited in the 1908
      <a href="/wiki/Salon_d%27Automne" title="Salon d'Automne">
       Salon d'Automne
      </a>
      . The following year his work was featured in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon_des_Ind%C3%A9pendants" title="Salon des Indpendants">
       Salon des Indpendants
      </a>
      . Of Duchamp's pieces in the show, critic
      <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Apollinaire" title="Guillaume Apollinaire">
       Guillaume Apollinaire
      </a>
      who was to become a friendcriticized what he called "Duchamp's very ugly nudes". Duchamp also became lifelong friends with exuberant artist
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">
       Francis Picabia
      </a>
      after meeting him at the 1911 Salon d'Automne, and Picabia proceeded to introduce him to a lifestyle of fast cars and "high" living.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1911, at Jacques' home in
      <a href="/wiki/Puteaux" title="Puteaux">
       Puteaux
      </a>
      , the brothers hosted a regular discussion group with
      <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">
       Cubist
      </a>
      artists including
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">
       Picabia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Delaunay" title="Robert Delaunay">
       Robert Delaunay
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Lger">
       Fernand Lger
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_de_La_Fresnaye" title="Roger de La Fresnaye">
       Roger de La Fresnaye
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gleizes" title="Albert Gleizes">
       Albert Gleizes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Metzinger" title="Jean Metzinger">
       Jean Metzinger
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gris" title="Juan Gris">
       Juan Gris
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Archipenko" title="Alexander Archipenko">
       Alexander Archipenko
      </a>
      . Poets and writers also participated. The group came to be known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puteaux_Group" title="Puteaux Group">
       Puteaux Group
      </a>
      , or the
      <a href="/wiki/Section_d%27Or" title="Section d'Or">
       Section d'Or
      </a>
      . Uninterested in the Cubists' seriousness or in their focus on visual matters, Duchamp did not join in discussions of Cubist theory, and gained a reputation of being shy. However, that same year he painted in a Cubist style, and added an impression of motion by using repetitive imagery.
     </p>
     <p>
      During this period Duchamp's fascination with transition, change, movement and distance became manifest, and like many artists of the time, he was intrigued with the concept of depicting the
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art" title="Fourth dimension in art">
       fourth dimension in art
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His painting
      <i>
       Sad Young Man on a Train
      </i>
      embodies this concern:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       First, there's the idea of the movement of the train, and then that of the sad young man who is in a corridor and who is moving about; thus there are two parallel movements corresponding to each other. Then, there is the distortion of the young manI had called this
       <i>
        elementary parallelism
       </i>
       . It was a formal decomposition; that is, linear elements following each other like parallels and distorting the object. The object is completely stretched out, as if elastic. The lines follow each other in parallels, while changing subtly to form the movement, or the form of the young man in question. I also used this procedure in the
       <i>
        Nude Descending a Staircase
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Works from this period included his first "machine" painting,
      <i>
       Coffee Mill (Moulin  caf)
      </i>
      (1911), which he gave to his brother Raymond Duchamp-Villon. The
      <i>
       Coffee Mill
      </i>
      shows similarity to the "grinder" mechanism of the
      <i>
       Large Glass
      </i>
      he was to paint years later.
     </p>
     <p>
      In his 1911,
      <i>
       Portrait of Chess Players
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Portrait de joueurs d'checs
      </i>
      ) there is the Cubist overlapping frames and multiple perspectives of his two brothers playing chess, but to that Duchamp added elements conveying the unseen mental activity of the players. (Notably, "chec" is French for "failure".)
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nude_Descending_a_Staircase_No.2">
       <i>
        Nude Descending a Staircase No.2
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nude Descending a Staircase No.2">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Nude_Descending_a_Staircase,_No._2" title="Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2">
       Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Duchamp's first work to provoke significant controversy was
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nude_Descending_a_Staircase,_No._2" title="Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2">
        Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2
       </a>
      </i>
      <i>
       (Nu descendant un escalier n 2)
      </i>
      (1912). The painting depicts the mechanistic motion of a nude, with superimposed facets, similar to motion pictures. It shows elements of both the fragmentation and synthesis of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">
       Cubists
      </a>
      , and the movement and dynamism of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Futurism_(art)" title="Futurism (art)">
       Futurists
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      He first submitted the piece to appear at the Cubist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon_des_Ind%C3%A9pendants" title="Salon des Indpendants">
       Salon des Indpendants
      </a>
      , but
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gleizes" title="Albert Gleizes">
       Albert Gleizes
      </a>
      (according to Duchamp in an interview with Pierre Cabanne, p.31)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_Brooke_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_Brooke-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      asked Duchamp's brothers to have him voluntarily withdraw the painting, or to paint over the title that he had painted on the work and rename it something else. Duchamp's brothers did approach him with Gleizes' request, but Duchamp quietly refused. However, there was no jury at the Salon des Indpendants and Gleizes was in no position to reject the painting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_Brooke_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_Brooke-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The controversy, according to art historian Peter Brooke, was not whether the work should be hung or not, but whether or not it should be hung with the Cubist group.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_Brooke_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_Brooke-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Of the incident Duchamp later recalled, "I said nothing to my brothers. But I went immediately to the show and took my painting home in a taxi. It was really a turning point in my life, I can assure you. I saw that I would not be very much interested in groups after that."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Yet Duchamp did appear in the illustrations to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Du_%22Cubisme%22" title='Du "Cubisme"'>
        Du "Cubisme"
       </a>
      </i>
      , he participated in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Salon_d%27Automne#La_Maison_Cubiste_.28Cubist_House.29" title="Salon d'Automne">
        La Maison Cubiste (Cubist House)
       </a>
      </i>
      , organized by the designer
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Mare" title="Andr Mare">
       Andr Mare
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/Salon_d%27Automne" title="Salon d'Automne">
       Salon d'Automne
      </a>
      of 1912 (a few months after the Indpendants); he signed the
      <a href="/wiki/Section_d%27Or" title="Section d'Or">
       Section d'Or
      </a>
      invitation and participated in the Section d'Or exhibition during the fall of 1912. The impression is, Brooke writes, "it was precisely because he wished to remain part of the group that he withdrew the painting; and that, far from being ill treated by the group, he was given a rather privileged position, probably through the patronage of Picabia".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_Brooke_15-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_Brooke-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He later submitted the painting to the 1913 "
      <a href="/wiki/Armory_Show" title="Armory Show">
       Armory Show
      </a>
      " in New York City. In addition to displaying works of American artists, this show was the first major exhibition of modern trends coming out of Paris, encompassing experimental styles of the European
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      , including
      <a href="/wiki/Fauvism" title="Fauvism">
       Fauvism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">
       Cubism
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">
       Futurism
      </a>
      . American show-goers, accustomed to realistic art, were scandalized, and the
      <i>
       Nude
      </i>
      was at the center of much of the controversy.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Leaving_.22retinal_art.22_behind">
       Leaving "retinal art" behind
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: Leaving "retinal art" behind'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      At about this time, Duchamp read
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Stirner" title="Max Stirner">
       Max Stirner
      </a>
      's philosophical tract,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Ego_and_Its_Own" title="The Ego and Its Own">
        The Ego and Its Own
       </a>
      </i>
      , the study of which he considered another turning point in his artistic and intellectual development. He called it "a remarkable book ... which advances no formal theories, but just keeps saying that the ego is always there in everything."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While in Munich in 1912, he painted the last of his Cubist-like paintings and he started "Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even" image, and began making plans for
      <i>
       The Large Glass
      </i>
       scribbling short notes to himself, sometimes with hurried sketches. It would be over 10 years before this piece was completed. Not much else is known about the two-month stay in Munich except that the friend he visited was intent on showing him the sights and the nightlife and that he was influenced by the works of the 16th century German painter Lucas Cranach the Elder in Munichs famed Alte Pinakothek, known for its Old Master paintings. Duchamp recalled that he daily took the short walk to visit this museum. Duchamp scholars have long recognized in Cranach the subdued ochre and brown color range Duchamp later employed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naumann_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Naumann-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The same year, Duchamp also attended a performance of a stage adaptation of
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Roussel" title="Raymond Roussel">
       Raymond Roussel
      </a>
      's 1910 novel,
      <i>
       Impressions d'Afrique
      </i>
      which featured plots that turned in on themselves, word play, surrealistic sets and humanoid machines. He credited the drama with having radically changed his approach to art, and having inspired him to begin the creation of his
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Bride_Stripped_Bare_By_Her_Bachelors,_Even" title="The Bride Stripped Bare By Her Bachelors, Even">
        The Bride Stripped Bare By Her Bachelors, Even
       </a>
      </i>
      , also known as
      <i>
       The Large Glass
      </i>
      . Work on
      <i>
       The Large Glass
      </i>
      continued into 1913, with his invention of inventing a repertoire of forms. He made notes, sketches and painted studies, and even drew some of his ideas on the wall of his apartment.
     </p>
     <p>
      Towards the end of 1912, he traveled with Picabia, Apollinaire and Gabrielle Buffet-Picabia through the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jura_mountains" title="Jura mountains">
       Jura mountains
      </a>
      , an adventure that Buffet-Picabia described as one of their "forays of demoralization, which were also forays of witticism and clownery ... the disintegration of the concept of art".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mink.2C_J._2004._Duchamp._Taschen_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mink.2C_J._2004._Duchamp._Taschen-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Duchamp's notes from the trip avoid logic and sense, and have a surrealistic, mythical connotation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp painted few canvases after 1912, and in those he did, he attempted to remove "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painterly" title="Painterly">
       painterly
      </a>
      " effects, and instead to use a technical drawing approach.
     </p>
     <p>
      His broad interests led him to an exhibition of aviation technology during this period, after which Duchamp said to his friend
      <a href="/wiki/Constantin_Br%C3%A2ncu%C8%99i" title="Constantin Brncui">
       Constantin Brncui
      </a>
      , "Painting is washed up. Who will ever do anything better than that propeller? Tell me, can you do that?".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mink.2C_J._2004._Duchamp._Taschen_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mink.2C_J._2004._Duchamp._Taschen-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brncui later sculpted
      <a href="/wiki/Bird_in_Space" title="Bird in Space">
       bird forms
      </a>
      , which U.S. Customs officials mistook for aviation parts and for which they attempted to collect import duties.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1913, Duchamp withdrew from painting circles and began working as a librarian in the Bibliothque Sainte-Genevive to be able to earn a living wage while concentrating on scholarly realms and working on his
      <i>
       Large Glass
      </i>
      . He studied math and physics  areas in which exciting new discoveries were taking place. The theoretical writings of
      <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincar">
       Henri Poincar
      </a>
      particularly intrigued and inspired Duchamp. Poincar postulated that the laws believed to govern matter were created solely by the minds that "understood" them and that no theory could be considered "true". "The things themselves are not what science can reach..., but only the relations between things. Outside of these relations there is no knowable reality",Poincar wrote in 1902.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reflecting the influence of Poincar's writings, Duchamp tolerated any interpretation of his art by regarding it as the creation of the person who formulated it, not as truth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp's own art-science experiments began during his tenure at the library. To make one of his favorite pieces,
      <i>
       3 Standard Stoppages
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       3 stoppages talon
      </i>
      ), he dropped three 1-meter lengths of thread onto prepared canvases, one at a time, from a height of 1 meter. The threads landed in three random undulating positions. He varnished them into place on the blue-black canvas strips and attached them to glass. He then cut three wood slats into the shapes of the curved strings, and put all the pieces into a croquet box. Three small leather signs with the title printed in gold were glued to each of the "stoppage" backgrounds. The piece appears to literally follow Poincar's
      <i>
       School of the Thread
      </i>
      , part of a book on classical mechanics.
     </p>
     <p>
      In his studio he mounted a bicycle wheel upside down onto a stool, spinning it occasionally just to watch it. Although it is often assumed that the
      <i>
       Bicycle Wheel
      </i>
      represents the first of Duchamp's
      <a href="/wiki/Readymades_of_Marcel_Duchamp" title="Readymades of Marcel Duchamp">
       "Readymades"
      </a>
      , this particular installation was never submitted for any art exhibition, and it was eventually lost. However, initially, the wheel was simply placed in the studio to create atmosphere: "I enjoyed looking at it just as I enjoy looking at the flames dancing in a fireplace."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After World War I was declared in 1914, with his brothers and many friends in military service and himself exempted, Duchamp felt uncomfortable in Paris. Meanwhile,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nude_Descending_a_Staircase_No._2" title="Nude Descending a Staircase No. 2">
        Nude Descending a Staircase No. 2
       </a>
      </i>
      had scandalized Americans at the
      <a href="/wiki/Armory_Show" title="Armory Show">
       Armory Show
      </a>
      , and helped secure the sale of all four of his paintings in the exhibition. Thus, being able to finance the trip, Duchamp decided to emigrate to the United States in 1915. To his surprise, he found he was a celebrity when he arrived in New York in 1915, where he quickly befriended art patron
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katherine_Dreier" title="Katherine Dreier">
       Katherine Dreier
      </a>
      and artist
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      . Duchamp's circle included art patrons
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Conrad_Arensberg" title="Walter Conrad Arensberg">
       Louise
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Conrad_Arensberg" title="Walter Conrad Arensberg">
       Walter Conrad Arensberg
      </a>
      , actress and artist
      <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Wood" title="Beatrice Wood">
       Beatrice Wood
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">
       Francis Picabia
      </a>
      , as well as other
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      figures. Though he spoke little English, in the course of supporting himself by giving French lessons and through some library work, he quickly learned the language. Duchamp became part of an artist colony in
      <a href="/wiki/Ridgefield,_New_Jersey" title="Ridgefield, New Jersey">
       Ridgefield, New Jersey
      </a>
      , across the
      <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">
       Hudson River
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For two years the Arensbergs, who would remain his friends and patrons for 42 years, were the landlords of his studio. In lieu of rent, they agreed that his payment would be
      <i>
       The Large Glass
      </i>
      . An art gallery offered Duchamp $10,000 per year in exchange for all of his yearly production, but Duchamp declined the offer, preferring to continue his work on
      <i>
       The Large Glass
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Soci.C3.A9t.C3.A9_Anonyme">
       Socit Anonyme
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Socit Anonyme">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Duchamp created the
      <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_Anonyme_(art)" title="Socit Anonyme (art)">
       Socit Anonyme
      </a>
      in 1920, along with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katherine_Dreier" title="Katherine Dreier">
       Katherine Dreier
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      . This was the beginning of his lifelong involvement in art dealing and collecting. The group collected modern art works, and arranged modern art exhibitions and lectures throughout the 1930s.
     </p>
     <p>
      By this time
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Pach" title="Walter Pach">
       Walter Pach
      </a>
      , one of the coordinators of the 1913
      <a href="/wiki/Armory_Show" title="Armory Show">
       Armory Show
      </a>
      , sought Duchamp's advice on modern art. Beginning with Socit Anonyme, Dreier also depended on Duchamp's counsel in gathering her collection, as did Arensberg. Later
      <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Guggenheim" title="Peggy Guggenheim">
       Peggy Guggenheim
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">
       Museum of Modern Art
      </a>
      directors
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Barr" title="Alfred Barr">
       Alfred Barr
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/James_Johnson_Sweeney" title="James Johnson Sweeney">
       James Johnson Sweeney
      </a>
      consulted with Duchamp on their modern art collections and shows.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dada">
       Dada
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="968" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg/220px-Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg/330px-Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg/440px-Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Fountain_(Duchamp)" title="Fountain (Duchamp)">
          Fountain
         </a>
        </i>
        1917
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">
       Dada
      </a>
      or Dadaism was an
      <a href="/wiki/Art_movement" title="Art movement">
       art movement
      </a>
      of the European
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      in the early 20th century. It began in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich" title="Zurich">
       Zurich
      </a>
      , Switzerland in 1916, spreading to
      <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
       Berlin
      </a>
      shortly thereafter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To quote Dona Budd's
      <i>
       The Language of Art Knowledge
      </i>
      ,
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Dada was born out of negative reaction to the horrors of
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        World War I
       </a>
       . This international movement was begun by a group of artists and poets associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Cabaret_Voltaire_(Zurich)" title="Cabaret Voltaire (Zurich)">
        Cabaret Voltaire
       </a>
       in Zurich. Dada rejected reason and logic, prizing nonsense, irrationality and intuition. The origin of the name Dada is unclear; some believe that it is a nonsensical word. Others maintain that it originates from the Romanian artists
       <a href="/wiki/Tristan_Tzara" title="Tristan Tzara">
        Tristan Tzara
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Janco" title="Marcel Janco">
        Marcel Janco
       </a>
       's frequent use of the words da, da, meaning yes, yes in the
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       language. Another theory says that the name "Dada" came during a meeting of the group when a paper knife stuck into a French-German dictionary happened to point to 'dada', a French word for 'hobbyhorse'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         25
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The movement primarily involved
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">
       visual arts
      </a>
      , literature, poetry,
      <a href="/wiki/Art_manifesto" title="Art manifesto">
       art manifestoes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">
       art theory
      </a>
      , theatre, and
      <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">
       graphic design
      </a>
      , and concentrated its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-war" title="Anti-war">
       anti-war
      </a>
      politics through a rejection of the prevailing standards in art through
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-art" title="Anti-art">
       anti-art
      </a>
      cultural works. In addition to being anti-war, Dada was also anti-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourgeois" title="Bourgeois">
       bourgeois
      </a>
      and had political affinities with the radical left.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dada activities included public gatherings, demonstrations, and publication of art/literary journals; passionate coverage of art, politics, and culture were topics often discussed in a variety of media. Key figures in the movement included
      <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ball" title="Hugo Ball">
       Hugo Ball
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Hennings" title="Emmy Hennings">
       Emmy Hennings
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hans_Arp" title="Hans Arp">
       Hans Arp
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Hausmann" title="Raoul Hausmann">
       Raoul Hausmann
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hannah_H%C3%B6ch" title="Hannah Hch">
       Hannah Hch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Baader" title="Johannes Baader">
       Johannes Baader
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tristan_Tzara" title="Tristan Tzara">
       Tristan Tzara
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">
       Francis Picabia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Huelsenbeck" title="Richard Huelsenbeck">
       Richard Huelsenbeck
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Grosz" title="Georg Grosz">
       Georg Grosz
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Heartfield" title="John Heartfield">
       John Heartfield
      </a>
      , Marcel Duchamp,
      <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Wood" title="Beatrice Wood">
       Beatrice Wood
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schwitters" title="Kurt Schwitters">
       Kurt Schwitters
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Richter_(artist)" title="Hans Richter (artist)">
       Hans Richter
      </a>
      , among others. The movement influenced later styles like the
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Downtown_music" title="Downtown music">
       downtown music
      </a>
      movements, and groups including
      <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">
       surrealism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nouveau_r%C3%A9alisme" title="Nouveau ralisme">
       Nouveau ralisme
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">
       pop art
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Fluxus" title="Fluxus">
       Fluxus
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Dada is the groundwork to abstract art and sound poetry, a starting point for performance art, a prelude to
       <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">
        postmodernism
       </a>
       , an influence on
       <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">
        pop art
       </a>
       , a celebration of antiart to be later embraced for anarcho-political uses in the 1960s and the movement that lay the foundation for
       <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">
        Surrealism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         26
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      New York Dada had a less serious tone than that of European Dadaism, and was not a particularly organized venture. Duchamp's friend
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Picabia" title="Francis Picabia">
       Francis Picabia
      </a>
      connected with the Dada group in Zrich, bringing to New York the Dadaist ideas of absurdity and "anti-art". Duchamp and Picabia first met in September 1911 at the Salon d'Automne in Paris, where they were both exhibiting. Duchamp showed a larger version of his
      <i>
       Young Man and Girl in Spring
      </i>
      1911, a work that had an Edenic theme and a thinly veiled sexuality also found in Picabia's contemporaneous
      <i>
       Adam and Eve
      </i>
      1911. According to Duchamp, "our friendship began right there".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A group met almost nightly at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_Arensberg" title="Walter Arensberg">
       Arensberg
      </a>
      home, or caroused in
      <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">
       Greenwich Village
      </a>
      . Together with
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      , Duchamp contributed his ideas and humor to the New York activities, many of which ran concurrent with the development of his
      <a href="/wiki/Readymades_of_Marcel_Duchamp" title="Readymades of Marcel Duchamp">
       Readymades
      </a>
      and 'The Large Glass.'
     </p>
     <p>
      The most prominent example of Duchamp's association with Dada was his submission of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fountain_(Duchamp)" title="Fountain (Duchamp)">
        Fountain
       </a>
      </i>
      , a urinal, to the
      <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Independent_Artists" title="Society of Independent Artists">
       Society of Independent Artists
      </a>
      exhibit in 1917. Artworks in the Independent Artists shows were not selected by jury, and all pieces submitted were displayed. However, the show committee insisted that
      <i>
       Fountain
      </i>
      was not art, and rejected it from the show. This caused an uproar amongst the Dadaists, and led Duchamp to resign from the board of the Independent Artists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomkinsBio_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TomkinsBio-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :181186
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Along with
      <a href="/wiki/Henri-Pierre_Roch%C3%A9" title="Henri-Pierre Roch">
       Henri-Pierre Roch
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Wood" title="Beatrice Wood">
       Beatrice Wood
      </a>
      , Duchamp published a Dada magazine in New York, titled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Blind_Man" title="The Blind Man">
        The Blind Man
       </a>
      </i>
      , which included art, literature, humor and commentary.
     </p>
     <p>
      When he returned to Paris after World War I, Duchamp did not participate in the Dada group.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Readymades">
       Readymades
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Readymades">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Readymades_of_Marcel_Duchamp" title="Readymades of Marcel Duchamp">
       Readymades of Marcel Duchamp
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      "Readymades" were found objects which Duchamp chose and presented as art. In 1913, Duchamp installed a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_Wheel" title="Bicycle Wheel">
        Bicycle Wheel
       </a>
      </i>
      in his studio. However, the idea of
      <i>
       Readymades
      </i>
      did not fully develop until 1915. The idea was to question the very notion of Art, and the adoration of art, which Duchamp found "unnecessary"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bbc-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       My idea was to choose an object that wouldn't attract me, either by its beauty or by its ugliness. To find a point of indifference in my looking at it, you see.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc-29">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         29
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <i>
       Bottle Rack
      </i>
      (1914), a bottle drying rack signed by Duchamp, is considered to be the first "pure" readymade.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prelude_to_a_Broken_Arm" title="Prelude to a Broken Arm">
        Prelude to a Broken Arm
       </a>
      </i>
      (1915), a snow shovel, also called
      <i>
       In Advance of the Broken Arm
      </i>
      , followed soon after. His
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fountain_(Duchamp)" title="Fountain (Duchamp)">
        Fountain
       </a>
      </i>
      , a urinal signed with the pseudonym "R. Mutt", shocked the art world in 1917.
      <i>
       Fountain
      </i>
      was selected in 2004 as "the most influential artwork of the 20th century" by 500 renowned artists and historians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2010_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC_2010-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marcel_Duchamp_Mona_Lisa_LHOOQ.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="474" data-file-width="300" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Marcel_Duchamp_Mona_Lisa_LHOOQ.jpg/220px-Marcel_Duchamp_Mona_Lisa_LHOOQ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/6e/Marcel_Duchamp_Mona_Lisa_LHOOQ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/6e/Marcel_Duchamp_Mona_Lisa_LHOOQ.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marcel_Duchamp_Mona_Lisa_LHOOQ.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/L.H.O.O.Q." title="L.H.O.O.Q.">
          L.H.O.O.Q.
         </a>
        </i>
        by Marcel Duchamp (1919)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1919, Duchamp made a
      <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">
       parody
      </a>
      of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">
        Mona Lisa
       </a>
      </i>
      by adorning a cheap reproduction of the painting with a mustache and goatee. To this he added the inscription
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/L.H.O.O.Q." title="L.H.O.O.Q.">
        L.H.O.O.Q.
       </a>
      </i>
      , a phonetic game which, when read out loud in French quickly sounds like
      <i>
       "Elle a chaud au cul"
      </i>
      . This can be translated as "She has a hot ass", implying that the woman in the painting is in a state of sexual excitement and availability. It may also have been intended as a Freudian joke, referring to
      <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">
       Leonardo da Vinci
      </a>
      's alleged homosexuality. Duchamp gave a "loose" translation of L.H.O.O.Q. as "there is fire down below" in a late interview with
      <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Schwarz" title="Arturo Schwarz">
       Arturo Schwarz
      </a>
      . According to
      <a href="/wiki/Rhonda_Roland_Shearer" title="Rhonda Roland Shearer">
       Rhonda Roland Shearer
      </a>
      , the apparent
      <i>
       Mona Lisa
      </i>
      reproduction is in fact a copy modeled partly on Duchamp's own face.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asrl_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-asrl-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Research published by Shearer also speculates that Duchamp himself may have created some of the objects which he claimed to be "found objects".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Large_Glass">
       <i>
        The Large Glass
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Large Glass">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Large_Glass" title="The Large Glass">
       The Large Glass
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1105" data-file-width="716" height="340" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg/220px-Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg/330px-Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg/440px-Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_LargeGlass.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Large_Glass" title="The Large Glass">
          The Large Glass
         </a>
        </i>
        (191523)
        <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">
         Philadelphia Museum of Art
        </a>
        Collection
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Duchamp worked on his complex
      <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">
       Futurism
      </a>
      inspired piece
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Bride_Stripped_Bare_by_Her_Bachelors,_Even" title="The Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even">
        The Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even (The Large Glass)
       </a>
      </i>
      from 1915 to 1923, with the exception of periods in Buenos Aires and Paris in 19181920. He executed the work on two panes of glass with materials such as lead foil, fuse wire, and dust. It combines chance procedures, plotted perspective studies, and laborious craftsmanship. He published notes for the piece,
      <i>
       The Green Box
      </i>
      , intended to complement the visual experience. They reflect the creation of unique rules of physics, and a mythology which describes the work. He stated that his "hilarious picture" is intended to depict the erotic encounter between a bride and her nine bachelors.
     </p>
     <p>
      The piece was inspired by a performance of the stage adaptation of
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Roussel" title="Raymond Roussel">
       Roussel
      </a>
      's novel
      <i>
       Impressions d'Afrique
      </i>
      which Duchamp attended in 1912. Notes, sketches and plans for the work were drawn on Duchamp's studio walls as early as 1913. In order to concentrate on the work free from material obligations, Duchamp found work as a librarian while living in France. After emigrating to the United States in 1915, he commenced his work on the piece financed by the support of the Arensbergs.
     </p>
     <p>
      The piece is partially constructed as a retrospective of Duchamp's works, including a three-dimensional reproduction of his earlier paintings
      <i>
       Bride
      </i>
      (1912),
      <i>
       Chocolate Grinder
      </i>
      (1914) and
      <i>
       Glider containing a water mill in neighboring metals
      </i>
      (19131915), which has opened for numerous interpretations. The work was formally declared "Unfinished" in 1923. Going home from its first public exhibition, the glass broke in its shipping crate and received a large crack in the glass. Duchamp repaired it, but left the cracks in the glass intact, accepting the chance element as a part of the piece.
     </p>
     <p>
      Until 1969 when the
      <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">
       Philadelphia Museum of Art
      </a>
      revealed Duchamp's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tant_donn%C3%A9s" title="tant donns">
        tant donns
       </a>
      </i>
      tableau,
      <i>
       The Large Glass
      </i>
      was thought to have been his last major work.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kinetic_works">
       Kinetic works
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kinetic works">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Duchamp's interest in
      <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_art" title="Kinetic art">
       kinetic works
      </a>
      can be discerned as early as the notes for
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Large_Glass" title="The Large Glass">
        The Large Glass
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_Wheel" title="Bicycle Wheel">
        Bicycle Wheel
       </a>
      </i>
      readymade, and despite losing interest in "retinal art", he retained interest in visual phenomena. In 1920, with help from
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      , Duchamp built a motorized sculpture,
      <i>
       Rotative plaques verre, optique de prcision
      </i>
      ("Rotary Glass Plates, Precision Optics"). The piece, which he did not consider to be art, involved a motor to spin pieces of rectangular glass on which were painted segments of a circle. When the apparatus spins, an optical illusion occurs, in which the segments appear to be closed concentric circles. Man Ray set up equipment to photograph the initial experiment, but when they turned the machine on for the second time, a belt broke, and caught a piece of the glass, which after glancing off Man Ray's head, shattered into bits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomkinsBio_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TomkinsBio-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :227228
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After moving back to Paris in 1923, at
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="Andr Breton">
       Andr Breton
      </a>
      's urging and through the financing of
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Doucet_(fashion_designer)" title="Jacques Doucet (fashion designer)">
       Jacques Doucet
      </a>
      , Duchamp built another optical device based on the first one,
      <i>
       Rotative Demisphre, optique de prcision
      </i>
      (Rotary Demisphere, Precision Optics). This time the optical element was a globe cut in half, with black concentric circles painted on it. When it spins, the circles appear to move backwards and forwards in space. Duchamp asked that Doucet not exhibit the apparatus as art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomkinsBio_28-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-TomkinsBio-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :254255
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Rotoreliefs
      </i>
      were the next phase of Duchamp's spinning works. To make the optical "play toys", he painted designs on flat cardboard circles and spun them on a phonographic turntable. When spinning, the flat disks appeared three-dimensional. He had a printer produce 500 sets of six of the designs, and set up a booth at a 1935 Paris inventors' show to sell them. The venture was a financial disaster, but some optical scientists thought they might be of use in restoring three-dimensional stereoscopic sight to people who have lost vision in one eye.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomkinsBio_28-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-TomkinsBio-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :301303
      </sup>
      In collaboration with Man Ray and
      <a href="/wiki/Marc_All%C3%A9gret" title="Marc Allgret">
       Marc Allgret
      </a>
      , Duchamp filmed early versions of the
      <i>
       Rotoreliefs
      </i>
      and they named the film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Anemic_Cinema" title="Anemic Cinema">
        Anmic Cinma
       </a>
      </i>
      (1926). Later, in
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Calder" title="Alexander Calder">
       Alexander Calder
      </a>
      's studio in 1931, while looking at the sculptor's kinetic works, Duchamp suggested that these should be called "
      <a href="/wiki/Mobile_(sculpture)" title="Mobile (sculpture)">
       mobiles
      </a>
      ". Calder agreed to use this novel term in his upcoming show. To this day, sculptures of this type are called "mobiles".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomkinsBio_28-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-TomkinsBio-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :294
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_ideas">
       Musical ideas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Musical ideas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Between 1912 and 1915, Duchamp worked with various musical ideas. At least three pieces have survived: two compositions and a note for a musical happening. The two compositions are based on
      <a href="/wiki/Aleatoric_music" title="Aleatoric music">
       chance operations
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Erratum Musical
      </i>
      , written for three voices, was published in 1934.
      <i>
       La Marie mise  nu par ses clibataires mme. Erratum Musical
      </i>
      is unfinished and was never published or exhibited during Duchamp's lifetime. According to the manuscript, the piece was intended for a mechanical instrument "in which the virtuoso intermediary is suppressed". The manuscript also contains a description for "An apparatus automatically recording fragmented musical periods", consisting of a funnel, several open-end cars and a set of numbered balls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These pieces predate
      <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">
       John Cage
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Changes" title="Music of Changes">
       Music of Changes
      </a>
      (1951), which is often considered the first modern piece to be conceived largely through random procedures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1968, Duchamp and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">
       John Cage
      </a>
      appeared together at a concert entitled "Reunion", playing a game of chess and composing
      <a href="/wiki/Aleatoric_music" title="Aleatoric music">
       Aleatoric music
      </a>
      by triggering a series of photoelectric cells underneath the chessboard.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toutfait.com_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-toutfait.com-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rrose_S.C3.A9lavy">
       Rrose Slavy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rrose Slavy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="734" data-file-width="571" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png/200px-Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png/300px-Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png/400px-Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Label_for_the_Belle_Haleine_cropped.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Rrose_S%C3%A9lavy" title="Rrose Slavy">
          Rrose Slavy
         </a>
        </i>
        (Marcel Duchamp). 1921. Photograph by
        <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
         Man Ray
        </a>
        . Art Direction by Marcel Duchamp. Silver print. 5-7/8" x 3"-7/8".
        <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">
         Philadelphia Museum of Art
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Rrose_S%C3%A9lavy" title="Rrose Slavy">
       Rrose Slavy
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      "Rrose Slavy", also spelled Rose Slavy, was one of Duchamp's pseudonyms. The name, a
      <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">
       pun
      </a>
      , sounds like the French phrase
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eros_(concept)" title="Eros (concept)">
        Eros
       </a>
       , c'est la vie
      </i>
      , which may be translated as "Eros, such is life". It has also been read as
      <i>
       arroser la vie
      </i>
      ("to make a toast to life"). Slavy emerged in 1921 in a series of photographs by
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      showing Duchamp dressed as a woman. Through the 1920s Man Ray and Duchamp collaborated on more photos of Slavy. Duchamp later used the name as the byline on written material and signed several creations with it.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="454" height="77" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fc/Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg/100px-Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fc/Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg/150px-Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fc/Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg/200px-Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg 2x" width="100"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_Sneeze.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Why Not Sneeze Rrose Slavy?
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      These included at least one sculpture,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Why_Not_Sneeze_Rrose_S%C3%A9lavy%3F" title="Why Not Sneeze Rrose Slavy?">
        Why Not Sneeze Rrose Slavy?
       </a>
      </i>
      (1921). The sculpture, a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Readymades_of_Marcel_Duchamp" title="Readymades of Marcel Duchamp">
       readymade
      </a>
      called an assemblage, consists of a mercury
      <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">
       oral
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">
       thermometer
      </a>
      , 152 white cubes (made of marble, but resembling sugar cubes), a piece of
      <a href="/wiki/Cuttlebone" title="Cuttlebone">
       cuttlebone
      </a>
      , and a tiny porcelain dish inside a
      <a href="/wiki/Birdcage" title="Birdcage">
       birdcage
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The inspiration for the name "Rrose Slavy" may have been
      <a href="/wiki/Belle_da_Costa_Greene" title="Belle da Costa Greene">
       Belle da Costa Greene
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.P._Morgan" title="J.P. Morgan">
       J.P. Morgan
      </a>
      's librarian of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Morgan_Library_%26_Museum" title="The Morgan Library &amp; Museum">
       Pierpont Morgan Library
      </a>
      . Following the death of J.P. Morgan, Sr., Greene became the Library's director, working there for a total of forty-three years. Empowered by the Morgans, she built the library collection, buying and selling rare
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manuscripts" title="Manuscripts">
       manuscripts
      </a>
      , books and art.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Rrose Slavy and the other pseudonyms Duchamp used may be read as a comment on the fallacy of romanticizing the conscious individuality or subjectivity of the artist, a theme that is also a prominent subtext of the
      <a href="/wiki/Readymades_of_Marcel_Duchamp" title="Readymades of Marcel Duchamp">
       Readymades
      </a>
      . Duchamp said in an interview,"You think you're doing something entirely your own, and a year later you look at it and you see actually the roots of where your art comes from without your knowing it at all."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_from_art_to_chess">
       Transition from art to chess
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transition from art to chess">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Man_Ray,_1920,_Three_Heads_(Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp),_gelatin_silver_print,_20.7_x_15.7_cm,_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2468" data-file-width="1891" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a5/Man_Ray%2C_1920%2C_Three_Heads_%28Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp%29%2C_gelatin_silver_print%2C_20.7_x_15.7_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg/220px-Man_Ray%2C_1920%2C_Three_Heads_%28Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp%29%2C_gelatin_silver_print%2C_20.7_x_15.7_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a5/Man_Ray%2C_1920%2C_Three_Heads_%28Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp%29%2C_gelatin_silver_print%2C_20.7_x_15.7_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg/330px-Man_Ray%2C_1920%2C_Three_Heads_%28Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp%29%2C_gelatin_silver_print%2C_20.7_x_15.7_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a5/Man_Ray%2C_1920%2C_Three_Heads_%28Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp%29%2C_gelatin_silver_print%2C_20.7_x_15.7_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg/440px-Man_Ray%2C_1920%2C_Three_Heads_%28Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp%29%2C_gelatin_silver_print%2C_20.7_x_15.7_cm%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Man_Ray,_1920,_Three_Heads_(Joseph_Stella_and_Marcel_Duchamp),_gelatin_silver_print,_20.7_x_15.7_cm,_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
         Man Ray
        </a>
        , 1920,
        <i>
         Three Heads
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stella" title="Joseph Stella">
         Joseph Stella
        </a>
        and Marcel Duchamp, painting bust portrait of Man Ray above Duchamp), gelatin silver print, 20.7 x 15.7 cm,
        <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">
         Museum of Modern Art
        </a>
        , New York
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1918, Duchamp took leave of the New York art scene, interrupting his work on the
      <i>
       Large Glass
      </i>
      , and went to
      <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
       Buenos Aires
      </a>
      , where he remained for nine months and often played
      <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">
       chess
      </a>
      . He
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodcarving" title="Woodcarving">
       carved
      </a>
      his own
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_set" title="Chess set">
       chess set
      </a>
      from wood with help from a local craftsman who made the
      <a href="/wiki/Knight_(chess)" title="Knight (chess)">
       knights
      </a>
      . He moved to Paris in 1919, and then back to the United States in 1920. Upon his return to Paris in 1923, Duchamp was, in essence, no longer a practicing artist. Instead, his main interest was chess, which he studied for the rest of his life to the exclusion of most other activities.
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp is seen, briefly, playing chess with
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      in the short film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Entr%27acte_(film)" title="Entr'acte (film)">
        Entr'acte
       </a>
      </i>
      (1924) by
      <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Clair" title="Ren Clair">
       Ren Clair
      </a>
      . He designed the 1925 Poster for the Third French Chess Championship, and as a competitor in the event, finished at fifty percent (33, with two draws). Thus he earned the title of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_master" title="Chess master">
       chess master
      </a>
      . During this period his fascination with chess so distressed his first wife that she glued his pieces to the
      <a href="/wiki/Chessboard" title="Chessboard">
       board
      </a>
      . Duchamp continued to play in the French Championships and also in the
      <a href="/wiki/Chess_Olympiad" title="Chess Olympiad">
       Chess Olympiads
      </a>
      from 19281933, favoring
      <a href="/wiki/Hypermodernism_(chess)" title="Hypermodernism (chess)">
       hypermodern
      </a>
      openings such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nimzo-Indian" title="Nimzo-Indian">
       Nimzo-Indian
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometime in the early 1930s, Duchamp reached the height of his ability, but realized that he had little chance of winning recognition in top-level chess. In the following years, his participation in chess tournaments declined, but he discovered
      <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_chess" title="Correspondence chess">
       correspondence chess
      </a>
      and became a chess journalist, writing weekly newspaper columns. While his contemporaries were achieving spectacular success in the art world by selling their works to high-society collectors, Duchamp observed, "I am still a victim of chess. It has all the beauty of artand much more. It cannot be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercialized" title="Commercialized">
       commercialized
      </a>
      . Chess is much purer than art in its social position."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On another occasion, Duchamp elaborated, "The chess pieces are the block alphabet which shapes thoughts; and these thoughts, although making a visual design on the chess-board, express their beauty abstractly, like a poem. ... I have come to the personal conclusion that while all artists are not chess players, all chess players are artists."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1932, Duchamp teamed with chess theorist
      <a href="/wiki/Vitaly_Halberstadt" title="Vitaly Halberstadt">
       Vitaly Halberstadt
      </a>
      to publish
      <i>
       L'opposition et cases conjugues sont rconcilies
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(chess)" title="Opposition (chess)">
       Opposition
      </a>
      and Sister Squares are Reconciled), known as
      <a href="/wiki/Corresponding_squares" title="Corresponding squares">
       corresponding squares
      </a>
      . This treatise describes the
      <a href="/wiki/Corresponding_squares#Lasker-Reichhelm_position" title="Corresponding squares">
       Lasker-Reichhelm position
      </a>
      , an extremely rare type of position that can arise in the
      <a href="/wiki/Chess_endgame" title="Chess endgame">
       endgame
      </a>
      . Using
      <a href="/wiki/Enneagram_(geometry)" title="Enneagram (geometry)">
       enneagram
      </a>
      -like charts that fold upon themselves, the authors demonstrated that in this position, the most Black can hope for is a
      <a href="/wiki/Draw_(chess)" title="Draw (chess)">
       draw
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The theme of the "endgame" is important to an understanding of Duchamp's complex attitude towards his artistic career. Irish playwright
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beckett" title="Samuel Beckett">
       Samuel Beckett
      </a>
      was an associate of Duchamp, and used the theme as the narrative device for the 1957 play of the same name,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Endgame_(play)" title="Endgame (play)">
        Endgame
       </a>
      </i>
      . In 1968, Duchamp played an artistically important chess match with avant-garde composer
      <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">
       John Cage
      </a>
      , at a concert entitled "Reunion". Music was produced by a series of photoelectric cells underneath the chessboard, triggered sporadically by normal game play.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toutfait.com_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-toutfait.com-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On choosing a career in chess, Duchamp said, "If
      <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Fischer" title="Bobby Fischer">
       Bobby Fischer
      </a>
      came to me for advice, I certainly would not discourage himas if anyone couldbut I would try to make it positively clear that he will never have any money from chess, live a monk-like existence and know more rejection than any artist ever has, struggling to be known and accepted."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Duchamp left a legacy to chess in the form of an enigmatic endgame problem he composed in 1943. The problem was included in the announcement for Julian Levi's gallery exhibition
      <i>
       Through the Big End of the Opera Glass
      </i>
      , printed on translucent paper with the faint inscription: "White to play and win". Grandmasters and endgame specialists have since grappled with the problem, with most concluding that there is no solution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_artistic_involvement">
       Later artistic involvement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Later artistic involvement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although Duchamp was no longer considered to be an active artist, he continued to consult with artists, art dealers and collectors. From 1925 he often traveled between France and the United States, and made New York's Greenwich Village his home in 1942. He also occasionally worked on artistic projects such as the short film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Anemic_Cinema" title="Anemic Cinema">
        Anemic Cinema
       </a>
      </i>
      (1926),
      <i>
       Box in a Valise
      </i>
      (193541),
      <i>
       Self Portrait in Profile
      </i>
      (1958) and the larger work
      <i>
       tant Donns
      </i>
      (194666). In 1943, he participated with
      <a href="/wiki/Maya_Deren" title="Maya Deren">
       Maya Deren
      </a>
      in her unfinished film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Witch%27s_Cradle" title="The Witch's Cradle">
        The Witch's Cradle
       </a>
      </i>
      , filmed in
      <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Guggenheim" title="Peggy Guggenheim">
       Peggy Guggenheim
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_This_Century_gallery" title="Art of This Century gallery">
       Art of This Century gallery
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      From the mid-1930s onwards, he collaborated with the
      <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">
       Surrealists
      </a>
      ; however, he did not join the movement, despite the coaxing of
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="Andr Breton">
       Andr Breton
      </a>
      . From then until 1944, together with
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">
       Max Ernst
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Eugenio_Granell" title="Eugenio Granell">
       Eugenio Granell
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="Andr Breton">
       Breton
      </a>
      , Duchamp edited the Surrealist periodical
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/VVV_(magazine)" title="VVV (magazine)">
        VVV
       </a>
      </i>
      , and also served as an advisory editor for the magazine
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/View_(magazine)" title="View (magazine)">
        View
       </a>
      </i>
      , which featured him in its March 1945 edition, thus introducing him to a broader American audience.
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp's influence on the art world remained behind the scenes until the late 1950s, when he was "discovered" by young artists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rauschenberg" title="Robert Rauschenberg">
       Robert Rauschenberg
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jasper_Johns" title="Jasper Johns">
       Jasper Johns
      </a>
      , who were eager to escape the dominance of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstract_Expressionism" title="Abstract Expressionism">
       Abstract Expressionism
      </a>
      . He was a co-founder of the international literary group
      <a href="/wiki/Oulipo" title="Oulipo">
       Oulipo
      </a>
      in 1960. Interest in Duchamp was reignited in the 1960s, and he gained international public recognition. In 1963, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasadena_Art_Museum" title="Pasadena Art Museum">
       Pasadena Art Museum
      </a>
      mounted his first retrospective exhibition, and there he appeared in an iconic photograph playing chess opposite nude model
      <a href="/wiki/Eve_Babitz" title="Eve Babitz">
       Eve Babitz
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anolik_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Anolik-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The photograph was later described by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Archives_of_American_Art" title="Smithsonian Archives of American Art">
       Smithsonian Archives of American Art
      </a>
      as being "among the key documentary images of American modern art".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karlstrom_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Karlstrom-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1966 the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" title="Tate Gallery">
       Tate Gallery
      </a>
      hosted a large exhibit of his work. Other major institutions, including the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Art_Museum" title="Philadelphia Art Museum">
       Philadelphia Art Museum
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">
       Metropolitan Museum of Art
      </a>
      , followed with large showings of Duchamp's work. He was invited to lecture on art and to participate in formal discussions, as well as sitting for interviews with major publications. As the last surviving member of the Duchamp family of artists, in 1967 Duchamp helped to organize an exhibition in Rouen, France, called
      <i>
       Les Duchamp: Jacques Villon, Raymond Duchamp-Villon, Marcel Duchamp, Suzanne Duchamp
      </i>
      . Parts of this family exhibition were later shown again at the
      <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_National_d%27Art_Moderne" title="Muse National d'Art Moderne">
       Muse National d'Art Moderne
      </a>
      in Paris.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibition_design">
       Exhibition design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Exhibition design">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Duchamp participated in the design of the 1938 International Surrealist Exhibition, which was held at the
      <i>
       Galerie des Beaux-arts
      </i>
      , Paris. The show featured more than 60 artists from different countries, including approximately 300 paintings, objects, collages, photographs, and installations. The surrealists wanted to create an exhibition which in itself would be a creative act, and Andre Breton named Duchamp,
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Paalen" title="Wolfgang Paalen">
       Wolfgang Paalen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvador_Dali" title="Salvador Dali">
       Salvador Dali
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">
       Max Ernst
      </a>
      to help him. At the exhibition's entrance he
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2015)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      placed
      <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dal">
       Salvador Dal
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rainy_Taxi" title="Rainy Taxi">
        Rainy Taxi
       </a>
      </i>
      , a work consisting of a
      <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">
       taxicab
      </a>
      rigged to produce a drizzle of water down the inside of its windows, with a shark-headed creature in the driver's seat, and a blond
      <a href="/wiki/Mannequin" title="Mannequin">
       mannequin
      </a>
      covered with live
      <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">
       snails
      </a>
      in the back. In this way Duchamp confronted guests entering the exhibition, who were in full evening dress.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Surrealist Street
      </i>
      filled one side of the lobby with mannequins dressed by various surrealists. The main hall was a simulation of a dark subterranean cave with 1,200 empty coal bags suspended from the ceiling. The floor was covered by Paalen with dead leaves and mud from the
      <a href="/wiki/Montparnasse_Cemetery" title="Montparnasse Cemetery">
       Montparnasse Cemetery
      </a>
      . In the middle of the grand hall underneath Duchamps coal sacks, Paalen installed an artificial water-filled pond with real
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Water_lily" title="Water lily">
       water lilies
      </a>
      and reeds, which he called
      <i>
       Avant La Mare
      </i>
      . Illumination was provided only by a single light bulb,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      so patrons were given
      <a href="/wiki/Flashlight" title="Flashlight">
       flashlights
      </a>
      with which to view the art (an idea of Man Ray), while the aroma of
      <a href="/wiki/Coffee_roasting" title="Coffee roasting">
       roasting coffee
      </a>
      filled the air. Around midnight, the visitors witnessed the dancing shimmer of a scantily dressed girl who suddenly arose from the reeds, jumped on a bed, shrieked hysterically, then disappeared just as quickly. Much to the surrealists' satisfaction, the exhibition scandalized many of the guests.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1942, for the
      <i>
       First Papers of Surrealism
      </i>
      show in New York, surrealists called on Duchamp to design the exhibition. He wove a three-dimensional web of string throughout the rooms of the space, in some cases making it almost impossible to see the works.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Duchamp made a secret arrangement with an associate's son to bring young friends to the opening of the show. When the formally-dressed patrons arrived, they found a dozen children in athletic clothes kicking and passing balls, and skipping rope. When questioned, the children were told to say "Mr. Duchamp told us we could play here". Duchamp's design of the catalog for the show included "found", rather than posed, photographs of the artists.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Throughout his adult life, Duchamp was a passionate smoker of Habana cigars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp became a United States citizen in 1955.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 1927, Duchamp married
      <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lydie_Sarazin-Levassor" title="fr:Lydie Sarazin-Levassor">
       Lydie Sarazin-Lavassor
      </a>
      ; however, they divorced six months later. It was rumored that Duchamp had chosen a marriage of convenience, because Sarazin-Lavassor was the daughter of a wealthy automobile manufacturer. Early in January 1928, Duchamp said that he could no longer bear the responsibility and confinement of marriage, and soon thereafter they were divorced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After Sarazin-Lavassor's death (June 23, 1988),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Duchamp allowed Mary Reynolds to reveal their complicatedand heretofore secretongoing 20-year relationship.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were together until her death in 1950 of uterine cancer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Between 1946 and 1951
      <a href="/wiki/Maria_Martins_(artist)" title="Maria Martins (artist)">
       Maria Martins
      </a>
      was his mistress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1954, he and
      <a href="/wiki/Alexina_Duchamp" title="Alexina Duchamp">
       Alexina "Teeny" Sattler
      </a>
      married, and they remained together until his death.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".C3.89tant_donn.C3.A9s">
       <i>
        tant donns
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: tant donns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tant_donn%C3%A9s" title="tant donns">
       tant donns
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Etant_donnes.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="292" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Etant_donnes.jpg/220px-Etant_donnes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/25/Etant_donnes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/25/Etant_donnes.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Etant_donnes.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tant_donn%C3%A9s" title="tant donns">
          tant donns
         </a>
        </i>
        , 19461966,
        <a href="/wiki/Mixed_media" title="Mixed media">
         mixed media
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">
         Philadelphia Museum of Art
        </a>
        . This was posthumously and permanently
        <a href="/wiki/Installation_art" title="Installation art">
         installed
        </a>
        in the museum in 1969
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Duchamp's final major art work surprised the art world that believed he had given up art for chess 25 years earlier. Entitled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tant_donn%C3%A9s" title="tant donns">
        tant donns
       </a>
       : 1 la chute d'eau / 2 le gaz d'clairage
      </i>
      ("Given: 1. The Waterfall, 2. The Illuminating Gas"), it is a tableau, visible only through a peep hole in a wooden door.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A nude woman can be seen lying on her back with her face hidden, legs spread, and one hand holding a gas lamp in the air against a landscape backdrop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Duchamp had worked secretly on the piece from 1946 to 1966 in his
      <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">
       Greenwich Village
      </a>
      studio while even his closest friends thought he had abandoned art. The torso of the nude figure is based on Duchamp's lover, the Brazilian sculptor
      <a href="/wiki/Maria_Martins_(artist)" title="Maria Martins (artist)">
       Maria Martins
      </a>
      , with whom he had an affair from 1946 to 1951.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_burial">
       Death and burial
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Death and burial">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Duchamp died suddenly and peacefully in the early morning of 2 October 1968 at his home in
      <a href="/wiki/Neuilly-sur-Seine" title="Neuilly-sur-Seine">
       Neuilly-sur-Seine
      </a>
      , France. After an evening dining at home with his friends
      <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">
       Man Ray
      </a>
      and
      <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Lebel_(critique)" title="fr:Robert Lebel (critique)">
       Robert Lebel
      </a>
      , Duchamp retired at 1:05 A.M., collapsed in his studio, and died of heart failure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp was an atheist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is buried in the Rouen Cemetery, in
      <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">
       Rouen
      </a>
      , France, with the
      <a href="/wiki/Epitaph" title="Epitaph">
       epitaph
      </a>
      , "D'ailleurs, c'est toujours les autres qui meurent" ("Besides, it's always the others who die"). Even in his death, Duchamp retained a sense of humor (a means for him of reaffirming his freedom, while undermining absolutes and certainties).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Duchamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Duchamp is considered by many critics to be one of the most important artists of the 20th century,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and his output influenced the development of postWorld War I
      <a href="/wiki/Art_of_Europe" title="Art of Europe">
       Western art
      </a>
      . He advised modern art collectors, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Guggenheim" title="Peggy Guggenheim">
       Peggy Guggenheim
      </a>
      and other prominent figures, thereby helping to shape the tastes of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_art_history" title="Western art history">
       Western art
      </a>
      during this period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomkinsBio_28-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-TomkinsBio-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He challenged conventional thought about artistic processes and rejected the emerging art market, through subversive anti-art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He famously dubbed a urinal art and named it
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fountain_(Duchamp)" title="Fountain (Duchamp)">
        Fountain
       </a>
      </i>
      . Duchamp produced relatively few artworks, while remaining mostly aloof of the
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      circles of his time. He went on to pretend to abandon art and devote the rest of his life to chess, while secretly continuing to make art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1958 Duchamp said of creativity,
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The creative act is not performed by the artist alone; the spectator brings the work in contact with the external world by deciphering and interpreting its inner qualifications and thus adds his contribution to the creative act.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
        <a href="#cite_note-61">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         61
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Duchamp in his later life explicitly expressed negativity towards art itself. In a BBC interview with Duchamp conducted by Joan Bakewell in 1966 Duchamp compared art with religion, whereby he stated that he wished to do away with art the same way many have done away with religion. Duchamp goes on to explain to the interviewer that "the word art etymologically means to do", that art means activity of any kind, and that it is our society that creates "purely artificial" distinctions of being an artist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A quotation erroneously attributed to Duchamp suggests a negative attitude toward later trends in 20th-century art:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       This Neo-Dada, which they call New Realism, Pop Art, Assemblage, etc., is an easy way out, and lives on what Dada did. When I discovered the ready-mades I sought to discourage aesthetics. In Neo-Dada they have taken my readymades and found aesthetic beauty in them, I threw the bottle-rack and the urinal into their faces as a challenge and now they admire them for their aesthetic beauty.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      However, this was actually written in 1961 by fellow Dadaist
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Richter_(artist)" title="Hans Richter (artist)">
       Hans Richter
      </a>
      , in the second person, i.e. "You threw the bottle-rack...". Although a marginal note in the letter suggests that Duchamp generally approved of the statement, Richter did not make the distinction clear until many years later.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Duchamp's attitude was actually more favorable, as evidenced by another statement made in 1964:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Pop Art is a return to "conceptual" painting, virtually abandoned, except by the Surrealists, since [Gustave] Courbet, in favor of retinal painting.... If you take a Campbell soup can and repeat it 50 times, you are not interested in the retinal image. What interests you is the concept that wants to put 50 Campbell soup cans on a canvas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
        <a href="#cite_note-66">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         66
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp_Prize" title="Marcel Duchamp Prize">
       Prix Marcel Duchamp
      </a>
      (Marcel Duchamp Prize), established in 2000, is an annual award given to a young artist by the
      <a href="/wiki/Centre_Georges_Pompidou" title="Centre Georges Pompidou">
       Centre Georges Pompidou
      </a>
      . In 2004, as a testimony to the legacy of Duchamp's work to the art world, his
      <i>
       Fountain
      </i>
      was voted "the most influential artwork of the 20th century" by a panel of prominent artists and art historians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_2010_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC_2010-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
