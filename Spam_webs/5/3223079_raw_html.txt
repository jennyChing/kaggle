           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             Everyone likes to think that their favorite horror movie was an enormous success but thats definitely not always the case. Sure, it makes sense for bad horror movies to completely fail at the box office. But some of the best films of years past have shared that same fate. There are a lot of factors that go into a movies lack of success, even when the filmis good.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             Some picturesjust come out at the wrong time. Either they are ahead of their time and dont gel with the interests of the era, or the reverse: They are too similar to other features being released at the same time and just get buried. Whatever thereasoning may be, here are ninegreat horror movies that bombed at the box office.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             <b>
              <i>
               Children of the Corn
              </i>
             </b>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             <i>
              Children of the Corn
             </i>
             is now regarded as a classic, but it was neither a critical or financial success when it was released. It has a total lifetime gross of $14,568,989.There were a lot of Stephen King adaptations hitting the screen at the time it was released and
             <i>
              Children of the Corn
             </i>
             was just another one in the mix. When it was panned by critics, audiences lost interest. The filmfell flat on its face. The most baffling thing about the movies failure is the fact that it just refused to die. It has to be the least successful movie to ever spawn such a long-lastingfranchise. It has not only eight sequels to date, but also a remake produced by the SyFy Channel.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/children-of-the-corn.jpg">
             <img alt="children-of-the-corn" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15302" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/children-of-the-corn.jpg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              <a class="zem_slink" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_Me_to_Hell" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_Me_to_Hell', 'Drag Me to Hell']);" rel="wikipedia" target="_blank" title="Drag Me to Hell">
               Drag Me to Hell
              </a>
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             <i>
              Drag Me to Hell
             </i>
             was touted as
             <i>
              Evil Dead
             </i>
             director Sam Raimis big return to horror after films like
             <i>
              For Love of the Game, The Quick and the Dead
             </i>
             and the
             <i>
              Spider-Man
             </i>
             trilogy. It got a lot of buzz from the horror community and the die-hard fans seemed to be genuinely excited about it. But they werent excited enough to go see it. The film raked in$42,000,000 domestically againsta budget of$30,000,000 makingonly $16,000,000 on its opening weekend. Its the lowest-grossing film of Raimis since the early days of his career and event hour it made a marginal profit, the film is thereby considered a commercial failure. Some have speculated that the movie was poorly handled in theaters, some have said that the rise of online piracy was in part to blame. Regardless, the filmhas managed to find an audienceon home video.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/drag-me-to-hell-banner.jpeg">
             <img alt="drag-me-to-hell-banner" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15304" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/drag-me-to-hell-banner.jpeg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              Grindhouse
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             The Quentin Tarantino/Robert Rodriguez Grindhouse Double Bill just didnt resonate with theater goers. It was a movie made for cinephiles, but not for mainstream audiences. Out of all the movies on this list, this entry was clearly doomed from the start. It was made as a love-letter to a style of theater-going that had long-since died out. Double features are barely shown anymore, and they while they are remembered fondly by a select few, mainstream audiences clearly did not get the appeal. I sawthis one in theaters and remember every patron, with the exception of group, leaving after the first movie. Nobody got it. The overall budget of both features was $67,000,000 and the filmsonly grossed $25,000,000 theatrically.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/grindhouse-2007.jpg">
             <img alt="grindhouse-2007" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15305" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/grindhouse-2007.jpg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              Pumpkinhead
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             Though it is now viewed as one of the best creature features of all time,
             <i>
              Pumpkinhead
             </i>
             took years to find its audience. Directed by FX wizard Stan Winston, it was held from release for nearly three years after its original distributor went bankrupt. When the filmwas released, it had no marketing of any kind and all those horror fans who had been reading Fangoria and getting excited for the film had all but forgotten about it when the picturewas finally released.
             <em>
              Pumpkinhead
             </em>
             earned a lifetime gross of $4,000,000 and did not become a hit on video until the early 90s, when itspawned a straight-to-video sequel in 1994.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Pumpkinhead-1988.jpeg">
             <img alt="Pumpkinhead-1988" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15306" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Pumpkinhead-1988.jpeg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              Mary Shelleys Frankenstein
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             <i>
              Bram Stokers Dracula
             </i>
             was one of the biggest hits of 1992. Given theenormous successof that film, it onlyseemed logical for Columbia to try and recapture that with an adaptation of
             <i>
              Frankenstein.
             </i>
             But nothing went according to plan. The movie was not as suave or sexy as
             <i>
              Dracula.
             </i>
             Its a very different story. The filmsticks much closer to the book than previous adaptations but people of the time just werent interested.
             <em>
              Mary Shellys Frankenstein
             </em>
             brought ina box office total of $22,000,000less than half of its $45,000,000 budget.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/frankenstein-1994.jpg">
             <img alt="frankenstein-1994" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15307" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/frankenstein-1994.jpg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              The Monster Squad
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             Now one of the most beloved cult classics of the 1980s,
             <i>
              The Monster Squad
             </i>
             was a dismal failure in theaters. With a total gross of $4,000,000 it is regarded as a complete and utterflop. There are a lot of possible reasons for this. It was released around the same time as huge hits like
             <i>
              Gremlins
             </i>
             and
             <em>
              The
             </em>
             <i>
              Goonies
             </i>
             which were both much lighter fare.
             <i>
              The Monster Squad
             </i>
             was naturally darker and parentsquickly decidedthat it was altogether too dark for children. Further working to the pictures disadvantage is the fact that itwasnt marketed well at ll. Nobody could tell if it was a movie for children or adults and therefore it never found its audience. The picturedidnt even start to be considered a cult classic until its DVD release 20 years later.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/the-monster-squad.jpg">
             <img alt="the-monster-squad" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15308" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/the-monster-squad.jpg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              Day of the Dead
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             George A. Romeros
             <i>
              Day of the Dead
             </i>
             is the final note in his original zombie trilogy. It was not a financial or critical success when it was first released but is now considered to be one of his best efforts. This was the most overtly political of Romeros
             <i>
              Dead
             </i>
             films. It takes a look at the brief hope that the zombies can be overcome. It leads viewers to believe that there might be some possibility ofsustaining life in this world and then lets the audience watch that hope be demolished by human error. But there was no audience for this film for a verylong time. It grossed only$5,000,000 at the US box office and only found its audience after its release to home video.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/day-of-the-dead-1985.png">
             <img alt="day-of-the-dead-1985" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15309" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/day-of-the-dead-1985.png" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              Vertigo
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             Alfred Hitchcocks
             <i>
              Vertigo
             </i>
             is widely considered one of the best films of all time. [Editors Note:
             <em>
              Vertigo
             </em>
             wasrecently rankedthe best film of all time by
             <i>
              Sight &amp; Sound
             </i>
             <em>
              Magazine;
             </em>
             an honor that had been held by
             <em>
              Citizen Kane
             </em>
             since 1962]Its a masterpiece of suspense.
             <i>
             </i>
             Its a more intimate filmthan some of the large scale thrills he had offered in the past
             <i>
             </i>
             and people wanted more of the same. This one did break even at the box office with a total gross of $2.8 million, but it only managed to do so by about $400,000. It made significantly less than the other lauded classics from the director, even though it is now almost universally revered.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/vertigo-hitchcock.jpg">
             <img alt="vertigo-hitchcock" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15310" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/vertigo-hitchcock.jpg" width="800"/>
            </a>
            <b>
             <i>
              The Thing
             </i>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             John Carpenters remake of
             <i>
              The Thing
             </i>
             is one of the most admired films of his career, as well as the horror genre in general. It has graced countless list recalling the best horror films of all time and often ranks right near the top. People regularlycite it as their favorite horror pictureof all time. But strangely enough, it isthe least financially successful movie of Carpenters entire career. With no exaggeration, the movie nearly killed the directorscareer. He lost jobs because of it. In fact, he was already slated to direct
             <i>
              Firestarter
             </i>
             right after
             <i>
              The Thing
             </i>
             wrapped, but he was let go when
             <i>
              Thing
             </i>
             was released. It may be almost universally loved now but this movie bombed in a big way.
             <i>
              The Thing
             </i>
             took in a US gross of roughly $14 Millioncompared to a budget of $15 Million. Carpenter and cinematographer Dean Cundey have said that the movie was unsuccessful because audiences werent ready for it. This was, after all, the summer of
             <i>
              E.T.
             </i>
             Audiences didnt want a mean alien, they wanted an alien that they could all relate to. They wanted to believe that they didnt need to watch the skies and that things were going to be okay, which is the opposite of the nihilistic themes running underneath Carpenters movie.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1" style="text-align: justify;">
            <span class="s1">
             <a href="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/the-thing-1982.jpg">
              <img alt="the-thing-1982" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-15312" height="400" src="http://wickedhorror.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/the-thing-1982.jpg" width="800"/>
             </a>
            </span>
           </p>
