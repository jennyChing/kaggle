      <a href="http://computershopper.com/feature/2010-ultimate-shopper-s-guide-how-to-buy-a-pc-power-supply">
       <img alt="Power Supply Guide" height="225" src="/var/ezwebin_site/storage/images/media/banners/2010-power-supply-guide-story-banner/582383-2-eng-US/2010-power-supply-guide-story-banner.jpg" style="border: 0px  ;" title="Power Supply Guide" width="560"/>

      <div id="feature">
       <h1>
        2010 Ultimate Guide: How to Buy a PC Power Supply
       </h1>
      </div>
      <div id="buyingguide">
       <h2>
        All About Juice: Wattage and Rails
       </h2>
       <p>
        The next consideration is choosing a supply with enough wattage to power all the components in your computer. Looking at individual components can give you a general idea how much power you need. For example, if your video card (often the most demanding component in a high-performance PC) specifies that it requires a 400-watt power supply, but you also have a demanding CPU (such as an older high-end Phenom or Core 2 Quad chip) and multiple hard drives, you'll want to go with a higher wattage rating. But assessing your needs that way is an inexact science.
       </p>
       <p>
        Another method: You can comb the support Web sites for the major components in your PCs; gather the individual power demands of the main parts, including your CPU, processor, and hard drives; and add those together to get a rough idea of how high a wattage your system requires. That works, but there's an easier way. A few power-supply calculators on the Internet can give you a fairly accurate appraisal of your power needs with little effort. All you do is select your components from a host of menus or checkboxes. You can find these by
        <a href="http://www.google.com/#hl=en&amp;source=hp&amp;q=%22power+supply+calculator%22&amp;aq=f&amp;aqi=g10&amp;aql=&amp;oq=&amp;fp=22b4dcbb1403dc0f" target="_blank">
         Googling for the phrase "power supply calculator."
        </a>
        A couple of good starter calculators are
        <a href="http://educations.newegg.com/tool/psucalc/index.html" target="_blank">
         NewEgg.com's excellent Power Supply Calculator
        </a>
        ...
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <div class="object-center noborder">
        <div class="content-view-embed-inline">
         <div class="noborder">
          <div class="attribute-image">
           <img alt="Power Supply Guide - Newegg power Supply Calculator" height="342" src="/var/ezwebin_site/storage/images/media/images/power-supply-guide-newegg-power-supply-calculator/583272-1-eng-US/power-supply-guide-newegg-power-supply-calculator_maxwidth.jpg" style="border: 0px  ;" title="Power Supply Guide - Newegg power Supply Calculator" width="550"/>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        ...and the longtime standard,
        <a href="http://extreme.outervision.com/psucalculatorlite.jsp" target="_blank">
         Outer Vision eXtreme's eXtreme Power Supply Calculator Lite
        </a>
        ...
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <div class="object-center noborder">
        <div class="content-view-embed-inline">
         <div class="noborder">
          <div class="attribute-image">
           <img alt="Power Supply Guide - Outer Vision Extreme power supply calculator" height="212" src="/var/ezwebin_site/storage/images/media/images/power-supply-guide-outer-vision-extreme-power-supply-calculator/583264-1-eng-US/power-supply-guide-outer-vision-extreme-power-supply-calculator_maxwidth.jpg" style="border: 0px  ;" title="Power Supply Guide - Outer Vision Extreme power supply calculator" width="550"/>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Outer Vision also offers a for-pay "Pro" version of its calculator that reports amperage demands (more on that below). Also, some power-supply manufacturers, such as
        <a href="http://corsair.com/psufinder/default.aspx" target="_blank">
         Corsair
        </a>
        and
        <a href="http://www.pcpower.com/Power_Supply_Selector.html" target="_blank">
         PC Power and Cooling
        </a>
        , offer product recommendations based on various configurations, as well.
       </p>
       <p>
        With one of the calculators, getting a ballpark wattage number is easy. But in addition, you'll want to take a look at the number of rails in a given supply, andmore cruciallythe amperage ratings of those rails. A rail, simply put, is an internal power path within a supply that provides a single voltage. A modern PC power supply will typically have a 3.3-volt rail, a 5-volt rail, and one or more 12-volt rails. The 12-volt rail is the critical one nowadays, as it's used by the most-demanding components of your PC: the processor and the graphics card.
       </p>
       <p>
        This all might seem confusing if you're not an electrical whiz, but it basically boils down to a simple fact: Each device that plugs into a 12-volt connector requires a certain amount of amperage. A power supply with a single rail will have a single amperage rating, while a multi-rail power supply will break down the amperage available on each rail.
        <a href="http://computershopper.com/best-prices/product/33749907//Corsair-HX750W---power-supply---750-Watt/availability" target="_blank">
         Corsairs single-rail HX750W
        </a>
        , for example, is rated to handle a maximum of 62 amps on its 12V+ rail.
        <a href="http://computershopper.com/best-prices/product/33560249//Antec-TP-750/availability" target="_blank">
         Antec's TP-850
        </a>
        , on the other hand, has four 12V+ rails rated for 25 amps each.
       </p>
       <p>
        The key difference, though: Multi-rail supplies cannot necessarily supply all the amps that their rails are rated for at one time. In the case of the TP-850, the total available amperage isn't actually the sum of the individual rails (which would add up to a whopping 100 amps), but the supplys "combined amperage" specification, which in this case is 62 amps. That combined amperage is doled out to different rails as needed. In the case of the TP-850, that could be as much as 25 amps on a rail, but the total available amps the power supply can provide at any given time is 62, not 100. So if two of the rails pulled a full 25 amps each at a certain moment, 12 amps would remain to split between the other two rails.
       </p>
       <p>
        Are multiple rails better than one high-amperage rail? Not necessarily. The ATX specification used to limit each individual 12-volt rail to 20 amps for safety purposes. However, when higher amperages turned out not to be a cause for concern, this limitation was dropped from the ATX spec. The number of rails mattered more when individual rails carried less power, as you could max out the available amperage on a single-rail system by connecting too many demanding components. Now that many single-rail power supplies have over 60 amps available on that one rail, that's much less of a concern.
       </p>
       <p>
        The total amps available for 12-volt devices, whether they're all on one rail or split between multiple rails, is what really matters. You need to understand the rails concept, however, to make sense of the specifications that power-supply vendors use when expressing amperage. On the packaging or specification sheets for most power supplies, you'll find a chart that breaks down the amperage available on each rail. Sometimes it's reproduced on the supply's label, as well, like on this Apevia supply, which has two 12-volt rails:
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <div class="object-center noborder">
        <div class="content-view-embed-inline">
         <div class="noborder">
          <div class="attribute-image">
           <img alt="Power Supply Guide - Power rails chart" height="290" src="/var/ezwebin_site/storage/images/media/images/power-supply-guide-power-rails-chart/583276-1-eng-US/power-supply-guide-power-rails-chart_maxwidth.jpg" style="border: 0px  ;" title="Power Supply Guide - Power rails chart" width="550"/>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        This information is also typically available on the manufacturer's Web site. But how to know what you need in terms of amps?
       </p>
       <p>
        Your graphics card will be the big reason that you need to pay attention to amperage demandsor not. Its the most demanding component, amperage-wise, in most PCs, so you'll want to check its own packaging or Web-site specifications for guidelines. For example, for the
        <a href="/components/reviews/asus-eah4870x2-graphics-card" target="_blank">
         Asus EAH4870X2
        </a>
        (a demanding dual-processor graphics card that uses the ATI Radeon HD 4870 X2 chipset), Asus recommends a 600-watt power supply with 40 amps of 12-volt current. The previously mentioned Corsair and Antec supplies are both sufficient for this 40-amp demand. On the flip side, if you're using motherboard-integrated graphics, odds are you won't need to concern yourself with amperage at all, as chipsets like these put little power demand on the system.
       </p>
