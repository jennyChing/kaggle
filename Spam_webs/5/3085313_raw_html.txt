         This Special Breading Is Why Japanese Deep Fried Food Tastes So Good

          <p>
           Ever wonder why
           <a href="http://looloo.com/explore?utf8=%E2%9C%93&amp;q=Japanese&amp;location=&amp;latitude=14.676041&amp;longitude=121.0437" target="_blank" title="Japanese restaurants from looloo">
            Japanese
           </a>
           deep-fried dishes like tonkatsu (a deep-fried pork cutlet) and korokke (deep-fried potato croquettes) have that nice crispy, crunchy texture?
          </p>
          <!-- In Post -->
          <div id="ad_unit_medium_rectangle_in_post">
           <script type="text/javascript">
            googletag.cmd.push(function() { googletag.display('ad_unit_medium_rectangle_in_post') });
           </script>
          </div>
          <p>
           Its because of the
           <strong style="font-size: 120%;">
            panko
           </strong>
           breading.
          </p>
          <p>
           Panko, pronounced as PAHN-ko, is a type of dry breadcrumb that the Japanese love to use to coat meat, fish, and vegetables.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://3c9bl93o71m619w9kn2rfwinkdh.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/kushiage.jpg">
            <img alt="Kushiage Breaded With Panko" class="alignnone wp-image-498 size-full" height="563" src="http://3c9bl93o71m619w9kn2rfwinkdh.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/kushiage.jpg" title="Kushiage Breaded With Panko" width="844"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Traditionally made from wheat flour, their flaky structure ensures that the crumbs dont compress, which creates a bread coating that is light and airy.
          </p>
          <p>
           What sets panko apart from regular bread crumbs is how its made.
          </p>
          <p>
           The bread is basically cooked by electrocution. An electric current is passed through the dough. The dough itself generates heat rather than the pan being heated by the oven.
          </p>
          <p>
           This method was supposedly created out of necessity. During World War II, the Japanese soldiers wanted to eat bread out in the battlefields. Unable to bake without an oven, the Japanese used the batteries from their
           <em>
            tanks
           </em>
           to bake their bread.
          </p>
          <p>
           What comes out is fluffy bread that has NO crust.
          </p>
          <p>
           The bread is then slightly dried and shredded which gives it its unique shape.
          </p>
          <p>
           Panko crumbs are long flat and thin and look spiky  like tiny flakes or shards. They look very different from regular bread crumbs which are round and chunky.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_495" style="width: 1034px">
           <a href="http://3c9bl93o71m619w9kn2rfwinkdh.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/panko-vs-breadcrumb.jpg">
            <img alt="Panko vs. Regular Bread Crumbs" class="wp-image-495 size-full" height="683" src="http://3c9bl93o71m619w9kn2rfwinkdh.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/panko-vs-breadcrumb.jpg" title="Panko vs. Regular Bread Crumbs" width="1024"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Photo from The Suburban Peasant
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           When fried, panko becomes crunchy and browns nicely.
          </p>
          <p>
           Check out photos ofpanko from
           <a href="https://looloo.com/r/2xqd" target="_blank" title="Ginza Bairin">
            Ginza Bairin
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="https://looloo.com/r/2l8m" target="_blank" title="Tonkatsu by Terazawa">
            Tonkatsu by Terazawa
           </a>
           , and
           <a href="https://looloo.com/r/2s5n" target="_blank" title="Yabu">
            Yabu
           </a>
           provided by looloo reviewers.
          </p>
          <p>
           Due to their shape, they stay crispy longer than regular bread crumbs. They also dont absorb as much oil so your food is not only crispy, its lighter in texture and taste less heavy and less greasy.
          </p>
          <p>
           I dont know why recipes would still want to use regular bread crumbs. Everything should be coated with panko.
          </p>
          <p>
           You end up with larger crumbs, making the food toastier and fluffier. You get a wonderfully tender, crispy bite with a lot of crunch.
          </p>

          </p>
