       <h1 class="entry-title">
        Magnetic Mud Science Experiment
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2014-07-15T12:09:39+00:00">
         July 15, 2014
        </span>
        by
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/author/rachel-2" rel="author">
           Rachel
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         In the past we have played with magnets. Weve
         <a href="http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/28674/make-a-compass" target="_blank">
          made our own compasses
         </a>
         and explored
         <a href="http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/9228/magnets-the-magic-dollar" target="_blank">
          dollar bills with magnets
         </a>
         . Recently though we discovered magnetic mud!!
        </p>
        <p>
         Have you ever heard of
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrofluid" target="_blank">
          ferrofluid?
         </a>
         Until a month or two ago I hadnt  boy was I missing out!
         <br/>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Today, we broke out our test tubes and left over iron oxide powder from when we
         <a href="http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/29800/diy-tinted-sunscreen-recipe" target="_blank">
          made our own tinted sunscreen
         </a>
         and made ferrofluid.
        </p>
        <p>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Our magnetic mudwould make a terrific science fair project!
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pibfi_pinterest ">
          <img alt="DIY ferrofluid a tutorial on how to make magnetic lava tubes" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-56356" height="974" src="//kidsactivitiesblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/DIY-ferrofluid-a-tutorial-on-how-to-make-magnetic-lava-tubes.jpg" width="650"/>
          <span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/56355/magnetic-mud-science-experiment&amp;media=http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/DIY-ferrofluid-a-tutorial-on-how-to-make-magnetic-lava-tubes.jpg&amp;description=Magnetic Mud Science Experiment')">
          </span>
         </span>
        </p>
        <h3>
         What is FerroFluid?
        </h3>
        <p>
         Ferrofluid is a suspension of tiny, tiny particles of metal that are responsive to magnetic fields (like iron or nickle) mixed intoan oil solution. It looks like a liquid and acts like a liquid, but as it contains little bits of magetic-responsive metal, it moves withmagnets. For our solution we used powdered rust (iron oxide).
        </p>
        <p>
         NOTE: You will want to be careful with kids in this experiment. Your kids should not eat the iron. As it is in powder form it *might* be able to be absorbed into the bloodstream if ingested and iron overdoses in kids can be very dangerous. Also, the iron oxide powder stains
         <em>
          everything.
         </em>
         You might want to mix it outdoors and with disposable containers. I mixed the iron and let the kids play with the test tubes when we were finished.
        </p>
        <p>
         *SO* cool. I think I played with it for a good twenty minuets when my kids were done. It is mesmerizing.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pibfi_pinterest ">
          <img alt="DIY ferrofluid a tutorial on how to make magnetic lava tubes" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-56357" height="974" src="//kidsactivitiesblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/DIY-Magnetic-Liquid.jpg" width="650"/>
          <span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/56355/magnetic-mud-science-experiment&amp;media=http://kidsactivitiesblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/DIY-Magnetic-Liquid.jpg&amp;description=Magnetic Mud Science Experiment')">
          </span>
         </span>
        </p>
        <h3>
         How to make ferrofluid (aka magnetic mud):
        </h3>
        <p>
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          Supplies  contains affiliate links:
         </span>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Vegetable Oil
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href='http://www.amazon.com/dp/B004OKDTX2/?tag=kidsactiblog-20 src="http://ws-na.amazon-adsystem.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&amp;ASIN=B004OKDTX2&amp;Format=_SL110_&amp;ID=AsinImage&amp;MarketPlace=US&amp;ServiceVersion=20070822&amp;WS=1&amp;tag=kidsactiblog-20' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Iron Oxide Powder
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          Disposable spoon and bowl
         </li>
         <li>
          Test Tube
         </li>
         <li>
          Face mask and/or gloves  you dont want to inhale the powdered metal or stain your hands.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Mix the iron oxide powder into 1/4 cup of oil. We mixed it a tablespoon at a time and stirred until it was completely mixed in, smooth, no clumps, but also a bit thick. Drop the mud into a test tube filled with water. Cork it and then use the magnet to move the iron mud around the tube. Use multiple magnets to see if you can get the blog to move in multiple directions at the same time.
        </p>
        <p>
         .
         <br/>
         Hope your kids have fun learning about metals, suspensions, magnetic qualities  all with our Iron Mud.
        </p>
