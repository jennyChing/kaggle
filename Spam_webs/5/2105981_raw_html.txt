     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Devil's Rejects
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2005 American
      <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">
       horror film
      </a>
      written and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Rob_Zombie" title="Rob Zombie">
       Rob Zombie
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">
       sequel
      </a>
      to his 2003 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_1000_Corpses" title="House of 1000 Corpses">
        House of 1000 Corpses
       </a>
      </i>
      . The film is centered on the run of three members of the psychopathic
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      antagonist
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Rob_Zombie_characters#Firefly_Family" title="List of Rob Zombie characters">
       family from the previous film
      </a>
      , now seen as
      <a href="/wiki/Antihero" title="Antihero">
       antiheroic
      </a>
      protagonists, with
      <a href="/wiki/Sid_Haig" title="Sid Haig">
       Sid Haig
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Moseley" title="Bill Moseley">
       Bill Moseley
      </a>
      and Zombie's wife
      <a href="/wiki/Sheri_Moon_Zombie" title="Sheri Moon Zombie">
       Sheri Moon Zombie
      </a>
      reprising their roles. At the time of its release and in the years since, the film has garnered a
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">
       cult following
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      This is the final film of
      <a href="/wiki/Matthew_McGrory" title="Matthew McGrory">
       Matthew McGrory
      </a>
      before his death the same year; the film is dedicated to his "loving memory".
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil%27s_Rejects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On May 18, 1978, Texas Sheriff John Quincey Wydell and a large posse of State Troopers issue a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_and_Destroy" title="Search and Destroy">
       Search and Destroy
      </a>
      mission on the Firefly family for over seventy-five
      <a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">
       homicides
      </a>
      and disappearances over the past several years. The family arm themselves and fire on the officers. Rufus is killed and Mother Firefly (
      <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Easterbrook" title="Leslie Easterbrook">
       Leslie Easterbrook
      </a>
      ) is taken into custody, while Otis and Baby escape. They steal a car, and after killing the driver they go to Kahiki Palms, a run-down motel.
     </p>
     <p>
      While at the motel, Otis and Baby take a band called Banjo and Sullivan hostage in their room, and Otis shoots the roadie when he returns. Meanwhile, Baby's father, Captain Spaulding, decides to rendezvous with Baby and Otis. En route, his truck runs out of gas and he assaults a woman before stealing her car. Back at the motel, Otis sexually assaults Roy's wife Gloria and demands Adam and Roy to come with him on an errand.
     </p>
     <p>
      Otis drives his two prisoners to a place where he buried weapons. While walking to the location, the two prisoners attack Otis, but he bludgeons Roy and cuts Adam's face off. Back at the motel, Adam's wife Wendy tries to escape out the bathroom window. When Gloria attempts to rebel, Baby kills her. Wendy runs out of the motel, but is caught by Captain Spaulding, who knocks her unconscious. Otis returns, and all three leave the motel together in the band's van.
     </p>
     <p>
      The motel maid comes to clean the room, and discovers the murder scene. The maid enters the bathroom, where she sees "The Devil's Rejects" written on the wall in blood, and is startled by Wendy, who is accidentally killed when she runs out to the highway to seek help. Wydell calls a pair of amoral bounty hunters  the "Unholy Two" , Rondo and Billy Ray, to help him find the Fireflys. While investigating, they discover an associate of Spaulding's named Charlie Altamont. Wydell begins to lose sanity when Mother Firefly reveals that she murdered his brother. After having a dream in which his brother asks him to avenge his death, Wydell stabs Mother Firefly to death. The surviving Fireflys gather at a brothel owned by Charlie, where he offers them shelter from the police.
     </p>
     <p>
      After he leaves the brothel, Wydell threatens Charlie to give up the Fireflys. With the help of the "Unholy Two", the sheriff takes the family back to the Firefly house where he tortures them, using similar methods they used on their own victims. He nails Otis' hands to his chair, and staples crime scene photographs to Otis's and Baby's stomach, then beats and shocks Captain Spaulding and Otis with a cattle prod, and taunts Baby about the death of her mother.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wydell sets the house on fire and leaves Otis and Spaulding to burn, but lets Baby loose outside so he can hunt her for sport. Charlie returns to save the Firefly family, but is killed by Wydell. Baby gets shot in the calf of her left leg, brutally horse-whipped, and then strangled by Wydell. Tiny suddenly arrives and intervenes, killing Wydell, and saving the Firefly family. Otis, Baby and Spaulding escape in Charlie's 1972
      <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_Eldorado" title="Cadillac Eldorado">
       Cadillac Eldorado
      </a>
      , leaving Tiny behind, who walks back into the burning house. The trio drive, badly injured and seemingly humbled by their experience, towards a police barricade. Refusing to surrender, Otis drives them towards the barricade, firing their guns, and all three are
      <a href="/wiki/Suicide_by_cop" title="Suicide by cop">
       shot to death by the police
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil%27s_Rejects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thissummergotohell.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="231" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/33/Thissummergotohell.jpg/220px-Thissummergotohell.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/33/Thissummergotohell.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/33/Thissummergotohell.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thissummergotohell.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Unused poster featuring Bill Moseley, Sheri Moon Zombie and Sid Haig.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      When
      <a href="/wiki/Rob_Zombie" title="Rob Zombie">
       Rob Zombie
      </a>
      wrote
      <i>
       House of 1000 Corpses
      </i>
      , he had a "vague idea for a story" about the brother of the sheriff that the Firefly clan killed coming back for revenge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobias_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tobias-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After
      <a href="/wiki/Lions_Gate_Entertainment" title="Lions Gate Entertainment">
       Lions Gate Entertainment
      </a>
      made back all of their money on the first day of
      <i>
       Corpses'
      </i>
      theatrical release, they wanted Zombie to make another film and he started to seriously think about a new story.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobias_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tobias-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With
      <i>
       Rejects
      </i>
      , Zombie has said that he wanted to make it "more horrific" and the characters less cartoonish than in
      <i>
       Corpses
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobias_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-tobias-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and that he wanted "to make something that was almost like a violent western. Sort of like a road movie."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutman_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lutman-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film was set in the mid-late 1970s. He has also cited films like
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Bunch" title="The Wild Bunch">
        The Wild Bunch
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_and_Clyde_(film)" title="Bonnie and Clyde (film)">
        Bonnie and Clyde
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Badlands_(film)" title="Badlands (film)">
        Badlands
       </a>
      </i>
      as influences on
      <i>
       Rejects
      </i>
      . When he approached William Forsythe about doing the film, he told the actor that the inspiration for how to portray his character came from actors like
      <a href="/wiki/Lee_Marvin" title="Lee Marvin">
       Lee Marvin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Shaw_(British_actor)" title="Robert Shaw (British actor)">
       Robert Shaw
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutman_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lutman-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sheri Moon Zombie does not see the film as a sequel: "It's more like some of the characters from
      <i>
       House of 1000 Corpses
      </i>
      came on over, and now they're the Devil's Rejects."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carnell_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-carnell-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Zombie hired Phil Parmet, who had shot the documentary
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harlan_County_USA" title="Harlan County USA">
        Harlan County USA
       </a>
      </i>
      , because he wanted to adopt a hand-held camera/documentary look.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutman_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-lutman-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Principal photography was emotionally draining for some of the actors. Sheri Moon Zombie remembers a scene she had to do with Forsythe that required her to cry. The scene took two to three hours to film and affected her so much that she did not come into work for two days afterward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lutman_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-lutman-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Rejects
      </i>
      went through the
      <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">
       MPAA
      </a>
      eight times earning an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NC-17" title="NC-17">
       NC-17
      </a>
      rating every time until the last one.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ridley_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ridley-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Zombie, the censors had a problem with the overall tone of the film. Specifically, censors did not like the motel scene between Bill Moseley and Priscilla Barnes, forcing Zombie to cut two minutes of it for the theatrical release. However, this footage was restored in the DVD version.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ridley_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ridley-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack">
       Soundtrack
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil%27s_Rejects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Soundtrack">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/The_Devil%27s_Rejects_(soundtrack)" title="The Devil's Rejects (soundtrack)">
       The Devil's Rejects (soundtrack)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Rob Zombie, who is a musician, decided to go with more southern rock to create the mood of the film. The
      <a href="/wiki/The_Devil%27s_Rejects_(soundtrack)" title="The Devil's Rejects (soundtrack)">
       soundtrack
      </a>
      itself was notable as being one of the first to be released on
      <a href="/wiki/DualDisc" title="DualDisc">
       DualDisc
      </a>
      , with the DVD side featuring a making-of featurette for the film and a photo gallery.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Release">
       Release
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil%27s_Rejects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">
       Box office
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil%27s_Rejects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Devil's Rejects
      </i>
      was released on July 22, 2005 in 1,757 theaters and grossed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">
       USD
      </a>
      $7.1 million on its opening weekend, recouping its roughly $7 million budget. It grossed $17 million in North America and $2.3 million internationally for a total of $19.4 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boxoffice_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-boxoffice-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
       Critical reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Devil%27s_Rejects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The film had mixed reviews with a 53% rating on
      <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">
       Rotten Tomatoes
      </a>
      , with the consensus "Zombie has improved as a filmmaker since
      <i>
       House of 1000 Corpses
      </i>
      and will please fans of the genre, but beware  the horror is nasty, relentless and sadistic";
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a 53 metascore on
      <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">
       Metacritic
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Prominent critic
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
       Roger Ebert
      </a>
      enjoyed the film and gave it three out of a possible four stars. He wrote, "There is actually some good writing and acting going on here, if you can step back from the [violent] material enough to see it".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert1_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ebert1-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, in his review for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Hills_Have_Eyes_(2006_film)" title="The Hills Have Eyes (2006 film)">
        The Hills Have Eyes
       </a>
      </i>
      , Ebert referenced
      <i>
       The Devil's Rejects
      </i>
      , writing, "I received some appalled feedback when I praised Rob Zombie's
      <i>
       The Devil's Rejects
      </i>
      , but I admired two things about it [that were absent from
      <i>
       The Hills Have Eyes
      </i>
      ]: (1) It desired to entertain and not merely to sicken, and (2) its depraved killers were individuals with personalities, histories and motives".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert2_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ebert2-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In his review for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      , Peter Travers gave
      <i>
       The Devil's Rejects
      </i>
      three out of four stars and wrote, "Let's hear it for the Southern-fried soundtrack, from
      <a href="/wiki/Buck_Owens" title="Buck Owens">
       Buck Owens
      </a>
      ' 'Satan's Got to Get Along Without Me' to
      <a href="/wiki/Lynyrd_Skynyrd" title="Lynyrd Skynyrd">
       Lynyrd Skynyrd
      </a>
      's 'Free Bird', playing over the blood-soaked finale, which manages to wed
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Bunch" title="The Wild Bunch">
        The Wild Bunch
       </a>
      </i>
      to
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thelma_and_Louise" title="Thelma and Louise">
        Thelma and Louise
       </a>
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travers_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Travers-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Roeper" title="Richard Roeper">
       Richard Roeper
      </a>
      gave the film "thumbs up" for being successful at its goal to be the "sickest, the most twisted, the most deranged movie" at that point of the year (2005).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In her review for the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">
        New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dana_Stevens_(critic)" title="Dana Stevens (critic)">
       Dana Stevens
      </a>
      wrote that the film "is a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Trompe-l%27%C5%93il" title="Trompe-l'il">
        trompe-l'il
       </a>
      </i>
      experiment in deliberately retro film-making. It looks sensational, but there is a curious emptiness at its core".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stevens-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">
        Entertainment Weekly
       </a>
      </i>
      gave the film a "C+" rating and wrote, "Zombie's characters are, to put it mildly, undeveloped".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gleiberman_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gleiberman-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Robert K. Elder, of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">
        Chicago Tribune
       </a>
      </i>
      , disliked the film, writing "[D]espite decades of soaking in bloody classics such as the original
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Texas_Chain_Saw_Massacre" title="The Texas Chain Saw Massacre">
        Texas Chainsaw Massacre
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/I_Spit_on_Your_Grave" title="I Spit on Your Grave">
        I Spit on Your Grave
       </a>
      </i>
      , Zombie didn't absorb any of the underlying social tension or heart in those films. He's no collage artist of influences, like
      <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">
       Quentin Tarantino
      </a>
      , crafting his movie from childhood influences.
      <i>
       Rejects
      </i>
      plays more like a junkyard of homages, strewn together and lost among inept cops, gaping plot holes and buzzard-ready dialog".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elder_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Elder-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Horror author
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">
       Stephen King
      </a>
      voted
      <i>
       The Devil's Rejects
      </i>
      the 9th best film of 2005 and wrote, "No redeeming social merit, perfect '70s C-grade picture cheesy glow; this must be what Quentin Tarantino meant when he did those silly
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kill_Bill" title="Kill Bill">
        Kill Bill
       </a>
      </i>
      pictures".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-King-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      James Berardinelli gave
      <i>
       The Devil's Rejects
      </i>
      half a star (out of a possible four stars) and called it a "vile, reprehensible movie," saying the action was "more formula than plot." He described the dialogue as "a pastiche (at least I think that's the intention) of the kind of bloodthirsty, overripe lines found" in a genre of films from the 1970s about "outcasts who defy society by destroying it." He was extremely critical of the acting and the production values, with an ending that was "a cataclysmic misfire", and overall was not "engaging cinema."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
