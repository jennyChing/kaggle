   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    <i>
     Apple Corps v Apple Computer
    </i>
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v._Apple_Computer&amp;redirect=no" title="Apple Corps v. Apple Computer">
       Apple Corps v. Apple Computer

     </span>
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      Between 1978 and 2006 there were a number of legal disputes between
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Apple_Corps" title="Apple Corps">
        Apple Corps
       </a>
      </b>
      (owned by
      <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">
       The Beatles
      </a>
      ) and the computer manufacturer
      <b>
       Apple Computer
      </b>
      (now
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">
       Apple Inc.
      </a>
      ) over competing
      <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">
       trademark
      </a>
      rights. The
      <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">
       High Court of Justice
      </a>
      handed down a judgment on 8 May 2006 in favour of Apple Computer, but the companies did not announce a final settlement until 5 February 2007.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_trademark_disputes">
       History of trademark disputes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v_Apple_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of trademark disputes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1978.E2.80.931981">
       19781981
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v_Apple_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 19781981">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1978,
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Corps" title="Apple Corps">
       Apple Corps
      </a>
      , the Beatles-founded
      <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">
       holding company
      </a>
      and owner of their record label,
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Records" title="Apple Records">
       Apple Records
      </a>
      , filed a lawsuit against Apple Computer for
      <a href="/wiki/Trademark_infringement" title="Trademark infringement">
       trademark infringement
      </a>
      . The suit was settled in 1981 with an undisclosed amount being paid to Apple Corps. This amount was estimated to be US$50250 million, but was later revealed to be $80,000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BW-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a condition of the settlement, Apple Computer agreed not to enter the music business, and Apple Corps agreed not to enter the computer business.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LEM_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LEM-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1986.E2.80.931989">
       19861989
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v_Apple_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 19861989">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1986, Apple Computer added
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_instrument_digital_interface" title="Musical instrument digital interface">
       MIDI
      </a>
      and audio-recording capabilities to its computers, which included putting the advanced Ensoniq 5503 DOC sound chip from famous synthesizer maker
      <a href="/wiki/Ensoniq#Multimedia_Sound_Cards_.26_Semiconductors" title="Ensoniq">
       Ensoniq
      </a>
      into the
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIGS#Hardware_features" title="Apple IIGS">
       Apple II
       <small>
        GS
       </small>
      </a>
      computer. In 1989, this led Apple Corps to sue again, claiming violation of the 1981 settlement agreement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LEM_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-LEM-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The outcome of this litigation effectively spelled the end of any further development of the highly profitable
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_II_series" title="Apple II series">
       Apple II
      </a>
      line, all forays at the time by Apple Computer into the multimedia field in parallel with the
      <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">
       Amiga
      </a>
      , and any future advanced built-in musical hardware in the
      <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">
       Macintosh
      </a>
      line.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1991">
       1991
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v_Apple_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1991">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1991, another settlement involving payment of around $26.5 million to Apple Corps was reached.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milesp581-582_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Milesp581-582-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This time, an Apple employee named
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Reekes" title="Jim Reekes">
       Jim Reekes
      </a>
      had included a sampled system sound called
      <i>
       Chimes
      </i>
      to the Macintosh operating system (the sound was later renamed to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sosumi" title="Sosumi">
        sosumi
       </a>
      </i>
      , to be read phonetically as "so sue me"). Outlined in the settlement was each company's respective trademark rights to the term "Apple". Apple Corps held the right to use Apple on any "creative works whose principal content is music", while Apple Computer held the right to use Apple on "goods or services... used to reproduce, run, play or otherwise deliver such content", but not on content distributed on physical
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recording_medium" title="Recording medium">
       media
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In other words, Apple Computer agreed that it would not package, sell or distribute physical music materials.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2003.E2.80.932006">
       20032006
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v_Apple_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20032006">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In September 2003, Apple Corps sued Apple Computer again, this time for
      <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">
       breach of contract
      </a>
      , in using the Apple logo in the creation and operation of Apple Computer's
      <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">
       iTunes Music Store
      </a>
      , which Apple Corps contended was a violation of the previous agreement. Some observers believed the wording of the previous settlement favoured Apple Computer in this case.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BW_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BW-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other observers speculated that if Apple Corps was successful, Apple Computer would be forced to offer a much larger settlement, perhaps resulting in Apple Corps becoming a major shareholder in Apple Computer, or perhaps in Apple Computer splitting the iPod and related business into a separate entity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The trial opened on 29 March 2006 in England,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      before a
      <a href="/wiki/Trial_court" title="Trial court">
       single judge
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">
       High Court
      </a>
      . In opening arguments, a lawyer for Apple Corps stated that in 2003, shortly before the launch of Apple Computer's on-line music store, Apple Corps rejected a $1 million offer from Apple Computer to use the Apple name on the iTunes store.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 8 May 2006 the court ruled in favour of Apple Computer,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with Justice Edward Mann holding that "no breach of the trademark agreement [had] been demonstrated".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hmc_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hmc-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Judge focused on section 4.3 of that agreement:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       4.3 The parties acknowledge that certain goods and services within the Apple Computer Field of Use are capable of delivering content within the Apple Corps Field of Use. In such case, even though Apple Corps shall have the exclusive right to use or authorize others to use the Apple Corps Marks on or in connection with content within subsection 1.3(i) or (ii) [the Apple Corps catalogue and any future music], Apple Computers  [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
         sic
        </a>
       </i>
       ] shall have the exclusive right to use or authorize others to use the Apple Computer Marks on or in connection with goods or services within subsection 1.2 [Apple Computer Field of Use] (such as software, hardware or broadcasting services) used to reproduce, run, play or otherwise deliver such content provided it shall not use or authorize others to use the Apple Computer Marks on or in connection with physical media delivering pre-recorded content within subsection 1.3(i) or (ii) (such as a compact disc of the Rolling Stones music).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hmc_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hmc-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The Judge held Apple Computer's use was covered under this clause.
     </p>
     <p>
      In response,
      <a href="/wiki/Neil_Aspinall" title="Neil Aspinall">
       Neil Aspinall
      </a>
      , manager of Apple Corps, indicated that the company did not accept the decision: "With great respect to the trial judge, we consider he has reached the wrong conclusion", and announced that it would "be filing an appeal and putting the case again to the
      <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">
       Court of Appeal
      </a>
      ". The judgment orders Apple Corps to pay Apple Computer's legal costs at an estimated UK2 million, but pending the appeal the judge declined Apple Computer's request for an interim payment of 1.5 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The verdict spawned the
      <a href="/wiki/Guy_Goma" title="Guy Goma">
       Wrong Guy
      </a>
      incident on BBC News 24, in which a job applicant mistakenly appeared on air after he was confused with computing expert
      <a href="/wiki/Guy_Kewney" title="Guy Kewney">
       Guy Kewney
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2007">
       2007
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apple_Corps_v_Apple_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2007">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There was a hint that relations between the companies were improving at the January 2007
      <a href="/wiki/Macworld" title="Macworld">
       Macworld
      </a>
      conference, when Apple
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CEO" title="CEO">
       CEO
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">
       Steve Jobs
      </a>
      featured Beatles content heavily in his
      <a href="/wiki/Keynote" title="Keynote">
       keynote
      </a>
      presentation and demonstration of the
      <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">
       iPhone
      </a>
      . During that year's All Things Digital conference, Jobs quoted the Beatles song "
      <a href="/wiki/Two_of_Us_(song)" title="Two of Us (song)">
       Two of Us
      </a>
      " in reference to his relationship with co-panelist
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">
       Microsoft
      </a>
      chairman
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">
       Bill Gates
      </a>
      . Speculation abounded regarding the much anticipated arrival of the Beatles' music to the
      <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">
       iTunes Store
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      On 5 February 2007, Apple Inc. and Apple Corps announced a settlement of their trademark dispute under which Apple Inc. will own all of the trademarks related to "Apple" and will license certain of those trademarks back to Apple Corps for their continued use. The settlement ends the ongoing trademark lawsuit between the companies, with each party bearing its own legal costs, and Apple Inc. will continue using its name and logos on iTunes. The settlement includes terms that are confidential, although newspaper accounts at the time stated that Apple Computer was buying out Apple Corps' trademark rights for a total of $500 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Commenting on the settlement, Steve Jobs, Apple's CEO said, "We love the Beatles, and it has been painful being at odds with them over these trademarks. It feels great to resolve this in a positive manner, and in a way that should remove the potential of further disagreements in the future."
     </p>
     <p>
      Commenting on the settlement on behalf of the shareholders of Apple Corps, Neil Aspinall, manager of Apple Corps said, "It is great to put this dispute behind us and move on. The years ahead are going to be very exciting times for us. We wish Apple Inc. every success and look forward to many years of peaceful co-operation with them."
     </p>
     <p>
      An announcement in April 2007 that Apple Corps had settled another long-running dispute with
      <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">
       EMI
      </a>
      (and that Neil Aspinall had retired and been replaced by
      <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Jones_(music_industry_executive)" title="Jeff Jones (music industry executive)">
       Jeff Jones
      </a>
      ) further fueled media speculation that The Beatles' catalogue would appear on iTunes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In early September 2007, an Apple press release for the new iPod touch, related iPod updates, and iPhone price cut was entitled "The Beat Goes On", the title of the Beatles' last press release before splitting up. Although Beatles content was still unavailable from the iTunes store, each Beatle's solo work could be accessed and downloaded on this service.
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">
       Paul McCartney
      </a>
      was quoted in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      as saying that their catalogue would be released through digital music stores such as iTunes in the first quarter of 2008,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but this did not happen until 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
