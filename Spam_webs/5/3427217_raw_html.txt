    <h2 id="post-2857">
     <a href="http://allfinancialmatters.com/2008/10/06/this-guy-sounds-exactly-like-me/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link: This Guy Sounds Exactly Like Me!">
      This Guy Sounds Exactly Like Me!
     </a>
    </h2>
    <p>
     <b>
      By JLP
     </b>
     | October 6, 2008
    </p>
    <div class="postspace2">
    </div>
    <!--content with more link-->
    <p>
     The Letters to the Editor section in todays Wall Street Journal contained the following response to Thomas Franks
     <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB122282690823092989.html" target="_blank" title="The GOP Blames the Victim">
      piece of drivel
     </a>
     (
     <em>
      free
     </em>
     ) from last Wednesday:
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      Ultimately, the responsibility is to the borrower, not everyone else. I was amazed in 2006 when my mortgage broker informed me of the amount of money I qualified for when purchasing my first home. Just because I was qualified for that amount did not mean I could afford a home in that price range. I settled on a modest home in a modest community  and peace of mind. People who bite off more than they can chew often choke.
     </p>
     <p>
      Michael H. Flanary
      <br/>
      Fairfield, Conn.
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     EXACTLY!  I couldnt have said it any better myself (probably because Ive already said it in the past!).
    </p>
    <p>
     I urge you to read Thomas Franks
     <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB122282690823092989.html" target="_blank" title="The GOP Blames the Victim">
      opinion piece
     </a>
     .  It drives me nuts when people call so many adults victims when they were the ones who signed all those forms at their mortgage loans origination.  When a borrower signs that contract, they are obligatedit doesnt matter if they read and understood the contract or not.  They made a choice when they signed the forms.
    </p>
    <p>
     Yes, Wall Street did its part in helping create this mess.  Im not denying that.  Wall Street created demand for subprime loans and mortgage brokers bit.  HOWEVER, it was still the borrowers who had the final say in the matter.  Had borrowers practiced prudence, no mess would have been made.
    </p>
    <p>
     Now, if the borrower signed the forms while the mortgage broker was holding a gun to their head, then yeah, they could be considered a victim.  Im also sure some fraud took place in which the mortgage broker falsified documents or outright lied to the borrower.  These borrowers could also be considered victims.
    </p>
    <p>
     That said, painting all borrowers who took out mortgages that they couldnt afford as victims, is just crazy.  Its also a sad statement of what America has becomea nation of whiney victims.
    </p>
    <p>
     The REAL victims in this mess are those of us who did the right thing and borrowed what we could afford to borrow and purchased homes we could afford.  Now we are being forced to subsidize and feel sorry for those who made poor decisions.
    </p>
