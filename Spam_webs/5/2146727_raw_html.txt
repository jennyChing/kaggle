      <p>
       Seitz's grocery store on Ridge Road was an old time hotspot for the Webster community to come get food, drink and the latest town news.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now, the old Seitz's building is back in the Webster spotlight as Knucklehead Craft Brewing  celebrated its grand opening Saturday.
      </p>
      <p>
       The idea was to start a local microbrewery with an urban, family atmosphere that would beckon the community to come socialize, said co-owner Len Dummer, who hatched the idea with friend George Cline.
      </p>
      <p>
       Local craft breweries are cropping up all over the U.S. as customers are looking to support local business and have a unique cuisine experience with their loved ones. The Rochester area has seen at least four new breweries in the last year alone.
      </p>
      <p>
       "This should be an environment where people want to have a conversation," Dummer said, adding that bar standbys like televisions and pool tables were not part of his plans. "This is a neighborhood place where we can give back to the community."
      </p>
      <p>
       He and Cline were both home brewers before getting to work on their dream. They opened in December and started small, with five barrels and six different "Ales with Attitude" on tap  from the crisp Kathy's Kreme ale to the dark and sweet Knockout Stout.
      </p>
      <p>
       "People are starting to use our beer names when they order here, which is neat," said Cline. When they were looking for a building to house the brewery, they stumbled upon the old Seitz's building  "It gave me goosebumps, because I used to ride past here on my paper route when I was a boy," Cline said.
      </p>
      <p>
       When Knucklehead first opened, customers were scrambling to experience the new and only craft brewery in town.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I didn't expect the crowds," said Cline. "It was an absolute zoo, and people were saying, 'we've been needing something like this.'"
      </p>
      <p>
       Ed and Carol Seitz, who ran Seitz's until 2003 when they sold the building and retired, were there to experience Saturday's official opening ceremony. Cline and Dummer remodeled much of the building's interior, but kept an old grocery cooler behind the bar, an original stove hood in the back kitchen, and named one of their ales the "Out-of-Seitz" Ale.
      </p>
      <p>
       When the owners came to Ed Seitz with the Knucklehead idea, "I told them it couldn't fail," he said. The family atmosphere is reminiscent of the old grocery store  "all the family was here doing something, building something, decorating the walls...I wish them all the best," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Dummer's wife Kathy  and two sons keep the beer flowing behind the bar, and Cline's wife Amy and daughter Alyssa lend their help as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       Kathy Dummer said she was on board with the idea from the beginning  "I told Len, 'go for it,'" she said. "It's been a long process, but we've been truly blessed."
      </p>
      <p>
       A host of government officials were there Saturday  some with beer in hand  to welcome the new business to the community.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I live right around the corner, and I've watched people show up here in waves," said Monroe County Executive Maggie Brooks. "This speaks 'local' to mepeople making a great product and sharing it with our community."
      </p>
      <p>
       Old and new visitors from around Rochester flowed into the bar throughout the opening ceremony, sampling beers and chatting with friends.
      </p>
      <p>
       "A place like this keeps people actually talking, instead of staring at televisions," said Phil Keyser,  of Rochester, who stopped in with his girlfriend Kim Fischer's family.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rochester resident Andrew Wojewodzic  has been riding the craft brewery trend for a while, but said Knucklehead stands out for both its beer and atmosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I've been to a lot of craft breweries, but this one is top-notch," he said. "There's something for everyone to like."
      </p>
      <p>
       Dummer said the brewery is hoping to add food and multiple seasonal drafts to its repertoire in the future, and is looking to "trade" some brews with other local breweries so that customers can get exposure to a variety of local brewing talent.
      </p>
      <p>
       Dummer, who ascribes to the Christian faith, said Knucklehead couldn't have succeeded without divine providence, and that he's honored to revitalize one of Webster's greatest historical neighborhood spots.
      </p>
      <p>
       "It's great to be making this a Webster landmark once again," he said.
      </p>
