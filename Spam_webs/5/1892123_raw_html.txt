         <h2 class="title">
          Black Scholes Model Probabilities. The difference between N(d1) and N(d2)
         </h2>
         <header class="details clearfix">
          <time class="detail left index-post-date" datetime="2011-03-04">
           Mar 04, 2011
          </time>
          <span class="detail left index-post-author">
           <em>
            by
           </em>
           <a href="http://financetrainingcourse.com/education/author/jawwad/" rel="author" title="Posts by Jawwad Farid">
            Jawwad Farid
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="detail left index-post-category">
           <em>
            in
           </em>
           <a href="http://financetrainingcourse.com/education/category/black-scholes/" title="View all posts in Black Scholes">
            Black Scholes
           </a>
          </span>
         </header>
         <!-- END .details -->
         <div class="single-content">
          <h1>
           Understanding the difference between N(d1) and N(d2)
          </h1>
          <p>
           Lars Tyge Nielsen provides an interpretation of N(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ) and N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) and an explanation behind the difference between them. He does this by considering the value ofEuropean call option on a stock which pays no dividends prior to the expiry date of the option as given by the following formula:
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            C= SN(d
            <sub>
             1
            </sub>
            )  Xe
            <sup>
             -rt
            </sup>
            N(d
            <sub>
             2
            </sub>
            )
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Where C is value of the European call option
           <br/>
           S is the current value of the stock price
           <br/>
           X is the exercise price
           <br/>
           t is the remaining time to expiry
           <br/>
           r is the risk free rate of interest
           <br/>
           N(.) is the cumulative standard normal distribution, the risk adjusted probabilities.
          </p>
          <p>
           To understand this process the payoff of the call option is divided into 2 components, the payment of exercise price and the receipt of stock. Both of these are dependent on the option beingexercised which will only occur if the option is in the money or alternatively when the stock price on the exercise date, S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           , rises above the exercise price, X, i.e.
          </p>
          <p>
           P(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)
          </p>
          <p>
           The expected value and present value of each component is then calculated:
          </p>
          <h2>
           Payment of Exercise Price and N(d2)
          </h2>
          <p>
           The expected value of payment of the exercise price is exercise price times probability of stock price exceeding exercise price (probability of exercise):
          </p>
          <p>
           -X* P(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)
          </p>
          <p>
           The present value of the expected value of payment of the exercise price is determined by discounting this expected value using the risk free interest rate over the time remaining to expiry of
          </p>
          <p>
           the option:
          </p>
          <p>
           -X* P(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* e
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           Hence comparing this with the second portion of the call option value equation above,
          </p>
          <p>
           -X* P(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* e
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
           =  Xe
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
           N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ),
          </p>
          <p>
           we see that N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) = P(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X), i.e. N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) is the risk adjusted probability that the option will be exercised.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Receipt of stock and N(d1)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Explanation of N(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ) is a bit more complex. We begin with the expected value of the contingent receipt of stock.
          </p>
          <p>
           The expected value of the receipt of stock is contingent on exercise of the option. It is therefore the product of the conditional expected value of the receipt of S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           given that
          </p>
          <p>
           exercise has occurred times the probability of exercise:
          </p>
          <p>
           Statistically this is written as
          </p>
          <p>
           E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* P(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)
          </p>
          <p>
           This equation can also be written as follows:
          </p>
          <p>
           E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           Note that the first term in this equation is a conditional expectation. It is the expected value of S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           given that we are now only considering those future values of S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
          </p>
          <p>
           which exceed the exercise price X. If this constraint were not added we would have E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           )= Se
           <sup>
            rt
           </sup>
           the unconditional expectation of S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           . Given the
          </p>
          <p>
           conditionality therefore E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X) will always be greater than E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           ). We may understand this concept through the following simple probability
          </p>
          <p>
           example.
          </p>
          <p>
           A six sided fair die is rolled. The probability of rolling 4 is 1/6. Now suppose we have additional information that tells us that the number rolled is greater than 3. In this instance what is the
          </p>
          <p>
           probability of a 4 having being rolled given that the number rolled is greater than 3. The conditional probability works out to 1/3 &gt; 1/6. The conditional probability is higher because we are
          </p>
          <p>
           only considering those outcomes that exceed three in our calculation.
          </p>
          <p>
           In a similar fashion E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X) &gt; E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           ) because the expected value will only consider those stock prices which exceeds the exercise price in
          </p>
          <p>
           the calculation of the expectation. Hence E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) &gt; E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           )* N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )=
          </p>
          <p>
           Se
           <sup>
            rt
           </sup>
           *N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           Let us now consider the present value of this expected value by discounting it with the risk free rate over the time remaining to option expiry. We have:
          </p>
          <p>
           E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )* e
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
           &gt; E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           )* e
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
           *N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )= S*N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           Comparing the left hand side of this inequality with first portion of the Black Scholes equation for the call option, SN(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ), we have:
          </p>
          <p>
           SN(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           )= E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           |S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X)* N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )* e
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
           &gt; E(S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           )* e
           <sup>
            -rt
           </sup>
           *N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) =S*N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           In other words N(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ) ensures that the discounted expected value of the contingent stock price received on exercise will be greater than this current value ofstock.
          </p>
          <p>
           Also see 
           <a href="http://financetrainingcourse.com/education/2012/11/understanding-black-scholes-formula-an-intuitive-derivation-of-nd2/" target="_blank">
            An intuitive derivation of N(d2) using the Black Scholes model
           </a>
            a post the covers an alternate derivation of N(d2) and includes an easy to follow video lecture that walk through that derivation step by step.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Difference between N(d1) and N(d2)
          </h2>
          <p>
           SN(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ) &gt; SN(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) ? N(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ) &gt; N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           As mentioned above N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) is simply the risk adjusted probability that the option will be exercise. Its linkage to X suggests that it only depends on when the event
          </p>
          <p>
           S
           <sub>
            T
           </sub>
           &gt;X occurs.
          </p>
          <p>
           On the other hand N(d1) will always be greater than N(d2) because in linking it with the contingent receipt of stock in the Black Scholes equation, N(d
           <sub>
            1
           </sub>
           ) must not only account forthe probability of exercise as given by N(d
           <sub>
            2
           </sub>
           ) but must also account for the fact that exercise or rather receipt of stock on exercise is dependent on the
           <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
            conditional
           </span>
           future values that the stock price takes on the expiry date. In particular this means stock prices being greater than the exercise price aretaken as a given condition when calculating the expected future value of stock on the expiry date.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="/education/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/nd1nd211.gif">

           </a>
          </p>
