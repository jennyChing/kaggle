         <p>

         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="And now, for the moment we've all been waiting for (Sean MacEntee / Flickr)" class="" height="213" src="https://farm5.staticflickr.com/4124/4996958736_83037acfa9_z.jpg" width="640"/>

         </div>
         <p>
          College is a huge decision for any young adult to make -- and with admission deadlines coming up, it's time to make that decision.
         </p>
         <p>
          Feeling overwhelmed? Deep breaths. There are four basic things to think about when deciding what college you want to attend.
         </p>
         <h2>
          1. Budget
         </h2>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="Oof. Private four-year college is good for approximately no one's wallet. (Tax Credits / Flickr)" class="" height="427" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5200/7408472762_c54bd3fb0b_z.jpg" width="640"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Oof. Private four-year college is good for approximately no one's wallet. (Tax Credits / Flickr)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          No one really realizes how expensive college actually is until they've gotten their acceptance letter and looked at the form outlining everything. HOLY CRAP.
         </p>
         <p>
          Unless you pick a
          <a href="http://gokicker.com/2015/01/09/obama-says-community-college-free-good-students/" target="_blank" title="Obama says community college should be free for good students">
           community college
          </a>
          , you won't get away with paying less than $15,000 a year ... and that's if you're lucky.
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course, there are some things that can help -- but I'm using "help" in the same way a two year old "helps" clean-up after dinner.
         </p>
         <p>
          The government will give you financial aid, sure. Have you ever looked at what that aid looks like, though? Loans. They're mostly loans.
         </p>
         <p>
          For some reason, the government has this silly idea that if your parents make the amount of money that your tuition requires annually, that money is earned exclusively to cover your tuition and not silly things like the mortgage, cars, bills, groceries, and so on. Nope.
         </p>
         <p>
          As far as the government's concerned, all those dollars will just go toward your college tuition. So the government doesn't need to give you money -- they can just give you loans. Thanks, guys.
         </p>
         <p>
          Scholarships are always a possibility, but they often only help a little because most people don't receive enough to cover their whole tuition. So when making your final college decision, keep in mind that your loans will have to be paid back.
         </p>
         <p>
          And remember: Just because a college is cheaper than another doesn't mean it's not as good. Grand Valley (where I go) is a little over $10,000 per year -- if you don't live on campus -- and it's an amazing school. Which brings me to my next point.
         </p>
         <h2>
          2. Proximity
         </h2>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="See ya, Mom. (gfpeck / Flickr)" class="" height="427" src="https://farm9.staticflickr.com/8011/7286335852_f0bd671362_z.jpg" width="640"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           See ya, Mom. (gfpeck / Flickr)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Any graduating high school student you talk to about college has one main criteria: Get as far away from their parents as possible.
         </p>
         <p>
          Look, everyone wants to move far, far away and never look back -- until the time actually comes to do that. Out-of-state schools are super expensive, plus chances are good that you will end up missing your friends and family. I know people who live an hour away and barely manage.
         </p>
         <p>
          Proximity to your childhood home is a huge factor to consider, and it should not be taken lightly. After all, the farther away you go, the more expenses you stand to rack up, including travel to and from campus and room and board. You really need to think about whether you can afford to live away from home.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you're going to school a bit closer, check to see if you have to live on campus. Some schools, like Michigan State University, require you to live on campus the first year, no matter how close you may live to Lansing. I live 20 minutes away from GVSU and refuse to waste money on campus housing. I'm mooching off of my parents for as long as I can -- and for the record, I'm still in debt.
         </p>
         <p>
          A lot of people will argue that you don't get "the full college experience" if you commute, but it's really not that bad. I totally get wanting to go out and party, but are parties really worth an extra 10 grand (or more) a year? I've been to plenty of parties and had plenty of classic college experiences, but I've done it for a lot cheaper than most.
         </p>
         <p>
          But much as money and proximity matter, don't forget you can't just pick a school because it's affordable. It has to actually offer you what you need, too.
         </p>
         <h2>
          3. Programs
         </h2>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="This kid knows what's up (or whoever made the meme does, anyway). (edtechworkshop / Flickr)" class="" height="276" src="https://farm3.staticflickr.com/2785/5850264509_3799442f0e_z.jpg" width="640"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           This kid knows what's up (or whoever made the meme does, anyway). (edtechworkshop / Flickr)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Unless you are psychic (or one of those rare people who's known what they want to be when they grow-up since kindergarten), you probably won't really know what you want to do with the rest of your life until you're actually in college.
         </p>
         <p>
          You'll think you do ... but then you'll sign up for some courses and have a panic attack as you realize that your chosen course of study is most definitely not how you want to spend your time. Worry not. This is a pretty standard experience.
         </p>
         <p>
          Because of this, though, it's important to pick a college that offers majors you are actually interested in. You don't need to narrow down your exact career path, just have a general idea of what you would like and what you wouldn't like.
         </p>
         <p>
          I got accepted into a school that mainly had business and medical programs. I suck at science and I hate business, so I obviously didn't go to that school even though it was the cheapest one of them all. While I didn't really know what I wanted to do, I definitely knew I would never want any of that.
         </p>
         <p>
          I considered all three of these things when I decided on Grand Valley. My dream school was Michigan State, but even with loans I couldn't afford to go there and live on campus (nor could I commute). On the flip side, Grand Valley offers pretty much the same programs, so I made the decision that made the most sense. Yes, I sometimes think about what my life would be like if I went to MSU, but I love my life at GVSU and I'll be a Laker for a lifetime.
         </p>
         <p>
          Which brings me to the big question to ask yourself:
         </p>
         <h2>
          4. Do I even want to go to college?
         </h2>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="You do you, OK? There's no one way to make your way in this world. (TORLEY / Flickr)" class="" height="480" src="https://farm4.staticflickr.com/3385/3674050796_d332b3b1af_o.png" width="640"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           You do you, OK? There's no one way to make your way in this world. (TORLEY / Flickr)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Stop thinking about if your parents, friends, or teachers want you to go.
          <strong>
           Think about what
           <em>
            you
           </em>
           want.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          If you have no interest in going to college, then don't! I always say education is never a waste of time, but it can be a waste of money.
         </p>
         <p>
          There is nothing wrong with not going to college. There are some great career opportunities for people who choose not to go, like trade school or enlisting in a government service or just finding a good paying job and hitting the work force.
         </p>
         <p>
          Guys,
          <a href="http://gokicker.com/2015/02/05/community-college-free-one-idea-college-must-change/" target="_blank" title="Before Community College Is Free, One Idea About College Must Change">
           college does not guarantee a wonderful job with a big paycheck
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          A college degree does not define your worth as a person, nor does it better or lessen you in comparison to others. There is no shame in attending a community college, a cheaper college that's closer to home, a crazy expensive college, or not attending college at all.
         </p>
         <p>
          Do what you want to do, what you think will make you the happiest. If someone can't understand that, then that's their issue, not yours.
         </p>
         <p>
          This is a big decision that will effect the rest of your life, so choose wisely and choose for yourself, not other people. Good luck!
         </p>

         </p>
