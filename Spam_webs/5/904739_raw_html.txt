        <h2>
         Mission Questions not Mission Statements
        </h2>
        <div class="clear-block">
         <div class="node" id="node-200">
          <span class="submitted">
           Posted by Michelle Holliday on 25 Oct 2014
          </span>
          <div class="content clear-block">
           <p>
            <img align="right" alt="" border="10" height="216" hspace="10" src="/sites/default/files/black%20white%20and%20red%20question.jpg" vspace="10" width="300"/>
            What if companies had mission
            <em>
             questions
            </em>
            instead of mission
            <em>
             statements
            </em>
            ? How much more engaging and inviting would this be than the typical bland pronouncements about being number one or being the best?
            <em>
             Why
            </em>
            do you want to be number one? What learning, discoveries and milestones  what unfolding story  is to be found on that path? What are you all wildly curious about? What compels you to come together in this work because you cant gain enough insight into the story alone?
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            Such a mission question (or, more likely, a set of them) implies that there is clear intention but also much that is unknown. And though this is an expression of vulnerability, it is not an admission of weakness. Instead, it is likely to be a source of strength and deep engagement. As storytelling master
            <a href="http://www.getstoried.com/about/team/">
             Michael Margolis
            </a>
            advises: "
            <strong>
             Your greatest source of untapped power is the part of your story that is unreconciled
            </strong>
            ."
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            This matches with my own experience. For the past ten years, my work has often involved helping organizations craft a manifesto - a concise expression of who they are, what they want, and what has to be true if they are to get what they want. The highly participatory and inquisitive process of getting there is always vital and engaging. But looking back, I realize that the subsequent process of creating a definitive statement always feels noticeably less alive. Instead of excited co-creation, there is debate. People wonder whether the document truly describes everyone involved, whether they can really claim to be
            <em>
             all that
            </em>
            , or whether they should be so pretentious as to publicly announce their loftiest aspirations. In contrast, a mission question seems more likely to be able to hold diversity even as it builds unity, to demonstrate humility alongside audacity, and to invite a broad community to see themselves in it  to recognize that it is also
            <em>
             their
            </em>
            question.
            <strong>
             The thing that makes people feel really alive and connected is being in the questions together.
            </strong>
            When that comes to a close, so does the life in their interactions.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <img align="left" alt="" border="10" height="240" hspace="10" src="/sites/default/files/color/question%20in%20between%20people.jpg" vspace="10" width="340"/>
            All of this makes perfect sense within the context of thrivability. When we see an organization as a living system, employees, customers and partners cant truly be considered separate from the organization. With this understanding, we see that the most appropriate and powerful relationship with them is one of ongoing conversation and co-creation. And any good conversation is, in essence, a genuinely curious collective exploration of an unanswered question. Its the living process of crafting a story together - of being in shared service of its unfolding.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            My friend
            <a href="https://www.facebook.com/kingofthesusans?fref=ts">
             Cameron Stiff
            </a>
            and I played with the idea of a mission question yesterday during a walk through the woods. His company,
            <a href="http://www.compostmontreal.com">
             Compost Montreal
            </a>
            , has the stated aim of eliminating the concept of waste. Each week, they pick up food scraps from houses and offices, diverting the material from landfills and converting it into useful compost. But the deeper potential Cameron senses for the company is the exploration of how composting can serve as a metaphor for the transformation that people are capable of, especially together. What if we embraced the concept of rot and deathand also renewal? he wondered, with growing excitement. What if every aspect of our business were in service of that exploration? For example, how could our hiring practices and our ways of working together serve that question? And what kinds of conversations could we engage our customers and community in?
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            <img align="right" alt="" border="10" height="283" hspace="10" src="/sites/default/files/color/question%20at%20end%20of%20road.jpg" vspace="10" width="278"/>
            When I think about the most powerful companies in the world, I struggle to imagine what their questions might be, beyond those of self-interested domination and profit for its own sake. My friend
            <a href="https://www.facebook.com/flemming.funch?fref=ts">
             Flemming Funch
            </a>
            had an astute take on this. The presence of clear uncertainty about outcomes might possibly be a way of discerning whether a company is up to some good or not, he proposed. If it is truly trying to solve a problem and do something that hasn't been done, great. If not, it is highly suspect, and probably just a cover for somebody's money machine.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            
            <strong>
             That we don't know how it will end,
            </strong>
             he says, 
            <strong>
             means that we're alive.
            </strong>
            So we should embrace the unknown and the discovery process, as that's the real interesting part, not something we should try to hide away.
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            All of this is likely to take some practice, and more than a little support. But what if we just start with a few questions of our own, like:What niggling curiosity do you have about where your work might take you? What's ripe with possibility in the unfolding story of your work?
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
            [Here is my favorite resource for crafting questions:
            <a href="http://www.principals.ca/documents/powerful_questions_article_(World_Cafe_Website).pdf">
             The Art of Powerful Questions
            </a>
            , by Eric E. Vogt, Juanita Brown and David Isaacs.]
           </p>
           <p class="MsoNormal">
           </p>

            <div class="content">
             <p>
              I love love the idea of a mission question. Here is a statement I made last week about living in a question:
             </p>
             <p>
              "I realized a while back that I am living in a question. In a question, there is tension, instability until there is a resolution. It's like when you play a B flat or C sharp or any musical note that is not natural. You know there is something coming after... That is what it is like to live in a (quest)ion. I am in a question."
             </p>
             <p>
              A mission question is actually more true than a mission statement because a question indicates a quest, which is what a mission is. One of my favorite quotes is, "in those places where we are most alive, we are questions not answers." And I agree that a question is more inviting and engaging than a statement. I would honestly rather use a mission question than a mission statement. It suggests we do not have the answers and we are on a quest to find it. A question is open and expansive. A statement is somewhat closed. AT their very essence, questions invite participation. Statements can be exclusive. Having said that, questions can be restrictive/exclusive too depending on how they are asked.
             </p>
             <p>
              I would think our mission questions at Zenith Cleaning include:
             </p>
             <p>
              What is cleaning?
              <br/>
              What is dirt?
              <br/>
              How can we use cleaning as a transformational activity?
              <br/>
              How can the practice of cleaning unite rather than divide?
              <br/>
              How can the act of cleaning transform and connect hearts and minds?
              <br/>
              What does it mean to clean an industry? - construction, healthcare, banking, energy, gaming, high tech, etc
              <br/>
              What is the connection between cleaning and creativity/innovation?
              <br/>
              How does love (compassion and empathy) affect business and society?
              <br/>
              How does an expanded understanding of the nature of reality affect business and society?
             </p>
             <p>
              I think the idea of a mission question is genius. Go Michelle!
             </p>

             <p>
              Mission questions are a great idea and seems to really resonate with innovative businesses. After I wrote about this idea in my book A More Beautiful Question (March 2014), it got picked up by a lot of thought leaders, and then I explored it more in articles I wrote for Forbes, BusinessWeek, Fast Company, and on the A More Beautiful Question blog (
              <a href="http://amorebeautifulquestion.com/?s=mission+question" title="http://amorebeautifulquestion.com/?s=mission+question">
               http://amorebeautifulquestion.com/?s=mission+question
              </a>
              ). My hope is that companies will actually start trying to restructure their mission statements as questions, and your championing of this idea with your audience should help. It's early days yet for this idea but please let me know if you hear of any company actually formally changing their statement to a question.
             </p>
