                            Too late for what?
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            If you slept through your 26th birthday, it's too late for you to experience
                            <i>
                             that
                            </i>
                            . It's too late for you to watch "LOST" in its premiere broadcast. (Though, honestly, you didn't miss much.) It's too late for you to fight in the Vietnam War. It's too late for you to go through puberty or attend nursery school. It's too late for you to learn a second language as proficiently as a native speaker*. It's probably too late for you to be breastfed.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It's not too late for you to fall in love.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It's not too late for you to have kids.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It's not too late for you to embark on an exciting career or series of careers.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It's not too late for you to read the complete works of Shakespeare; learn how to program computers; learn to dance; travel around the world; go to therapy; become an accomplished cook; sky dive; develop an appreciation for jazz; write a novel; get an advanced degree; save for your old age; read "In Search of Lost Time"; become a Christian, then an atheist, then a Scientologist; break a few bones; learn how to fix a toilet; develop a six-pack ...
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Honestly, I'm 47, and I'll say this to you, whippersnapper: you're a fucking kid, so get over yourself. I'm a fucking kid, too. I'm almost twice your age, and I'm just getting
                            <i>
                             started
                            </i>
                            ! My dad is in his 80s, and he wrote two books last year.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             You don't get to use age as an excuse. Get off your ass!
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Also, learn about what economists call "sunk costs." If I give someone $100 on Monday, and he spends $50 on candy, he'll probably regret that purchase on Tuesday. In a way, he'll still think of himself as a guy with $100half of which is wasted.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            What he really is is a guy with $50, just as he would be if I'd handed him a fifty-dollar bill. A sunk cost from yesterday should not be part of today's equation. What he should be thinking is this: "What should I do with my $50?"
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            What you
                            <i>
                             are
                            </i>
                            isn't a person who has wasted 27 years. You are a person who has X number of years ahead of you. What are you going to do with them?
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <hr class="qtext_hr"/>
                            <br/>
                            * What I'd intended as a throwaway comment, about the difficulty of second-language acquisition after childhood, has generated interest and disagreement. I will admit upfront I am not an expert on the matter, and was mostly informed by research I'd read about.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It claimed there's a window of childhood, after which the brain stops being able to hear certain soundsone's not used by a child's native languagewhich is why it's so hard to learn to speak a second language without an accent. Some people may master it, but not many. (How many people do you know, after 25, learned a foreign language and can speak it so well, natives have no idea they're listening to a foreigner?) It's also challenging to learn all the idiomatic expressions that native speakers have known since they were small children.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            However, since having written this answer, I've learned that the Science behind this is very controversial. As I'm not an expert, let me refer you to the wikipedia article (and it's linked resources).
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            "In second-language acquisition, the strongest evidence for the critical period hypothesis is in the study of accent, where most older learners do not reach a native-like level. However, under certain conditions, native-like accent has been observed, suggesting that accent is affected by multiple factors, such as identity and motivation, rather than a critical period biological constraint (Moyer, 1999; Bongaerts et al., 1995; Young-Scholten, 2002)."
                            <br/>

                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in_feed" master_h="320" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-ddc2d95abc1ac04e9c42721cbe13cf7e?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="480" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-ddc2d95abc1ac04e9c42721cbe13cf7e?convert_to_webp=true"/>

                            <br/>
                            I have been looking at this question for over 6 months now just getting inspired by the comments and conversation going on in this thread. It's even more funny because I was referenced this post after I wrote a blog post called
                            <span class="qlink_container">
                             <a class="external_link" href="https://medium.com/better-humans/4d1d8fae13e9" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "medium.com")' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                              Is it too late to be awesome?
                             </a>
                            </span>
                            - which ended being pretty popular and when I came here I saw why.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Perhaps this is a shameless plug, or a cry out to start a support group for those in this thread,
                            <b>
                             but I ended up pitching the idea to SXSW
                            </b>
                            , and I will be speaking there this conference around the topic, and those planning on being there this year, I welcome you to come. (more details at the end of this post).
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <hr class="qtext_hr"/>
                            <i>
                             Here's my take on this topic:
                            </i>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            In June 2013, a conversation was had between friends around those that we admire in the tech/entrepreneur space. Included in those were the 'usual suspects', Kevin Rose, Tim Ferriss, Seth Godin, Dustin Curtis, etc. Before long, there was a lot of questioning and lamenting around it being too late to be awesome. Out of this conversation, a blog post was born that ended up being one of the top shared blogs in June on
                            <span class="qlink_container">
                             <a class="external_link" href="http://Medium.com" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "medium.com")' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                              Medium
                             </a>
                            </span>
                            . We were surprised to learn just how many people were affected by this feeling of 'unaccomplishment' due to a wide variety of confidence-killing factors.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            When you are content to be simply yourself and dont compare or compete, everyone will respect you.
                            <br/>
                             Tao Te Ching
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            When we dig a little deeper than the surface of fighting ourselves mentally, we can start to identify unhealthy relationships that have been formed with comparison and perfectionism. Being authentic is important, and without it, these poisons can form and us back from creating, connecting, or simply just being our natural selves. We want to "make a difference", "make our mark", or "be awesome", and the answer is not in "what more we can do", but rather "how can we come alive?".
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            With distractions of other's perceived success, information overload, and other societal pressure, we must slow down, discover ourselves and learn how to be comfortable in our own skin. Then  and only then  will we be able to get out of our own way, and achieve true happiness by "doing awesome".
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            "Don't ask what the world needs; ask what makes you come alive, and go do it. Because what this world needs is more people who have come alive."
                            <br/>
                             Howard Thurman
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Don't get me wrong, I'm still struggling (and I'm 31), and while I have done some cool things, I still don't consider it awesome enough to feel accomplished, and maybe that's just "it" right? Maybe WE may never feel that way, but outside perspectives are never fully understood by us.

                            Baby steps.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Forget about the next hour. Forget about the entirety of the day ahead.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Ask yourself, what is one small step that I can take right now?
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            For example, you might say, "Do the homework for my easiest class."
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Ok, that's a start. But let's make it even easier.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Cut your baby step in half. "Do the reading for my easiest class, but not the problem set yet."
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Are you willing to do the reading? Does it feel crazy easy, as easy as peeling an orange or stretching?
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            If not, cut in half again. "Find the reading for my easiest class."
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Ok, maybe that feels doable. You are willing to get up, locate the book on the shelf, and bring it back to your chair.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            That's it. You're done. Rest until you find that you want to take another baby step.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            This approach is a way of easing the worries of a part of your brain that is somehow terrified to begin.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It doesn't seem like it could ever work, but it's surprisingly powerful to take even a micro-step in the direction that scares you.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Hope this helps,

                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            These days we very often see stories like this [
                            <span class="qlink_container">
                             <a class="external_link" href="http://goo.gl/IRIR5" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "goo.gl")' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                              Meet the 17-Year-Old Who Is Reinventing News
                             </a>
                            </span>
                            ].
                            <br/>
                            Exceptionally young people hitting it big or successful people telling of how they started out in their teens. Mark Zuckerberg at 20, Richard Branson at 16
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            As we don't get to read the stories of the billions of 27 year olds who haven't done 'big things' by their age it becomes very easy to feel like you've missed the boat.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            The important things that I remind myself every day are:
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             We should never ever define ourselves as a comparison to someone else.
                            </b>
                            Doing so will only mean we're forever a follower and never a leader (that is, a leader of our own lives; let alone other peoples).
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It is fine to feel like we've not truly maximised our lives so far, but how lucky and fantastic it is to be able to recognise those feelings at such a young age. Many people go through life with the same feelings, but simply don't bring themselves to stop and recognise them until much later in life.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            The fact that you are even asking this question (and posting it here) demonstrates that you've recognised it, and doing so means you can act on it. We have the capacity to actively stop ourselves from having to ask this same question when we're 50.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            I actually find it helps to visualise writing such a question as a 50 or 60 year old. Imagine you're 50 and thinking the same things you're thinking now. What does wasted time actually mean? What haven't you done? What haven't you felt? Where haven't you been? Try and imagine the actual emotions you'd be feeling as you'd write that at that time. Write down the things that stick out the strongest, the things you feel like you've missed the most.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Once you've done that, you have the awesome ability to do what no actual 50 or 60 year old can do..
                            <b>
                             rewind time 20-30 years and plonk it in front of you!
                            </b>
                            <i>
                             You've given yourself the ultimate gift.
                            </i>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            I find this gives me an awesome perspective in which I realise that we really are just kids and that for us,
                            <i>
                             life is just beginning.
                            </i>

                           <div id="__w2_OZ6gwut_container">
                            Unfortunately for 'real' people, the media is obsessed with the tiny minority who succeed early and display this very publicly.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            This is then amplified by the high profile 'subject', for PR purposes, to perpetuate their success.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Justin Bieber, Michael Jackson, Britney Spears, River Phoenix, Justin Timberlake, Bill Gates, Jimi Hendrix, Steve Jobs, James Dean, Richard Branson, Whitney Houston, One Direction, Amy Winehouse, Mark Zuckerburg. Need I go on.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             Notice a trend in there somewhere?
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            You are probably being influenced, (like all of us), in how you assess your own progress, compared to these people. It can be a dangerous game to play.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            It takes a tremendous amount of luck, as well as talent, to get into the right position at the right time. Not many people who make it will tell you that, often preferring to put it down to their hard work. That is because they believe that this is the case, not because they are intentionally misleading you. I know that because, to an extent, I've done it.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            You also may not have considered that even if you were on the list of young successes. It is very hard to follow that early success later in life. Your expectations of yourself are higher and based on that youthful virtual reality you experienced once, you can never improve on your past.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            That can be a tough pill to swallow and despite all the money in the world, many struggle with that.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Look at any list of young successes from just 10 years ago and count the number who have disappeared, died or been in rehab.
                            <b>
                             Lots.
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Half the list of super successful people above are dead for a start off.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             This is not an excuse for you to give up trying however.
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Try to think of life as a long road journey.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             The journey can be as exciting or as boring as you choose to make it.
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Wherever you are on the journey, there are new experiences, as long as you welcome them and seek them out. Some you can plan in advance.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Often, you need to get out of the car to experience them. Otherwise, you will see them flash past the window and feel like it is too late to stop.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <ul>
                             <li>
                              Do something every day which contributes to your progress on the journey and always be learning and experiencing new things.
                             </li>
                            </ul>
                            <br/>
                            <ul>
                             <li>
                              Don't put off experiences which can be done today by getting out of the car, for a tomorrow which may never arrive.
                             </li>
                            </ul>
                            <br/>
                            <ul>
                             <li>
                              Build a vision of where you want to get to in 1, 5 and 10 years and then think about the steps you need to complete in the next 30 days to move towards it, but don't set deadlines that are too harsh. Do the first step on the list today.
                             </li>
                            </ul>
                            <br/>
                            <ul>
                             <li>
                              Like any long journey, you will hit diversions, obstacles, traffic lights, speed bumps, closed roads and all manner of other problems. There will be crashes - you might be involved in them. Like any long road journey, if you want to get the destination enough, you won't turn back, you will reroute. The car might break down or need repairing. Just accept it will happen now, and carry on.
                             </li>
                            </ul>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             Most importantly, don't wait for all the traffic lights between your house and your destination, to turn green at the same time, before you set off.
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             They won't!
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             Get in the car and start driving.
                            </b>
