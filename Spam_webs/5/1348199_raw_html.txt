   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    John Fare
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       John Fare
      </b>
      (sometimes
      <b>
       John Charles Fare
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       John Fahey
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       John Far
      </b>
      ) is a fictional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performance_artist" title="Performance artist">
       performance artist
      </a>
      who allegedly used robotic surgery to remove parts of his body onstage as part of his act. His final performance was allegedly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicide_by_beheading" title="Suicide by beheading">
       suicide by beheading
      </a>
      . The story originated in 1968 and is generally considered an
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">
       urban legend
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cramer_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cramer-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drake2004_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-drake2004-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parfrey1990_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-parfrey1990-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_for_story">
       Sources for story
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Fare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources for story">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The original version was "The Hand" by N.B. Shein, published in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Insect_Trust_Gazette" title="Insect Trust Gazette">
        Insect Trust Gazette
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1968.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shein1968_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shein1968-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston1970_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boston1970-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In November 1972, Tim Craig published a plagiarized and embellished version of Shein's original story in reply to a letter to the editor of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Studio_International" title="Studio International">
        Studio International
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The reader was inquiring about an artist named Fahey who ended his career by having his head amputated onstage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steinberg_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-steinberg-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Craig's embellished and plagiarized version of Shein's original, John Charles Fare was born in 1936 in Toronto and attended
      <a href="/wiki/Forest_Hill_College" title="Forest Hill College">
       Forest Hill College
      </a>
      . In 1959 he moved to London to study architecture at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartlett_School_of_Architecture" title="Bartlett School of Architecture">
       Bartlett School of Architecture
      </a>
      , but soon left to live in
      <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">
       Copenhagen
      </a>
      . He was briefly held in a mental health facility for exposing himself in public at performances. After his release, he was re-arrested for gluing objects to a car. The car's owner, musician and inventor Golni Czervath, did not press charges and befriended Fare. The two developed a robotic operating table with painter Gilbert Andoff. The first performance was a lobotomy on Fare in June 1964. All performances were performed on a Friday. By the time Fare performed at the Isaacs Gallery in Toronto on 17 September 1968, he "was short one thumb, two fingers, eight toes, one eye, both testicles, and several random patches of skin." The amputated parts were preserved in alcohol. That evening, he had his right hand amputated. Fare's body was fitted with small microphones, which transmitted his pulse and breathing frequency in a distorted fashion. Craig said Fare had performed six more shows between 1968 and 1972.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1985
      <a href="/wiki/Danny_Devos" title="Danny Devos">
       Danny Devos
      </a>
      wrote to Isaacs Gallery founder
      <a href="/wiki/Avrom_Isaacs" title="Avrom Isaacs">
       Avrom Isaacs
      </a>
      enquiring about John Fare and his supposed performance in 1968. The response included a statement in writing that the story of John Fare "has no factual basis," adding "there was no such person as John Fare as far as I know."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isaacs1985_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-isaacs1985-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tool_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tool-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Response">
       Response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Fare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The story was reprinted in a
      <a href="/wiki/Fanzine" title="Fanzine">
       fanzine
      </a>
      made in collaboration with the band
      <a href="/wiki/Coil_(band)" title="Coil (band)">
       Coil
      </a>
      in 1987.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tool_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tool-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      That publication included more correspondence with Isaacs, who said, "I know of no such person as John Fare. In the sixties I had a series of mixed media concerts in my gallery, and out of this came the myth of John Fare. Every five years or so, someone rediscovers the myth and writes me a letter such as yours."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tool_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-tool-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fare's alleged performance was emulated during a
      <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal_Emissions" title="Nocturnal Emissions">
       Nocturnal Emissions
      </a>
      concert in London in 1997.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Writing about the event, a British music journalist recounts: "Fare cuts an eccentric figure. He wears trousers made from zips and has a diagram of a brain tattooed onto his shaven scalp. The performance artist placed his left hand on a chopping board with the fingers spread. Fares assistant, Jill Orr, is partially sighted and she slammed an axe between her boyfriends pinkies with increasing speed. Eventually the axe severed Fares little finger. This was the end of the performance art element within the evenings entertainment".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fare has been mentioned in connection with
      <a href="/wiki/Body_art" title="Body art">
       body art
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      industrial culture,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the practices of
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Schwarzkogler" title="Rudolf Schwarzkogler">
       Rudolf Schwarzkogler
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Flanagan" title="Bob Flanagan">
       Bob Flanagan
      </a>
      , and, like other performance artists, has been seen as a successor of the
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_martyrs" title="Christian martyrs">
       Christian martyrs
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He has also been mentioned in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardian_(newspaper)" title="Guardian (newspaper)">
        Guardian
       </a>
      </i>
      in connection with the German artist
      <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Schneider" title="Gregor Schneider">
       Gregor Schneider
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Critic Audrone Zukauskaite examined the durability of this legend in
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Art_Lies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Art Lies (page does not exist)">
        Art Lies
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zukauskaite_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zukauskaite-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Artists
      <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Lester" title="Gabriel Lester">
       Gabriel Lester
      </a>
      , Mariana Castillo Deball, "the estate of John Fare," Ren Gabri, Mario Garcia Torres, and Juozas Laivys explored themes of the story in a 2007 Gallery GB Agency exhibit in France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lesauvage_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lesauvage-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While Lester acknowledged Fare was "an apparition, an artist thought to have existed," he said Fare embodied the culmination of romantic myth of the artist cursed, "someone who has probably never existed, and yet lives forever."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lesauvage_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lesauvage-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
