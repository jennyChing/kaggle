   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Aircraft principal axes
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>

       Yaw, pitch, and roll
      </a>

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      This article is about yaw, pitch, and roll as symmetry axes of a plane.  For meaning in mechanics, see
      <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia#Principal_axes_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">
       Moment of inertia#Principal axes of inertia
      </a>
      .  For Euler angles with the same names, see
      <a href="/wiki/Euler_angles#Tait-Bryan_angles" title="Euler angles">
       Euler angles#Tait-Bryan angles
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="533" data-file-width="709" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg/250px-Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg/375px-Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg/500px-Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yaw_Axis_Corrected.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The position of all three axes
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">
       aircraft
      </a>
      in flight is free to rotate in three dimensions:
      <i>
       pitch
      </i>
      , nose up or down about an axis running from wing to wing;
      <i>
       yaw
      </i>
      , nose left or right about an axis running up and down; and
      <i>
       roll
      </i>
      , rotation about an axis running from nose to tail. The axes are alternatively designated as
      <i>
       lateral
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       vertical
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       longitudinal
      </i>
      . These axes
      <a href="/wiki/Moving_frame" title="Moving frame">
       move with the vehicle
      </a>
      and rotate relative to the Earth along with the craft. These definitions were analogously applied to
      <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
       spacecraft
      </a>
      when the first manned spacecraft were designed in the late 1950s.
     </p>
     <p>
      These rotations are produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
       torques
      </a>
      (or
      <a href="/wiki/Moment_(physics)" title="Moment (physics)">
       moments
      </a>
      ) about the principal axes. On an aircraft, these are produced by means of moving control surfaces, which vary the distribution of the net
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic">
       aerodynamic
      </a>
      force about the vehicle's
      <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
       center of mass
      </a>
      .
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elevator_(aircraft)" title="Elevator (aircraft)">
       Elevators
      </a>
      (moving flaps on the horizontal tail) produce pitch, a
      <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">
       rudder
      </a>
      on the vertical tail produces yaw, and
      <a href="/wiki/Aileron" title="Aileron">
       ailerons
      </a>
      (flaps on the wings that move in opposing directions) produce roll. On a spacecraft, the moments are usually produced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Reaction_control_system" title="Reaction control system">
       reaction control system
      </a>
      consisting of small rocket thrusters used to apply asymmetrical thrust on the vehicle.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_axes">
       Principal axes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft_principal_axes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principal axes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw,_Pitch_%26_Roll.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1570" data-file-width="1080" height="581" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw%2C_Pitch_%26_Roll.png/400px-MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw%2C_Pitch_%26_Roll.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw%2C_Pitch_%26_Roll.png/600px-MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw%2C_Pitch_%26_Roll.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw%2C_Pitch_%26_Roll.png/800px-MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw%2C_Pitch_%26_Roll.png 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MISB_ST_0601.8_-_Yaw,_Pitch_%26_Roll.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Yaw/heading, pitch and roll angles and associated vertical (down), transverse and longitudinal axes
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Normal_axis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Normal axis (page does not exist)">
        Normal axis
       </a>
       , or yaw axis  an axis drawn from top to bottom, and perpendicular to the other two axes. Parallel to the
       <i>
        fuselage station
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateral_axis" title="Lateral axis">
        Lateral axis
       </a>
       , transverse axis, or pitch axis  an axis running from the pilot's left to right in piloted aircraft, and parallel to the wings of a winged aircraft. Parallel to the
       <i>
        buttock line
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longitudinal_axis" title="Longitudinal axis">
        Longitudinal axis
       </a>
       , or roll axis  an axis drawn through the body of the vehicle from tail to nose in the normal direction of flight, or the direction the pilot faces. Parallel to the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Waterline" title="Waterline">
         waterline
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Normally, these axes are represented by the letters X, Y and Z in order to compare them with some reference frame, usually named x, y, z. Normally, this is made in such a way that the X is used for the longitudinal axis, but there are
      <a href="/wiki/Axes_conventions" title="Axes conventions">
       other possibilities
      </a>
      to do it.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_axis_.28yaw.29">
       Vertical axis (yaw)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft_principal_axes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vertical axis (yaw)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The vertical
      <b>
       yaw axis
      </b>
      is defined to be
      <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
       perpendicular
      </a>
      to the wings with its origin at the center of gravity and directed towards the bottom of the aircraft. Yaw moves the nose of the aircraft from side to side. A positive yaw, or heading angle, moves the nose to the right.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref1_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ref1-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The rudder is the primary control of yaw.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clancy_16.6_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Clancy_16.6-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lateral_axis_.28pitch.29">
       Lateral axis (pitch)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft_principal_axes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lateral axis (pitch)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       pitch axis
      </b>
      (also called
      <b>
       lateral
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       transverse axis
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MISB_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MISB-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) passes through the plane from wingtip to wingtip. Pitch moves the aircraft's nose up and down. A positive pitch angle raises the nose and lowers the tail. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elevator_(aircraft)" title="Elevator (aircraft)">
       elevators
      </a>
      are the primary control of pitch.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clancy_16.6_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Clancy_16.6-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Longitudinal_.28roll.29">
       Longitudinal (roll)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft_principal_axes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Longitudinal (roll)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       roll axis
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       longitudinal axis
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MISB_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MISB-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) passes through the plane from nose to tail. The angular displacement about this axis is called
      <b>
       bank
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clancy_16.6_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Clancy_16.6-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The pilot changes bank angle by increasing the lift on one wing and decreasing it on the other. A positive roll angle lifts the left wing and lowers the right wing. The
      <a href="/wiki/Aileron" title="Aileron">
       ailerons
      </a>
      are the primary control of bank. The
      <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">
       rudder
      </a>
      also has a secondary effect on bank.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_other_systems_of_axes">
       Relationship with other systems of axes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft_principal_axes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship with other systems of axes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      These axes are related to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_axis_(mechanics)" title="Principal axis (mechanics)">
       principal axes of inertia
      </a>
      , but are not the same. They are geometrical symmetry axes, regardless of the mass distribution of the aircraft.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In aeronautical and aerospace engineering intrinsic rotations around these axes are often called
      <a href="/wiki/Euler_angles" title="Euler angles">
       Euler angles
      </a>
      , but this conflicts with existing usage elsewhere. The calculus behind them is similar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Frenet%E2%80%93Serret_formulas" title="FrenetSerret formulas">
       FrenetSerret formulas
      </a>
      . Performing a rotation in an intrinsic reference frame is equivalent to right-multiplying its characteristic matrix (the matrix that has the vector of the reference frame as columns) by the matrix of the rotation.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft_principal_axes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first aircraft to demonstrate active control about all three axes was the
      <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">
       Wright brothers
      </a>
      '
      <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers#1902_Glider" title="Wright brothers">
       1902 glider
      </a>
      .
