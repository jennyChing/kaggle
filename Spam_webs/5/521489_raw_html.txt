     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Hans Brinker, or The Silver Skates
       </b>
      </i>
      (full title:
      <i>
       Hans Brinker; or, the Silver Skates: A Story of Life in Holland
      </i>
      ) is a novel by American author
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Mapes_Dodge" title="Mary Mapes Dodge">
       Mary Mapes Dodge
      </a>
      , first published in 1865. The novel takes place in the
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Netherlands
      </a>
      and is a colorful fictional portrait of early 19th-century Dutch life, as well as a tale of youthful honor.
     </p>
     <p>
      The book's title refers to the beautiful
      <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
       silver
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_skate" title="Ice skate">
       skates
      </a>
      to be awarded to the winner of the
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_skating" title="Speed skating">
       ice-skating race
      </a>
      Hans Brinker hopes to enter. The novel introduced the sport of Dutch
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_skating" title="Speed skating">
       speed skating
      </a>
      to Americans, and in U.S. media Hans Brinker is still considered the prototypical speed skater.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The book is also notable for popularizing the story of the little Dutch boy who plugs a
      <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">
       dike
      </a>
      with his finger.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Brinker,_or_The_Silver_Skates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dodge, who never visited the Netherlands until after the novel was published, wrote the novel at age 34. She was inspired by her reading of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lothrop_Motley" title="John Lothrop Motley">
       John L. Motley
      </a>
      's lengthy, multi-volume history works:
      <i>
       The Rise of the Dutch Republic
      </i>
      (1856), and
      <i>
       History of the United Netherlands
      </i>
      (1860-1867).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dodge subsequently did further bibliographical research into the country. She also received much firsthand information about Dutch life from her immigrant Dutch neighbors, the Scharffs,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gannon.2C_Susan_R._p._55_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gannon.2C_Susan_R._p._55-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Dodge noted in her preface to the 1875 edition of the book that the story of Hans Brinker's father was "founded strictly upon fact".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Full of Dutch cultural and historical information, the book became an instant bestseller, outselling all other books in its first year of publication except
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">
       Charles Dickens
      </a>
      '
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Our_Mutual_Friend" title="Our Mutual Friend">
        Our Mutual Friend
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gannon.2C_Susan_R._p._55_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gannon.2C_Susan_R._p._55-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The novel has since been continuously in print, most often in multiple editions and formats, and remains a children's classic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Brinker,_or_The_Silver_Skates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">
       Holland
      </a>
      , poor but industrious and honorable 15-year-old Hans Brinker and his younger sister Gretel yearn to participate in December's great ice skating race on the
      <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">
       canal
      </a>
      . They have little chance of doing well on their handmade
      <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
       wooden
      </a>
      skates, but the prospect of the race and the prize of the silver skates excites them and fires their dreams.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hans' father, Raff Brinker, is sick and
      <a href="/wiki/Amnesia" title="Amnesia">
       amnesiac
      </a>
      , with violent episodes, because of a head injury caused by a fall from a dike, and he cannot work. Mrs. Brinker, Hans, and Gretel must all work to support the family and are looked down upon in the community because of their low income and poor status. Hans has a chance meeting with the famous surgeon Dr. Boekman and begs him to treat their father, but the doctor is expensive and gruff in nature following the loss of his wife and disappearance of his son. Eventually, Dr. Boekman is persuaded to examine the Brinkers' father. He diagnoses pressure on the brain, which can be cured by a risky and expensive operation involving
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trepanation" title="Trepanation">
       trephining
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Hans offers his own money, saved in the hope of buying
      <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
       steel
      </a>
      skates, to the doctor to pay for his father's operation. Touched by this gesture, Dr. Boekman provides the surgery for free, and Hans is able to buy good skates for both himself and Gretel to skate in the race. Gretel wins the girls' race, but Hans lets a friend  who needs it more  win the precious prize, the Silver Skates, in the boys' race.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mr. Brinker's operation is successful, and he is restored to health and memory. Dr. Boekman is also changed, losing his gruff ways, thanks in part to being able to be reunited with his lost son through the unlikely aid of Mr. Brinker. The Brinkers' fortunes are changed further by the almost miraculous recovery of Mr. Brinker's savings, thought lost or stolen ten years ago.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Brinker parents live a long and happy life. Dr. Boekman helps Hans go to
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_school" title="Medical school">
       medical school
      </a>
      , and Hans becomes a successful doctor. Gretel also grows up to enjoy a happy adult life.
     </p>

        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Brinker,_or_The_Silver_Skates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Popular culture: the legend of the boy and the dike">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1976" data-file-width="1336" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg/170px-Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg/255px-Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg/340px-Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hans_Brinker_Madurodam.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Tourism statue in
        <a href="/wiki/Madurodam" title="Madurodam">
         Madurodam
        </a>
        , Netherlands, of the nameless boy plugging a dike
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A small fictional story within the novel has become well known in its own right in American popular culture. The story,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      read aloud in a schoolroom in England, is about a Dutch boy who saves his country by putting his finger in a leaking dike. The boy stays there all night, in spite of the cold, until the adults of the village find him and make the necessary repairs.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the book, the boy and the story are called simply "The Hero of
      <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">
       Haarlem
      </a>
      ". Although the hero of the dike-plugging tale remains nameless in the book, Hans Brinker's name has sometimes erroneously been associated with the character.
     </p>
     <p>
      This small tale within
      <i>
       Hans Brinker or The Silver Skates
      </i>
      has generated numerous versions and adaptations in American media. Poet
      <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_Cary" title="Phoebe Cary">
       Phoebe Cary
      </a>
       at whose New York City literary gatherings Dodge was a regular guest
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
       wrote a lengthy
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">
       poem
      </a>
      about it called "The Leak in the Dike", published
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posthumous_work" title="Posthumous work">
       posthumously
      </a>
      in 1873,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which has been widely anthologized in books of poetry for schoolchildren.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cary also gave the boy a name: Peter.
     </p>
     <p>
      The tale has also inspired full-fledged children's books of its own, which include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        The Hole in the Dike
       </i>
       , by Norma Green (1974)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The Boy Who Held Back the Sea
       </i>
       , by Lenny Hort (1987)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Statues_of_the_boy_and_the_dike">
       Statues of the boy and the dike
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Brinker,_or_The_Silver_Skates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statues of the boy and the dike">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For
      <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">
       tourism
      </a>
      purposes, statues of the fictional dike-plugging boy have been erected in Dutch locations such as
      <a href="/wiki/Spaarndam" title="Spaarndam">
       Spaarndam
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Madurodam" title="Madurodam">
       Madurodam
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harlingen,_Netherlands" title="Harlingen, Netherlands">
       Harlingen
      </a>
      . The statues are sometimes mistakenly titled "Hans Brinker"; others are known as "Peter of Haarlem". The story of the dike-plugging boy is, however, not widely known in the Netherlands  it is a piece of American, rather than Dutch, folklore.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_story_of_the_boy_and_the_dike">
       Origin of the story of the boy and the dike
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Brinker,_or_The_Silver_Skates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Origin of the story of the boy and the dike">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Versions of the story prior to
      <i>
       Hans Brinker
      </i>
      appear in several English-language publications from 1850 onward, including the following British and American publications:
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United Kingdom:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       An 1850 edition of Sharpe's
       <i>
        London Journal of Entertainment and Instruction
       </i>
       : "The Little Hero of Haarlem"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         19
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         20
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The February 23, 1850, edition of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Cook" title="Eliza Cook">
         Eliza Cook
        </a>
        's Journal
       </i>
       : "The Brave Little Hollander"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         21
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The 1855 edition of Beeton's
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boys_Own" title="Boys Own">
         Boy's Own Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       : "The Little Dutch Hero"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         22
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        The 'Sixth' Standard Reader
       </i>
       , compiled by J.S. Laurie (1863): "The Little Dutch Hero"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         <span>
          [
         </span>

          ]
         </span>

       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In the U.S.:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper's Magazine">

      <li>
       Julia Matilda Olin's 1856 book,
       <i>
        A Winter at Wood Lawn

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">

       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The actual authorship and genesis of the story of the boy and the dike is currently unknown, but it is possibly from a hypothetical but unidentified story by French author
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugenie_Foa" title="Eugenie Foa">
       Eugenie Foa
      </a>
      (17961852), appearing as an alleged English translation, "The Little Dykeman", in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Merry%27s_Museum" title="Merry's Museum">
        Merry's
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine in 1868.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In sum, although Dodge was not the originator of the story of the boy and the dike, the immense popularity of her novel
      <i>
       Hans Brinker or The Silver Skates
      </i>
      made the story very widely known. The story within a story of the nameless little boy's heroism also parallels and emphasizes Hans Brinker's own heroism in the novel.
     </p>
