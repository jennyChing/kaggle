     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-156385 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="156385" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="article-inline-half">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half/public/blogs/122718/2014/07/156385-160600.jpg?itok=knMYE4cK" title=""/>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            Recently, someone Ive never met introduced me in an email as my good friend Adam. A few days later, a virtual stranger who has emailed me a few times posted an article by my friend Adam. And then a student from a
            <em>
             one-day workshop
            </em>
            that I taught listed me as a job reference, and when asked to describe our relationship, wrote professor and friend. After I endorsed a book, a reporter referred to the author as a friend of Adams, when our total interaction has consisted of a series of work emails and one phone call.
           </p>
           <p>
            I like all of these people, but I wouldnt describe
            <em>
             any
            </em>
            of them as my friendsI think that would misrepresent how well we know each other and the kind of the bond between us. Yet in the
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/social-networking" title="Psychology Today looks at Facebook">
             Facebook
            </a>
            era, the boundaries on
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/friends" title="Psychology Today looks at friendship">
             friendship
            </a>
            have expanded dramatically. Someone recently called my brother-in-law a dear friend, but didnt bother to attend his wedding. Judging from my recent Facebook friend requests, my "friends" apparently include a person who ignored me in grad school, a second cousins high school classmate, a colleagues mentee, a Pee Wee soccer teammate I vaguely remember, and some guy who sat at a table near me at a restaurant once.
           </p>
           <p>
            If you want to avoid committing the faux pas of describing a colleague or an acquaintance as a friend, here are some rules for when its fair game to use the term:
           </p>
           <ol>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Youve actually met in person.
             </strong>
             From the caption of a famous
             <em>
              New Yorker
             </em>
             cartoon: On the internet, nobody knows youre a dog. If youve only connected with someone by email or phone, even if they are a real person, theres no substitute for the trust that can be developed from meeting face-to-face.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              You know embarrassing things about each other that dont show up in a Google search.
             </strong>
             Studies consistently
             <a href="http://psycnet.apa.org/psycinfo/1995-09363-001">
              show
             </a>
             that self-disclosureopening up and making yourself vulnerableis one of the strongest drivers of close relationships. My friends know that I have questionable taste in music, and so they refrain from dissing Bryan Adams. They accept the fact that I read the first
             <em>
              Twilight
             </em>
             book, cover to cover (my wife made me do it)and the rest of them (that was my doing).
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              You can call each other without scheduling a conversation.
             </strong>
             Unless the person in question is a head of state, if you have to get an appointment on someones calendar to talk, you havent cleared their friendship bar.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              You never discuss the weather.
             </strong>
             When you ask a friend How are you doing? you dont have to follow up with No, really, how are you doing? Friends dont bother with small talk. They can go months without talking, and pick up as if theyve never skipped a beat. They dive right into deep conversations about
             <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/relationships" title="Psychology Today looks at love">
              love
             </a>
             , life, and that exasperating conclusion of
             <em>
              Lost
             </em>
             where nothing was actually resolved.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              You help each other without keeping score.
             </strong>
             In professional relationships, I
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Give-Take-Helping-Others-Success/dp/0143124986/">
              find
             </a>
             that most people follow the norm of reciprocity: When we do someone a favor, we expect a comparable one in return. In friendships, the
             <a href="http://psp.sagepub.com/content/19/6/684.short">
              norm shifts
             </a>
             from reciprocity to
             <em>
              <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/altruism" title="Psychology Today looks at generosity">
               generosity
              </a>
             </em>
             . We focus on what our friends need, not what we can get back from them. Instead of keeping tallies of credits and debts, friends give whenever they can. As Jack Handey says, If you wear a toupee, why not let your friends try it on for a while? Come on, were not going to hurt it.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Youve had meaningful experiences together.
             </strong>
             Men and women alike
             <a href="http://spr.sagepub.com/content/28/6/723.short">
              expect
             </a>
             friendships to involve mutual activities and shared
             <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/memory" title="Psychology Today looks at memories">
              memories
             </a>
             . If youve never gone to a movie or been shopping together; never played a sport or game together; never attended a party together; or never decorated someones car with shaving cream together, youre probably not friends.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              You give the critical feedback that we dont want to hear, but need to hear.
             </strong>
             Friendships have what the organizational scholars Jane Dutton and Emily Heaphy call
             <a href="http://webuser.bus.umich.edu/janedut/High%20Quality%20Connections/Power%20High%20Quality.pdf">
              <em>
               tensility
              </em>
             </a>
             the carrying capacity to withstand criticism and bounce back from strain. We wouldnt want to assume that compassion is always gentle, George Saunders
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Poking-Dead-Frog-Conversations-Today%C2%92s/dp/0143123785">
              observes
             </a>
             . A harsh truth can be compassionate[I]f a friend is wearing something ridiculous, you can say, You look like an idiot, and maybe that will save him.
            </li>
           </ol>

Around a billion people are disabled. You can learn a lot by getting to know some people who are surviving real challenges in their lives. I've learned to accept living without shopping malls, restaurants, cliques and clubs, theatres and art galleries. My only outings are medical appointments and emergency runs to the hospital, but I've got my online friends, and that's enough.

                 Sure, anyone can say anything online, but the same is true offline. There is nothing inherently preventing you from opening up to an online acquaintance or "friend," any more than offline. In fact, for me, it's easier for me to talk to people honestly online. In person I tend to lock up and hold back on what I want to say. Online it's the complete opposite and I can write up many thousand word responses as if it were the easiest thing in the world.
                </p>
                <p>
                 At the end of the day, your real friends are whoever you determine to be your friends. There doesn't need to be a checklist, this isn't a bureaucracy.
                </p>
               </div>
