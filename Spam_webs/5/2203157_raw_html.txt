              <h1 class="post-title entry-title">
               Where You Live Influences Your Satisfaction with Life
              </h1>
              <div class="post-meta the-icons pmeta-alt">
               <span class="post-author vcard">
                By
                <a href="http://psychcentral.com/news/author/news-editor" target="authorwindow">
                 Rick Nauert PhD
                </a>
                <br/>
                ~ 2 min read
               </span>
              </div>
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div class="post-content">
              <div class="entry-content">
               <p>
                <a href="http://g4.psychcentral.com/news/u/2014/05/map-homes-SS.jpg">
                 <img alt="Where You Live Influences Your Satisfaction with Life " class="" height="200" id="newsimg" src="http://g4.psychcentral.com/news/u/2014/05/map-homes-SS.jpg" width="200"/>
                </a>
                Where a person lives appears to play a major role in determining if an obese person is satisfied with his or her life.
               </p>
               <p>
                Researchers from the University of Colorado  Boulder studied more than 1.3 million people from across the United States on a county level.
               </p>
               <div align="center">
                <div id="mj_adblock">
                 <script type="text/javascript">
                  //&lt;![CDATA[ 
 OAS_RICH('Right2'); 
 //]]&gt;
                 </script>
                </div>
               </div>
               <p>
                They discovered that how one compares weight-wise with others in his or her community plays a key role in determining how satisfied the person is with his or her life.
               </p>
               <p>
                The most interesting finding for us was that, in U.S. counties where obesity is particularly prevalent, being obese has very little negative effect on ones life satisfaction, said study co-author Philip M. Pendergast.
               </p>
               <p>
                In addition, we found that being normal weight has little benefit in counties where obesity is especially common. This illustrates the importance of looking like the people around you when it comes to satisfaction with life.
               </p>
               <p>
                The study, Obesity (Sometimes) Matters: The Importance of Context in the Relationship between Obesity and Life Satisfaction, appears in the
                <em>
                 Journal of Health and Social Behavior
                </em>
                .
               </p>
               <p>
                As part of their study, Pendergast and co-author Tim Wadsworth, evaluated peoples life satisfaction in the context of the county in which they lived and then compared their findings across counties with varying rates of obesity.
               </p>
               <p>
                Where obesity is more common, there is less difference among obese, severely obese, and non-obese individuals life satisfaction, but where obesity is less common, the difference in life satisfaction between the obese (including the severely obese) and non-obese is greater, said Pendergast.
               </p>
               <div align="center">
                <div id="mj_adblock">
                 <script type="text/javascript">
                  //&lt;![CDATA[ 
 OAS_RICH('x02'); 
 //]]&gt;
                 </script>
                </div>
               </div>
               <p>
                In that light, obesity in and of itself, does not appear to be the main reason obese individuals tend to be less satisfied with their lives than their non-obese peers.
               </p>
               <p>
                Instead, it appears to be societys response to or stigmatization of those that are different from what is seen as normal that drives this relationship.
               </p>
               <p>
                According to the researchers, before accounting for where people live, severely obese men and women have 29 percent and 43 percent lower odds, respectively, than their non-obese counterparts of reporting that they are very satisfied with their lives.
               </p>
               <p>
                However, the story is very different among people in counties where obesity is common.
               </p>
               <p>
                Among men, about 79 percent of the gap in the probability of severely obese and non-obese individuals reporting that they are very satisfied with their lives is eliminated if one moves from a county in the 5th percentile for obesity in the U.S. (with an obesity rate of 24 percent) to a county in the 95th percentile (with a rate of 46 percent).
               </p>
               <p>
                In the same scenario among women, the gap is reduced by about 60 percent.
               </p>
               <p>
                Although women generally pay a higher emotional cost for being obese, the role of context in moderating the relationship between obesity status and life satisfaction appears to be similar for men and women, Pendergast said.
               </p>
               <p>
                Researchers believe women are more sensitive to perceptions of obesity because of cultural norms.
               </p>
               <p>
                Think about the advertising we see on television or in magazines  we are bombarded by images of thin women, and we are told that is the ideal, said Pendergast, who noted that the study offers a potential explanation for the exponential increase in obesity in the United States over the past 30 years.
               </p>
               <p>
                Our findings demonstrate that where obesity is most prevalent, the difference in life satisfaction between the obese and non-obese is smaller for women and almost non-existent for men, he said.
               </p>
               <p>
                The same relationship is likely to exist over time and, as such, the emotional cost and advantage of obesity and non-obesity, respectively, may be decreasing as the prevalence of obesity increases. If this is the case, then some of the motivation for remaining thin is lessening over time, perhaps offering further insight into why obesity prevalence has increased so dramatically in recent years.
               </p>
               <p>
                Source:
                <a href="http://www.asanet.org/ ">
                 American Sociological Association
                </a>
               </p>
