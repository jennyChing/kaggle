     <p>
      <b>
       Preved
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      ) is a term used in the
      <a href="/wiki/Padonkaffsky_jargon" title="Padonkaffsky jargon">
       Padonkaffsky jargon
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">
       meme
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Internet" title="Russian Internet">
       Russian-speaking Internet
      </a>
      which developed out of a heavily circulated picture, and consists of choosing alternative spellings for words for comic effect. The picture, a modified version of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lurie" title="John Lurie">
       John Lurie
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercolor" title="Watercolor">
       watercolor
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bear_Surprise" title="Bear Surprise">
        Bear Surprise
       </a>
      </i>
      , whose popularity was stoked by emails and blogs, features a man and a woman having sex in the clearing of a forest, being surprised by a bear calling "Surprise!" with its paws raised. In later Russian adaptations, the bear shouts "Preved!" (a deliberate misspelling of
      <i>
       privet
      </i>
      ,
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
       "hi!"). In keeping with a popular trend of
      <a href="/wiki/Image_macro" title="Image macro">
       image manipulation
      </a>
      , the iconic bear (dubbed
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9C%D0%B5%D0%B4%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%B4" title="ru:">
        Medved
       </a>
      </i>
      - a misspelling of
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      , bear) has been inserted into many other pictures where his appearance adds a new dimension to the joke.
     </p>
     <p>
      The word and the bear image have found their way into the mainstream mass media, such as a poster for the Russian edition of
      <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">
       Newsweek
      </a>
      . On July 6, 2006 there was an online conference of
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">
       Vladimir Putin
      </a>
      prior to which the question
      <i>
       "PREVED, Vladimir Vladimirovich! How do you regard MEDVED?"
      </i>
      became the most popular, with 28,424 votes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      No answer was given, but the
      <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">
       Associated Press
      </a>
      , informing on the questions collection process, reportedly interpreted it as a reference to then-vice-prime-minister
      <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">
       Dmitry Medvedev
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (The third most popular question was "
      <a href="/wiki/How_does_one_patch_KDE2_under_FreeBSD%3F" title="How does one patch KDE2 under FreeBSD?">
       How does one patch KDE2 under FreeBSD?
      </a>
      ".)
     </p>
     <p>
      Eventually, it has become known that the author of the altered picture with the word
      <i>
       "preved"
      </i>
      was user Lobzz from site Dirty.ru, real name Roman Yatsenko. The authorship of the word itself is still unclear, although the "unfinished" version,
      <i>
       "prevet"
      </i>
      was traced to 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Preved
      </i>
      is identified by a specific pattern of alternate spelling which emerged from the word. In this pattern,
      <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
       voiceless consonants
      </a>
      are replaced with their voiced counterparts, and
      <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
       unstressed
      </a>
      vowels are interchanged pair-wise 
      <i>
       a
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       o
      </i>
      stand in for each other, as do
      <i>
       e
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      . The words
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       uchasneg
      </i>
      , a misspelling of
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        (
       <i>
        uchastnik
       </i>
       )
      </span>
      , "user" or "participant"),
      <i>
       preved
      </i>
      itself, and
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       kagdila
      </i>
      , a misspelling of
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         (
       <i>
        kak dela
       </i>
       )
      </span>
      , "how are you") illustrate this pattern.
     </p>
     <p>
      The larger trend of alternate spellings, called "olbansky yazyk" ("
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olbanian_language" title="Olbanian language">
       Olbanian language
      </a>
      ", misspelled "Albanian") developed from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Padonki" title="Padonki">
       padonki
      </a>
      movement which originated on sites such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Udaff.com" title="Udaff.com">
       udaff.com
      </a>
      . That trend uses the opposite conversion from the Preved trend  voiced consonants are replaced with their voiceless counterparts (which are sometimes doubled). For vowels,
      <i>
       o
      </i>
      is replaced with
      <i>
       a
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       e
      </i>
      with
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      . For example,
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       vtor
      </i>
      , "author") would be spelled
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       fftar
      </i>
      ) or
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       ftar
      </i>
      ). The latter exhibits a sort of
      <a href="/wiki/Eye_dialect" title="Eye dialect">
       eye dialect
      </a>
      .
     </p>
