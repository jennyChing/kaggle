    <h1>
     Are Solar Rebates Still Available?
    </h1>
    <br/>
    <p>
    </p>
    <p>
     We all like to save moolah, especially when it comes to paying bills. Since they were introduced, solar rebates have been a hot topic in Australia. Households have snapped up various federal and state and territory government rebates and incentives, to cut costs and their environmental footprint.
    </p>
    <p>
     But are solar rebates still available in Australia? And how much can you claim?
    </p>
    <p>
     <strong>
      Can I still get a solar credits subsidy?
     </strong>
    </p>
    <p>
     A few years back, under the Solar Homes and Communities Plan, you could get up to an $8,000 rebate on your solar installation costs. But in 2009, the plan was dissolved and in its place sprouted the Solar Credits Subsidy.
    </p>
    <p>
     Why? Because the Australian Government set a new target  to ensure renewable energy made up 20 per cent of Australias energy mix. The Solar Credits program formed part of the new national scheme. It was divided into the Large-scale Renewable Energy Target (LRET) and the Small-scale Renewable Energy Scheme (SRES). The small-scale scheme provided incentives for installing solar panels and solar hot water systems.
    </p>
    <p>
     Under the subsidy, if you installed a new small-scale renewable energy generator and connected it to the main grid, you could save a substantial sum. It worked by applying solar credits to the first 1.5 kilowatts of capacity that were installed. The amount you received depended on how much energy the system generated, and where it was installed. The certificates could then be used to get an upfront discount or delayed cash payment.
    </p>
    <p>
     But before you get too excited, the Solar Credits Subsidy was canned as of 1 July 2013. However, the Small-scale Renewable Energy Scheme is still on offer.
    </p>
    <p>
     <strong>
      What about feed-in tariffs?
     </strong>
    </p>
    <p>
     Subsidies have been slashed. But you can still
     <a href="http://www.3quotes.com.au/solar-power/how-much-money-can-i-save-going-solar.html">
      save on your energy bill
     </a>
     by creating clean energy. How? With feed-in tariffs (FiTs).
     <img alt="Feed-in Tariff" src="http://www.3quotes.com.au/content/images/feed-in-tariff.jpg" style="width: 400px; height: 400px; float: right;"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     When you connect your photovoltaic (PV) solar panels to the electricity supply grid, they start producing more energy than your household needs. The excess energy is supplied to the mains power grid, and you are effectively reimbursed for it. This is whats known as a feed-in tariff.
    </p>
    <p>
     <strong>
      Solar incentives in your neck of the woods
     </strong>
    </p>
    <p>
     Since the
     <a href="http://www.3quotes.com.au/solar-power/is-it-worthwhile-to-go-solar-after-rebates-cut-on-june-30th.html">
      Federal Government no longer offers a solar rebate scheme
     </a>
     , its left up to each state or territory. Here are a few solar incentives on offer in each region:
    </p>
    <ul>
     <li>
      <strong>
       ACT:
      </strong>
      ActewAGL Solar Buyback scheme pays households for the electricity generated from grid-connected solar power systems.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       New South Wales:
      </strong>
      The Solar Bonus Scheme gives households a feed-in tariff when they connect solar generators to the grid. The scheme is no longer open to new applicants and ends in 2016.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       Victoria:
      </strong>
      The Victorian Energy Efficiency Target (VEET) provides certificates for customers who replace an old hot water system with a solar hot water system.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       South Australia:
      </strong>
      Now closed to new applicants, the solar feed-in scheme provides households with a feed-in tariff.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       Queensland:
      </strong>
      The Solar Bonus Scheme is a feed-in tariff which pays customers for the excess energy produced from solar photovoltaic (PV) systems.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       Tasmania:
      </strong>
      The Aurora Energy renewable energy grid connection scheme is a feed-in tariff which rewards customers who install small-scale solar power systems.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       Northern Territory:
      </strong>
      The Power and Water Corporation Grid Buy Back Rate pays for the electricity generated from households that install solar power systems. A Solar Hot Water Retrofit Rebate of up to $1000 is also offered when electric hot water systems are replaced with solar.
      <br/>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       Western Australia:
      </strong>
      Under the Horizon Power Renewable Energy Buyback Offer, households are paid for the electricity generated by installed PV systems.
     </li>
    </ul>
    <p>
     Still unsure? Contact your council, state or territory government or speak with your solar installer.
    </p>
