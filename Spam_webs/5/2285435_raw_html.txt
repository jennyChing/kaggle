        <p>
         Republicans will hold their 2016 national convention more than a month earlier than their 2012 event for one simple reason: money.
        </p>
        <p>
         Two years ago, Mitt Romney raised $1 billion but found himself
         <a href="http://www.nationalreview.com/corner/327793/romney-campaign-borrowed-20-million-robert-costa" target="_blank">
          out of cash that August
         </a>
         due to campaign finance laws that essentially force candidates to divide their spending between pre-and-post convention accounts. Moving the convention up, the GOP reasons, will help make those rules a non-issue.
        </p>

        <p id="continue">
         The Republican National Committee announced Tuesday that
         <a href="http://www.politico.com/story/2014/07/2016-rnc-location-cleveland-108659.html?hp=t3_3" target="_blank">
          Cleveland would host its 2016 convention
         </a>
          and that the party was aiming for a late June or early July event. The early summer timing is a sharp break with recent history  when both parties have traditionally held their conventions in late August or early September. The GOP hasnt held a July convention since 1980 and it hasnt held a June convention since 1948.
        </p>
        <p>
         (
         <a href="http://www.politico.com//story/2014/07/2016-rnc-location-cleveland-108659.html" target="_blank">
          Also on POLITICO: Cleveland to host 2016 GOP convention
         </a>
         )
        </p>
        <p>
         But times have changed. The decision of Barack Obama and Romney to forgo election matching funds, along with the explosion of super PACs and political nonprofits, means the old rules on convention timing no longer apply.
        </p>
        <p>
         The reasons conventions kept getting pushed closer and closer to Election Day was because candidates always took public money, and they could only spend public money between the convention and the actual election, said RNC Chairman Reince Priebus in an Tuesday interview with Fox News.
        </p>
        <p>
         But so now the candidates dont take this money and so whats happening is a candidate can be broke, but they are not able to raise general election money until the convention is held, he added. So if you have a candidate thats broke after a primary in May, that candidate is basically a duck in the pond until you get nominated.
        </p>
        <p>
         But its not a cost-free choice. A convention earlier in the year could mean the public isnt fully engaged in the presidential race and voters arent paying attention, former Obama campaign manager and White House adviser David Plouffe tweeted, calling the GOPs decision Reinces Folly.
        </p>
        <p>
         (
         <a href="http://www.politico.com/story/2014/07/cleveland-facts-2016-republican-convention-108664.html" target="_blank">
          Also on POLITICO: Cleveland: 10 things to know)
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Where convention is will be less important than when it is. GOP likely made huge strategic blunder with June choice,
         <a href="https://twitter.com/davidplouffe/status/486543935628918784" target="_blank">
          Plouffe tweeted
         </a>
         . The 2 big moments you control in a Pres camp. are VP pick and convention. Both will be over too early. $$ reasoning weak,
         <a href="https://twitter.com/davidplouffe/status/486546247311187968" target="_blank">
          he added
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Prior to 2008, candidates from both parties had always accepted public money to run their fall general election campaigns in exchange for agreeing to abide by spending limits. A late summer convention helped maximize the amount of non-general election, non-public money a candidate could spend before those spending limits came into effect.
        </p>
        <p>
         But that system essentially came to an end in 2008, when Obama became the first candidate to opt out of the public financing system for the general election that year.
        </p>
        <p>
         Obama would go on to raise about $750 million in 2008  an eye-popping sum at the time. By 2012, both Romney and Obama had opted out of the system and both would go on to
         <a href="http://www.politico.com/story/2012/12/barack-obama-mitt-romney-both-topped-1-billion-in-2012-84737.html" target="_blank">
          raise more than $1 billion apiece
         </a>
         . Republicans and Democrats are both widely expected to opt-out of public financing again 2016  convinced that both digital fundraising and big donors offer a major advantage over a modest sum of guaranteed public money.
        </p>
        <p>
         Candidate Barack Obamas decision to break to public financing system changed the strategic calculus for when it is advantageous for a party to hold its convention, said Charlie Spies, a Republican election attorney with Clark Hill who also co-founded a pro-Romney super PAC.
        </p>
        <p>
         But while Romney raised comparable amounts to Obama, he was still limited on when he could spend it. Even though he wasnt using the matching funds, campaign finance law divides up candidate cash into pre-and-post nomination pools. Romney became the presumptive GOP nominee in late April but was not officially nominated until late August, and until that moment, he could only spend primary election dollars. So Romneys campaign ran out of primary election cash and had to take a $20 million line of credit from the Bank of Georgetown that August.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the meantime,
         <a href="http://bigstory.ap.org/article/obama-romney-pursue-divergent-ad-strategies-0" target="_blank">
          Obama was on the attack
         </a>
         . With no primary opponent, Obama didnt have to worry about burning cash early in the year and had plenty saved up for the summer. The presidents advisers made a crucial decision hit Romney with a barrage of negative advertising. Obama and the Democratic National Committee would go on to outspend Romney $173 million to $75 million that summer. In 1996, the incumbent Democrat Bill Clinton did the same thing to challenger Bob Dole.
        </p>
        <p>
         Democrats are unlikely to follow suit in moving their own convention to early summer.; Sources knowledgeable about the partys internal planning process say that the DNC is likely to stick to late August or early September for the 2016 event. (The Democratic Convention would be after the GOPs in any event  the party out of the White House traditionally goes first.)
        </p>
        <p>
         The Democrats have
         <a href="http://www.politico.com/story/2014/07/dnc-convention-2016-site-visit-schedule-108552.html" target="_blank">
          narrowed
         </a>
         their convention choices to Birmingham, Brooklyn, Cleveland, Columbus, Philadelphia and Phoenix.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          The updated version of this story has corrected the date for when the GOP last held a June convention.
         </em>
        </p>
