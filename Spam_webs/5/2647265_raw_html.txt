   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    New York Society for the Suppression of Vice

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       New York Society for the Suppression of Vice
      </b>
      (NYSSV or SSV) was an institution dedicated to supervising the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_morality" title="Public morality">
       morality of the public
      </a>
      , founded in 1873. Its specific mission was to monitor compliance with state laws and work with the courts and district attorneys in bringing offenders to justice. It and its members also pushed for additional
      <a href="/wiki/Comstock_laws" title="Comstock laws">
       laws
      </a>
      against perceived immoral conduct. While the NYSSV is better remembered for its opposition to literary works, it also closely monitored the news-stands, commonly found on city sidewalks and in transportation terminals, which sold the popular magazines of the day.
     </p>
     <p>
      The NYSSV was founded by
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Comstock" title="Anthony Comstock">
       Anthony Comstock
      </a>
      and his supporters in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_Men%27s_Christian_Association" title="Young Men's Christian Association">
       Young Men's Christian Association
      </a>
      . It was chartered by the New York state legislature, which granted its agents powers of search, seizure and arrest, and awarded the society 50% of all fines levied in resulting cases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After his death in 1915, Comstock was succeeded by
      <a href="/wiki/John_S._Sumner" title="John S. Sumner">
       John S. Sumner
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1947, the organization's name was changed to the Society to Maintain Public Decency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After Sumner's retirement in 1950, the organization was dissolved. The New York Society for the Suppression of Vice is not to be confused with its namesake, the earlier, 19th-century
      <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Suppression_of_Vice" title="Society for the Suppression of Vice">
       Society for the Suppression of Vice
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Noteworthy_actions_pursued_by_the_NYSSV">
       Noteworthy actions pursued by the NYSSV

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">
        Broadway
       </a>
       play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sapho_(play)" title="Sapho (play)">
         Sapho
        </a>
       </i>
       . All were found innocent at trial.

        <a href="#cite_note-4">

          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>

       </sup>

       1915: Forced off the market

        Stanisaw Przybyszewski
       </a>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens_(novel)" title="Homo sapiens (novel)">
         Homo sapiens
        </a>
       </i>

        <a href="#cite_note-Sova2006-5">

          [
         </span>
         5

          ]

       </sup>

        Theodore Dreiser
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_%22Genius%22_(novel)" title='The "Genius" (novel)'>
         The Genius
        </a>
        .

         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          <span>

         </a>
        </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Sanger" title="Margaret Sanger">
        Margaret Sanger
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">

          ]
         </span>

       </sup>

       <i>

         Jurgen, A Comedy of Justice
        </a>
       </i>

       <a href="/wiki/James_Branch_Cabell" title="James Branch Cabell">
        James Branch Cabell
       </a>
       and ended up giving it considerable publicity and boosting its sales.
      </li>
      <li>
       1919: At its urging a police raid at the
       <a href="/wiki/Everard_Baths" title="Everard Baths">
        Everard Baths
       </a>
       resulted in nine arrests.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1920: After the magazine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Review" title="The Little Review">
         The Little Review
        </a>
       </i>
       serialized a passage of the book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">
         Ulysses
        </a>
       </i>
       dealing with the main character masturbating, the New York Society for the Suppression of Vice, who objected to the book's content, took action to attempt to keep the book out of the United States. At a
       <a href="/wiki/Obscenity_trial_of_Ulysses_in_The_Little_Review" title="Obscenity trial of Ulysses in The Little Review">
        trial in 1921
       </a>
       the magazine was declared obscene and as a result
       <i>
        Ulysses
       </i>
       was banned in the United States.
      </li>
      <li>
       1922: Encouraged the arrest of bookstore employee Raymond D. Halsey for selling the "obscene" novel
       <i>
        Mademoiselle de Maupin
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Thophile Gautier">
        Thophile Gautier
       </a>
       , which depicted adultery and homosexuality. Halsey was acquitted, and successfully sued the Society for false arrest and malicious prosecution. This case established that literary experts could offer testimony in support of a book to guide the judge's opinion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1920s and '30s: Prosecuted a long war against the so-called "girlie pulps," which featured titillating fiction, sometimes accompanied with nude photography.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         10
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1925: Attacked as indecent the magazines
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Artists_and_Models_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Artists and Models (magazine) (page does not exist)">
         Artists and Models
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Art_Lovers%27_Magazine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Art Lovers' Magazine (page does not exist)">
         Art Lovers' Magazine
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1927: Attacked publisher
       <a href="/wiki/Bernarr_Macfadden" title="Bernarr Macfadden">
        Bernarr Macfadden
       </a>
       's newspaper, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_York_Graphic" title="New York Graphic">
         New York Graphic
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1927: Shut down
       <a href="/wiki/Mae_West" title="Mae West">
        Mae West
       </a>
       's first starring role on Broadway, the play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sex_(play)" title="Sex (play)">
         Sex
        </a>
       </i>
       . West spent ten days in jail.
      </li>
      <li>
       1929: Seized 3,000 books from three book dealers; titles included
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">
         Ulysses
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley's Lover">
         Lady Chatterley's Lover
        </a>
       </i>
       , and novels by
       <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">
        Oscar Wilde
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Harris" title="Frank Harris">
        Frank Harris
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Clement_Wood" title="Clement Wood">
        Clement Wood
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1930: Forced
       <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">
        pulp
       </a>
       publisher
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Hersey" title="Harold Hersey">
        Harold Hersey
       </a>
       to suppress the depiction of violence and lawlessness in his new line of gang pulps, which included
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gangster_Stories" title="Gangster Stories">
         Gangster Stories
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Racketeer_Stories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Racketeer Stories (page does not exist)">
         Racketeer Stories
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       1932: Falsely arrested a bookseller for displaying a book on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nudism" title="Nudism">
        nudism
       </a>
       in his store's window. John S. Sumner, secretary of the society, was ordered to pay the bookseller $500 in restitution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         15

          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Erskine_Caldwell" title="Erskine Caldwell">
        Erskine Caldwell
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/God%27s_Little_Acre" title="God's Little Acre">
         God's Little Acre
        </a>
       </i>
       declared obscene.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         16
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>

       1934: Raided magazine "back-number" shops to confiscate four new magazines with the titles
       <i>

         Real Boudoir Tales
        </a>
       </i>

       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Real_Temptation_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Real Temptation Tales (page does not exist)">
         Real Temptation Tales
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Real_Forbidden_Sweets&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Real Forbidden Sweets (page does not exist)">
         Real Forbidden Sweets
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Real_French_Capers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Real French Capers (page does not exist)">
         Real French Capers
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         <span>

         </span>

         <span>
          ]
         </span>

       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Tully" title="Jim Tully">

       </a>

       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ladies_in_the_Parlor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ladies in the Parlor (page does not exist)">

       <i>

         19

          ]
         </span>

       <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Wilson" title="Edmund Wilson">
        Edmund Wilson

       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Memoirs_of_Hecate_County" title="Memoirs of Hecate County">
