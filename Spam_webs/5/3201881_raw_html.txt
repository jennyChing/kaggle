     <p>
      "
      <b>
       Get Up, Stand Up
      </b>
      " is a
      <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">
       reggae
      </a>
      song written by
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">
       Bob Marley
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Tosh" title="Peter Tosh">
       Peter Tosh
      </a>
      . It originally appeared on
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley_and_the_Wailers" title="Bob Marley and the Wailers">
       The Wailers
      </a>
      ' 1973 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Burnin%27_(The_Wailers_album)" title="Burnin' (The Wailers album)">
        Burnin'
       </a>
      </i>
      . It was recorded and played live in numerous versions by The Wailers and Bob Marley and The Wailers, along with solo versions by Peter Tosh and
      <a href="/wiki/Bunny_Wailer" title="Bunny Wailer">
       Bunny Wailer
      </a>
      . It was later included on the compilations
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Legend_(Bob_Marley_%26_The_Wailers_album)" title="Legend (Bob Marley &amp; The Wailers album)">
        Legend
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rebel_Music_(Bob_Marley_%26_The_Wailers_album)" title="Rebel Music (Bob Marley &amp; The Wailers album)">
        Rebel Music
       </a>
      </i>
      , as well as Live recordings such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Live_at_the_Roxy_(Bob_Marley_%26_The_Wailers_album)" title="Live at the Roxy (Bob Marley &amp; The Wailers album)">
        Live at the Roxy
       </a>
      </i>
      among others.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1973, "Get Up, Stand Up" peaked at number 33 on the
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Top_40" title="Dutch Top 40">
       Dutch Top 40
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1986, it peaked at number 49 in New Zealand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Premise_and_usage_in_concerts">
       Premise and usage in concerts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Up,_Stand_Up&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Premise and usage in concerts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Marley wrote the song while touring Haiti, deeply moved by its poverty and the lives of Haitians, according to his then-girlfriend
      <a href="/wiki/Esther_Anderson_(Jamaican_actress)" title="Esther Anderson (Jamaican actress)">
       Esther Anderson
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The song was frequently performed at Marley's concerts, often as the last song. "Get Up, Stand Up" was also the last song Marley ever performed on stage, on 23 September 1980 at the Stanley Theater, now the
      <a href="/wiki/Benedum_Center" title="Benedum Center">
       Benedum Center
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">
       Pittsburgh
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">
       Pennsylvania
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      On his DVD
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Live_at_the_Hollywood_Bowl_(Ben_Harper_film)" title="Live at the Hollywood Bowl (Ben Harper film)">
        Live at the Hollywood Bowl
       </a>
      </i>
      , artist
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Harper" title="Ben Harper">
       Ben Harper
      </a>
      relates a childhood experience in which, during a 1978 Bob Marley concert at the Starlight Amphitheater, Peter Tosh showed up unannounced as this song was being performed, took the microphone from Marley and started singing the last verse of the song to thunderous applause. Tosh was on tour opening for the Rolling Stones at the time.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings_by_the_Wailers">
       Recordings by the Wailers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Up,_Stand_Up&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recordings by the Wailers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The song was re-recorded and re-released by the three major Wailers on their own solo releases, each with varying arrangements and approaches to the third verse, which claims that "Almighty God is a living man". Bob Marley and the Wailers released a Bob Marley only version on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live!_(Bob_Marley_%26_The_Wailers_album)" title="Live! (Bob Marley &amp; The Wailers album)">
       Live!
      </a>
      in 1975, this version was notable for the "WO-YO!" refrain after the third verse. Tosh would include his own solo version on his second album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Equal_Rights_(album)" title="Equal Rights (album)">
        Equal Rights
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1977. Bunny Wailer was the last to release his own version on
      <i>
       Protest
      </i>
      . This version actually featured Tosh due to his involvement in recording the album before his death.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Covers.2C_remixes.2C_parodies">
       Covers, remixes, parodies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Get_Up,_Stand_Up&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Covers, remixes, parodies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The song is playable in the video game
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rock_Band_3" title="Rock Band 3">
         Rock Band 3
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The song was parodied on an episode of the television series
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">
         Futurama
        </a>
       </i>
       , entitled "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Route_of_All_Evil" title="The Route of All Evil">
        The Route of All Evil
       </a>
       ". In the episode, Jamaican accountant Hermes Conrad alters the lyrics of the song by singing "Stamp it, file it, send it over night."
      </li>
      <li>
       The song is found on Toots &amp; the Maytals' LP
       <i>
        Pass the Pipe
       </i>
       . Although sung with completely different lyrics and melody, the songwriting credit is given to the Wailers.
      </li>
      <li>
       The song was remixed with "
       <a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_Jamrock_(song)" title="Welcome to Jamrock (song)">
        Welcome to Jamrock
       </a>
       " by Marley's son
       <a href="/wiki/Damian_Marley" title="Damian Marley">
        Damian
       </a>
       for his album of the same name and later included on the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto_IV_soundtrack" title="Grand Theft Auto IV soundtrack">
         Grand Theft Auto IV soundtrack
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The song has been covered by
       <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Chapman" title="Tracy Chapman">
        Tracy Chapman
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The title line was referenced in
       <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">
        Common's
       </a>
       song Book of Life from the album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Resurrection" title="Resurrection">
         Resurrection
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       A smooth jazz version of the song has been covered by
       <a href="/wiki/Lee_Ritenour" title="Lee Ritenour">
        Lee Ritenour
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dave_Grusin" title="Dave Grusin">
        Dave Grusin
       </a>
       and featured on the compilation CD,
       <i>
        A Twist of Marley
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Toronto based reggae/metal band
       <a href="/wiki/Zeroscape" title="Zeroscape">
        Zeroscape
       </a>
       covered the song on their new
       <i>
        Friday Night EP
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       It was also covered by
       <a href="/wiki/Axis_of_Justice" title="Axis of Justice">
        Axis of Justice
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Serj_Tankian" title="Serj Tankian">
        Serj Tankian
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       A cover version is included in the album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tribute_to_the_Legend:_Bob_Marley" title="Tribute to the Legend: Bob Marley">
         Tribute to the Legend: Bob Marley
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Cultura_Prof%C3%A9tica" title="Cultura Proftica">
        Cultura Proftica
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Bob Marley's son
       <a href="/wiki/Ziggy_Marley" title="Ziggy Marley">
        Ziggy Marley
       </a>
       does a jazz compilation with a heavy reggae based influence in which he uses the hook to "Get Up, Stand Up".
      </li>
      <li>
       The song has been covered by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Butts_Band" title="The Butts Band">
        The Butts Band
       </a>
       for their 1975 album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hear_and_Now_(album)" title="Hear and Now (album)">
         Hear And Now
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Shaggy_(musician)" title="Shaggy (musician)">
        Shaggy
       </a>
       made a cover of this song, which is featured on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mr._Lover_Lover_%E2%80%93_The_Best_of_Shaggy..._Part_1" title="Mr. Lover Lover  The Best of Shaggy... Part 1">
         Mr. Lover Lover  The Best of Shaggy... Part 1
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Covered by
       <a href="/wiki/Ojos_de_Brujo" title="Ojos de Brujo">
        Ojos de Brujo
       </a>
       for their 2007 live album
       <i>
        Techari
       </i>
       (lyrics are in Spanish).
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Playing_for_Change" title="Playing for Change">
        Playing for Change
       </a>
       project includes a version of the song on the 2014 album
       <i>
        PFC 3: Songs Around the World
       </i>
       . Performers include
       <a href="/wiki/Keb%27_Mo%27" title="Keb' Mo'">
        Keb' Mo'
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Keith_Richards" title="Keith Richards">
        Keith Richards
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The 2014 album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Songs_from_a_Stolen_Spring" title="Songs from a Stolen Spring">
         Songs from a Stolen Spring
        </a>
       </i>
       features a version of the song performed by
       <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Tilbrook" title="Glenn Tilbrook">
        Glenn Tilbrook
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Squeeze_(band)" title="Squeeze (band)">
        Squeeze
       </a>
       fame. The version is
       <a href="/wiki/Mashup_(music)" title="Mashup (music)">
        meshed
       </a>
       with "Beyond These Doors" by Egyptian singer
       <a href="/wiki/Dina_El_Wedidi" title="Dina El Wedidi">
        Dina El Wedidi
       </a>
       .
