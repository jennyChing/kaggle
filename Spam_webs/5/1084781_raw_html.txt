     <p>
      <b>
       Primate cognition
      </b>
      is the study of the intellectual and behavioral skills of non-human
      <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">
       primates
      </a>
      , particularly in the fields of
      <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
       psychology
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral_biology" title="Behavioral biology">
       behavioral biology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Primatology" title="Primatology">
       primatology
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropology
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Primates are capable of high levels of cognition; some make tools and use them to acquire foods and for social displays;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>

       Studies in primate cognition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Studies in primate cognition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_of_mind">
       Theory of mind
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theory of mind">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Premack and Woodruff's 1978 article "Does the chimpanzee have a
      <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_mind" title="Theory of mind">
       theory of mind
      </a>
      ?" was a contentious issue because of the problem of inferring from
      <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">
       animal behavior
      </a>
      the existence of
      <a href="/wiki/Thought" title="Thought">
       thinking
      </a>
      , of the existence of a concept of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_(psychology)" title="Self (psychology)">
       self
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Self-awareness" title="Self-awareness">
       self-awareness
      </a>
      , or of particular thoughts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Non-human research still has a major place in this field, however, and is especially useful in illuminating which nonverbal behaviors signify components of theory of mind, and in pointing to possible stepping points in the evolution of what many claim to be a uniquely human aspect of social cognition. While it is difficult to study human-like theory of mind and mental states in species which we do not yet describe as "minded" at all, and about whose potential mental states we have an incomplete understanding, researchers can focus on simpler components of more complex capabilities.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, many researchers focus on animals' understanding of intention, gaze, perspective, or knowledge (or rather, what another being has seen). Part of the difficulty in this line of research is that observed phenomena can often be explained as simple stimulus-response learning, as it is in the nature of any theorizers of mind to have to extrapolate internal mental states from observable behavior. Recently, most non-human theory of mind research has focused on monkeys and great apes, who are of most interest in the study of the evolution of human social cognition.
     </p>
     <p>
      There has been some controversy over the interpretation of evidence purporting to show theory of mind abilityor inabilityin animals. Two examples serve as demonstration: first, Povinelli et al. (1990)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      presented chimpanzees with the choice of two experimenters from which to request food: one who had seen where food was hidden, and one who, by virtue of one of a variety of mechanisms (having a bucket or bag over his head; a blindfold over his eyes; or being turned away from the baiting) does not know, and can only guess. They found that the animals failed in most cases to differentially request food from the "knower." By contrast, Hare, Call, and Tomasello (2001)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      found that subordinate chimpanzees were able to use the knowledge state of dominant rival chimpanzees to determine which container of hidden food they approached.
     </p>
     <p>
      Tomasello and like-minded colleagues who originally argued that great apes did not have theory of mind have since reversed their position. Povinelli and his colleagues, however, maintain that Tomasello's group has misinterpreted the results of their experiments. They point out that most evidence in support of great ape theory of mind involves naturalistic settings to which the apes may have already adapted through past learning. Their "reinterpretation hypothesis" explains away all current evidence supporting attribution of mental states to others in chimpanzees as merely evidence of risk-based learning; that is, the chimpanzees learn through experience that certain behaviors in other chimpanzees have a probability of leading to certain responses, without necessarily attributing knowledge or other intentional states to those other chimpanzees. They therefore propose testing theory of mind abilities in great apes in novel, and not naturalistic settings. Kristin Andrews takes the reinterpretation hypothesis one step further, arguing that it implies that even the well-known false-belief test used to test children's theory of mind is susceptible to being interpreted as a result of learning.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Language">
       Language
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Nim_Chimpsky" title="Nim Chimpsky">
       Nim Chimpsky
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Great_ape_language" title="Great ape language">
       great ape language
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The modeling of human language in animals is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_language" title="Animal language">
       animal language
      </a>
      research. Nim Chimpsky, a chimpanzee, was successfully taught 125 signs during his life, though some disagree on whether this can be constituted as true language. There have been other, more successful animal language projects, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Kanzi" title="Kanzi">
       Kanzi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Koko_(gorilla)" title="Koko (gorilla)">
       Koko
      </a>
      , as well as some parrots.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been suggested that Nim Chimpsky's limited success was due to short training sessions, rather than true language immersion.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

       ]
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tool_use">
       Tool use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tool use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals#Primates" title="Tool use by animals">
       Tool use by animals  Primates
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5036" data-file-width="2138" height="518" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg/220px-Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg/330px-Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg/440px-Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gorrila_tool_use-Efi.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Tool use by a gorilla
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are many reports of primates making or using tools, both in the wild or when captive. Chimpanzees, orangutans, gorillas, capuchin monkeys, baboons, and mandrills have all been reported as using tools. The use of tools by primates is varied and includes hunting (mammals, invertebrates,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      fish), collecting honey,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      processing food (nuts, fruits, vegetables and seeds), collecting water, weapons and shelter.
     </p>
     <p>
      Tool making is much rarer, but has been documented in orangutans,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      bonobos and bearded capuchin monkeys. Research in 2007 shows that chimpanzees in the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fongoli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fongoli (page does not exist)">
       Fongoli
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">
       savannah
      </a>
      sharpen sticks to use as
      <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">
       spears
      </a>
      when hunting, considered the first evidence of systematic use of weapons in a species other than humans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Captive gorillas have made a variety of tools.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vancatova_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Vancatova-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the wild, mandrills have been observed to clean their ears with modified tools. Scientists filmed a large male mandrill at
      <a href="/wiki/Chester_Zoo" title="Chester Zoo">
       Chester Zoo
      </a>
      (UK) stripping down a twig, apparently to make it narrower, and then using the modified stick to scrape dirt from underneath its toenails.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gill2011_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gill2011-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is some controversy over whether tool use represents a higher level of physical cognition. Some studies suggest primates could use tools due to environmental or motivational clues, rather than an understanding of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_physics" title="Folk physics">
       folk physics
      </a>
      or a capacity for future planning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Problem_solving">
       Problem solving
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Problem solving">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1913,
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_K%C3%B6hler" title="Wolfgang Khler">
       Wolfgang Khler
      </a>
      started writing a book on problem solving titled
      <i>
       The Mentality of Apes
      </i>
      (1917). In this research, Khler observed the manner in which chimpanzees solve problems, such as that of retrieving bananas when positioned out of reach. He found that they stacked wooden crates to use as makeshift ladders in order to retrieve the food. If the bananas were placed on the ground outside of the cage, they used sticks to lengthen the reach of their arms.
     </p>
     <p>
      Khler concluded that the chimps had not arrived at these methods through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial-and-error" title="Trial-and-error">
       trial-and-error
      </a>
      (which American psychologist
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_Thorndike" title="Edward Thorndike">
       Edward Thorndike
      </a>
      had claimed to be the basis of all animal learning, through his
      <a href="/wiki/Law_of_effect" title="Law of effect">
       law of effect
      </a>
      ), but rather that they had experienced an
      <a href="/wiki/Insight" title="Insight">
       insight
      </a>
      (sometimes known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Eureka_effect" title="Eureka effect">
       Eureka effect
      </a>
      or an aha experience), in which, having realized the answer, they then proceeded to carry it out in a way that was, in Khlers words, unwaveringly purposeful.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Asking_questions_and_giving_negative_answers">
       Asking questions and giving negative answers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asking questions and giving negative answers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the 1970s and the 1980s there had been suggestions that apes are unable to ask questions and to give negative answers. According to the numerous published studies
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      apes are able to answer human questions, and the vocabulary of the acculturated apes contains question words. Despite these abilities, according to the published research literature, apes are not able to ask questions themselves, and in human-primate conversations questions are asked by the humans only. Ann and David Premacks designed a potentially promising methodology to teach apes to ask questions in the 1970s: In principle interrogation can be taught either by removing an element from a familiar situation in the animals world or by removing the element from a language that maps the animals world. It is probable that one can induce questions by purposefully removing key elements from a familiar situation. Suppose a chimpanzee received its daily ration of food at a specific time and place, and then one day the food was not there. A chimpanzee trained in the interrogative might inquire Where is my food? or, in Sarahs case, My food is? Sarah was never put in a situation that might induce such interrogation because for our purposes it was easier to teach Sarah to answer questions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A decade later Premacks wrote: "Though she [Sarah] understood the question, she did not herself ask any questions -- unlike the child who asks interminable questions, such as What that? Who making noise? When Daddy come home? Me go Granny's house? Where puppy? Sarah never delayed the departure of her trainer after her lessons by asking where the trainer was going, when she was returning, or anything else".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite all their achievements, Kanzi and Panbanisha also have not demonstrated the ability to ask questions so far.
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Jordania" title="Joseph Jordania">
       Joseph Jordania
      </a>
      suggested that the ability to ask
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Questions" title="Questions">
       questions
      </a>
      could be the crucial
      <b>
       cognitive threshold
      </b>
      between
      <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
       human
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">
       ape
      </a>
      mental abilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Jordania suggested that asking questions is not a matter of the ability of using syntactic structures, that it is primarily a matter of cognitive ability. Questions can be (and are) asked without the use of syntactic structures, with the help of the question's intonation only (as is the case in children's early pre-linguistic development).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="g_factor_of_intelligence_in_primates">
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       factor of intelligence in primates
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Primate_cognition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: g factor of intelligence in primates">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G_Factor_in_Non-Humans" title="G Factor in Non-Humans">
       g Factor in Non-Humans
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The general factor of intelligence, or
      <a href="/wiki/G_factor_(psychometrics)" title="G factor (psychometrics)">
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       factor
      </a>
      , is a psychometric construct that summarizes the correlations observed between an individuals scores on various measures of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_abilities" title="Cognitive abilities">
       cognitive abilities
      </a>
      . First described in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans">
       humans
      </a>
      , the
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      factor has since been identified in a number of non-human species.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reader_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reader-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">
       Primates
      </a>
      in particular have been the focus of
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      research due to their close
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomic" title="Taxonomic">
       taxonomic
      </a>
      links to humans. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_Components_Analysis" title="Principal Components Analysis">
       Principal Components Analysis
      </a>
      run in a
      <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">
       meta-analysis
      </a>
      of 4000 primate behaviour papers including 62 species found that 47% of the individual variance in cognitive ability tests was accounted for by a single factor, controlling for socio-ecological variables.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reader_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reader-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This value fits within the accepted range of the influence of
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IQ" title="IQ">
       IQ
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, there is some debate as to the influence of
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      on all primates equally. A 2012 study identifying individual chimpanzees that consistently performed highly on cognitive tasks found clusters of abilities instead of a general factor of intelligence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This study used individual-based data and claim that their results are not directly comparable to previous studies using group data that have found evidence for
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      . Further research is required to identify the exact nature of
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      in primates.
     </p>
