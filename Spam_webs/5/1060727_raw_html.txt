         <article id="story-body" itemprop="articleBody">
          <p>
           BOULDER, Colo.  Recreational marijuana is both illegal and controversial in most of the
country, and its relationship to food does not rise much above a joke about brownies or a stoner
chefs late-night pork-belly poutine.
          </p>
          <p>
           But cooking with cannabis is emerging as a legitimate and lucrative culinary pursuit.
          </p>
          <p>
           In Colorado, which has issued more than 160 edible-marijuana licenses, skilled line cooks are
leaving respected restaurants to take higher-paying jobs infusing cannabis into food and
drinks.
          </p>
          <p>
           In Washington, 1 of 4 states that allows recreational marijuana sales, a large cannabis bakery
dedicated to affluent customers with good palates is to open soon in Seattle.
          </p>
          <p>
           Major New York publishing houses and noted cookbook authors are pondering marijuana projects,
and chefs on both coasts and in food-forward countries such as Denmark are staging underground
meals with modern twists such as compressed watermelon, smoked cheese and marijuana-oil
vinaigrette.
          </p>
          <p>
           It really wont be long until it becomes part of haute cuisine and part of respectable culinary
culture, instead of just an illegal doobie in the backyard, said Ken Albala, director of the
food-studies program at the University of the Pacific in San Francisco.
          </p>
          <p>
           But there are two problems: First, its hard to control how high people get when they eat
marijuana. And second, it really doesnt taste that good.
          </p>
          <p>
           Still, what if chefs could develop a culinary canon around marijuana that tamed both its taste
and mood-altering effects, and diners came to appreciate dishes with marijuana the way one
appreciates good bourbon?
          </p>
          <p>
           I am sure someone is going to grow some that is actually delicious, and well all learn about
it, said Ruth Reichl, a former editor of
           <em>
            Gourmet
           </em>
           magazine.
          </p>
          <p>
           Cuisine is a product of people who cook and the ideologies they bring into the kitchen and what
they are able to do with the instruments they have on hand, said Adam Gomolin, a lawyer and
amateur chef who helped found the crowd-funded publishing company Inkshares.
          </p>
          <p>
           Matt Gray, of the website The Stoners Cookbook
           <em>
            ,
           </em>
           predicts that the legal-marijuana industry will be worth $10.2 billion in five years and
that edible marijuana could be as much as 40 percent of that.
          </p>
          <p>
           To get a consistent, controllable effect, marijuana is best heated and combined with fats such
as butter or cream.
          </p>
          <p>
           But it could also work  albeit less effectively  as a seasoning.
          </p>
          <p>
           Karin Lazarus runs Sweet Mary Jane, a commercial bakery in Boulder. Her recipes will be featured
in
           <em>
            Sweet Mary Jane: 75 Delicious Cannabis-Infused High-End Desserts
           </em>
           , due out in June.
          </p>
          <p>
           Lazarus, who
           <em>
            New York
           </em>
           magazine called the Martha Stewart of weed baking, makes confections such as
the Scouts Honor, a play on the Thin Mint cookie, and tart key lime and white chocolate
truffles.
          </p>
          <p>
           Because law prohibits tasting dosed products at work, she first works out recipes without using
marijuana, and then adds cannabis-infused sugar, oil or butter. She tests the products in a
laboratory. They get taste-tested as well, but not at work.
          </p>
          <p>
           For the moment, her products are for the medical-marijuana market, which allows for higher doses
than food sold under a recreational license.
          </p>
          <p>
           Cooks who work with cannabis are apt to compare it to cooking with wine or spirits. But
opponents counter that a bottle of young red wine brings an important flavor component to a dish
such as beef bourguignon. In cannabis cookery, the point is usually to mask the taste.
          </p>
          <p>
           But Melissa Parks, a Denver chef who only rarely uses cannabis and began cooking with it to help
a friend with cancer, says pot can be delicious.
          </p>
          <p>
           There are dozens of strains, and some might smell like lemon grass or strawberry or sage or
wheatgrass, she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Different strains also offer different highs. A well-placed dose of cannabis might provide just
enough elevation in an appetizer or a calming finish to a meal.
          </p>
          <p>
           A lot of people could argue that a lot of alcohol doesnt taste good, either, Reichl said. So
maybe you wont need to drink wine with your dinner.
          </p>
