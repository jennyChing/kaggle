       <h1 itemprop="headline name">
        The secrets of the art world waiting to be found
       </h1>
       <h2 itemprop="alternativeHeadline description">
        Paintings and music are full of sly messages that are only partially hidden, 
  as if the artist is challenging us to discover them
       </h2>

           By
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/journalists/ivan-hewett/" rel="author" title="Ivan Hewett">
            Ivan Hewett
           </a>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p class="publishedDate">
          7:18PM GMT 07 Mar 2015
         </p>
         <meta content="2015-03-07" itemprop="datePublished"/>
         <p class="comments">
          <img alt="Comments" src="/template/ver1-0/i/share/comments.gif"/>
          <a dsqid="11456615" href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/culture/art/11456615/The-secrets-of-the-art-world-waiting-to-be-found.html#disqus_thread">

        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           Artists like to keep their secrets, but this week the portrait painter Daphne Todd let one slip out. Live on Radio 4, she confessed that she once added a pair of painted horns to her portrait of an obnoxious young gentleman, who had kept her waiting when she turned up for a sitting.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           Which obnoxious young gentleman? is the question were all dying to have answered. But Todd isnt saying. Shes hidden the horns under several layers of paint, but is confident they will bleed through to the surface one day. It may be 50 years or 100 years, who knows. But they are there if you X-ray it.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           Artistic secrets of this kind could stay secret for ever, if the artist didnt spill the beans. But theyre not the only kind. Art is full of sly messages that are often only partially hidden, as if the artist is challenging us to find them. I found one myself years ago, in the Muse des Beaux Arts in Brussels. This museum has a fabulously rich collection of Flemish art, including whole rooms of Breughel. One of these paintings (I cant remember which, so Breughels secret is safe) has a riotous scene of outdoor drunken revelry, in the foreground.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           My eye wandered over the main scene, and then drifted up to the rolling hills and patches of woodland in the background. Dotted here and there were quite a few tiny figures, hoeing, carrying wood, drinking water from a pitcher. Then I saw something I couldnt quite believe: a male figure facing away from us, pulling his cloak apart. Facing him is a woman, her mouth a round O of horror. No doubt about it: Breughel is portraying a flasher.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           This amazing detail wasnt mentioned in the catalogue, or in the scholarly literature on the painting. So even if it wasnt literally a secret, it certainly felt like one. This is an example of one sort of secret  something we dont know is there until someone stumbles across it. Its an unknown unknown, to use Donald Rumsfelds phrase. There are famous examples of these, like the elongated anamorphic skull in Holbeins The Ambassadors, which can only be seen when you view the painting from one side.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="related-links-float-design related_links_inline" id="tmg-related-links">

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           More interesting are the known unknowns  things that are plainly in view, but which we cant grasp.
          </p>
          <p>
           Huge swathes of medieval and Renaissance painting fall into this category, with their complex assemblages of symbols were no longer able to read. Whether its a Biblical scene, or a mythological one, were often sorely tested when it comes to the simple business of saying who is who. Apart from scholars, who can distinguish St Catherine of Siena from St Catherine of Egypt, in an altarpiece?
          </p>
          <p>
           These symbols come laden with meanings and values, which at one time were common property in the culture. When Masaccio or Bellini include them in a painting, they may be obscure to us but you dont feel the artist is being deliberately misleading, or trying to make a personal point. By the time we get to Hogarth, the great chronicler of the vices and follies of 18th-century England, theres been a profound change.
          </p>
          <p>
           Here the symbols have a satirical edge, and you get the sense Hogarth enjoys teasing the viewer by mixing things that are obvious with things that are baffling. In his painting The Roast Beef of Old England, he portrays a ridiculous bandy-legged Frenchman struggling under the weight of a side of roast beef. Thats an obvious dig at the French, but not many will grasp the meaning of the crow perched on the gate, or the white dove hanging on the inn sign above the cross. The first refers to the treacherous Jacobites, who had found refuge in France. The other is a satirical reference to the Catholic Church, perhaps hinting that Catholics were more adept at tippling than praying.
          </p>
          <p>
           This semi-obscurity is what allows artists to get away with things that stated baldly in print would be banned. When the Anglican Bishop Warburton saw Hogarths Enthusiasm Delineated, he was appalled by its portrayal of lewd preachers and over-excited congregations. But he took comfort in the fact that, as he put it, the worst parts of it have a good chance of not being understood by the people.
          </p>
          <p>
           Turn to the other arts, and you find the same truth applies: anything that we cant spot or cant understand is effectively a secret. Music is an especially rich field for secrets, because its a slippery art form, hard to pin down to a specific meaning. Also, notes can be paired off with letters to spell out messages, which you could never spot without being forewarned. Robert Schumann was obsessed with musical codes, and his music often spells out the name of a current girlfriend. Bachs name when turned into notes spells out a four-note phrase, which he included in several works, including the unfinished Art of Fugue.
          </p>
          <p>
           Shostakovich works in a different way, using musical phrases that (according to some) act as coded messages of defiance against the Soviet regime. But is the finale of the 5th Symphony really a grotesque parody of a Stalinist parade? And does his use of Stalins favourite tune Suliko in the 1st Cello Concerto really have a subversive edge?
          </p>
          <p>
           Once you get into meanings, ambiguity and doubt are bound to enter the picture. Which brings me back to Daphne Todds portrait. What exactly are those horns on the obnoxious young gentleman supposed to signify? They could simply mean hes as obnoxious as the devil, but lets not forget adding horns to an image of a man was a way of hinting that the poor devil had been cuckolded.
          </p>
          <p>
           Perhaps Todds revenge is even sweeter than it seems.
          </p>

            var openArticleContainer = function () {
                if (!settings.panelOpen) {
                	$("#readNextArticleContainer").show();//show/hide because height is calculated incorrectly for elements with display:none
                    $("#readNextArticleContainer").hide();
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_open', 'version_1']);
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                    settings.autoOpen = false; //set auto open to false once user has viewed this once
                }
                return false;
            };

            var closeArticleContainer = function () {
                if (settings.panelOpen) {
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                }
                return false;
            };


            var isScrolledIntoView = function(elem) {
                if($(elem).length) { //check if element exists
                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));
                } else {
                    return false;
                }
            };

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {
                    return index == arr.indexOf(el);
                });
            }

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
