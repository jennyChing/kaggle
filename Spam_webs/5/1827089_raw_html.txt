         <div class="wysiwyg article-for-flyin article-content">
          <p>
           It was three years ago last week that I started practicing yoga. I got into it because I was looking for a way to stay in shape when my knees and other health challenges more or less brought an end to my thirty five year career as a runner. I thought I was in pretty good shape but my first yoga class seemed like it would never end. Three years later, Ive taken close to 1,000 classes and, about a month ago, completed a 200 hour yoga teacher training program.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its hard for me to believe Ive spent so much time on a yoga mat. Theres been a lot more to it than bending and stretching. Over the past three years, Ive written a number of posts about what Ive learned from yoga. I spent some time reviewing them last week and, honestly, I got a little choked up. It was humbling to realize that skills and knowledge I take for granted now are things that I literally couldnt imagine doing three years ago.
          </p>
          <p>
           As much as anything else, yoga for me has been a vehicle for learning. Thats been my point in writing about it here. You dont have to do yoga to learn about life and leadership. Its worked for me. Something else might work for you.
          </p>
          <p>
           In any case, based on my own historical record, here are five things Ive learned about life and leadership from three years of practicing yoga.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            1. Reps Matter
           </strong>
           : My first class three years ago was with an instructor named Jeanne. When I introduced myself to her before class, she said, If you take three classes a week, yoga will change your body. If you take more than three classes a week, it will change your life. She was right on both counts.
           <em>
            Repetition shapes your body, your mind and your habits. Choose your reps wisely.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            2.
            <a href="http://eblingroup.com/2011/06/new-leadership-lessons-from-yoga.html">
             Instruction Matters
            </a>
            :
           </strong>
           Even if you choose the right reps, its important to have solid instruction. There are two big reasons for that. With no instruction or poor instruction, all those reps can groove in as bad habits that youll have to work very hard to get rid of. With good instruction, youll learn how to go beyond what you thought you could do.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            3.
            <a href="http://eblingroup.com/2010/12/leadership-lessons-from-yoga.html">
             Improvement Comes Incrementally, Then Suddenly
            </a>
            :
           </strong>
           The last three years has been one surprise after another in terms of my physical capabilities. The first three months were about developing the strength and range of motion to do upward facing bow pose. The second year and into the third was about learning to a headstand in the middle of the room. This summer was about learning how to get up in handstand and doing it without balancing my feet against the wall. If all of that sounds like a slow process, it is. What Ive learned is that
           <em>
            improvement comes incrementally, then suddenly.
           </em>
           You can work on something for days or weeks or months and not really see much progress. Then suddenly, you can do it. An overnight success, so to speak, except it took a long time to get there.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            4.
            <a href="http://eblingroup.com/2013/06/focus-on-what-you-can-do-now.html">
             Your Capabilities Arent Determined By Your Limitations
            </a>
            :
           </strong>
           If yoga has taught me anything its this 
           <em>
            my capabilities arent determined by my limitations
           </em>
           .Ive been consistently amazed by what Ive been able to learn and do over the past three years. It makes me realize that limitations come in two flavors  mental and physical. They are both subject to change.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            5.
            <a href="http://eblingroup.com/2012/04/3-new-leadership-lessons-from-yoga.html">
             Show Up and Deal with Whats Presented
            </a>
            :
           </strong>
           Are there days when I feel crummy and dont want to go to class? Most definitely. Do I go anyway? Almost always. Ive learned that if I show up, Im going to feel better at the end nine times out of ten. Yoga has taught me that
           <em>
            there is no such thing as a permanent condition. Everything is subject to change and it does.
           </em>
           It just makes sense, then, to show up and deal with whats presented.
          </p>
          <p>
           Im pretty convinced that you cant be a perfectionist and enjoy yoga. Theres always something new to learn. When youre learning, youre not perfect.
           <em>
            If you have to be perfect, youre not going to learn anything new.
           </em>
           All the time Ive spent on a yoga mat learning something new has taught me that I dont have to be so damn hard on myself. Ironically, Im probably doing better work off the mat because Ive lightened up on the pressure I put on myself to be perfect.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            What about you? If youre into yoga, what has it taught you about life and leadership? If there is some other routine or practice that you follow, what is it and what have you learned from it?
           </em>
          </p>

             Henry Stockton/Shutterstock.com
            </a>
           </em>
          </p>
