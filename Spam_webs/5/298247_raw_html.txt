   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Jos Guadalupe Posada
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

     <p>
      <b>
       Jos Guadalupe Posada
      </b>
      (February 2, 1852  January 20, 1913
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) was a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_people" title="Mexican people">
       Mexican
      </a>
      political
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printmaker" title="Printmaker">
       printmaker
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engraver" title="Engraver">
       engraver
      </a>
      whose work has influenced many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American" title="Latin American">
       Latin American
      </a>
      artists and
      <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">
       cartoonists
      </a>
      because of its satirical acuteness and social engagement. He used skulls,
      <a href="/wiki/Calavera" title="Calavera">
       calaveras
      </a>
      , and skeletons to make political and cultural critiques.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Posada2.Catrina.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="956" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Posada2.Catrina.jpeg/220px-Posada2.Catrina.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Posada2.Catrina.jpeg/330px-Posada2.Catrina.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Posada2.Catrina.jpeg/440px-Posada2.Catrina.jpeg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Posada2.Catrina.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         La Calavera Catrina
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Posada was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Aguascalientes" title="Aguascalientes">
       Aguascalientes
      </a>
      on February 2, 1852. His education in his early years was drawn from his older brother Cirilo, a country school teacher, who taught him reading, writing and drawing. As a young teenager he went to work in the workshop of Trinidad Pedroso, who taught him
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithograph" title="Lithograph">
       lithography
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">
       engraving
      </a>
      . In 1871, before he was out of his teens, his career began with a job as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_cartoonist" title="Political cartoonist">
       political cartoonist
      </a>
      for a local newspaper in Aguascalientes,
      <i>
       El Jicote
      </i>
      ("The Bumblebee"). The newspaper closed after 11 issues, reputedly because one of Posada's cartoons had offended a powerful local politician.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He then moved to the nearby city of
      <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n,_Guanajuato" title="Len, Guanajuato">
       Len, Guanajuato
      </a>
      . There Posada was married to Maria de Jess Vela on September 20, 1875. In Leon, a former associate of his from Aguascalientes assisted him in starting a printing and commercial illustration shop. They focused on commercial and
      <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">
       advertising
      </a>
      work, book illustrations, and the printing of posters and other representations of historical and religious figures. Included among these figures were the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_of_Guadalupe" title="Virgin of Guadalupe">
       Virgin of Guadalupe
      </a>
      , the Virgin, the Holy Child of Atocha and Saint Sebastian. In 1883, following his success, he was hired as a teacher of lithography at the local Preparatory School. The shop flourished until 1888 when a disastrous flood hit the city. He subsequently moved to Mexico City. His first regular employment in the capital was with
      <i>
       La Patria Ilustrada
      </i>
      , whose editor was
      <a href="/wiki/Ireneo_Paz" title="Ireneo Paz">
       Ireneo Paz
      </a>
      , the grandfather of the later famed writer
      <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Paz" title="Octavio Paz">
       Octavio Paz
      </a>
      . He later joined the staff of a publishing firm owned by Antonio Vanegas Arroyo and while at this firm he created a prolific number of book covers and illustrations. Much of his work was also published in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensationalist" title="Sensationalist">
       sensationalistic
      </a>
      broadsides depicting various current events.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2281" data-file-width="4538" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg/240px-Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg/360px-Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg/480px-Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gran_calavera_el%C3%A9ctrica2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        "Gran calavera elctrica" (Grand electric skull) by Jos Guadalupe Posada, 1900-1913. Restored reproduction.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Posada's best known works are his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Calavera" title="Calavera">
        calaveras
       </a>
      </i>
      , which often assume various costumes, such as the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/La_Calavera_Catrina" title="La Calavera Catrina">
        Calavera de la Catrina
       </a>
      </i>
      , the "Skull of the Female
      <a href="/wiki/Dandy" title="Dandy">
       Dandy
      </a>
      ", which was meant to
      <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">
       satirize
      </a>
      the life of the
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">
       upper classes
      </a>
      during the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Porfirio_D%C3%ADaz" title="Porfirio Daz">
       Porfirio Daz
      </a>
      . Most of his imagery was meant to make a
      <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">
       religious
      </a>
      or satirical point. Since his death, however, his images have become associated with the Mexican
      <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">
       holiday
      </a>
      <i>
       Da de los Muertos
      </i>
      , the "
      <a href="/wiki/Day_of_the_Dead" title="Day of the Dead">
       Day of the Dead
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1156" data-file-width="792" height="321" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG/220px-JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG/330px-JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG/440px-JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JG_Posada_Print_shop.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        J.G. Posada print shop, Mexico, ca 1900 (Posada not present)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Largely forgotten by the end of his life, Posada's engravings were brought to a wider audience in the 1920s by the French artist
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charlot" title="Jean Charlot">
       Jean Charlot
      </a>
      , who encountered them while visiting
      <a href="/wiki/Diego_Rivera" title="Diego Rivera">
       Diego Rivera
      </a>
      . While Posada died in
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       poverty
      </a>
      , his images are well known today as examples of
      <a href="/wiki/Folk_art" title="Folk art">
       folk art
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Mural" title="Mural">
       muralist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Clemente_Orozco" title="Jos Clemente Orozco">
       Jos Clemente Orozco
      </a>
      knew Posada when he was young, and would look at him work though a window on the way to school, and credited Posada's work as an influence on his own.
     </p>
