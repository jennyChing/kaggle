          <h1>
           You Had me at Hello: Pronunciation Patterns of Famous Movie Quotes
          </h1>
         </header>
         <p>
          <img alt="movie quotes" class="size-medium wp-image-8194 alignleft" height="300" src="//saundz.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/gone-with-the-wind-261x300.jpg" style="margin-left: 20px;margin-right: 20px" width="261"/>
          Only a few films take on legendary status as classics  a result of multiple elements, like a script and its delivery, coming together for great effect.What is your favorite Hollywood classic? Maybe The Wizard of Oz comes to mind, or maybe its Terminator.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           SEE ALSO:
          </strong>
          <a href="//saundz.com/pronunciation-lesson-from-arnold-schwarzenegger/" title="English Pronunciation Lesson from Arnold Schwarzenegger">
           English Pronunciation Lesson from Arnold Schwarzenegger
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Now consider a famous quote in that movie.In many great classic films theres at least one line that stands out as memorable, so memorable that if you said it to complete strangers, theyd likely know where it came from.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h3>
          1.General context
         </h3>
         <p>
          Nonetheless, there are thousands of lines in those films, so what makes some of them, like The Godfathers Im going to make him an offer he cant refuse, get stuck in your head, but not This Hollywood big-shots going to give you what you want  both lines from the same scene, both spoken by the same character?
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/SeldwfOwuL8?wmode=transparent" width="640">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           Cornell University study of a databases list of top 1,000 film quotes found that indefinite articles are more common than definite articles for a similar reason.If it were Im going to make him the offer he cant refuse, then we would wonder, Which offer?.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <h3>
          2.Simple syntax
         </h3>
         <p>
          If we look at the patterns of famous movie lines, one of the most fundamental traits is universality.
          <em>
           Theres no place like home
          </em>
           applies to contexts outside of Oz of course: it might be used in real-world contexts.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are other, less abstract factors in English that communicate generalization, including usage and syntax.
         </p>
         <p>
          These factors focus on not getting the brain hung up on particulars.Most pronouns, for example, are in first or second person so that they dont take the mind away from the scene, causing our brains to veer off into Who is he? rather than stay involved in the moment.When third person pronouns are used, such as in the mentioned The Godfather quote, youll usually find the action and not its object emphasized.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/g2wD5TaMf2k?wmode=transparent" width="640">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           In a similar vein, simple syntax doesnt require the brain to pause for processing, like this quote from Scarface.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <h3>
          3.Distinct Vocabulary and Pronunciation
         </h3>
         <p>
          Now lets look at the distinguishing traits that actually do make a quote stand out enough for fame.
         </p>
         <p>
          Although famous lines often have straight-forward syntax, their vocabulary can be distinct, such as Casablancas, 
          <em>
           Heres to looking at you, kid
          </em>
           and Gone with the Winds 
          <em>
           Frankly, my dear, I dont give a damn.
          </em>
           See Clark Gable in action:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/PAqxWa9Rbe0?wmode=transparent" width="640">
           </iframe>
           <br/>
           The actor contributes vitally to a lines panache through intonation, emphasis, not to mention visual attitude, and often regional or foreign dialect is key.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <h3>
          4.Dramatic Intonation
         </h3>
         <p>
          If the language is appropriately un-self-conscious, then what we remember is these principles of delivery (along with the backdrop of a scenes atmosphere).
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/mevxenJ6Mtc?wmode=transparent" width="640">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           A dramatic drop of intonation (as Clint Eastwood has shown time and time again) communicates powerful authority, while a dramatic intonational rise could convey memorable desperation.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          In a similar way, foreign accent or a specific regional dialect, may make both an actor and a quote more exotic and, therefore, more memorable.We saw this with Schwarzenegger and many other actors in recent times.
         </p>
         <h3>
          5.Charm and Magic of Holywood
         </h3>
         <p>
          Linguistically, a quote becomes famous through a combination of unobtrusive language structure, enough distinction to be unique without distraction, delivery, and maybe just a bit of Hollywood magic.
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/-_kwXNVCaxY?wmode=transparent" width="640">
           </iframe>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
