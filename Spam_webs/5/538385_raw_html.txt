     <p>
      A
      <b>
       Bingham plastic
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscoplastic" title="Viscoplastic">
       viscoplastic
      </a>
      material that behaves as a rigid body at low stresses but flows as a
      <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
       viscous
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">
       fluid
      </a>
      at high stress. It is named after
      <a href="/wiki/Eugene_C._Bingham" title="Eugene C. Bingham">
       Eugene C. Bingham
      </a>
      who proposed its mathematical form.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is used as a common
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">
       mathematical model
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Mud" title="Mud">
       mud
      </a>
      flow in
      <a href="/wiki/Drilling_engineering" title="Drilling engineering">
       drilling engineering
      </a>
      , and in the handling of
      <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">
       slurries
      </a>
      . A common example is
      <a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">
       toothpaste
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steffe_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Steffe-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which will not be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extruded" title="Extruded">
       extruded
      </a>
      until a certain
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      is applied to the tube. It then is pushed out as a solid plug.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">
       Explanation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:304px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bingham1a.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="301" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bingham1a.svg/302px-Bingham1a.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bingham1a.svg/453px-Bingham1a.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Bingham1a.svg/604px-Bingham1a.svg.png 2x" width="302"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bingham1a.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 1. Bingham Plastic flow as described by Bingham
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Figure 1
      </b>
      shows a graph of the behaviour of an ordinary viscous (or Newtonian) fluid in red, for example in a pipe. If the pressure at one end of a pipe is increased this produces a stress on the fluid tending to make it move (called the
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">
       shear stress
      </a>
      ) and the volumetric flow rate increases proportionally. However for a Bingham Plastic fluid (in blue), stress can be applied but it will not flow until a certain value, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yield_stress" title="Yield stress">
       yield stress
      </a>
      , is reached. Beyond this point the flow rate increases steadily with increasing shear stress. This is roughly the way in which Bingham presented his observation, in an experimental study of paints.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These properties allow a Bingham plastic to have a textured surface with peaks and ridges instead of a featureless surface like a Newtonian fluid.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:304px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bingham2a.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="301" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Bingham2a.svg/302px-Bingham2a.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Bingham2a.svg/453px-Bingham2a.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Bingham2a.svg/604px-Bingham2a.svg.png 2x" width="302"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bingham2a.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 2. Bingham Plastic flow as described currently
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Figure 2
      </b>
      shows the way in which it is normally presented currently.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steffe_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Steffe-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The graph shows
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">
       shear stress
      </a>
      on the vertical axis and
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_rate" title="Shear rate">
       shear rate
      </a>
      on the horizontal one. (Volumetric flow rate depends on the size of the pipe, shear rate is a measure of how the velocity changes with distance. It is proportional to flow rate, but does not depend on pipe size.) As before, the Newtonian fluid flows and gives a shear rate for any finite value of shear stress. However, the Bingham Plastic again does not exhibit any shear rate (no flow and thus no velocity) until a certain stress is achieved. For the Newtonian fluid the slope of this line is the
      <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
       viscosity
      </a>
      , which is the only parameter needed to describe its flow. By contrast the Bingham Plastic requires two parameters, the
      <b>
       yield stress
      </b>
      and the slope of the line, known as the
      <b>
       plastic viscosity
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The physical reason for this behaviour is that the liquid contains particles (e.g. clay) or large molecules (e.g. polymers) which have some kind of interaction, creating a weak solid structure, formerly known as a
      <b>
       false body
      </b>
      , and a certain amount of stress is required to break this structure. Once the structure has been broken, the particles move with the liquid under viscous forces. If the stress is removed, the particles associate again.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The material is an elastic solid for
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">
       shear stress
      </a>
      <i>
       
      </i>
      , less than a critical value
      <img alt="\tau_0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/3/e/53eafda0d7d4671b6f275f4977e8709a.png"/>
      . Once the critical shear
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">
       stress
      </a>
      (or "
      <a href="/wiki/Yield_(engineering)" title="Yield (engineering)">
       yield stress
      </a>
      ") is exceeded, the material flows in such a way that the
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_rate" title="Shear rate">
       shear rate
      </a>
      , 
      <i>
       u
      </i>
      /
      <i>
       y
      </i>
      (as defined in the article on
      <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
       viscosity
      </a>
      ), is directly proportional to the amount by which the applied shear stress exceeds the yield stress:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\frac {\partial u} {\partial y} = \left\{\begin{matrix} 0 &amp;, \tau &lt; \tau_0 \\ (\tau - \tau_0)/ {\mu_\infty} &amp;, \tau \ge \tau_0 \end{matrix}\right." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/6/376382c5fe282124eb4fafcdb1ad782c.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Friction_factor_formulae">
       Friction factor formulae
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Friction factor formulae">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In fluid flow, it is a common problem to calculate the pressure drop in an established piping network.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Once the friction factor,
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      , is known, it becomes easier to handle different pipe-flow problems, viz. calculating the pressure drop for evaluating pumping costs or to find the flow-rate in a piping network for a given pressure drop. It is usually extremely difficult to arrive at exact analytical solution to calculate the friction factor associated with flow of non-Newtonian fluids and therefore explicit approximations are used to calculate it. Once the friction factor has been calculated the pressure drop can be easily determined for a given flow by the
      <a href="/wiki/Darcy%E2%80%93Weisbach_equation" title="DarcyWeisbach equation">
       DarcyWeisbach equation
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ f = \ {2 h_f g D \over L V^2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/5/a/b5aadca0865565f7564bc8ad8a335bc0.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ h_f}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/6/4/664a5f52e23e28438c6222c477dd5fe8.png"/>
       is the frictional head loss (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : m)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ f}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/1/7/7170130cf621e07c1201097b9940f344.png"/>
       is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darcy_friction_factor" title="Darcy friction factor">
        Darcy friction factor
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : Dimensionless)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ L}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/d/1/2d1d4926ec03714293bd3edd294040ca.png"/>
       is the pipe length (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : m)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ g}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/0/f70723604cc1c6242913e0f0d80e3272.png"/>
       is the gravitational acceleration (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : m/s)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ D}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/8/b/18b89a22675f9f60278ff14f19ae5ffd.png"/>
       is the pipe diameter (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : m)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ V}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/e/c/fec82571ad396bddba70bd184845d11f.png"/>
       is the mean fluid velocity (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : m/s)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Laminar_flow">
       Laminar flow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Laminar flow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An exact description of friction loss for Bingham plastics in fully developed laminar pipe flow was first published by Buckingham.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His expression, the
      <i>
       Buckingham-Reiner
      </i>
      equation, can be written in a dimensionless form as follows:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ f_L = \ {64 \over Re}\left[1 + {He\over 6 Re} - {64\over3}\left({He^4\over {f}^3 Re^7}\right)\right]" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/0/f/00fbbea68775d8dc5d1a762ccfb7fc06.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ f}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/1/7/7170130cf621e07c1201097b9940f344.png"/>
       is the laminar flow friction factor (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : Dimensionless)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ Re}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/3/7b38383096e53896c16f4fcdeec9ba05.png"/>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">
        Reynolds number
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : Dimensionless)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ He}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/4/b/a4b113c827801e4a8d2ceb272cde856d.png"/>
       is the Hedstrom number (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : Dimensionless)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">
       Reynolds number
      </a>
      and the Hedstrom number are respectively defined as:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \mathrm{Re} = { \rho {\ V} D  \over {\mu}} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/6/a/e6ade892875d6475389a2997b974d7bb.png"/>
       , and
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \mathrm{He} = { \rho  {\ D^2 } {\ \tau_o} \over {{\mu}^2}} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/4/3/643e86fb232959f825e0cb15837ab0dc.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \rho}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/5/d/b5d7e3d3ec19389caebc2a2c34241b33.png"/>
       is the mass density of fluid (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : kg/m
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ \mu}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/4/1/141bd860cd139ee8cce3f7e5a79c1a7f.png"/>
       is the dynamic
       <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
        viscosity
       </a>
       of fluid (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : kg/m s)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ \tau_o}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/9/0/b90dd94ff1f7437909d17d4ab7ae3474.png"/>
       is the yield point (yield strength) of fluid (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : MPa)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Turbulent_flow">
       Turbulent flow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Turbulent flow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Darby and Melson developed an empirical expression
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darby_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Darby-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that was then refined, and is given by:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darbyetal_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Darbyetal-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ f_T = \ {10^a} \ {Re^{-0.193}} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/3/b/93ba08085cdcf1aa05ede00426520c74.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <img alt="{\bold \ f_T}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/5/8/85800916fd46e1017aad110b0c0b196d.png"/>
       is the turbulent flow friction factor (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">
        SI units
       </a>
       : Dimensionless)
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt=" \ a = -1.47\left[1 + 0.146{\ e^{-2.9\times {10^{-5}}He}}\right] " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/6/7/667ed6661b9d420933359ef93bea9a67.png"/>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      (This friction factor appears to be a Fanning friction factor, and will need to be multiplied by 4 to be used in the Darcy pressure drop equation mentioned at the top of the page)
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Approximations_of_the_Buckingham-Reiner_equation">
       Approximations of the
       <i>
        Buckingham-Reiner
       </i>
       equation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Approximations of the Buckingham-Reiner equation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although an exact analytical solution of the
      <i>
       Buckingham-Reiner
      </i>
      equation can be obtained because it is a fourth order polynomial equation in
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      , due to complexity of the solution it is rarely employed. Therefore, researchers have tried to develop explicit approximations for the
      <i>
       Buckingham-Reiner
      </i>
      equation.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Swamee-Aggarwal_Equation">
       Swamee-Aggarwal Equation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Swamee-Aggarwal Equation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Swamee Aggarwal
      </i>
      equation is used to solve directly for the DarcyWeisbach friction factor
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      for laminar flow of Bingham plastic fluids.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is an approximation of the implicit
      <i>
       Buckingham-Reiner
      </i>
      equation, but the discrepancy from experimental data is well within the accuracy of the data. The
      <i>
       Swamee-Aggarwal
      </i>
      equation is given by:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\ f_{L}=\ {64 \over Re}+{64 \over Re}\left({He \over {6.2218Re}}\right)^{{0.958}}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/0/9/709af6419dd685f7fbfff1bf523451a9.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Danish-Kumar_Solution">
       Danish-Kumar Solution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Danish-Kumar Solution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Danish
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      have provided an explicit procedure to calculate the friction factor
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      by using the Adomian decomposition method.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The friction factor containing two terms through this method is given as:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" f_L = \frac{K_1 + \dfrac{4 K_2}{\left( K_1 + \frac{K_1 K_2}{K_1^4 + 3 K_2}\right)^3}}{1+ \dfrac{3 K_2}{\left(K_1 + \frac{K_1 K_2}{K_1^4 + 3 K_2}\right)^4}}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/3/b/13b0be195dc9f6dab9936fcc29255602.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ K_1 = \ {16 \over Re} + {16 He \over 6{Re^2}}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/b/f/bbf90212b5952e5332861c4b08fa2753.png"/>
       , and
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ K_2 = \ - {16 {He^4} \over 3{Re^8}}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/1/bc177d84d64b238abc4e5d58b48cabdb.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_equation_for_friction_factor_for_all_flow_regimes">
       Combined equation for friction factor for all flow regimes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Combined equation for friction factor for all flow regimes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Darby-Melson_equation">
       Darby-Melson equation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingham_plastic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Darby-Melson equation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1981, Darby and Melson, using the approach of Churchill
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and of Churchill and Usagi,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      developed an expression to get a single friction factor equation valid for all flow regimes:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darby_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Darby-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ f = \ {\left[{f_L}^m + {f_T}^m\right]}^{1\over m}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/d/f/ddfb78bdec895d979c4f61c992febbb0.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \ m = \ 1.7 + {40000\over Re} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/e/0/0e02de543aff3dbba53f8726eda6014e.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Both
      <i>
       Swamee-Aggarwal
      </i>
      equation and the
      <i>
       Darby-Melson
      </i>
      equation can be combined to give an explicit equation for determining the friction factor of Bingham plastic fluids in any regime. Relative roughness is not a parameter in any of the equations because the friction factor of Bingham plastic fluids is not sensitive to pipe roughness.
     </p>
