         <p>
          A simple website design shouldnt be a daunting all-or-nothing ordeal  you can simplify your design by taking small steps. Simple tasks like putting the focus only on the essential elements of your website, getting rid of the unnecessary, reducing the number of pages your site has, getting more content above the fold, and limiting the number of colors you use. You can always fine-tune and improve your simple design, but the important thing here is to get started.
         </p>
         <p>
          Ready to start simplifying your website design? Awesome. Without further ado, here are
          <strong>
           5 starting points to simplify your website design
          </strong>

         <p>
          This first step probably seems forehead-slapping obvious:
          <em>
           of
          </em>
          <em>
           course
          </em>
          <em>
          </em>
          <em>
           I should put the focus on the essential elements in my site, what am I, an idiot?
          </em>
          But a surprising number of websites fail to achieve this and the result is a big mess of important and unimportant elements spewed onto a page. Im as guilty of doing this in the past as anybody. Its hard to be objective and prioritize whats important or not, because
          <em>
           everything
          </em>
          seems essential. If you want your website design to be simpler, identify what
          <strong>
           needs to be focus
          </strong>
          , just like with any good visual design or piece of art. And that means putting the focus only on the essential elements.
         </p>
         <h4>
          <strong>
           Use the 80-20 rule
          </strong>
         </h4>
         <p>
          What 20% of whats on a page gives 80% of the value and content that people go there for? It could be the copy, some social proof (review snippets, testimonials, media badges), and a signup form or call-to-action button, for example. Thats the 20% right there. On your website, as well as on each individual page,
          <strong>
           focus on displaying only the 20% of site elements that are delivering 80% of that usefulness.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          This isnt a technical step but a principle that you can use as your guide to simplify your website design constantly. The 80-20 rule will help simplify your website design by pushing you to trim your site elements down to the essentials. Whats really cool is that the 80-20 rule can also help increase your desired results that you hope to achieve on your website. For example, an increased conversion rate in visitors subscribing, signing up, or buying. How?
          <strong>
           Youre making it so there are less distractions
          </strong>
          and things for visitors to click on to leave the page.
         </p>
         <p>
          As we all know, were always looking for an excuse not to purchase something at the final step, and any reason to navigate away is a good one. Reduce those reasons and click-away options with the 80-20 rule.
         </p>
         <p>
          Chris Pearson, creator of the popular
          <a href="http://diythemes.com">
           Thesis
          </a>
          <a class="aal" href="http://hongki.at/elegant" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="WordPress theme">
           WordPress theme
          </a>
          framework,
          <a href="http://mixergy.com/chris-pearson-thesis-interview/">
           says this is exactly what helped increase sales on his website
          </a>
          .
         </p>

          <strong>
           get rid of all unnecessary elements
          </strong>
          . In other words, the 80% of website elements that will get you only 20% of results. It could be social media sharing widgets, sidebar elements, blog post meta details (date, time, author, number of comments, etc), or links in the footer (this is especially a huge culprit a lot of the times, particularly when the visitor is looking for the aforementioned excuse to navigate away from the page).
         </p>

         <p>
          A large part of simplifying your website design is to simply have fewer places to explore and click around. You can do that by
          <strong>
           trimming the page count.
          </strong>
          Either get rid of unnecessary pages that deep down inside you know arent needed, or at the very least, fuse multiple pages into one. I mean, you dont
          <em>
           really
          </em>
          need to separate "about the site" and "about me" pages.
         </p>
         <p>
          Firstly, get in the mindset of the visitor  if you were to arrive on your website,
          <em>
           what are the key things would you want to do?
          </em>
          For instance
          <em>
           find out what your stuff is about?
          </em>
          Or
          <em>
           contact you?
          </em>
          Next,
          <strong>
           make sure that your pages facilitate what is necessary and nothing more
          </strong>
          . Dont keep unnecessary pages on your website because you think you need to, or because other websites have them. When you reduce the number of pages on your website, not only it is easier for your visitors to focus on your content because theres less places to click around, but your navigation menu is simpler too.
         </p>
         <p>
          Weve all been on websites with too many nav menu items. We dont know where to start navigating because we get overwhelmed by the choices. And when we get overwhelmed by being presented with too many choices, we go with choosing nothing. By having as few nav menu items as possible, you make your website not only simpler but more inviting and friendlier to visitors.
         </p>

          </div>
         </div>
         <p>
          Its easy to get carried away with colors. Why settle on 2 or 3 colors when you can have 12 or 13? But in order to simplify your website design visually, you need to limit your color scheme.
          <strong>
           When in doubt, use fewer colors.
          </strong>
          Itll vary based on your design of course, but try sticking with no more than 2 or 3 colors to start off. If you need more subtlety and texture to your visual design, use shades of the same color  light blue for the background and darker blue for header and menu items.
         </p>
         <p>
          Im as guilty as anyone with getting wild with colors in the past. Ill add this color, then another, and another  and before I know it, it looks like a rainbow diarrhea all over my website. You can have your website constructively simplified, but if the colors distract the eyes when you look at it rather than complimenting the content, then all that effort was for naught. So use fewer colors with your website design instead.
         </p>

          <strong>
           Having trouble choosing a color to start with?

         <p>
          Hopefully youre now armed and ready to start simplifying your website design. Youll be more proud showing off your website because itll look gosh-darned sexy and lastly, your visitors will have a much more enjoyable experience browsing in your website.
         </p>
         <p>
          To recap, here are the 5 starting points to simplify your website design:
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Put the focus only on the essential elements
          </li>
          <li>
           Get rid of all unnecessary elements
          </li>
          <li>
           Reduce the number of pages
          </li>
          <li>
           Get more content above the fold
          </li>
          <li>
           Limit your color palette
          </li>
