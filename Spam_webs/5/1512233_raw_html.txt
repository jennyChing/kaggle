    <h1>
     The Math Behind the Beauty
    </h1>
    <p class="smallCenter">
     By M. Bourne
    </p>
    <div class="imgLt">
     <img alt="Jessica Simpson - mathematics of beauty" height="173" src="http://intmstat.com/numbers/beautyMath/jessicaSimpson5_26.jpg" width="120"/>
     <br/>
     Jessica Simpson
    </div>
    <div class="dunmiss">
     <h3>
      On this page...
     </h3>
     <p>
      Don't miss the
      <a href="#beautiful">
       beauty interactive
      </a>
      .
     </p>
    </div>
    <p>
     What has mathematics got to do with beauty? Actually, a lot. Physical attraction depends on
     <b>
      ratio
     </b>
     .
    </p>
    <p>
     Our attraction to another person's body increases if that body is symmetrical and in proportion. Likewise, if a face is in proportion, we are
more likely to notice it and find it beautiful.
    </p>
    <p>
     Scientists believe that we perceive proportional bodies to be more healthy.
    </p>
    <div class="clearBoth imgCenter">
     <img alt="davinci's Vitruvian Man showing Golden Ratio" height="314" src="http://intmstat.com/numbers/beautyMath/body_sm_ratios.jpg" width="300"/>
     <br/>
     Leonardo da Vinci's "Vitruvian Man",
     <br/>
     showing the golden ratio in body dimensions
    </div>
    <p>
     Leonardo da Vinci's drawings of the human body emphasised its proportion. The ratio of the following distances is the Golden Ratio:
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     (foot to navel) : (navel to head)
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     Similarly, buildings are more attractive if the proportions used follow the Golden Ratio.
    </p>
    <h2>
     Golden Ratio
    </h2>
    <p>
     The Golden Ratio (or "Golden Section") is based on
     <b>
      Fibonacci Numbers
     </b>
     , where every number in the sequence (after the second) is the sum of
the previous 2 numbers:
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, ...
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     We will see (below) how the Fibonnaci Numbers lead to the
     <b>
      Golden Ratio:
     </b>
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      <span class="intmath">
       
      </span>
      = 1.618 033 ...
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <h2 id="beautiful">
     Physical Beauty
    </h2>
    <p>
     Why do many people feel that Jessica Simpson is beautiful?
    </p>
    <div class="imgLt">
     <img alt="Marquardt's mask" height="185" src="http://intmstat.com/numbers/beautyMath/mask1.gif" width="135"/>
     <br/>
     Image
     <a href="http://www.beautyanalysis.com/research/perfect-face/facial-masks/">
      source
     </a>
    </div>
    <p>
     This mask of the human face is based on the Golden Ratio. The proportions of the length of the nose, the position of the eyes and the length
  of the chin, all conform to some aspect of the Golden Ratio.
    </p>
    <div class="imgRt">
     <img alt="Jessica Simpson and Marquardt mask" height="235" src="http://intmstat.com/numbers/beautyMath/jessica_mask2.jpg" width="184"/>
    </div>
    <p>
     When placed over the photo of Jessica Simpson, we see there is a good fit (that is, the proportions of her face fit the geometrically "nice"
  proportions of the mask, based on the Golden Ratio).
    </p>
    <p>
     Her beauty is mathematical!
    </p>
    <p>
     You can play with this concept in the following interactive.
    </p>

    <p>
     Let's look at the ratio of each number in the Fibonacci sequence to the one before it:
    </p>
    <div style="width:200px; float:left; margin:10px 20px;">
     <p>
      `1/1 = 1`
     </p>
     <p>
      `2/1 = 2`
     </p>
     <p>
      `3/2 = 1.5`
     </p>
     <p>
      `5/3 = 1.666...`
     </p>
     <p>
      `8/5 = 1.6`
     </p>
    </div>
    <div style="width:200px; float:left; margin:10px 20px;">
     <p>
      `13/8 = 1.625`
     </p>
     <p>
      `21/13 = 1.61538...`
     </p>
     <p>
      `34/21 = 1.61905...`
     </p>
     <p>
      `55/34 = 1.61764...`
     </p>
     <p>
      `89/55 = 1.61861...`
     </p>
    </div>
    <p class="clearBoth">
     If we keep going, we produce an interesting number which mathematicians call "phi" (
     <b>
      Golden Ratio
     </b>
     or
     <b>
      Golden
Section
     </b>
     ):
    </p>
    <blockquote>
     <p>
      `Phi = 1.618 033 988 7...`
     </p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>
     This ratio was used by architects and artists throughout history to produce objects of great beauty (like Michelangelo's "David" and
the Greek temples.)
