     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Mikoyan MiG-31
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        -31
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">
       NATO reporting name
      </a>
      :
      <b>
       Foxhound
      </b>
      ) is a supersonic
      <a href="/wiki/Interceptor_aircraft" title="Interceptor aircraft">
       interceptor aircraft
      </a>
      developed for the uses of the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Forces" title="Soviet Air Forces">
       Soviet Air Forces
      </a>
      . The aircraft was designed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan" title="Mikoyan">
       Mikoyan
      </a>
      design bureau as a replacement for the earlier
      <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-25">
       MiG-25 "Foxbat"
      </a>
      ; the MiG-31 is based on, and shares design elements with, the MiG-25 itself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_Book_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Great_Book-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The MiG-31 has the distinction of being one of the fastest combat jets in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It continued to be operated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Air_Force" title="Russian Air Force">
       Russian Air Force
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Republic_of_Kazakhstan" title="Armed Forces of the Republic of Kazakhstan">
       Kazakhstan Air Force
      </a>
      following the end of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
       Cold War
      </a>
      and the collapse of the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      in the early 1990s. The Russian Defense Ministry expects the MiG-31 to remain in service until at least 2030.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan_MiG-31&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The MiG-25, despite Western panic about its tremendous performance, made substantial design sacrifices in capability for the sake of achieving high speed, altitude and rate of climb. It lacked maneuverability at interception speeds and was difficult to fly at low altitudes. The MiG-25's speed was limited to
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach
      </a>
      2.83 in operations, but it could reach a maximum speed of Mach 3.2 or more with the risk of damaging the engines beyond repair.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dawes_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dawes-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Development of the MiG-25's replacement began with the Ye-155MP (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       -155
      </span>
      )
      <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">
       prototype
      </a>
      which first flew on 16 September 1975.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_p323_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eden_p323-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although it bore a superficial resemblance to a stretched MiG-25 with a longer fuselage to accommodate the radar operator's cockpit, it was in many respects a new design. The MiG-25 used 80% nickel steel in its structure to allow welding.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eden_p308_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eden_p308-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An important development was the advanced radar, capable of both look-up and
      <a href="/wiki/Look-down/shoot-down" title="Look-down/shoot-down">
       look-down/shoot-down
      </a>
      engagement (locating targets above and below the aircraft), as well as multiple target tracking. This gave the Soviet Union an interceptor able to engage the most likely Western intruders (low flying cruise missiles and bombers) at long range.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topwar.ru_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-topwar.ru-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Like its MiG-25 predecessor, the MiG-31 was surrounded by early speculation and misinformation concerning its design and abilities. The West learned of the new interceptor from Lieutenant
      <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Belenko" title="Viktor Belenko">
       Viktor Belenko
      </a>
      , a pilot who defected to
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      in 1976 with his MiG-25P. Belenko described an upcoming "Super Foxbat" with two seats and an ability to intercept cruise missiles. According to his testimony, the new interceptor was to have air intakes similar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-23" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-23">
       Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-23
      </a>
      , which the MiG-31 does not have, at least not in production variants.
     </p>
     <p>
      Serial production for MiG-31 started in 1979.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The MiG-31 is able to maintain combat effectiveness despite the potential use of active and passive radar jammers and thermal decoys by adversaries. A group of four MiG-31 interceptors is able to control an area of air space across a total length of 800900km;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topwar.ru_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-topwar.ru-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      its radar possessing its highest detection range of 200km in distance (radius) and the typical width of detection along the front of 225km.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arms-expo.ru_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-arms-expo.ru-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      MiG-31 production ended in 1994.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopaedia-russia.ru_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-encyclopaedia-russia.ru-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan_MiG-31&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overview">

     <div class="thumb tright">

       </a>

     <p>
      Like the MiG-25, MiG-31 is a large twin-engine aircraft with side-mounted air
      <a href="/wiki/Intake_ramp" title="Intake ramp">
       intake ramps
      </a>
      , a shoulder-mounted wing with an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(wing)" title="Aspect ratio (wing)">
       aspect ratio
      </a>
      of 2.94, and twin vertical tailfins. Unlike the MiG-25, it has two seats, with the rear occupied by a dedicated
      <a href="/wiki/Weapon_systems_officer" title="Weapon systems officer">
       weapon systems officer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr-71-vs_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sr-71-vs-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The MiG-31 is limited to only 5
      <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">
       <i>
        g
       </i>
      </a>
      when travelling at supersonic speeds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dawes_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dawes-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While flying under combat weight, its
      <a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">
       wing loading
      </a>
      is marginal and its
      <a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">
       thrust-to-weight ratio
      </a>
      is favorable. However, the MiG-31 is not designed for
      <a href="/wiki/Dogfight" title="Dogfight">
       close combat or rapid turning
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dawes_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dawes-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The wings and airframe of the MiG-31 are stronger than those of the MiG-25, permitting supersonic flight at low altitudes. Its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviadvigatel_D30-F6" title="Aviadvigatel D30-F6">
       D30-F6
      </a>
      jet engines, each rated at 152kN thrust, allow a maximum speed of
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach
      </a>
      1.23 at low altitude. High-altitude speed is temperature-redlined to Mach 2.83  the thrust-to-drag ratio is sufficient for speeds in excess of Mach 3, but such speeds pose unacceptable hazards to engine and airframe life in routine use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dawes_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dawes-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Passive_electronically_scanned_array" title="Passive electronically scanned array">

      </a>

       </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Vympel" title="Vympel">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-modernforces.ru_27-0">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cockpit">
       Cockpit
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     <p>
      The aircraft is a two-seater with the rear seat occupant controlling the radar. Although cockpit controls are duplicated across cockpits, it is normal for the aircraft to be flown only from the front seat. The pilot flies the aircraft by means of a
      <a href="/wiki/Centre_stick" title="Centre stick">
       centre stick
      </a>
      and left hand
      <a href="/wiki/Throttle" title="Throttle">
       throttles
      </a>
      . The rear cockpit has only two small vision ports on the sides of the canopy. It is argued that the presence of the WSO (Weapon Systems Operator) in the rear cockpit improves aircraft effectiveness since the WSO is entirely dedicated to radar operations and weapons deployment. This decreases the workload of the pilot and increases efficiency. Both cockpits are fitted with zero/zero ejection seats which allow the crew to eject at any altitude and airspeed.

        <span>

     <p>
      The upgraded MiG-31B maximum range of detection of air targets increased to 320km, automatic tracking accepted up to ten targets, and the latest units can track up to 24 targets and simultaneously attack up to 6 targets. On-Board computer Argon-K selects the four most important, which simultaneously are engaged by four air-to-air long-range
      <a href="/wiki/R-33_(missile)" title="R-33 (missile)">
       R-33S
      </a>
      missiles. Thermal search is interfaced with radar and is designed for passive review of the airspace, and for the issuance of
      <a href="/wiki/R-40_(missile)" title="R-40 (missile)">
       R-40
      </a>
      TD and
      <a href="/wiki/R-60_(missile)" title="R-60 (missile)">
       R-60
      </a>
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/GOS" title="GOS">
       TGS
      </a>
      missiles targeting.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The basic difference between earlier versions and the iG-31BM
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>

        </span>
        57

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_accidents">
       Notable accidents
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mikoyan_MiG-31&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Notable accidents">
        edit

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On 6 September 2011, a MiG-31 crashed near Bolgary village, Perm region, Russia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The aircraft crashed shortly after takeoff, killing both pilots. Another, non-fatal crash occurred in 2010. The entire fleet was grounded pending an investigation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the night of 23 April 2013, a Kazakhstan Air Force MiG-31 crashed during a training flight near the village of Prostornoye in the Karaganda Region of Kazakhstan, killing the pilot and injuring the navigator. The plane crashed due to technical failure. The same plane underwent a major overhaul at a plant in Rzhev, northwest Russia in December 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      On 4 September 2014, a MiG-31 crashed during exercises at a distance of 25 kilometres from the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Armavir,_Russia" title="Armavir, Russia">
       Armavir
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
