     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       cognates
      </b>
      are words that have a common
      <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
       etymological
      </a>
      origin. This learned term derives from the Latin
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cognatus" title="wikt:cognatus">
        cognatus
       </a>
      </i>
      (blood relative).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In linguistic research it is generally understood as excluding
      <a href="#Within_the_same_language">
       doublets
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loan_words" title="Loan words">
       loan words
      </a>
      , although broader definitions are used in other areas such as language teaching.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_cognate_words">
       Characteristics of cognate words
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics of cognate words">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cognates do not need to have the same meaning, which
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic_drift" title="Semantic drift">
       may have changed
      </a>
      as the languages developed separately. For example, consider
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/starve#English" title="wikt:starve">
        starve
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
       Dutch
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sterven#Dutch" title="wikt:sterven">
        sterven
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sterben" title="wikt:sterben">
        sterben
       </a>
      </i>
      ("to die"); these three words all derive from the same
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">
       Proto-Germanic
      </a>
      root,
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/sterban%C4%85" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/sterban">
        *sterban
       </a>
      </i>
      ("die").
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dish#English" title="wikt:dish">
        dish
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Tisch#German" title="wikt:Tisch">
        Tisch
       </a>
      </i>
      ("table"), with their flat surfaces, both come from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_Language" title="Latin Language">
       Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/discus#Latin" title="wikt:discus">
        discus
       </a>
      </i>
      , but it would be a mistake to identify their later meanings as the same.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cognates also do not need to have obviously similar forms: e.g., English
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/father" title="wikt:father">
        father
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/p%C3%A8re#French" title="wikt:pre">
        pre
       </a>
      </i>
      , and Armenian
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D5%B0%D5%A1%D5%B5%D6%80" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       hayr
      </i>
      ) all descend directly from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">
       Proto-Indo-European
      </a>
      <i>
       *phtr
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Within_the_same_language">
       Within the same language
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Within the same language">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cognate
      <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics)" title="Doublet (linguistics)">
       doublets
      </a>
      can exist within the same language, with meanings which may be anything from slightly to totally different. For example, English
      <i>
       ward
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       guard
      </i>
      (&lt;PIE
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/wer-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/wer-">
        *wer-
       </a>
      </i>
      , "to perceive, watch out for") are cognates, as are
      <i>
       shirt
      </i>
      (garment on top) and
      <i>
       skirt
      </i>
      (garment on bottom) (&lt;PIE
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/sker-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/sker-">
        *sker-
       </a>
      </i>
      , "to cut"). In some cases, such as "shirt" and "skirt", one of the cognate pairs has an ultimate source in another language related to English, while the other one is native, as happened with many loanwords from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      borrowed during the
      <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">
       Danelaw
      </a>
      . Sometimes, both cognates come from other languages, often the same one but at different times. For example, the word
      <i>
       chief
      </i>
      (meaning the leader of any group) comes from the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">
       Middle French
      </a>
      <i>
       chef
      </i>
      ("head"), and its modern pronunciation preserves the Middle French consonant sound; the word
      <i>
       chef
      </i>
      (the leader of the cooks) was borrowed from the same source centuries later, by which time the consonant had changed to a "sh"-sound in French. Such word sets can also be called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etymological_twins" title="Etymological twins">
       etymological twins
      </a>
      , and of course they may come in groups of higher numbers, as with, for example, the words
      <i>
       wain
      </i>
      (native),
      <i>
       waggon/wagon
      </i>
      (Dutch) and
      <i>
       vehicle
      </i>
      (Latin) in English.
     </p>
     <p>
      A word may also
      <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
       enter another language
      </a>
      , develop a new form or meaning there, and be re-borrowed into the original language; this is called a
      <i>
       Rckwanderer
      </i>
      (German for "one who wanders back"). For example, the Greek word
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       kinma
      </i>
      , "movement") became French
      <i>
       cinma
      </i>
      (cf. American English
      <i>
       movie
      </i>
      ) and then later returned to Greece as
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       sinema
      </i>
      , "the art of film", "movie theater"). Now in Greece
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       kinma
      </i>
      , "movement") and
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       sinema
      </i>
      , "filmmaking, cinema") exist together as a doublet (see next section).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An example of very different and non-obvious English-language doublets is
      <i>
       grammar
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       glamour
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="False_cognates">
       False cognates
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: False cognates">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/False_cognate" title="False cognate">
       False cognate
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/False_cognate" title="False cognate">
       False cognates
      </a>
      are words that are commonly thought to be related (have a common origin), but which linguistic examination reveals to be unrelated. For example, on the basis of superficial similarities, the Latin verb
      <i>
       habre
      </i>
      and German
      <i>
       haben
      </i>
      , both meaning 'to have', appear to be cognates. But because of the way words in the two languages evolved from
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
       Proto-Indo-European
      </a>
      (PIE) roots, they cannot be cognate (see for example
      <a href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_law" title="Grimm's law">
       Grimm's law
      </a>
      ). German
      <i>
       haben
      </i>
      , like English
      <i>
       have
      </i>
      , comes from PIE
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/kh%E2%82%82py%C3%A9-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/khpy-">
        *khpy-
       </a>
      </i>
      'to grasp', and its real cognate in Latin is
      <i>
       capere
      </i>
      , 'to seize, grasp, capture'. Latin
      <i>
       habre
      </i>
      , on the other hand, is from PIE
      <i>
       *gab
      </i>
      , 'to give, to receive', and hence cognate with English
      <i>
       give
      </i>
      and German
      <i>
       geben
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Likewise, English
      <i>
       much
      </i>
      and Spanish
      <i>
       mucho
      </i>
      look similar and have a similar meaning but are not cognates, as they evolved from different roots:
      <i>
       much
      </i>
      from Proto-Germanic
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/mikilaz" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/mikilaz">
        *mikilaz
       </a>
      </i>
      &lt; PIE
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/meg-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/meg-">
        *me-
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       mucho
      </i>
      from Latin
      <i>
       multum
      </i>
      &lt; PIE
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/mel-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/mel-">
        *mel-
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
