       <h1>
        Saying No to NSAIDs: Alternatives for Managing Crohns Pain
       </h1>
       <ul class="inline">
        <li class="byline" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
         By
         <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/authors/beth-orenstein/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
          <span itemprop="name">
           Beth W.  Orenstein

    <div class="row-fluid">
     <h2 class="eh-deck">
      Many of the NSAID pain relievers found on drugstore shelves don't mix with Crohn's. Instead, try these strategies for Crohn's pain management.
     </h2>
     <span class="ehshare share-inline clearfix" data-count="true" data-description="People%2bwith%2bCrohn%2527s%2bshouldn%2527t%2btake%2bmost%2bover-the-counter%2bNSAIDs%2bto%2bease%2bpain.%2bLearn%2babout%2bsafer%2balternatives%2bfor%2bCrohn%2527s%2bpain%2bmanagement." data-migratedbyline="" data-networks='["facebook", "twitter", "pinterest", "email"]' data-title="Saying%2bNo%2bto%2bNSAIDs%253a%2bAlternatives%2bfor%2bManaging%2bCrohn%25e2%2580%2599s%2bPain" data-track-self="false" data-url="http%3a%2f%2fwww.everydayhealth.com%2fhs%2fcrohns-disease-treatment-management%2fno-nsaids-alternatives-crohns-pain%2f">

     <div class="article-body" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Article">
      <p>
       <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease-treatment-management.aspx">
        Treating Crohn's
       </a>
       pain requires a careful approach to medication  and that means knowing which drugs are safe and which aren't.
      </p>
      <p>
       Anti-inflammatory drugs are often used as a first-line treatment for Crohn's. However, taking typical over-the-counter options for pain  like nonsteroidal
       <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease-treatment-management/5-asas-anti-inflammatory-drugs-for-crohns.aspx">
        anti-inflammatory drugs
       </a>
       (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen  is something you want to avoid if you have Crohns. Many of these pain relievers can increase bleeding and further damage the lining of your intestines, potentially causing ulcers. But you don't have to put up with pain. There are a number of other pain-relief strategies available.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Crohns Pain Management Techniques
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       The best way to control Crohns pain is to follow the
       <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease-treatment-management/treatment-options-for-crohns-disease.aspx">
        treatment plan
       </a>
       that you and your doctor have determined is best for you. If your doctor gives you a prescription drug for Crohns, that should alleviate the pain. These drugs include aminosalicylates, corticosteroids, immunomodulators, and biologics. If you take medication for Crohns, its important to take it exactly as directed by your doctor.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some people stop taking their medication when theyre in remission and feel fine, says Peter Doyle Higgins, MD, PhD, a gastroenterologist at the University of Michigan Health System in Ann Arbor. Remission can last months or even years, but you're inviting pain if you dont stick to your treatment plan.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also, if your Crohns pain is from an infection and your doctor gives you an antibiotic to treat it, be sure to take all the doses in that prescription, too  even if you start to feel better before youve taken all the medication.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you need an over-the-counter pain reliever for pain from another source, like a headache, choose acetaminophen. This is safe for people with Crohns as long as its in safe doses, Dr. Higgins says. Ask your doctor what's reasonable for you, and never exceed your doctors recommended dose.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Ease Pain Without Medicine
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease-treatment-management/stress-less-with-crohns.aspx">
        Stress
       </a>
       doesnt cause Crohns, but it can aggravate your symptoms, including abdominal pain. Managing stress can help you can control Crohns symptoms, too. Consider adding stress-relieving techniques like yoga, tai chi, massage therapy, or meditation to your day. Other alternative Crohns pain management techniques include:
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Deep breathing.
       </strong>
       This simple exercise can help relax your abdominal muscles and potentially lower your pain level. Pick a comfortable chair in a quiet spot and close your eyes. Slow down your breathing and just contemplate the word "relax. Breathe in from your diaphragm, then contract your stomach muscles as you exhale away tension and discomfort.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Guided imagery.
       </strong>
       Guided imagery can also be a Crohns pain management technique. Think of pleasant sights, sounds, tastes, and aromas to distract you from your Crohns pain.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Heat.
       </strong>
       When your stomach is sore, a heating pad or hot water bottle can provide some relief. For a do-it-yourself version, fill a long sock with rice, tie it closed, and carefully heat it in the microwave. Higgins says to practice caution when using heat, though, to avoid burns. Don't keep a heating pad on high for too long.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        A warm soak.
       </strong>
       A warm bath can be soothing for mild cramps and intestinal pain. Plus, adding a little salt to the water can help ease pain from an anal fissure.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Nutritional supplements.
       </strong>
       Research published in the journal Clinical and Translational Gastroenterology in April 2013 found that people with Crohns who were deficient in vitamin D and took supplements reported some symptom relief. Ask your doctor if vitamins and mineral supplements should be part of your Crohns pain management and might ease painful symptoms. Options include anti-inflammatory omega-3s and good-bacteria probiotics.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, don't forget to give yourself a little TLC. When managing Crohns, its important to take care of yourself, Higgins says. Eat well, exercise regularly, and get good sleep to feel better in general.
      </p>
