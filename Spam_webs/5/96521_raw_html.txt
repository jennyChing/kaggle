        <h1 itemprop="headline">
         US Authorities Used 225-Year-Old Law To Bypass Phone's Password
        </h1>
        <div class="meta-wrap">
         <div class="meta">
          <a class="tracking_article_author" data-tracking-name="Jamie Condliffe" href="/author/jamie-condliffe/" rel="author">
           <span itemprop="author">
            Jamie Condliffe
           </span>
          </a>
          <time datetime="2014-11-28T12:15:00+11:00" itemprop="datePublished">
           28 November 2014 12:15 PM
          </time>
         </div>
         <div class="social tracking_article_social">
          <a class="btn btn-mini share anchor-link" data-target="#post-833605 .collapse" data-toggle="collapse" data-tracking-name="Share" href="#share" rel="nofollow" title="Share">
           <i class="icon-share-alt">
           </i>
           Share
          </a>
          <a class="btn btn-mini discuss anchor-link" data-tracking-name="Discuss" href="#comments" rel="nofollow" title="Discuss">
           <i class="icon-comment">
           </i>
           Discuss
          </a>
          <a class="btn btn-mini bookmark disabled" data-post-id="833605" data-target="/wp-admin/admin-ajax.php?id=login_to_bookmark" data-toggle="modal" data-tracking-name="Bookmark" title="Bookmark this post">
           <i class="icon-bookmark">
           </i>
           Bookmark
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div id="content_post" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="media_wrap image xlarge center clear">
          <a href="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/t_original/ikr2vc1gil0uyyoq4231.jpg" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://i.kinja-img.com']);" rel="modal">
           <img alt="The DOJ Used 225-Year-Old Law to Bypass a Phone's Password" class="size_xlarge" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/t_original/ikr2vc1gil0uyyoq4231.jpg"/>
          </a>
         </div>
         <p>
          When it comes to encryption, some of the Department of Justices views are
          <a href="http://www.gizmodo.com.au/2014/11/doj-says-iphone-encryption-will-kill-a-child-which-cmon">
           interesting
          </a>
          . Now, it transpires that its been using laws that date back 225 years to get phones unlocked too.
          <br/>
          <span id="more-833605">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2014/11/25/case-suggests-how-government-may-get-around-phone-encryption/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://blogs.wsj.com']);">
           The Wall Street Journal reports
          </a>
          that, back last month, its prosecutors managed to convince a federal magistrate in Manhattan to order an unnamed phone maker to provide what it calls reasonable technical assistance to unlock a password-protected phone. The phone in question was rumoured to contain evidence required in a credit-card-fraud case.
         </p>
         <p>
          The warrant issued to source help in unlocking the phone cited the All Writs Act. Thats part of a 1789 law that gives courts broad authority to carry out their duties. What happened next remains under wraps, though the phone manufacturer in question 
          <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2014/11/25/case-suggests-how-government-may-get-around-phone-encryption/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://blogs.wsj.com']);">
           which the Journal speculates may be Apple
          </a>
           was given five days to lay the contents of the phone out for inspection.
         </p>
         <p>
          While a spokesman for the US attorneys office
          <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2014/11/25/case-suggests-how-government-may-get-around-phone-encryption/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://blogs.wsj.com']);">
           told the Journal
          </a>
          that its not that unusual for the government to use an All Writs order to get a phone-maker to unlock a phone, a partner at Perkins Coie LLP, which works closely with tech firms, also said that theres danger in this. He was, of course, referring to the lengths that tech companies must go to in order to help the government.
         </p>
         <p>
          This certainly isnt the first  nor will it be the last  case of judges ordering tech giants to help them in their enquiries by unlocking phones. But you cant help but wonder if maybe, just maybe, some laws that didnt date back to 1789 might be a useful addition. [
          <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2014/11/25/case-suggests-how-government-may-get-around-phone-encryption/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://blogs.wsj.com']);">
           WSJ
          </a>
          ]
         </p>
