   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Great Lakes tectonic zone
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_Tectonic_Zone&amp;redirect=no" title="Great Lakes Tectonic Zone">
       Great Lakes Tectonic Zone
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The Great Lakes tectonic zone is bounded by
      <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">
       South Dakota
      </a>
      at its tip and heads northeast to south of
      <a href="/wiki/Duluth,_Minnesota" title="Duluth, Minnesota">
       Duluth, Minnesota
      </a>
      , then heads east through northern
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">
       Wisconsin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marquette,_Michigan" title="Marquette, Michigan">
       Marquette, Michigan
      </a>
      , and then trends more northeasterly to skim the northern-most shores of lakes Michigan and Huron before ending in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudbury,_Ontario" title="Sudbury, Ontario">
       Sudbury, Ontario
      </a>
      , Canada, area.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the Late
      <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">
       Archean
      </a>
      Era the
      <a href="/wiki/Algoman_orogeny" title="Algoman orogeny">
       Algoman orogeny
      </a>
      added landmass to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superior_craton" title="Superior craton">
       Superior province
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Volcano#Convergent_plate_boundaries" title="Volcano">
       volcanic activity
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">
       continental collision
      </a>
      along a boundary that stretches from present-day South Dakota, U.S., into the
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">
       Lake Huron
      </a>
      region near
      <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">
       Sudbury
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      This crustal boundary is the
      <b>
       Great Lakes tectonic zone
      </b>
      . It is 1,400km (870mi) long, and separates the older
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaen" title="Archaen">
       Archean
      </a>
      gneissic terrane to the south from younger Late Archean
      <a href="/wiki/Greenstone_belt" title="Greenstone belt">
       greenstone
      </a>
      -granite
      <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">
       terrane
      </a>
      to the north.
     </p>
     <p>
      The zone is characterized by active compression during the Algoman orogeny (about
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,700">
        2,700
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      ), a pulling-apart (
      <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">
       extensional
      </a>
      ) tectonics (2,450 to 2,100 million years ago), a second compression during the Penokean orogeny (1,900 to 1,850 million years ago), a second extension during Middle
      <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">
       Proterozoic
      </a>
      time (1,600 million years ago) and minor reactivation during
      <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">
       Phanerozoic
      </a>
      time (the past 500 million years).
     </p>
     <p>
      Collision began along the Great Lakes tectonic zone (GLTZ) with the Algoman mountain-building event and continued for tens of millions of years. During the formation of the GLTZ, the gneissic
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minnesota_River_Valley_subprovince&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minnesota River Valley subprovince (page does not exist)">
       Minnesota River Valley subprovince
      </a>
      was thrust up onto the Superior province's edge as it consumed the Superior province's oceanic crust. Fragmentation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kenorland" title="Kenorland">
       Kenorland
      </a>
      supercontinent began
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,450">
        2,450
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      and was completed by
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,100">
        2,100
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wyoming_craton" title="Wyoming craton">
       Wyoming province
      </a>
      is the continental landmass that is hypothesized to have rifted away from the southern Superior province portion of Kenorland, before moving rapidly west and docking with the
      <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">
       Laurentia
      </a>
      supercontinent 1,850 to 1,715 million years ago.
      <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation#Geology" title="Sedimentation">
       Sedimentation
      </a>
      from the GLTZ-rifting environment continued into the Penokean orogeny, which is the next major
      <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">
       tectonic
      </a>
      event in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region_(North_America)" title="Great Lakes region (North America)">
       Great Lakes region
      </a>
      . Several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">
       earthquakes
      </a>
      have been documented in Minnesota, Michigan's Upper Peninsula and Sudbury in the last 120 years along the GLTZ.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Location">
       Location
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wawa,_Quetico_%26_Wabigoon_Subprovinces_of_southern_Superior_Province.PNG">
        <img alt="This map shows the Wawa, Quetico and Wabigoon subprovinces of the Superior province.        </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wawa,_Quetico_%26_Wabigoon_Subprovinces_of_southern_Superior_Province.PNG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Wawa subprovince is the wide green belt to the south; it is part of the Superior province. The Minnesota River Valley subprovince is also shown.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      During the Late Archean Eon the Algoman orogeny  which occurred about
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,750">
        2,750
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
       added landmass through volcanic activity and continental collision along a boundary that stretches from present-day South Dakota, U.S., into the Sudbury, Ontario, Canada, region. The farthest west into South Dakota is 99W,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which is about 55km (34mi) from the Minnesota  South Dakota border. This crustal boundary is the Great Lakes tectonic zone (GLTZ). It is a 1,400km (870mi) long paleo
      <a href="/wiki/Suture_(geology)" title="Suture (geology)">
       suture
      </a>
      that separates the more than 3,000-million-year-old Archean
      <a href="/wiki/Gneiss" title="Gneiss">
       gneissic
      </a>
      terrane to the south  Minnesota River Valley subprovince  from the 2,700-million-year-old Late Archean greenstone-
      <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">
       granite
      </a>
      terrane to the north 
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wawa_Subprovince&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wawa Subprovince (page does not exist)">
       Wawa Subprovince
      </a>
      of the Superior province.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-domes_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-domes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The GLTZ is 50km (30mi) wide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ojakangas_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ojakangas-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">
       Mechanism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Collision">
       Collision
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Collision">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orogenic_wedge.jpg">
        <img alt="This diagram shows the dynamics of two colliding continental plates." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="213" data-file-width="580" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Orogenic_wedge.jpg/350px-Orogenic_wedge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Orogenic_wedge.jpg/525px-Orogenic_wedge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Orogenic_wedge.jpg 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orogenic_wedge.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        This diagram shows the dynamics of two colliding continental plates. The Superior province was underridden by the Minnesota River Valley subprovince plate.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The collision of the gneissic Minnesota River Valley (MFV) subprovince onto the southern edge of the Superior province was another process in the slow change in tectonics
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which marks the end of the Archean Eon. This gneissic terrane originally extended several hundred kilometers east to west, making it more of a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Protocontinent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Protocontinent (page does not exist)">
       protocontinent
      </a>
      than a future Superior province belt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The boundary that separates the two colliding bodies is the Great Lakes tectonic zone; it is a
      <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">
       fault
      </a>
      zone of highly deformed rocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Collision began along the GLTZ around
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,700">
        2,700
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      and continued for tens of millions of years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The collision is interpreted to have happened obliquely at an angle,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basement_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-basement-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      beginning in the west.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Suturing">
       Suturing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Suturing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The MRV subprovince experienced two distinct high-grade
      <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">
       metamorphic
      </a>
      events, one
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=3,050">
        3,050
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      and the other
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,600">
        2,600
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first was probably during formation of the terrane, the second was during suturing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The growth of the Superior province greenstone-granitic terranes ended with the suturing of the Minnesota River Valley gneiss terrane to the
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       basaltic
      </a>
      Wawa subprovince.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tohver_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tohver-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :145
      </sup>
      Suturing, the last stage of closure, started in South Dakota and continued eastward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the formation of the GLTZ, the MRV protocontinent consumed the Superior province's
      <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust#Life_cycle" title="Oceanic crust">
       oceanic crust
      </a>
      as the subprovince came in from the south.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Suturing of one continental block onto another usually occurs because a
      <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
       subduction
      </a>
      zone exists beneath one of the blocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The subduction zone consumes the oceanic crust connected to the other block.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After the oceanic crust is consumed, the two blocks meet and the subducting oceanic crust pulls the attached continental block under the other.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the collision with the Superior province, the MRV gneissic block was thrust up onto the Superior province's edge; this resulted in a thrusted crumpled fault tens of kilometers wide producing a mountain range, and a
      <a href="/wiki/Shear_(geology)#Shear_zone" title="Shear (geology)">
       shear zone
      </a>
      which defines the boundary between the two terranes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tectonism along the zone began during the docking of the two terranes into a single continental mass, and culminated in the early Proterozoic, where deformation took place under low to intermediate pressures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morey_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-morey-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rifting">
       Rifting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rifting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:197px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Divergent_border_lmb.png">
        <img alt="This diagrams shows a hotspot under diverging continental plates." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="130" data-file-width="195" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/51/Divergent_border_lmb.png" width="195"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Divergent_border_lmb.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hotspot causing rifting of tectonic plates
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After suturing, the region was tectonically quiet for a few hundred million years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Algoman Mountains had been built and then eroded into sediments that covered the area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fragmentation of this Archean
      <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">
       supercontinent
      </a>
      began around
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,450">
        2,450
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      under a
      <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">
       hotspot
      </a>
      near Sudbury and was completed by around
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,100">
        2,100
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tohver_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tohver-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :145
      </sup>
      This is when the Wyoming province is hypothesized to have drifted away from the Superior province.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cessation_of_rifting">
       Cessation of rifting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cessation of rifting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The pattern of sedimentation from this rifting environment continued into the Penokean orogeny, which is the next major tectonic event in the Great Lakes region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the Penokean orogeny (1,850 to 1,900 million years ago),
      <a href="/wiki/Compression_(geology)" title="Compression (geology)">
       compression
      </a>
      deformed the sequences in the Lake Superior region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morey_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-morey-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        </span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">

      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Strike-slip_tectonics" title="Strike-slip tectonics">

      </a>
      zone south of Marquette, passing under the large Marquette

      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_of_rock">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_American_provinces_and_orogens.PNG">
        <img alt="This is a North American map showing Archean provinces and Proterozoic and Phanerozoic orogenies." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="592" data-file-width="504" height="470" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/North_American_provinces_and_orogens.PNG/400px-North_American_provinces_and_orogens.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/North_American_provinces_and_orogens.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/North_American_provinces_and_orogens.PNG 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

         </a>

        This map shows the locations of the Superior province, Penokean orogeny, Minnesota River Valley subprovince, Great Lakes teconic zone (Minnesota's portion) and the present-day location of the Wyoming province.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Early Archean rocks generally form elongate, domal or circular bodies that are several kilometers thick.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sood_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sood-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :3
      </sup>
      Late-stage
      <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">
       dikes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">
       sills
      </a>
      of diabase, quartz-feldspar fine-grained instrusive rocks (
      <a href="/wiki/Aplite" title="Aplite">
       aplite
      </a>
      ) and quartz-feldspar-mica coarse-grained intrusive rocks (
      <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">
       pegmatite
      </a>
      ) are common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sood_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sood-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :3
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of the region's
      <a href="/wiki/Crystal#Crystalline_rocks" title="Crystal">
       crystalline rock
      </a>
      bodies of Late Archean age are part of the greenstone-granite terrane of northern Minnesota, northwestern Wisconsin and the western part of the Upper Peninsula of Michigan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sood_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sood-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">
       Lithologies
      </a>
      of the rocks are usually gneissose and
      <a href="/wiki/Migmatite" title="Migmatite">
       migmatitic
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sood_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sood-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">

      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">

        </span>

        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

       <a href="#cite_note-sood-8">

        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :3
      </sup>

        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

       <a href="#cite_note-bickford-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9

      </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sood_8-9">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8
      </sup>

      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,600">
        2,600
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      , after the suturing of the MRV gneissic terrane onto the Superior province.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-15">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similar intrusions farther east along the GLTZ show later dates, reinforcing the theorized closure from west to east.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_4-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

        </span>
        8
        <span>

       </a>
      </sup>

       :1

         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">

       </a>
      </sup>

       </a>
      </sup>

      </a>

       Wyoming province separation hypothesis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Wyoming province separation hypothesis">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="General_information">
       General information
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: General information">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An episode of hotspot gabbro magmatism occurred
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,480">
        2,480
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      at the eastern edge of the Wyoming
      <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">
       craton
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :1
      </sup>
      south of current-day Sudbury. Continental rifting is exhibited by emplacement of
      <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">
       mafic
      </a>
      igneous rocks on each side of the rift margins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :9
      </sup>
      By
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,100">
        2,100
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      the Superior and Wyoming provinces had completely separated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tohver_6-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-tohver-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :145
      </sup>
      From about 2,100 to 1,865 million years ago the Wyoming craton drifted in a westward direction until it docked
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=1,865">
        1,865
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      with the Superior province,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :10
      </sup>
      northwest of its original position.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Before_rifting">
       Before rifting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Before rifting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The final assembly of supercontinent Kenorland was finished by 2,600 to 2,550 million years ago; the southern Superior province  with the Minnesota River Valley subprovince attached  and the current-day southeastern border of the Wyoming province abutted each other from the Sudbury area westerly about 625km (390mi) to the Wisconsin-Michigan state line on Lake Superior.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        note 2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The hotspot was 125km (80mi) south of the East Bull Lake suite, approximately under present-day Sudbury. The Blue Draw Metagabbros  in the Black Hills of South Dakota  were 625km (390mi) west of Sudbury and 150km (90mi) south of the western-most contact of the two provinces on the Wyoming province.
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </div>
     <p>
      The 2,170-million-year-old intrusive events that affected the Superior and the Wyoming cratons indicate that the plume had moved 330km (210mi) west, centered in the opening between the Superior province and the rifting Wyoming province.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8
      </sup>
      The Wyoming province was rotating away, with the Blue Draw Metagabbro being the pivot point. Harlan's reconstruction of this pivot is shown to the right. At this time the two provinces are in contact at only one point north of the Blue Draw Metagabbro; that point of contact was 875km (540mi) from Sudbury and 95km (60mi) southwest of Duluth, Minnesota. The Blue Draw Metagabbro is now 935km (580mi) west of Sudbury and remains about 150km (90mi) south of the Superior-Wyoming provinces' junction.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="After_complete_separation">
       After complete separation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: After complete separation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The 2,125- to 2,090-million-year-old mafic magmatic events affecting the Superior and Wyoming cratons show the hotspot having moved 500km (310mi) west from Sudbury, and the two provinces have rifted so that they are separated by 100km (60mi).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8
      </sup>
      That narrowest distance between the two cratons is 1,150km (710mi) from Sudbury, in east-central South Dakota. The Blue Draw Metagabbro is now 950km (590mi) west of Sudbury and 200km (120mi) south of the Superior province's southern border.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Supporting_evidence">
       Supporting evidence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Before_rifting_2">
       Before rifting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Lakes_tectonic_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Before rifting">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png">
        <img alt="This map shows the location of the Matachewan Dike Swarms, which are northeast of Lake Superior and southwest of James Bay." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="417" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png/220px-Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png/330px-Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matachewan_and_Mistassini_dike_swarms.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Matachewan Dike Swarms
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Swarms of mafic dikes and sills are typical of continental rifting and can be used to time supercontinent breakup.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :2
      </sup>
      Intrusion of the 2,475- to 2,445-million-year-old Matachewan-Hearst Mafic Dike Swarm and the 2,490- to 2,475-million-year-old East Bull Lake suite of layered mafic intrusive rocks are interpreted as indicating early Paleoproterozoic, mantle-hotspot driven rifting centered near Sudbury, Ontario, during the onset of Kenorland breakup.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :2
      </sup>
      Radiometric dating shows that the Wyoming province's Blue Draw Metagabbro was undergoing rifting at
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,480">
        2,480
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      , the same time the emplacement of the 250km (160mi) long belt of mafic layered intrusions in the Sudbury region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :9
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the northern Black Hills of southwest South Dakota the 2,600- to 2,560-million-year-old Precambrian cystalline core, the Blue Draw Metagabbro, is a 1km (0.62mi) thick layered sill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :1
      </sup>
      The East Bull Lake intrusive suite, in the southern Superior province near Sudbury, Ontario, aligns spatially with the Blue Draw Metagabbro if the Superior and Wyoming cratons are restored to the Kenorland configuration proposed by Roscoe and Card (1993).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :2
      </sup>
      These layered mafic intrusions are of similar thickness and identical age, and occur along a rifted belt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :1
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Recent paleomagnetic and geochronological data from the central Wyoming craton support the hypothesis that the
      <a href="/wiki/Huronian_Supergroup" title="Huronian Supergroup">
       Huronian
      </a>
      (in southern Ontario) and Snowy Pass (in southeastern Wyoming)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_unit#Lithostratigraphic_units" title="Geological unit">
       supergroups
      </a>
      were adjacent to each other at
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,170">
        2,170
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      and may have evolved as a single sedimentary rift basin between 2,450 and 2,100 million years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :2
      </sup>
      These Huronian and Snowy Pass sedimentary rocks are similar, each having 2,450- to 2,100-million-year-old epicratonic rifts succeeded by a 2,100- to 1,800-million-year-old passive sedimentary margins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :2
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="During_rifting_2">
       During rifting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>

     <p>
      Much of the southeastern Superior province was bisected by the more than 300,000km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      (120,000sqmi) 2,172- to 2,167-million-year-old Biscotasing Diabase Swarm which trended northeast from Sudbury.

       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :9
      </sup>
      In southcentral Wyoming province there is a 2,170  8-million-year-old quartz diorite dike of
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_River_Range#Geology" title="Wind River Range">
       Wind River Range
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">

        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">

      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="After_complete_separation_2">
       After complete separation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>

     <p>
      By
      <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2,100">
        2,100
       </a>
       million years ago
      </span>
      , the Wyoming craton is thought to have completely separated from the southern Superior province, this is consistent with the occurrence of a 2,076- to 2,067-million-year-old hotspot centered just south of the Superior province and east of the MRV.

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dahl_14-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-dahl-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
