       The Encyclopedia Wand
      </a>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The encyclopedia wands a theoretical puzzle. []The idea is t'engrave the entire encyclopedia into a single toothpick. Know how you do it? [] You take your information, your encyclopedia text, and you transpose it into numerics. You assign everything a two-digit number, periods and commas included. 00 is a blank, A is 01, B is 02, and so on. Then after youve lined them all up, you put a decimal point before the whole lot. So now youve got a very long sub-decimal fraction. 0.173000631 Next, you engrave a mark at exactly that point along the toothpick. If 0.50000s your exact middle on the toothpick, then 0.3333s got t'be a third of the way from the tip. [] Thats how you can fit data of any length in a single point on a toothpick. Only theoretically, of course.
     </p>
     <!-- more -->
     <p>
      While I didnt care much for Murakamis Hard-Boiled Wonderland as a novel, this particular section really struck me. More mathematically-minded folks may smile and nod and quietly dismiss it as cute pop-sci fluff. But for me, reading this chapter brought old forgotten neurons flashing back into action.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since graduating, Ive tried to keep growing and challenging myself intellectually. Especially as many of my friends have collected degree after degree in pursuit of academic invincibility. But it wasnt until recently that I realized most of my learning and critical thinking has been of a single style. New ideas, while consumed regularly, have all been part of some broader, descriptive/historical category, found mostly through stories and essays. Sensing this, I felt suddenly confined, stagnant. Frustrated with getting myself trapped in such a gradual, overlooked way. Like that frog in the boiling water.
     </p>
     <p>
      I first read that Murakami passage months ago. I had meant to write about it then, along with my intention to take a calculus or carpentry class or something. But ha ha/yeah right/who am I kidding, that sure hasnt happened and probably wont anytime soon, thanks to a lack of both time and desire. I do mention it now though, after reading the introduction to 2007s Best American Essays collection this morning. Its penned byDavid Foster Wallace, and his closing paragraph just made it all feel somehow relevant again:
     </p>
     <p>
      [A] model for what free, informed adulthood might look like in the context of Total Noise: not just the intelligence to discern ones own error or stupidity, but the humility to address it, absorb it, and move on and out therefrom, bravely, toward the next revealed error. This is probably the sincerest, most biased account of Best your Decider can give: these pieces are modelsnot templates, but modelsof ways I wish I could think and live in what seems to me this world.
     </p>
