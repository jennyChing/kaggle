        <a href="http://www.nbcnews.com/business/business-news/how-much-will-college-cost-25-years-n200246">
         <span>
          the rising cost of student debt
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         has given birth to an odd phenomenon: a population of ostensibly generous older men who appear poised to solve the higher-education crisis, one student at a time. Once a relatively underground subculture, this benevolent group of men is coming to the rescue across the country, essentially volunteering to subsidize the students tuition costs. But that description could be, shall I say,
        </span>
        <em>
         <span>
          sugarcoating
         </span>
        </em>
        <span>
         it.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         Yes, these men are ponying up their moneyplus morefor financially struggling students. However, its not free money, and its not all students. In other words, these benefactors typically expect some compensation from their beneficiariesstudents who generally tend to be women willing to accept the help from the men in exchange for providing some tender loving care. And, at least, flaunting their good looks.
        </span>
       </span>
       <br/>
       <br class="kix-line-break"/>
       <span>
        <span>
         "Sugar daddies"the official moniker granted to these wealthy menand the microcosm they occupy arent anything new, but theyve become more mainstream in recent years. That theyve emerged as
        </span>
        <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/education/archive/2014/09/how-sugar-daddies-are-financing-college-education/379533/">
         <span>
          a noteworthy group
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         during America's student-debt crisis is indicative of their growing prevalenceas well as that of "sugar babies," the ones entrenched in that crisis. And the subculture"daddies" and "babies" alikeappears to be expanding rapidly. 2014 saw a huge spike in sugar babies nationwide, especially in the southern states, according to new data from
        </span>
        <a href="https://www.seekingarrangement.com/about-us">
         <span>
          SeekingArrangement
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         , a site where "babies" and "daddies" sign up and connect. The trend itself, let alone writing about it, might seem frivolous or demeaning. But the data could clarify what's going wrong with the system and where those problems lie.
        </span>
       </span>
       <br/>
       <br class="kix-line-break"/>
       <span>
        <span>
         The
        </span>
        <a href="http://www.projectonstudentdebt.org/">
         <span>
          latest figures
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         on student-loan debtnow an average of $28,400 per personare frightening. This number has
        </span>
        <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/numbers/congatulations-to-class-of-2014-the-most-indebted-ever-1368/">
         <span>
          steadily risen
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         over the past few years, and, worse yet, its likely much higher than estimated considering only 57 percent of public and private nonprofit colleges volunteered to report their statistics this past year. Moreover, these debt figures exclude for-profit colleges, which are notorious for their
         <a href="http://projectonstudentdebt.org/files/pub/CDR_2014_NR.pdf">
          especially high student debt-default rates.
         </a>
        </span>
        <br class="kix-line-break"/>
        <br class="kix-line-break"/>
        <span>
         What might have been little more than a nuisance in the past has turned into an outright hindrance to many students financial security: It takes about
        </span>
        <a href="http://business.financialpost.com/2012/09/04/student-debt-average-payback-takes-14-years/">
         <span>
          14 years
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         on average to pay off the debt. As a result, young women across the country are turning to sugar daddies in droves. Many of them use SeekingArrangement, which describes itself as "the worlds largest Sugar Daddy dating site." More than 1.4 million students have signed up as members, including nearly 1 million in the U.S., according to the company. The website claims that 42 percent of its members are students, many of whom are incentivized by SeekingArrangement to join; people who sign up with a .edu email address or show proof of enrollment, for example, receive "premium memberships" for free.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         The whole thing may seem shady, but in its defense SeekingArrangement has strict rules prohibiting the exchange of money on its site. It also apparently has an in-house team that does background checks on members. Understandably, the company is mired in controversy. One
        </span>
        <span>
         <em>
          New York Post
         </em>
        </span>
        <span>
         contributor even accused the sugar-baby industry of trying to
        </span>
        <a href="http://nypost.com/2014/02/09/sugar-daddy-website-has-coeds-rationalizing-prostitution/">
         <span>
          justify prostitution
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         , one of the many claims to which SeekingArrangement eventually responded with a disclaimer. Last year, the company set up a FAQ-esque page, "a refresher course in the definitions of Sugar" that aimed to delineate the so-called
        </span>
        <a href="https://www.seekingarrangement.com/blog/four-differences-between-sugar-prostitution/">
         <span>
          differences between sugar baby-ing and prostitution
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         .
        </span>
        <br class="kix-line-break"/>
        <br class="kix-line-break"/>
        <span>
         But for many, thats all old news. Now, the latest data reveals not only that the phenomenon is spreading, but also that its gaining traction in certain areas much more than in others.
        </span>
       </span>
       <br/>
       <br class="kix-line-break"/>
       <span>
        <span>
         The University of Texas at Austin, in particular, saw a massive growth in sign-ups between 2013 and 2014.
        </span>
        With a 227 percent increase the growth far outpaced all
        <span>
         other schools in the country when it came to the sugar phenomenon, according to SeekingArrangement. In fact, according to the company, last year was the first time several Texas schools even appeared on the list. (Four schools in the Lone Star State made the most recent top-50 list). So while sheer sugar-baby numbers are important, growth rates are telling, too. Here are the top-five schools in terms of growth in sign-ups between 2013 and 2014:
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <big>
        <strong>
         Colleges With the Highest Growth in "Babies" in 2014
        </strong>
       </big>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:58.7719298246%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2015/01/Growth2014-4/63e8403b5.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="credit">
        The Atlantic
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <hr/>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         Angela Bermudo, a spokeswoman for SeekingArrangement, speculated that Austins ranking as the 10th "sugar-daddy capital in North America," has made it especially convenient for students in the area to sign up. Austin currently has an 8.03 on an index titled "Top 30 Sugar Daddy Cities"yes, that actually is a thingwhich shows the number of sugar daddies per 1,000 males.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_education&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16" title="">

         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         Bermudo also reasoned that the growth rate could be symptomatic of a grapevine effect in that more and more people on the UT campus are talking about it, leading to skyrocketing participation numbers. SeekingArrangements popularity, according to Bermudo, is concentrated in certain pocketsspecific campuses or areas. "Growth mostly happens through word-of-mouth, particularly between peers," she said. "What happens is that students hear about this opportunity [and] are convinced to join by a friend who has tried it."
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <big>
       <strong>
        Cities With the Highest Density of "Daddies"
       </strong>
      </big>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:62.2807017544%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2015/01/distri/a86141626.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="credit">
        The Atlantic
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <hr/>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <br class="kix-line-break"/>
       <span>
        <span>
         Surprisinglyor not, depending on whom you speak toa large majority of the schools with the most new sugar babies in 2014 (43 out of the top 50) are public institutions, which typically charge much less for tuition than private schools. They also tend to cater to larger share of low-income students, a group more likely to require financial "assistance." Still, in terms of sheer numbers, it was a private schoolNew York University, which this year
        </span>
        <a href="http://colleges.usnews.rankingsandreviews.com/best-colleges/nyu-2785">
         <span>
          charges $46,170 a student
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         for tuition and feesthat this year became the first college ever to cross the "1000 sugar babies" threshold. After all, the
         <a href="http://www.cbsnews.com/pictures/10-most-expensive-cities-in-america/">
          cost of living in New York City is arguably higher
         </a>
         than it is in any other U.S. cityan even-harder reality for a financially strapped college student. Arizona State University followed right behind NYU, with 923 sugar babies.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <big>
       <strong>
        U.S. Colleges With the Highest Number of "Babies"
       </strong>
      </big>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:62.6315789474%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2015/01/Totalmembership/c757cd1d0.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="credit">
        The Atlantic
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <hr/>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         The regional data is noteworthy, too. The South had a huge boon in sugar babies last year: Nearly half, or 21, of the 50 colleges on the list are located in region, with an average of 153 new sign-ups per school. That statistic might come as a bit of a surprise considering the areas with the highest debt rates are
        </span>
        <a href="http://projectonstudentdebt.org/files/pub/classof2013.pdf">
         <span>
          concentrated in the Northeast and Midwest
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         .
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <big>
       <strong>
        Regions With the Most New Sign-ups
       </strong>
      </big>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:75.2631578947%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2015/01/Regiondata/117d43083.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="credit">
        The Atlantic
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <hr/>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         Statistics aside, the fact that this path has become increasingly popular among so many young women is a damning indictment of the country's higher-education system. This is something that SeekingArrangement is acutely aware of. In fact, its
         <a href="https://www.seekingarrangement.com/blog/unique-ways-pay-college/">
          marketing has expanded
         </a>
         in the past few years
        </span>
       </span>
       the release of this data a testament to thatto specifically attract more students. But a
       <span>
        <span>
         s morally suspect as seeking a "baby" arrangement may seem, for many college students this "outside help" is increasingly the only way out of a lifetime shackled to debt.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>

        <h3 class="hed">
         Why the Iran Deal Makes Obama's Critics So Angry
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/peter-beinart/" title="Peter Beinart">
          Peter Beinart
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         The nuclear agreement highlights the limits of American powersomething the presidents opponents wont accept.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Mankind faces a crossroads,
          <a href="http://www.skeptic.ca/Woody_Allen_Speech_to_Graduates.htm">
           declared
          </a>
          Woody Allen. One path leads to despair and utter hopelessness. The other, to total extinction. Let us pray we have the wisdom to choose correctly.
         </p>
         <p>
          The point is simple: In life, what matters most isnt how a decision compares to your ideal outcome. Its how it compares to
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/alternatives-obama-iran-nuclear-deal-israel/389751/">
           the alternative at hand
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The same is true for the Iran deal,
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-33518524">
           announced Tuesday
          </a>
          <span>
           between Iran and six world powers
          </span>
          . As Congress begins debating the agreement, its opponents have three real alternatives. The first is to kill the deal, and the
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/the-iran-nuclear-deal-by-the-numbers/389592/">
           interim agreement
          </a>
          that preceded it, and do nothing else, which means few restraints on Irans nuclear program. The second is war. But top American and Israeli officials have warned that military action against Iranian nuclear facilities could ignite a catastrophic regional conflict and would be ineffective, if not counterproductive, in delaying Irans path to the bomb. Meir Dagan, who oversaw the Iran file as head of Israels external spy agency, the Mossad, from 2002 to 2011,
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/04/world/middleeast/04mossad.html">
           has said
          </a>
          an attack would mean regional war, and in that case you would have given Iran the best possible reason to continue the nuclear program. Michael Hayden, who ran the CIA under George W. Bush from 2006 to 2009,
          <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2012/01/19/bushs-cia-director-we-determined-attacking-iran-was-a-bad-idea/">
           has warned
          </a>
          that an attack would guarantee that which we are trying to prevent: an Iran that will spare nothing to build a nuclear weapon.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <a class="read-more" data-omni-click="inherit" href="/international/archive/2015/07/iran-nuclear-deal-obama/398450/">
        Continue Reading
       </a>
      </li>
      <li class="most-popular-article ">
       <a class="article-link" data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'mostpop',$li,r'398465.'" href="/international/archive/2015/07/iran-nuclear-weapons-deal-obama/398465/">
        <figure class="lead-img">
         <img class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/mt/2015/07/RTR2DHZS/hero_wide_640.jpg?GE2DGNRYG44TGOBVFYYA===="/>
         <figcaption class="credit">
          Morteza Nikoubazl / Reuters
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <h3 class="hed">
         The Single Most Important Question to Ask About the Iran Deal
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/jeffrey-goldberg/" title="Jeffrey Goldberg">
          Jeffrey Goldberg
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         <span>
          Making sense of a morally dubious agreement that might be a practical necessity
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          <em>
           Look, 20 years from now, Im still going to be around, God willing. If Iran has a nuclear weapon, its my name on this. 
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/05/obama-interview-iran-isis-israel/393782/">
            President Barack Obama, May 21, 2015
           </a>
          </em>
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <p>
          The theocratic regime that rules Irana regime that is a committed and proficient sponsor of terrorism, according to John Kerrys State Department
          <span>
           
          </span>
          will be more powerful tomorrow than it is today, thanks to the
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/07/15/world/middleeast/iran-nuclear-deal-is-reached-after-long-negotiations.html?hp&amp;action=click&amp;pgtype=Homepage&amp;module=span-ab-top-region&amp;region=top-news&amp;WT.nav=top-news">
           agreement it has just negotiated
          </a>
          with the Obama administration, Americas European allies, and two U.S. adversaries as well.
         </p>
         <p>
          This sad conclusion is unavoidable. The lifting of crippling sanctions, which will come about as part of the nuclear deal struck in Vienna, means that at least
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/05/obama-interview-iran-isis-israel/393782/">
           $150 billion
