     <p>
      <b>
       Hasty pudding
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Pudding" title="Pudding">
       pudding
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Porridge" title="Porridge">
       porridge
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">
       grains
      </a>
      cooked in
      <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">
       milk
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      . In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , it invariably refers to a version made of ground
      <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
       corn
      </a>
      . Hasty pudding is notably mentioned in a verse of the early American song
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Doodle" title="Yankee Doodle">
        Yankee Doodle
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Britain">
       In Britain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Britain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Since the 16th century at least, hasty pudding has been a
      <a href="/wiki/British_cuisine" title="British cuisine">
       British
      </a>
      dish of
      <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">
       wheat
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">
       flour
      </a>
      cooked in boiling milk or water until it reaches the consistency of a thick
      <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">
       batter
      </a>
      or an
      <a href="/wiki/Oatmeal" title="Oatmeal">
       oatmeal
      </a>
      porridge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hasty pudding was used as a term for the latter by
      <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Glasse" title="Hannah Glasse">
       Hannah Glasse
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Art_of_Cookery" title="The Art of Cookery">
        The Art of Cookery
       </a>
      </i>
      (1747).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_North_America">
       In North America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In North America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Extant North American recipes include wheat, oat, and corn-based puddings.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eliza_Leslie.27s_recipes">
       Eliza Leslie's recipes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eliza Leslie's recipes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Leslie" title="Eliza Leslie">
       Eliza Leslie
      </a>
      , an influential American
      <a href="/wiki/Cookbook" title="Cookbook">
       cookbook
      </a>
      author of the early 19th century, includes a recipe for
      <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">
       flour
      </a>
      hasty pudding in her 1840
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Leslie" title="Eliza Leslie">
        Directions for Cookery, In Its Various Branches
       </a>
      </i>
      , and calls the corn type "Indian mush". She calls an oatmeal version
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Burgoo" title="Burgoo">
        burgoo
       </a>
      </i>
      . She stresses the need for slow cooking rather than haste, and also recommends the use of a special
      <i>
       mush-stick
      </i>
      for stirring to prevent lumps. (This mush-stick is perhaps related to the
      <a href="/wiki/Spurtle" title="Spurtle">
       spurtle
      </a>
      , or the
      <i>
       pudding stick
      </i>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">
       nursery rhyme
      </a>
      beating.)
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Corn_or_Indian_mush">
       Corn or Indian mush
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corn or Indian mush">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A North American version, known as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mush_(maize)" title="Mush (maize)">
        corn mush
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
        Indian
       </a>
       mush
      </i>
      , is in its simplest form corn meal cooked slowly in water until it thickens. Like
      <a href="/wiki/Grits" title="Grits">
       grits
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Polenta" title="Polenta">
       polenta
      </a>
      , it may be eaten hot, or left to cool and solidify, when slices of the cold pudding may then be fried. This hasty pudding was once a popular American food because of its low cost, long shelf life, and versatility, and was eaten with both sweet and savory accompaniments, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">
       maple syrup
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">
       molasses
      </a>
      , or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salted_meat" title="Salted meat">
       salted meat
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_pudding">
       Indian pudding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indian pudding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Indian pudding is a traditional
      <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
       New England
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">
       dessert
      </a>
      , "a cold-weather classic".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seavey_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-seavey-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Seventeenth-century English colonists brought hasty pudding to North America and transformed it completely. Lacking wheat, they substituted
      <a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">
       cornmeal
      </a>
      , a grain they learned to cultivate from the
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       indigenous peoples
      </a>
      , which led to the new name
      <i>
       Indian pudding
      </i>
      , derived from their name for cornmeal,
      <i>
       Indian meal
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weiss_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-weiss-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They substituted milk, which was plentiful, for water and added locally available sweeteners, either
      <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">
       molasses
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">
       maple syrup
      </a>
      , and spices when available, typically
      <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">
       cinnamon
      </a>
      and ground
      <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">
       ginger
      </a>
      . Other traditional ingredients include butter and eggs for a smoother consistency and raisins and nuts for flavor and contrasting texture. Finally, Indian pudding is baked in a slow oven for several hours, transforming its texture from the porridge-like quality of hasty pudding to a smoother texture more typical of custard puddings. According to Kathleen Wall,
      <a href="/wiki/Plimoth_Plantation" title="Plimoth Plantation">
       Plimoth Plantation
      </a>
      's expert on colonial cooking, "The longer it cooks, the more liquid the gritty cornmeal absorbs, and the more it absorbs, the smoother the texture of your pudding."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seavey_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-seavey-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was commonplace in the colonial era and enjoyed a revival as part of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Day" title="Thanksgiving Day">
       Thanksgiving Day
      </a>
      celebrations in the late 19th century. It was found in most American cookbooks before 1900.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 20th century's commercial puddings with their industrially perfect smooth consistency displaced Indian pudding and its cooking time had little appeal for the modern home cook. It is still associated with autumn holidays and occasionally revived by restaurants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weiss_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-weiss-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is usually served warm and sometimes accompanied by vanilla ice cream or whipped cream.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_.22Yankee_Doodle.22">
       In "Yankee Doodle"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: In "Yankee Doodle"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Hasty pudding is referred to in a verse of the early American song "
      <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Doodle" title="Yankee Doodle">
       Yankee Doodle
      </a>
      ":
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Fath'r and I went down to camp
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Along with Captain Goodin',
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          6
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        And there we saw the men and boys
       </dd>
       <dd>
        As thick as hasty puddin'
       </dd>
      </dl>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_dishes">
       Similar dishes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hasty_pudding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Similar dishes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Polenta" title="Polenta">
       Polenta
      </a>
      is the Italian version of hasty pudding, with
      <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
       corn
      </a>
      substituted for the wheat originally used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
       Romans
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/M%C4%83m%C4%83lig%C4%83" title="Mmlig">
       Mmlig
      </a>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Romanian_cuisine" title="Romanian cuisine">
       Romanian
      </a>
      version, also made with
      <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
       corn
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the recipe for the
      <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_cuisine" title="Brazilian cuisine">
       Brazilian
      </a>
      pudding
      <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
       <i>
        curau
       </i>
      </span>
      (
      <small>
       Portuguese pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">
        [kuaw]
       </a>
      </span>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Sweet_corn" title="Sweet corn">
       sweet corn
      </a>
      grains are taken raw together with milk and most often coconut milk to a blender until uniformly liquid, then cooked until it becomes thick (shredded coconut might be added in the last minutes), with cinnamon powder sprinkled at the end.
     </p>
