       <h1 class="postTitle">
        A History of Cremation
       </h1>
       <div class="blogMeta">
        March 13th, 2015 | By  | Posted in
        <a href="http://www.losangelescremation.net/category/uncategorized/" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in Uncategorized">
         Uncategorized
        </a>
        |
       </div>
       <div class="theContent">
        <p>
         <img align="right" alt="" height="200" src="http://www.losangelescremation.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/handful-of-gray-ash-on-white-background.jpg" width="300px"/>
         Cremation is a process that has been taking place for thousands of decades. There is evidence of decorative pottery urns found among the remains of the Slavic people of Russia dating back to 3000 B.C. The topic of death and particularly the processes associated with the care and disposal of a body during death is an intriguing one.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Early Cremation
        </h2>
        <p>
         As stated previously, there is evidence of cremation from urns as early as 3000 B.C. in Europe and the Near East. The spread of cremation during the Bronze Age to the British Isles as well as Portugal and Spain took place between 2500 to 1000 B.C. Evidence of cremation cemeteries from this time period in Italy, Hungary, and Ireland also is found. By 25 B.C., cremation was a widely used and accepted method of burial in many countries and cultures.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Religious Beliefs
        </h2>
        <p>
         While cremation was a widely practiced method in many parts of the world by 25 B.C., there were exceptions to this rule where Christians and Jewish religions were concerned. Christians believed the cremation process to be a pagan ritual, and the Jewish religion preferred a more traditional sepulchre entombment upon burial.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Earth Burial and Modern Cremation
        </h2>
        <p>
         Due to the widely held Christian beliefs in most of Europe, by 400 A.D. cremation was almost abolished and replaced with an earth burial, except in times of sweeping plagues or war. This remained the standard burial practice until a development by Italian Professor Brunetti of a dependable chamber. The model was perfected in 1873 and displayed at the Vienna Exposition, which reignited the cremation practice worldwide.
        </p>
