       <h2>
        Social Signals Type 1: Customer Relationship Signals
       </h2>
       <p>
        Chances are, after someone buys your product, they dont go into a black hole forever (unless, of course, you are selling black hole tours). What really happens is that your customers use your product, buy upgrades or replacements and even talk about it with their friends. You can take advantage of these signals by listening and learning, then acting in a way that resonates specifically with their needs and desires. Watch out for these kinds of
        <i>
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          signals from your existing customers:
         </span>
        </i>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <b>
          Anticipate Buying Signals
         </b>
          What do they want to buy and when?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Customer Service Signals
         </b>
          What issues do they have with your product?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Customer Satisfaction Signals
         </b>
          How happy were they with your product?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Customer Personality Signals
         </b>
          What will an individual react strongly (and positively) to?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Customer Segmentation Signals
         </b>
          What will resonate with all (or most) of your customers?
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h2>
        Social Signals Type 2: Prospective Customer Relationship Signals
       </h2>
       <p>
        Not everyone is your customer  but they can be. Social media is full of people who need and want your product, but just dont know it yet. By monitoring for prospective customer relationship signals you can learn what they want/need as well as how to best show them the problems you can solve for them.
        <em>
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          Prospective customers will show these kinds of signals
         </span>
        </em>
        that you can react to:
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <b>
          Market Research Signals
         </b>
          Where is your industry going? What can you do to stay ahead of the curve?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Anticipate Buying Signals
         </b>
          What problems do people have that your product can solve?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Conquest Customer Signals
         </b>
          What can you do to help out when a customer of your competitor is unhappy?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Prospect Personality Signals
         </b>
          What will entice an individual to try your product themselves?
         <b>
         </b>
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Prospective Customer Segmentation Signals
         </b>
          What campaigns or promotions will appeal to your entire market segment?
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h2>
        Social Signals Type 3: Competitor Signals
       </h2>
       <p>
        Very few businesses exist without competition  and frankly, thats a good thing. The idea that your competition will drive you and help you grow is alive and well in the social media landscape. Using your social media management software and analysis of competitor signals, you can not only see what your competition is doing, but what you can do to improve yourself and your product.
        <em>
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          Look out for this kind of information from your competitors
         </span>
        </em>
        :
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <b>
          Competitor Market Signals
         </b>
          What are your competitors doing? Can it hurt/help your own marketing efforts?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Competitor Customer Service Signals
         </b>
          What are your competitors customers unhappy/happy about?
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Competitor Research Signals
         </b>
          What needs does your competitors product meet/ not meet?
        </li>
       </ul>
