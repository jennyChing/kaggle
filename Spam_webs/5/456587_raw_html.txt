      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Unlock The Value Of Data Discovery For Your Business
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="bylines">
       <cite class="entity_block type_inline_block">

      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         <em>
          by Lindy Ryan and Manan Goel
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Discovery and BI share the same missionto derive insights from data and deliver value to the business. Both processes rely on data, require some degree of business context and knowledge, and pivot between analysis and verification to be actionable. Discovery and BI each provide business benefits, but they are distinctly different. So what does that mean for your organization?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          From Rear-View to Forward
         </strong>
         <strong>
          
         </strong>
         <strong>
          Looking
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Traditional BI focuses on establishing pre-defined business goals and metrics, and then verifying data against them. This process is designed to understand data through a disciplined process of analyze, design and develop. It looks inherently backwards to consume data as-is through reporting and analysis, then it achieves a rearview-mirror perspective into the business.
        </p>
        <p>
         Discovery, by contrast, begins not with a pre-definition but with a goal. Rather than the rely-and-verify approach of BI, discovery approaches the data in an iterative process of explore, discover, verify and operationalize. This method uncovers new insights and then builds and operationalizes new analytic models that provide value back to the business.
        </p>
        <p>
         A new mindset or culture that creates an iterative and agile environment within an organization is critical in achieving benefits for the business. This culture enables business users from analysts to data scientists to help organizations by unlocking the value of discovery.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ultimately, the fundamental difference between BI and discovery is simple: one starts with a pre-definition of the data while the other ends with a new definition that drives new insights into the data.
         <br/>
         <!--nextpage-->
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Reap the Highest Reward
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The key to delivering the most value through discovery is to enable as many users as possibleboth in quantity and varietyacross the organization to participate in it. Every data user in the company can be a vessel to discovery, from casual users to power users and business analysts to the data scientists and analytic modelers.
        </p>
        <p>
         Along with enabling more users to take part in the process, discovery-centric organizations should also seek to extend the role of traditional data stewards. By evolving the position from a standard project steward to one that includes working with new datasuch as a big data steward that would use data from websites, social media, machine-to-machine interactions and large transactionscompanies gain insights from more data sources to deliver a more comprehensive view of the business. Organizations can also leverage the role of their data scientists to recommend algorithms, verify and test analytics models, and more importantly, conduct research and mentor others within the organization to maximize their discovery processes.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Foster a Discovery Culture
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Because discovery is iterative and agile in nature, it is dependent on providing access and data integration abilities so analysts can work with all data, not just the information stored in the data warehouse. Unlike a BI Competency Center (BICC)-based or BI-centric approach, a discovery environment operates under the new mental model of fail fast, discover more. As such, it must be embedded into the fabric of the business and focus on enabling a collaborative process that gives analysts the agility to access, assemble, verify, deploy and share processes that allow them to rapidly explore data for insights.
        </p>
        <p>
         This approach leverages all forms of business analyticsincluding behavior, path and pattern, graph, textual, statistical and predictiveto achieve business goals and add value. But for the process to be successful, business users must have some level of independence from IT while leveraging intuitive, visually optimized tools to support exploration.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Change the Company Mindset
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Enabling a discovery culture isnt without its set of challenges. For example, it can be difficult for successful BI-delivery organizations to accept iterative failures as beneficial since that approach contrasts with the traditional, and prevalent, build-to-last mindset. In contrast, a built-to-change mentality is needed to be agile, discover opportunities faster and capitalize on them before competitors.
         <br/>
         <!--nextpage-->
         <br/>
         Additionally, organizations face new challenges with their governance processes. Roles and responsibilities for accessing and working with data should be established, as should definitions of ownership and delegation for the semantic context that is uncovered. Results of discovery, along with monitoring the operationalization of new analytic models, must also be part of governance procedures.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The New Approach to BI
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         While discovery and BI share some commonalities, they are fundamentally different in approach and process. Data discovery is expected to become the prominent approach to BI over the next several years. To maximize the value of discovery, organizations are making the modern analyst a partner in the process by enabling them with the environment, access and tools they require to be self-sufficient.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Lindy Ryan is the research director for data discovery and visualization at Radiant Advisors. She leads research and analyst activities in data discovery, visualization and data science.
         </em>
        </p>

         </em>
