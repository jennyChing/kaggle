         <p>
          Nowadays, everywhere you look there are charities and conservationists posting pleas for help with the latest issue to face our environment. Whether it be dwindling populations of tigers, melting ice caps or oil drilling in a protected zone, it is a continuous barrage of depressing facts and figures.
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course, they are all extremely important and personally I do my best to help promote such issues and pledge to fund projects which I think are very promising. However, recently I have begun to worry that there are so many conservation issues out there nowthat the public are beginning to become immune to the doom and gloom approach taken to attract interest. I wonder if people are starting to adopt the attitude of what hope is left and is there any point in trying now?. It seems that almost anything someone does or buys can step on the toes of aconservation issue, and it takes a strong willed person to go out of their way to change their daily routine for the better.
         </p>
         <p>
          I am in no way saying that charities should stop bringing such issues to light, but maybe a new approach is needed. Instead of just asking for donations to fix an issue, or telling us to turn lights off in our homes, maybe it would work well to give statistics which
          <strong>
           show the difference we can make on a personal level
          </strong>
          .I worry that promotions saying if everyone did their bit an issue would be fixed cause people to think that not everyone will do it, so why should they bother too.
         </p>
         <p>
          Take recycling as an example. Most of us have recycling bins in our houses now, but a lot of us are guilty of still throwing some recyclable materials in the landfill bin, purely because it was the closest bin to hand. Did you know that
          <strong>
           r
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            ecycling one aluminum can saves enough energy to run a TV for three hours
           </span>
          </strong>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           ? Thats the kind of fact that makes someone sit up and think wow, I really can make a difference myself.
          </span>
         </p>
         <!--Ad Injection:top-->
         <div style="padding-top:5px;padding-bottom:5px;">
          <center>
           <a href="http://www.naturettl.com/join-business/">
            <img src="http://cdn2.naturettl.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/728x90.jpg"/>
           </a>
          </center>
         </div>
         <p>
          As wildlife photographers, we are likely more exposed to these issues than others purely because of the types of people we interact with. Personally, I think the issues that affect wildlife are a lot harder to address than encouraging recycling. The majority of people have never seen deforestation with their own eyes. How can you expect someone to strongly relate to something they only hear and read about? I was filming in a remote forest in Cambodia, and I saw two silvered langurs huddling together at the top of the tree. You could see fear in their eyes as they watched trees being felled just 100 or 200 metres from them; the sound of the chainsaws drowning out the natural forest noises.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Are There Too Many Conservation Issues?" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-1567" height="367" src="http://cdn.naturettl.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/featured3-1024x480.jpg" width="785"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The reason I am writing this opinion piece in the inspiration section, is that I think it is important we do our best as wildlife photographers and filmmakers to bring these issues to light
          </strong>
          . The television industry is extremely powerful, and natural history documentaries are becoming increasingly popular. I think there needs to be more natural history programmes that look deeply at conservation issues, and dont focus purely on pretty images of animals in their environment. Instead,
          <strong>
           we need to have a mixture
          </strong>
          . A documentary that actually shows forests burning to be replaced by palm oil and the animals that lose their homes will help people to relate to the problem,
          <strong>
           provided it also shows solutions
          </strong>
          . For example, the BBC recently aired a fantastic production called
          <a href="http://www.google.co.uk/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=&amp;esrc=s&amp;source=web&amp;cd=1&amp;cad=rja&amp;uact=8&amp;ved=0CCIQFjAA&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.bbc.co.uk%2Fprogrammes%2Fb0465vqh&amp;ei=ose7U_vGCo3n7AbdpYGICA&amp;usg=AFQjCNF_-zGKGKaXG6v68tz9oxZJN3YNUA&amp;sig2=tSJnruCFoWEME_6Cj68qnQ&amp;bvm=bv.70138588,d.ZGU" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.google.co.uk/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=&amp;esrc=s&amp;source=web&amp;cd=1&amp;cad=rja&amp;uact=8&amp;ved=0CCIQFjAA&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.bbc.co.uk%2Fprogrammes%2Fb0465vqh&amp;ei=ose7U_vGCo3n7AbdpYGICA&amp;usg=AFQjCNF_-zGKGKaXG6v68tz9oxZJN3YNUA&amp;sig2=tSJnruCFoWEME_6Cj68qnQ&amp;bvm=bv.70138588,d.ZGU', ' I Bought a Rainforest']);" target="_blank">
           <em>
            I Bought a Rainforest
           </em>
          </a>
          . Filmmaker Charlie Hamilton-James travelled to the Amazon rainforest and showed the viewers the real situation that faces the forest there. However, the series ended showing somevery positive progress being made by the Crees Foundation and exactly how they work to fight deforestation.
         </p>
         <p>
          A conservation issue, if it wants to be addressed, must have the general public being able to relate to it and shown how they can help on a personal level. Statistics can encourage support if they tell people that there is a point, and that their own personal efforts will help to bring about change.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           What do you think? Engage in the comments below.
          </strong>
         </p>
