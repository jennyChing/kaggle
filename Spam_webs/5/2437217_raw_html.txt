          <p>
           <a href="http://theworldweshare.com/?attachment_id=2811" rel="attachment wp-att-2811">
            <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-2811" height="199" src="http://theworldweshare.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/11/6483131_s-300x199.jpg" title="Grizzly Bear Yellowstone Park" width="300"/>
           </a>
           Wyomings Yellowstone National Parks grizzly bears are an endangered species once more  but thats a good thing. In 2007 the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service took the bears off of the endangered list, causing uproar amongst conservationists. But a federal appeals court overturned this move on Tuesday, saying that wildlife managers werewrong to not protect grizzly bears anymore. So what came to the bears rescue? Climate change.
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            It could not reasonably be denied that white-bark pine loss presented at least a potential threat to the Yellowstone grizzly population, Judge Richard C. Tallman ruled.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Yellowstones grizzlies feed on white-bark pine trees, which have been ravagedas of late due to beetle infestation. The beetle population has explodeddue towarmertemperatures over the past few years, causing them to damageand kill off a large number of the bears food source.
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Its unprecedented in its intensity and scale, Diana Tomback, a white-bark pine expertfrom the University of Colorado at Denver, told the L.A. Times. Studies show that the majority of watersheds in the greater Yellowstone ecosystem have been ravaged and there are lots of places where there is 90%-plus mortality of beetle-ravaged mature trees.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Conservationists are afraid that the lack of white-bark pine trees will cause the bears to move into more heavily populated areas to search for food, which would cause more clashes with humans. They argued (successfully) that the grizzlies need protection because of this.
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Since delisting (the bears), the Fish and Wildlife Service has said itself that white-bark pine should be listed independently as an endangered species, attorney AndrewWetzler, who worked on behalf of one of the conservation groupsarguing for the bears,explained to the L.A. Times. I think it reflects the growing recognition, not just by the courts but by the executive branch as well, that global warming is completely transforming natural places across the West. Grizzlies are very large canaries in the coal mine, as are the trees that are dying across the West.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Climate change to the rescue (sort of).
          </p>
