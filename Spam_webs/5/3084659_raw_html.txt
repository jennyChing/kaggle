     <p>
      The
      <b>
       General Crisis
      </b>
      is the term used by some historians to describe the period of widespread conflict and instability that occurred from the early 17th century to the early 18th century in
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Europe" title="History of Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      and in more recent historiography in the world at large.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The concept is much debated by historians and there is no consensus.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="What_was_the_.22General_Crisis.22.3F">
       What was the "General Crisis"?
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_General_Crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title='Edit section: What was the "General Crisis"?'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term was coined by English Marxist historian
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hobsbawm" title="Eric Hobsbawm">
       Eric Hobsbawm
      </a>
      in his pair of 1954 articles entitled The Crisis of the Seventeenth Century published in
      <i>
       Past and Present
      </i>
      , and cemented by his contemporary,
      <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Trevor-Roper" title="Hugh Trevor-Roper">
       Hugh Trevor-Roper
      </a>
      , in a 1959 article entitled The General Crisis of the Seventeenth Century published in the same journal. Hobsbawm discussed an economic crisis in Europe; Trevor-Roper saw a wider crisis, a crisis in the relations between society and the State.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAston196567_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAston196567-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Trevor-Roper argued that the middle years of the 17th century in Western Europe saw a widespread break-down in politics, economics and society caused by a complex series of demographic, religious, economic and political problems. In this general crisis, various events such as the
      <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">
       English Civil War
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Fronde" title="Fronde">
       Fronde
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">
       France
      </a>
      , the climax of the
      <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">
       Thirty Years' War
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      and revolts against the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_monarchs" title="List of Spanish monarchs">
       Spanish Crown
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">
       Portugal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">
       Naples
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
       Catalonia
      </a>
      were all manifestations of the same problem. The most important cause of the general crisis,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in Trevor-Ropers opinion, was the conflict between Court and Country; that is between the increasingly powerful centralizing, bureaucratic, sovereign princely states represented by the court, and the traditional, regional, land-based aristocracy and gentry representing the country. In addition, the intellectual and religious changes introduced by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
       Renaissance
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">
       Protestant Reformation
      </a>
      were important secondary causes of the general crisis. The general crisis thesis generated much controversy between those, such as the Marxist historian Eric Hobsbawm, who believed in the general crisis thesis but saw the problems of 17th-century Europe as being more social and economic in origin than Trevor-Roper would allow, and those who simply denied there was any general crisis. Current historians interested in the General Crisis include
      <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Parker_(historian)" title="Geoffrey Parker (historian)">
       Geoffrey Parker
      </a>
      , who has authored a book on the subject.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Global_patterns">
       Global patterns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_General_Crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Global patterns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many historians have argued the 17th century was an era of crisis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParkerSmith1997_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParkerSmith1997-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAston1965_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAston1965-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Many other historians have rejected the idea.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2015)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Today there are historians who promote the crisis model,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      arguing it provides an invaluable insight into the warfare, politics, economics,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and even art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" title="Thirty Years War">
       Thirty Years War
      </a>
      (1618
      <a href="/wiki/1648" title="1648">
       48
      </a>
      ) focused attention on the massive horrors that wars could bring to entire populations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The 1640s in particular saw more state breakdowns around the world than any previous or subsequent period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParkerSmith1997_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParkerSmith1997-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAston1965_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAston1965-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, the largest state in Europe, temporarily disappeared. In addition, there were secessions and upheavals in several parts of the Spanish empire, the worlds first global empire. In Britain the entire Stuart monarchy (England, Scotland, Ireland, and its North American colonies) rebelled. Political insurgency and a spate of popular revolts seldom equaled shook the foundations of most states in Europe and Asia. More wars took place around the world in the mid-17th century than in almost any other period of recorded history. The crises spread far beyond Europefor example
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_China" title="Ming China">
       Ming China
      </a>
      , the most populous state in the world, collapsed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParker20081053.E2.80.9379_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParker20081053.E2.80.9379-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Chinas Ming dynasty and Japans
      <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">
       Tokugawa shogunate
      </a>
      had radically different economic, social, and political systems. However they experienced a series of crises during the mid-17th century that were at once interrelated and strikingly similar to those occurring in other parts of the world at the same time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Wakeman" title="Frederic Wakeman">
       Frederic Wakeman
      </a>
      argues that the crisis which destroyed the Ming dynasty was partly a result of the climatic change as well as and Chinas already significant involvement in the developing world economy. Bureaucratic dishonesty worsened the problem. Moreover, the Qing dynastys success in dealing with the crisis made it more difficult for it to consider alternative responses when confronted with severe challenges from the West in the 19th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicts_and_Wars">
       Conflicts and Wars
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_General_Crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conflicts and Wars">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Examples which have been given for general crisis and state breakdown during this period include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" title="Thirty Years War">
        Thirty Years War
       </a>
       in Germany (161848)
      </li>
      <li>
       The Economic Crisis in the
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
        Holy Roman Empire
       </a>
       (1619
       <a href="/wiki/1623" title="1623">
        23
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Wars of the Three Kingdoms">
        Wars of the Three Kingdoms
       </a>
       (163951),
       <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">
        The Protectorate
       </a>
       (165359), and the
       <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">
        Glorious Revolution
       </a>
       (1688) in Britain and Ireland
      </li>
      <li>
       The collapse of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">
        Ming Dynasty
       </a>
       and rise of the

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inflation">
       Inflation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_General_Crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inflation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Precious metals from newly discovered deposits in
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       America
      </a>
      , especially silver from
      <a href="/wiki/Potos%C3%AD" title="Potos">
       Potos
      </a>
      in modern
      <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
       Bolivia
      </a>
      and from
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      , were shipped by
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      as well as to Europe. Silver was less prevalent in China than in Europe and consequently more valuable. The continuous flow of silver from the New World caused a continuous flow of wealth from China to Spain. Similarly, the relative value of silver and gold was fixed by traditional law in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
       Ottoman Empire
      </a>
      . The fixed exchange rate resulted in wealth being drawn out of the Ottoman Empire as the value of silver could not be adjusted downwards. The silver imports also caused general price inflation throughout Europe. Rapid population growth until the mid-decades of the seventeenth century also initially contributed to inflation in some areas.

       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographic_decline">
       Demographic decline
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_General_Crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demographic decline">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For those who extend the General Crisis outside Europe, one symptom is dramatic population decline. For example, with the collapse of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">
       Ming Dynasty
      </a>
      the population of China fell by approximately 50 million between 1600 and 1644, a decrease of over 30%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Likewise, Germany's population was reduced by approximately 15% to 30% in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" title="Thirty Years War">
       Thirty Years War
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TYW-1618-48-TWC_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TYW-1618-48-TWC-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">
       Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth
      </a>
      also lost about a third of its population. Many theories have been proposed as to why population dropped so rapidly during this period. Renowned economist Robert Malthus proposed the theory of population limitation as a result of carrying capacity. In his theory, food supplies simply did not grow as fast as population. As such, the subsistence gap between food and population created a check on the population. In applying this theory to the demographic crisis of this period, much focus has been placed on the growth and expansion of the new monarchies of late medieval Europe. These newly centralized and bureaucratized monarchies with their greater demands for war placed greater demands on the populaces of their realms. The previously mentioned climatic change also helped to exacerbate these new problems of war.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">
       Climate change
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_General_Crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate change">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The General Crisis overlaps fairly neatly with the so-called
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">
       Little Ice Age
      </a>
      which some authorities locate in the 17th century. Of particular interest is the overlap with the
      <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">
       Maunder Minimum
      </a>
      . Across the Northern Hemisphere, the mid-17th century experienced almost unprecedented death rates. Geoffrey Parker has suggested that environmental factors may have been in part to blame, especially the global cooling trend of this period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParkerSmith2008_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParkerSmith2008-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
