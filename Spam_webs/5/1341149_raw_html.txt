     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Bizarre
       </b>
      </i>
      was a British
      <a href="/wiki/Alternative_media" title="Alternative media">
       alternative magazine
      </a>
      published from 1997 to 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-den_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-den-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is published by
      <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Publishing" title="Dennis Publishing">
       Dennis Publishing
      </a>
      , and is a sister publication to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fortean_Times" title="Fortean Times">
        Fortean Times
       </a>
      </i>
      .

     <p>
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      covers
      <a href="/wiki/Alternative_culture" title="Alternative culture">
       alternative culture
      </a>
      through interviews with
      <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">
       counterculture
      </a>
      personages, and articles about
      <a href="/wiki/LGBT_culture" title="LGBT culture">
       LGBT culture
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_subculture" title="Drug subculture">
       drug
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fetish_subculture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fetish subculture (page does not exist)">
       fetish
      </a>
      , and other
      <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">
       subcultures
      </a>
      . It also reviews the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      directors, musicians, authors, and visual artistsand of those who have a
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">
       cult following
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The magazine's news coverage includes unusual news events from around the world; development and impact of legislation concerning
      <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">
       censorship
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">
       civil liberties
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_offence" title="Sex offence">
       sex offences
      </a>
      , and occasionally, incidents of
      <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">
       human rights
      </a>
      abuses. Articles in
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      have examined the Manchester police's
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Spanner" title="Operation Spanner">
       Operation Spanner
      </a>
      of 1987,
      <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_Investigatory_Powers_Act_2000" title="Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000">
       Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000
      </a>
      , British legislation banning "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_pornography" title="Extreme pornography">
       extreme pornography
      </a>
      ", and the
      <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_Act_2000" title="Terrorism Act 2000">
       Terrorism Act 2000
      </a>
      . After the
      <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Sophie_Lancaster" title="Murder of Sophie Lancaster">
       murder of Sophie Lancaster
      </a>
      in 2007,
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      campaigned for awareness of bigotry against people who exhibit some form of
      <a href="/wiki/Deviance_(sociology)" title="Deviance (sociology)">
       cultural deviance
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lad_mag" title="Lad mag">
       lad mags
      </a>
      , issues of
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      commonly feature a semi-nude female model on the front cover, and reviews of weird gadgets, films, music, and websites.
     </p>

     <p>
      Earlier issues of
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      included a sealed section featuring censored pornography, in which images of anuses, genitalia, semen, and sex acts were obscured. The censorship was self-imposed to avoid alienating mainstream
      <a href="/wiki/Newsagent%27s_shop" title="Newsagent's shop">
       newsagent's shops
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booksellers" title="Booksellers">
       booksellers
      </a>
      .

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bizarre_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      was launched as a bimonthly title by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Brown_Publishing" title="John Brown Publishing">
       John Brown Publishing
      </a>
      in February 1997 and was edited by Fiona Jerome. It was an immediate success and changed to monthly issuance a year after its launch. Circulation peaked at more than 120,000 in 2000, but later the same year declined to less than 30,000
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      when I Feel Good (IFG) bought the magazine for 5 million. IFG was a company founded by former James Brown, the former editor of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Loaded_(magazine)" title="Loaded (magazine)">
        Loaded
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine. When IFG collapsed,
      <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Publishing" title="Dennis Publishing">
       Dennis Publishing
      </a>
      acquired
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      . The current editor of
      <i>
       Bizarre
      </i>
      is David McComb.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      Bizarre announced the end of publication in early 2015, with the February issue being its last.
