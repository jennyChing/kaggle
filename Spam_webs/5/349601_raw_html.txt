     <p>
      <b>
       Mirra Alfassa
      </b>
      (21 February 1878  17 November 1973), known to her followers as
      <b>
       The Mother
      </b>
      , was the spiritual collaborator of
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Aurobindo" title="Sri Aurobindo">
       Sri Aurobindo
      </a>
      . Her full name at birth was
      <b>
       Blanche Rachel Mirra Alfassa
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She came to
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Aurobindo" title="Sri Aurobindo">
       Sri Aurobindo
      </a>
      's spiritual retreat on 29 March 1914 in
      <a href="/wiki/Puducherry" title="Puducherry">
       Pondicherry
      </a>
      , India. Having to leave Pondicherry during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      , she spent most of her time in Japan where she met poet
      <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">
       Rabindranath Tagore
      </a>
      . Finally she returned to Pondicherry and settled there in 1920. After 24 November 1926, when Sri Aurobindo retired into seclusion, she founded her
      <a href="/wiki/Ashram" title="Ashram">
       ashram
      </a>
      (Sri Aurobindo Ashram), with a handful of disciples. She became the spiritual guide of the community.
     </p>
     <p>
      The experiences of the last thirty years of Alfassa's life were captured in the 13-volume work
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Agenda_(Mother)" title="The Agenda (Mother)">
        The Agenda
       </a>
      </i>
      . In those years she attempted the physical transformation of her body in order to become what she felt was the first of a new type of human individual by opening to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermind_(Integral_thought)" title="Supermind (Integral thought)">
       Supramental Truth Consciousness
      </a>
      , a new power of spirit that Sri Aurobindo had allegedly discovered. Sri Aurobindo considered her an
      <a href="/wiki/Incarnation" title="Incarnation">
       incarnation
      </a>
      of the Mother Divine and called her by that name: The Mother. Sri Aurobindo wrote a book about Alfassa in which he promoted her by the name " The Mother".
     </p>

     <p>
      Mirra (or Mira) Alfassa was born in Paris in 1878, of a
      <a href="/wiki/Jews_in_Turkey" title="Jews in Turkey">
       Turkish Jewish
      </a>
      father, Mose Maurice Alfassa (5 July 1843 - 13 September 1918), and an
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Egypt" title="History of the Jews in Egypt">
       Egyptian Jewish
      </a>
      mother, Mathilde Ismalun (26 August 1857 - 9 December 1944). She had an elder brother named Matto Mathieu Maurice Alfassa (13 July 1876 - 12 August 1942) who held numerous French governmental posts in Africa. The family migrated to France the year before she was born.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For the first eight years of her life she lived at 62
      <a href="/wiki/Boulevard_Haussmann" title="Boulevard Haussmann">
       Boulevard Haussmann
      </a>
      in Paris.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alfassa says that at age five she felt she did not belong in this world, and her sadhana (spiritual discipline) began then.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She claims that she would lapse into bliss and go into a trance sometimes when she was placed in an easy chair or during a meal, much to the annoyance of her mother, who regarded this behaviour as a social embarrassment. Between eleven and thirteen she claims she had a series of psychic and spiritual experiences revealed to her the existence of God, and
      <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
       man
      </a>
      's possibility of uniting with Him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At age 12 she was practicing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occultism" title="Occultism">
       occultism
      </a>
      and claimed to be travelling
      <a href="/wiki/Astral_projection" title="Astral projection">
       out of her body
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the experiences she claims she had, at the age of 13 every night for nearly a year, was of going out of her body and rising straight above the city:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At age 14 Alfassa was sent to a studio to learn art, and a year later she wrote as a school essay named
      <i>
       The Path of Later On
      </i>
      (Alfassa 1893). In 1893 she travelled to
      <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
       Italy
      </a>
      with her mother. While at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doge%27s_Palace,_Venice" title="Doge's Palace, Venice">
       Doge's Palace
      </a>
      in Venice she said she recalled a scene from a
      <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">
       past life
      </a>
      where she was strangled and thrown out into the canal (
      <i>
       The Mother  Some dates
      </i>
      ). At 16 she joined the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecole_des_Beaux_Arts" title="Ecole des Beaux Arts">
       Ecole des Beaux Arts
      </a>
      where she acquired the nickname "the Sphinx", and later exhibited at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Salon" title="Paris Salon">
       Paris Salon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 13 October 1897 she married Henri Franois Morisset (6 April 1870 - 15 November 1956), a student of
      <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Moreau" title="Gustave Moreau">
       Gustave Moreau
      </a>
      . Mirra and Henri had a son named Andr Henri Morisset (23 August 1898 - 29 March 1982). The Morisset family lived at Atelier, 15 Rue Lemercier, Paris, and Mirra became a part of the Parisian artistic circles, befriending the likes of
      <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">
       Auguste Rodin
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monet" title="Monet">
       Monet
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alfassa stated that between nineteen and twenty she had achieved a conscious and constant union with the Divine Presence, without the help of books or teachers. Soon after, she discovered
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivekananda" title="Vivekananda">
       Vivekananda
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Raja_Yoga_(book)" title="Raja Yoga (book)">
        Raja Yoga
       </a>
      </i>
      . She says about a year or two later she met an Indian in Paris who advised her to read the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhagavad-Gita" title="Bhagavad-Gita">
        Bhagavad-Gita
       </a>
      </i>
      , taking
      <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">
       Krishna
      </a>
      as a symbol of the inner or immanent Divine. She obtained a French translation whichshe relates was quite poor but still enabled her to understand the substance of it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Alfassa said that in her meditations she saw several spiritual figures, all of whom offered her help of one type or another.
     </p>
     <p>
      Around 1904 she encountered in her dreams a dark Asiatic figure whom she called Krishna. She said that this figure guided her in her inner journey. She came to have total implicit faith in Krishna, and was hoping to meet him one day in real life (Karmayogi no date). Around 1905 she met the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occultist" title="Occultist">
       occultist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Th%C3%A9on" title="Max Thon">
       Max Thon
      </a>
      , who explained her psychic experiences to her. She paid two extended visits (on the second one she was accompanied by or later joined by Morisset) to Thon's estate at
      <a href="/wiki/Tlemcen" title="Tlemcen">
       Tlemcen
      </a>
      , Algeria, to live with and learn occultism firsthand from Thon and his wife,
      <a href="/wiki/Alma_Theon" title="Alma Theon">
       Alma Theon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Alfassa had a very high regard for Thon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alfassa and Henri separated in 1908, and Alfassa then moved to 49 Rue des Lvis, Paris.
     </p>
     <p>
      Around this time Alfassa had regular meetings with students and seekers who were attracted to psychical phenomena or to mysticism. In 1906 Alfassa and her brother Matto founded in Paris a group named
      <i>
       l'Ide Nouvelle
      </i>
      ("The New Idea"). This group met at her home on Wednesday evenings, first at Rue Lemercier, then at 49 Rue des Lvis, and finally at 9 Rue du Val de Grace. Her book "Words of Long Ago" (vol.2 of the Collected Works) is an account of one of these meetings, along with talks she gave to the
      <i>
       L'Union de Pense Fminine
      </i>
      , which was a new study group she had established. In a conversation with
      <a href="/wiki/Prithwindra_Mukherjee" title="Prithwindra Mukherjee">
       Prithwindra Mukherjee
      </a>
      , one of the members of this group,
      <a href="/wiki/Alexandra_David-N%C3%A9el" title="Alexandra David-Nel">
       Alexandra David-Nel
      </a>
      , recalled those meetings and of Alfassa: "We spent marvellous evenings together with friends, believing in a great future. At times we went to the Bois de Boulogne gardens, and watched the grasshopper-like early aeroplanes take off. I remember her elegance, her accomplishments, her intellect endowed with mystical tendencies. In spite of her great love and sweetness, in spite even of her inherent ease of making herself forgotten after achieving some noble deed, she couldn't manage to hide very well the tremendous force she bore within herself."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1912 Alfassa organised a group of around 20 people named
      <i>
       Cosmique
      </i>
      , who had the aim of gaining self-knowledge and self-mastery. Although she had not yet met Aurobindo, some of her ideas at the time paralleled his.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These were later included at the start of her small book,
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Conversations_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Conversations (book) (page does not exist)">
        Conversations
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      On 5 May 1911 Mirra married Paul Antoine Richard (17 June 1874 - June 1967). Richard had travelled to India, seeking election to the French Senate from
      <a href="/wiki/Puducherry" title="Puducherry">
       Pondicherry
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and while there had met
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Aurobindo" title="Sri Aurobindo">
       Sri Aurobindo
      </a>
      in Pondicherry in mid-April 1910. Richard informed Alfassa of Sri Aurobindo and Sri Aurobindo remained in "material and spiritual correspondence" with the Richards for the next four years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1912 she wrote her first
      <i>
       Prayers and Meditations
      </i>
      (the original entry probably dating to the previous year). These were published as part of the
      <i>
       Collected Works
      </i>
      (Mother's Birth Centenary Edition vol. 1).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meeting_Sri_Aurobindo_Ghose">
       Meeting Sri Aurobindo Ghose
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirra_Alfassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meeting Sri Aurobindo Ghose">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On 7 March 1914, Alfassa and Richard embarked for India aboard the
      <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">
       steamer
      </a>
      <i>
       Kaga Maru
      </i>
      , reaching Pondicherry on the 29th. She later said that when she saw
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Aurobindo" title="Sri Aurobindo">
       Sri Aurobindo
      </a>
      for the first time, she recognised him as the person she saw in her visions of a dark Asiatic figure, whom she had earlier referred to as "Krishna". Next day she noted in her journal, It matters not if there are hundreds of beings plunged in densest ignorance. He whom we saw yesterday is on earth; his presence is enough to prove that a day will come when darkness shall be transformed into light, when Thy reign shall be indeed established upon earth."
     </p>
     <p>
      Years before Sri Aurobindo first met Alfassa and Richard, he had given up his revolutionary activities for Indian independence from British rule, and retreated to Pondicherry (where he was safe from arrest by the British) to work on the spiritual transformation of humanity and of life on earth.
     </p>
     <p>
      After a short period of intense
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadhana" title="Sadhana">
       sadhana
      </a>
      , Sri Aurobindo would sometimes give evening talks. In 1913 he moved to No.41 Rue Franois Martin, called the Guest House, where he would receive visitors in the morning (this would have been when Alfassa and Paul Richard met him), and after the group meditation (usually about 4. p.m.) he would host informal evening gatherings of his early disciples.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alfassa said that when she first met Sri Aurobindo, she found that her thoughts ceased to run, her mind became quiet, and silence began to gather momentum, until two or three days later there was only the silence and the
      <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">
       yogic
      </a>
      consciousness. In 1958 in the
      <i>
       Agenda
      </i>
      (vol I pp.1634) she told that the two experiences, the consciousness in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychic_Being" title="Psychic Being">
       psychic depths of the being
      </a>
      realised in 1910, and the stillness connection with the Divine above the head realised when first meeting Sri Aurobindo, have remained with her ever since.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 29 March, Richard suggested that Sri Aurobindo publish a journal dealing with a synthesis of the latter's philosophical ideas. The journal was named
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arya" title="Arya">
        Arya
       </a>
      </i>
      , and it became the vehicle for most of Sri Aurobindo's writings, which would later appear in book form (
      <i>
       The Mother  Some dates
      </i>
      ). The first issue of the monthly journal came out on 15 August 1914, Sri Aurobindo's birthday.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alfassa and Richard stayed at Pondicherry until February 1915, but had to return to Paris because of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">
       First World War
      </a>
      . They spent a year in France before traveling to Japan where they stayed for four years, first in Tokyo (1916 to 1917) and then
      <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">
       Kyoto
      </a>
      (19171920). They were also accompanied by Dorothy Mary Hodgson, an Englishwoman who had known Alfassa in France (Das p.209) and who regarded Alfassa as her guru.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During her stay, Alfassa adopted the Japanese way of life, mannerisms and dress, and visited many Buddhist places of pilgrimage (Das 1978 p.173) One Japanese friend recalled much later: "She came here to learn Japanese and to be one of us. But we had so much to learn from her and her charming and unpredictable ways" (Kobayashi, in Das 1978 p.193). In 1919 she met
      <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">
       Rabindranath Tagore
      </a>
      , who was staying at the same hotel. A group photograph in the Rabindra Museum collection at
      <a href="/wiki/Santiniketan" title="Santiniketan">
       Santiniketan
      </a>
      includes the two. Tagore presented Alfassa with the
      <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">
       typewriter
      </a>
      he was using at the time; she later gave it to
      <a href="/wiki/Prithwindra_Mukherjee" title="Prithwindra Mukherjee">
       Prithwindra Mukherjee
      </a>
      in the mid-50s for "writing good poems"; this still remains at the Sri Aurobindo Ashram (
      <i>
       ibid
      </i>
      p.206). Many years later (in 1956) she also recounted meeting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolstoy" title="Tolstoy">
       Tolstoy
      </a>
      's son while in Japan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 24 April 1920 Alfassa returned with Richard to Pondicherry from Japan, accompanied by Dorothy Hodgson. On 24 November, she moved to live near Aurobindo in the Guest House at Rue Franois Martin. Richard did not stay long; he spent a year traveling around North India (Das 1978 p.209;
      <i>
       The Mother  Some dates
      </i>
      ) as a sanyasi. (Some time later he initiated divorce proceedings, having already remarried in the meantime).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dorothy Hodgson meanwhile received the name
      <i>
       Vasavadatta
      </i>
      (abbreviated as
      <i>
       Datta
      </i>
      ) ("Consecrated") and was one of the earliest western devotees, even before the Ashram was established in 1926.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1921, when Sri Aurobindo said that they had brought the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermind_(Sri_Aurobindo)" title="Supermind (Sri Aurobindo)">
       Supermind
      </a>
      down to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vital_(Sri_Aurobindo)" title="Vital (Sri Aurobindo)">
       Vital Plane
      </a>
      , Alfassa appeared (according to witnesses and her own accounts) to have a body like that of an eighteen- or twenty-year-old, while Aurobindo was also glowing with health.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      But these changes were lost when they took the Supermind down to the work of transformation in the "
      <a href="/wiki/Subconscious" title="Subconscious">
       Subconscient
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      In January 1922, Alfassa, already called "The Mother" and some other disciples began regular evening talks and group meditations. In September or October of that year, Sri Aurobindo and The Mother moved to no.9 Rue de la Marine, where the same informal routine of Sri Aurobindo's evening gatherings of his early disciples
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (and Alfassa's talks and meditations) continued. As the number of disciples arriving increased, Alfassa organised what would later become the Ashram, more from the wish of the sadhaks than her or Sri Aurobindo's own plans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22The_Mother.22_of_the_Ashram">
       "The Mother" of the Ashram
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirra_Alfassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "The Mother" of the Ashram'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On 24 November 1926 the
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Aurobindo_Ashram" title="Sri Aurobindo Ashram">
       Sri Aurobindo Ashram
      </a>
      was founded . At the time there were no more than 24 disciples in the Ashram (
      <i>
       ibid
      </i>
      pp.2334).
     </p>
     <p>
      In December of that year, Sri Aurobindo decided to withdraw from public view. At this point he identified Alfassa with the Divine Mother, and instructed his followers to do the same. He informed his disciples that henceforth Alfassa would take full charge of the Ashram and he would live in retirement.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sri Aurobindo considered Alfassa to be an
      <a href="/wiki/Avatar" title="Avatar">
       Avatar
      </a>
      (incarnation) of the Supreme
      <a href="/wiki/Shakti" title="Shakti">
       Shakti
      </a>
      . In 1927 he wrote, "The One whom we adore as the Mother is the Divine Conscious Force
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1927, Sri Aurobindo and Alfassa moved to Rue Franois Martin, where they stayed for the remainder of their lives (
      <i>
       The Mother  Some dates
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early years, Alfassa appeared on the Ashram balcony to initiate the day with her blessings. She would also meet the heads of the various departments of the growing Ashram every morning, and then the sadhaks individually. Once again, in the evening at 5:30pm, she conducted meditation and met sadhaks.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1938
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Woodrow_Wilson" title="Margaret Woodrow Wilson">
       Margaret Woodrow Wilson
      </a>
      , the daughter of US President
      <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">
       Woodrow Wilson
      </a>
      , came to the Ashram and chose to remain there for the rest of her life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the war, Sri Aurobindo and Alfassa declared their support of the Allies. They said that victory of the Nazis would have been a disaster for the spiritual work, and claimed to have participated in world history, changing the course of World War II by working on the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Subtle_realm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Subtle realm (page does not exist)">
       subtle levels
      </a>
      (e.g. Purani 1982 p.746, Reddy 2000, Van Vrekhem 2001).
     </p>
     <p>
      Through letters, Alfassa had remained in contact with her son Andre Morisset ever since leaving for Japan, though he was prevented from visiting by the outbreak of World War II. In 1949 he finally arrived in
      <a href="/wiki/Puducherry" title="Puducherry">
       Pondicherry
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Work_of_Physical_Transformation">
       Work of Physical Transformation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirra_Alfassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Work of Physical Transformation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1950 Sri Aurobindo died.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From 1960 till her death in 1973, Alfassa had a number of near-weekly meetings with one of her disciples,
      <a href="/wiki/Satprem" title="Satprem">
       Satprem
      </a>
      . These conversations were published in French and English in the 13-volume set known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Agenda_(Mother)" title="The Agenda (Mother)">
       <i>
        The Agenda
       </i>
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1962, at the age of 84, she was forced by an illness to withdraw from close physical contact with disciples (
      <i>
       Agenda
      </i>
      vol.3), although she continued to give public
      <a href="/wiki/Dar%C5%9Bana" title="Darana">
       Darshans
      </a>
      four times a year, at which thousands of devotees gathered.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In her discussions, she is alleged to have had a number of formidable spiritual experiences in the 1950s through the 1970s. Her experiences are supposed to have intensified through the later 1960s and 70s.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1956 she established the Sri Aurobindo Ashram, Delhi Branch, together with Surendranath Jauhar and Mother's International School. In 1967 plans were made and some land acquired to found a city of spiritual seekers in
      <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">
       Gujarat
      </a>
      , which she named Ompuri and in 1968, Alfassa began Auroville .
     </p>
     <p>
      Mirra Alfassa died on 17 November 1973; three days later her body was placed in the Samadhi, the vault in the courtyard of the Ashram where Sri Aurobindo's body was placed in 1950.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Auroville">
       Auroville
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirra_Alfassa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Auroville">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Matrimandir,_Soul_of_the_Auroville,_near_Pondicherry.JPG">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Matrimandir,_Soul_of_the_Auroville,_near_Pondicherry.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Matrimandir, in Auroville, near Pondicherry
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Auroville" title="Auroville">
       Auroville
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the 1968, Alfassa helped create Auroville, a model universal township with a view to enhance the forces of evolution and to initiate a new renaissance in India and the world. At present, in 2015, Auroville is more of a large spread out community with a population of 2500 people from 50 countries. However, planning and development undercurrents are for outgrowing the present stage and emerge as a unique town envisioned by Alfassa.
     </p>
