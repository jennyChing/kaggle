     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Rubicon
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Rubico
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
       Italian
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Rubicone
      </i>
      ) is the name both of a shallow
      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       river
      </a>
      in northeastern
      <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
       Italy
      </a>
      just south of
      <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">
       Ravenna
      </a>
      , and the name historically given to a river that was
      <a href="/wiki/Crossing_the_Rubicon" title="Crossing the Rubicon">
       famously crossed
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
       Julius Caesar
      </a>
      in 49 BC. While it is not a proven fact, historians generally agree that the two rivers are indeed one and the same; this was not always the case.
     </p>
     <p>
      The modern-day river runs for about 80 kilometers, from the
      <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">
       Apennine Mountains
      </a>
      to the
      <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">
       Adriatic Sea
      </a>
      through the southern
      <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
       Emilia-Romagna
      </a>
      region, between the towns of
      <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">
       Rimini
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cesena" title="Cesena">
       Cesena
      </a>
      . The Latin word
      <i>
       <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
        rubico
       </span>
      </i>
      comes from the adjective
      <i>
       <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
        rubeus
       </span>
      </i>
      , meaning "red". The river was so named because its waters are colored red by mud deposits.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubicon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_republic" title="Roman republic">
       Roman republic
      </a>
      , the river Rubicon marked the boundary between the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">
       Roman province
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Gaul" title="Cisalpine Gaul">
       Cisalpine Gaul
      </a>
      to the north-east and Italy proper (controlled directly by Rome and its
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Socii" title="Socii">
        socii
       </a>
      </i>
      (allies)) to the south. On the north-western side, the border was marked by the river
      <a href="/wiki/Arno" title="Arno">
       Arno
      </a>
      , a much wider and more important waterway, which flows westward from the Apennine Mountains (its source is not far from Rubicon's source) into the Tyrrhenian Sea.
     </p>
     <p>
      Governors of Roman provinces were appointed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promagistrates" title="Promagistrates">
       promagistrates
      </a>
      with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">
        imperium
       </a>
      </i>
      (roughly, "right to command") in their province(s). The governor would then serve as the general of the Roman army within the territory of his province(s).
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">
       Roman law
      </a>
      specified that only the elected
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_magistrates" title="Roman magistrates">
       magistrates
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_consul" title="Roman consul">
       consuls
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">
       praetors
      </a>
      ) could hold
      <i>
       imperium
      </i>
      within Italy. Any promagistrate who entered Italy at the head of his troops forfeited his
      <i>
       imperium
      </i>
      and was therefore no longer legally allowed to command troops.
     </p>
     <p>
      Exercising
      <i>
       imperium
      </i>
      when forbidden by the law was a capital offence. Furthermore, obeying the commands of a general who did not legally possess
      <i>
       imperium
      </i>
      was also a capital offence. If a general entered Italy whilst exercising command of an army, both the general and his soldiers became outlaws and were automatically condemned to death. Generals were thus obliged to disband their armies before entering Italy.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Julius_Caesar">
       Julius Caesar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubicon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Julius Caesar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
       Julius Caesar
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="356" data-file-width="532" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif/300px-C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif/450px-C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C%C3%A6SAR_PAUSED_ON_THE_BANKS_OF_THE_RUBICON.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
          Julius Caesar
         </a>
        </i>
        paused on the banks of the Rubicon.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 49 BC, perhaps on January 10,
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
       C. Julius Caesar
      </a>
      led a single
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">
       legion
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Legio_XIII_Gemina" title="Legio XIII Gemina">
       Legio XIII Gemina
      </a>
      , south over the Rubicon from Cisalpine Gaul to Italy to make his way to Rome. In doing so, he (deliberately) broke the law on
      <i>
       imperium
      </i>
      and made armed conflict inevitable. Suetonius depicts Caesar as undecided as he approached the river, and attributes the crossing to a supernatural apparition. It was reported that Caesar dined with
      <a href="/wiki/Sallust" title="Sallust">
       Sallust
      </a>
      , Hirtius, Oppius, Lucius Balbus and Sulpicus Rufus on the night after his famous crossing into Italy January 10.[20]
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">
       Suetonius
      </a>
      , Caesar uttered the famous phrase
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alea_iacta_est" title="Alea iacta est">
        lea iacta est
       </a>
      </i>
      ("the
      <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">
       die
      </a>
      has been cast").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The phrase "crossing the Rubicon" has survived to refer to any individual or group committing itself irrevocably to a risky or revolutionary course of action, similar to the modern phrase "passing the
      <a href="/wiki/Point_of_no_return" title="Point of no return">
       point of no return
      </a>
      ". Caesar's decision for swift action forced
      <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">
       Pompey
      </a>
      , the lawful consuls (
      <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Claudius_Marcellus_Maior" title="Gaius Claudius Marcellus Maior">
       C. Claudius Marcellus
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Cornelius_Lentulus_Crus" title="Lucius Cornelius Lentulus Crus">
       L. Cornelius Lentulus Crus
      </a>
      ), and a large part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">
       Roman Senate
      </a>
      to flee Rome in fear. Caesar's subsequent victory in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_civil_war" title="Caesar's civil war">
       Caesar's civil war
      </a>
      ensured that punishment for the infraction would never be rendered. This took place during the time of the Roman Republic.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Location.2C_confusion_and_resolution">
       Location, confusion and resolution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubicon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location, confusion and resolution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After Caesar's crossing, the Rubicon was a geographical feature of note until about 42 BC, when
      <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">
       Octavian
      </a>
      merged the Province of
      <a href="/wiki/Cisalpine_Gaul" title="Cisalpine Gaul">
       Gallia Cisalpina
      </a>
      into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italia_(Roman_Empire)" title="Italia (Roman Empire)">
       Italia
      </a>
      and the river ceased to be the extreme northern border of Italy. The decision robbed the Rubicon of its importance, and the name gradually disappeared from the local
      <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
       toponymy
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      After the
      <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">
       fall of the Western Roman Empire
      </a>
      , and during the first centuries of the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      , the coastal plain between
      <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">
       Ravenna
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">
       Rimini
      </a>
      was flooded many times. The Rubicon, like other small rivers of the region, often changed its course during this period. For this reason, and to supply fields with water after the revival of agriculture in the late Middle Ages, during the 14th and 15th centuries, hydraulic works were built to prevent other floods and to regulate streams. As a result of this work, these rivers started to flow in straight courses, as they do today.
     </p>
     <p>
      With the revival of interest in the topography of ancient Roman Italy during the 15th century, the matter of identifying the Rubicon in the contemporary landscape became a topic of debate among
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanist" title="Renaissance humanist">
       Renaissance humanists
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To support the claim of the Pisciatello, a spurious inscription forbidding the passage of an army in the name of the Roman people and Senate, the so-called
      <i>
       Sanctio,
      </i>
      was placed by a bridge on that river. The
      <a href="/wiki/Quattrocento" title="Quattrocento">
       Quattrocento
      </a>
      humanist
      <a href="/wiki/Flavio_Biondo" title="Flavio Biondo">
       Flavio Biondo
      </a>
      was deceived by it;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the actual inscription is conserved in the Museo Archeologico, Cesena.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As the centuries went by, several rivers of the Adriatic coast between Ravenna and Rimini have at times been said to correspond to the ancient Rubicon.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Via_Aemilia" title="Via Aemilia">
       Via Aemilia
      </a>
      (National Road  9) still follows its original Roman course as it runs between hills and plain; it would have been the obvious course to follow as it was the only major Roman road east of the
      <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">
       Apennine Mountains
      </a>
      leading to and from the
      <a href="/wiki/Po_Valley" title="Po Valley">
       Po Valley
      </a>
      . Attempts to deduce the original course of the Rubicon can be made only by studying written documents and other archaeological evidence such as Roman milestones, which indicate the distance between the ancient river and the nearest Roman towns.
     </p>
     <p>
      The starting point of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_road" title="Roman road">
       Roman road
      </a>
      (some kind of "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mile_zero" title="Mile zero">
       mile zero
      </a>
      "), from which distances were counted, was always the crossing between the
      <a href="/wiki/Cardo" title="Cardo">
       Cardo
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Decumanus_Maximus" title="Decumanus Maximus">
       Decumanus
      </a>
      , the two principal streets in every Roman town, running north-south and east-west respectively. In a section of the
      <a href="/wiki/Tabula_Peutingeriana" title="Tabula Peutingeriana">
       Tabula Peutingeriana
      </a>
      , an ancient document showing the network of Roman roads, a river in north-eastern Italy labeled "
      <i>
       fl. Rubic
      </i>
      " is shown at a position 12 Roman miles (18km) north of Rimini along the coastline; 18km is the distance between Rimini and a place called "Ad Confluentes," drawn west of the Rubicon, on the Via Aemilia. However, the river-bed shape observed in Pisciatello and the Rubicone river in the present day, well below Roman age soil layers, is likely to indicate that any possible course modification of rivers could have occurred only very close to the coastline, and therefore only slight.
     </p>
     <p>
      Furthermore, the features of the present-day Rubicone river (North-South course, orthogonal to the Emilia road) and the Emilia road itself (a straight reach before and after the crossing, and a turn just passing by San Giovanni in Compito, so marking a possible administrative boundary) are common to typical geographical oriented limits of Roman age, being what made this a clue of actual identification of the present-day Rubicone River with
      <b>
       Fiumicino.
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1933, after various efforts that spanned centuries, the Fiumicino, which crossed the town of Savignano di Romagna (now
      <a href="/wiki/Savignano_sul_Rubicone" title="Savignano sul Rubicone">
       Savignano sul Rubicone
      </a>
      ), was officially identified as the former Rubicon. The final proof confirming this theory came only in 1991,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      when three Italian scholars (Pignotti, Ravagli, and Donati), after a comparison between the
      <a href="/wiki/Tabula_Peutingeriana" title="Tabula Peutingeriana">
       Tabula Peutingeriana
      </a>
      and other ancient sources, (including
      <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
       Cicero
      </a>
      ), showed that the distance from Rome to the Rubicon River was 200 Roman miles. Key elements of their work are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The locality of San Giovanni in Compito (now a western quarter of Savignano) has to be identified with the old
       <i>
        Ad Confluentes.
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Compito
       </i>
       means "road junction," and is synonymous with
       <i>
        confluentes.
       </i>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       The distance between
       <i>
        Ad Confluentes
       </i>
       and Rome, according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Tabula_Peutingeriana" title="Tabula Peutingeriana">
        Tabula Peutingeriana
       </a>
       , is 201 Roman miles.
      </li>
      <li>
       The distance from today's San Giovanni in Compito and the Fiumicino river is 1
       <a href="/wiki/Mile#Roman_mile" title="Mile">
        Roman mile
       </a>
       (1.48km).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Present">
       Present
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubicon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Present">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Today there is very little evidence of Caesars historical passage.
      <a href="/wiki/Savignano_sul_Rubicone" title="Savignano sul Rubicone">
       Savignano sul Rubicone
      </a>
      is an industrial town and the river has become one of the most polluted in the
      <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
       Emilia-Romagna
      </a>
      region. Exploitation of underground waters along the upper course of the Rubicon has reduced its flowit was a minor river even during Roman times (parvi Rubiconis ad undas as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcus_Annaeus_Lucanus" title="Marcus Annaeus Lucanus">
       Lucan
      </a>
      said, roughly translated "to the waves of [the] tiny Rubicon")and has since lost its natural route, except in its upper course between low and woody hills.
     </p>
