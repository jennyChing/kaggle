    <figure>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        Inspired by Andrew Kim's gorgeous
        <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/1670245/a-students-smart-microsoft-rebranding-is-better-than-the-real-thing" target="_self">
         Microsoft rebranding concept
        </a>
        , Dutch visual designer Aziz Firat
        <a href="http://www.azizfirat.com/blog/2014/7/11/rebranding-samsung" target="_blank">
         decided to unite the many elements
        </a>
        of Samsung's discordant branding "strategy" behind a clean new icon.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        The icon takes the form of a stylized "S" folded like a ribbon, which gives Samsung an powerful and recognizable symbol for the back of its devices.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        It also does double-duty as a home button design for smartphones, tablets, and other mobile products.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        Firat's concept isn't entirely without fault. He says the icon is meant to represent simplicity, quality, and unity, which sounds wonderful, but he's really branding a bizarro world version of Samsung.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        Still, the icon does offer consistency to the branding itself.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        And while rebranding alone can't make Samsung's actual products embody the simplicity, quality, and unity that Firat's new icon would have them aspire to, a revamped visual identity can signal to the world that a company is ready to take such concepts seriously.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>

         <div class="container">
          <article class="node full-view kind-slideshow slug-fastcodesign " data-resource-id="3034123" id="post-3034123" itemscope="" itemtype="http://nik.io/v1/schema/Article">
           <span content="http://www.fastcodesign.com/3034123/how-a-single-icon-could-make-a-world-of-difference-for-samsung" itemprop="permalink">
           </span>
           <h4 class="supertag slug-is-fastcodesign">

             <div class="twelve columns">
              <h1 class="title" itemprop="title headline">
               Designer Rebrands Samsung's Logo To Make It As Iconic As Apple's
              </h1>
             </div>
             <span class="deck pull-three" itemprop="deck description">
              <p>
               A Dutch designer imagines a better way to brand the Korean giant.
              </p>
             </span>
            </div>
            <div class="tools row collapse">
             <div class="twelve columns hide-on-print">
              <div class="share-box">
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </header>
           <div class="row collapse">
            <div class="body prose twelve columns" itemprop="body articleBody">
             <p>
              For 21 years, the Samsung name as served as the company logo, occasionally superimposed over a wobbly blue oval. It's the kind of logo that's fine on washing machines and televisions, but incredibly boring on something personal, like a smartphone. Never is this more apparent than when compared to the branding of Samsung's arch-enemy in Cupertino, which is simply the silhouette of an apple.
             </p>
             <p>
              Regardless, Samsung sells
              <a href="http://www.independent.co.uk/life-style/gadgets-and-tech/samsung-ranked-as-worlds-largest-tech-companyeven-bigger-than-apple-8697842.html" target="_blank">
               more smartphones and tablets
              </a>
              than even Apple does. Doesn't it deserve branding just as good?
             </p>
             <p>
              Inspired by Andrew Kim's gorgeous
              <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/1670245/a-students-smart-microsoft-rebranding-is-better-than-the-real-thing" target="_self">
               Microsoft rebranding concept
              </a>
              , Dutch visual designer
              <a class="profile" href="//www.fastcompany.com/person/aziz-firat">
               Aziz Firat
              </a>
              <a href="http://www.azizfirat.com/blog/2014/7/11/rebranding-samsung" target="_blank">
               decided to unite the many elements
              </a>
              of Samsung's discordant branding "strategy" behind a clean new icon.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//d.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2014/08/3034123-inline-i-3-how-a-single-icon-could-make-a-world-of-difference-for-samsung.png"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              The icon takes the form of a stylized "S" folded like a ribbon, which not only gives Samsung a powerful and recognizable symbol for the back of its devices, but does double-duty as a home button design for smartphones, tablets, and other mobile products.
             </p>
             <p>
              Firat's concept isn't entirely without fault. He says the icon is meant to represent simplicity, quality, and unitywhich sounds wonderfulbut he's really branding a bizarro world version of Samsung. There's
              <a href="http://www.wired.com/2014/04/samsung-galaxy-s5/" target="_blank">
               currently nothing about Samsung products
              </a>
              that represent the combination of simplicity, quality, and unity.
             </p>
             <aside class="pullquote">
              <q>
               It's about time Samsung focuses on how it presents itself outside of those ridiculous, self-parodying mission statements.
              </q>
             </aside>
             <p>
              Still, the icon does offer consistency to the branding itself. Samsung uses different logos in different typefaces almost willy-nilly across their product line: The
              <a href="http://www.samsung.com/us/galaxy-s-5-the-next-big-thing-is-here/?cid=ppc-" target="_blank">
               marketing materials
              </a>
              for Samsung's flagship phone, the Galaxy S5, use two different "Samsung" logos and three different fonts in just the product splash shot alone. This not only makes a Samsung product less instantly recognizable, it makes Samsung look like it doesn't care about detail.
             </p>
             <p>
              And while rebranding alone can't make Samsung's actual products embody the simplicity, quality, and unity that Firat's new icon would have them aspire to, a revamped visual identity can signal to the world that the company is ready to take such concepts seriouslywhich might be why the company
              <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/05/08/us-samsung-elec-design-idUSBREA4702I20140508" target="_blank">
               is restructuring its design team
              </a>
              . It's about time Samsung focuses on how it presents itself outside of those
              <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/3029287/samsungs-design-statement-proves-it-is-a-company-of-robots-bent-on-enslaving-man" target="_self">
               ridiculous, self-parodying mission statements
              </a>
              .
             </p>
            </div>

               [Images by Aziz Firat]
              </p>
