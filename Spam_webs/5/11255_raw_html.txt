    <h1>
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
    </h1>
    <img border="" src="../../images/species/a/adasaurus-size.jpg"/>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Name:
     </b>
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     (Adas lizard).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Phonetic:
     </b>
     Ay-dah-sore-us.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Named By:
     </b>
     Rinchen Barsbold - 1983.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Classification:
     </b>
     Chordata, Reptilia, Dinosauria,
Theropoda, Dromaeosauridae, Dromaeosaurinae.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Species:
     </b>
     <i>
      A. mongoliensis
     </i>
     
(type).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Diet:
     </b>
     Carnivore.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Size:
     </b>
     About 1.8 meters long.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Known locations:
     </b>
     Mongolia, Bayankhongor Province
- Nemegt Formation.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Time period:
     </b>
     Late (late Campanian to early
Maastrichtian) of the Cretaceous.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Fossil representation:
     </b>
     Initially based upon the
description of a partial skull and incomplete post cranial skeleton of
single dinosaur, a second partial skeleton has now been described for
the genus.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     At
just under two meters long,
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     is roughly
on the smaller
average end of the size range for
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/dromaeosaurs.html">
      dromaeosaurs
     </a>
     .
Close study of
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     remains have identified it as a
dromaeosaurine
dromaeosaurid, meaning that it is more closely related to
dromaeosaurs such as
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/y/yurgovuchia.html">
      <i>
       Yurgovuchia
      </i>
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/d/dromaeosaurus.html">
      <i>
       Dromaeosaurus
      </i>
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/a/achillobator.html">
      <i>
       Achillobator
      </i>
     </a>
     and
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/u/utahraptor.html">
      <i>
       Utahraptor
      </i>
     </a>
     .
Like with its relatives,
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     was a hunter of
other animals, quite probably other dinosaurs. The primary killing
weapons for
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     would have been the two
enlarged sickle claws on
its feet, which may have been used to jab at prey. Although not
confirmed, because
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     is a dromaeosaurid
dinosaur, it is
expected to have had at least some feathers present on the body in
life.
     <br/>
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     was named after an evil spirit called Ada which appears in Mongolian
mythology. The species name mongoliensis simply means From
Mongolia.
     <i>
      Adasaurus
     </i>
     is one of many dinosaurs
recovered from the
world famous Nemegt Formation, which also holds other notable
dinosaur genera such as
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/a/alioramus.html">
      <i>
       Alioramus
      </i>
     </a>
     ,

     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/g/gallimimus.html">
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
     </a>
     ,

     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/a/avimimus.html">
      <i>
       Avimimus
      </i>
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/t/therizinosaurus.html">
      <i>
       Therizinosaurus
      </i>
     </a>
     and
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/t/tarbosaurus.html">
      <i>
       Tarbosaurus
      </i>
     </a>
     amongst an ever increasing number of
discovered dinosaurs.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Further reading
     </b>
     <br/>
     Carnivorous dinosaurs from the Cretaceous of Mongolia, Rinchen
Barsbold - 1983.
    </p>
