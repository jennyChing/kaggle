     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Bushido Blade
       </b>
      </i>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       <span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">
        ,
       </span>
       <i>
        Bushid Burdo
       </i>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">
         <span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">
          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </span>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">
       3D
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fighting_game" title="Fighting game">
       fighting
      </a>
      video game developed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_Weight_(company)" title="Light Weight (company)">
       Light Weight
      </a>
      and published by
      <a href="/wiki/Square_(company)" title="Square (company)">
       Square
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">
       Sony
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_(console)" title="PlayStation (console)">
       PlayStation
      </a>
      . The game features one-on-one armed combat. Its name refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japanese
      </a>
      warrior code of honor,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushid%C5%8D" title="Bushid">
       Bushid
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon its release, the realistic fighting engine in
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      was seen as innovative, particularly the game's unique Body Damage System.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A direct sequel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bushido_Blade_2" title="Bushido Blade 2">
        Bushido Blade 2
       </a>
      </i>
      , was released on the PlayStation a year later. Another game with a related title and gameplay,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kengo" title="Kengo">
        Kengo: Master of Bushido
       </a>
      </i>
      , was also developed by Light Weight for the
      <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_2" title="PlayStation 2">
       PlayStation 2
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">
       Gameplay
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bushido_Blade_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The bulk of the gameplay in
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      revolves around one-on-one
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third-person_narrative" title="Third-person narrative">
       third-person
      </a>
      battles between two opponents. Unlike most fighting games, however, no time limit or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_(game_mechanism)" title="Health (game mechanism)">
       health gauge
      </a>
      is present during combat. Most hits will cause instant death, while traditional fighting games require many hits to deplete an opponent's health gauge. It is possible to wound an opponent without killing them. With the game's "Body Damage System," opponents are able to physically disable each other in increments with hits from an equipped weapon, slowing their attacking and running speed, or crippling their legs forcing them to crawl.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Instructions_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Instructions-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Notably, the
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North American
      </a>
      release of
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      had one minor graphical change:
      <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">
       blood
      </a>
      was added, replacing the yellow flash that appears during a fatal blow.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The game features eight weapons to choose from in many of its modes, including
      <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">
       katana
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodachi" title="Nodachi">
       nodachi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arming_sword" title="Arming sword">
       long sword
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sabre" title="Sabre">
       saber
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Longsword" title="Longsword">
       broadsword
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Naginata" title="Naginata">
       naginata
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rapier" title="Rapier">
       rapier
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sledgehammer" title="Sledgehammer">
       sledgehammer
      </a>
      . Each weapon has a realistic weight and length, giving each one fixed power, speed, and an ability to block. A variety of attack combinations can be executed by the player using button sequences with the game's "Motion Shift System," where one swing of a weapon is followed through with another.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Instructions_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Instructions-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many of these attacks are only available in one of three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stances_(positioning)" title="Stances (positioning)">
       stances
      </a>
      , switched using the shoulder buttons or axis controls depending on controller layout: high, neutral, and low. The player also has a choice of one out of six
      <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">
       playable characters
      </a>
      . Similar to the weapons, each one has a different level of strength and speed, and a number of unique special attacks. Some characters have a subweapon that can be thrown as well. All the characters have differing levels of proficiency with the selectable weapons and have a single preferred weapon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Characters in
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      also have the ability to run, jump, and climb within the 3D environments. Because battles are not limited to small arenas, the player is encouraged to freely explore during battle. The castle compound which most of the game takes place in acts as a large hub area of interconnected smaller areas including a
      <a href="/wiki/Cherry_blossom" title="Cherry blossom">
       cherry blossom
      </a>
      grove, a moat, and a bridge labyrinth. Some areas, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">
       bamboo
      </a>
      thicket, allow some interaction.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the game's single player story mode,
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      contains a two-player versus mode and a link mode that supports the
      <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Link_Cable" title="PlayStation Link Cable">
       PlayStation Link Cable
      </a>
      . Other single player options include a practice mode and a
      <a href="/wiki/First_person_(video_games)" title="First person (video games)">
       first person
      </a>
      mode. Slash mode pits the player's katana-wielding character against a long string of 100 enemies, one after the other.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bushido_Blade_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Despite characters,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_(literature)" title="Theme (literature)">
       themes
      </a>
      and weapons similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Samurai_cinema" title="Samurai cinema">
       samurai cinema
      </a>
      set in
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Japan#Feudal_Japan_.281185-1603.29" title="History of Japan">
       Feudal Japan
      </a>
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      takes place during the modern era (this is shown, for example, when the player reaches the helicopter landing pad phase set in a large city).
     </p>
     <p>
      A fictional, 500 year old
      <a href="/wiki/Dojo" title="Dojo">
       dojo
      </a>
      known as Meikyokan lies within this region, and teaches the disciplines of the master Narukagami Shinto. A society of
      <a href="/wiki/Assassination" title="Assassination">
       assassins
      </a>
      known as Kage ("Shadow") also resides within the dojo. Once led by the honorable swordsman Utsusemi, he lost his position to Hanzaki, another skilled member of the dojo, in a fierce battle. Hanzaki gained respect as the Kage leader, until he discovered a cursed sword known as Yugiri. He began to change, disregarding the group's honor and the traditions held by its students.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One day, a Kage member escapes the confines of the dojo with its secrets. Several other members of the society, under penalty of death, are sent to dispatch the defector, only catching up to him (or her) within the ruins of the surrounding Yin and Yang Labyrinth Castle. In single player mode the players take on the role of the escaped assassin (independent of whatever character they choose), fighting their way out by killing their comrades one by one. Elements of the game story differ with each character selected.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">
       Characters
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bushido_Blade_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characters">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <b>
       Playable characters
      </b>
      :
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Utsusemi
       </b>
       is the former Meikyokan dojo master, having previously handed off his duties to Hanzaki. He turns his interest to training Tatsumi and Red Shadow until Hanzaki begins abusing his power.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Tatsumi
       </b>
       is the youngest student at Meikyokan and, although not a member of Kage, he has lived among them most of his life. His novice swordsmanship is put to the test when he attempts to escape the compound.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Red Shadow
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Hotarubi
       </b>
       ) is a skilled female assassin who joined Kage under Utsusemi's guidance. Fearing the worst for her master, she sets out to confront Hanzaki before Utsusemi can.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Mikado
       </b>
       is a former
       <a href="/wiki/Miko" title="Miko">
        shrine maiden
       </a>
       who eventually found her way to Kage. She leaves the group in hopes of returning to her former life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Kannuki
       </b>
       is an assassin whose entire village in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">
        Ryukyu Islands
       </a>
       was slaughtered by Black Lotus, a fellow member of Kage. Upon discovering it was by Hanzaki's order, Kannuki sets out to destroy them both.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Black Lotus
       </b>
       is a loyal member of Kage who strictly follows the code of Bushido. After slaughtering all the villagers in Kannuki's hometown, Black Lotus begins to question his loyalty to Hanzaki and seeks answers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_5-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guide-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Schuvaltz Katze
       </b>
       (secret character): A hitman hired by Hanzaki. Uses a gun, and is thus able to use long-range attacks, putting him at a slight advantage. However he is unable to fight at all once his legs are damaged - putting the fight to an abrupt end. He is selectable after completing Slash Mode on Hard without dying, but is only in VS mode.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <b>
       Non-playable characters
      </b>
      :
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Sanzaka
       </b>
       : Powder-masked and extremely deadly. He is described as being "in a different league as the others". Uses a weapon similar to the naginata, except it has a blade on both sides.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Hokkyoku Tsubame
       </b>
       : A high-ranking, female assassin on Hanzaki's side. She uses a sword that is used in a similar manner to the rapier, although it is slightly wider. She is one of the faster characters, and can use "spin" attacks.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Hanzaki
       </b>
       : Master of Narukagami and the "
       <a href="/wiki/Boss_(video_gaming)" title="Boss (video gaming)">
        boss
       </a>
       " of the game. Uses a sword known as 'Yugiri' which he does so the same the player would a katana, except for one move - which is for the Nodachi.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Kindachi
       </b>
       : A hidden enemy. A silent warrior in red armour who uses a sword which seems to be a more decorated version of the Nodachi. Can be fought by Red Shadow, Utsusemi, and Mikado. He appears in Mikado's ending.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Hongou Takeru
       </b>
       : A hidden enemy. A young man with a sword that has a few moves for the long sword, but is otherwise unique. He is an enemy of Tatsumi's and a student a place known as "Shainto" (this is not revealed until the sequel) He can be fought with Tatsumi and Black Lotus.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Audio">
       Audio
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bushido_Blade_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Audio">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The score for
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      was created by
      <a href="/wiki/Namco" title="Namco">
       Namco
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arika" title="Arika">
       Arika
      </a>
      composer Shinji Hosoe with contributions by Ayako Saso and Takayuki Aihara. It was released with the soundtrack for Square's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Driving_Emotion_Type-S" title="Driving Emotion Type-S">
        Driving Emotion Type-S
       </a>
      </i>
      , also composed by the trio, on a two-disc set in
      <a href="/wiki/2001_in_music" title="2001 in music">
       2001
      </a>
      . Unlike many other Square soundtracks of the era which were released by
      <a href="/wiki/DigiCube" title="DigiCube">
       DigiCube
      </a>
      , the music, copyrighted by Hosoe, was published by his own Super Sweep Records company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Music_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Music-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      disc contains 23 tracks.
     </p>
     <p>
      Much of the music utilizes the
      <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">
       flute
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">
       violin
      </a>
      , as well as a traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Japanese_music" title="Traditional Japanese music">
       Japanese instrument
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Shamisen" title="Shamisen">
       shamisen
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Music_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Music-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      also uses
      <a href="/wiki/Voice_acting" title="Voice acting">
       voice acting
      </a>
      from voice actors such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chikao_%C5%8Ctsuka" title="Chikao tsuka">
       Chikao tsuka
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Makio_Inoue" title="Makio Inoue">
       Makio Inoue
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hidekatsu_Shibata" title="Hidekatsu Shibata">
       Hidekatsu Shibata
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      was presold in
      <a href="/wiki/Convenience_store" title="Convenience store">
       convenience stores
      </a>
      in Japan prior to its release, similar to Square's decision to presell its hit
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_VII" title="Final Fantasy VII">
        Final Fantasy VII
       </a>
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Lawson_(store)" title="Lawson (store)">
       Lawson
      </a>
      stores.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      was the 25th best selling game of 1997 in Japan, selling nearly 388,000 copies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In November 2000,
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      was voted by the readers of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Famitsu" title="Famitsu">
        Weekly Famitsu
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine as number 85 in its top 100 PlayStation games of all time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The game was later reprinted, along with a handful of other Square Enix titles, under the developer's "Legendary Hits" label.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The game was also added to the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_PSone_Classics_(Japan)" title="List of PSone Classics (Japan)">
       PSone Classics
      </a>
      roster on the Japanese
      <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Store" title="PlayStation Store">
       PlayStation Store
      </a>
      in 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classics_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Classics-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      was critically well received in North America.
      <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">
       IGN
      </a>
      rated the game an 8.7 out of 10. The review stated "I can't recommend
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      enough. Simply amazing." The gameplay mechanics were considered foreign by some, and required some practice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ign-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">
       GameSpot
      </a>
      similarly rated the game well with an 8.9 out of 10 rating, stating "
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      is a bold undertaking, but a remarkably successful one."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gs_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gs-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006, the game was ranked number 190 on
      <a href="/wiki/1UP.com" title="1UP.com">
       1UP.com
      </a>
      's list of The Greatest 200 Videogames of Their Time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2010,
      <a href="/wiki/GamesRadar" title="GamesRadar">
       GamesRadar
      </a>
      included
      <i>
       Bushido Blade
      </i>
      on the list of the seven "'90s games that need HD
      <a href="/wiki/Video_game_remake" title="Video game remake">
       remakes
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
