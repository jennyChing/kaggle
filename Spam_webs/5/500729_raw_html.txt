            <p>
             It's long been rumored that a cheaper, smaller version of the iPhone is on the horizon, but now both
             <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704657104576142262842435544.html">
              <em>
               The Wall Street Journal
              </em>
             </a>
             and
             <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-02-10/apple-said-to-work-on-cheaper-more-versatile-iphone-models.html">
              <em>
               Bloomberg
              </em>
             </a>
             are offering up fresh intelligence on the so-called iPhone Nano, which could be half the size of the iPhone 4 and far more affordable. "And some people thought this may be a relatively dull year for Apple rumors"
             <a href="http://techcrunch.com/2011/02/10/iphone-nano/">
              says MG Siegler at
              <em>
               TechCrunch
              </em>
             </a>
             . Here, a brief guide to the rumored phone:
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Just how small is it?
             </strong>
             <br/>
             Reports vary. "People familiar with the matter" who are
             <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704657104576142262842435544.html">
              cited by
              <em>
               The Wall Street Journal
              </em>
             </a>
             say the cheaper iPhones would be about half the size of the iPhone 4, with an "edge-to-edge'" touchscreen, voice-based navigation and a virtual keyboard. Meanwhile,
             <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-02-10/apple-said-to-work-on-cheaper-more-versatile-iphone-models.html">
              <em>
               Bloomberg
              </em>
              quotes a source
             </a>
             who claims to have seen a prototype of the mystery phone and says it is 33 percent smaller than the iPhone 4 and has no "home button." The smaller size is workable because the new phone would rely on cloud computing to store media,
             <a href="http://www.cultofmac.com/more-detail-on-apples-iphone-nano-exclusive/81768">
              according to
              <em>
               Cult of Mac
              </em>
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              How much would it cost?
             </strong>
             <br/>
             The new phone would be sold to carriers for about half the price of the standard iPhone,
             <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704657104576142262842435544.html">
              according to
              <em>
               The Wall Street Journal
              </em>
             </a>
             . Mobile companies currently pay an average of $625 for iPhones, and sell them to consumers starting at $199, making their money from a minimum two-year service contract.
             <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-02-10/apple-said-to-work-on-cheaper-more-versatile-iphone-models.html">
              According to
              <em>
               Bloomberg
              </em>
             </a>
             , Apple is considering selling the new phone to consumers for around $200  but without the contract requirement.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              How reliable are these rumors?
             </strong>
             <br/>
             Though a cheaper, more modest iPhone is a possibility,
             <a href="http://www.cultofmac.com/iphone-nano-rumors-may-be-nothing-new-but-this-time-theyre-probably-true-opinion/81711">
              says Graham Bower at
              <em>
               Cult of Mac
              </em>
             </a>
             <em>
              ,
             </em>
             it "seems unlikely" that the new phone would be smaller, given the likely difficulty of use and production costs. If anything, "a cheaper iPhone [would] be larger than the iPhone 4."
             <a href="http://www.pcworld.com/article/219501/iphone_nano_rumors_return.html">
              Jared Newman at
              <em>
               PC World
              </em>
             </a>
             has similar "doubts": I don't see how Apple could reduce the cost so drastically, but if
             <em>
              Bloomberg
             </em>
             's report is accurate, "it'll be huge."
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              What's Apple's motivation?
             </strong>
             <br/>
             If Apple could make the phone affordable without locking consumers into a contract, it "would be potentially huge,"
             <a href="http://techcrunch.com/2011/02/10/iphone-nano/">
              agrees Siegler at
              <em>
               TechCrunch
              </em>
             </a>
             . Apple and consumers would no longer be "slaves" to cell phone carriers. And, it could help Apple greatly
             <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-02-10/apple-said-to-work-on-cheaper-more-versatile-iphone-models.html">
              expand its market share
             </a>
             , both domestically and in China and India.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              When might it come out?
             </strong>
             <br/>
             <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-02-10/apple-said-to-work-on-cheaper-more-versatile-iphone-models.html">
              According to a
              <em>
               Bloomberg
              </em>
              source
             </a>
             , Apple tentatively plans to unveil the new phone in the middle of this year... but, at this point, anything could happen. Or not.
            </p>
