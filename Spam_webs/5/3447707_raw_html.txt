     <p>
      <b>
       Inequity aversion
      </b>
      (IA) is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_preference" title="Social preference">
       preference
      </a>
      for fairness and resistance to incidental inequalities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fehr_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fehr-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The social sciences that study inequity aversion include
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
       psychology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropology
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">
       ethology
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_studies">
       Human studies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inequity_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Human studies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Inequity aversion research on humans mostly occurs in the discipline of
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      though it is also studied in
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociology
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Research on inequity aversion began in 1978 when studies suggested that humans are sensitive to inequities in favor of as well as those against them, and that some people attempt overcompensation when they feel "guilty" or unhappy to have received an undeserved reward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A more recent definition of inequity aversion (resistance to inequitable outcomes) was developed in 1999 by Fehr and Schmidt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fehr_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fehr-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They postulated that people make decisions so as to minimize inequity in outcomes. Specifically, consider a setting with individuals {1,2,...,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      } who receive pecuniary outcomes
      <i>
       x
       <sub>
        i
       </sub>
      </i>
      . Then the utility to person
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      would be given by
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="U_i(\{x_i, x_j\}) = x_i - \frac{\alpha_i}{n-1} \times \sum{\max(x_j - x_i,0)} - \frac{\beta_i}{n-1} \times \sum{\max(x_i - x_j,0)}," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/8/4/9840847a2934a1a4705cf2e527e25d6a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where  parametrizes the distaste of person
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      for disadvantageous inequality in the first nonstandard term, and  parametrizes the distaste of person
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      for advantageous inequality in the final term.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Punishing_unjust_success_and_game_theory">
       Punishing unjust success and game theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inequity_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Punishing unjust success and game theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Fehr and Schmidt showed that disadvantageous inequity aversion manifests itself in humans as the "willingness to sacrifice potential gain to block another individual from receiving a superior reward". They argue that this apparently self-destructive response is essential in creating an environment in which bilateral bargaining can thrive. Without inequity aversion's rejection of injustice, stable cooperation would be harder to maintain (for instance, there would be more opportunities for successful
      <a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" title="Free rider problem">
       free riders
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/James_H._Fowler" title="James H. Fowler">
       James H. Fowler
      </a>
      and his colleagues also argue that inequity aversion is essential for cooperation in multilateral settings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In particular, they show that subjects in
      <i>
       random income
      </i>
      games (closely related to public goods games) are willing to spend their own money to reduce the income of wealthier group members and increase the income of poorer group members even when there is no cooperation at stake.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus, individuals who free ride on the contributions of fellow group members are likely to be punished because they earn more, creating a decentralized incentive for the maintenance of cooperation.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_economics">
       Experimental economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inequity_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Experimental economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Inequity aversion is broadly consistent with observations of behavior in three standard
      <a href="/wiki/Experimental_economics" title="Experimental economics">
       economics experiments
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Dictator_game" title="Dictator game">
        Dictator game
       </a>
        The subject chooses how a reward should be split between himself and another subject. If the dictator acted self-interestedly, the split would consist of 0 for the partner and the full amount for the dictator. While the most common choice is indeed to keep everything, many dictators choose to give, with the second most common choice being the 50:50 split.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Ultimatum_game" title="Ultimatum game">
        Ultimatum game
       </a>
        The dictator game is played, but the recipient is allowed to veto the entire deal, so that both subjects receive nothing. The partner typically vetos the deal when low offers are made. People consistently prefer getting nothing to receiving a small share of the pie. Rejecting the offer is in effect paying to punish the dictator (called the
       <i>
        proposer
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trust_game" title="Trust game">
        Trust game
       </a>
        The same result as found in the dictator game shows up when the dictator's initial endowment is provided by his partner, even though this requires the first player to trust that something will be returned (reciprocity). This experiment often yields a 50:50 split of the endowment, and has been used as evidence of the inequity aversion model.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      In 2005, John List modified these experiments slightly to determine if something in the construction of the experiments was prompting specific behaviors. When given a choice to steal money from the other player, even a single dollar, the observed altruism all but disappeared. In another experiment, the two players were given a sum of money and the choice to give or take any amount from the other player. In this experiment, only 10% of the participants gave the other person any money at all, and fully 40% of the players opted to take all of the other player's money.
     </p>
     <p>
      The last such experiment was identical to the former, where 40% were turned into a gang of robbers, with one catch: the two players were forced to earn the money by stuffing envelopes. In this last experiment, more than two thirds of the players neither took nor gave a cent, while just over 20% still took some of the other player's money.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2011, Ert Erev and Roth
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ran a model prediction competition on two datasets, each of which included 120 two-player games. In each game player 1 decides whether to "opt out" and determine the payoffs for both players, or to "opt in" and let player 2 decide about the payoff allocation by choosing between actions "left" or "right." The payoffs were randomly selected, so the dataset included games like the Ultimatum, Dictator, and Trust, as well as other games. The results suggested that inequity aversion could be described as one of many strategies that people might use in such games.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other research in experimental economics addresses risk aversion in decision making
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the comparison of inequality measures to subjective judgments on perceived inequalities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmielCowell_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AmielCowell-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_of_companies">
       Studies of companies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inequity_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Studies of companies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Surveys of employee opinions within firms have shown modern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_economist" title="Labor economist">
       labor economists
      </a>
      that inequity aversion is very important to them. Employees compare not only relative
      <a href="/wiki/Salary" title="Salary">
       salaries
      </a>
      but also relative performance against that of co-workers. Where these comparisons lead to guilt or envy, inequity aversion may lower employee morale. According to Bewley (1999), the main reason that
      <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">
       managers
      </a>
      create formal pay structures is so that the inter-employee comparison is seen to be "fair", which they considered "key" for morale and job performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is natural to think of inequity aversion leading to greater
      <a href="/wiki/Solidarity" title="Solidarity">
       solidarity
      </a>
      within the labor pool, to the benefit of the average employee. However, a 2008 paper by Pedro Rey-Biel shows that this assumption can be subverted, and that an employer can use inequity aversion to get higher performance for less pay than would be possible otherwise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is done by moving away from formal pay structures and using off-
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">
       equilibrium
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Performance-related_pay" title="Performance-related pay">
       bonus payments
      </a>
      as incentives for extra performance. He shows that the optimal
      <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">
       contract
      </a>
      for inequity aversion employees is less generous at the optimal production level than contracts for "standard agents" (who don't have inequity aversion) in an otherwise identical two-employee model.
     </p>

     <p>
      In 2005 Avner Shaked distributed a "pamphlet" entitled "The
      <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">
       Rhetoric
      </a>
      of Inequity Aversion" that attacked the inequity aversion papers of Fehr &amp; Schmidt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An alternative
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmielCowell_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AmielCowell-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to the concept of a general inequity aversion is the assumption, that the
      <i>
       degree
      </i>
      and the structure of inequality could lead either to acceptance or to aversion of inequality.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_studies">
       Non-human studies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inequity_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-human studies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An experiment on
      <a href="/wiki/Capuchin_monkey" title="Capuchin monkey">
       capuchin monkeys
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Brosnan" title="Sarah Brosnan">
       Brosnan, S
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Frans_de_Waal" title="Frans de Waal">
       de Waal, F
      </a>
      ) showed that the subjects would prefer receiving nothing to receiving a reward awarded inequitably in favor of a second monkey, and appeared to target their anger at the researchers responsible for the inequitable distribution of food.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">
       Anthropologists
      </a>
      suggest that this research indicates a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological" title="Biological">
       biological
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">
       evolutionary
      </a>
      sense of social "fair play" in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">
       primates
      </a>
      , though others believe that this is learned behavior or explained by other mechanisms.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      There is also evidence for inequity aversion in
      <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">
       chimpanzees
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (though see a recent study questioning this interpretation
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ). The latest study shows that chimpanzees play the Ultimatum Game in the same way as children, preferring equitable outcomes. The authors claim that we now are near the point of no difference between humans and apes with regard to a sense of fairness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Recent studies suggest that animals in the
      <a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">
       canidae
      </a>
      family also recognize a basic level of fairness, stemming from living in cooperative societies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_cognition" title="Animal cognition">
       Animal cognition
      </a>
      studies in other biological
      <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">
       orders
      </a>
      have not found similar importance on
      <i>
       relative
      </i>
      "equity" and "justice" as opposed to
      <i>
       absolute
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">
       utility
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_inequity_aversion">
       Social inequity aversion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inequity_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social inequity aversion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Fehr and Schmidt's model may partially explain the widespread opposition to
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">
       economic inequality
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
       democracies
      </a>
      , but a distinction should be drawn between inequity aversion's "guilt" and
      <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">
       egalitarianism
      </a>
      's "
      <a href="/wiki/Compassion" title="Compassion">
       compassion
      </a>
      ", which does not necessarily imply
      <i>
       injustice
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Inequity aversion should not be confused with the arguments against the
      <i>
       consequences
      </i>
      of inequality. For example, the pro-
      <a href="/wiki/Publicly_funded_health_care" title="Publicly funded health care">
       publicly funded health care
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Slogan" title="Slogan">
       slogan
      </a>
      "Hospitals for the poor become poor hospitals" directly objects to a predicted decline in medical care, not the health-care
      <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">
       apartheid
      </a>
      that is supposed to cause it. The argument that average medical outcomes improve with reduction in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healthcare" title="Healthcare">
       healthcare
      </a>
      inequality (at the same total spending) is separate from the case for public healthcare on the grounds of inequity aversion.
     </p>
