               Mint fools the receptors in your mouth into thinking they are cold.

           <div class="by-line-top hide-desktop">
           </div>
           <p>
            You're chewing mint gum or sucking on a peppermint candy and draw in a breath of air and no matter how warm it is, the air feels icy cold. Why does this happen? It's a trick mint and the chemical called menthol play on your brain that convinces your taste receptors they are exposed to cold.
           </p>
           <p>
            Sensory neurons in your skin and mouth contain a
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/chemistryglossary/a/proteindef.htm">
             protein
            </a>
            called
            <em>
             transient receptor potential cation channel subfamily M member 8
            </em>
            (TRPM8).
           </p>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
           </div>
           <div class="radlinks ads-half" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">
           </div>
           <div data-defer="onPageLoad" id="videoPlaceholderLarge">
           </div>
           <p class="cb-split">
            TRPM8 is an ion channel, meaning it regulates the flow of ions between cellular membranes much as an aquatic channel regulates transit between bodies of water. Cold temperatures permit Na
            <sup>
             +
            </sup>
            and Ca
            <sup>
             2+
            </sup>
            ions to cross the channel and enter the nerve cell, changing its electric potential and causing the neuron to fire a signal to your brain which it interprets as a sensation of cold.
           </p>
           <p>
            Mint contains an organic compound called
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/factsstructures/ig/Chemical-Structures---M/Menthol.htm">
             menthol
            </a>
            that binds to TRPM8, making the ion channelopen as if the receptor was exposed to cold and signaling this information to your brain. In fact, menthol sensitizes the neurons to the effect doesn't wear off as soon as your spit out mint toothpaste or stop chewing a breath mint. If you take a sip of cold water right afterward, the cool temperature will feel especially cold.
           </p>
           <p>
            Other chemicals affect temperature receptors, too. For example,
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/factsstructures/ig/Chemical-Structures---C/Capsaicin.htm">
             capsaicin
            </a>
            in hot peppers causes a sensation of heat. What do you think would happen if you combined the heat of peppers with the cold of mint?
           </p>
