     <p>
      <b>
       Augusta National Golf Club
      </b>
      , located in
      <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">
       Augusta, Georgia
      </a>
      , is one of the most famous
      <a href="/wiki/Country_club" title="Country club">
       golf clubs
      </a>
      in the world. Founded by
      <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Jones_(golfer)" title="Bobby Jones (golfer)">
       Bobby Jones
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Clifford_Roberts" title="Clifford Roberts">
       Clifford Roberts
      </a>
      on the site of the former Fruitland (later Fruitlands) Nursery, the course was designed by Jones and
      <a href="/wiki/Alister_MacKenzie" title="Alister MacKenzie">
       Alister MacKenzie
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-itcpgat11_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-itcpgat11-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and opened for play in January 1933. Since 1934, it has played host to the annual
      <a href="/wiki/Masters_Tournament" title="Masters Tournament">
       Masters Tournament
      </a>
      , one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_major_golf_championships" title="Men's major golf championships">
       four major championships
      </a>
      in professional
      <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">
       golf
      </a>
      , and the only major played each year at the same course. It was the number one ranked course in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Golf_Digest" title="Golf Digest">
        Golf Digest
       </a>
      </i>
      's 2009 list of
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       America's
      </a>
      100 greatest courses
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is currently the number ten ranked course on
      <i>
       Golfweek Magazine
      </i>
      's 2011 list of best classic courses in the United States, in terms of course architecture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The golf club's exclusive membership policies have drawn criticism, particularly because there were no African-American members admitted until 1990,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatoday-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as a former policy requiring all
      <a href="/wiki/Caddy" title="Caddy">
       caddies
      </a>
      to be black, which was omitted from the club's bylaws in 1959.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caddies_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-caddies-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The club began granting membership to women in August 2012. Prior to the acceptance of female members, Augusta National defended its position by citing that in 2011, more than 15% of the non-tournament rounds were played by female players who were member guests or spouses of active members.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-woman_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-woman-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In August 2012, it admitted its first two female members,
      <a href="/wiki/Condoleezza_Rice" title="Condoleezza Rice">
       Condoleezza Rice
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Darla_Moore" title="Darla Moore">
       Darla Moore
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatwomen_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatwomen-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The golf club has defended the membership policies, stressing that it is a private organization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Unlike most other private or public golf courses in the United States, Augusta National has never been
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golf_handicap#Course_rating_and_slope" title="Golf handicap">
       rated
      </a>
      . During the
      <a href="/wiki/1990_Masters_Tournament" title="1990 Masters Tournament">
       1990 Masters Tournament
      </a>
      , a team of
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Golf_Association" title="United States Golf Association">
       USGA
      </a>
      raters, organized by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Golf_Digest" title="Golf Digest">
        Golf Digest
       </a>
      </i>
      , evaluated the course and gave it an unofficial rating of 76.2. It was re-evaluated in 2009 and given an unofficial rating of 78.1.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rating_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-rating-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The golf course architecture website GolfClubAtlas.com has said, "Augusta National has gone through more changes since its inception than any of the world's twenty or so greatest courses. To call it a MacKenzie course is false advertising as his features are essentially long gone and his routing is all that is left." The authors of the site also add that MacKenzie and Jones were heavily influenced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Course_at_St_Andrews" title="Old Course at St Andrews">
       Old Course at St Andrews
      </a>
      , and intended that the ground game be central to the course. However, almost from Augusta's opening, Roberts sought to make changes to minimize the ground game, and effectively got free rein to do so because MacKenzie died shortly after the course's opening and Jones went into inactivity due to World War II and then
      <a href="/wiki/Syringomyelia" title="Syringomyelia">
       a crippling illness
      </a>
      . The authors add, "With the ground game gone, the course was especially vulnerable to changes in technology, and this brought on a slew of changes from at least 15 different 'architects'."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Golf Course Histories has an aerial comparison of the architectural changes for Augusta National Golf Club for the year 1938 versus 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Amongst the slew of changes to the course were several made by architect
      <a href="/wiki/Perry_Maxwell" title="Perry Maxwell">
       Perry Maxwell
      </a>
      in 1937, including an important alteration involving the current 10th hole. When Augusta National originally opened for play in January 1933, the opening hole (now the 10th) was a relatively benign par 4 that played just in excess of 400 yards. From an elevated tee, the hole required little more than a short iron or wedge for the approach. Enter Perry Maxwell. He moved the green in 1937 to its present location  on top of the hill, about 50 yards back from the old site  and transformed it into the toughest hole in Masters Tournament history.
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Crenshaw" title="Ben Crenshaw">
       Ben Crenshaw
      </a>
      referred to Maxwell's work on the 10th hole as "one of the great strokes in golf architecture".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Augusta_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Augusta-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amen_Corner">
       Amen Corner
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Augusta_National_Golf_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amen Corner">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The second shot at the 11th, all of the 12th, and the first two shots at the 13th hole at Augusta are nicknamed "Amen Corner". This term was first used in print by author
      <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Warren_Wind" title="Herbert Warren Wind">
       Herbert Warren Wind
      </a>
      in his April 21, 1958,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">
        Sports Illustrated
       </a>
      </i>
      article about the Masters that year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siap58_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-siap58-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a
      <i>
       Golf Digest
      </i>
      article in April 1984, 26 years later, Wind told about its origin. He said he wanted a catchy phrase like baseball's "
      <a href="/wiki/Third_baseman" title="Third baseman">
       hot-corner
      </a>
      " or football's "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffin_corner_punt" title="Coffin corner punt">
       coffin-corner
      </a>
      " to explain where some of the most exciting golf had taken place (the Palmer-Venturi rules issue at twelve in particular). Thus "Amen Corner" was born. He said it came from the title of a jazz record he had heard in the mid-1930s by a group led by
      <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">
       Chicago
      </a>
      's Mezz Mezzrow,
      <i>
       Shouting in that Amen Corner
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a
      <i>
       Golf Digest
      </i>
      article in April 2008, writer Bill Fields added some new updated information about the origin of the name. He wrote that Richard Moore, a golf and jazz historian from
      <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">
       South Carolina
      </a>
      , tried to purchase a copy of the old Mezzrow 78 RPM disc for an "Amen Corner" exhibit he was putting together for his Golf Museum at Ahmic Lake, Ontario. After extensive research, Moore found that the record never existed. As Moore put it, Wind, himself a jazz buff, must have "unfortunately bogeyed his mind, 26 years later". While at Yale, he was no doubt familiar with, and meant all along, the popular version of the song (with the correct title, "Shoutin' in that Amen Corner" written by Andy Razaf), which was recorded by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsey_Brothers_Orchestra" title="Dorsey Brothers Orchestra">
       Dorsey Brothers Orchestra
      </a>
      , vocal by Mildred Bailey (Brunswick label No. 6655) in 1935. Moore told Fields that, being a great admirer of Wind's work over the years, he was reluctant, for months, to come forth with his discovery that contradicted Wind's memory. Moore's discovery was first reported in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Golf_World" title="Golf World">
        Golf World
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine in 2007, before Fields' longer article in
      <i>
       Golf Digest
      </i>
      in 2008.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1958
      <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Palmer" title="Arnold Palmer">
       Arnold Palmer
      </a>
      outlasted
      <a href="/wiki/Ken_Venturi" title="Ken Venturi">
       Ken Venturi
      </a>
      to win the tournament with heroic escapes at Amen Corner. Amen Corner also played host to Masters moments such as
      <a href="/wiki/Byron_Nelson" title="Byron Nelson">
       Byron Nelson
      </a>
      's birdie-eagle at 12 and 13 in 1937, and
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Snead" title="Sam Snead">
       Sam Snead
      </a>
      's water save at 12 in 1949 that sparked him to victory.
     </p>

      </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rae.27s_Creek">
       Rae's Creek
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Augusta_National_Golf_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rae's Creek">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Rae's Creek cuts across the southeastern corner of the Augusta National property. It flows along the back of the 11th green, in front of the 12th green, and ahead of the 13th tee. This is the lowest point in elevation of the course. The Hogan and Nelson Bridges cross the creek after the 12th and 13th tee boxes, respectively. The
      <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">
       creek
      </a>
      was named after former property owner John Rae, who died in 1789.

      Rising from the approximately 30 by 40-foot (9.1 by 12.2 m) (111 m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) room is the clubhouse's 11-foot (3.4m) square
      <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">
       cupola
      </a>
      . The cupola features windows on all sides. The Crow's Nest consists of one room with partitions and dividers that create three cubicles with one bed each, and one cubicle with two beds. There is also a full bathroom with an additional sink. The sitting area has a game table, sofa, and chairs, telephone and television. Placed throughout the Crow's Nest are books on golf, and lining the walls are photos and sketches depicting past Masters and other golf scenes.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Eisenhower Cabin
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      One of ten cabins on the Augusta National property, it was built by the club's membership for member Dwight D. Eisenhower after his election to the presidency. The cabin was built according to
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">
       Secret Service
      </a>
      security guidelines, and is adorned by an eagle located above the front porch.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Founders Circle
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Founders Circle is a memorial located in front of the course's clubhouse, at the end of Magnolia Lane. Plaques at Founders Circle honor Bobby Jones and Clifford Roberts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Hogan Bridge
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      There is a bridge over Rae's Creek that connects the fairway of hole 12 to its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Putting_green" title="Putting green">
       green
      </a>
      . It is constructed of stone and covered with
      <a href="/wiki/Artificial_turf" title="Artificial turf">
       artificial turf
      </a>
      . The bridge was dedicated to
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Hogan" title="Ben Hogan">
       Ben Hogan
      </a>
      in 1958 to commemorate his 72-hole score of 274 strokes five years earlier, the course record at the time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amen_corner_25-0">

     <dl>
      <dt>
       Magnolia Lane
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The main
      <a href="/wiki/Driveway" title="Driveway">
       driveway
      </a>
      leading from Washington Road to the course's clubhouse is called Magnolia Lane. The lane is flanked on either side by 60
      <a href="/wiki/Magnolia" title="Magnolia">
       magnolia
      </a>
      trees, each grown from seeds planted by the
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Berckmans" title="Louis Berckmans">
       Berckmans
      </a>
      family in the 1850s. Magnolia Lane is 330 yards (300m) long and was paved in 1947. There were formerly 61 trees along the road, but a severe thunderstorm on April 4, 2011, the night before practice day, felled one of the 61 magnolia trees.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">

        </span>
        26

       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <dt>
       Nelson Bridge
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Nelson Bridge is a stonework bridge over Rae's Creek that connects the
      <a href="/wiki/Teeing_ground" title="Teeing ground">
       teeing ground
      </a>
      of hole 13 to its fairway. In 1958, it was dedicated to Byron Nelson to honor his performance in the
      <a href="/wiki/1937_Masters_Tournament" title="1937 Masters Tournament">
       1937 Masters
      </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amen_corner_25-1">

     <dl>
      <dt>
       Par 3 Fountain
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The Par 3 Fountain is next to the No. 1 tee on the Par 3 course. The fountain has a list of Par 3 contest winners, starting with Sam Snead's win in 1960.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-augusta.com_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-augusta.com-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Record Fountain
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The Record Fountain was built to commemorate the 25th anniversary of the Masters Tournament. Located left of the No. 17 tee, it displays course records and Masters Tournament champions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-augusta.com_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-augusta.com-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Sarazen Bridge
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Sarazen Bridge is a
      <a href="/wiki/Footbridge" title="Footbridge">
       footbridge
      </a>
      that crosses the pond on hole 15 that separates the fairway from the green. Made of stone, it was named for
      <a href="/wiki/Gene_Sarazen" title="Gene Sarazen">
       Gene Sarazen
      </a>
      for a memorable
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albatross_(golf)#Albatross" title="Albatross (golf)">
       double eagle
      </a>
      "Shot heard 'round the World" in the
      <a href="/wiki/1935_Masters_Tournament" title="1935 Masters Tournament">
       1935 Masters Tournament
      </a>
      that propelled him to victory. Players must cross the Sarazen Bridge to get onto the 15th green. The bridge itself is a hazard of sorts due to its close proximity to the left side of the 15th fairway. Errant shots have been known to carom off its stone surface for a variety of results, good and bad. Due to a sloping embankment in front of the 15th green, balls will often roll off the green and into the water.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">
       Membership
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Augusta_National_Golf_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Membership">

     <p>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">
        USA Today
       </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usat2004list_28-0">

         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>

        </span>

       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      Augusta invited and accepted its first African-American member in 1990 following a controversy at
      <a href="/wiki/Shoal_Creek_Golf_and_Country_Club" title="Shoal Creek Golf and Country Club">
       Shoal Creek Golf and Country Club
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatoday-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shoal Creek, an all-white golf club in Alabama, refused membership to black players and faced demands that the PGA Championship not be held there following racist comments by the club's founder.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Augusta welcomed its first female members in 2012. Chairman Billy Payne declined to discuss the club's then continued refusal to admit women in his 2012 pre-Masters press conference.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-woman_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-woman-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Augusta National subsequently extended membership to
      <a href="/wiki/Condoleezza_Rice" title="Condoleezza Rice">
       Condoleezza Rice
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Darla_Moore" title="Darla Moore">
       Darla Moore
      </a>
      on August 20, 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatwomen_8-1">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2002_membership_controversy">
       2002 membership controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Augusta_National_Golf_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2002 membership controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Augusta National and Chairman Hootie Johnson are widely known for a disagreement beginning in 2002 with
      <a href="/wiki/Martha_Burk" title="Martha Burk">
       Martha Burk
      </a>
      , then chair of the Washington-based
      <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Women%27s_Organizations" title="National Council of Women's Organizations">
       National Council of Women's Organizations
      </a>
      , over admission of female members to Augusta National.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2003_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt2003-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Burk said she found out about the club's policies in a
      <i>
       USA Today

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Green_jacket">
       Green jacket
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Augusta_National_Golf_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Green jacket">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Every member of Augusta National receives a green
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports_coat" title="Sports coat">
       sports coat
      </a>
      with the club's logo on the left breast. The idea of the green jacket originated with club co-founder Clifford Roberts. Many believe it is because he wanted patrons visiting during the tournament to be able to readily identify members. Since Sam Snead's victory in 1949 the winner of each year's Masters Tournament has received a green jacket, although he does not receive membership. The jacket is presented to the new winner by the winner of the previous tournament. If the previous champion is either unavailable or has won consecutive tournaments, then the current chairman acts as the presenter. Until 1967, the jackets were manufactured by
      <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Brothers" title="Brooks Brothers">
       Brooks Brothers
      </a>
      and since have been made by Hamilton of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">
       Cincinnati, Ohio
      </a>
      , with the imported wool produced at the Victor Forstmann plant in
      <a href="/wiki/Dublin,_Georgia" title="Dublin, Georgia">
       Dublin, Georgia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The current Masters champion is the only owner of a green jacket permitted to remove it from the grounds of Augusta National, and only for a period of one year. Before this time limit was in place, the jacket of a few long-past Masters champions had been sold, after their deaths, to collectors. Consequently the members of Augusta National have gone to great lengths to secure the remaining examples. Now, two jackets remain outside the grounds of Augusta National with the club's permission. When
      <a href="/wiki/Gary_Player" title="Gary Player">
       Gary Player
      </a>
      first won the Masters in 1961, he brought his jacket home to
      <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
       South Africa
      </a>
      . For years the board insisted that Player return the jacket but Player kept "forgetting" or coming up with humorous creative excuses why he did not return the jacket. After becoming something of a running joke, Augusta National's members allowed him to keep it, where it is on display in his personal museum. The second jacket belongs to
      <a href="/wiki/1938_Masters_Tournament" title="1938 Masters Tournament">
       1938
      </a>
      champion
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Picard" title="Henry Picard">
       Henry Picard
      </a>
      . Before the traditions surrounding one of golf's greatest awards were well established, the jacket was removed by Picard from Augusta National. It is now currently on display in the "Picard Lounge" at
      <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Golf_Club" title="Canterbury Golf Club">
       Canterbury Golf Club
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Beachwood,_Ohio" title="Beachwood, Ohio">
       Beachwood, Ohio
      </a>
      . Along with Snead, the nine previous winners were also awarded green jackets in 1949, and these became known as the "original ten" jackets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcotmgj_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tcotmgj-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Horton_Smith" title="Horton Smith">
       Horton Smith
      </a>
      's jacket, awarded for his wins in
      <a href="/wiki/1934_Masters_Tournament" title="1934 Masters Tournament">
       1934
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1936_Masters_Tournament" title="1936 Masters Tournament">
       1936
      </a>
      , sold at auction in September 2013 for over $682,000; the highest price ever paid for a piece of golf memorabilia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gjcauc_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gjcauc-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Smith died in 1963 and it had been in the possession of his brother Ren's stepsons for decades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcotmgj_47-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tcotmgj-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
