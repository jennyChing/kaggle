     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Yacht Rock
       </b>
      </i>
      was an online video series following the
      <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">
       fictionalized
      </a>
      lives and careers of American
      <a href="/wiki/Soft_rock" title="Soft rock">
       soft rock
      </a>
      stars of the late 1970s and early 1980s. The series debuted on
      <a href="/wiki/Channel_101" title="Channel 101">
       Channel 101
      </a>
      at the June 26, 2005 screening. It placed in the top five at subsequent screenings until the June 25, 2006 screening, where it placed seventh and was canceled. The show remained a popular download on Channel 101, convincing the creators to make two additional episodes independently. The 11th episode, featuring
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jason_Lee_(entertainer)" title="Jason Lee (entertainer)">
       Jason Lee
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Bacon" title="Kevin Bacon">
       Kevin Bacon
      </a>
      , debuted during a screening at the
      <a href="/wiki/Knitting_Factory" title="Knitting Factory">
       Knitting Factory
      </a>
      in New York City on December 27, 2007 and was later included with the other episodes on Channel 101.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On May 5, 2010, the 12th and final episode of
      <i>
       Yacht Rock
      </i>
      was released onto YouTube and Channel 101.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_and_inspiration">
       Creation and inspiration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yacht_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Creation and inspiration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The series was written, directed, and produced by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._D._Ryznar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. D. Ryznar (page does not exist)">
       J. D. Ryznar
      </a>
      , co-produced by David Lyons and Hunter D. Stair, and edited by Lane Farnham. The production has a "bad-on-purpose aesthetic".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpost_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wpost-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ryznar and Stair devised the series after noticing the incestuous recording careers of such bands as
      <a href="/wiki/Steely_Dan" title="Steely Dan">
       Steely Dan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Toto_(band)" title="Toto (band)">
       Toto
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/The_Doobie_Brothers" title="The Doobie Brothers">
       The Doobie Brothers
      </a>
      and the singer-songwriters
      <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Loggins" title="Kenny Loggins">
       Kenny Loggins
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_McDonald_(singer)" title="Michael McDonald (singer)">
       Michael McDonald
      </a>
      . For example, McDonald co-wrote Loggins' "
      <a href="/wiki/This_Is_It_(Kenny_Loggins_song)" title="This Is It (Kenny Loggins song)">
       This Is It
      </a>
      " and Loggins co-wrote McDonald's band The Doobie Brothers' "
      <a href="/wiki/What_a_Fool_Believes" title="What a Fool Believes">
       What a Fool Believes
      </a>
      " and McDonald also performed backing vocals for several other 'yacht rock' artists, including Steely Dan and
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Cross" title="Christopher Cross">
       Christopher Cross
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Ryznar admits to having a fascination with the music of the period. As he explained, "Getting into Steely Dan really started this for me. As did the ability to buy dollar records at
      <a href="/wiki/Amoeba_Music" title="Amoeba Music">
       Amoeba
      </a>
      and put them on tapes for my car. Kenny Loggins has made his way into all the
      <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">
       pilots
      </a>
      I've been involved with except [one]."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As Ryznar told
      <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">
       Reuters
      </a>
      contributor Andy Sullivan, "I'm making fun of the songwriting process, but the music is generally treated pretty lovingly."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_show">
       The show
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yacht_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The show">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Yacht Rock'
      </i>
      s episodes were "hosted" by "Hollywood" Steve Huey, a legitimate music critic for
      <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">
       AllMusic
      </a>
      . The term "Yacht Rock" is never used throughout the series by any characters except for by Huey during his introductions; instead, it is always referred to as "smooth music."
     </p>
     <p>
      The series depicts some realistic aspects of the music, but builds exaggerated storylines around them. For example, main protagonists Loggins and McDonald receive inspiration from a fictional
      <a href="/wiki/Impresario" title="Impresario">
       impresario
      </a>
      named Koko Goldstein, whose death in Episode 2 ultimately leads them to go their separate ways musically. Another example is the series' depiction of several real-life characters. McDonald is an idealistic and earnest singer/songwriter, but takes both smooth music and himself far too seriously. Loggins is his easygoing friend and frequent collaborator who eventually abandons smooth music in favor of commercial rock and roll in the 80s, which strains their friendship. The portrayal of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Oates" title="John Oates">
       John Oates
      </a>
      as the abusive, foulmouthed leader of
      <a href="/wiki/Hall_%26_Oates" title="Hall &amp; Oates">
       Hall &amp; Oates
      </a>
      , exerting sometimes violent control over the milquetoast
      <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Hall" title="Daryl Hall">
       Daryl Hall
      </a>
      , is clearly different from reality, in which Hall is the main lead vocalist and songwriter with no hint of a rivalry.
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Cross" title="Christopher Cross">
       Christopher Cross
      </a>
      is depicted as a wide-eyed, timid hayseed whose song "
      <a href="/wiki/Sailing_(Christopher_Cross_song)" title="Sailing (Christopher Cross song)">
       Sailing
      </a>
      " is lauded as the "smoothest song ever." Loggins' former partner
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Messina_(musician)" title="Jim Messina (musician)">
       Jim Messina
      </a>
      is a bitter wino who hates Loggins for his success and perceived betrayal.
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">
       Michael Jackson
      </a>
      is depicted as a hard-rock enthusiast who believes his partnership with guitarist
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Van_Halen" title="Eddie Van Halen">
       Eddie Van Halen
      </a>
      will lead to an endless parade of female sexual conquests.
      <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Baxter" title="Jeff Baxter">
       Jeff "Skunk" Baxter
      </a>
      , the Doobie Brothers' lead guitarist, is seen threatening to kick McDonald "out of the Doobies" if he doesn't write them another hit. (The real Baxter did bring fellow Steely Dan alumnus McDonald into the band but, as they achieved their greatest commercial success, Baxter left the Doobie Brothers because of his displeasure with their new commercial sound and attitude.)
      <a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">
       The Eagles
      </a>
      (portrayed here as jock-like meatheads) and Steely Dan (portrayed as snarky nerds, with
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Fagen" title="Donald Fagen">
       Donald Fagen
      </a>
      speaking in an incoherent babble of
      <a href="/wiki/Scat_singing" title="Scat singing">
       scat
      </a>
      that only the truly smooth can understand) really did insert lyrical references to each other in their music as depicted in the show, but these were actually friendly in nature, not part of a longtime grudge involving baseball bats and lunch-money shakedowns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_name">
       The name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yacht_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "Yacht rock" is a pejorative name
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      used retrospectively to refer to the soft rock format that peaked in popularity between the years of 1975 and 1984. In part, the term relates to the stereotype of the
      <a href="/wiki/Yuppie" title="Yuppie">
       yuppie
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Yacht" title="Yacht">
       yacht
      </a>
      owner, enjoying smooth music while out for a sail. Additionally, since sailing was a popular leisure activity in Southern California, many "yacht rockers" made nautical references in their lyrics, videos, and album artwork, particularly the anthemic track "
      <a href="/wiki/Sailing_(Christopher_Cross_song)" title="Sailing (Christopher Cross song)">
       Sailing
      </a>
      " by Christopher Cross. Notable artists also include
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_McDonald_(singer)" title="Michael McDonald (singer)">
       Michael McDonald
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Loggins" title="Kenny Loggins">
       Kenny Loggins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Boz_Scaggs" title="Boz Scaggs">
       Boz Scaggs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Steely_Dan" title="Steely Dan">
       Steely Dan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Toto_(band)" title="Toto (band)">
       Toto
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ryznar commented that the term was intended to refer to the "more elite studio artists" of the period, such as Michael McDonald and Kenny Loggins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_B._Lyons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David B. Lyons (page does not exist)">
       David B. Lyons
      </a>
      , who co-produced the show and played Koko Goldstein, noted that a friend of his devised the term "marina rock" in college to refer to a more "working-class" group of artists that didn't achieve the same high profile, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Seals_and_Crofts" title="Seals and Crofts">
       Seals and Crofts
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Higgins" title="Bertie Higgins">
       Bertie Higgins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Holmes" title="Rupert Holmes">
       Rupert Holmes
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Looking_Glass_(band)" title="Looking Glass (band)">
       Looking Glass
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Marina Rock
      </i>
      is actually a term for soft rock of the same era with a nautical subject matter. Therefore, there aren't specific Marina Rock artists as much as there are songs that fall into the category such as "
      <a href="/wiki/Ride_Captain_Ride" title="Ride Captain Ride">
       Ride Captain Ride
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Blues_Image" title="Blues Image">
       Blues Image
      </a>
      , "
      <a href="/wiki/I%27m_Your_Captain_(Closer_to_Home)" title="I'm Your Captain (Closer to Home)">
       I'm Your Captain (Closer to Home)
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Funk_Railroad" title="Grand Funk Railroad">
       Grand Funk Railroad
      </a>
      , "
      <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Island_(song)" title="Thunder Island (song)">
       Thunder Island
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Jay_Ferguson_(American_musician)" title="Jay Ferguson (American musician)">
       Jay Ferguson
      </a>
      and "
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Cross_(Crosby,_Stills_and_Nash_song)" title="Southern Cross (Crosby, Stills and Nash song)">
       Southern Cross
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Crosby,_Stills,_Nash_%26_Young" title="Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young">
       Crosby, Stills and Nash
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite the show's intentions, music journalists have begun using the term
      <i>
       yacht rock
      </i>
      for all of the similar-sounding music of the period, including bands such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ambrosia_(band)" title="Ambrosia (band)">
       Ambrosia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/10cc" title="10cc">
       10cc
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Cruise" title="Pablo Cruise">
       Pablo Cruise
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Firefall" title="Firefall">
       Firefall
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/England_Dan_%26_John_Ford_Coley" title="England Dan &amp; John Ford Coley">
       England Dan &amp; John Ford Coley
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Orleans_(band)" title="Orleans (band)">
       Orleans
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ace_(band)" title="Ace (band)">
       Ace
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Player_(band)" title="Player (band)">
       Player
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The artists retrospectively grouped under the yacht rock umbrella dominated the
      <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">
       Grammy Awards
      </a>
      , with Christopher Cross and Toto sweeping the major awards in 1981 and 1983 respectively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, these artists were not a hit with most rock critics at the time, who dismissed it as being
      <a href="/wiki/Arena_rock" title="Arena rock">
       corporate rock
      </a>
      that was overproduced, generic, and
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_of_the_road_(music)" title="Middle of the road (music)">
       middle of the road
      </a>
      , instead favoring
      <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">
       punk
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">
       new wave
      </a>
      acts such as
      <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">
       The Clash
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Blondie_(band)" title="Blondie (band)">
       Blondie
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Patti_Smith" title="Patti Smith">
       Patti Smith
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">
       Elvis Costello
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While Ryznar and the show popularized the term "yacht rock," it had existed previously. Its earliest-known appearance came in 1990 from Dave Larsen, popular music critic for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Daily_News" title="Dayton Daily News">
        Dayton Daily News
       </a>
      </i>
      , describing an upcoming
      <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Buffett" title="Jimmy Buffett">
       Jimmy Buffett
      </a>
      concert in Cincinnati.
     </p>

Fearing for his friend's life, McDonald tries to rescue him, and in the process, finds his relevance. By the end of the episode the loose ends of the past 11 episodes are tied together (including the revelation that all of Yacht Rock had been a plan by Koko - to lead to the song

          <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Freedom_(Michael_McDonald_song)" title="Sweet Freedom (Michael McDonald song)">
           Sweet Freedom
          </a>
         </i>
         ), but left with a cliff hanger ending as to who murdered Koko.
