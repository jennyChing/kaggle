          <span class="arttle">
           <h1>
            Hu's your daddy? Not yet. Uncle Sam flexes aging muscle
           </h1>
          </span>
         </div>
         <span class="byline" style="padding:10px 0px;display:inline-block;">
          <span class="imghov" style="top:4px">
           <span id="auim">
           </span>
           <label>
            The author has posted comments on this article
           </label>
          </span>
          <a href="/toireporter/author-Chidanand-Rajghatta.cms" rel="author">
           Chidanand Rajghatta
          </a>
          , TNN
          <span style="font-size:9px;">
           |
          </span>
          Jan 21, 2011, 09.23PM IST
         </span>
         <div style="margin-left:5px;">
          <div style="height:25px;border-bottom:1px solid #ccc; margin-bottom:10px;">

         <div id="bxml">
          <div class="section1">
           <div class="Normal">
            WASHINGTON: "Speak softly; carry a big stick," was turn-of-last century American President Teddy Roosevelt's geo-political prescription for a fledgling world power. "Lie low for another 50 years before challenging
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/United-States">
             America
            </a>
            ," Chinese statesman
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Deng-Xiaoping">
             Deng Xiaoping
            </a>
            counselled his developing country some 20 years ago. Leaders of the
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/United-States">
             United States
            </a>
            and China both tested the respective proposition of their forbears during a bilateral summit this week that saw the two 21st century powers circle each other warily amid an the usual outpouring of diplomatic niceties and cliches.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Leading a country that is perceived to be weakening economically, President
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Barack-Obama">
             Obama
            </a>
            abandoned the speak softly advise to publicly hector his Chinese counterpart
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Hu-Jintao">
             Hu Jintao
            </a>
            on issues ranging from human rights to currency manipulation to intellectual theft. No topic was off limit as Obama touched on Taiwan, Tibet,
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Iran">
             Iran
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Sudan">
             Sudan
            </a>
            , the Korean peninsula to mark out US interests across the globe before letting it be known that Washington was not about to cede its role as a Pacific power, much less a world force, notwithstanding China's dramatic rise.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            It was a compelling performance that impressed US pundits, one of whom was relieved that he had demonstrated that "America is not quite so weak and disoriented as the Chinese might have imagined after the financial crisis and the soggy Iraq-Afghanistan wars." As if to rub in the fact that the US is not overawed by China's rise, and has other Asian options, a top Coca-Cola honcho startled everyone at a reception with a gaffe involving raising a toast for President Hu in Japanese - saying "kanpai" (cheers) instead of the similar sounding "ganbei" in Mandarin.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <div id="gads">
             <iframe align="center" allowtransparency="true" frameborder="0" height="190" hspace="0" id="adshowbtm" marginheight="0" marginwidth="0" name="adshowbtm" scrolling="no" src="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/configspace/ads/PM_article_middle.html" title="Advertisement" vspace="0" width="660">
             </iframe>
            </div>
            Obama was a more circumspect though, not only getting "ganbei" correctly at the State banquet (the only time he took a shot at Mandarin unlike his many attempts at Hindi during Prime Minister Singh's visit), but also deploying his daughter Sasha to demonstrate China's centrality to American economic revival and its future. It turns out that nine-year old Sasha is learning Mandarin in school, and she had the privilege of trying out some phrases with Hu in a vivid demonstration of China's growing importance. For all the geo-political bashing, the US President and his aides made no secret of China's primacy in America's future, particularly its economic revival, revealing in the process that they considered India's a distant second in the sweepstakes.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            On China's part, Hu remained sufficiently deferential to the declining superpower, acknowledging Washington's concerns about North Korean aggression and human rights, and yielding a little on each issues without keeling over to please America. In fact, none of the perceived "concessions" were relayed to the audience in China, which was fed Beijing's nationalistic stand on its "core interests" relating to
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Taiwan">
             Taiwan
            </a>
            and
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Tibet">
             Tibet
            </a>
            in a demonstration to the domestic constituency of China's rising world status.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            In fact, Hu went out of his way to assuage US concerns about China's rising economic and military power, declaring that his country "will never seek hegemony or pursue an expansionist policy," while maintaining that ties between the two sides need to be governed by a belief in "equality" and "mutual respect." Not even a less than warm reception on Capitol Hill, where lawmakers grilled him over the same issues Obama raised, rattled his calm demeanour as he flew out to
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Chicago">
             Chicago
            </a>
            , Obama's home town, for a quiet demonstration of China's economic and cultural muscle.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            He needn't have gone that far. Even as Hu was engaging Obama, one US Senator discovered (late) that most of the patriotic baubles sold in gift shops in
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Washington,-D.C.">
             Washington DC
            </a>
            , including busts of US Presidents from  George Washington to Barack Obama, are made in China (which wasn't exactly breaking news). "It appears that a museum owned by the people of the United States, celebrating the history of the United States, cannot find companies in this country employing American workers that are able to manufacture statues of our founding fathers, or our current president," Senator
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Bernie-Sanders">
             Bernie Sanders
            </a>
            fumed in a letter to the Director of the
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Smithsonian-Institution">
             Smithsonian Museum
            </a>
            . "That is pretty pathetic! I was not aware that the collapse of our manufacturing base had gone that far."
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Indeed, it has, not to speak of the fact that Americans are hooked beyond help to cheap Chinese goods. As for
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Beijing">
             Beijing
            </a>
            , it too rubbed it in by grandly announcing that the two giant pandas - Tian Tian and Mei Xiang - that it had loaned to the Smithsonian National Zoo for ten years would stay on for five more years. "Taking full consideration of the fondness of the American people towards the giant panda, China reached agreement with the US on the extension of the agreement on the giant pandas," a Chinese official said, in an ironic twist the geo-political jousting between the two countries.
            <br/>
           </div>
