             <h2>
              <strong>
               Cracked Lobster Tail
              </strong>
             </h2>
             <strong>
              Why to grill it:
             </strong>
             Lobster is delicious but delicate, so people put it on a pedestal (or a roll). But dont be afraid to throw the tails on the grill, because that fiery crispness goes mouth-wateringly well with fatty meat.
             <br/>
             <strong>
              How to do it:
             </strong>
             Take the tails -- separated from the rest of the body, because the claws are too small for this -- then split them open down the middle and expose the meat. Place the tail meat side down for no more than a couple minutes on direct heat to score some grill lines, then flip it back over on the shell and close the grill, letting it steam to soft perfection. Drench it in butter and seasoning right off the flame and try not to eat it before it hits the picnic table.

            <div class="caption">
             <h2>
              <strong>
               Sweetbread (Pancreas or Thymus Gland of a Calf)
              </strong>
             </h2>
             <strong>
              Why to grill it:
             </strong>
             The grills smokiness adds a nice contrast to the lip-smacking richness of this butcher shop standby.
             <br/>
             <strong>
              How to do it:
             </strong>
             Its best to talk through some options with your butcher on prep. LaFrieda here recommends asking for the heart cut, which is essentially just the largest slice. Then butcher it into manageable pieces and skewer 'em. This stuff is hard to overcook, so sear the bejesus out of it and get ready to gobble some gland.
             <br/>
             <h2>
              <strong>
               Wild Turkey Legs
              </strong>
             </h2>
             <strong>
              Why to grill it:
             </strong>
             The legs of a wild turkey are much more muscle-y than those of a farm-raised cousin. Meaning you get a richer, darker meat experience. Grilling sets a nice, deep char onto that complexity and yields supremely tasty results.
             <br/>
             <strong>
              How to do it:
             </strong>
             With wild meat, the toughest part is the prep -- you need to have the proper know-how for plucking and cleaning it. Seeking a butchers or seasoned hunters help isnt a bad idea. Now the easy part: slap it on the grill and walk away until it's cooked through.

            <div class="caption">
             <h2>
              <strong>
               Skin-OffDuck Breast
              </strong>
             </h2>
             <strong>
              Why to grill it:
             </strong>
             Pan-seared (and oven-finished) duck is commonplace in a nice restaurant, but open flame lets you cook the skin and breast to separate states of meaty perfection.
             <br/>
             <strong>
              How to do it:
             </strong>
             Because the skin and fat are going to take much longer to render than the breast will take to cook, separate them entirely from the meat and give them a head start on the flame. Once the skin crisps up, start on the breast, giving it a few minutes on each side. Then lay the skin right on top when you serve it.
             <br/>
             <h2>
              <strong>
               Squirrel
              </strong>
             </h2>
             <strong>
              Why to grill it:
             </strong>
             We cant personally vouch for this because we didnt have a squirrel handy. But because LaFrieda is an absolute meat obsessive, his recommendations for crazy grill stuff knew no bounds. Grilling up a whole squirrel is apparently one of his favorite summer meals.
             <br/>
             <strong>
              How to do it:
             </strong>
             Again, this is a wild animal here, so youll need to seek some advice on prepping the meat. But once youre ready, it can be grilled whole right on the grates, or you can rig up a rotisserie spit over the coals.
