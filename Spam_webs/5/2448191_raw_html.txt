          <h1>
           Food and Mood
          </h1>
          <p class="subhead">
          </p>
          <div class="box-byblock c">
           <div class="byline">
            By
            <a href="http://www.healthline.com/health-blogs/bio/tara-gidus" rel="author">
             Tara Gidus
            </a>
            , MS, RD, CSSD, LD/N | Published Sep 12, 2012
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="pageTools">
           <!-- AddThis Button BEGIN -->
           <div addthis:description="Blogger Tara Gidus foods and supplements that can improve mood and reduce stress." addthis:title="Food and Mood" addthis:url="http://www.healthline.com/health-blogs/diet-diva/food-and-mood" class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style c" id="addthis_toolbox">
            <div id="socialIcons c">
             <a class="at300b fbStd" id="facebookIcon" title="Send to Facebook">
              <img alt="" border="0" class="facebookOff" height="18" src="http://www.healthline.com/resources/healthline/images/hl_addthis_fb.v1.20150707133157.gif" style="display:none" width="19"/>
             </a>
             <a class="addthis_button_tweet">
             </a>
             <a class="addthis_button_google_plusone" g:plusone:size="medium" style="width:75px;">
             </a>
             <a class="addthis_button_print">
             </a>
             <a class="addthis_button_email">
             </a>
            </div>
           </div>
           <!-- AddThis Button END -->
          </div>
          <div class="textBlock">
           <p>
            <span class="imageAreaBordered rightAligned">
             <img alt="A woman holds her temples" height="200" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/mood.jpg" style="float: right;" width="170"/>
            </span>
            Are you tired? Sad? Tense? Mad? Whether its anxiety about
an upcoming deadline, mood swings from a rollercoaster of a day, or just plain
hormonal changes, you can actually help your mood by eating certain foods.
Instead of reaching for the family size bag of potato chips, try switching to
some of these nutritional choices!
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Fish,
Flax, and Walnuts
             </strong>
             : Alabacore, tuna, salmon, herring or any other fatty fish
contain omega 3s which actually help protect brain tissue and promote healthy
brain cell production.
             <a href="http://www.healthline.com/health-blogs/diet-diva/health-benefits-omega-3" target="_blank">
              Omega
3s
             </a>
             also trigger production of serotonin, your feel good, mood enhancing
neurotransmitter. If you dont like fish, you can add flax or walnuts to your
diet.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Magnesium:
             </strong>
             This also helps our bodys regulate serotonin activity which is also a natural
appetite suppressant. Since PMS can sometimes lower levels of magnesium, it can
often cause stress eating. And what do we reach for when we are stress eating?
Junk food! Try eating high magnesium foods such as spinach, black beans,
pumpkin seeds, bananas, brown rice, or tofu.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Whole
Grains:
             </strong>
             Complex carbohydrates like whole grain rice, pasta, and bread are
ideal for raising serotonin levels and providing the brain glucose, the primary
energy source for optimal brain functioning. Whole grains are also loaded with
             <a href="http://www.healthline.com/health-blogs/diet-diva/health-benefits-b-vitamins" target="_blank">
              B
vitamins
             </a>
             which may help decrease irritability and depression.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Caffeine:
             </strong>
             We all know
             <a href="http://www.healthline.com/health-blogs/diet-diva/caffeine">
              caffeine
             </a>
             can affect our mood especially when you try to cut it out cold turkey. Try
using decaffeinated coffee or soda to minimize mood effects.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              High choline
foods:
             </strong>
             Our brain needs choline
(vitamin B4) to produce acetylcholine which boosts memory, muscle control, and
behavior. Foods that are high in choline include
             <a href="http://www.healthline.com/health-blogs/diet-diva/health-benefits-eggs">
              ,
egg yolks
             </a>
             , wheat germ, peanuts, broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, fish, dairy
and other meat products.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Folate:
             </strong>
             Along
with many other health benefits, folate or folic acid will help boost your
cognitive function and reduce the risk of different brain conditions like
Alzheimers. Foods high in folate include breads, cereals, green leafy
vegetables like spinach and kale as well as beans,
             <a href="http://www.almondboard.com/Pages/Default.aspx?redirect=false" target="_blank">
              nuts
             </a>
             ,
peas, avocados, bananas, and oranges.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Vary your food intake to help your brain function, including
mood, at optimal levels. The key to keeping your brain young and sharp is to
eat a variety of fruits, vegetables, grains and proteins. Balance is the key!
           </p>
