     <article class="post-44448 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-environmental-technology tag-conflict-free-microprocessor tag-conflict-minerals tag-conflict-free tag-conflict-free-minerals tag-conflictfree tag-congo tag-csr tag-enough-project tag-intel tag-resolve">

       <div class="inner">
        <div class="leftCol">
         <div class="media">
          <div class="ratio_enforcer">
           <div class="container">
            <img alt="cheuk.jpg" class="attachment-full-featured-width" height="411" src="http://iq.intel.com/wp-content/uploads/cheuk.jpg" width="548"/>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="story-content">
          <p>
           At the 2014 Consumer Electronics Show, a major milestone was shared with the world that reflects significant changes in how new products are made and how people think about them before they make a purchase. A new computer, for instance, may or may not have a microprocessor made with conflict minerals.
          </p>
          <p>
           Intel CEO Brian Krzanich, during his keynote kicking off the International CES in Las Vegas this week, told the industry that
           <a href="http://www.fastcoexist.com/3024292/starting-now-all-intel-microprocessors-are-conflict-free-heres-how-the-company-did-it">
            microprocessors
           </a>
           , the brains inside tablets, laptop and desktop computers, are now being manufactured by Intel, beginning to end, using 
           <a href="http://intel.com/conflictfree">
            conflict-free* minerals
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="" src="http://blogs.intel.com/iq/files/2014/09/conflicfreekeynote2.jpg"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           Intel is one of the worlds largest manufacturers of microprocessors, said Marcus Bleasdale in a recent interview with
           <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2014/01/140109-conflict-minerals-intel-marcus-bleasdale-congo/">
            National Geographic
           </a>
           . The photographers work appeared in
           <a href="http://ngm.nationalgeographic.com/2013/10/conflict-minerals/gettleman-text">
            The Price of Precious,
           </a>
           a National Geographic story depicting the suffering of people caught in the middle of the conflict mineral activities in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC).
          </p>
          <p>
           What they did is huge, said Bleasdale, who has become passionate about this issue after visiting DRC. It gives the effort momentum. Almost one-fourth of the smelters used by electronic companies have been audited as conflict-free. Plus, more and bigger mines in the DRC are coming on tap as certified conflict-free.
          </p>
          <p>
           Carolyn Duran, supply chain director and conflict mineral program manager at Intel, explains how that this change has come about through a concerted efforts that identify the origins of mined minerals, particularly tungsten, tantalum, tin and gold, which are used to produce microprocessors and many common consumer electronics devices. These minerals come from many parts of the world including DRC, a place where profits from the sale of these minerals were often generated through forced labor and used to fund civil war and brutality.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="" height="450" src="http://blogs.intel.com/iq/files/2014/09/1613.jpg" width="600"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Gold-laden pebbles, fresh from the Chien Mechant Mine, are crushed into silt in preparation for gold panning in the Lubona Village, Democratic Republic of Congo. Photo byResolve.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           By focusing on smelters, where the raw ore is converted into metals, Duran said that Intel and others were able to develop an effective method for inspecting and verifying mineral sources.
          </p>
          <p>
           She and others from Intel visited some 20 countries to scrutinize more than 63 smelters, and their findings paved the way for what became the global Conflict Free Sourcing Initiative (CFSI).
          </p>
          <p>
           Smelters exist all around the world, said Duran. Many are concentrated around the locations of the minerals.
          </p>
          <p>
           In China, for example, tungsten, tantalum, tin and gold are mined and ore is imported from other countries. Indonesia is where a significant portion of the worlds tin is mined and smelted. Smelters focusing on recycled materials are prevalent in Japan and Europe, according to Duran.
          </p>
          <p>
           I have personally been to gold recyclers in Japan, tin smelters in Indonesia and fold refiners in Canada, she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           All but a few of the 20 or so counties visited by her team agreed to work directly with Intel.
          </p>
          <p>
           Most wanted to learn about the audit program and get guidance from us as to what documentation they needed for a successful audit, she said. They really opened their doors to us so we could better understand their sources of origin, processes and systems.
          </p>
          <p>
           For some metals and some smelters its taken longer than others, but Duran said that most smelter owners are convinced that this issue is important.
          </p>
          <p>
           Smelters have a role to play in this, regardless of whether they source from their region or not, she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Demonstrating Change
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Smelters still seek out the lowest cost options, but they have embraced the need for supply chain transparency, according to Duran
          </p>
          <p>
           They are changing their systems and processes so they can demonstrate the source of origin for the materials they process  whether its ore or recycled material.
          </p>
          <p>
           She sees the pace of change has been specific to each metal.
          </p>
          <p>
           We started our efforts with tantalum in 2009, said Duran. By early 2012, enough of the supply chain had set expectations on the tantalum smelting industry that it was necessary for tantalum smelters to get validated in order to sell their material in the market. What started out as a few companies urging smelters to take action and engage in validation activities has now turned to tantalum smelters actively seeking out the audit program and asking to participate.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="" height="500" src="http://blogs.intel.com/iq/files/2014/09/phoenix_metals_registeringtagsinlogbooksanddatabase_11-11-2013__1_2.jpg" width="450"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Ore bag tags from iTSCi (ITRI Tin Supply Chain Initiative), a joint industry system of traceability and due diligence, are logged upon receipt of materials at a concentrators facility in Kigali, Rwanda. Photo byIntel.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           Duran believe that patience and tenacity helped things progress this far and they will be required in order for continued success. In the early days of her teams efforts all she could say was that she hoped, but I didnt know for sure, that things were improving at smelters. But as time went on, she started seeing changes in behavior of smelters. After
           <a href="http://www.enoughproject.org/reports/striking-gold-how-m23-and-its-allies-are-infiltrating-congos-gold-trade">
            Enough Project
           </a>
           reported a significant reduction in funding from the region, it became evident that this program was making a difference.
          </p>
          <p>
           It feels like we are so far away from the issues on the ground [where smelters operator], but we are not, said Duran. What we do can and does make a difference as long as we keep with it.
          </p>
          <p>
           She wishes more progress was made for all four metals, pointing out that while tantalum smelters went through the audit processes steadily, its been more challenging for those smelting tungsten, tin and gold.
          </p>
          <p>
           We are getting there though, and thats what matters.
          </p>
          <p>
           The challenge for the electronics industry in making conflict-free products is explored in this CES conversation moderated by Marc Gunther, editor-at-large at The Guardian, Krzanich was joined by actress and activist Robin Wright, and Sasha Lezhnev, policy director for Enough Project.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           *
           <em>
            Conflict free and conflict-free means DRC conflict free, which is defined by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission rules to mean products that do not contain conflict minerals (tin, tantalum, tungsten and/or gold) that directly or indirectly finance or benefit armed groups in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) or adjoining countries. We also use the term conflict-free in a broader sense to refer to suppliers, supply chains, smelters and refiners whose sources of conflict minerals do not finance conflict in the DRC or adjoining countries.
           </em>
          </p>
