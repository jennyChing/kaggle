          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           There's a new mystery surrounding a respiratory virus suspected of sickening children in 45 states since August: temporary paralysis.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           The virus, called enterovirus 68, can start out like the
           <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/topics/lifestyle/health/cold-and-flu.htm" id="ramplink_common cold_" target="_blank">
            common cold
           </a>
           but can quickly turn serious and send children to the hospital with breathing problems. And now, the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention says it is investigating whether the virus led to temporary limb paralysis in nine children in Colorado.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           The virus is similar to the rhinovirus that causes the common cold, according to the CDC. But unlike a cold, the infection can lead to severe respiratory symptoms such as
           <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/topics/lifestyle/health/emphysema.htm" id="ramplink_wheezing_" target="_blank">
            wheezing
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           "It's the wheezing you have to watch out for," said ABC News' chief health and medical editor, Dr. Richard Besser, referring to the whistling sound generated when air moves through narrowed breathing tubes.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Health/paralysis-link-suspected-enterovirus-spreads/story?id=25831511">
            Paralysis Link Suspected as Enterovirus Spreads
           </a>
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Health/respiratory-virus-enterovirus-d68-infected-thousands/story?id=25519869">
            Respiratory Virus, Enterovirus D68, Likely Infected Thousands But Just Tip of Iceberg
           </a>
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Here are six things you should know about the outbreak.
          </p>
          <div class="clearboth">
           <!-- clear -->
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="listItem noThumb" id="item_0">
          <div class="listItemHeader">
           <table>
            <tr>
             <td class="title">
              <strong>
               The CDC is Investigating the link between Enterovirus 68 and paralysis.
              </strong>
             </td>
            </tr>
           </table>
          </div>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Nine children in Denver have reported neurological symptoms after having a respiratory virus, according to the CDC, which says it is now investigating a link between enterovirus 68 and paralysis. The patients are all 18 years old and younger, and four of them have tested positive for enterovirus 68.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           "It is a spectrum of arm or leg weakness that can be as mild as weakness or as severe as paralysis," said Dr. Larry Wolk, the chief medical officer and executive director for the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment. "What ties them all together though are findings of spots or lesions in the grey matter of the spinal cord on MRI scans."
          </p>
          <div class="clearboth">
           <!-- empty -->
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="listItem noThumb" id="item_1">
          <div class="listItemHeader">
           <table>
            <tr>
             <td class="title">
              <strong>
               This Isnt the First Enterovirus 68 Outbreak in the U.S.
              </strong>
             </td>
            </tr>
           </table>
          </div>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Georgia and Pennsylvania reported clusters of enterovirus 68 almost exactly five years ago in September 2009, according to a
           <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/mm6038a1.htm" target="external">
            2011 CDC report
           </a>
           . Arizona had a small cluster of cases in August and September 2010, according to the same report.
          </p>
          <div class="clearboth">
           <!-- empty -->
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="listItem noThumb" id="item_2">
          <div class="listItemHeader">
           <table>
            <tr>
             <td class="title">
              <strong>
               No One Knows How It Started
              </strong>
             </td>
            </tr>
           </table>
          </div>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           While this isn't the first time enterovirus 68 has popped up in the U.S., health officials are still trying to figure out why the virus has re-emerged.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           "This is a very common time for outbreaks. Kids come back to school, they like to share things, they bring them home to their little brothers and sisters," said Besser, adding that most enterovirus outbreaks occur in the summer. "But this one, this particular enterovirus is very rare, and they have no idea why it showed up this year."
          </p>
          <div class="clearboth">
           <!-- empty -->
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="listItem noThumb" id="item_3">
          <div class="listItemHeader">
           <table>
            <tr>
             <td class="title">
              <strong>
               No One Knows How It Spreads
              </strong>
             </td>
            </tr>
           </table>
          </div>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">
           Studies on enterovirus 68 are limited, and so is knowledge about how the virus spreads.
          </p>
          <p itemprop="articleBody">

           </a>
