    Dkklfar and Ljslfar
   </h1>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
       Norse mythology
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       Dkklfar
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      "Dark Elves", singular
      <b>
       Dkklfr
      </b>
      ) and
      <b>
       Ljslfar
      </b>
      (Old Norse "Light Elves", singular
      <b>
       Ljslfr
      </b>
      ) are two contrasting types of
      <a href="/wiki/Elf" title="Elf">
       elves
      </a>
      ; the prior dwell within the earth and are most swarthy, while the latter live in
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lfheimr" title="lfheimr">
       lfheimr
      </a>
      , and are "fairer than
      <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B3l_(sun)" title="Sl (sun)">
       the sun
      </a>
      to look at". The Dkklfar and the Ljslfar are attested in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">
        Prose Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      , written in the 13th century by
      <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">
       Snorri Sturluson
      </a>
      , and in the late Old Norse poem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hrafnagaldr_%C3%93%C3%B0ins" title="Hrafnagaldr ins">
        Hrafnagaldr ins
       </a>
      </i>
      . Scholars have produced theories about the origin and implications of the dualistic concept.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Attestations">
       Attestations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Attestations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prose_Edda">
       <i>
        Prose Edda
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prose Edda">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the
      <i>
       Prose Edda
      </i>
      , the Dkklfar and the Ljslfar are attested in chapter 17 of the book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">
        Gylfaginning
       </a>
      </i>
      . In the chapter,
      <a href="/wiki/Gylfi" title="Gylfi">
       Gangleri
      </a>
      (the king
      <a href="/wiki/Gylfi" title="Gylfi">
       Gylfi
      </a>
      in disguise) asks the enthroned figure of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">
       High
      </a>
      what other "chief centres" there are in the heavens outside of the spring
      <a href="/wiki/Ur%C3%B0arbrunnr" title="Urarbrunnr">
       Urarbrunnr
      </a>
      . Gangleri responds that there are many fine places in heaven, including a place called lfheimr (Old Norse 'Elf Home' or 'Elf World'). High says that the Ljslfar live in lfheimr, while the Dkklfar dwell underground and lookand particularly behavequite unlike the Ljslfar. High describes the Ljslfar as "fairer than the sun to look at", while the Dkklfar are "blacker than pitch".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES19-20_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FAULKES19-20-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESS1848-Ch17_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ESS1848-Ch17-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As chapter 17 continues, Gangleri asks what will protect the beautiful hall of
      <a href="/wiki/Giml%C3%A9" title="Giml">
       Giml
      </a>
      , previously described as "the southernmost end of heaven", when the fires of
      <a href="/wiki/Surtr" title="Surtr">
       Surtr
      </a>
      "burn heaven and earth" (
      <a href="/wiki/Ragnar%C3%B6k" title="Ragnark">
       Ragnark
      </a>
      ). High responds that there are in fact other heavens. The first called
      <a href="/wiki/Andlang" title="Andlang">
       Andlngr
      </a>
      , he says, is "south of and above this heaven of ours" and "we believe" Giml is located in the third heaven "still further above that one",
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADdbl%C3%A1in" title="Vdblin">
       Vblinn
      </a>
      . High adds that "we believe it is only light-elves who inhabit these places for the time being".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESS1848-Ch17_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ESS1848-Ch17-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAULKES-20_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FAULKES-20-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hrafnagaldr_.C3.93.C3.B0ins">
       <i>
        Hrafnagaldr ins
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hrafnagaldr ins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There occurs an additional mention of the
      <i>
       dkklfar
      </i>
      in the late Old Norse poem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hrafnagaldr_%C3%93%C3%B0ins" title="Hrafnagaldr ins">
        Hrafnagaldr ins
       </a>
      </i>
      ('Odin's Raven-
      <a href="/wiki/Galdr" title="Galdr">
       galdr
      </a>
      '), stanza 25.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_and_interpretations">
       Theories and interpretations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theories and interpretations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As the concept is only recorded in
      <i>
       Gylfaginning
      </i>
      and the late poem
      <i>
       Hrafnagaldr ins
      </i>
      , it is unclear whether the distinction between the two types of elves originated with Snorri, or if he was merely recounting a concept already developed.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Question_of_Christian_influence">
       Question of Christian influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Question of Christian influence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The sub-classification perhaps resulted from
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christian
      </a>
      influence, by way of importation of the concept of
      <a href="/wiki/Good_and_evil" title="Good and evil">
       good and evil
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angels" title="Angels">
       angels
      </a>
      of light and darkness.
      <a href="/wiki/Anne_Holtsmark" title="Anne Holtsmark">
       Anne Holtsmark
      </a>
      aired this view,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though with some reservation, since "good vs. evil" dualism is not confined to Christian thinking.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        a
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Aside from some additional observations to encourage the hypothesis,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Holtsmark has been credited with demonstrating that Snorri borrowed from Christian writings, specifically that "Snorris description of
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADdbl%C3%A1in" title="Vdblin">
       Vblinn
      </a>
      [the third heaven populated by light-elves] was almost certainly influenced by (and possibly based on) the account of the angels in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elucidarius" title="Elucidarius">
        Elucidarius
       </a>
      </i>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        b
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dissenters of the view that elves was a later invention, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Simek" title="Rudolf Simek">
       Rudolf Simek
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Turville-Petre" title="Gabriel Turville-Petre">
       Gabriel Turville-Petre
      </a>
      feel rather that "dark" and "light" aspects of the same beings not inherently unlikely, death and fertility cults often being closely related.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMEK56_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SIMEK56-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dwarves">
       Dwarves
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dwarves">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Since the
      <i>
       Prose Edda
      </i>
      describes the
      <i>
       dkklfar
      </i>
      as being
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subterranean" title="wikt:subterranean">
       subterranean
      </a>
      dwellers, they may be
      <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Germanic_mythology)" title="Dwarf (Germanic mythology)">
       dwarves
      </a>
      under another name, in the opinion of a number of scholars such as
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lindow" title="John Lindow">
       John Lindow
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LINDOW110_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LINDOW110-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lassen105_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lassen105-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Prose Edda
      </i>
      also uniquely mentions the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Svart%C3%A1lfar" title="Svartlfar">
        svartlfar
       </a>
      </i>
      ('black elves'),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lassen105_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lassen105-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but there are reasons to believe these also refer to merely dwarfs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        c
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Consequently, Lindow and other commentators have remarked that there may not have been any distinction intended between dark-elves and black-elves by those who coined and used those terms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Lotte_Motz" title="Lotte Motz">
       Lotte Motz
      </a>
      's paper on elves commingles, and hence equates "dark-elves" and "black-elves" from the outset.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grimm.27s_trinity">
       Grimm's trinity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%B6kk%C3%A1lfar_and_Lj%C3%B3s%C3%A1lfar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Grimm's trinity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">
       Jacob Grimm
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shippey-grimm_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shippey-grimm-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      surmised that the proto-elf (
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/urspr%C3%BCnglich" title="wikt:ursprnglich">
        ursprnglich
       </a>
      </i>
      ) was probably a "light-colored, white, good spirit" while the dwarfs may have been conceived as "black spirits" by relative comparison. But the "two classes of creatures were getting confounded," and there arose a need to coin the term "light-elf" (
      <i>
       ljslfar
      </i>
      , or
      <i>
       hvtlfar
      </i>
       "white-elves")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to refer to the "elves proper". This was counterpart to the "dark-elf" (
      <i>
       dokklfar
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimm-eng2-444_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grimm-eng2-444-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        d
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Preferring it over duality, Grimm postulated three kinds of elves (
      <i>
       lioslfar
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       docklfar
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       svartlfar
      </i>
      ) present in Norse mythology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      But Grimm's "tripartite division" (as Shippey calls it) faced "trouble" in Snorri's statement that dark-elves were pitch-black, as this would lead to the "first reduction" that "dark-elves = black-elves." As a solution, Grimm "pronounce[es] Snorri's statement fallacious," and hypothesizes that "dark elves" were not really 'dark' but rather 'dingy' or 'pale'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shippey-grimm_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-shippey-grimm-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimm-eng2-445_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grimm-eng2-445-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      And while conceding that "such a Trilogy still [lacks] decisive proof,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimm-eng2-446_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grimm-eng2-446-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      draws parallels from the white, brown and black subterranean in
      <a href="/wiki/Pomerania" title="Pomerania">
       Pomeranian
      </a>
      legend,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimm-eng2-446_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grimm-eng2-446-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the white, pale, and black troops of spirits come to claim souls in the tale of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Solomon_and_Marcolf" title="Solomon and Marcolf">
        Solomon and Marcolf
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimm-eng2-446_26-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grimm-eng2-446-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
