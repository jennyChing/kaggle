      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Unsatisfactory childhood: Anthony Horowitz." src="/content/dam/images/g/h/0/b/8/q/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.1mko5u.png/1431581623979.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Unsatisfactory childhood: Anthony Horowitz.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Francesco Guidicini/Headpress
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       <strong>
        Throughouthis adult life, Englishauthor AnthonyHorowitz
       </strong>
       has kept faith with his childhood memories: the ghosts of unforgotten feelings of helplessness, and the impotent realisation that discipline and punishment never hurt others more than it hurt himself.
      </p>
      <p>
       Horowitzremembers a lot, but doesn't recall exactly how many copies he has sold of his 10-volume series of young-adult novels featuring the teenage spy Alex Rider. He seems to have lost count at 17 million, although he thinks the figure may be as high as 19 million. He has also written many other children's books, created the TV series
       <em>
        Foyle's War
       </em>
       and
       <em>
        MidsomerMurders
       </em>
       , and has authored two "authorised" Sherlock Holmes mysteries and an upcoming official James Bond thriller.
      </p>
      <p>
       He lives with his wife, television producer Jill Green, in a third-storey apartment in Clerkenwell, central London. Their younger sonCassian, 25, lives with two friends in the flat below, and their elder son, Nicholas, 26, and a friend occupy the bottom floor.
      </p>
      <p>
       "We have a rule that we never go into each other's property unless we're invited," he says, "but we see each other all the time. There's a lift between the flats, and food parcels and laundry travel up and down. We have dinner together at least once a week. I'm very fortunate, after having such an unfortunate experience with family as a boy, to have what I honestly think of as the most perfect family around me now."
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       I'm having lunch withHorowitzat Canteen restaurant in the Royal Festival Hall in London. He is a handsome, solicitous, unweathered-looking 60-year-old, with the booming voice of a stage actor, who was born in Stanmore, Middlesex. "I was brought up in a very large house," he says, "by a slightly Victorian father, in a family set-up that I never really quite understood. I don't know whether my parents liked each other, because they didn't let me know.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Ican'ttell you with any certainty what my father did for a living. He was a solicitor by trade, but his business dealings were unusual, to say the least. Itwas a very wealthy, very privileged and rather stark, emotionally, upbringing: nannies, maids, cooks, but not many parents. That said, I adored my mother.I still very much miss her."
      </p>
      <p>
       He says he was a fat boy, without confidence. "Why do writers become children's authors?" he asks. "If you look at many of them  Enid Blyton,RoaldDahl  a sort of unsatisfactory childhood is a big part of it: not necessarily unhappy, but unsatisfactory.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I never talk about an unhappy childhood  although I was unhappy  because when there are so many children in the world with nothing who are being killed and starved and beaten, for some guy to sit in a canteen in London and say, 'Well, I had this unhappy childhood,' strikes me as slightly disgusting. But it was unsatisfactory."
      </p>
      <p>
       He struggled through prep school as a boarder atOrleyFarm School in Harrow, north London. "For me," he says, "school was beatings and floggings and blood going down your legs, and about cold and foul food and an atmosphere of constant fear, teachers who nowadays would be in prison for the way they treated us. But it was worse than that: it was a psychological, physical brutalisation that lasted for five long years. I still see myself screaming and crying in the last week of the holidays  every holidays  going on crash diets three days before going back to school.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Why did my parents keep me there? Why didn't they understand, and talk to me and think about it?I guess it was the time. And also, they were Jewish middle-class parents, who believed that is the English way of education and it is good for our boy: stiffupper lip, cold showers."
      </p>
      <p>
       ButOrleyFarm, he says, gave him his first reason to write. "I became the dormitory storyteller in the middle of the night: Jimmy and Edward, and their adventures running away fromOrleyFarm and going around the world. And that's why I found the one thing in my life that I was good at, which was narrative. I used to get beaten for talking after lights out. I was always getting bloody beaten."
      </p>
      <p>
       He wrote his first play at age 10. "Did my school encourage me? Of course not. They just ignored it. My father mocked me for wanting to be a writer. Heridiculed my ambition. But when I was 12 or 13, I was already practising my autograph for when I'd haveto sign books."
      </p>
      <p>
       He survivedOrleyFarm and was sent to Rugby School, where "three wonderful English teachers" encouraged him to develop his talent. While he was at Rugby,Horowitzheard that his headmaster atOrleyFarm was dying in hospital, and he went to his bedside. "The only reason I went to visit him was to make sure the bastard was really going to die," he says. "I just wanted to see him weak."
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        WhenHorowitzwas 22, his father diedof cancer
       </strong>
       . By then, all his father's moneyhad disappeared. "Every last penny," saysHorowitz. "So we went from being millionaires to having pretty much nothing.
      </p>
      <p>
       "My father was sort of on the edge of the law, and he put all his money into a Swiss bank, and one day  for fear that somebody might find it  he visited the Swiss bank and, as far as we know, he put the money into a suitcase, took it out of the Swiss bank and put it into another Swiss bank, and didn't tell us which one."
      </p>
      <p>
       The family never found it. "My mother went from being a socialite with a mink coat and jewellery and a huge house to being bankrupt." Her husband, it turned out, owed millions. "Everything was sold."
      </p>
      <p>
       Horowitz'smother moved into a small house with a mortgage and went to work as a secretary in a building company. "She was happier in the next 10 years than she'd ever been before. She often said that to me. She found herself as an independent person instead of just a wife. And then she got cancer and died, too."
      </p>
      <p>
       Horowitzwent to work in advertising for McCann Erickson, to help pay his mother's bills. His account director was Jill Green. "The first day we met, we had a serious row," he says. "She asked if she could work with any other writer in the building except me. I said I couldn't stand working with her. I knew, on that day, we'd get married. Because she was brilliant. She was the only person who could stand up to me."
      </p>
      <p>
       Horowitzwrote his first children's book, The Sinister Secret of Frederick K. Bower, while at the advertising agency. He thinks perhaps he was "trying to relive a childhood Ihadn'thad  At that time  and this was before J.K.Rowling  it was quite a weird thing to do. You didn't want to admit you were a children's author, especially if you were 23, male and unmarried. Nowadays, of course, kids' writers are pop stars."
      </p>
      <p>
       His first books did moderately well in a market that was considered fairly small, until Rowling blew it open in the late 1990s. His early novels included
       <em>
        GrooshamGrange
       </em>
       , in which the names of every one of his teachers atOrleyFarm was given to a monster  be it a werewolf, vampire or mummy  and each met with a grisly end.
      </p>
      <p>
       He returned toOrleyFarm once, in his 30s, to give a talk to students. He found a rebuilt, modern, day school. "But I had the closestI'veever come to a seizure. I'm not an emotional person. I'm a very easygoing, superficial person, but I felt faint, my heart was palpitating, there was cold sweat on my hands and I couldn't breathe.I really thought I was going to pass out.
      </p>
      <p>
       "And, at that single moment, it just came home to me that all these jokes, all this layering on the surface, was hiding a very real harm that had been done to me at that school. I got out as fast as I could.I'venever been back."
      </p>
      <p>
       He was an immediately successful screenwriter, and was contracted to write the Holmes and Bond books. The Holmes novels were a pleasure to write, but Bond's personality was more problematic. "You have to ask yourself, 'Do his attitudes to women stack up for a 21st-century audience?' You want to be true to the ethos of those books, but how do you create a Bond girl  a Bond woman  when Ian Fleming thought Pussy Galore was a good name?"
      </p>
      <p>
       ButHorowitzis at the Royal Festival Hall today  and the Sydney Writers' Festival next week  to talk about his kids' books, and the theatre is packed with children. "When I was eight years old," he tells them, "my parents sent me to a particularly horrible school. The school recently wrote to me, 'Stop bad-mouthing us, would you, please? If you don't, you'll hear from our lawyers.'
      </p>
      <p>
       "Anyway, the school was calledOrleyFarm and it was in north London. I'm sure it's a wonderful school now, and their lawyers can stay away. But, when I was there, it was a very horrible place"
      </p>
