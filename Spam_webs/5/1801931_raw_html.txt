     <p>
      <b>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Rizal" title="w:Jos Rizal">
        Jos Rizal
       </a>
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/June_19" title="June 19">
       June 19
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/1861" title="1861">
       1861
      </a>
      
      <a href="/wiki/December_30" title="December 30">
       December 30
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/1896" title="1896">
       1896
      </a>
      ) was a
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippines" title="w:Philippines">
       Filipino
      </a>
      nationalist, doctor, writer, and
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymath" title="w:Polymath">
       polymath
      </a>
      whose works and martyred death made him a hero of the
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Revolution" title="w:Philippine Revolution">
       Philippine Revolution
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">
       Quotes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Rizal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Quotes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In the Middle Ages, everything bad was the work of the devil, everything good, the work of God. Today, the French see everything in reverse and blame the Germans for it.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Fr. Pastells (11 November 1892)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        To doubt God is to doubt one's own conscience, and in consequence it would be to doubt everything.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Fr. Pastells (4 April 1893)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       No, let us not make God in our image, poor inhabitants that we are of a distant planet lost in infinite space. However brilliant and sublime our intelligence may be, it is scarcely more than a small spark which shines and in an instant is extinguished, and it alone can give us no idea of that blaze, that conflagration, that ocean of light.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Fr. Pastells (4 April 1893)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        I believe in revelation, but not in revelation which each religion claims to possess... but in the living revelation which surrounds us on every side  mighty, eternal, unceasing, incorruptible, clear, distinct, universal as is the being from whom it proceeds, in that revelation which speaks to us and penetrates us from the moment we are born until we die.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Fr. Pastells (4 April 1893)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Each one writes history according to his convenience.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Blumentritt, written at Leipzig,(22 August 1886)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Today is Christmas Eve. Whether or not Christ was born exactly on this date is not important. But chronological accuracy has nothing to do with tonight's event. A grand genius had been born who preached truth and love; who suffered because of his mission; and on account of his sufferings the world has become better, if not saved. Only it gives me nausea to see how some people abuse his name to commit numerous crimes. If he is in heaven, he will certainly protest!
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Blumentritt (24 December 1886)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hw-tiberius.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="570" data-file-width="357" height="351" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Hw-tiberius.jpg/220px-Hw-tiberius.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Hw-tiberius.jpg/330px-Hw-tiberius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/Hw-tiberius.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hw-tiberius.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        crowns of flowers and laurels are the inventions of free people.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Is it not sad, I said to my countrymen, that we have to learn from a foreigner about ourselves? Thanks to the German scholars we get accurate information about ourselves, and when everything in our country has been destroyed and we wish to verify the historical correctness of certain facts we shall have to come to Germany to search for these facts, in German museums and books!
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Blumentritt (13 April 1887)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       The Philippines should be grateful to you if you would write a complete history of our country from an impartial point of view.. But don't expect thanks and laurels--
       <b>
        crowns of flowers and laurels are the inventions of free people.
       </b>
       But perhaps your children may gather the fruit of what the father planted.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Blumentritt (13 April 1887)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       We want the happiness of the Philippines, but we want to obtain it through noble and just means. If I have to commit villainy to make her happy, I would refuse to do so, because I am sure that
       <b>
        what is built on sand sooner or later would tumble down.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Blumentritt, (31 January 1887)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       One only dies once, and if one does not die well, a good opportunity is lost and will not present itself again.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to Mariano Ponce, (1890)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        To live is to be among men, and to be among men is to struggle, a struggle not only with them but with oneself; with their passions, but also with one's own.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to his family, Dapitan (c. 1884)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       ...Does your Excellency know the spirit of (my) country? If you did, you would not say that I am "a spirit twisted by a German education," for the spirit that animates me I already had since childhood, before I learned a word of German. My spirit is "twisted" because I have been reared among injustices and abuses which I saw everywhere, because since a child I have seen many suffer stupidly and because I also have suffered. My "twisted spirit" is the product of that constant vision of the moral ideal that succumbs before the powerful reality of abuses, arbitrariness, hypocrisies, farces, violence, perfidies and other base passions. And "twisted" like my spirit is that of hundreds of thousands of Filipinos who have not yet left their miserable homes, who speak no other language except their own, and who, if they could write or express their thoughts, would make my
       <i>
        Noli me tangere
       </i>
       very tiny indeed, and with their volumes there would be enough to build pyramids for the corpses of all the tyrants...
       <ul>
        <li>
         Open letter to Barrantes on the
         <i>
          Noli
         </i>
         , published in
         <i>
          La Solidaridad
         </i>
         (15 February 1890)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Genius has no country. It blossoms everywhere. Genius is like the light, the air. It is the heritage of all.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Toast to the artists
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Luna" title="w:Juan Luna">
          Juan Luna
         </a>
         and
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Resurreccion_Hidalgo" title="w:Felix Resurreccion Hidalgo">
          Felix Hidalgo
         </a>
         : Madrid, Spain (25 June 1884)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <br/>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       It was a world which granted privileges to some and imposed prohibitions on others...Endowed with strength and eager to learn, one had to drag himself in a narrow prison cell when he could see an open field, a vast horizon in the distance; when he could feel the beatings of a heart; and
       <b>
        when he believed himself entitled to enjoy the beauty of a dream.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         "Laughter and Tears", an essay (c.1884)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Friar! What a strange name. I don't remember having created such a thing! (God speaking to the angel Gabriel)
       <ul>
        <li>
         "The Lord Gazes at the Philippine Islands", an allegory. (date unknown)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Filipinos don't realize that victory is the child of struggle, that joy blossoms from suffering, and redemption is a product of sacrifice.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         "Como se gobiernan las Filipinas" (How one governs in the Philippines), published in
         <i>
          La Solidaridad
         </i>
         (15 December 1890)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Death has always been the first sign of European civilization when introduced in the Pacific.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Annotations to Morga's
         <i>
          Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas
         </i>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        No one has a monopoly of the true God, nor is there a nation or religion that can claim, or at any rate prove, that it has been given the exclusive right to the Creator or sole knowledge of His Being.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Annotations to Morga's
         <i>
          Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas
         </i>
         - translated by Austin Craig
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>

     <ul>
      <li>
       The sea, the sea is everything! Its sovereign mass
       <br/>
       brings to me atoms of a myriad faraway lands;
       <br/>
       Its bright smile animates me in the limpid mornings
       <br/>
       And when at the end of day my faith has failed me
       <br/>
       My heart echoes the sound of its sorrow in the sands.
       <br/>
       <ul>
        <li>
         "Mi Retiro", st.6 - translated by Nick Joaquin.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        The world laughs at another man's pain.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         "Song of the Wanderer", st.8 - translated by Nick Joaquin.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        He who would love much has also much to suffer.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         "To My__" (December 1890)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       Muse who in the past inspired me to sing of the throes of love:
       <br/>
       Go and repose.
       <br/>
       What I need is a sword, rivers of gold,
       <br/>
       and acrid prose.
       <br/>
       <ul>
        <li>
         "To My__" (December 1890)- translated by Nick Joaquin
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>

        No good water comes from a muddy spring. No sweet fruit comes from a bitter seed.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Letter to the Young Women of Malolos
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
