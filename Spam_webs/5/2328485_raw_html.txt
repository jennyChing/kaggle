        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         5 Things to Do in Iloilo, the City of Love
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-09-06T23:53:56+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          September 6, 2014
         </time>
         by
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/author/aleah/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Aleah
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="entry-comments-link">
          <a href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/2014/09/things-to-do-iloilo/#comments">
           30 Comments
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
        <!-- Quick Adsense WordPress Plugin: http://quicksense.net/ -->
        <div style="float:left;margin:10px 10px 10px 0;">
         <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
         </script>
         <!-- 300x250image -->
         <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7829765565657609" data-ad-slot="7805810141" style="display:inline-block;width:300px;height:250px">

        <p>
         <em>
          From food trips and a tour of their cultural heritage, there are so many things you can do in Iloilo City that spending a few days there definitely isnt enough.
          <br/>
         </em>
         <br/>
         I cant believe I havent made it to Panay until this year. The island, comprising the provinces of Aklan, Antique, Capiz, and Iloilo, turned out to be full of surprises.
        </p>
        <p>
         I loved it so much that just over a month after the
         <a href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/tag/city-of-love/" target="_blank" title="city of love">
          <strong>
           Blog the City of Love
          </strong>
         </a>
         <strong>
          Campaign
         </strong>
         was done, I booked another ticket back to the island.
        </p>
        <p>
         Thanks to the sponsorship of
         <a href="https://www.facebook.com/injaptowerhotel" target="_blank" title="Injap">
          <strong>
           Injap Tower Hotel
          </strong>
         </a>
         , I (together with other blogger participants) was able to appreciate all of Iloilos charms, not just its food which Id heard a lot of, but more importantly, its cultural heritage that it has managed to preserve through the years.
        </p>
        <p>
         There are so many things to do in Iloilo. If you should ever find yourself here, the following are just some of my suggestions in the City of Love.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/lizares.jpg">
          <img alt="lizares" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6141" height="461" src="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/lizares.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          1. Visit Jaro Cathedral and the miraculous Our Lady of the Candles
          <br/>
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         While the province of Iloilo has a LOT of old churches that are worth checking out, theres one in the city itself that you should visit. The
         <strong>
          Jaro Cathedral
         </strong>
         , originally built in 1874, has an additional distinction aside from its age.
        </p>
        <p>
         The image of
         <strong>
          Our Lady of the Candles
         </strong>
         is displayed on the front facade. Its reportedly the only Marian figure in the Philippines being canonically crowned by the late Pope John Paul II.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972113238" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5595/14972113238_aea9856b43_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Devotees say that Our Lady grants wishes. While not exactly religious, I paid homage to Our Lady when we were there. Wishes or no, I was just grateful to have arrived in Iloilo safe and sound.
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          2. Admire the grandeur of Lizares Mansion and the beauty of Casa Mariquit
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         When the sugar industry was at its peak, a lot of families in Iloilo became ultra-rich. Their wealth were reflected on their houses, and some of these old mansions and ancestral homes still exist today. Two of such homes we visited were the
         <strong>
          Lizares Mansion
         </strong>
         and
         <strong>
          Casa Mariquit
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <strong>
          Lizares Mansion
         </strong>
         , now a property of the Angelicum School, was built in 1937 by Don Emiliano Lizares for his family. See how big and imposing it is? Its even grander inside, and it just shows the luxurious life of the sugar barons in their day.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/lizares-mansion.jpg">
          <img alt="lizares-mansion" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6142" height="461" src="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/lizares-mansion.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Unfortunately, World War II came and the family had to leave it to seek refuge in another town. The Japanese used the mansion as their headquarters and reportedly tortured, killed, and buried Filipino prisoners of war in the basement.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/15155709731" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" class=" aligncenter" height="461" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5579/15155709731_95cd8e1548_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Locals tell of it being haunted, of hearing things that go bump in the night and of seeing people that werent really there. According to the priest, though, such manifestations dont bother them. They got the property at a huge discount and turned it into a chapel.
        </p>
        <p>
         I would have loved to see the upstairs rooms; unfortunately, the huge winding staircase was closed to the public. I didnt see the basement, too. The priest said it got filled up with sand, silt, and clay when typhoons battered the city.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/15158298512" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" class=" aligncenter" height="461" src="https://farm4.staticflickr.com/3910/15158298512_8ca4e4f673_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Another old house in Iloilo is the 200-year-old
         <strong>
          Casa Mariquit
         </strong>
         , the house built for Mariquit Javellana Lopez by her father Ramon Javellana. Despite its age, its still very well-maintained, with the hardwood floors scrubbed, chandeliers polished, and the paintings and other memorabilia kept clean.
        </p>
        <p>
         I loved the staircase, as well as the old mirror in the second floor. What must it be like to be a member of such a wealthy family?
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972018880" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" class=" aligncenter" height="502" src="https://farm4.staticflickr.com/3863/14972018880_ae491f2b1c_b.jpg" width="713"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         This ancestral home, located along Isabel St. in Jaro, is one of the few in Iloilo that are open to the public. The caretaker lives in the house behind it; when you get there, just go to the back and he will let you in. Admission: Php50/person.
        </p>
        <p>
         Trivia: Doa Mariquit was the wife of Fernando Lopez, the former Vice President of the Philippines for three terms!
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          3. Stroll along Calle Real
          <br/>
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Calle Real
         </strong>
         , Iloilos heritage street, is a beautiful stretch of restored old establishments that are still being used to this day. If you look closely, you can see the year the buildings were built right on the front facade of each structure.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/calle-real.jpg">
          <img alt="calle real" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6144" height="461" src="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/calle-real.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         It really makes me happy to see governments restoring rather than destroying heritage sites. If that street were in Manila, all the buildings would probably have been torn down already to make way for more SM malls or condominiums.
        </p>
        <p>
         According to
         <a href="http://www.exploreiloilo.com/" target="_blank" title="Iloilo">
          Explore Iloilo
         </a>
         ,
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          Most of the commercial and residential establishments are of one or two-story buildings which display European and American of the late 19th and early 20th century influence such as art-nouveau, art-deco, baroque, Venetian-inspired or platoresque. The first levels are used as shops or stores and the second level are used as either offices but mostly as residential spaces.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         The best time to walk around Calle Real is on Sundays, when its closed to vehicular traffic from 3-9pm.
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          4. Go on a food trip
          <br/>
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972113508" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5595/14972113508_86c7262210_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         All visits to Iloilo City should always begin and end with a food trip. If you feel like having Singaporean food, head to
         <strong>
          <a href="https://www.facebook.com/horizoncafe.com.ph" target="_blank" title="Horizon Cafe">
           Horizon Caf
          </a>
         </strong>
         at Injap Tower Hotel. Their Chicken Hainanese is to die for. I also loved their version of cendol, ngoh hiang (pork rolls), and kopi. Plus, the view is priceless!
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/15158299022" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5593/15158299022_5fc401f848_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Another restaurant I liked was
         <strong>
          <a href="https://www.facebook.com/bistrocarcosa" target="_blank" title="carcosa">
           Bistro Carcosa
          </a>
         </strong>
         in The Uptown Place. It has good views of the riverreportedly perfect for sunset viewingbut better yet, the food is excellent and affordable. I had the carnitas taco which was called a snack. With its size though (P90), its a meal in itself!
        </p>
        <p>
         For cheap, fresh seafood, videoke, and drinking,
         <strong>
          Villa Beach
         </strong>
         is the place to go. Locals have told me its not good to swim there, but its definitely recommended as a place to eat and enjoy the company of friends.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/madge-cafe-coffee.jpg">
          <img alt="madge-cafe-coffee" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6145" height="461" src="http://www.solitarywanderer.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/madge-cafe-coffee.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Want authentic Iloilo brewed coffee for only P25, served in a can? Head to
         <strong>
          Madge Caf
         </strong>
         . No, its not another Starbucks-lookalike; its actually much better than any high-end coffee shop out there!
        </p>
        <p>
         True, its not air-conditioned, but nothing can beat the ambiance of Madge. Its located inside the La Paz Public Market (ask where it is as you cant see it from outside), and your coffee will be brewed the old-fashioned way as you watch.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972110007" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm4.staticflickr.com/3915/14972110007_c31e0070cd_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         You will definitely not see any Keurig coffeemaker being used here, just old-fashioned straining. I would recommend ordering the strong coffee with milk. It was so delicious I had two!
        </p>
        <p>
         If, on the other hand, you want to be more adventurous with your food, have a taste of unique gelato flavors from Nothing But Desserts, located in Smallville, Iloilos center of nightlife. There are several flavors, all at P70 per cup, including Chili Gelato, Bittergourd (Ampalaya), and Ginger. They really taste like the real thing! Strawberry and Chocolate are available as well.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972668658" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5552/14972668658_1657e2f4a9_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972516729" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm4.staticflickr.com/3880/14972516729_4d80bb37a4_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         I loved several offerings from Nothing but Desserts: the mango oatmeal cookie (P15), Buko Royale (P95), and Mango Mystic (P75). I can eat here several times without breaking the bank, and their brewed coffee is good, too.
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          5. Have a massage
          <br/>
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         For me, no trip is complete without having a massage. I would highly recommend the Spa Circle at the 5th floor of Injap Tower Hotel. You would think its expensive, being located in a hotel, right?
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/67117584@N04/14972016230" title="Iloilo City by Aleah Phils, on Flickr">
          <img alt="Iloilo City" height="461" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5559/14972016230_bbfbce7977_b.jpg" width="691"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         An hour of my favorite massage, thougha combination of Thai (stretching) and Swedish (oil)only cost P400. Definitely a steal.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         ***
        </p>
        <p>
         I only spent three days in Iloilo, a very short time given the number of things you can do there. Im really looking forward to going back and experiencing more of what the City of Love can offer!
        </p>

           How about you? What do you recommend in Iloilo City?
