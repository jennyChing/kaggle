       <div class="article-body--text ">
        <div class="ad-gpt-main">
         <div class="gpt-tag" id="gpt-ad-vertical-top">
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Unless you're Johnny Depp in
          <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2-frM2Lf8KE" target="_blank">
           <em>
            A Nightmare on Elm Street
           </em>
          </a>
         </strong>
         , you're likely not afraid of beds. And watching
         <em>
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Death-Bed-The-That-Eats/dp/B0001XAKQK" target="_blank">
           Death Bed: The Bed That Eats
          </a>
         </em>
         will do little to change that. The saga of a bed that, well, eats people, director George Barry's 1977 indie is the worst film (
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PE45groHd6Q" target="_blank">
          you can watch the whole thing here
         </a>
         ) that got lost over time, worthy of being ranked alongside
         <em>
          Plan 9 from Outer Space
         </em>
         and
         <a href="http://www.esquire.com/blogs/culture/the-room-anniversary-tommy-wiseau">
          <em>
           The Room
          </em>
         </a>
         as a true classic of amazing awfulness.
         <em>
          Death Bed
         </em>
         isn't generally included in discussions of the lousiest film ever, primarily because, thanks to distributor disinterest, it never made it into theaters. That failure seemed to signal the project's ultimate demise, and it did, at least for 26 years. Yet in 2001, Barry was perusing some online message boards and discovered that one of the distributors who'd originally passed on the film had, in fact, made a VHS copy, which had led to a host of European bootlegs and midnight-movie exhibitions  la
         <em>
          The Rocky Horror Picture Show
         </em>
         . Driven to see his only directorial effort properly resurrected, Barry finally secured the film a 2003 DVD release courtesy of Cult Epics, thereby bringing to the masses one of the most execrable pieces of horror cinema to ever grace a screen.
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-breaker-container">
         <div class="ad-gpt-breaker ad-article-breaker-mobile">
          <div class="ad-article-breaker-text">
           Advertisement - Continue Reading Below
          </div>
          <div class="gpt-tag" id="gpt-ad-vertical-top-mobile">
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         Or, at least, Barry brought it to the attention of comedian Patton Oswalt, who raised its pop-culture profile by targeting it in a rant on his 2007
         <em>
          Werewolves and Lollipops
         </em>
         album. Oswalt
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=01l1WIC9mBo" target="_blank">
          fumed
         </a>
         over the fact that so many cinematic endeavors remain unrealized while
         <em>
          Death Bed
         </em>
         found its way into production. Now slated to be
         <a href="http://www.whatsonwilmington.com/event.php?id=17182" target="_blank">
          adapted for the stage
         </a>
         in Wilmington, North Carolina, later this year by playwright Gwenyfar Rohler, whose motivation came in part from the fact that she's friends with Jock Brandis, who originally constructed the bed (and will be her collaborator on this jokey theatrical version),
         <em>
          Death Bed
         </em>
         practically begs to be turned into a joke, in large part because it takes itself surprisingly seriously. For a story about a demonically possessed four-poster king bed that consumes humans  as well as fruit, wine, fried chicken, suitcases, and
         <span>
          Pepto
         </span>
         <span>
          -
         </span>
         <span>
          Bismol
         </span>
         (!)  and which is split into the tongue-in-cheek segment titles "Breakfast," "Lunch," "Dinner," and "The Just Desserts," Barry's film is a frequently straight-faced affair. Rarely has carnivorous furniture been treated with such gravity, and thus elicited greater unintentional chuckles.
        </p>

        <p>
         <em>
          Death Bed
         </em>
         opens with 55 seconds of a black screen and the sound of munching, because as is soon revealed, the bed in question makes
         <em>
          CHOMP CHOMP CHOMP
         </em>
         sounds while devouring its prey. The sound of chewing is especially baffling since the bed's digestive system involves using yellow foam, not teeth, to suck its victims into its mattress, whose interior is visualized as a pool of acid-like yellow liquid. This, of course, makes no sense, but then neither does the bed's snoring and laughter. Or, for that matter, that it used to live in a city, but now resides in a giant countryside manor that it soon destroys, leaving it for a stone cellar that's otherwise decorated with only a wall painting of the bed.
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         <div class="gpt-tag" id="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         Behind that painting lives the ghost of one of the bed's early victims, a Victorian artist whose whispered voiceover (provided by Patrick Spence-Thomas) elucidates the bed's history. It turns out, the bed was created by a demon who took human form to have sex with a beauty it wanted as its bride. When the demon was spurned, it cried tears of blood on what would have been their marital bed, which then turned the bed into a person-eating monster. I know, I know  it's a tale as old as time. But
         <em>
          Death Bed
         </em>
         ups its weirdness by lacing its action with recurring lesbian elements, most notably when two lovers wind up at the cellar and proceed to ostracize their third-wheel friend by sharing lusty looks with each other. Coming on the heels of other sexual scenes marked by gratuitous toplessness, such woman-on-woman eroticism thrusts the proceedings further into the realm of porn-y titillation, all of it scored, naturally, to the sound of the bed panting heavily as it watches women undress, or disrobes them itself.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Death Bed
         </em>
         indulges in more mythological mumbo-jumbo as it makes its way to a finale of blood rituals, unholy rebirths, and all sorts of hushed dialogue from the artist specter, who doesn't move his mouth while speaking because, well, he's a lazy ghost? Barry's film is shot in cruddy full-frame 16mm, awash in yellow-goo effects, and severely in need of some editorial tidying up, as evidenced by a late sequence in which a woman attempts to escape from and is then pulled back into the bed, which goes on for about 10 times longer than necessary. A laughable mixture of gore, nudity, and bewildering supernatural silliness that's imbued with the passion of a genre enthusiast but crafted with less skill than your average Z-grade cheapie, it's a work of staggering dreadfulness: too inane and incompetent to love, and too insanely out-there to ever forget.
        </p>
