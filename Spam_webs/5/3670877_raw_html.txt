       <h1>
        Materials that shrink when heated
       </h1>
       <h5 class="data">
        February 9, 2012
       </h5>
      </header>
      <section class="content-holder content-table content-story">
       <!--begin:content-->
       <section class="news-content">
        <!--begin:article-block-->
        <section class="content article-block">
         <article>
          <div class="first-block">
           <figure class="image-block">
            <div class="image-block-ins">
             <a href="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/gfx/news/hires/2012/netflixlight.jpg" rel="lightbox" title="">
              <img alt="Netflix light on flicks as viewers soak up TV shows" src="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/csz/news/800/2012/netflixlight.jpg"/>
             </a>
            </div>
            <figcaption class="image-block-caption">
             Invar, also known as FeNi36, is an iron-nickel alloy notable for its lack of expansion or contraction with temperature changes.
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            One common reason that people with fillings experience toothache is that their fillings expand at a different rate to the original tooth when, for example, drinking a hot drink. Contrary to intuition, however, not all materials expand when heated - some actually contract. Recent research on these so-called negative thermal expansion (NTE) materials has led to the discovery of alloys exhibiting unexpectedly large thermal contraction.
           </p>
          </div>
          <!-- 1st block -->
          <div class="article-banner first-banner">
           <script language="JavaScript">
            &lt;!--

            var ad_channel_cmn = '+0559369967', google_hints="";

           </script>
          </div>
          <p>
           Controlling the thermal expansion of composites is important for producing nanometer-scale
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/electronic+circuits/" rel="tag">
            electronic circuits
           </a>
           , as well as the next-generation fuel cells and
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/thermoelectric+devices/" rel="tag">
            thermoelectric devices
           </a>
           . An ability to combine NTE materials with 'normal' materials which expand upon heating ensures a reduction in thermal expansion in a composite material - something that people with tooth fillings would appreciate. An example of such a composite is Invar, an iron-nickel alloy with a uniquely low coefficient of thermal expansion. As a result it is used where high dimensional stability is required, such as precision instruments, clocks or seismic creep gauges.
          </p>
          <p>
           Koshi Takenaka at the Department of
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/crystalline+materials/" rel="tag">
            Crystalline Materials
           </a>
           Science, Nagoya University in Japan works on NTE materials for practical applications. In the latest issue of
           <i>
            Science and Technology of Advanced Materials
           </i>
           he summarizes the physical mechanisms governing NTE with emphasis on recent developments.
          </p>
          <p>
           Takenaka notes that, "NTE materials will expand our capability of thermal-expansion control, opening a new paradigm of materials science and technology thermal-expansion-adjustable composites". One challenge facing the scientist is that the addition of NTE materials to composites leads to undesirable instabilities at interfaces. New methods for producing stable interfaces between the host composite and NTE compensators are of critical importance. Nevertheless, the so-called 'one-component' materials - such as manganese antiperovskites, zirconium vanadates, and
           <a class="textTag" href="http://www.physorg.com/tags/hafnium/" rel="tag">
            hafnium
           </a>
           tungstates - exhibiting negligible thermal expansion offer a promising route towards achieving this goal.
          </p>
