        <h1 itemprop="name">
         Do autopilots give pilots a false sense of security?
        </h1>
        <div class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
         <span class="byline__author-headshot">
          <img alt="DEFAULT" class="bio__img" src="/content/dam/images/h/v/f/4/t/image.imgtype.columnistThumbnail.50x50.png/1408400814901.png" title=""/>

        <p>
         Autopilots, or autoflight systems as some pilots prefer to call them, are used to varying degrees during a flight, depending on aircraft type, the operating environment, the airline's own operating procedures and the individual pilots.
        </p>
        <p>
         These computer-based systems are much better than humans at performing some of the tasks that are required to get an aircraft safely and efficiently from A to B, for example navigation.
        </p>
        <p>
         Beyond question, autoflight systems have contributed to improvements in flight safety. Even when these systems are engaged, the pilot still performs a number of important functions vital to the safe management of the aircraft.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, they can also allow pilots to mentally disconnect, leading to a loss of situational awareness. A recent flight simulator study conducted by NASA's Ames Research Center on a group of pilots, each with an average of 18,000 hours of flight time, concluded that reliance on autoflight systems had eroded the pilots' ability to make complex cognitive decisions.
        </p>
        <p>
         Further, the more automated a flight was, the more pilots' minds tended to wander, leading to a kind of attention deficit disorder that can prove fatal.
        </p>
        <p>
         In 2009, an Air France Airbus A330 bound from Rio de Janeiro to Paris ran into a storm over the Atlantic. When its air-speed sensors became coated with ice they began giving false readings, causing the autopilot to disengage and alert the pilot, which is exactly what is programmed to do. However instead of putting the nose down and increasing airspeed, the pilot put the aircraft into a climb. By this time airspeed sensors were again functioning and signalling a potentially catastrophic loss of airspeed yet the pilot continued to climb, and the resulting stall caused the aircraft to plunge into the ocean with the loss of all 228 passengers and crew.
        </p>
        <p>
         This followed a parallel incident just a few months earlier when a Continental Connection flight over New York crashed killing all 49 on board. In yet another example, in 1983 a Korean Air Lines plane was shot down by Soviet aircraft when it drifted 300 miles off course. The official report attributed the crash to the crew's lack of alertness.
        </p>
        <p>
         In this context, it seems appropriate to ask is this what happened to Malaysia Airlines flight MH370? Were the pilots asleep at the wheel?
        </p>
