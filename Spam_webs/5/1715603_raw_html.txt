        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         Summer Skincare Fundamentals
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-07-16T10:39:12+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          July 16, 2014
         </time>
         by
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/author/healthperch/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Health Perch

         </span>

        <h2 style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/summer-skincare-header.jpg">
          <img alt="Summer Skin Care Fundamentals" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1075" height="319" src="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/summer-skincare-header.jpg" width="1068"/>
         </a>
        </h2>
        <h2 style="text-align: center;">
         Keep your skin beautiful and healthy in warm weather
        </h2>
        <p>
         For the majority of the country, summer equals lots of sunshine, extra time outdoors, and more exposed skin. While a healthy summer tan is still thought of as widely desirable by many in the U.S., any change in a persons natural skin color is a sign of skin damage. Before you spend time in the garden, at the beach, or on a hike, find out the meaning behind SPF, how to properly apply sunscreen, and what to eat to protect skin from the inside out.
        </p>
        <h3>
         <strong>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/skin-cancer-information/skin-cancer-facts#general" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/skin-cancer-information/skin-cancer-facts#general', 'Skin Cancer: The Naked Truth']);">
            <span style="color: #000000;">
             Skin Cancer: The Naked Truth
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
          <br/>
         </strong>
        </h3>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Skin cancer is the most common form of cancer in the U.S. More than
          <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/skin-cancer-information/skin-cancer-facts#general" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/skin-cancer-information/skin-cancer-facts#general', 'two million']);">
           two million
          </a>
          people are diagnosed with it annually.
         </li>
         <li>
          One in 50 people will be diagnosed with melanoma of the skin during their lifetime.
         </li>
         <li>
          About 86 percent of melanomas can be attributed to exposure to UV radiation from the sun.
         </li>
         <li>
          The risk for melanoma doubles if a person has had more than five sunburns in his or her lifetime.
         </li>
         <li>
          More than 170,000 cases of non-melanoma skin cancer in the U.S. each year are associated with indoor tanning.
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12472495" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12472495', 'Avoiding sun']);">
           Avoiding sun
          </a>
          in the first place is the only tried and true way to steer clear of melanoma.
         </li>
         <li>
          A ten-year study found daily use of an
          <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/news/sunscreen/daily-sunscreen-use-cuts-melanoma-risk-in-half-study-finds" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/news/sunscreen/daily-sunscreen-use-cuts-melanoma-risk-in-half-study-finds', 'SPF 16 sunscreen reduced melanoma']);">
           SPF 16 sunscreen reduced melanoma
          </a>
          incidence by half.
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/cancer/skin/basic_info/prevention.htm" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cdc.gov/cancer/skin/basic_info/prevention.htm', 'Between 10am and 4pm ']);">
           Between 10am and 4pm
          </a>
          is the most hazardous for UV exposure in the continental U.S.
         </li>
         <li>
          Some medications such as
          <a href="http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','download','http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf']);">
           antibiotics and antihistamines
          </a>
          can increase a persons sensitivity to the sun.
         </li>
         <li>
          Currently, there are
          <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/uva-and-uvb/understanding-uva-and-uvb" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/uva-and-uvb/understanding-uva-and-uvb', '17 active ingredients']);">
           17 active ingredients
          </a>
          approved by the FDA for use in sunscreens.
         </li>
         <li>
          Beyond cancer risk, UV radiation induces other harmful changes to the skin including
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16613515" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16613515', 'thickening, wrinkle formation, and inflammation.']);">
           thickening, wrinkle formation, and inflammation.
          </a>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <h3>
         <a href="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/summer-skin-care-001.png">
          <img alt="Summer Skin Care Statistics" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1072" height="647" src="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/summer-skin-care-001.png" width="1068"/>
         </a>
        </h3>
        <h3>
         <strong>
          SPF to UV: The Letters You Need to Know
         </strong>
        </h3>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          <a href="http://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/melanoma/basics/definition/con-20026009" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/melanoma/basics/definition/con-20026009', 'Melanoma:']);">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            <strong>
             Melanoma:
            </strong>
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         The most serious type of skin cancer, melanoma develops in melanocytes, the cells that produce melanin, the pigment that colors skin. Melanoma usually forms on skin, but can also affect eyes and internal organs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          <strong>
           <a href="http://ghr.nlm.nih.gov/glossary=ultravioletrays" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://ghr.nlm.nih.gov/glossary=ultravioletrays', 'UV Radiation:']);">
            <span style="color: #000000;">
             UV Radiation:
            </span>
           </a>
          </strong>
         </span>
         A form of energy produced by the sun, this known carcinogen can damage the skin, promote premature aging, and weaken the immune system.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          UVA:
         </strong>
         Also known as long-wave rays, UVA penetrates the skin more deeply than UVB rays and accounts for up to
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10907526" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10907526', '95 percent']);">
          95 percent
         </a>
         of the UV radiation reaching the Earths surface. These rays can also pass through
         <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/uva-and-uvb/understanding-uva-and-uvb" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/uva-and-uvb/understanding-uva-and-uvb', 'clouds and glass.']);">
          clouds and glass.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/uva-and-uvb/understanding-uva-and-uvb" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/uva-and-uvb/understanding-uva-and-uvb', 'UVB:']);">
            <span style="color: #000000;">
             UVB:
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
         </strong>
         Also known as shortwave rays, UVB is the primary culprit for sunburn and damages the more superficial layers of the skin. UVB rays are especially damaging at high altitudes and off of reflective surfaces such as snow or ice, which explains that mid-winter ski-goggle sunburn.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="color: #000000;">
           <a href="http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','download','http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf']);">
            <span style="color: #000000;">
             UV Index:
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
         </strong>
         The intensity of the suns rays can vary with the changing weather forecast. When the UV index is especially high for particular locations, the Environmental Protection Agency issues a UV alert. Check out
         <a href="http://www2.epa.gov/sunwise/uv-index" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www2.epa.gov/sunwise/uv-index', 'epa.gov']);">
          epa.gov
         </a>
         to monitor the index for your hometown on a day-by-day basis.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Broad Spectrum:
         </strong>
         A sunscreen with broad spectrum on the label means it protects against UVB and UVA radiation.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          SPF:
         </strong>
         SPF stands for sun protection factor. A sunscreens
         <a href="http://www.tga.gov.au/consumers/sunscreens-2012.htm#.U3oqJVhdV3w" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.tga.gov.au/consumers/sunscreens-2012.htm#.U3oqJVhdV3w', 'level of protection']);">
          level of protection
         </a>
         is measured by determining how much time it takes for intense ultraviolet radiation to burn skin thats been liberally applied with sunscreen compared to unprotected skin. If it takes 150 seconds to burn with the sunscreen, and only 10 seconds without it, the SPF is 150/10, or 15. When applied liberally and frequently,
         <a href="http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','download','http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf']);">
          SPF 15
         </a>
         protects the skin from 93 percent of UVB radiation while SPF 30 protects against 97 percent. While sunscreens as high as
         <a href="http://www.ewg.org/2013sunscreen/fda-fails-consumers/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ewg.org/2013sunscreen/fda-fails-consumers/', 'SPF 100']);">
          SPF 100
         </a>
         are available in the U.S. market, there are some potential downsides. People who use higher-SPF products may wait too long to reapply sunscreen and expose skin to the sun for longer. They may also have a false sense of security that their added protection lasts all day.Plus, because higher SPFs offer far
         <a href="http://www.ewg.org/2013sunscreen/whats-wrong-with-high-spf/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ewg.org/2013sunscreen/whats-wrong-with-high-spf/', 'less protection']);">
          less protection
         </a>
         against UVA rays versus UVB, the risk for sunburn may be reduced, but other types of sun damage may not be.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','download','http://www.epa.gov/sunwise/doc/sunscreen.pdf']);">
            <span style="color: #000000;">
             Skin Type:
            </span>
           </a>
          </strong>
         </span>
         The Food and Drug Administration classifies skin type on a scale of one to six. Those with types one and two have fair skin and tend to burn quickly and more severely while those with five and six have darker skin and do not burn easily. Though some skin types are more susceptible to skin damage, everyone is subject to the harmful effects of sun exposure regardless of race or ethnicity.
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          <a href="http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/sun-protection/sunscreen/the-skin-cancer-foundations-guide-to-sunscreens" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.skincancer.org/prevention/sun-protection/sunscreen/the-skin-cancer-foundations-guide-to-sunscreens', 'Application Guidelines']);">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            <strong>
             Application Guidelines
            </strong>
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         Sunscreen is only as effective as its SPF ranking so long as its applied properly. Read these tips to get the most out of your sun protection.
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Start fresh every year.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Since the active ingredients in sunscreen can deteriorate over time, the same bottle youve been using for three years straight may not be as effective. Start with a fresh one every spring. If youre applying it frequently and liberally, youll need a new one every year anyway.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Choose broad-spectrum.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Remember, you want to protect against both UVB and UVA radiation.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Apply ahead of time.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Sunscreen should be lathered on to dry skin about 30 minutes before heading outdoors for periods of 20 minutes or more. Some people may want to use sunscreen for any amount of time in the sun.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Dont forget the little things.
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Legs, back, face: Check. Its easy to remember to lather up most areas of the body, but ears, tops of the feet, lips, and the back of the neck sometimes are forgotten.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Apply frequently and liberally.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Every two hours, reapply. As a rule of thumb, dermatologists suggest about an ounce of sunscreen (thats enough to fill a shot glass) for the average adult to cover the exposed areas of the body.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Dont rely just on that SPF.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Sunscreen isnt a cure-all, especially when the UV index is especially high. If youll be exposed to the sun for an extended period of timefor example, a day at the beachspend some time in the shade or bring along protective clothing, sunglasses, and a hat.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Remember waterproof isnt foolproof.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Labels promoting water resistant, sweat resistant, and waterproof can be
          <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3543289/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3543289/', 'misleading.']);">
           misleading.
          </a>
          These types of products may add extra skin protection while swimming and staying active outdoors, but they still need to be reapplied.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           No sun? Still wear it.
           <br/>
          </strong>
          Even if the suns not out or if its not particularly hot, ultraviolet light can still penetrate clouds and burn skin.
         </li>
        </ol>
        <h3>
         <strong>
          Eat This: Sun Protective Food Groups
         </strong>
        </h3>
        <p>
         While sunscreen should still be an important part of your summer skin care regime, research suggests regularly eating some of our favorite foods can add extra armor against the suns powerful rays.
        </p>
        <h5>
         <strong>
          Flavonoids
         </strong>
        </h5>
        <p>
         Research says:
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7948931" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7948931', 'Flavonoids']);">
          Flavonoids
         </a>
         , especially
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24139463" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24139463', 'catechins']);">
          catechins
         </a>
         (found primarily in green and black tea), protect against
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23351338" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23351338', 'sunburn inflammation']);">
          sunburn inflammation
         </a>
         and longer-term
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3263051/#ref77" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3263051/#ref77', 'UV radiation damage.']);">
          UV radiation damage.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Where to find it: Green Tea, Black Tea, Dark Chocolate, Blueberries, Cherries, Hazelnuts, Pecans
        </p>
        <h5>
         <strong>
          Beta Carotene
         </strong>
        </h5>
        <p>
         Research says:
         <a href="http://umm.edu/health/medical/altmed/condition/erythema" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://umm.edu/health/medical/altmed/condition/erythema', 'Carotenoids']);">
          Carotenoids
         </a>
         , including
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23053552" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23053552', 'beta carotene']);">
          beta carotene
         </a>
         , guard the skin against UV-induced erythema (aka redness).
        </p>
        <p>
         Where to find it: Carrots, Sweet Potatoes, Spinach, Kale, Butternut Squash, Cantaloupe, Broccoli
        </p>
        <h5>
         <strong>
          Lycopene
         </strong>
        </h5>
        <p>
         Research says: This
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23053552" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23053552', 'potent antioxidant ']);">
          potent antioxidant
         </a>
         helps diminish sensitivity to
         <a href="http://ajcn.nutrition.org/content/71/3/795.full" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://ajcn.nutrition.org/content/71/3/795.full', 'ultraviolet light.']);">
          ultraviolet light.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Where to find it: Tomatoes, Guavas, Watermelon, Red Peppers, Grapefruit, Carrots, Mangoes.
        </p>
        <h5>
         <strong>
          Omega-3 fatty acids
         </strong>
        </h5>
        <p>
         Research says: Though the skin-protective effects of
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24265065" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24265065', 'Omega-3s ']);">
          Omega-3s
         </a>
         are less researched than other antioxidants such as carotenoids, recent studies suggest the
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16872755" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16872755', ' fatty acids']);">
          fatty acids
         </a>
         help prevent
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21569104" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21569104', 'skin cancer.']);">
          skin cancer.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Where to find it: Walnuts, Salmon, Sardines, Flax, Soybeans, Brussels Sprouts, Shrimp
        </p>
        <h5>
         <strong>
          Polyphenols
         </strong>
        </h5>
        <p>
         Research says: Snacking on
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3288507/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3288507/', 'polyphenol-rich foods']);">
          polyphenol-rich foods
         </a>
         may be an effective strategy to supplement sunscreen protection.
        </p>
        <p>
         Where to find it: Tea, Red Wine, Blueberries, Coffee, Strawberries, Cherries, Blackberries, Dark Chocolate
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/summer-skin-care-002.png">
          <img alt="Eat This: Sun Protective Food Groups" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1073" height="2498" src="http://www.northwestpharmacy.com/healthperch/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/summer-skin-care-002.png" width="1068"/>
         </a>
         <strong>
          Cooling the Burn
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         While proper sun protection is incredibly important in order to help you stay safe all year long, sunburns happen. If you are spend too much
         <a href="http://www.redbookmag.com/beauty-fashion/tips-advice/summer-skin-care-tips-from-dermatologists#slide-2" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.redbookmag.com/beauty-fashion/tips-advice/summer-skin-care-tips-from-dermatologists#slide-2', 'time in the sun']);">
          time in the sun
         </a>
         , dermatologists suggest wearing loose, breathable clothing (like cotton) and soothing your skin with a washcloth soaked in cool whole milk. Over-the-counter pain relievers such as
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0003711/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmedhealth/PMH0003711/', 'ibuprofen']);">
          ibuprofen
         </a>
         can help ease the sting and reduce swelling. So long as the skin is not blistering, moisturizing cream may help relieve discomfort as well. And while
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16623024" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16623024', 'aloe vera']);">
          aloe vera
         </a>
         is often touted the go-to treatment for sunburn, the jurys still out on its
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19218914" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19218914', 'effectiveness.']);">
          effectiveness.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         At the end of the day, especially a sun-filled one, taking the right preventive measures against the suns harmful rays is incredibly important, both in the short term and the long term. Caring for our skin should be a lifelong endeavor. For more than just wrinkle protection, its never too early to use daily sunscreen to help prevent skin cancer.
        </p>

        </p>
