        <div class="post-body__content">
         <p>
          Things aren't how they used to be. In the past, programs with limited functions made it easier to pinpoint which tools you should be learning as a beginner. These days, however, given how many tools, tips and tricks there are to learn, you can often feel overwhelmed and not know where to start.
         </p>
         <p>
          I've been using Adobe Illustrator for well over 10 years now and
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/the-life-of-a-tuts-editor-meet-sharon-milne--cms-22754" target="_self">
           I'm obsessed with vector
          </a>
          . I'm here to share with you my 10 essential tips and tools all Adobe Illustrator beginners should learn, and link you to a tutorial or two which will help you learn how to use them. These items were all tools and tips which have become part of my regular vector process and without them, I think I'd wither and die. Or just not create as well as I'd hope. It's the same thing, reallylet's be honest.
         </p>
         <p>
          So enough, let's get stuck into this list.
         </p>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           1.
          </span>
          Don't Be Afraid of the Pen Tool
         </h2>
         <p>
          The
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/illustrators-pen-tool-the-comprehensive-guide--vector-141" target="_self">
           Pen Tool
          </a>
          can be a scary tool for beginners. It took me a while to be able to "tame" it, but don't let this put you off. Once you've mastered this tool, you can take on anything in Adobe Illustrator.
         </p>
         <p>
          I'd highly recommend you check out this fantastic tutorial by Web Design Editor
          <a href="http://tutsplus.com/authors/ian-yates" target="_self">
           Ian Yates
          </a>
          on
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/illustrators-pen-tool-the-comprehensive-guide--vector-141" target="_self">
           how to use the Pen Tool
          </a>
          . It even has a downloadable exercise at the end of the tutorial.
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Illustrators Pen Tool The Comprehensive Guide" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/pen-1.png"/>
         </figure>
         <p>
          It is true that you can create some pretty awesome vector graphics without needing the Pen Tool. If you check out the tutorials by regular Tuts+ Instructor
          <a href="http://tutsplus.com/authors/andrei-marius" target="_self">
           Andrei Marius
          </a>
          , the majority of his tutorials are done without the Pen Tool. He is solid proof you can create without the Pen Tool.This being said, my advice is to push yourself and go for it. It's not as bad as you think!
         </p>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           2.
          </span>
          Master Clipping Masks
         </h2>
         <p>
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/the-basics-of-clipping-paths-and-opacity-masks--vector-2799" target="_self">
           Clipping Masks
          </a>
          were a game changer for me. So much so that I wish I had discovered them a lot sooner in my Adobe Illustrator journey.
         </p>
         <p>
          Masks limit a pattern, group of shapes, paths or whatever you wish to a specific area, and have a multitude of uses. I found them an incredible time saver and for this reason, I think every beginner should learn to use them.
         </p>
         <p>
          I highly recommend our
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/the-basics-of-clipping-paths-and-opacity-masks--vector-2799" target="_self">
           beginners tutorial on Clipping and Opacity Masks
          </a>
          . This is a perfect tutorial which shows you the benefits of both in an easy-to-follow format.
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Beginners tutorial on Clipping and Opacity Masks" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/ClipandOpacity10.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           3.
          </span>
          Become One With the Pathfinder Panel
         </h2>
         <p>
          I love the
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-pathfinder-panel--vector-3306" target="_self">
           Pathfinder panel
          </a>
          . It's true. When it comes to creating shapes made out of other shapes, the Pathfinder panel has a selection of options to help you create the perfect shape. It's especially useful given trends of flat design and illustrations made from basic shapes. Check out our
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-pathfinder-panel--vector-3306" target="_self">
           comprehensive guide to the Pathfinder panel
          </a>
          by regular Tuts+ Instructor
          <a href="http://tutsplus.com/authors/cheryl-graham" target="_self">
           Cheryl Graham
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Shape Modes with Pathfinder panel" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/02_ShapeModes.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <p>
          I'm not finished though. Once you've learnt the many ways of the Pathfinder panel, kick it up a notch and
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/create-a-cheeky-zombie-with-basic-shapes-and-the-shape-builder-tool--vector-6013" target="_self">
           learn how to use the Shape Builder Tool
          </a>
          . It's like Shape Modes, but a lot quicker!
         </p>
         <p>
          Find out more about
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/courses/shape-creation-in-adobe-illustrator" target="_self">
           Shape Creation with Adobe Illustrator
          </a>
          with our handy Tuts+ Course.
         </p>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           4.
          </span>
          Embrace the Appearance Panel
         </h2>
         <p>
          Do you find yourself duplicating the same shape over and over again to add different fills at different opacities over the top of each other? Well you're doing it wrong. You could be doing the same thing but with just one shape, by using the
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/42-awesome-graphic-styles-and-appearance-panel-tutorials-on-tuts--cms-20833" target="_self">
           Appearance panel
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The Appearance panel and Graphic Styles seemed like something only advanced users knew when I first started out. I was intimidated by it. But once you learn how to use the Appearance panel, it will rock your world. Here's something to blow your mind: guess how many anchor points and shapes make up the following single sliced kiwi fruit?
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="How many anchor points and shapes make up a single kiwi" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/kiwi_final.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <p>
          If you answered anything other than four anchor points/one shape, you're so wrong and you should
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/create-a-sliced-kiwi-fruit-with-only-one-shape-in-adobe-illustrator--vector-16349" target="_self">
           learn how to use the Appearance panel
          </a>
          ! Just
          <em>
           one
          </em>
          shape was used to create this, and it demonstrates exactly how powerful the Appearance panel is.
         </p>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           5.
          </span>
          Learn How to Make Brushes
         </h2>
         <p>
          If there's one function of Adobe Illustrator which I geek out on, it has to be the ability to make and use my own custom brushes. Finding new uses for brushes and
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/create-a-jellyfish-with-brushes-in-adobe-illustrator-cs5--vector-5184" target="_self">
           creative ways to apply them
          </a>
          is my vector drug.
         </p>
         <p>
          I find I use art brushes more than any other type, especially these
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/create-cs5-width-profile-brushes-in-any-version-of-adobe-illustrator-cs--vector-3798" target="_self">
           Width Profile inspired brushes
          </a>
          (this is actually one of my first tutorials for Tuts+!).
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Width Profile Brushes" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/final.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <p>
          There are many different types of brushes you can create, and once you learn them inside and out, you'll find many practical and experimental ways to use them. Check out our roundup of
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/26-awesome-brush-tutorials-for-adobe-illustrator-on-tuts--vector-25248" target="_self">
           our favourite Brush tutorials
          </a>
          here on Tuts+.
         </p>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           6.
          </span>
          Practice Layers Panel Zen
         </h2>
         <p>
          Now this doesn't really benefit the end result of your vector work (unless you're selling your work as a vector file), but I strongly recommend that beginners learn how to organise their vector files. That means everything from organising and naming your Layers panel to getting rid of colours and resources in your file which are not being used.
         </p>
         <p>
          We all know that being organised makes things easier in the long run, as there's less confusion. So to take one thing away from the learning process to make it easier for you, practice Layers panel Zen. Check out this awesome tutorial by
          <a href="http://tutsplus.com/authors/loungekat" target="_self">
           Kate McInnes
          </a>
          on
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/quick-tip-how-to-organize-and-save-a-vector-file--vector-4205" target="_self">
           how to organise and save a vector file
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Look at that organised Layers panel" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/preview.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           7.
          </span>
          Fall in Love With Color
         </h2>
         <p>
          There are many aspects of Adobe Illustrator which are there to make life that little bit easier. One of those for me are the Swatch libraries, which come with every version of Illustrator. These range from funky gradients to palettes of skin tones to default patterns and textures.
         </p>
         <p>
          These can be found by going into your
          <strong>
           Swatches
          </strong>
          panel and going into the drill-down menu. From there select
          <strong>
           Open Swatch Library
          </strong>
          and then marvel at the selection you're presented with.
         </p>
         <p>
          I'm a big fan of the skin tone palettes and the basic pattern textures. Check them out and have a play with them.
         </p>
         <p>
          In addition, there is the awesomeness of Recolor Artwork and Adobe Color (previously called Adobe Kuler). Find out more about these and more from my
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/capture-colors-with-the-adobe-kuler-app-for-the-iphone--vector-12494" target="_self">
           Adobe Color/Kuler app tutorial
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Recolor Artwork sexy" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/recolorartwork.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           8.
          </span>
          Have Fun With Pattern Options
         </h2>
         <p>
          It wasn't until Illustrator CS6 that patterns were made easy with Pattern Options, and I tell you, it's like the heavens have opened and blessed every vector artist. Pattern Options, even if you don't actively create patterns, is one of the most fun functions you can come across. I may be overselling it a bit but I remember when it was released, all I wanted to do was create patterns.
         </p>
         <p>
          But you can create so much more with Pattern Options. Think of the endless possibilities of creating your own seamless textures! That's what grabbed me and helped me kick my artwork up a notch.
         </p>
         <p>
          Check out this great beginners tutorial on
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/tutorials/create-a-sweet-honeycomb-pattern-in-adobe-illustrator--cms-20656" target="_self">
           creating a honeycomb pattern
          </a>
          by the wonderful
          <a href="http://tutsplus.com/authors/mary-winkler" target="_self">
           Mary Winkler
          </a>
          . This utilizes the Pattern Options tool and gives you a taster of what can be created.
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Honeycomb goodness" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/inal.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           9.
          </span>
          Save Your Vector Art Correctly
         </h2>
         <p>
          I have this great annoyance with some vector artists. As talented as they can be with coming up with fantastic artwork, they fall at the last hurdle. And I don't want you to make the same mistake. It's a fundamental function which even if you ignore everything I've written here today, I want you to take on board... learn how to save your vector art correctly.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you're saving your vector as a gif, jpg or png, I want you, for the love of all that's scalableplease, I'm begging hereto learn how to Save for Web. It's really pretty easy but often overlooked.
         </p>
         <p>
          The first step is to make sure that your Artboard is correctly exposing all the artwork you want saved. You can use the
          <strong>
           Artboard Tool
          </strong>
          (
          <strong>
           Shift-O
          </strong>
          ) to do this.
         </p>
         <p>
          Secondly, go to
          <strong>
           File &gt; Save for Web
          </strong>
          . Select your file type and BAM!
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course if you're confused about what to save your file as, this handy tutorial by
          <a href="http://tutsplus.com/authors/cheryl-graham" target="_self">
           Cheryl Graham
          </a>
          helps you
          <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/understanding-the-different-file-types-in-adobe-illustrators-save-for-web--vector-16399" target="_self">
           understand the different file types
          </a>
          in the Save for Web dialog box.
         </p>
         <figure class="post_image">
          <img alt="Save for Web Dialog Box" src="https://cms-assets.tutsplus.com/uploads/users/33/posts/23163/image/01_dialog.jpg"/>
         </figure>
         <h2>
          <span class="sectionnum">
           10.
          </span>
          Accept It Doesn't Happen Overnight
         </h2>
         <p>
          The last tip isn't specific to Adobe Illustrator but to most new skills you begin learning, but I feel it's very true for those getting into vector art. It's to understand that you're not going to be a pro overnight. Don't beat yourself up if you can't get it right first, second, tenth time when you're starting out. Vector art is hard for anyonenew.
         </p>
         <p>
          Over the past couple of years, more than most, I've seen vector art grow from strength to strength. The need for it in design and illustration has made it such a sought-after skill, and it's worth adding to your toolkit.
         </p>
         <p>
          Have fun with it, take part in the many vector communities out there, and challenge yourself. Just don't be afraid to fail. Pick yourself up and keep at it, as it's a rewarding medium to work with.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Keep Pushing Yourself, You'll Get There!
         </h2>
         <p>
          You don't know how easy you've got it. As a vector beginner, you've got a lot of resources out there, both within Adobe Illustrator and beyond, with the sheer amount of tutorial content out there for you. Keep researching and keep pushing yourself. Don't let my green-faced envy put you off hereput your head up high and embrace this scalable path you're venturing on. It's a good one. It's an amazing one.
         </p>
         <p>
          For further reading, check out these two great articles to kick off further adventures into Adobe Illustrator:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/back-to-school-special-30-easy-adobe-illustrator-tutorials--vector-13616" target="_self">
            Back to School Special: 30 Easy Adobe Illustrator Tutorials
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a href="http://design.tutsplus.com/articles/the-a-to-z-of-adobe-illustrator--vector-20799" target="_self">
            The A to Z of Adobe Illustrator
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
