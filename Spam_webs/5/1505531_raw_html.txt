        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Mens soccer finds unity through diversity
        </h1>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         The No. 24 USF Bulls men's soccer team may have not grown up with the same culture, but the team operates as one unit.
        </h2>
       </header>
       <div class="entry clearfix">
        <div class="post-thumbnail">
         <a href="http://bullsradio.org/?attachment_id=708">
          <img alt="" src="http://bullsradio.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/mens-soccer-pic.jpg" title="mens soccer pic"/>
         </a>
         <span class="wp-caption-text">
          Photo Credit: Jason Roberts - Envision Sports Media
         </span>
        </div>
        <section class="share-buttons-container clearfix">
         <div class="share-button">
          <div class="fb-like" data-font="verdana" data-layout="button_count" data-send="false" data-show-faces="true" data-width="450">
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="share-button">
          <a class="twitter-share-button" href="https://twitter.com/share">
           Tweet
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="share-button">
          <div class="g-plusone" data-size="medium">
          </div>
         </div>
        </section>
        <p>
         The No. 24 USF Bulls mens soccer team are from all walks of life and different parts of the world. They may not have grown up with the same language or customs, but the team operates as one unit.
        </p>
        <p>
         Of the 28 players on the roster, 17 are from foreign countries. Those international players come from 11 nations located across three continents outside of North America.
        </p>
        <p>
         When compared with the AAC, USF leads in the amount of international players and countries represented. They surpass the second-most diverse team, UConn, by seven international players.
        </p>
        <p>
         Some members of the team, such as England native Tyler Blackwood and Venezuela native Gerardo Hernandez, said they were reluctant to play soccer in the U.S. because they did not believe it was a good standard. They already played other countries on their respective national youth teams.
        </p>
        <p>
         Other players, such as Bahamas native Terry Delancy, had the desire to play outside of their birth country.
        </p>
        <p>
         The level here is higher than where I lived because (New Providence) is only 21-by-7 miles, Delancy said. There was only so much experience you can get being with the same people.
        </p>
        <p>
         The global mix of players on the team was a quality that attracted Hernandez. He knew the Venezuelans already playing for USF.
        </p>
        <p>
         I knew I was coming to a good environment, Hernandez said. I talked to them before coming and they said (George Kiefer) liked diversity.
        </p>
        <p>
         Edwin Moalosi, who moved to the U.S. from Botswana four years ago, said he was familiar with how the team was coached before knowing about the diversity.
        </p>
        <p>
         When you are far from home, you dont just need a team, Moalosi said. Even if the team is good but you dont live well with them, its going to make you homesick either way. Its better if you go where theres better people.
        </p>
        <p>
         Moalosi left early from the group interview due to a previously scheduled commitment, but he did not make it far from the practice field. A large group of bees sat on the side of the fields entrance steps, catching the attention of Moalosi. He crouched before them as if he was studying the swarm.
        </p>
        <p>
         He eventually made a motion towards the bees as if he was going to touch them. His teammates watched from a distance.
        </p>
        <p>
         See? Thats culture right there, Blackwood said. We would never get near that and he goes and touches it.
        </p>
        <p>
         Like Moalosi, Blackwood also transferred after arriving to the U.S. four years ago. The Englishman saw the cohesion on and off the field, as players went from practicing together to eating team meals and playing the FIFA video game.
        </p>
        <p>
         One of the teams activities included an American activity that Wesley Charpie planned.
        </p>
        <p>
         I grew up on the water and thats what a Florida boy is, Charpie said. I brought (Blackwood) and (Hernandez) on the boat and they loved it.
        </p>
        <p>
         I didnt realize (boating) was a thing, Blackwood said. That sandbar thing? I didnt know that was a thing.
        </p>
        <p>
         In addition to the activities, players on the team share their countrys music. Blackwood said the sounds in the locker room includes songs from Africa, England, Latin America and the United States. Dancing sometimes accompanies the music.
        </p>
        <p>
         The international playlist developed into a tradition fans also experience. The assorted mix of music is played over Corbett Stadiums speakers during pregame warmups.
        </p>
        <p>
         During the game, the multicultural lineup of players are comfortable relying on each other to score. Eight different players, including Hernandez, Moalosi and Blackwood, have recorded a total of 21 goals this season.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Bulls create scoring plays through teamwork, an effort that leads the AAC in assists. USF attributed 26 assists coming from eight players. Charpie has four, and Moalosi has five.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the backfield, Charpie helps the defense remain tough against opponents. USF allows a conference second-best average of 1.09 goals a game.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whether the team is playing a game or sharing activities off the field, most of the players are far from home. The experiences they share make up for the distance.
        </p>
        <p>
         The way I feel at home, Hernandez said. I feel like that here.
        </p>
