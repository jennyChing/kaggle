    Crom Cruach
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Crom Cruach
      </b>
      (Old Irish pronunciation
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPA_for_Irish" title="IPA for Irish">
       /km ku/
      </a>
      ; modern Irish
      <i>
       Cromm Craich
      </i>
      ) was a god of
      <a href="/wiki/Protohistory_of_Ireland" title="Protohistory of Ireland">
       pre-Christian Ireland
      </a>
      . According to Christian writers, he was propitiated with
      <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">
       human sacrifice
      </a>
      and his worship was ended by
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">
       Saint Patrick
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      He is also referred to as
      <i>
       Crom Crich
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Cenn Cruach
      </i>
      /
      <i>
       Crich
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPA_for_Irish" title="IPA for Irish">
       /kn: kux/
      </a>
      ) and
      <i>
       Cenncroithi
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPA_for_Irish" title="IPA for Irish">
       /kn: ko/
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      He is related to the later mythological and folkloric figure
      <a href="/wiki/Crom_Dubh" title="Crom Dubh">
       Crom Dubh
      </a>
      . The festival for Crom Cruach is called
      <i>
       Domhnach
      </i>
      Crom Dubh, Crom Dubh Sunday.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mythology_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mythology-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The references in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinsenchas" title="Dinsenchas">
        dinsenchas
       </a>
      </i>
      ("place-lore") poem in the 12th century to sacrifice in exchange for milk and grain suggest that Crom had a function as fertility god. The description of his image as a gold figure surrounded by twelve stone or bronze figures has been interpreted by some as representing the sun surrounded by the signs of the
      <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">
       zodiac
      </a>
      , suggesting a function as solar deity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mythology_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mythology-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
       Name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crom_Cruach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Crom Cruach's name takes several forms and can be interpreted in several ways.
      <i>
       Crom
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       cromm
      </i>
      ) can mean "bent, crooked, stooped, bowed, curved, crescent".
      <i>
       Cenn
      </i>
      can mean "head" or "the head, chief".
      <i>
       Cruach
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       crach
      </i>
      ) can be an adjective, "bloody, gory", or a noun, meaning variously "slaughter", "(corn)stack", or "pile, heap, mound". Plausible meanings include "bloody crooked one", "crooked stack of corn", "crooked one of the mound", "bloody head", "head of the stack of corn" or "head of the mound".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Synthesis_of_published_material" title="Wikipedia:No original research">
         <span title="The material near this tag may be based upon an improper synthesis of sources. (October 2014)">
          improper synthesis?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_references">
       Literary references
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crom_Cruach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary references">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to an Irish
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinsenchas" title="Dinsenchas">
        dinsenchas
       </a>
      </i>
      ("place-lore") poem in the 12th century
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Leinster" title="Book of Leinster">
       Book of Leinster
      </a>
      , Crom Cruach's
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_image" title="Cult image">
       cult image
      </a>
      , consisting of a gold figure surrounded by twelve stone figures, stood on
      <a href="/wiki/Magh_Sl%C3%A9cht" title="Magh Slcht">
       Magh Slcht
      </a>
      ("the plain of prostration") in
      <a href="/wiki/County_Cavan" title="County Cavan">
       County Cavan
      </a>
      , and was propitiated with first-born sacrifice in exchange for good yields of milk and grain. Crom is said to have been worshipped since the time of
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89rim%C3%B3n" title="rimn">
       rimn
      </a>
      . An early
      <a href="/wiki/High_King_of_Ireland" title="High King of Ireland">
       High King
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tigernmas" title="Tigernmas">
       Tigernmas
      </a>
      , along with three quarters of his army, is said to have died while worshipping Crom on
      <a href="/wiki/Samhain" title="Samhain">
       Samhain
      </a>
      eve, but worship continued until the cult image was destroyed by St. Patrick with a sledgehammer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This incident figures prominently in medieval legends about St. Patrick, although it does not appear in his own writings, nor in the two 7th century biographies by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muirchu_moccu_Machtheni" title="Muirchu moccu Machtheni">
       Muirchu
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/T%C3%ADrech%C3%A1n" title="Trechn">
       Trechn
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 9th century
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vita_tripartita_Sancti_Patricii" title="Vita tripartita Sancti Patricii">
        Tripartite Life of Saint Patrick
       </a>
      </i>
      the deity is called Cenn Cruach, and his cult image consists of a central figure covered with gold and silver, surrounded by twelve bronze figures. When Patrick approaches it he raises his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crozier" title="Crozier">
       crozier
      </a>
      , the central figure falls face-down, with the imprint of the crozier left in it, and the surrounding figures sink into the earth. The "demon" who inhabits the image appears, but Patrick curses him and casts him to hell.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jocelin_(Bishop_of_Glasgow)" title="Jocelin (Bishop of Glasgow)">
       Jocelin's
      </a>
      12th century
      <i>
       Life and Acts of St. Patrick
      </i>
      tells much the same story. Here the god is called Cenncroithi, interpreted as "the head of all gods", and when his image falls the silver and gold covering it crumble to dust, with the imprint of the crozier left on bare stone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the old Irish tale from the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Lismore" title="Book of Lismore">
       Book of Lismore
      </a>
      , "The Siege of Druim Damhgaire or Knocklong" (Forbhais Droma Dmhghire), Crom is associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Moloch" title="Moloch">
       Moloch
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">
       Archaeology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crom_Cruach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archaeology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A decorated stone which has been interpreted by some as the cult image of Crom Cruach was found at Killycluggin, County Cavan, in 1921 (Site number 93, Killycluggin townland, Archaeological Inventory of County Cavan, Patrick ODonovan, 1995, p.19).
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._O%27Kelly" title="Michael J. O'Kelly">
       O'Kelly
      </a>
      , however, refers to this image as
      <a href="/wiki/Crom_Dubh" title="Crom Dubh">
       Crom Dubh
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Roughly dome-shaped and covered in Iron Age
      <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tne culture">
       La Tne
      </a>
      designs, it was discovered broken in several pieces and partly buried close to a Bronze Age stone circle, inside which it probably once stood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The site has several associations with St. Patrick. Nearby is
      <i>
       Tobar Padraig
      </i>
      (St. Patrick's Well), and Kilnavert Church, which is said to have been founded by the saint. Kilnavert was originally called
      <i>
       Fossa Slcht
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Rath Slcht
      </i>
      , from which the wider Magh Slcht area was named.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although now much damaged, the stone can be reconstructed from the different surviving pieces.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (January 2014)">
          original research?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      At the base of the stone there were four rectangular adjoining panels measuring 90cm each in width giving a circumference of 3m 60cm when it was first carved. The height of each panel was about 75cm. When excavated and placed upright on its flat base, it was found to lean obliquely to the left from the vertical, perhaps explaining the name
      <i>
       Crom
      </i>
      , "bent, crooked". The Killycluggin Stone, as it is known, is now in the Cavan County Museum, while a replica stands near the road about 300 metres from the original site.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 14th century Book of McGovern, written in Magh Slcht, contains a poem which states that Crom was situated at Kilnavert beside the road and that the local women used to tremble in fear as they passed by. There is still a local tradition in the area that the Killycluggin stone is the Crom stone.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is another standing stone identified
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2014)">
          according to whom?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      with Crom Craich in Drumcoo townland,
      <a href="/wiki/County_Fermanagh" title="County Fermanagh">
       County Fermanagh
      </a>
      . A nearby street is named Crom Craich Way after it. It has the figure of a man walking engraved on it which either represents Saint Patrick or a druid, depending on when it was engraved.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (January 2014)">
          original research?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
