        <p>
         The latest unemployment numbers are finally pointing to something weve been suspecting all along: that were now in the midst of a recession.  But for those folks out there who arent thoroughly convinced, or who would like to become acquainted with the topic of recessions  after all, this is something that may have some influence on our spending and saving habits in the near term 
         <strong>
          Id like to present this FAQ on recessions.
         </strong>
         So lets get to know our personal finances public enemy #1 
        </p>
        <h3>
         Signs and Predictors of a Recession
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          What are the symptoms of a recession?
         </strong>
         Here are some of the
         <a href="http://www.sfgate.com/cgi-bin/article.cgi?f=/c/a/2008/04/05/MN3P1005OK.DTL&amp;hw=recession&amp;sn=027&amp;sc=410">
          signs
         </a>
         that the recession is finally here:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          three sequential months of job losses
         </li>
         <li>
          the largest number of job losses in 5 years occurred in March 2008
         </li>
         <li>
          the national unemployment rate rose from 4.8 percent to 5.1 percent
         </li>
         <li>
          increase in mortgage defaults due to subprime loans
         </li>
         <li>
          financial institutions are in near collapse (Bear Stearns?)
         </li>
         <li>
          foreclosure spikes nation-wide
         </li>
         <li>
          stock market down from previous highs
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <span id="more-866">
         </span>
        </p>

        <h3>
         Definition of a Recession
        </h3>
        <div style="float:right; margin: 0px 10px 10px 10px;">
         <img alt="corporate head statue" height="238" src="http://www.thedigeratilife.com/images/recessionsm2.jpg" style="border:1px solid #000;background:#FFF;" width="350"/>
        </div>
        <p>
         Though it may sound like the end is nigh when the media starts to freely promote the notion that were finally in the thick of an economic storm (but really just the trough of an economic cycle), you may want to put things in perspective.  A recession is part of a normal economic cycle and is defined as follows:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          According to the National Bureau of Economic Research, or NBER, a recession can be defined as a significant decline in economic activity spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months, normally visible in real GDP (gross domestic product), real income, employment, industrial production and wholesale-retail sales.
         </p>
         <p>
          While other
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_recessions">
           sources
          </a>
          officially describe it as a decline in a countrys gross domestic product (GDP), or negative real economic growth, for two or more successive quarters for a year.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         The determination of a recession is typically delayed, and identified only after some months have past where weakness has been tracked and recognized on various economic fronts and in market indicators.
        </p>
        <p>
         It may not be so bad when you know its just temporary.  Or when you realize its already happening way after its already started. Or maybe its not so bad because you happen to be lucky enough to live and work in a recession-proof locale.  Or we could just be lulled into a false sense of security since the last couple of recessions weve had havent been painful enough to be truly memorable.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is worth noting though, that recessions have been shorter and shallower than theyve typically been way back in history.   Following are some interesting tables and charts depicting the history of recessions in the United States.
        </p>

         <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="boxF_17703499" width="460">
          <tr>
           <td class="boxFI_17703499">
            <div class="textSmall">
             <em>
              source: NBER
             </em>
            </div>
           </td>
          </tr>
         </table>
        </div>
        <p>
         As youll see, the last couple of decades have yielded some pretty tame recessions that have lasted us only 8 months each.  Will this current one be any different?
         <strong>
          Many say were due to return to normal or average recession standards.
         </strong>
         For clarity, this is how recessions are classified or characterized:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          A mild recession is one whose duration is less than 10 months and which results in small declines in jobs, retail sales, output and real GDP.
         </p>
         <p>
          An average recession typically persists for 11 months with more moderate declines in the jobs, sales, output, real GDP and interest rates.
         </p>
         <p>
          While a severe recession is known to last for 16 months, with economic indicators experiencing more dramatic drops.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Should we then expect a longer down period?  Given the causes and triggers of this downtrend, and the general difficult financial landscape our nation is facing, it may not be surprising to see a more protracted contraction this time around.
        </p>
        <h3>
         How The Economic Cycle and The Stock Market Are Linked
        </h3>
        <p>
         All this recession talk is of course, fascinating, but as investors, Im sure youd be more interested in finding out how recessions affect the stock market and consequently, your portfolios.  Well, looking back through history (from the 1950s) and at how the stock market has behaved during some of these periods, I can see that the 16 month recession triggered by the
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1973_oil_crisis">
          global oil crisis
         </a>
         in the early 1970s as relatively being the harshest and having the most devastating effect on the average investment portfolio.
        </p>

        </p>
        <p>
         These numbers can give you some perspective on how your portfolio may behave during recessionary conditions.  Despite the grim  mood that recessions can evoke,
         <strong>
          the positive takeaway here is that the general long-term stock market trend is UP.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Other insightful words about the relationship between economic cycles and the stock market come from Joseph Davis, Ph.D. (a principal and economist for
         <a href="http://www.vanguard.com">
          Vanguards
         </a>
         Investment Counseling &amp; Research (IC&amp;R) and Fixed Income Groups):
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          Just as recessions havent always heralded a bear market for stocks, periods of economic growth havent always been accompanied by major gains on Wall Street. Vanguard IC&amp;R research has found that the average monthly return on the S&amp;P 500 has been higher during economic expansions than during recessions (1.05% versus 0.76%), but the difference is not statistically significant statistical noise, Mr. Davis suggested. Perhaps less surprising, stock market volatility has been significantly higher during recessions, as risk premiums rise during periods of economic uncertainty.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The primary reason there hasnt been a strong relationship between recessions and stock market returns comes down to timing,
          </strong>
          Mr. Davis said. Its inherently difficult for equity investors to time the economy by getting out ofor back intothe stock market at just the right moment.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Sounds to me that this is just one more argument for avoiding drastic changes to your investment strategy or portfolio as a knee-jerk response to economic tidal shifts.
        </p>

         Ack! Were In A Slump! So What Can I Do?

        <p>

         <div class="format_text" id="comment-body-254777">
          <p>
           Its not just that recessions affect stock returns. Stock returns also affect recessions. When people lose large percentages of the accumulated wealth of a lifetime, they become afraid to spend for many years. When people become afraid to spend, businesses fail and many people lose their jobs. The root problem here is the insane stock prices of the late 1990s, which made a huge price crash (and the recession/depression that always follows from an out-of-control bull) an inevitability.
          </p>
          <p>
           There are only four times in U.S. history when we got to a P/E10 value above 25. On the first three occasions, we went to price levels 50 percent below where we are today. It is not certain, but the strong likelihood is that we have another huge price crash ahead of us.
          </p>
          <p>
           I believe that humans can only take in so much bad news at one time. We took in all we could in September 2008 and it is now going to take another year or two or three before we will be able to fully admit how much long-term damage we did to ourselves by permitting prices to get so out of control in the 1990s.
          </p>
          <p>
           There will come a day when prices will go back up. But I question how many of those who have been following the conventional advice will be able to hold on to their stocks through another 50 percent price drop. The bottom line is that I believe that further drops in stock prices (caused by the bull of the 1990s) will cause this recession/depression to go on for a good long time yet.
          </p>
          <p>
           My hope is that we will learn something from this experience and begin considering more realistic ways to invest. I believe that all investors should be taking price into consideration when setting their stock allocation. That would make out-of-control bulls and the recessions/depressions that follow from them impossible.
          </p>
          <p>
           Rob
          </p>
