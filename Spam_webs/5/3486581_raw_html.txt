     <p>
      <b>
       Photios I
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       Phtios
      </i>
      ;
      <abbr title="circa">
       c.
      </abbr>
      <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
       810
      </span>
       c.
      <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
       893
      </span>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_a">
       <a href="#cnote_a">
        a[]
       </a>
      </sup>
      also spelled
      <b>
       Photius
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'f' in 'find'">
           f
          </span>
          <span title="/o/ long 'o' in 'code'">
           o
          </span>
          <span title="// 'sh' in 'shy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ) or
      <b>
       Fotios
      </b>
      , was the
      <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople">
       Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople
      </a>
      from 858 to 867 and from 877 to 886;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is recognized in the
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">
       Eastern Orthodox Church
      </a>
      as
      <b>
       St. Photios the Great
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Photios is widely regarded as the most powerful and influential Patriarch of Constantinople since
      <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">
       John Chrysostom
      </a>
      , and as the most important intellectual of his time, "the leading light of the ninth-century renaissance".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was a central figure in both the
      <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Kievan_Rus%27" title="Christianization of Kievan Rus'">
       conversion of the Slavs to Christianity
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Photian_schism" title="Photian schism">
       Photian schism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Photios was a well-educated man from a noble Constantinopolitan family. Photius's great uncle was a previous Patriarch of Constantinople,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Tarasios_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Tarasios of Constantinople">
       Tarasius
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He intended to be a monk, but chose to be a scholar and statesman instead. In 858, Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_III" title="Michael III">
       Michael III
      </a>
      (r. 842867) deposed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Ignatius_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Ignatius of Constantinople">
       Patriarch Ignatius of Constantinople
      </a>
      , and Photios, still a layman, was appointed in his place.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Amid power struggles between the pope and the Byzantine emperor, Ignatius was reinstated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Photios resumed the position when Ignatius died (877), by order of the Byzantine emperor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The new pope,
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_VIII" title="Pope John VIII">
       John VIII
      </a>
      , approved Photios's reinstatement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Catholics regard a
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_Constantinople_(Roman_Catholic)" title="Fourth Council of Constantinople (Roman Catholic)">
       Fourth Council of Constantinople (Roman Catholic)
      </a>
      as anathematizing Photios as legitimate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Eastern Orthodox regard a second council named the
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_Constantinople_(Eastern_Orthodox)" title="Fourth Council of Constantinople (Eastern Orthodox)">
       Fourth Council of Constantinople (Eastern Orthodox)
      </a>
      , reversing the first, as legitimate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The contested Ecumenical Councils mark the end of unity represented by the
      <a href="/wiki/First_seven_Ecumenical_Councils" title="First seven Ecumenical Councils">
       first seven Ecumenical Councils
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photios_I_of_Constantinople&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Secular_life">
       Secular life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photios_I_of_Constantinople&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Secular life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Most of the primary sources treating Photios's life are written by persons hostile to him. Modern scholars are thus cautious when assessing the accuracy of the information these sources provide.
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_b">
       <a href="#cnote_b">
        b[]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Little is known of Photios's origin and early years. It is known that he was born into a notable family and that his uncle
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Tarasius_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Tarasius of Constantinople">
       Tarasios
      </a>
      had been the Patriarch of Constantinople from 784806 under both Empress
      <a href="/wiki/Irene_of_Athens" title="Irene of Athens">
       Irene
      </a>
      (r. 797802) and Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Nikephoros_I" title="Nikephoros I">
       Nikephoros I
      </a>
      (r. 802811).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the second
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconoclasm_(Byzantine)" title="Iconoclasm (Byzantine)">
       Iconoclasm
      </a>
      , his family suffered persecution since his father, Sergios, was a prominent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconophile" title="Iconophile">
       iconophile
      </a>
      . Sergios's family returned to favor only after the restoration of the icons in 842.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Certain scholars assert that Photios was, at least in part, of
      <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">
       Armenian
      </a>
      descent
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_c">
       <a href="#cnote_c">
        c[]
       </a>
      </sup>
      while other scholars merely refer to him as a "
      <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greeks" title="Byzantine Greeks">
       Greek Byzantine
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Byzantine writers also report that Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_III" title="Michael III">
       Michael III
      </a>
      (r. 842867) once angrily called Photios "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khazar" title="Khazar">
       Khazar
      </a>
      -faced", but whether this was a generic insult or a reference to his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">
       ethnicity
      </a>
      is unclear.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although Photios had an excellent education, we have no information about how he received this education.
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_d">
       <a href="#cnote_d">
        d[]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The famous library he possessed attests to his enormous erudition (theology, history, grammar, philosophy, law, the natural sciences, and medicine).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tat102_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tat102-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most scholars believe that he never taught at
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Constantinople" title="University of Constantinople">
       Magnaura
      </a>
      or at any other university;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Vasileios N. Tatakes asserts that, even while he was patriarch, Photios taught "young students passionately eager for knowledge" at his home, which "was a center of learning".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tat102_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tat102-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Photios says that, when he was young, he had an inclination for the monastic life, but instead he started a secular career. The way to public life was probably opened for him by (according to one account) the marriage of his brother Sergios to Irene, a sister of the Empress
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodora_(9th_century)" title="Theodora (9th century)">
       Theodora
      </a>
      , who upon the death of her husband Emperor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theophilos,_Byzantine_Emperor" title="Theophilos, Byzantine Emperor">
       Theophilos
      </a>
      (r. 829842) in 842, had assumed the regency of the Byzantine Empire. Photios became a captain of the guard (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Protospatharios" title="Protospatharios">
        prtospatharios
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and subsequently chief imperial secretary (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Protasekretis" title="Protasekretis">
        protaskrtis
       </a>
      </i>
      ). At an uncertain date, Photios participated in an embassy to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasids" title="Abbasids">
       Abbasids
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">
       Baghdad
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Patriarch_of_Constantinople">
       Patriarch of Constantinople
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photios_I_of_Constantinople&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patriarch of Constantinople">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Photios's ecclesiastical career took off spectacularly after
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)#Byzantine_Empire" title="Caesar (title)">
        Caesar
       </a>
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Bardas" title="Bardas">
       Bardas
      </a>
      and his nephew, the youthful Emperor Michael, put an end to the administration of the regent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodora_II" title="Theodora II">
       Theodora
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logothete_of_the_drome" title="Logothete of the drome">
       logothete of the drome
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Theoktistos" title="Theoktistos">
       Theoktistos
      </a>
      in 856. In 858, Bardas found himself opposed by the then Patriarch
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_Ignatius_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch Ignatius of Constantinople">
       Ignatios
      </a>
      , who refused to admit him into
      <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">
       Hagia Sophia
      </a>
      , since it was believed that he was having an affair with his widowed daughter-in-law. In response, Bardas and Michael engineered Ignatios's deposition and confinement on the charge of treason, thus leaving the patriarchal throne empty. The throne was soon filled with a kinsman of Bardas, Photios himself; he was tonsured on December 20, 858, and on the four following days he was successively ordained lector, sub-deacon, deacon and priest. He was consecrated as Patriarch of Constantinople on Christmas Day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The deposition of Ignatios and the sudden promotion of Photios caused scandal and ecclesiastical division on an ecumenical scale as the
      <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">
       Pope
      </a>
      and the rest of the western bishops took up the cause of Ignatios. The latter's deposition without a formal ecclesiastical trial meant that Photios's election was uncanonical, and eventually
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Nicholas_I" title="Pope Nicholas I">
       Pope Nicholas I
      </a>
      sought to involve himself in determining the legitimacy of the succession. His legates were dispatched to Constantinople with instructions to investigate, but finding Photios well ensconced, they acquiesced in the confirmation of his election at a synod in 861. On their return to Rome, they discovered that this was not at all what Nicholas had intended, and in 863 at a synod in Rome the pope deposed Photios, and reappointed Ignatius as the rightful patriarch, triggering a
      <a href="/wiki/Photian_schism" title="Photian schism">
       schism
      </a>
      . Four years later, Photios was to respond on his own part by calling a Council and
      <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">
       excommunicating
      </a>
      the pope on grounds of heresy  over the question of the double procession of the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">
       Holy Spirit
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The situation was additionally complicated by the question of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papacy" title="Papacy">
       papal
      </a>
      authority over the entire Church and by disputed jurisdiction over newly converted
      <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
       Bulgaria
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This state of affairs changed with the murder of Photios's patron
      <a href="/wiki/Bardas" title="Bardas">
       Bardas
      </a>
      in 866 and of Emperor Michael III in 867, by his colleague
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basil_I_the_Macedonian" title="Basil I the Macedonian">
       Basil the Macedonian
      </a>
      , who now usurped the throne. Photios was deposed as patriarch, not so much because he was a protg of Bardas and Michael, but because Basil I was seeking an alliance with the Pope and the western emperor. Photios was removed from his office and banished about the end of September 867, and Ignatios was reinstated on November 23. Photios was condemned by the
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_Constantinople_(Roman_Catholic)" title="Fourth Council of Constantinople (Roman Catholic)">
       Council of 869870
      </a>
      , thus putting an end to the schism. During his second patriarchate, however, Ignatios followed a policy not very different from that of Photios.
     </p>
     <p>
      Not long after his condemnation, Photios had reingratiated himself with Basil, and became tutor to the Byzantine emperor's children. From surviving letters of Photios written during his exile at the Skepi monastery, it appears that the ex-patriarch brought pressure to bear on the Byzantine emperor to restore him. Ignatios's biographer argues that Photios forged a document relating to the genealogy and rule of Basil's family, and had it placed in the imperial library where a friend of his was a librarian. According to this document, the Byzantine emperor's ancestors were not mere peasants as everyone believed but descendants of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsacid_Dynasty_of_Armenia" title="Arsacid Dynasty of Armenia">
       Arsacid Dynasty of Armenia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      True or not, this story does reveal Basil's dependence on Photios for literary and ideological matters. Following Photios's recall, Ignatios and the ex-patriarch met, and publicly expressed their reconciliation. When Ignatios died on October 23, 877, it was a matter of course that his old opponent replaced him on the patriarchal throne three days later. Shaun Tougher asserts that from this point on Basil no longer simply depended on Photios, but in fact he was dominated by him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Photios now obtained the formal recognition of the Christian world in a
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_Constantinople_(Eastern_Orthodox)" title="Fourth Council of Constantinople (Eastern Orthodox)">
       council convened at Constantinople
      </a>
      in November 879. The legates of
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_VIII" title="Pope John VIII">
       Pope John VIII
      </a>
      attended, prepared to acknowledge Photios as legitimate patriarch, a concession for which the pope was much censured by Latin opinion. The patriarch stood firm on the main points contested between the Eastern and Western Churches, the demanded apology to the Pope, the ecclesiastical jurisdiction over
      <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
       Bulgaria
      </a>
      , and the addition of the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filioque_clause" title="Filioque clause">
        filioque
       </a>
      </i>
      to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicene_creed" title="Nicene creed">
       Nicene creed
      </a>
      by the Western church. Eventually, Photios refused to apologize or accept the
      <i>
       filioque
      </i>
      , and the papal legates made do with his return of Bulgaria to Rome. This concession, however, was purely nominal, as Bulgaria's return to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_rite" title="Byzantine rite">
       Byzantine rite
      </a>
      in 870 had already secured for it an autocephalous church. Without the consent of
      <a href="/wiki/Boris_I_of_Bulgaria" title="Boris I of Bulgaria">
       Boris I of Bulgaria
      </a>
      (r. 852889), the papacy was unable to enforce its claims.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the altercations between Emperor Basil I and his heir
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_VI_the_Wise" title="Leo VI the Wise">
       Leo VI
      </a>
      , Photios took the side of the Byzantine emperor. In 883, Basil accused Leo of conspiracy and confined the prince to the palace; he would have even have Leo blinded had he not been dissuaded by Photios and
      <a href="/wiki/Stylianos_Zaoutzes" title="Stylianos Zaoutzes">
       Stylianos Zaoutzes
      </a>
      , the father of
      <a href="/wiki/Zoe_Zaoutzaina" title="Zoe Zaoutzaina">
       Zoe Zaoutzaina
      </a>
      , Leo's mistress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 886, Basil discovered and punished a conspiracy by the domestic of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hikanatoi" title="Hikanatoi">
        Hikanatoi
       </a>
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/John_Kourkouas" title="John Kourkouas">
       John Kourkouas
      </a>
      and many other officials. In this conspiracy, Leo was not implicated, but Photios was possibly one of the conspirators against Basil's authority.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Basil died in 886 injured while hunting, according to the official story. Warren T. Treadgold believes that this time the evidence points to a plot on behalf of Leo VI, who became emperor, and deposed Photios, although the latter had been his tutor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Photios was replaced by the Byzantine emperor's brother
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch_Stephen_I_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarch Stephen I of Constantinople">
       Stephen
      </a>
      , and sent into exile to the monastery of Bordi in
      <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">
       Armenia
      </a>
      . It is confirmed from letters to and from
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Stephen_V" title="Pope Stephen V">
       Pope Stephen
      </a>
      that Leo extracted a resignation from Photios. In 887, Photios and his protg,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Theodore_Santabarenos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Theodore Santabarenos (page does not exist)">
       Theodore Santabarenos
      </a>
      , were put on trial for treason before a tribuneal headed by senior officials, headed by
      <a href="/wiki/Andrew_the_Scythian" title="Andrew the Scythian">
       Andrew the Scythian
      </a>
      . Although the sources sympathetic to Photios give the impression that the trial ended without a conviction, the chronicle of Pseudo-Symeon clearly states that Photios was banished to the monastery of Gordon, where he later died. Yet it appears that he did not remain reviled for the remainder of his life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Photios continued his career as a writer throughout his exile, and Leo probably rehabilitated his reputation within the next few years; in his
      <i>
       Epitaphios
      </i>
      on his brothers, a text probably written in 888, the Emperor presents Photios favorably, portraying him as the legitimate archbishop, and the instrument of ultimate unity, an image that jars with his attitude to the patriarch in the previous year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Confirmation that Photios was rehabilitated comes upon his death: according to some chronicles, his body was permitted to be buried in Constantinople. In addition, according to the anti-Photian biographer of Ignatius, partisans of the ex-patriarch after his death endeavored to claim for him the "honor of sainthood". Furthermore, a leading member of Leo's court,
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_Choirosphaktes" title="Leo Choirosphaktes">
       Leo Choirosphaktes
      </a>
      , wrote poems commemorating the memory of several prominent contemporary figures, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_the_Mathematician" title="Leo the Mathematician">
       Leo the Mathematician
      </a>
      and the Patriarch Stephen, and he also wrote one on Photios.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shaun Tougher notes, however, that "yet Photios's passing does seem rather muted for a great figure of Byzantine history [...] Leo [...] certainly did not allow him back into the sphere of politics, and it is surely his absence from this arena that accounts for his quiet passing."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Eastern Orthodox Church venerates Photios as a saint; his feast day is February 6.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Assessments">
       Assessments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photios_I_of_Constantinople&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Assessments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Photios is one of the most famous figures not only of 9th-century Byzantium but of the entire history of the Byzantine Empire. One of the most learned men of his age and revered  even by some of his opponents and detractors  as the most prolific theologian of his time, he has earned his fame due to his part in ecclesiastical conflicts, and also for his intellect and literary works
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELouth2007Chapter_Seven:_.22Renaissance_of_Learning:_East_and_West.22.2C_p._171_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELouth2007Chapter_Seven:_.22Renaissance_of_Learning:_East_and_West.22.2C_p._171-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETougher199768_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETougher199768-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Analyzing his intellectual work, Tatakes regards Photios as "mind turned more to practice than to theory". He believes that, thanks to Photios, humanism was added to Orthodoxy as a basic element of the national consciousness of the Byzantines. Tatakes also argues that, having understood this national consciousness, Photios emerged as a defender of the Greek nation and its spiritual independence in his debates with the Western Church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tat103_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tat103-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrian_Fortescue_(priest)" title="Adrian Fortescue (priest)">
       Adrian Fortescue
      </a>
      regards him as "the most wonderful man of all the Middle Ages", and stresses that "had not given his name to the great schism, he would always be remembered as the greatest scholar of his time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFortescue2001138_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFortescue2001138-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">
       Writings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photios_I_of_Constantinople&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The most important of the works of Photios is his renowned
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Photius)" title="Bibliotheca (Photius)">
        Bibliotheca
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Myriobiblon
      </i>
      , a collection of extracts and abridgements of 280 volumes of classical authors (usually cited as Codices), the originals of which are now to a great extent lost. The work is especially rich in extracts from historical writers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some older scholarship speculated that the
      <i>
       Bibliotheca
      </i>
      was in fact compiled in
      <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">
       Baghdad
      </a>
      at the time of Photius's embassy to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">
       Abbasid
      </a>
      court, since many of the mentioned works were rarely cited during the so-called Byzantine Dark Ages c. 630-c. 800, and it was known that the Abbasids were interested in works of Greek science and philosophy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, specialists of this period of Byzantine history, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lemerle" title="Paul Lemerle">
       Paul Lemerle
      </a>
      , have shown that Photius could not have compiled his
      <i>
       Bibliotheca
      </i>
      in Baghdad because he clearly states in both his introduction and his postscript that when he learned of his appointment to the embassy, he sent his brother a summary of books that he read
      <i>
       previously
      </i>
      , "since the time I learned how to understand and evaluate literature" i.e. since his youth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moreover, the Abbasids were interested only in Greek science, philosophy and medicine; they did not have Greek history, rhetoric, or other literary works translated; nor did they have Christian patristic writers translated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Yet the majority of works in
      <i>
       Bibliotheca
      </i>
      are by Christian patristic authors, and most of the secular texts in
      <i>
       Bibliotheca
      </i>
      are histories, grammars or literary works, usually rhetoric, rather than science, medicine or philosophy. This further indicates that the majority of the works cannot have been read while Photius was in the Abbasid empire.
     </p>
     <p>
      To Photios, we are indebted for almost all we possess of
      <a href="/wiki/Ctesias" title="Ctesias">
       Ctesias
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Memnon_of_Heraclea" title="Memnon of Heraclea">
       Memnon of Heraclea
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Conon_(mythographer)" title="Conon (mythographer)">
       Conon
      </a>
      , the lost books of
      <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">
       Diodorus Siculus
      </a>
      , and the lost writings of
      <a href="/wiki/Arrian" title="Arrian">
       Arrian
      </a>
      . Theology and ecclesiastical history are also very fully represented, but poetry and ancient philosophy are almost entirely ignored. It seems that he did not think it necessary to deal with those authors with whom every well-educated man would naturally be familiar. The literary criticisms, generally distinguished by keen and independent judgment, and the excerpts vary considerably in length. The numerous biographical notes are probably taken from the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Hesychius_of_Miletus" title="Hesychius of Miletus">
       Hesychius of Miletus
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Lexicon
      </i>
      , published later than the
      <i>
       Bibliotheca
      </i>
      , was probably in the main the work of some of his pupils. It was intended as a book of reference to facilitate the reading of old classical and sacred authors, whose language and vocabulary were out of date. For a long time, the only manuscripts of the
      <i>
       Lexicon
      </i>
      were the
      <i>
       Codex Galeanus
      </i>
      , which passed into the library of
      <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">
       Trinity College, Cambridge
      </a>
      and Berolinensis graec. oct. 22, both of which were incomplete. But then in 1959, Linos Politis of the University of Thessalonika discovered a complete manuscript in a monastery in western Macedonia, codex Zavordensis 95.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His most important theological work is the
      <i>
       Amphilochia
      </i>
      , a collection of some 300 questions and answers on difficult points in Scripture, addressed to Amphilochius, archbishop of Cyzicus. Other similar works are his treatise in four books against the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manichaean" title="Manichaean">
       Manichaeans
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulicians" title="Paulicians">
       Paulicians
      </a>
      , and his controversy with the Latins on the Procession of the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">
       Holy Spirit
      </a>
      . Photios also addressed a long letter of theological advice to the newly converted Boris I of Bulgaria. Numerous other
      <i>
       Epistles
      </i>
      also survive.
     </p>
     <p>
      Photios is also the writer of two "mirrors of princes", addressed to Boris-Michael of Bulgaria (Epistula 1, ed. Terzaghi) and to Leo VI the Wise (Admonitory Chapters of Basil I).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The chief contemporary authority for the life of Photios is his bitter enemy, Nicetas the Paphlagonian, the biographer of his rival Ignatios.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first English translation, by Holy Transfiguration Monastery, of the "

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">
       Notes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photios_I_of_Constantinople&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="refbegin" style="">
      <p>
       <span id="cnote_a">
        <b>
         <a href="#ref_a">
          ^
         </a>
        </b>
       </span>
       <b>
        a:
       </b>
       The exact dates of Photios's birth and death are not known. Most sources list circa 810 and others circa 820 as his year of birth. He died some time between 890 and 895 (probably 891 or 893).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         37
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="cnote_b">
        <b>
         <a href="#ref_b">
          ^
         </a>
        </b>
       </span>
       <b>
        b:
       </b>
       The case of pseudo-Simeon's
       <i>
        Chronicle
       </i>
       is characteristic: the author argues that Photios was educated after an agreement he concluded with a Jewish magician who offered him knowledge and secular recognition, in case he renounced his faith.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         38
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="cnote_c">
        <b>
         <a href="#ref_c">
          ^
         </a>
        </b>
       </span>
       <b>
        c:
       </b>
       David Marshall Lang argues that "Photius [...] was only one of many Byzantine scholars of Armenian descent".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         39
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Charanis" title="Peter Charanis">
        Peter Charanis
       </a>
       notes that "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_John_VII_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch John VII of Constantinople">
        John the Grammarian
       </a>
       , Photius, Caesar Bardas and
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_VI_the_Wise" title="Leo VI the Wise">
        Leo the Philosopher
       </a>
       seem to have been the prime movers. All four were, at least in part, of Armenian descent [...] as for Photius, the fact is that his mother Irene, was the sister of
       <a href="/wiki/Arshavir" title="Arshavir">
        Arshavir
       </a>
       , the Arshavir who had married Calomaria the sister of Bardas and the empress Theodora."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         40
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Adontz" title="Nicholas Adontz">
        Nicholas Adontz
       </a>
       stresses that "Arshavir, Photius' uncle, must not be confused with Arshavir, the brother of John the Grammarian".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         41
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="cnote_d">
        <b>
         <a href="#ref_d">
          ^
         </a>
        </b>
       </span>
       <b>
        d:
       </b>
       G. N. Wilson regards
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_the_Mathematician" title="Leo the Mathematician">
        Leo the Mathematician
       </a>
       as Photios's teacher, but
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lemerle" title="Paul Lemerle">
        Paul Lemerle
       </a>
       notes that Leo was not one the persons with whom Photios had a correspondence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         42
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
