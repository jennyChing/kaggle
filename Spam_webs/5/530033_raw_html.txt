     <p>
      A
      <b>
       gord
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       medieval
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_people" title="Slavic people">
       Slavonic
      </a>
      fortified settlement, also occasionally known as a
      <b>
       burgwall
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Slavic burgwall
      </b>
      after the German term for such sites. The ancient peoples were known for building wooden fortified settlements. The reconstructed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centum-satem_isogloss" title="Centum-satem isogloss">
       Centum-satem isogloss
      </a>
      word for such a settlement is g'herdh,
      <i>
       gord
      </i>
      , related to the
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
       Germanic
      </a>
      *
      <i>
       gard
      </i>
      and *
      <i>
       gart
      </i>
      (as in
      <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">
       Stuttgart
      </a>
      etc.). This
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Slavic" title="Proto-Slavic">
       Proto-Slavic
      </a>
      word (*gord) for town or city, later differentiated into
      <b>
       grad
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">
       Cyrillic
      </a>
      : ),
      <b>
       gard
      </b>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <b>
       gorod
      </b>
      (Cyrillic: ), etc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1898_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Taylor1898-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor1864_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Taylor1864-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthropologische_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Anthropologische-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Similar strongholds were built during the late
      <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">
       Bronze
      </a>
      and early
      <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">
       Iron Ages
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Lusatian_culture" title="Lusatian culture">
       Lusatian culture
      </a>
      (ca. 1300 BC  500 BC), and later in the 7th - 8th centuries BC in modern-day
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">
       Belarus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">
       Slovakia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">
       Czech Republic
      </a>
      and eastern
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      . These settlements were usually founded on strategic sites such as hills, riverbanks, lake islands or peninsulas.
     </p>
     <p>
      A typical gord was a group of wooden houses, built either in rows or in circles, surrounded by one or more rings of walls made of earth and wood, a
      <a href="/wiki/Palisade" title="Palisade">
       palisade
      </a>
      and/or
      <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">
       moats
      </a>
      . Some gords were ring-shaped, with a round, oval or occasionally polygonal fence or wall surrounding a hollow. Others, built on a natural hill or a man-made mound, were cone-shaped. Those with a natural defense on one side, such as a river or lake, were usually horseshoe-shaped.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most gords were built in densely populated areas, and situated in places which presented particular natural advantages. However, as Slavic tribes combined to form states, gords were also built for defense purposes in less populated border areas.
     </p>
     <p>
      Those gords that served as a ruler's residences or which lay on trade routes quickly expanded. A
      <i>
       suburbium
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
       Polish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">
       <i>
        podgrodzie
       </i>
      </span>
      ) formed near or below the gord; its population serviced the residents of the gord and could shelter within the gord's walls in the event of danger. Eventually the
      <i>
       suburbium
      </i>
      would have its own fence or wall. In the
      <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
       High Middle Ages
      </a>
      , the gord normally evolved into a
      <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">
       castle
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">
       citadel
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Kremlin" title="Kremlin">
       kremlin
      </a>
      ), and the
      <i>
       suburbium
      </i>
      into a
      <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">
       town
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Some other gords did not stand the test of time and were abandoned or destroyed, gradually turning into more or less discernible mounds or rings of earth (known in
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       gorodische
      </i>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
       Polish
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       grodzisko
      </i>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
       Ukrainian
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       horodyshche
      </i>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
       Slovak
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       hradisko
      </i>
      and in
      <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
       Czech
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       hradit
      </i>
      ). Notable archeological sites include
      <a href="/wiki/Biskupin" title="Biskupin">
       Biskupin
      </a>
      , Poland and Bilsk, Ukraine (see
      <a href="/wiki/Gelonus" title="Gelonus">
       Gelonus
      </a>
      ).
     </p>

     <p>
      The Proto-Slavic word *
      <i>
       gord
      </i>
      means a "fenced area", compare to Ukrainian
      <b>
       horod
      </b>
      yty, Czech o
      <b>
       hrad
      </b>
      it, Russian o
      <b>
       grad
      </b>
      it, Croatian/Serbian o
      <b>
       grad
      </b>
      iti and Polish o
      <b>
       grad
      </b>
      za meaning "fence off". The word ultimately finds its root in the
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
       Proto-Indo-European language
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognates
      </a>
      are many English words related to enclosure: "yard", "girdle," and "court." In some modern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_language" title="Slavic language">
       Slavic languages
      </a>
      , *
      <i>
       gord
      </i>
      has evolved into words for a "
      <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">
       garden
      </a>
      " (likewise a fenced area, from which Latin
      <i>
       hortus
      </i>
      , and English
      <i>
       horticulture
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       orchard
      </i>
      ):
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
        Ukrainian
       </a>
       <i>
        
       </i>
       (gorod or horod)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       <i>
        
       </i>
       (ogorod)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
        Serbian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">
        Macedonian
       </a>
       <i>
        
       </i>
       (gradina)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       <i>
        ogrd
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       <i>
        zhrada
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       <i>
        zahrada
       </i>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In some Slavic languages, *
      <i>
       gord
      </i>
      has evolved into a word for "town" or "city":
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       <i>
        gorod
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Pomeranian_language" title="Pomeranian language">
        Ancient Pomeranian
       </a>
       and modern
       <a href="/wiki/Kashubian_language" title="Kashubian language">
        Kaszubian
       </a>
       <i>
        gard
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">
        Croatian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">
        Macedonian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
        Serbian
       </a>
       <i>
        
       </i>
       (grad)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       <i>
        hrad
       </i>
       (nowadays means
       <i>
        castle
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The Polish word
      <i>
       podgrodzie
      </i>
      meant settlement near a town (Slavic prefix
      <i>
       pod-
      </i>
      means "under" or "below", equivalent of Latin
      <i>
       sub-
      </i>
      ) because in fact
      <i>
       grd
      </i>
      was built at hill's top frequently. It survived to these days in names of several villages (i.e.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Podgrodzie,_Subcarpathian_Voivodeship" title="Podgrodzie, Subcarpathian Voivodeship">
       Podgrodzie, Subcarpathian Voivodeship
      </a>
      ) and town's districts (i.e. district of
      <a href="/wiki/Olsztyn" title="Olsztyn">
       Olsztyn
      </a>
      ). It's also still found in
      <a href="/wiki/Puttgarden" title="Puttgarden">
       Puttgarden
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Wagria" title="Wagria">
       Wagria
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Putgarten" title="Putgarten">
       Putgarten
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCgen" title="Rgen">
       Rgen
      </a>
      in Germany.
     </p>
