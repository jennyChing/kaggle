      <h1>
       <a href="http://365tomorrows.com/07/29/counseling/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Counseling">
        Counseling
       </a>
      </h1>
      <span class="entryBy">
       by
      </span>
      <a href="http://365tomorrows.com/author/submission/" rel="author" title="Posts by submission">
       submission
      </a>
      <br clear="all"/>
      <span class="entryDate">
       July 29th, 2009
      </span>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Author : Ian Rennie
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       Richard reached for the jug of water on the coffee table and stopped, face caught between a frown and a smile.  He sat back in his chair and spoke to the couple.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mrs Lyell, Patricia, you were saying that Thomas had been distant lately.
      </p>
      <p>
       The woman on the couch glanced at her husband uneasily, then spoke.
      </p>
      <p>
       For the last three weeks when Ive got home from work, hes been sat in the front room with the lights off.  He doesnt talk to me when I get in, just waits for me to say something.  Hell sit there in silence until I do.  He never starts conversations any more, wont sit at the table with me for dinner.  It feels like Ive done something wrong and he wont tell me what it is.
      </p>
      <p>
       Richard turned to the man on the couch.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thomas, do you have anything you want to say about this?
      </p>
      <p>
       The man stared back, stubborn.  Richard knew without asking that he was here only at the womans insistence.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes, I dont have much to talk about. he said, pausing after this for so long that Richard was about to ask a follow up question when he continued, I dont do much any more, so I dont have much to say.  Im happy to talk, I just dont know what to say.
      </p>
      <p>
       Patricia shot a despairing look at Richard, who kept his eyes on Thomas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mrs Lyell, the problem is that your husband is dead.
      </p>
      <p>
       The woman looked up in shock at the words, and then, just as quickly, looked at her husband.  He seemed not to react.  Richard continued, gentle words with iron cores.
      </p>
      <p>
       He died of a heart attack two years ago.  You had him restored from a digital backup last year, but hes not your husband any more.  Hes an electronic representation.  He cant touch anything, because hes a projection.  Im only able to talk to him today because we have a projection rig in the building.  He doesnt do much because he cant leave the house.  Hes not a real person.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tears welled in Patricias eyes.
      </p>
      <p>
       But I dont think that!  Hes perfectly real to me.  I dont think any of the things you said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Richard looked over at Thomas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Your husband does.  Dont you, Thomas?
      </p>
      <p>
       The hologram of Thomas Lyell looked at the floor, refusing to meet the counselors gaze.  Finally he nodded.  Richard turned back to the sobbing widow.
      </p>
      <p>
       Patricia, after the heart attack, they gave you grief counseling.  They never gave it to Thomas.  You dont need marriage counseling, you need bereavement therapy.
      </p>
      <p>
       The consultation ended fairly quickly after that.  The problem was identified, and Thomas was already looking more hopeful five minutes later when he was switched off for transit back to the house.  As Mrs Lyell was leaving, Richards assistant popped her head around the door.
      </p>
      <p>
       Your next client isnt for an hour, Dr Furr.  Want me to switch you off in the meantime?
      </p>
      <p>
       No, I like the view out of the window at this time of day.  Are you heading to lunch?
      </p>
      <p>
       Yeah, Ill be back in 45 minutes.
      </p>
      <p>
       See you then.
      </p>
      <p>
       She left.  Richard sat in his chair and stared at the water jug.
      </p>
      <p>
       He was thirsty.  Hed been thirsty for four years, ever since they had switched him on and a lawyer he had never met before explained about the car crash.  The water jug was an affectation, something to make him feel more human.
      </p>
      <p>
       These days, despite what he said to his clients, feeling human was hard to come by.
      </p>
      <p>

      <h4>
       What is Flash Fiction?
      </h4>
      <div class="textwidget">
       <p>
        <i>
         "Flash fiction is fiction with its teeth bared and its claws extended, lithe and muscular with no extra fat. It pounces in the first paragraph, and if those claws arent embedded in the reader by the start of the second, the story began a paragraph too soon. There is no margin for error. Every word must be essential, and if it isnt essential, it must be eliminated."
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        Kathy Kachelries, Founding Member
