             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               "Arancino," after the resemblance to a "little orange," is the usual name for this sort of stuffed, fried rice ball. "Piccola mela" does roll off the tongue, true, but "Italian apple" is more evocative. Mine was filled with sauteed spinach and garlic ($4). At an indoor market, a satisfying snack; nearer the deep fryer, surely even better.
              </span>
             </p>

            <a href="http://www.eatingintranslation.com/2014/05/el-bochinche.html">
             El Bochinche
            </a>
           </h3>
           <div class="entry-content">
            <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
            <div class="entry-body">
             <div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 10px; margin-right: 10px;">
              <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://newyorkfood.typepad.com/.a/6a00d8341c58bb53ef01a3fd048e7b970b-popup" onclick="window.open( this.href, '_blank', 'width=640,height=480,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0' ); return false" style="float: left;">
               <img alt="Mote pillo, El Bochinche, Ridgewood, Queens " class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d8341c58bb53ef01a3fd048e7b970b img-responsive" src="http://newyorkfood.typepad.com/.a/6a00d8341c58bb53ef01a3fd048e7b970b-250wi" style="width: 240px; border: 2px solid #000000; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="Mote pillo, El Bochinche, Ridgewood, Queens "/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 10px; margin-right: 10px;">
              <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://newyorkfood.typepad.com/.a/6a00d8341c58bb53ef01a511b43b4d970c-popup" onclick="window.open( this.href, '_blank', 'width=640,height=480,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0' ); return false" style="float: left;">
               <img alt="Mote pillo, El Bochinche, Ridgewood, Queens" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d8341c58bb53ef01a511b43b4d970c img-responsive" src="http://newyorkfood.typepad.com/.a/6a00d8341c58bb53ef01a511b43b4d970c-250wi" style="width: 240px; border: 2px solid #000000; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="Mote pillo, El Bochinche, Ridgewood, Queens"/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               St. Brigid's, a Roman Catholic church on the Ridgewood-Bushwick border, was named in 1887 for a patron saint of Ireland, and in the early years of the 20th century it came to serve a largely German-American congregation. Today many congregants and neighbors are Ecuadorian, judging by the abundance and pedigree of the street food nearby on a recent Sunday.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               This plate of mote pillo ($5) has its roots in Cuenca, a city in Ecuador's southern highlands also famous for
               <a height="400" href="http://www.eatingintranslation.com/2010/04/mi-pequeno-almavar.html" target="_blank" width="600">
                fanesca
               </a>
               . At the simplest mote pillo consists of eggs and hominy scrambled in the presence of onion, garlic, cilantro, and (for color) achiote. A few members of our scouting party suggested that meat would improve it, and indeed in Cuenca a related dish, mote sucio, also includes kernels of hominy (mote) that have been sauteed with pork, or in pork gravy, so they become "sucio" ("dirty").
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               The question then arises: What is "pillo"? The only translations I've been able to find suggest "rascally" or "roguish," which is a stretch for a dish that is not even very piquant (at least in this instance). If you can supply a better etymology for the pillo in mote pillo, please do.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               On the side: quaker (Quack-air, not shown, $3), a beverage that smoothly blends oatmeal, name-brand or not, with a little orange juice. In Ecuador, quaker traditionally includes naranjilla, also known as
               <a height="400" href="http://www.eatingintranslation.com/2008/01/aqui-colombia-a.html" target="_blank" width="600">
                lulo
               </a>
               ; the O.J. is a U.S. substitution. This quaker was also flavored with apple and cinnamon (nicely noted, missmasala!), giving the impression, in the best possible flashback-to-childhood sense, of packaged instant oatmeal, now in drinkable form.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               El Bochinche
               <br/>
               Street cart on St. Nicholas Ave. between Linden St. and Gates Ave., Ridgewood, Queens
               <br/>
               Friday through Sunday, early afternoon till early evening, year-round
              </span>
             </p>

              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">

               <a height="400" href="http://www.eatingintranslation.com/2011/04/red-hook-ball-fields.html" target="_blank" width="600">
                Red Hook
               </a>
               . Apart from a truck selling soft-serve ice cream, a recent weekend visit turned up only a pair of Ecuadorian vendors grilling chuzos, or skewered meats. The busier of the two also deep-fried hot dogs and sliced potatoes for salchipapas (about $4). The name is a portmanteau of "salchicha" and "papas" (sausage and potatoes); this batch was also well-dressed with optional lettuce, tomato, onion, and mayo. Foot traffic supplied most of this vendor's clientele, but over the course of a quarter-hour she also supplied curb service to several cars that pulled up alongside.

Obviously it's from a bird, mate, but who can say what bloody flavour it is? Not me; I've never tried huevo de avestrus.

              </a>
             </div>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               True, "alcapurrias dos veces frito" doesn't have the ring of "twice-fried fries." But you get the idea: First they're deep-fried at a relatively low temperature to cook them through, then on higher heat to create that crisp shell. (French fries, whose preparation is often the subject of
               <a height="400" href="http://aht.seriouseats.com/archives/2010/01/the-burger-lab-why-double-fry-french-fries.html" target="_blank" width="600">
                exhaustive analysis
               </a>
               , may be a slightly different story, since they're all-potato and much thinner.)
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               As at the yearly
               <a height="400" href="http://www.eatingintranslation.com/2014/06/festival-cultur.html" target="_blank" width="600">
                Festival Cultural de la Calle 152
               </a>
               , the two operations are performed simultaneously, side by side, to satisfy a continual demand for the ground-beef-filled frituras. The bright color of this alcapurria (ahl-cah-Poor-ree-ah, $1.50 each) comes from an unusually generous ration of
               <a height="400" href="http://gernot-katzers-spice-pages.com/engl/Bixa_ore.html" target="_blank" width="600">
                annatto
               </a>
               .
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               Also shown: a sandwich de pernil ($6); the cut edges of the loaf seemed to have been wiped with garlic. Ensalada de pulpo, pinchos de pollo, bacalaitos, pastellitos, pasteles en hoja, and morcilla were also on offer; a rotisserie was not in action this day.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               Las Alcapurrias
               <br/>
               Outdoor stall in the used-car and auto-repair lot at the corner of Cooper and Wyckoff Aves., Ridgewood, Queens
               <br/>
               Saturdays and Sundays, 10:00-8:00
               <br/>
               (Via a tip from an EIT reader and devoted bicycle rider; thanks, Mark!)
              </span>
