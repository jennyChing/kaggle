       <h1>
        Mr Fluffy: Homeowner with mesothelioma brings landmark case against Commonwealth over asbestos in his roof
       </h1>
       <div class="byline">
        Exclusive by the National Reporting Team's
        <a href="/news/lorna-knowles/5632058" target="_self" title="">

        </a>

       <p>
        A man who says he got mesothelioma from exposure to "Mr Fluffy" loose-fill asbestos in his home is bringing a landmark legal case against the Commonwealth.
       </p>
       <p>
        Chris Georgiou is suing the Federal Government in the New South Wales Dust Diseases Tribunal, alleging it was negligent because it failed to stop the installation of the Mr Fluffy product in homes and failed to warn residents of the health dangers.
       </p>
       <p>
        His lawyer said if successful, the case could pave the way for many more claims.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Georgiou, a retired jeweller, told the ABC he did not have a safe at home, so he used to hide valuable items in his roof space.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I didn't want to have them in the house. So I hid them in the roof, on top of the fluffy asbestos," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I used to use a ladder or a chair and I would put my hand in. So I was touching the asbestos."
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Georgiou also went into the roof space several times with his son to install pink batts and aluminium foil.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I was full of fluffy on my clothes, I had to change and put them in the bin for washing," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        He worries that his son, who lives in America, might also get the deadly cancer.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It's a hidden sickness. I don't know how long it will take him to know he's safe. How does he know he's safe?" Mr Georgiou said.
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content wysiwyg full">
        <div class="inner">
         <strong>
          Do you know more about this story? Email
          <a href="mailto:investigations@abc.net.au" target="_self" title="">
           investigations@abc.net.au
          </a>
         </strong>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Georgiou and his wife bought their house in the Canberra suburb of Macgregor in 1978.
       </p>
       <p>
        In 1992 the family moved out briefly while ACT Government contractors removed the loose-fill asbestos.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Georgiou sold the house, where he raised his four children, in 2003 and it was another decade before he was diagnosed with mesothelioma.
       </p>
       <p>
        He suffers shortness of breath and has fluid on his lungs, which must be drained regularly.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It's devastating because since I retired and I sold the shop, I had the best time - the best time in my life," Mr Georgiou said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I retired. I make so many trips overseas. Now everything is stopped."
       </p>
       <h2>
        Commonwealth warned of asbestos dangers
       </h2>
       <p>
        In a statement of claim, Mr Georgiou's lawyers said the Commonwealth breached its duty of care to Mr Georgiou because it was warned by a physicist in 1968 that asbestos insulation in houses was dangerous.
       </p>
       <p>
        "In mid-1968, the ACT Health Services Branch, then a Commonwealth body, requested Mr Gersch Major, a physicist, to investigate the possible hazards that might arise from the installation of asbestos fibre insulation in houses in the ACT," the statement read.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Gersch inspected two houses in the ACT where the product was being installed and noted: "The men doing the insulation work were exposed to excessive asbestos dust and it was unwise for them to be working with this material".
       </p>
       <p>
        He added the Government should consider whether to stop Dirk Jansen, the owner of the Mr Fluffy company, from operating.
       </p>
       <p>
        According to the statement of claim, by the time the asbestos was installed in Mr Georgiou's house (between 1975 and 1978) even more knowledge was available about the health dangers of asbestos and the fact that even relatively small exposures to asbestos could cause mesothelioma.
       </p>
       <p>
        It alleges the Commonwealth was negligent because it failed to prevent Mr Jansen installing the product in homes and failed to warn residents of the dangers.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The Commonwealth knew, or ought to have known, of the risk of harm to residents of houses such as the Macgregor house, including the plaintiff, if it did not exercise its powers," the statement read.
       </p>
       <p>
        As a consequence, Mr Georgiou suffered malignant pleural mesothelioma, shock, severe chest pain and breathlessness, fatigue, nausea, weight loss and "imminent death".
       </p>
       <p>
        Comcare, the Commonwealth agency handling the claim, has not yet been required to file a statement in reply.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Test case among other Mr Fluffy incidents
       </h2>
       <p>
        Mr Georgiou's lawyer Theodora Ahilas, head of the asbestos litigation unit at Maurice Blackburn Lawyers, said his was an important test case.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Mr Georgiou is a robust 80-plus [year old] gentleman living his golden years diagnosed with an insidious disease known as mesothelioma, which is a disease which is only caused by asbestos exposure," she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Unfortunately there is no known threshold for exposure giving rise to mesothelioma.
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content quote right">
        <blockquote class="pullquote">
         <div class="quote">
          <p>
           We've seen the beginning but I don't think we've seen the end of this type of case.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="cite">
          Lawyer Theodora Ahilas
         </div>
        </blockquote>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        "Any exposure to asbestos can give rise to mesothelioma."
       </p>
       <p>
        She said the disease causes terrible pain and suffering.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It is a cancer of the lining of the pleura. It is generally a fatal condition. It is a preventable disease [and] we have the highest incidence of it in the world," Ms Ahilas said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It's something that shouldn't have happened to Mr Georgiou or anyone else."
       </p>
       <p>
        Ms Ahilas said an increasing number of people were developing the cancer after being exposed to asbestos in their homes, often during renovations.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It's a very important issue to address these days because as we have more exposure to DIY home renovations, it's important to identify that any exposure like this could give rise to a disease like this in the future," she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "We currently have about four or five other people who have contacted us, who want to be advised on personal injuries relating to Mr Fluffy.
       </p>
       <p>
        "We've seen the beginning but I don't think we've seen the end of this type of case.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I think there will be more people who will come forward with this type of exposure."
       </p>
