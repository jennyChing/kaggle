         <div id="article_body">
          <p>
           <strong>
            Overclocking
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           I was greatly satisfied by the overclock achieved on the X2 555 Black Edition; I got almost 250MHz higher than its predecessor the
           <a href="http://www.neoseeker.com/Articles/Hardware/Reviews/pii550/12.html">
            X2 550
           </a>
           , thanks to the C3 revision. In fact, it is the best stable overclock I achieved on a Deneb die, on standard room temperature air cooling. I began by increasing the core multiplier along with the voltage, to find out that a multiplier of x21 was too high. Now you would wonder why, because 20.5x200MHz also equals 4100MHz; with the HT link reference clock set at stock, the Gigabyte motherboard overclocks it slightly, effectively bringing the processor at 4125MHz. Once I found out that the ideal core clock was around 4100MHz, I played with the HT link and CPU-NB multipliers. The memory controller overclocked very well, reaching almost 2900MHz with only 1.30V.
          </p>
          <p>
           Here are the final settings I used:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Baseclock of 205MHz
           </li>
           <li>
            CPU multiplier of x20, for a clock of 4100MHz
           </li>
           <li>
            HT multiplier of x13, for a clock of 2665MHz
           </li>
           <li>
            Memory ratio of 1:4, for a clock of 1640MHz
           </li>
           <li>
            CPU-NB multiplier of x14, for a clock of 2870MHz
           </li>
           <li>
            CPU voltage of 1.525V
           </li>
           <li>
            CPU-NB voltage of 1.30V
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <img height="324" src="http://i.neoseeker.com/a/pii_555/piix2oc.png" width="670"/>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Power Consumption
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <img height="304" src="http://i.neoseeker.com/a/pii_555/power.png" width="350"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           The Phenom II X2 555 consumes on average 8W more than the 550 under full load, due to the 100MHz higher frequency. At idle it remains the same though, way under that of the E8400.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Conclusion
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           I definitely like the idea of creating a new dual-core "Callisto" processor on the C3 revision. I was very impressed by the overclock I managed to get out of it; 4100MHz is the highest fully stable frequency I got on a Phenom II, on standard air cooling. Recently, I've put the 3.1GHz Phenom II X2 under LN2 and I was greatly satisfied, coming out on top of all dual-cores in a local competition. Needless to say I look forward to try this new one now!
          </p>
          <p>
           I did expect a power consumption at least the same or lower than the X2 550 though, seeing that AMD could reduce the TDP of the Phenom II X4 965 down to 125W with that revision. Unfortunately, no progress was made on that, but it is not dramatic; an extra 8W under full load is not what will make a difference.
          </p>
          <p>
           As for its performance, it is obviously great to see it replace its predecessor for the same price, thus offering you more bang for your buck! Also, let's not forget it is a Black Edition! I was impressed to see it beat the similarly priced Pentium G6950 almost in every case. It was not the same game against the Core i3-530 though, which is Hyper-Threading enabled, but costs $25 more. Of course, the E8400 is not in the race anymore with its scandalous price of $168.
          </p>
