      <h2>
       Welcome to my blog!
      </h2>
      <p>
       I have created this blog as a way to document my journey through the ever changing field of technology. Navigating the different frameworks that seem to pop up almost every week can be tricky - not to mention time consuming. I will do my best to offer fresh and relevant content, but do drop me a line on twitter if you have ideas for content you would like to see covered. My handle is
       <a href="https://twitter.com/helgevold">
        @helgevold
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Generally I am interested in a wide array of technological topics, but lately my content has been very JavaScript focused. The JavaScript community is particularly active and relevant at the moment. If you haven't embraced JavaScript beyond document.getElementById yet - do yourself a favor and do it today. Otherwise you might find it even harder to catch up later when you've fallen even further behind.
      </p>
      <p>
       That said I am not advocating overuse of JavaScript frameworks. I always recommend that you learn stellar JavaScript fundamentals before jumping into frameworks. Only use frameworks where direct and measurable value is added. Not only will this show you that frameworks are often redundant -  it will also keep your skills much more flexible than if you lock hands with a particular framework. If you read my blog I will show you several examples of how to limit the footprint of framework dependencies to ensure more flexible and portable code. Given the fluid framework situation I can't emphasize enough how important it is to not buy into the gospel of any one framework.
      </p>
      <p>
       This whole summary would be a giant waste of time if I didn't also address unit testing. Lack of unit testing is a big no no in my book. The biggest obstacle to successful unit testing is writing code without  tests in mind. In several of my articles I will describe how to structure code in a way that lets you write realistic and useful unit tests. Quality tests is really what it comes down to. Far too many developers get themselves into a bind where their code is too hard to test. The result is often test code that is orders of magnitude more complicated than the code under test. Hopefully I am not talking about you, but if I am, fret not, I will help you work on your testing skills.
      </p>
