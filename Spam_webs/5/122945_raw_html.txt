     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Jim Henson Hour
       </b>
      </i>
      is a short-lived
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">
       television series
      </a>
      that aired on
      <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">
       NBC
      </a>
      in 1989. It was developed as a showcase for
      <a href="/wiki/The_Jim_Henson_Company" title="The Jim Henson Company">
       The Jim Henson Company
      </a>
      's various puppet creations, including the popular
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muppet" title="Muppet">
       Muppet
      </a>
      characters. Only nine of the twelve episodes produced managed to air on NBC before the low-rated program was cancelled. Two episodes later aired on
      <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon" title="Nickelodeon">
       Nickelodeon
      </a>
      in 1992 and 1993, and the final episode never aired in the US, but did air in the UK in 1990.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Format">
       Format
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jim_Henson_Hour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Format">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Jim Henson Hour
      </i>
      was modeled after the old
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_anthology_television_series" title="Walt Disney anthology television series">
        Walt Disney Presents
       </a>
      </i>
      specials, in which every week Disney would show off the latest innovations and creations of his production company. At the beginning of each episode,
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Henson" title="Jim Henson">
       Jim Henson
      </a>
      would enter an oddly-decorated set (alongside the Thought Lion puppet from his series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Storyteller" title="The Storyteller">
        The Storyteller
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and introduce the evening's show. Beyond that, the series never had a set structure. The room where Jim and the Thought Lion performed their introduction was computer animated.
     </p>
     <p>
      Three of the thirteen installments were hour-long mini-movies:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The faux
       <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">
        film noir
       </a>
       "Dog City", narrated by Muppet
       <a href="/wiki/Rowlf_the_Dog" title="Rowlf the Dog">
        Rowlf the Dog
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       "Monster Maker", in which an alienated teenager begins secretly working at a special-effects company
      </li>
      <li>
       "Living with Dinosaurs", in which a young boy's stuffed Dinosaur comes to life and helps him deal with a troubled family life.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Other shows like "Secrets of the Muppets" went behind the scenes at Henson studios, showing how the Muppets are built and operated.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ordinarily, however, the hour was split into two thirty-minute segments. These shows would always start with a modernized variation of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Muppet_Show" title="The Muppet Show">
        The Muppet Show
       </a>
      </i>
      , titled
      <i>
       MuppeTelevision
      </i>
      . That would often lead into more serious and sometimes darker content, such as a rerun of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Storyteller" title="The Storyteller">
        The Storyteller
       </a>
      </i>
      . Occasionally, a light-hearted story or more Muppet antics would close out the hour in the second half.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first episode produced
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sesame_Street..._20_Years_%26_Still_Counting" title="Sesame Street... 20 Years &amp; Still Counting">
        Sesame Street... 20 Years &amp; Still Counting
       </a>
      </i>
      was aired as a lone special. Henson's series officially premiered a week later.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="MuppeTelevision">
       <i>
        MuppeTelevision
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jim_Henson_Hour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: MuppeTelevision">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       MuppeTelevision
      </i>
      regularly occupied the first half of
      <i>
       The Jim Henson Hour
      </i>
      . It was an updated version of the classic series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Muppet_Show" title="The Muppet Show">
        The Muppet Show
       </a>
      </i>
      , the new twist being that
      <a href="/wiki/The_Muppets" title="The Muppets">
       the Muppets
      </a>
      were now running an entire cable television network rather than a single variety show. The Muppets broadcast their network's programming from a unique control room called "Muppet Central". Regulars included past favorites
      <a href="/wiki/Kermit_the_Frog" title="Kermit the Frog">
       Kermit the Frog
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonzo_the_Great" title="Gonzo the Great">
       The Great Gonzo
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Link_Hogthrob" title="Link Hogthrob">
       Link Hogthrob
      </a>
      in addition to new characters Digit, Leon the
      <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">
       Lizard
      </a>
      , Lindbergh the
      <a href="/wiki/Kiwi" title="Kiwi">
       Kiwi
      </a>
      , Vicki,
      <a href="/wiki/Clifford_(Muppet)" title="Clifford (Muppet)">
       Clifford
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waldo_C._Graphic" title="Waldo C. Graphic">
       Waldo C. Graphic
      </a>
      , and Jacques
      <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">
       Roach
      </a>
      . Also appearing as a series regular was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bean_Bunny" title="Bean Bunny">
       Bean Bunny
      </a>
      , who had previously starred in the
      <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">
       HBO
      </a>
      TV special
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_the_Bunny_Picnic" title="The Tale of the Bunny Picnic">
        The Tale of the Bunny Picnic
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Fozzie_Bear" title="Fozzie Bear">
       Fozzie Bear
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Miss_Piggy" title="Miss Piggy">
       Miss Piggy
      </a>
      appeared only intermittently, as their performer
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Oz" title="Frank Oz">
       Frank Oz
      </a>
      was busy with a directorial career. Miss Piggy did get her own thirty-minute special in one show, called
      <i>
       Miss Piggy's Hollywood,
      </i>
      in which she and Gonzo tried to interview unwilling celebrities.
     </p>
     <p>
      The house band for MuppeTelevision was called Solid Foam, taking the place of the psychedelic
      <a href="/wiki/Dr._Teeth_and_The_Electric_Mayhem" title="Dr. Teeth and The Electric Mayhem">
       Electric Mayhem
      </a>
      band that had appeared in most previous Muppet projects. The band included:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Digit on keyboard
      </li>
      <li>
       Flash on saxophone and vocals
      </li>
      <li>
       Clifford on bass guitar and vocals
      </li>
      <li>
       Beard on guitar and vocals
      </li>
      <li>
       The unnamed Female Drummer of the Solid Foam on drums
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Electric Mayhem regulars Zoot and
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_(Muppet)" title="Animal (Muppet)">
       Animal
      </a>
      did eventually creep into Solid Foam in the episode "Food." Only Clifford would sustain any existence outside of MuppeTelevision.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._Teeth_and_the_Electric_Mayhem" title="Dr. Teeth and the Electric Mayhem">
       Dr. Teeth
      </a>
      also appeared in the background of a few of Solid Foam's music videos.
     </p>
     <p>
      MuppeTelevision also tends to get interrupted on some occasions by an illegal TV station called
      <i>
       Gorilla Television
      </i>
      run by Ubu the
      <a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">
       Gorilla
      </a>
      , Chip, and Zondra.
     </p>
     <p>
      Continuing in
      <i>
       The Muppet Show
      </i>
      tradition, every episode had a celebrity guest star.
      <a href="/wiki/Louie_Anderson" title="Louie Anderson">
       Louie Anderson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ted_Danson" title="Ted Danson">
       Ted Danson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Robinson" title="Smokey Robinson">

          in "The Soldier and Death"  Taken from an early
          <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">
           Russian
          </a>
          folk tale retold in English by
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Michell_Ransome" title="Arthur Michell Ransome">

          <i>

           </a>

          <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">
           soldier
          </a>
          (played by
          <a href="/wiki/Bob_Peck" title="Bob Peck">
           Bob Peck
          </a>
          ) returns home after 20 years of war, with three
          <a href="/wiki/Biscuit" title="Biscuit">
           biscuits
          </a>
          in his knapsack. On his way he meets three
          <a href="/wiki/Begging" title="Begging">

          <a href="/wiki/Whistling" title="Whistling">
           whistle
          </a>
          , one the jolliest dance, and the final man, who gets the last biscuit despite the soldier being hungry himself, in return gives him a pack of magic playing cards and a musty sack that has the power to trap anything ordered into it. Using the sack, the soldier manages to trap a flock of
          <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">
           geese
          </a>
          , and so manages to feed himself. Upon arriving at an abandoned castle overrun with small
          <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">
           devils
          </a>
          , he plays them in a game of cards, winning 40 barrels of gold, and when they try to kill him, he captures them in the sack only letting them go when they promise to never return. He makes one of them swear to serve him and keeps its foot as leverage. Quickly becoming rich and famous because he removed the devils from a palace that is owned by the
          <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">
           Tzar
          </a>
          (played by
          <a href="/wiki/John_Franklyn-Robbins" title="John Franklyn-Robbins">
           John Franklyn-Robbins
          </a>
          ), his luck runs short when the soldier's son becomes deathly ill. Calling upon the devil, the soldier is given a glass goblet that allows the owner to see
          <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)" title="Death (personification)">
           Death
          </a>
          (performed by
          <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alistair_Fullarton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alistair Fullarton (page does not exist)">
           Alistair Fullarton
          </a>
          ). If Death is at the foot of the person's bed (as was the case with his son), he or she will recover if sprinkled with water from the goblet. If Death is at the head of the bed, nothing can be done. Then the Tzar becomes ill and the soldier (seeing Death at the head of his bed) makes a bargain with Death: his life in exchange for the Tzar's. Death takes his offer and gives the illness to the soldier, curing the Tzar. Lying in his death bed, he summons Death into his sack, and stops death from happening everywhere. But as time goes on, he sees people everywhere who are waiting for death that will not come. So he frees Death, who fears the soldier and his sack so much that he refuses to take the soldier's life. The soldier, old and weary of life, seeks out a way to die. He travels down to the underworld, forcing the devils at the gates (the same ones from before) to give him two hundred souls and a map to heaven. Terrified of the sack, the devils agree to his demands. Upon reaching the gates of heaven, he asks to be let in with the souls while begging for forgiveness from
          <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
           God
          </a>
          , but is denied by the
          <a href="/wiki/Gatekeeper" title="Gatekeeper">
           gatekeeper
          </a>
          . He gives the sack to one of the souls, asking the soul to summon him into the sack when he has passed through the gates. But since there is no memory in heaven, the soul forgets and the soldier is condemned to live forever upon the Earth. In closing, the storyteller remarks (with a smile) that the soldier is still probably about his business. As the Storyteller tosses the bag aside, a devil emerges from the bag unnoticed by the Storyteller or the dog.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cancellation_and_.22lost.22_episodes">
       Cancellation and "lost" episodes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jim_Henson_Hour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: Cancellation and "lost" episodes'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The show frequently acknowledged its own low ratings, with segments offering satirical takes on what viewers would rather watchviolent movies, ridiculous stunts, etc. In the end, the show produced just twelve episodes, three of which did not make it to air before cancellation.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1992, children's cable network
      <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon" title="Nickelodeon">
       Nickelodeon
      </a>
      aired
      <i>
       Secrets of the Muppets
      </i>
      , one of the lost episodes. They followed with another unaired episode,
      <i>
       Living with Dinosaurs
      </i>
      , in 1993. The final hourconsisting of the MuppeTelevision installment "Food" and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Storyteller" title="The Storyteller">
        Storyteller
       </a>
      </i>
      episode "The Three Ravens"aired in the UK in 1990. This is the only episode of
      <i>
       The Jim Henson Hour
      </i>
      that has never aired in the US.
     </p>
     <p>
      After
      <i>
       The Jim Henson Hour
      </i>
      , the Muppets would not have another prime-time TV show until
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Muppets_Tonight" title="Muppets Tonight">
        Muppets Tonight
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1996, six years after Jim Henson's death.
     </p>
     <p>
      Today, the
      <i>
       MuppeTelevision
      </i>
      segments are bundled with the original
      <i>
       Muppet Show
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Muppets Tonight
      </i>
      episodes into a single syndication package.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">
       Ownership
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jim_Henson_Hour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ownership">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Following the sale of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Muppets" title="The Muppets">
       The Muppets
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">
       The Walt Disney Company
      </a>
      in 2004, the rights to various portions of the show have been split between Disney and the Jim Henson Company. The Walt Disney Company owns all of the MuppeTelevision segments (including the 15-minute episode shown with
      <i>
       Dog City
      </i>
      ),
      <i>
       Miss Piggy's Hollywood
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       The Secrets of the Muppets
      </i>
      , while The Jim Henson Company retains ownership of the rest of the series.
     </p>
