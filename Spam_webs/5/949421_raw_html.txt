        <p>
         I love books. Always have done. I could read before I started school (thanks to my mum!)  and I spent my childhood and early teens with my nose in a book.
        </p>
        <p>
         At university, I studied English literature for three wonderful years. And when I got married, we had our wedding reception in the Bodleian Library in Oxford, with actual books on the tables for our guests to sign
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3360" height="375" src="http://www.aliventures.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Books-DorianGrey.jpg" title="Books-DorianGrey" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         So, for quite a while, I couldnt see the appeal of e-readers. Surely, they could never replicate the experience of reading an actual book  with rustling pages, dog-eared corners, and that book smell. (Please tell me Im not the only person who sniffs books.)
        </p>
        <p>
         But then two book-loving acquaintances got Kindles:
         <a href="http://www.godspell.org.uk/wordpress/">
          Tony Price
         </a>
         (former librarian, current vicar) and
         <a href="http://fictionfire.co.uk/">
          Lorna Fergusson
         </a>
         (author and creative writing teacher). I began to rethink the whole ebook thing. And now, our household has not one but two Kindles, and Ive got my beady eye on the newly-announced Kindle Fire.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          If youre on the fence about getting a Kindle  or sceptical, like I was  then heres why you might want to take the plunge
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-3359">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3361" height="375" src="http://www.aliventures.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Books-and-Kindles.jpg" title="Books-and-Kindles" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <em>
          The Kindle on the top is the newer model (Kindle 3) in standard size.
          <br/>
          The Kindle on the bottom is a bit older (Kindle 2) in the DX size.
         </em>
        </p>
        <h2>
         The Reading Experience
        </h2>
        <p>
         My main worry with the Kindle was that it wouldnt feel like
         <em>
          reading
         </em>
         . After all, I dont particularly like reading long texts on the computer.
        </p>
        <p>
         Rest assured,
         <strong>
          it becomes perfectly natural after just a few pages.
         </strong>
         Once Im into a book, it doesnt make much difference whether Im reading it on paper or on the Kindle  its the story that matters.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Kindle also keeps your place automatically  very handy.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive got the bigger DX version, which I tend to hold in both hands (or balance on something)  my husband has the normal-sized version, which is easy to hold in one hand. Since Im tiny and my husband is a giant, we look rather comical with our mismatched Kindles
        </p>
        <h2>
         Travelling
        </h2>
        <p>
         We went on a three-week trip round Europe in September, and read somewhere close to 30 books between us. (You can
         <a href="http://www.aliventures.com/holiday-reading/">
          find my reading list here
         </a>
         .) If wed taken those 30 books in printed form, wed have definitely been struggling with our luggage
        </p>
        <p>
         If you travel even a couple of times a year, youll find the Kindle very handy. Not only can you take as many books as you want, you can also buy books on the go. And you can keep music or audio books on there (theres a headphone socket on the Kindle) if you want.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unlike all my other electronic devices, the Kindle has a long enough battery life to easily last through a 10-hour plane trip. So long as you turn off the 3G/wifi connection, your Kindle will give you hours of reading time without needing a recharge.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Internet Access
        </h2>
        <p>
         If you get the 3G version of the Kindle, you get
         <em>
          worldwide
         </em>
         internet access. That means that you can download books from anywhere  but it also means you can use the Kindles (admittedly rudimentary) web browser.
        </p>
        <p>
         This is incredibly useful if youre in a hotel without wifi, or out and about, and you need to look something up quickly. You can even tweet from the Kindle!
        </p>
        <h2>
         Cheap and Free Books
        </h2>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignright size-full wp-image-3362" height="500" src="http://www.aliventures.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/Books-CharlotteBronte.jpg" title="Books-CharlotteBronte" width="375"/>
         Books that are out of copyright (70+ years after the authors death, in the UK) are freely available to download straight from Amazon. These include classic books by Jane Austen, Charles Dickens, George Eliot, and so on.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          If you look at the Kindle top 100 bestsellers, youll see lots of books priced at under $5.
         </strong>
         Some are just 99 cents. You
         <em>
          might
         </em>
         be able to pick up books that cheaply at second-hand sales locally, but the Kindle offers a lot more convenience and choice. A fair percentage of these cheaper books are self-published by (increasingly net savvy) indie authors. Theres no overhead for the publisher, so the reader gets a great deal and the author actually nets
         <em>
          more
         </em>
         royalties per book.
        </p>
        <p>
         You can also transfer files from your computer  so if you have a book in PDF form, you can put it onto your Kindle.
        </p>
        <h2>
         What I Dont Like About the Kindle
        </h2>
        <p>
         Precious little! Mine is a Kindle 2, so I wish it was the newer, smoother, model.
         <img alt=";-)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://www.aliventures.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/icon_wink.gif"/>
         Im also not sure that DX was the right choice for me  I thought Id be reading more PDFs on it, but in practice, Ive mostly just read Kindle books.
        </p>
        <p>
         Id like a better web browser, but, in fairness, thats not really what the Kindles designed to do, and Im grateful that it has a web browser at all.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         So  should you get a Kindle?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          If you love books, Id say a definite
          <em>
           yes!
          </em>
         </strong>
         Youll find yourself buying and reading more books, and quite probably expanding your reading tastes  its easier to try something new when its cheap (or free!) The Kindle is relatively cheap (compared with, say, an iPad) and if you have more than one Kindle in your household, you can even read the same ebook at the same time.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Choosing Your Kindle
        </h2>
        <p>
         There are several types of Kindle, so Ill try to make the choice a bit easier!
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Go for a basic Kindle (normal size, wifi only) if you dont plan to use it when travelling and you have wifi at home
         </li>
         <li>
          Go for a 3G Kindle if youre going to be travelling with it (even just commuting). Note that, in the UK, this means buying the keyboard version.
         </li>
         <li>
          Go for a Kindle with a keyboard if you plan to type short notes or do a lot of book-searching on the Kindle itself
         </li>
         <li>
          Go for a Kindle DX (the big one) if you want to read lots of PDFs on your Kindle  Amazon.com only: UK readers, you have to buy the international DX
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         You can see the range of Kindles on
         <a href="http://www.amazon.co.uk/gp/product/B0051QVF7A/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=alisgar-21&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1634&amp;creative=19450&amp;creativeASIN=B0051QVF7A">
          Amazon.co.uk here
         </a>
         (scroll down a bit to see the comparison chart) and on
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0051QVESA/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=aliventures-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=217145&amp;creative=399373&amp;creativeASIN=B0051QVESA">
          Amazon.com here
         </a>
         (again, scroll down).
        </p>
        <p>
         Disclaimer: Ive not tried the new keyboard-less version of the Kindle. I dont type very much on mine, though, so I imagine that using the on-screen keyboard would be fine.
        </p>
        <p>
         And if you dont want to fork out for a Kindle,
         <strong>
          remember that you dont need to own a Kindle in order to read Amazons ebooks.
         </strong>
         You can download Kindle apps for your iPad, iPhone, Android or even your PC too.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          If you own a Kindle, Id love to hear your take on all this in the comments. And if you have a different type of ereader, let me know how it compares with the Kindle!
         </em>
        </p>

           HI Ali,
           <br/>
           I got a Kindle 2 for my daughter who loves to read and she even discourages me from getting her the newer version.  She loves hers and kept telling me that I should get one.  It took a while.
          </p>
          <p>
           Yes, Im a book lover, and I still enjoy reading regular old physical books, but I wouldnt be without my Kindle.  I own a Kindle 3.1, which is the keyboard model that amazon claims holds up to 1500 books and with a one-month battery charge (not using the lamp). I also bought the leather case with the pull out reading lamp.  And I would buy both again, although I would of course get the newer version (have not tried the touch screen or Fire versions.
          </p>
          <p>
           What I like about it:
          </p>
          <p>
           As you pointed out, you can haul around a tremendous selection of books in a small and light package.  I take mine whenever I go places when I am likely to have to wait, like a doctors office and any government agency!
          </p>
          <p>
           I like the better prices and the freebies.
          </p>
          <p>
           I use the reading lamp a lot.  Since the screen is not backlit, having that lamp makes for more comfortable reading in low-light areas.
          </p>
          <p>
           I love that I can change the type size for comfortable reading. At almost age 70 now this helps!
           <br/>
           I also use the PC version frequently. I find the Kindle keyboard awkward and frankly a pain to use.  So I like searching, hiliting (all of which I do a lot!), and making notes via my PC. When I download a book, I download it to both my Kindle and my PC Kindleso I can go either way.
          </p>
          <p>
           I especially enjoy late in the evening getting cozy in my bed are reading on my Kindle.  The page is slightly off white and the type is dark in contrast, so the reading is easy on my eyes.  Plus its lighter that a lot of books, particularly large ones.
          </p>
          <p>
           What could be improved (and yes, I have told Amazon these things):
           <br/>
           1.  A separate small, thin, plug-in keyboard easier to use would be wonderful.
           <br/>
           2.  The charging port is on the bottom edge, which makes it awkward to read while charging (I often rest my Kindle on my tummy).
           <br/>
           3.  Searching and leaving through a book could be improved.  Sometimes the search capacity doesnt work well; other times its fine.
           <br/>
           4.  And, to me, those page-turning buttons on the left and right edges, while quite convenient for easy
           <br/>
           page turns, are way to easy to accidentally hit and turn a page when you dont want to.
          </p>
          <p>
           I have to get to a local store and check out both this color version and the new touch screen model.
          </p>
          <p>
           Overall, if you read a lot, I highly recommend a Kindle (but keep in mind it is the only electronic portable reader I have tried), especially at the latest low prices.  The convenience and readability are awesome.
          </p>
          <p>
           And,Ali, Im still enjoying your freelance online course.

          <p>
           Just a few thoughts 
          </p>
          <p>
           If you want to read the Kindle while it is charging, change the orientation of the screen by pressing the Aa button.
          </p>
          <p>
           If you dont whether to get wifi or 3G, I highly recommend 3G. If you dont have wifi connection at home you wont be able to get books without doing a USB transfer. A USB transfer isnt hard but does require a computer at hand to get the new book on your device. If you travel and want to be able to purchase, 3G is best. Wifi isnt as prevalent outside of the US.
          </p>
          <p>
           Not sure you want to pay for books since Kindle can sometimes be as expensive as paper books? There are over 10,000 public libraries who are now working with Overdrive and Amazon to allow you to check out ebooks from your library and read them on your Kindle (even audio books that are not DRM protected). In order to do this you do need to have a wifi connection to get the books (3G doesnt work) or if you dont have a wifi connection youll have to use USB transfer.
          </p>
          <p>
           I do also recommend getting the extended warranty. Amazon is amazing about customer support and replacing devices but its a good idea to spend the extra money to get the extra coverage.

           I have both a Kindle 3 and a Sony Reader (PRS-500).
           <br/>
           Of the two, I now exclusively use the Sony. There are three reasons:
          </p>
          <p>
           1. I dont like the lack of native EPUB on the Kindle. I know I can email it across but that just isnt how I work. A personal thing.
          </p>
          <p>
           2. I dont like Amazon. Their continual attempted power grabs throughout the publishing chain are a major threat to the book industry (not just meaning dead-tree publishers).
          </p>
          <p>
           3. I like to *own* my books. If I buy from Amazon then I can read on any device that *Amazon* blesses with suitable software. Im locked in to them. To move my Kindle purchases onto the Sony I had to break the DRM. I shouldnt have to do that; they are supposed to be *my* books. I paid for them. Yes, EPUB often has DRM (Adobe usually) but they are not limited to one suppliers choice of approved devices.
          </p>
          <p>
           So, if you love books then yes, get an ereader. Easier for the eyes and massively portable.
           <br/>
           Please, though, think carefully before going for the Amazon lock-in.
