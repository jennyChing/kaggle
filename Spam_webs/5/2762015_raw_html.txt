     <p>
      <b>
       Lollardy
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       Lollardry
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Lollardism
      </b>
      ) was a political and religious movement that existed from the mid-14th century to the
      <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">
       English Reformation
      </a>
      . It was initially led by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">
       John Wycliffe
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a prominent
      <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">
       theologian
      </a>
      who was dismissed from the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">
       University of Oxford
      </a>
      in 1381 for criticism of the
      <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
       Church
      </a>
      , especially in his doctrine on the
      <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">
       Eucharist
      </a>
      . The Lollards' demands were primarily for reform of
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">
       Western Christianity
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lollardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lollards_QE4_126.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2228" data-file-width="3193" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Lollards_QE4_126.jpg/220px-Lollards_QE4_126.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Lollards_QE4_126.jpg/330px-Lollards_QE4_126.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Lollards_QE4_126.jpg/440px-Lollards_QE4_126.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lollards_QE4_126.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Lollards' prison in Lambeth Palace
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Lollard
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Lollardi
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Loller
      </i>
      was the popular derogatory nickname given to those without an academic background, educated if at all only in
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      , who were reputed to follow the teachings of John Wycliffe in particular, and were certainly considerably energized by the translation of the Bible into the English language. By the mid-15th century, "lollard" had come to mean a
      <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">
       heretic
      </a>
      in general. The alternative, "Wycliffite", is generally accepted to be a more neutral term covering those of similar opinions, but having an academic background.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term is said to have been coined by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" title="Anglo-Irish">
       Anglo-Irish
      </a>
      cleric,
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Crumpe" title="Henry Crumpe">
       Henry Crumpe
      </a>
      , but its origin is uncertain. According to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
      </i>
      , it most likely derives from
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">
       Middle Dutch
      </a>
      <i>
       <span lang="dum" xml:lang="dum">
        lollaerd
       </span>
      </i>
      "mumbler, mutterer" from a verb
      <i>
       lollen
      </i>
      "to mutter, mumble". It appears to be a derisive expression applied to various people perceived as hereticsfirst the
      <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">
       Franciscans
      </a>
      and later the followers of Wycliffe. Originally the word was a colloquial name for a group of the harmless buriers of the dead during the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">
       Black Death
      </a>
      , in the 14th century, known as
      <a href="/wiki/Alexians" title="Alexians">
       Alexians
      </a>
      , Alexian Brothers or Cellites. These were known colloquially as
      <i>
       <span lang="dum" xml:lang="dum">
        lollebroeders
       </span>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">
       Middle Dutch
      </a>
      ), "mumbling brothers", or
      <i>
       "
       <span lang="goh" xml:lang="goh">
        Lollhorden
       </span>
       "
      </i>
      , from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_High_German_language" title="Old High German language">
       Old High German
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="goh" xml:lang="goh">
       <i>
        lollon
       </i>
      </span>
      , meaning "to sing softly" from their chants for the dead.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Three other possibilities for the derivation of
      <i>
       Lollard
      </i>
      are mentioned by the Oxford English Dictionary,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       name
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         lolium
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vicia_sativa" title="Vicia sativa">
        Common Vetch or tares
       </a>
       , supposedly a reference to the biblical
       <a href="/wiki/Parable_of_the_Tares" title="Parable of the Tares">
        Parable of the Tares
       </a>
       (Matthew 13:24-3);
      </li>
      <li>
       after a
       <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">
        Franciscan
       </a>
       named Lolhard who converted to the
       <a href="/wiki/Waldensians" title="Waldensians">
        Waldensian
       </a>
       way, becoming eminent as a preacher in
       <a href="/wiki/Guyenne" title="Guyenne">
        Guienne
       </a>
       , then under English domination, influencing lay English piety. He was burned at
       <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">
        Cologne
       </a>
       in the 1370s; coincidentally, a Waldensian teacher named Lolhard was tried for heresy in Austria in 1315.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        Middle English
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="enm" xml:lang="enm">
         loller
        </span>
       </i>
       (akin to the verb
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/loll" title="wikt:loll">
         loll
        </a>
       </i>
       , and ultimately to
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lull" title="wikt:lull">
         lull
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        lollen, lallen
       </i>
       group discussed above), "a lazy vagabond, an idler, a fraudulent beggar"; but this word is not recorded in this sense before 1582, while it is recorded as an alternative spelling of
       <i>
        Lollard
       </i>
       .
      </li>
     </ol>

     <p>
      One group of Lollards petitioned Parliament with
      <a href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Conclusions_of_the_Lollards" title="The Twelve Conclusions of the Lollards">
       The Twelve Conclusions of the Lollards
      </a>
      by posting them on the doors of Westminster Hall in February 1395. While by no means a central authority of the Lollards, the Twelve Conclusions reveal certain basic Lollard ideas. The first Conclusion rejects the acquisition of temporal wealth by Church leaders as accumulating wealth leads them away from religious concerns and toward greed. The fourth Conclusion deals with the Lollard view that the
      <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">
       Sacrament
      </a>
      of Eucharist is a contradictory topic that is not clearly defined in the Bible. Whether the bread remains bread or becomes the literal body of Christ is not specified uniformly in the gospels. The sixth Conclusion states that officials of the Church should not concern themselves with secular matters when they hold a position of power within the Church because this constitutes a conflict of interest between matters of the spirit and matters of the State. The eighth Conclusion points out the ludicrousness, in the minds of Lollards, of the reverence that is directed toward images in the Church. "If the cross of Christ, the nails, spear, and crown of thorns are to be honoured, then why not honour Judas's lips, if only they could be found?".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHudson1988306_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHudson1988306-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Lollards stated that the Catholic Church had been corrupted by temporal matters and that its claim to be the true church was not justified by its heredity. Part of this corruption involved prayers for the dead and
      <a href="/wiki/Chantry" title="Chantry">
       chantries
      </a>
      . These were seen as corrupt since they distracted priests from other work and that all should be prayed for equally. Lollards also had a tendency toward
      <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">
       iconoclasm
      </a>
      . Expensive church artwork was seen as an excess; they believed effort should be placed on helping the needy and preaching rather than working on expensive decorations.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icons" title="Icons">
       Icons
      </a>
      were also seen as dangerous since many seemed to be worshiping the icons more than God.
     </p>
     <p>
      Believing in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priesthood_of_all_believers" title="Priesthood of all believers">
       lay priesthood
      </a>
      , the Lollards challenged the Churchs authority to invest or deny the divine authority to make a man a priest. Denying any special status to the priesthood, Lollards thought
      <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">
       confession
      </a>
      to a priest was unnecessary since according to them priests did not have the ability to forgive sins. Lollards challenged the practice of
      <a href="/wiki/Clerical_celibacy" title="Clerical celibacy">
       clerical celibacy
      </a>
      and believed priests should not hold government positions as such temporal matters would likely interfere with their spiritual mission.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lollardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="624" height="370" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg/220px-Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg/330px-Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg/440px-Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wycliffe_John_Gospel.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Beginning of the Gospel of John from a pocket Wycliffe translation that may have been used by a roving Lollard preacher (late 14th century)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      At first, although Lollardy was denounced as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_heresy" title="Christian heresy">
       heresy
      </a>
      , Wycliffe and the Lollards were sheltered by
      <a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt" title="John of Gaunt">
       John of Gaunt
      </a>
      and other anti-clerical nobility, who may have wanted to use Lollard-advocated clerical reform to acquire new sources of revenue from Englands monasteries. The
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">
       University of Oxford
      </a>
      also protected Wycliffe and similar academics on the grounds of academic freedom and, initially, allowed such persons to retain their positions despite their controversial views. Lollards first faced serious persecution after the
      <a href="/wiki/Peasants%27_Revolt" title="Peasants' Revolt">
       Peasants' Revolt
      </a>
      in 1381. While Wycliffe and other Lollards opposed the revolt, one of the peasants leaders,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Ball_(priest)" title="John Ball (priest)">
       John Ball
      </a>
      , preached Lollardy. The royalty and nobility then found Lollardy to be a threat not only to the Church, but to English society in general. The Lollards' small measure of protection evaporated. This change in status was also affected by the 1386 departure of John of Gaunt who left England to pursue the
      <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
       Crown of Castile
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A group of gentry active during the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">
       Richard II
      </a>
      (137799) were known as "Lollard Knights" either during or after their lives due to their acceptance of Wycliffe's claims.
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knighton" title="Henry Knighton">
       Henry Knighton
      </a>
      , in his Chronicle, identifies the principal Lollard Knights as Thomas Latimer, John Trussel, Lewis Clifford, John Peachey, Richard Storey, and Reginald Hilton.
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Walsingham" title="Thomas Walsingham">
       Thomas Walsingham
      </a>
      's Chronicle adds William Nevil and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Clanvowe" title="John Clanvowe">
       John Clanvowe
      </a>
      to the list, and other potential members of this circle have been identified by their wills, which contain Lollard-inspired language about how their bodies are to be plainly buried and permitted to return to the soil whence they came. There is little indication that the Lollard Knights were specifically known as such during their lifetimes; they were men of discretion, and unlike Sir
      <a href="/wiki/John_Oldcastle" title="John Oldcastle">
       John Oldcastle
      </a>
      years later, rarely gave any hint of open rebellion. However, they displayed a remarkable ability to retain important positions without falling victim to the various prosecutions of Wycliffe's followers occurring during their lifetimes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Religious and secular authorities strongly opposed Lollardy. A primary opponent was
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Arundel" title="Thomas Arundel">
       Thomas Arundel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">
       Archbishop of Canterbury
      </a>
      , assisted by bishops like
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_le_Despenser" title="Henry le Despenser">
       Henry le Despenser
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Norwich" title="Bishop of Norwich">
       Norwich
      </a>
      , whom the chronicler
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Walsingham" title="Thomas Walsingham">
       Thomas Walsingham
      </a>
      praised for his zeal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">
       King Henry IV
      </a>
      (despite being John of Gaunt's son) passed the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/De_heretico_comburendo" title="De heretico comburendo">
        De heretico comburendo
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1401, which did not specifically ban the Lollards, but prohibited translating or owning the Bible and authorised burning heretics
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution_by_burning" title="Execution by burning">
       at the stake
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oldcastleburning.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="680" data-file-width="612" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/Oldcastleburning.jpg/170px-Oldcastleburning.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/Oldcastleburning.jpg/255px-Oldcastleburning.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/Oldcastleburning.jpg/340px-Oldcastleburning.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oldcastleburning.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/John_Oldcastle" title="John Oldcastle">
         Sir John Oldcastle
        </a>
        being burnt for insurrection and Lollard heresy.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      By the early 15th century, stern measures were undertaken by Church and state which drove Lollardy underground. One such measure was the 1410 burning at the stake of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Badby" title="John Badby">
       John Badby
      </a>
      , a layman and craftsman who refused to renounce his Lollardy. He was the first layman
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution_(legal)" title="Execution (legal)">
       executed
      </a>
      in England for the crime of heresy.
     </p>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Oldcastle_Revolt" title="Oldcastle Revolt">
       Oldcastle Revolt
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sir
      <a href="/wiki/John_Oldcastle" title="John Oldcastle">
       John Oldcastle
      </a>
      , a close friend of
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">
       King Henry V
      </a>
      (and the basis for
      <a href="/wiki/Falstaff" title="Falstaff">
       Falstaff
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespearean_histories" title="Shakespearean histories">
       Shakespearean history
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_part_1" title="Henry IV, part 1">
        Henry IV
       </a>
      </i>
      ), was brought to trial in 1413 after evidence of his Lollard beliefs was uncovered. Oldcastle
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERichardson200787.E2.80.9389_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichardson200787.E2.80.9389-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      escaped from the
      <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">
       Tower of London
      </a>
      and organized an insurrection, which included an attempted kidnapping of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_England" title="King of England">
       king
      </a>
      . The rebellion failed, and Oldcastle was executed.
      <a href="/wiki/John_Oldcastle#Open_rebellion" title="John Oldcastle">
       Oldcastle's revolt
      </a>
      made Lollardy seem even more threatening to the state, and persecution of Lollards became more severe. A variety of other
      <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">
       martyrs
      </a>
      for the Lollard cause were executed during the next century, including the Amersham Martyrs in the early 1500s and
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Harding" title="Thomas Harding">
       Thomas Harding
      </a>
      in 1532, one of the last Lollards to be persecuted. A gruesome reminder of this persecution is the 'Lollards Pit' in Thorpe Wood, now Thorpe Hamlet, Norwich, Norfolk,
      <i>
       where men are customablie burnt.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rackham_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rackham-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Lollards were effectively absorbed into
      <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">
       Protestantism
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">
       English Reformation
      </a>
      , in which Lollardy played a role. Since Lollards had been underground for more than a hundred years, the extent of Lollardy and its ideas at the time of the Reformation is uncertain and a point of debate. Ancestors of
      <a href="/wiki/Blanche_Parry" title="Blanche Parry">
       Blanche Parry
      </a>
      (the closest person to Queen
      <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">
       Elizabeth I
      </a>
      for 56 years) and of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lady_Troy" title="Lady Troy">
       Lady Troy
      </a>
      (who raised Edward VI and Elizabeth I) had Lollard connections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERichardson200710.E2.80.9311.2C_87.E2.80.9389_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichardson200710.E2.80.9311.2C_87.E2.80.9389-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, many critics of the Reformation, including
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">
       Thomas More
      </a>
      , associated Protestants with Lollards. Leaders of the
      <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">
       English Reformation
      </a>
      , including Archbishop
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cranmer" title="Thomas Cranmer">
       Thomas Cranmer
      </a>
      , referred to Lollardy as well, and
      <a href="/wiki/Cuthbert_Tunstall" title="Cuthbert Tunstall">
       Bishop Cuthbert of London
      </a>
      called Lutheranism the "foster-child" of the Wycliffite heresy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scholars debate whether Protestants actually drew influence from Lollardy or whether they referred to it to create a sense of tradition. The extent of Lollardy in the general populace at this time is also unknown, but the prevalence of Protestant
      <a href="/wiki/Iconoclasm" title="Iconoclasm">
       iconoclasm
      </a>
      in England suggests Lollard ideas may still have had some popular influence if
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zwingli" title="Zwingli">
       Zwingli
      </a>
      was not the source, as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutherans" title="Lutherans">
       Lutherans
      </a>
      did not advocate iconoclasm. The similarity between Lollards and later English Protestant groups such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">
       Baptists
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">
       Puritans
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">
       Quakers
      </a>
      also suggests some continuation of Lollard ideas through the Reformation.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Representations_in_art_and_literature">
       Representations in art and literature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lollardy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Representations in art and literature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Church used art as an anti-Lollard weapon. Lollards were represented as
      <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">
       foxes
      </a>
      dressed as monks or priests preaching to a flock of
      <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">
       geese
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Misericord" title="Misericord">
       misericords
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These representations alluded to the story of the preaching fox found in popular Medieval literature such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reynard_cycle" title="Reynard cycle">
       <i>
        The History of Reynard the Fox
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        The Shifts of Raynardine
       </i>
       (the son of Raynard)
      </a>
      . The fox lured the geese closer and closer with its words until it was able to snatch a victim to devour. The moral of this story was that foolish people are seduced by false doctrines.
     </p>
