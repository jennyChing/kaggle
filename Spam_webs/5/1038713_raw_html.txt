     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Interview
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2014 American
      <a href="/wiki/Political_satire" title="Political satire">
       political satire
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">
       comedy film
      </a>
      directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Seth_Rogen" title="Seth Rogen">
       Seth Rogen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Evan_Goldberg" title="Evan Goldberg">
       Evan Goldberg
      </a>
      in their second directorial work, following
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/This_Is_the_End" title="This Is the End">
        This Is the End
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Dan_Sterling" title="Dan Sterling">
       Dan Sterling
      </a>
      wrote the screenplay from a story by him, Rogen, and Goldberg. The film stars Rogen and
      <a href="/wiki/James_Franco" title="James Franco">
       James Franco
      </a>
      as journalists who set up an interview with
      <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">
       North Korean
      </a>
      leader
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">
       Kim Jong-un
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Randall_Park" title="Randall Park">
       Randall Park
      </a>
      ), and are recruited by the
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">
       CIA
      </a>
      to assassinate him.
     </p>
     <p>
      Rogen and Goldberg developed the idea for
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      in the late 2000s, planning to have
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">
       Kim Jong-il
      </a>
      as the assassination target as he was the North Korean leader at the time. In 2011, after Jong-il's death and Jong-un assuming his position, Rogen and Goldberg re-developed the script with the focus on Jong-un's character. The announcement for the film was made in March 2013, along with the beginning of pre-production.
      <a href="/wiki/Principal_photography" title="Principal photography">
       Principal photography
      </a>
      began in
      <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">
       Vancouver
      </a>
      in October 2013 and concluded in December 2013.
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 2014, the North Korean government threatened action against the United States if
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">
       Columbia Pictures
      </a>
      released the film. Columbia delayed the release from October 10 to December 25, and reportedly re-edited the film to make it more acceptable to North Korea. In November, the computer systems of parent company
      <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment">
       Sony Pictures Entertainment
      </a>
      were
      <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment_hack" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment hack">
       hacked
      </a>
      by the "Guardians of Peace", a group the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">
       FBI
      </a>
      claims has ties to North Korea. After leaking several other then-upcoming Sony films and other sensitive internal information, the group demanded that Sony pull
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      , which it referred to as "the movie of terrorism". On December 16, 2014, the Guardians of Peace threatened
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_terrorist_incidents" title="List of terrorist incidents">
       terrorist attacks
      </a>
      against cinemas that played
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Major cinema
      <a href="/wiki/Chain_store" title="Chain store">
       chains
      </a>
      opted not to release the film, causing Sony to release
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      for online
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_rental" title="Movie rental">
       rental
      </a>
      and purchase on December 24, 2014, followed by a
      <a href="/wiki/Limited_release" title="Limited release">
       limited release
      </a>
      at select cinemas the next day.
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      has earned $40 million in digital rentals, making it Sony's most successful digital release, and over $11 million at the box office. The film received mixed reviews for its humor and subject matter, though the performances of Franco, Park, and
      <a href="/wiki/Diana_Bang" title="Diana Bang">
       Diana Bang
      </a>
      were highly praised.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Interview_(2014_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dave Skylark, host of the talk show
      <i>
       Skylark Tonight
      </i>
      , interviews celebrities about personal topics and gossip. After Dave and his crew celebrate their thousandth episode, they discover that
      <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">
       North Korean
      </a>
      leader
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">
       Kim Jong-un
      </a>
      is a fan of
      <i>
       Skylark Tonight
      </i>
      , prompting the show's producer Aaron Rapoport to arrange an interview. Aaron travels to rural
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      to receive instructions from Sook-yin Park, a North Korean
      <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">
       propagandist
      </a>
      , and Dave accepts the task of interviewing Kim.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next day,
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">
       CIA
      </a>
      Agent Lacey proposes that Dave and Aaron assassinate Kim using a
      <a href="/wiki/Transdermal_patch" title="Transdermal patch">
       transdermal strip
      </a>
      that will expose Kim to
      <a href="/wiki/Ricin" title="Ricin">
       ricin
      </a>
      via handshake, facilitating a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'tat">
        coup d'tat
       </a>
      </i>
      ; they reluctantly agree. Upon their arrival in North Korea, one of Kim's bodyguards discovers the ricin strip and chews it, believing it to be gum. Lacey airdrops two more strips from an
      <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">
       UAV
      </a>
      and Aaron smuggles them into the palace.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dave spends the day with Kim, playing basketball and partying. Kim persuades Dave that he is misunderstood, and they become friends. At dinner, the bodyguard exposed to ricin has a
      <a href="/wiki/Epileptic_seizure" title="Epileptic seizure">
       seizure
      </a>
      and inadvertently kills Kim's other bodyguard before dying. The next morning, Dave feels guilty and discards one of the ricin strips, then thwarts Aaron's attempt to poison Kim with the second strip. After a dinner mourning the death of Kim's bodyguard, Dave discovers Kim's malicious character and that a nearby grocery store is merely
      <a href="/wiki/Potemkin_village" title="Potemkin village">
       a faade
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Aaron and Sook confess their attraction to each other; Sook reveals that she despises Kim and apologizes for defending the regime. Dave, Aaron, and Sook form a plan to break Kim's
      <a href="/wiki/North_Korea%27s_cult_of_personality" title="North Korea's cult of personality">
       cult of personality
      </a>
      by causing him to cry on air. During the internationally televised interview with Kim, Dave addresses increasingly sensitive topics and challenges Kim's need for
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">
       his father
      </a>
      's approval. Despite his initial resistance, Kim eventually cries uncontrollably and soils himself, ruining his reputation. Sook and Aaron seize control of the broadcasting center and fend off guards trying to halt the broadcast.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kim shoots Dave, who survives due to a
      <a href="/wiki/Bulletproof_vest" title="Bulletproof vest">
       bulletproof vest
      </a>
      under his shirt. Dave, Aaron, and Sook regroup and escape the presidential palace, hijacking Kim's tank in order to get to their pickup point. Kim boards a helicopter and pursues Dave, Aaron, and Sook with the military. He orders that North Korea's nuclear missiles be prepared for launch, but before he can issue the command for them to fire, Dave destroys the helicopter from the tank, killing Kim. Sook guides Dave and Aaron to an escape route, and they are rescued by
      <a href="/wiki/SEAL_Team_Six" title="SEAL Team Six">
       SEAL Team Six
      </a>
      members disguised as North Korean troops. Back in the US, Dave writes a book about his experience, and North Korea moves toward becoming a
      <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
       democracy
      </a>
      with Sook as interim president.
     </p>

     <p>

       Evan Goldberg
      </a>
      developed the idea for
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      in the late 2000s, joking about what would happen if a journalist was required to assassinate a world leader.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December2014RS_7-0">

       </a>
      </sup>
      They picked North Korea leader
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">
       Kim Jong-il
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">
       Kim Jong-un
      </a>

      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Show" title="Daily Show">
        Daily Show
       </a>
      </i>
      writer
      <a href="/wiki/Dan_Sterling" title="Dan Sterling">
       Dan Sterling
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">
       Hillary Clinton
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">
       State Department
      </a>

        </span>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toilet_humor" title="Toilet humor">
       toilet humor
      </a>

        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sony_Pictures_Entertainment_hack">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">

      <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment_hack" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment hack">
       Sony Pictures Entertainment hack
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sony_Pictures_Entertainment_entrance_1.jpg">

     <p>
      On November 24, 2014, the computer networks of Columbia Pictures' parent company, Sony Pictures Entertainment, were hacked by an anonymous group identifying themselves as the "Guardians of Peace".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>

       </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Internet_leak" title="Internet leak">
       leaked
      </a>
      internal emails, employee records and several recent and unreleased Sony Pictures films, including
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Annie_(2014_film)" title="Annie (2014 film)">
        Annie
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mr._Turner_(film)" title="Mr. Turner (film)">
        Mr. Turner
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Still_Alice" title="Still Alice">
        Still Alice
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/To_Write_Love_on_Her_Arms_(film)" title="To Write Love on Her Arms (film)">
        To Write Love on Her Arms
       </a>
      </i>
      . The North Korean government denied involvement in the hack.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian-invoke911_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-guardian-invoke911-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On December 8, further materials were leaked, including a demand that Sony pull "the movie of terrorism", widely interpreted as referring to
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <p>
      The leak revealed June 2014 e-mails between Sony Pictures Entertainment CEO
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lynton" title="Michael Lynton">
       Michael Lynton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/RAND_Corporation" title="RAND Corporation">
       RAND Corporation
      </a>
      defense analyst Bruce Bennett. Bennett advised against toning down
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      <span style="padding-left:0.2em;">
       '
      </span>
      s graphic Jong-un death scene, in the hope that it would "start some real thinking in South Korea and, I believe, in the North once the DVD leaks into the North". Bennett expressed his view that "the only resolution I can see to the North Korean nuclear and other threats is for the North Korean government to eventually go away", which he felt would be likeliest to occur following an assassination of Kim. Lynton replied that a senior figure in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">
       United States Department of State
      </a>
      agreed. Bennett responded that the office of
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._King" title="Robert R. King">
       Robert R. King
      </a>
      , U.S. Special Envoy for North Korean Human Rights Issues, had determined that the North Korean statements had been "typical North Korean bullying, likely without follow-up".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In an interview with CNN, Bennett said Lynton sits on the board of trustees of the RAND Corporation, which had asked Bennett to talk to Lynton and give his opinion on the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn.com_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cnn.com-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Bennett felt
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      was "coarse" and "over the top", but that "the depiction of Kim Jong-un was a picture that needed to get into North Korea. There are a lot of people in prison camps in North Korea who need to take advantage of a change of thinking in the north." Bennett felt that if the DVD were smuggled into the country it might have an effect "over time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn.com_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cnn.com-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Jen_Psaki" title="Jen Psaki">
       Jen Psaki
      </a>
      , a spokesperson for the United States Department of State, confirmed that
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_R._Russel" title="Daniel R. Russel">
       Daniel R. Russel
      </a>
      , the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_State_for_East_Asian_and_Pacific_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of State for East Asian and Pacific Affairs">
       Assistant Secretary of State for East Asian and Pacific Affairs
      </a>
      , had spoken to Sony executives; she reiterated that "entertainers are free to make movies of their choosing, and we are not involved in that".

       <a href="#cite_note-45">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cancellation_of_wide_theatrical_release">
       Cancellation of wide theatrical release
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Interview_(2014_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cancellation of wide theatrical release">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following the hackers' threats on December 16, Rogen and Franco canceled scheduled publicity appearances and Sony pulled all television advertising.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Theater_Owners" title="National Association of Theater Owners">
       National Association of Theater Owners
      </a>
      stated that they would not object to cinema owners delaying the film to ensure the safety of movie-goers. Shortly afterwards, the
      <a href="/wiki/ArcLight_Hollywood" title="ArcLight Hollywood">
       ArcLight
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Carmike_Cinemas" title="Carmike Cinemas">
       Carmike
      </a>
      cinema chains announced that they would not screen the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On December 17, Sony canceled the New York City premiere. Later that day, other major theater chains including
      <a href="/wiki/AMC_Theatres" title="AMC Theatres">
       AMC
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cinemark_Theatres" title="Cinemark Theatres">
       Cinemark
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cineplex_Entertainment" title="Cineplex Entertainment">
       Cineplex
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Regal_Entertainment_Group" title="Regal Entertainment Group">
       Regal
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Theatres" title="Southern Theatres">
       Southern Theatres
      </a>
      either delayed or canceled screenings of the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety-interviewdropped_56-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-variety-interviewdropped-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The chains reportedly came under pressure from the malls where many theaters are located, which feared that the terror threat would harm their holiday sales. They also feared expensive lawsuits in the event of an attack; Cinemark, for instance, contended that it could not have foreseen the
      <a href="/wiki/2012_Aurora_shooting" title="2012 Aurora shooting">
       2012 Aurora shooting
      </a>
      , which took place at one of its multiplexes, a defense that would not hold in the case of an attack at a screening of
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The same day, Sony stated that "we respect and understand our partners' decision and, of course, completely share their paramount interest in the safety of employees and theater-goers ... We are deeply saddened at this brazen effort to suppress the distribution of a movie, and in the process do damage to our company, our employees, and the American public. We stand by our filmmakers and their right to free expression and are extremely disappointed by this outcome."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety-canceled_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-variety-canceled-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sony stated that it had no plans to release the film on any platform, including home video.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vod_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vod-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On January 9, 2015, Sony Entertainment CEO
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lynton" title="Michael Lynton">
       Michael Lynton
      </a>
      said "We probably in retrospect should have said we're exploring other options, because that's exactly what we were doing."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

       The Interview
      </i>
      opened to a limited release in the United States on December 25, 2014, across 331 theaters
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88">
       <a href="#cite_note-88">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        88
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and earned over $1 million on its opening day.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">
        Variety
       </a>
      </i>
      called the opening gross "an impressive launch for a title playing in only about 300 independent theaters in the U.S."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89">
       <a href="#cite_note-89">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        89
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It went on to earn over $1.8 million in its opening weekend, and by the end of its run on January 25, 2015, had grossed $6.1 million at the box office.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90">
       <a href="#cite_note-90">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        90
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Within four days of its online release on December 24, 2014,
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      earned over $15 million through online rentals and purchases. It became Sony Pictures' highest-grossing online release, outselling
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage_(film)" title="Arbitrage (film)">
        Arbitrage
       </a>
      </i>
      ($14 million),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bachelorette_(film)" title="Bachelorette (film)">
        Bachelorette
       </a>
      </i>
      ($8.2 million), and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Snowpiercer" title="Snowpiercer">
        Snowpiercer
       </a>
      </i>
      ($7 million).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VOD_91-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VOD-91">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        91
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is the top-selling
      <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">
       Google Play
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">
       YouTube
      </a>
      film of 2014.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92">
       <a href="#cite_note-92">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        92
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By January 20, 2015, the film had earned more than $40 million from online sales and rentals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93">
       <a href="#cite_note-93">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        93
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sony expected
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      to break even through video on demand sales and saving millions of dollars on marketing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94">
       <a href="#cite_note-94">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        94
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Theater_Owners" title="National Association of Theater Owners">
       National Association of Theater Owners
      </a>
      contended that Sony would lose at least $30 million due to poor box office performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95">
       <a href="#cite_note-95">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        95
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">
       Critical response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Interview_(2014_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Critical response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      received mixed reviews from critics. On review aggregation website
      <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">
       Rotten Tomatoes
      </a>
      , the film holds a 53% approval rating, based on 123 reviews, with an average rating of 5.6/10. The site's consensus reads: "Unfortunately overshadowed by controversy (and under-screened as a result),
      <i>
       The Interview'
      </i>
      s screenplay offers middling laughs bolstered by its two likable leads."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96">
       <a href="#cite_note-96">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        96
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On
      <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">
       Metacritic
      </a>
      , the film has a score of 52 out of 100, based on 33 critics, indicating "mixed or average reviews".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97">
       <a href="#cite_note-97">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        97
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">
       IGN
      </a>
      's Roth Cornet wrote that "though it's unlikely to stand out as one of the shrewdest political satires of its time, [it] is a clever, unrestrained andmost importantlysidesplitting parody that pokes fun at both a vapid media and one of the world's most dangerous dictators."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98">
       <a href="#cite_note-98">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        98
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Edward Douglas of
      <a href="/wiki/CraveOnline" title="CraveOnline">
       ComingSoon.net
      </a>
      said the film was "hilarious, but it will probably get us nuked".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99">
       <a href="#cite_note-99">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        99
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Jordan Hoffman of
      <i>
       The Guardian
      </i>
      gave the film three out of five stars and wrote that "if this unessential but agreeable movie really triggered an international response, this is life reflecting art in a major way."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100">
       <a href="#cite_note-100">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        100
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scott Foundas of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">
        Variety
       </a>
      </i>
      panned the film as "cinematic
      <a href="/wiki/Waterboarding" title="Waterboarding">
       waterboarding
      </a>
      " and "about as funny as a communist food shortage, and just as protracted", but praised the performances of Randall Park and Diana Bang.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScottFoundasVariety_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ScottFoundasVariety-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mike Hale of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      also praised Park and Bang, but wrote that "after seeing
      <i>
       The Interview
      </i>
      and the ruckus its mere existence has caused, the only sensible reaction is amazement at the huge disconnect between the innocuousness of the film and the viciousness of the response."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hale_101-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hale-101">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        101
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
