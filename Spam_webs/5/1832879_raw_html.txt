         <h3>
          ALL ABOUT T1
         </h3>
         <p>
          Lets talk about the transition drill for the race for T1 (Swim to Bike).
         </p>
         <p>
          This will be the longest and most difficult of your 2 transitions. T2 is relatively easy to we wont need to discuss that one in detail.
         </p>
         <p>
          For T1 you will be running out of the swim area and heading to your racked bike.
         </p>

             <a href="http://www.irontrainingtips.com/how-to-transition-faster-and-with-less-stress-download-the-e-book/" title="How to transition faster and with less stress (Download the E-book)">
              How to transition faster and with less stress (Download the E-book)
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.irontrainingtips.com/secrets-to-a-successful-ironman-swim/" title="Secrets to a Successful Ironman Swim">
              Secrets to a Successful Ironman Swim
             </a>
            </li>
           </ul>
          </div>
         </div>
         <h3>
          EXITING THE SWIM
         </h3>
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_55" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="Exiting the swim to transition T1 is the most difficult of the two transitions in a triathlon but with a few pointers, you'll save time and keep calm Flickr (c) foleymo" class="size-full wp-image-55" height="426" src="http://www.irontrainingtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/5019711488_1acaf03cc5_z.jpg" width="640"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Exiting the swim to transition T1 is the most difficult of the two transitions in a triathlon but with a few pointers, youll save time and keep calm Flickr (c) foleymo
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          As you exit the swim start removing your wetsuit to your waist and put your goggles on your head. If you are using a race watch, now is the time to switch between disciplines.
         </p>
         <p>
          Many a time youll see athletes running into T1 and scratching their heads, losing vital time looking for their bikes. When they were in T1 earlier before the race, everything looked familiar but now from a different angle and different mindset, they are lost.
         </p>
         <h3>
          SITING
         </h3>
         <p>
          So the first part of T1 transition is siting. Find an object you can sight which is not on the bike racks (e.g. Dont choose a neighbors bike). Example objects you can choose are flags (some triathlons plant flags around transition area) or signs. Some athletes count out the number of paces (running) from T1 entry like a treasure map (e.g. 10 paces forward, 5 paces right).
          <br/>
          Choose a strategy that works for you. Make this the first thing you do when you approach T1.
         </p>
         <h3>
          WETSUIT
         </h3>
         <p>
          The first thing you do when you arrive at your bike is remove your wetsuit.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you are racing a larger event such as Ironman that uses changing tents, then this step will happen before siting. You will run from water exit to the tent and change your gear. Then run out to T1.
         </p>
         <h3>
          FEET
         </h3>
         <p>
          Using the spare bottle either in your transition bag (Ironman) or laid out on your towel, quickly wash any debris off your feet. Youll pick up a lot of stuff from the swim exit that can cause blisters while cycling without socks on the bike.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its a personal preference if you choose to wear socks. If you have cycling / triathlon shoes for your bike you should not need socks but, again, its personal preference and a payoff between time and comfort. If you choose to wear socks, washing your feet is still a good idea.
         </p>
         <p>
          Make sure you stand on a towel while changing and washing your feet to avoid picking up any gravel on your feet.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you are racing with shoes attached to the bike, move on to the helmet, otherwise put on your shoes.
         </p>
         <h3>
          HELMET
         </h3>
         <p>
          Put on your helmet and secure your strap. Make sure your helmet is on before you unrack the bike and exit transition. Exiting a transition without a helmet properly attached can result in a time penalty or being sent back by a judge.
         </p>
         <h3>
          RACE NUMBER
         </h3>
         <p>
          If you are taking part in a wetsuit event then you can wear your race belt and number under the wetsuit. This will save a few seconds and any issues related to lost number belts (it happens).
         </p>
         <p>
          The alternative is to place your race belt either on the rack (if there is space) or on the front bars of your bike. When you wear the race belt, have the number facing backwards (you will need to change this around at T2).
         </p>
         <h3>
          SUNGLASSES
         </h3>
         <p>
          Get onto your bike after the mountline and you are off. Use the first few minutes on the bike to get comfortable, get your bearings and then put on your sunglasses.
         </p>
