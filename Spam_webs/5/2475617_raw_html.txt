     <p>
      <b>
       Swedish bagpipes
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       sckpipa
      </b>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
       Swedish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv">
       <i>
        Svensk sckpipa
       </i>
      </span>
      ) are a variety of
      <a href="/wiki/Bagpipes" title="Bagpipes">
       bagpipes
      </a>
      from Sweden. The term itself generically translates to "bagpipes" in Swedish, but is used in English to describe the specifically Swedish bagpipe from the
      <a href="/wiki/Dalarna" title="Dalarna">
       Dalarna
      </a>
      region.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_bagpipes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">
       Medieval
      </a>
      paintings in churches suggest that the instrument was spread all over Sweden. The instrument was practically extinct by the middle of the 20th century; the instrument that today is referred to as Swedish bagpipes is a construction based on instruments from the western parts of the district called Dalarna, the only region of Sweden where the bagpipe tradition survived into the 20th century.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">
       Revival
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_bagpipes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Revival">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In late 1930s, the
      <a href="/wiki/Ethnology" title="Ethnology">
       ethnologist
      </a>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mats_Rehnberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mats Rehnberg (page does not exist)">
       Mats Rehnberg
      </a>
      found some bagpipes in the collections of the museum
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nordiska_museet" title="Nordiska museet">
       Nordiska museet
      </a>
      , and he wrote a thesis on the subject. Rehnberg managed to find the last carrier of Swedish bagpipe tradition,
      <a href="/wiki/Gudmunds_Nils_Larsson" title="Gudmunds Nils Larsson">
       Gudmunds Nils Larsson
      </a>
      in the village
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dala-J%C3%A4rna" title="Dala-Jrna">
       Dala-Jrna
      </a>
      . Rehnberg visited Larsson together with a music teacher, Ture Gudmundsson, who managed to reconstruct an instrument, with which Ture later played and recorded two tunes for the national publicly funded radio broadcaster
      <a href="/wiki/Sveriges_Radio" title="Sveriges Radio">
       Sveriges Radio
      </a>
      . During the following decades only a few instruments were made, until 1981 when woodworker Leif Eriksson started to manufacture a model of bagpipes that he himself had developed, and the folk fiddler
      <a href="/wiki/Per_Gudmundson" title="Per Gudmundson">
       Per Gudmundson
      </a>
      learned to play it. Eriksson's bagpipes was a compromise between the roughly ten different instruments held in museums, and he made some slight modifications to make the instruments better suited for playing with other instruments such as the fiddle.
     </p>
     <p>
      Today there are several Swedish folk music groups that include the bag pipes in their setting, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Hedningarna" title="Hedningarna">
       Hedningarna
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Svanevit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Svanevit (page does not exist)">
       Svanevit
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dr%C3%A5m&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drm (page does not exist)">
       Drm
      </a>
      (the two latter involving
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Ask-Upmark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erik Ask-Upmark (page does not exist)">
       Erik Ask-Upmark
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Rynefors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anna Rynefors (page does not exist)">
       Anna Rynefors
      </a>
      ). Players of the Swedish pipes are now eligible to compete playing traditional music before a jury to earn the title of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Riksspelman" title="Riksspelman">
        riksspelman
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">
       Construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_bagpipes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="264" data-file-width="442" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg/220px-S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg/330px-S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg/440px-S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S%C3%A4ckpipa_av_alban_faust.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        One of Alban Faust's modernised sets of Swedish bagpipes. Chanters in A and G, three
        <a href="/wiki/Drone_(music)" title="Drone (music)">
         drones
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">
         bellows
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The bag is notably smaller than that of many other bagpipes. This, however, is no major problem as the pipes require relatively little air. The chanter has a single cane reed and a cylindrical bore, with a range of one octave. It is essentially diatonic (with a melodic ascending A minorA major with a flat thirdscale starting on E) since cross-fingering has little effect.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ut%C3%B6kad_skala_svensk_s%C3%A4ckpipa.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="60" data-file-width="321" height="41" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Ut%C3%B6kad_skala_svensk_s%C3%A4ckpipa.jpg/220px-Ut%C3%B6kad_skala_svensk_s%C3%A4ckpipa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Ut%C3%B6kad_skala_svensk_s%C3%A4ckpipa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Ut%C3%B6kad_skala_svensk_s%C3%A4ckpipa.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ut%C3%B6kad_skala_svensk_s%C3%A4ckpipa.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Range of the standard sckpipa
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">
       Evolution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish_bagpipes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evolution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A double hole for the C hole instead of a single one can be bored, one of which can be covered, for example with beeswax, to produce C, and uncovered to produce C#. This makes the key of A major possible.
      </li>
      <li>
       The 'tuning hole', a hole traditionally placed on the underside of the chanter and which is used for tuning the bottom note of the chanter (with beeswax to make the hole smaller), can be placed on the top side instead, enabling it to be used as a fingerhole. This adds a low D to the scale.
      </li>
      <li>
       A key can be fitted to operate a hole above the usual fingerholes, to give the piper an additional high F#.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The fact that the chanter, with its cylindrical bore and single reed, is extremely unaffected by crossfingering, and that the drone is tuned to the same note and octave as the bottom note of the chanter, makes it possible to play in a closed or semi-closed manner, enabling the player to quickly play the bottom note in between other notessince this will blend with the sound of the drone, it gives the illusion of silence, and the possibility to play staccato.
     </p>
     <p>
      The tone of the instrument is quite soft, not too different from that of a
      <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">
       harmonica
      </a>
      or an
      <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">
       accordion
      </a>
      .
     </p>
