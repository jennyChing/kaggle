      <h1 class="content-title">
       Jane Eyre Introduction
      </h1>
      <ul class="pager">
       <li>
        <a class="" href="summary.html" title="Summary">
         NEXT
         <i class="fa fa-caret-right">
         </i>
        </a>
       </li>

      <h2>
       In A Nutshell
      </h2>
      <div class="PrimaryContent" id="div_PrimaryContent">
       <div class="shmoop-video" data-url="jane-eyre" id="jane-eyre">
       </div>
       <p>
        <em>
         ____________________________________________________________________________________
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Want more deets? We've also got a complete
         <a href="http://www.shmoop.com/courses/jane-eyre/" target="_blank">
          Online Course
         </a>
         about
        </em>
        Jane Eyre
        <em>
         , with three weeks' worth of readings and activities to make sure you know your stuff.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         ____________________________________________________________________________________
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        You can't get much more romantic than
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        : a poor, unloved, and
        <a href="http://the-toast.net/2015/05/07/how-to-cast-jane-eyre/" target="_blank">
         unattractive
        </a>
        orphan uses her awesome personality to win over a wealthy sort-of-aristocrat and live happily ever after.
       </p>
       <p>
        Oh, and by "awesome personality" we mean "blunt and somewhat annoyingly obsessed with duty." And let's not forget to mention that the sort-of-aristocrat is (1) mean, (2) ugly, and (3) comes with more baggage than an Airbus. And "happily ever after"? Ha. That comes in the last few pages of a very long (and
        <em>
         very
        </em>
        messed-upthink "psychopathic mind games" and "imprisoned people in the attic") courtship.
       </p>
       <p>
        What we're saying is,
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        isn't exactly the harlequin romance novel that a movie poster like
        <a href="http://images2.fanpop.com/images/photos/6900000/Jane-Eyre-Movie-Poster-1996-jane-eyre-6939407-509-755.jpg" target="_blank">
         this
        </a>
        might suggest. But don't worry: it's still a crowd-pleaser. Madness, disability, missionaries, and a tasty sprinkle of the
        <a href="http://www.shmoop.com/literature-glossary/gothic-literature.html" target="_blank">
         gothic
        </a>
        make
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        a pretty compelling read for a book that was published (under the pseudonym Currer Bell) in the wayback days of 1847.
       </p>
       <p>
        Still, there's a lot more going on than the makings of a Hollywood blockbuster... even though
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        has graced the screen a whopping thirty-three times. At the heart of
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        is a struggle that's almost certainly close to your heart: the struggle to grow up and live a life that's authentic and meaningful.
       </p>
       <p>
        So does living an authentic life mean following your cousin across the world to convert the "heathens"? Does it mean living as a wealthy spinster and teaching the rural poor how to knit? Or does it mean marrying your brooding, crippled landlord who has a sordid, secret past?
       </p>
       <p>
        It's
        <em>
         Choose Your Own Adventure
        </em>
        , nineteenth-century style: if you choose wisely you'll gain everlasting love and a swanky mansion; if you choose poorly... well, we just hope you like spending
        <em>
         a lot
        </em>
        of time in attics.
       </p>
       <a href="http://www.shmoop.com/jane-eyre/flashcards.html">
        <img src="http://media1.shmoop.com/images/general/flashcard_embed_image_5.png" style="width:100%; height:auto;"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <a name="wsic">
      </a>
      <h1 class="content-title">
       Why Should I Care?
      </h1>
      <h2>
      </h2>
      <div class="PrimaryContent">
       <p>
        When you look at
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        , you might just see a long novel about a gal in an ugly gray dress whose lifea lot of the timetotally sucks. Whether shes gagging on burned porridge at her horrible boarding school or discovering that her fianc is already married to someone else or wandering around on the moor starving to death, life is often painful for Jane.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        The thing is, its not painful to
        <em>
         read
        </em>
        about it. In fact, we start to get kind of obsessed with all the gory details after a while. Did Jane and Rochester's wedding really get interrupted at the altar just now? Why did Rochester decide to keep his wife locked in the attic? How many mistresses did he have? Is he Adle's dad or not? Will Jane marry her cousin or agree to bigamy? Is there a ghost at Thornfield Hall... or is it a vampire?
       </p>
       <p>
        They could make a reality show out of this; it would be like
        <a href="http://abc.go.com/primetime/supernanny/index?pn=index" target="_blank">
         <em>
          Supernanny
         </em>
        </a>
        +
        <a href="http://abc.go.com/primetime/bachelorette/index?pn=index" target="_blank">
         <em>
          The Bachelorette
         </em>
        </a>
        +
        <a href="http://www.scifi.com/ghosthunters/" target="_blank">
         <em>
          Ghost Hunters
         </em>
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Of course, apart from the whole thirst-for-voyeurism thing that we all have,
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        also offers something else: a tale about The Man getting you down. Over and over, Janes put into situations where shes too young, too poor, or too powerless to win, but she has to try anyway. And we
        <em>
         all
        </em>
        know about that.
       </p>
       <p>
        Weve all been the kid who was picked on by some random adult like Mrs. Reed or Mr. Brocklehurst or your fourth-grade math teacher just because that person has a stick up their you-know-what.
       </p>
       <p>
        Weve all had to accept that everyone would believe the adults just because they are adults, so they get away with it. Maybe some of us have also been the young employee who gets pressured to do something immoral or just to work late
        <em>
         again
        </em>
        by our boss.
       </p>
       <p>
        Or the girlfriend who finds out that her boyfriends taking someone else out on Friday night. That "someone else" might not be an insane vampiric arsonistbut hey, parallels only go so far.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        What were trying to say is: half of
        <em>
         Jane Eyre
        </em>
        is pure get-the-popcorn, omigawd-she-didnt spectacle, and the other half looks pretty familiar. Even if you've never spent time as a governess in a moldering mansionhey, even if you're a dudeyou've likely felt "puny and insignificant" (2.9.23) at some point in your life.
       </p>
       <p>
        And there is nothing more relatable than watching the underdog get kicked around... and
        <em>
         nothing
        </em>
        more satisfying than watching her triumph.
       </p>
       <a href="http://www.shmoop.com/my/flashcards/overview/26B2DF71E1B04F56B678685D562FAC85/">
        <img src="http://media1.shmoop.com/images/general/flash_yourself_1.png" style="width:100%; height:auto;"/>
       </a>
      </div>
