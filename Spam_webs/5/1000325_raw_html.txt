           When it comes to the Universe, it isnt just the stuff thats in it thats important. Sure, when it comes to what makes everything up, you want to know about nuclei and electrons, as well as what makes those nuclei up, photons, and any other particle its possible to create.
          </p>

          <p class="graf--p" id="7ac5" name="7ac5">
           But if you want to really understand it, simply knowing whats in it isnt going to get you very far on its own. Its also how all that stuff interacts with itself and everything else. To the best of our knowledge, there are four fundamental forces in the Universe, and theyre all absolutely essential to our existence.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="33aa" name="33aa">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 519px;">

          <p class="graf--p" id="b05a" name="b05a">
           Some of them are familiar, like gravitation. On the largest scales in the Universe, gravitation is not only the
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            most
           </em>
           important force, but arguably the
           <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">
            only
           </strong>
           important force in play. The amount of mass and energy inherent to objects determines how spacetime itself is curved, and this curvature of spacetime in turn determines how objects move and accelerate.
          </p>

           </figcaption>

          <p class="graf--p" id="0f75" name="0f75">
           Theres no anti-mass or anti-energy that causes some objects to be gravitationally repelled while others are gravitationally attracted. Gravity is
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            always
           </em>
           attractive, and we can interpret mass/energy as the lone type of gravitational charge, if we want to.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="6731" name="6731">
           But other forces and interactions can be more complicated than gravity in this regard. Take the electromagnetic force, for example, or the forces that occur when we examine charged particles.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="c1e1" name="c1e1">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 605px; max-height: 351px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 57.99999999999999%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="351" data-image-id="1*lkqYUF1sAD_Mo0xuNkshIg.gif" data-width="605" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*lkqYUF1sAD_Mo0xuNkshIg.gif"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit:
            <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="http://Maxwells-Equations.com/" href="http://Maxwells-Equations.com/" rel="nofollow">
             http://Maxwells-Equations.com/
            </a>
            ,copyright 2012.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="0359" name="0359">
           Instead of one type of charge where like-attracts-like, we have
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            two
           </em>
           types of electric charge: positive and negative, where like charges
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            repel
           </em>
           and unlike charges attract. Its very different than gravity and somewhat more complicated, but there are some applications of this that ought to feel very familiar. We make extraordinary use of this feature, after all, in practically everything we do on Earth.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="5f9e" name="5f9e">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 846px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 120.9%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*6-jCJWf7PGJQQOEt6WiqKg.jpeg" data-height="1139" data-image-id="1*6-jCJWf7PGJQQOEt6WiqKg.jpeg" data-width="942" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*6-jCJWf7PGJQQOEt6WiqKg.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Science Photo Library.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="8533" name="8533">
           A neutral atom, for instance, is a good example of electromagnetism, where the positively charged nucleus is orbited by a swarm of negatively charged electrons. The electrons repel each other, but theyre all attracted to the nucleus even moreso. As long as the total charge of the atom is zero and there is no sufficiently strong external radiation, the atom will remain stable and neutral. This is the fundamental building block of
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            every
           </em>
           thingliving and non-livingon our world.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="e4ab" name="e4ab">
           We even understand, at a fundamental quantum level, how this works. The attraction and repulsion between all charged particles is mediated by the same particle: the photon.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="2efd" name="2efd">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 611px; max-height: 355px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 58.099999999999994%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="355" data-image-id="1*wgb6ruurF4Csy9ChXsSbXg.jpeg" data-width="611" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*wgb6ruurF4Csy9ChXsSbXg.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Ask a Mathematician / Ask a Physicist.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="6efc" name="6efc">
           It just takes one particle to take care of both attraction and repulsion, because of the relatively simple structuretwo charges, like-repels-like and opposites attractof electromagnetism. But things get a lot more complicated if we go
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            inside
           </em>
           the nucleus, and ask just how it is, at a fundamental level, that these tiny, charged structures hold themselves together.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="section-inner sectionLayout--outsetRow" data-paragraph-count="2">
          <figure class="graf--figure graf--layoutOutsetRow is-partialWidth" id="9a42" name="9a42" style="width: 42.776%;">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 73.8%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*LWC5A8UMDSfk1T0393mk5g.jpeg" data-height="582" data-image-id="1*LWC5A8UMDSfk1T0393mk5g.jpeg" data-width="789" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/600/1*LWC5A8UMDSfk1T0393mk5g.jpeg"/>
           </div>
          </figure>
          <figure class="graf--figure graf--layoutOutsetRowContinue is-partialWidth" id="d55d" name="d55d" style="width: 57.224%;">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 55.1%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*qwjqNSBvBMUwOjiMHqpl5g.png" data-height="479" data-image-id="1*qwjqNSBvBMUwOjiMHqpl5g.png" data-width="869" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/600/1*qwjqNSBvBMUwOjiMHqpl5g.png"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption" style="width: 174.752%; left: -74.752%;">
            Images credit: Derek Owens, 2009 (L); Matt Strassler, via
            <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="http://profmattstrassler.com/articles-and-posts/particle-physics-basics/the-structure-of-matter/protons-and-neutrons/" href="http://profmattstrassler.com/articles-and-posts/particle-physics-basics/the-structure-of-matter/protons-and-neutrons/" rel="nofollow">
             http://profmattstrassler.com/articles-and-posts/particle-physics-basics/the-structure-of-matter/protons-and-neutrons/
            </a>
            (R).
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
         </div>
         <div class="section-inner layoutSingleColumn">
          <p class="graf--p" id="baed" name="baed">
           An atomic nucleus, of course, is made up of protons and neutrons, except for hydrogen, which is just a proton by itself. But seeing as how protons have a positive electric charge and neutrons have no electric charge at all, there must be some sort of extra forcea force
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            even stronger
           </em>
           than the electromagnetic forceto hold these nuclei together. Otherwise, anything made out of more than one proton (anything other than hydrogen) would push itself apart.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="5bb7" name="5bb7">
           In fact, the creatively-named
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction" rel="nofollow">
            strong force
           </a>
           is required to hold even the individual protons and neutrons themselves together. Because a proton and neutron themselves are not fundamental, but composed of even smaller, fractionally-charged particles known as quarks.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="dbad" name="dbad">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 435px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 62.1%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="435" data-image-id="1*Xh7b5ipj7ciFXZ1ioiSdzQ.jpeg" data-width="700" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*Xh7b5ipj7ciFXZ1ioiSdzQ.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Learn EveryWare,  2009 Alberta Education, with an error, edited out by me.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c73a" name="c73a">
           The electric forces inside of a proton, for instance, would cause the nucleus itself to fly apart if there werent
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            another
           </em>
           type of charge attached to each of these quarks: in addition to electric charge, they also possess
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge" rel="nofollow">
            color charge
           </a>
           , which comes in not one type (like gravity), nor two (like electromagnetism), but
           <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">
            three
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="194c" name="194c">
           Only unlike gravitation and electromagnetism, you cant just have a color charge off by itself: you need a red, green, and blue together, to add up to colorless, just like red, green, and blue light together add up to white.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="50dd" name="50dd">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 600px; max-height: 362px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 60.3%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="362" data-image-id="1*z6Cn9-4Dme7jrM_RhetZFw.jpeg" data-width="600" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*z6Cn9-4Dme7jrM_RhetZFw.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Focusbox.net, retrieved from Nuno Canaveira at nColour.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="433e" name="433e">
           Just like theres matter and antimatter, there are quarks and anti-quarks, and so there are colors (red, green and blue) and
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            anti
           </em>
           -colors: cyan is the anti-red, magenta is the anti-green, and yellow is the anti-blue. So to add up to 
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.unit5.org/chemistry/Elementary%20Particles.htm" href="http://www.unit5.org/chemistry/Elementary%20Particles.htm" rel="nofollow">
            colorless
           </a>
           , you either need three quarks (or three anti-quarks), or one quark and one anti-quark.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="cb79" name="cb79">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 657px; max-height: 356px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 54.2%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="356" data-image-id="1*PbfBCeoMSxZYntoZnen2LQ.jpeg" data-width="657" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*PbfBCeoMSxZYntoZnen2LQ.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: McLean County Unit District Number 5,
            <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="http://www.unit5.org/" href="http://www.unit5.org/" rel="nofollow">
             http://www.unit5.org/
            </a>
            .
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c63b" name="c63b">
           Its a little bit weird: if red + green + blue makes white, but red + anti-red also makes white, does that mean that green+blue is the same as anti-red?
           <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">
            Yes
           </strong>
           , yes it does, at least in terms of color. Which means you can pair a quark with either two other quarks, with an antiquark, or
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            possibly
           </em>
           even with
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaquark" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaquark" rel="nofollow">
            three other quarks and one antiquark
           </a>
           . As long as the color comes out white (or colorless), youre in business.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="7247" name="7247">
           And thats why you can have combinations of three quarks, like protons and neutrons, or combinations of one quark and one anti-quark, like
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson" rel="nofollow">
            mesons
           </a>
           . But unlike gravity, which bends spacetime, or electromagnetism, where photons (with no charge) are exchanged, the strong force works by exchanging a new type of particlethe
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluons" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluons" rel="nofollow">
            gluon
           </a>
           which carries both a color
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            and
           </em>
           an anti-color!
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="b129" name="b129">
           These gluons are responsible for holding both individual particleslike protons, neutrons, and
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion" rel="nofollow">
            pions
           </a>
           together, as well as for binding larger atomic nuclei together.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="7254" name="7254">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 600px; max-height: 516px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 86%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="516" data-image-id="1*R9LVp_nG-mjRvXsQlXfCSw.jpeg" data-width="600" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*R9LVp_nG-mjRvXsQlXfCSw.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: CERN / European Organization for Nuclear Research,
            <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="http://www.physik.uzh.ch/" href="http://www.physik.uzh.ch/" rel="nofollow">
             http://www.physik.uzh.ch/
            </a>
            .
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="59bc" name="59bc">
           How does this work? With three colors (red, green, and blue) and three anti-colors (anti-red = cyan, anti-green = magenta, and anti-blue = yellow), you might think there are nine types of gluons that you can get from matching each color with each anti-color. Thats a good first thought, and its
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            almost
           </em>
           right.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="c755" name="c755">
           Imagine youre a red quark, and you emit a red/anti-green gluon. Youre going to turn the red quark into a green quark, because color is conserved like that, and then that gluon is going to find a green quark, and turn it red. In this fashion, colors get exchanged.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="b7c3" name="b7c3">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 651px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 93.10000000000001%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*uayqRLU0cFHthmJ51TDcVA.gif" data-height="685" data-image-id="1*uayqRLU0cFHthmJ51TDcVA.gif" data-width="736" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*uayqRLU0cFHthmJ51TDcVA.gif"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons user Qashqaiilove.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="9d87" name="9d87">
           That turns out to be a good explanation for
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            six
           </em>
           of the gluons: red/anti-green, red/anti-blue, green/anti-red, green/anti-blue, blue/anti-red, and blue/anti-green.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="2779" name="2779">
           But what about the others: there should also be red/anti-red, green/anti-green, and blue/anti-blue, right?
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="25da" name="25da">
           <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">
            Almost
           </strong>
           , it turns out. Because each of those has no inherent color, those quantum states are allowed to mix together. In quantum physics, whenever
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_principle" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_principle" rel="nofollow">
            mixing isnt forbidden, it happens
           </a>
           , and that happens whenever any theoretical systems have identical quantum numbers, as is the case here. So instead of pure red/anti-red, green/anti-green and blue/anti-blue, you get mixtures of red/anti-red, green/anti-green, and blue/anti-blue states.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="6495" name="6495">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 600px; max-height: 159px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 26.5%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="159" data-image-id="1*hD7AUwMUMS6W4BqjECDSEA.jpeg" data-width="600" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*hD7AUwMUMS6W4BqjECDSEA.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Me, your gluon hero.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="33a6" name="33a6">
           But one of themthe one thats an equal mixture of all three color/anti-color pairsis
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            truly
           </em>
           colorless, and doesnt physically exist. So there are only
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluons#Numerology_of_gluons" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluons#Numerology_of_gluons" rel="nofollow">
            eight physical gluons
           </a>
           . (The actual math of this comes from the group theory of SU(3), if youre interested in learning the rigorous explanation.)
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="970d" name="970d">
           And its the exchange of these gluons between the quarks and antiquarks that keep protons, neutrons, mesons, baryons, and all other atomic nuclei together. This is why,
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="https://medium.com/starts-with-a-bang/there-are-no-free-quarks-ddec8cb831ea" href="https://medium.com/starts-with-a-bang/there-are-no-free-quarks-ddec8cb831ea">
            if you try to rip any two quarks or antiquarks apart
           </a>
           , the energy required increases, eventually reaching a point where youll simply
           <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">
            pull a particle/antiparticle pair out of the vacuum
           </strong>
           , creating extra particles in the process.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="c803" name="c803">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 648px; max-height: 152px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 23.5%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-height="152" data-image-id="1*lIjoyuFc33raKa4OUIFhbA.png" data-width="648" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*lIjoyuFc33raKa4OUIFhbA.png"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Images credit (top and bottom): Flip Tanedo of Quantum Diaries, via
            <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="http://www.quantumdiaries.org/2010/10/22/qcd-and-confinement/" href="http://www.quantumdiaries.org/2010/10/22/qcd-and-confinement/" rel="nofollow">
             http://www.quantumdiaries.org/2010/10/22/qcd-and-confinement/
            </a>
            .
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="1a46" name="1a46">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 174px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 24.9%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*IWD73Y_h7w19FICVtak1VQ.png" data-height="180" data-image-id="1*IWD73Y_h7w19FICVtak1VQ.png" data-width="724" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*IWD73Y_h7w19FICVtak1VQ.png"/>
           </div>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p" id="a010" name="a010">
           There is
           <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
            much
           </em>
           more to
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" rel="nofollow">
            the strong interactions
           </a>
           than what Ive described here, and if you want to go deeper, I recommend this
           <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.frankwilczek.com/Wilczek_Easy_Pieces/298_QCD_Made_Simple.pdf" href="http://www.frankwilczek.com/Wilczek_Easy_Pieces/298_QCD_Made_Simple.pdf" rel="nofollow">
            jaunt by Nobel Laureate Frank Wilczek
           </a>
           . Whether you do or not, the strong force is what holds every atomic nucleus together; without it, wed simply be a lifeless sea of fundamental particles, too repulsive to hold together in any meaningful fashion, and incapable of building any elements other than hydrogen in the entire Universe.
          </p>
          <p class="graf--p" id="8d03" name="8d03">
           No stars would ever shine, no complex molecules would ever form, and there would never be a rocky planet anywhere in the Universe: just clumps of gas and great, empty voids.
          </p>
          <figure class="graf--figure" id="78f0" name="78f0">
           <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 700px;">
            <div class="aspect-ratio-fill" style="padding-bottom: 100%;">
            </div>
            <img class="graf-image" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="1*mmN3UNdZ7ctkPtIFvcBoxw.jpeg" data-height="2048" data-image-id="1*mmN3UNdZ7ctkPtIFvcBoxw.jpeg" data-width="2048" src="https://d262ilb51hltx0.cloudfront.net/max/800/1*mmN3UNdZ7ctkPtIFvcBoxw.jpeg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="imageCaption">
            Image credit: Roy Uyematsu.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p class="graf--p graf--last" id="dd87" name="dd87">
           And yet, here we are, so much more than a cosmic soup, with galaxies, stars, planets, heavy elements, molecules, life, and you and me. Its the strongest force in the Universe, and we owe everything interesting about our existence to it. Without it, none of this would be possible.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
