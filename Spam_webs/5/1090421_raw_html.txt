                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     China leads the world in solar and wind power production
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8673258729323557292" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     Yup, bet you thought it was the United States, right?
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; text-align: right;">
                      <tbody>
                       <tr>
                        <td style="text-align: center;">
                         <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-7VhSmAxYHuQ/VUM9m98NSaI/AAAAAAAABNA/jsrMeG5GDA8/s1600/china.png" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                          <img border="0" height="266" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-7VhSmAxYHuQ/VUM9m98NSaI/AAAAAAAABNA/jsrMeG5GDA8/s1600/china.png" width="320"/>
                         </a>
                        </td>
                       </tr>
                       <tr>
                        <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                         China, the world's leader in alt. energies
                        </td>
                       </tr>
                      </tbody>
                     </table>
                     China has been pulling itself up quickly in a swift bid to become the world's newest superpower, what with the all-out Olympics, super-industrialization, and military spending. One of the most important factors that make a national superpower, however, is the ability of self-sustenance (or, at the very least, the illusion of self-sustenance).
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     That's why China has focused so steadily on increasing its alternative energy capabilities -- and it's working. In the blink of an eye, China has overtaken the rest of the world in solar and wind power production rates. This is great news for the Asian mega-nation, but what does it mean for the rest of the world? At best, just that we'll all have to play a little catch-up.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     At worst, though, it could mean that the bureaucratic back-up in the U.S. government will finally burst as this (and other) political and socioeconomic strains apply serious pressure to the not-so-gentle giant's vice grip.
