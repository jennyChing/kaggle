     <h1>
      9 Ways to Prepare and Protect Yourself Before Working Abroad
     </h1>
     <div class="meta">
      <span>
       By
       <a href="http://www.ytravelblog.com/author/cazcraig/" rel="author" title="Posts by Caz">
        Caz
       </a>
      </span>
      <span class="date">
       Tuesday, July 29th, 2014
      </span>
      <span class="comments">
       22 Comments
      </span>
     </div>
     <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
     <p style="text-align: right;">
      Brought to you by
      <a href="http://www.nuffnang.com.au/blogger/nuffnang-policy-on-sponsored-posts." target="_blank">
       Nuffnang
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="http://www.ytravelblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/997713746603-L.jpg">
       <img alt="How to prepare yourself before working abroad" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-43994" height="532" src="http://www.ytravelblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/997713746603-L-710x532.jpg" width="710"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      Working abroad is one of the most exciting ways to travel long-term.
     </p>
     <p>
      Not only does it make the travel experience cheaper, but it turns the everyday mundane, like visiting the bank, into an exotic adventure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Craig and I have followed the working holiday strategy for over 10 years. Weve worked in London, Dublin, Bangkok, and Raleigh, North Carolina. We loved every minute of it and we highly recommend everyone experiences working abroad at least once (trust me, youll get addicted).
     </p>
     <p>
      But, to ensure you have the best experience possible, we recommend you take some necessary steps to protect, prepare and keep you happy.
     </p>
     <h2>
      1. Have reserve money
     </h2>
     <p>
      Working abroad means you often dont have to save as much money in the first place to travel long-term. Its one of the major reasons we recommend doing it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its (mostly) cheaper to travel on local currency and youre always replenishing the purse with each new weekly pay check. But, its really important that you do arrive with reserve money in the bank.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its highly likely something will go wrong.
     </p>
     <p>
      Perhaps you wont get your first pay check for at least a month, you might need to pay a security bond for your apartment, or have to furnish a new place.
     </p>
     <p>
      And of course you want to travel a little bit too.
     </p>
     <h2>
      2. Be prepared for culture shock
     </h2>
     <p>
      I know plenty of people who have been outwitted by culture shock and returned home only weeks into their working abroad adventure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Change brings chaos, which is only elevated by being in a strange environment.
     </p>
     <p>
      Youll love being an expat at first as everything is so new and exciting, but pretty soon that high will come crashing down and youll notice how different everything is  in a sucky way!
     </p>
     <p>
      Thats okay, this is part of the change process. If you understand its just
      <em>
       that old pest culture shock
      </em>
      , youll move through it. Therell be tears and outbursts, but this is great, just let it flow out.
     </p>
     <p>
      The best way to move through culture shock is to continue to explore and experience what is so great about the area youve moved to.
     </p>
     <p>
      Keep the travel experiences and the fun alive and dont get bogged down by the everyday life of another country.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wed frequently go on weekend trips, attend concerts and festivals, and party with new friends. This made the transition so much smoother.
     </p>
     <h2>
      3. Cover yourself with international health insurance for expats
     </h2>
     <p>
      Medical bills can be extraordinary in other countries  Im looking at you America!
     </p>
     <p>
      Theres nothing worse than being sick or injured in another country away from the security of your home medical system and the love of your family.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dont freak out about this, just cover yourself so you have peace of mind.
     </p>
     <p>
      For Australians moving abroad, consider
      <a href="http://bs.serving-sys.com/BurstingPipe/adServer.bs?cn=tf&amp;c=20&amp;mc=click&amp;pli=10290747&amp;PluID=0&amp;ord=%5btimestamp%5d" target="_blank">
       BUPA international health insurance.
      </a>
      I was stoked to discover this offer from BUPA, as Ive never come across international health insurance for expats before. I would have loved to use this for peace of mind.
     </p>
     <p>
      With BUPA international health insurance you get 100% coverage on many treatments (doctors, surgeons, anaesthetist, and other specialists) as well as some pre-existing condition. BUPA has over 780,000 medical providers worldwide and will cover you in over 190 countries,
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       And guess what?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      They even have USA cover available!
     </p>
     <p>
      Im really happy about this as we are considering moving back to the States and health insurance in the US is something I worry about. It was organized through my work last time we lived there, but this time well be working for ourselves. This takes a lot of the hassle out of trying to find a US company to cover us.
     </p>
     <p>
      New customers who purchase international health insurance with BUPA before 31st August receive a virtual visa card with $100. You could maybe buy a piece of furniture with that for your new home!
     </p>
     <p>
      BUPA members also get 15% off
      <a href="http://www.bupa.com.au/health-insurance/campaigns/international/travel-insurance" target="_blank">
       travel insurance.
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      (P.S If youre an inpat, i.e. doing the working abroad thing in Australia,
      <a href="http://bs.serving-sys.com/BurstingPipe/adServer.bs?cn=tf&amp;c=20&amp;mc=click&amp;pli=10514133&amp;PluID=0&amp;ord=[timestamp]" rel="no follow" target="_blank">
       BUPA can cover you too!
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <h2>
      4. Prepare your paperwork
     </h2>
     <p>
      I never realised how important this was until I was in Africa and having to chase down paperwork from London, Australia, Bangkok and Dublin. OMG what a nightmare!
     </p>
     <p>
      Make sure you get all references and statement of services from your current job in your home country, and from any other job you now do in your working abroad experience. You just never know when you might need them.
     </p>
     <p>
      Before you leave for your working abroad experience ensure you have all the paperwork you need for your new job: degree certificates, references, statement of services, resume, resources, police checks.
     </p>
     <p>
      And everything you need for your visa: the actual visa, bank statements, anything that proves ties to your home country.
     </p>
     <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_43993" style="width: 483px">
      <a href="http://www.ytravelblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/packing-for-long-term-travel-6.jpg">

      </a>

      </p>

     <h2>
      5. Pack with intention
     </h2>
     <p>
      There is a tendency to want to pack your fluffy teddy from when you were six, and every picture frame in your house. Its nice to bring the comfort of home with you, but youre travelling to break out of the comfort.
     </p>
     <p>
      Do your research.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <em>
        What will the seasons be like?
       </em>
      </li>
      <li>
       <em>
        Do you need any special clothes or equipment for work?
       </em>
      </li>
      <li>
       <em>
        What are the lifestyle and material costs in your new destination?
       </em>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Pack considerately and as minimally as you can.
     </p>
     <p>
      You can save a lot of hassle and shipping costs by purchasing what you need in your new destination. If it works out cheaper to bring your own things, then consider freight shipping and organise it to leave at least six weeks before you arrive so it arrives when you do.
     </p>
     <h2>
      6. Research costs
     </h2>
     <p>
      You dont want to go into bill shock in a new country.
     </p>
     <p>
      Make a list of all you need to live comfortably now. Cross out the ones that truly arent that necessary, like cable TV.
     </p>
     <p>
      Now research what the costs of those things will be in your new country. Im sure there will be some you now discover arent worth paying for and will eliminate from your list.
     </p>
     <p>
      You can prepare for your working abroad experience by researching the best price and value the different companies offer for those things you really need.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example phone and internet coverage, rent, transportation costs, and vehicle hire.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Dont forget to research what sort of tax you will be liable to pay and if you are entitled to receive any back.
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       lot of people overpay
       <span class="il">
        tax
       </span>
       without realising it. You could be due
       <span class="il">
        tax
       </span>
       <span class="il">
        back
       </span>
       if you worked abroad or were taxed incorrectly or perhaps didnt work for the full year.
       <a href="https://www.taxback.com/en/tax-refunds/?utm_ref=ytravel-blog" target="_blank">
        TaxBack.com
       </a>
       can check out your details and tell you for free what youre owed. They provide
       <span class="il">
        tax
       </span>
       refunds for 16 countries worldwide including Ireland, the US, Australia, Canada and New Zealand.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      7. Research your new area
     </h2>
     <p>
      One way to overcome culture shock is to try to have a little sameness from your life in your own country. Research your new home so you can find where those things are and incorporate them into your new life.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, you might love visiting farmers markets, or running through national parks. Find where those places are in your new area.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its also great to have a good idea of the layout of your new region and where the best places to live are. This will save a lot of headache and time for when you arrive.
     </p>
     <p>
      The less planning and running around you have to do when you arrive, the better youll feel and the less impact culture shock will have.
     </p>
     <p>
      Also note the transportation options. It might be a good idea to hire a car for the first couple of weeks to help you in organising a new place to live and any way you need to furnish it. It will also help you get a good idea of the layout of the city or town.
     </p>
     <p>
      Make sure you also know the transportation routes available for you.
     </p>
     <h2>
      8. Pre-arrange meet ups and start building relationships before you leave
     </h2>
     <p>
      The world is so much easier for this now with social media and online sites like Meetup.com. You can easily connect with people with the same interests as you in your new country.
     </p>
     <p>
      If you are moving to a new country with a job already organised you can start connecting with colleagues.
     </p>
     <p>
      I moved to the US with a job already in place through a company that employed teachers from around the world. I already had a network of friends in place and we connected straight away at our Orientation meeting. It made a huge difference to our ability to settle in and enjoy the experience.
     </p>
     <p>
      When I moved to London, I was on my own without a job, but I knew of some people from home living there. We werent really friends, but I contacted them to meet up and help me have a little of that home security. It was awesome and one of them is still one of my best friends.
     </p>
     <p>
      Quickly making friendships in your new home is the best way to ensure you wont be outplayed by culture shock.
     </p>
     <h2>
      9. Create your bucket list
     </h2>
     <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_43992" style="width: 650px">
      <a href="http://www.ytravelblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Visit-USA3.jpg">
       <img alt="Bryce Canyon, Utah, USA" class="wp-image-43992 size-full" height="480" src="http://www.ytravelblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Visit-USA3.jpg" width="640"/>
      </a>
      <p class="wp-caption-text">
       Working in the USA allowed us to do a big road trip and visit places like Bryce Canyon in Utah
      </p>
     </div>
     <p>
      So youre not really moving abroad just for work. Your main purpose is to travel and check off those bucket list items.
     </p>
     <p>
      Whenever I know Im moving to a new country, I buy my Lonely Planet books and sit down to my computer to do my research. My excitement is often enough fuel to fly me overseas myself!
     </p>
     <p>
      Your bucket list is essential. It will remind you why you are doing this during the tough times and will keep you focused on saving and taking the time out on weekends and during holidays to get out and see the local area.
     </p>
     <p>
      Okay! Now youre feeling a little more secure in your working abroad experience, its time to get going! Look out. Amazing adventures ahead.
     </p>

          Great tips! I think joining a sports club, a gym, or a sports team would also be a good idea to meet new people and make new friends. When I lived in England, I took courses in tap-dancing and flamenco dancing. Lots of fun, and made some new friends along the way as well.
         </p>
         <p>
          And if you move somewhere where English is not the main language, you could also take a language course: makes it easier to chat with locals or make friends among locals, and its also a great way to meet other expats who struggle with the new language and who are going through the same culture shock as you are.
         </p>
