     <p>
      <b>
       Acid erosion
      </b>
      , also known as
      <b>
       dental erosion
      </b>
      , is a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Tooth_wear" title="Tooth wear">
       tooth wear
      </a>
      . It is defined as the irreversible loss of tooth structure due to chemical dissolution by acids not of
      <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">
       bacterial
      </a>
      origin. Dental erosion is the most common chronic disease of children ages 517,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although it is only relatively recently that it has been recognised as a dental health problem.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dugnore2004_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dugnore2004-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is generally widespread ignorance of the damaging effects of acid erosion; this is particularly the case with erosion due to fruit juices, because they tend to be seen as healthy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Erosion is found initially in the
      <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">
       enamel
      </a>
      and, if unchecked, may proceed to the underlying
      <a href="/wiki/Dentin" title="Dentin">
       dentin
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PH_scale.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="696" data-file-width="729" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/PH_scale.png/250px-PH_scale.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/PH_scale.png/375px-PH_scale.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/PH_scale.png/500px-PH_scale.png 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PH_scale.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Frequently consumed foods and drinks below
        <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">
         pH
        </a>
        5.05.7 may initiate dental erosion.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The most common cause of erosion is by acidic foods and drinks. In general, foods and drinks with a
      <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">
       pH
      </a>
      below 5.05.7 have been known to trigger dental erosion effects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DentalErosionMandel_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DentalErosionMandel-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Numerous clinical and laboratory reports link erosion to excessive consumption of drinks. Those thought to pose a risk are soft drinks and fruit drinks, fruit juices such as
      <a href="/wiki/Orange_juice" title="Orange juice">
       orange juice
      </a>
      (which contain
      <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">
       citric acid
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">
       carbonated drinks
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Cola" title="Cola">
       colas
      </a>
      (in which the
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">
       carbonic acid
      </a>
      is not the cause of erosion, but citric and
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">
       phosphoric acid
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . Additionally,
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      has been shown to erode teeth, with the pH of wine as low as 3.03.8.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DentalErosionMandel_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DentalErosionMandel-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other possible sources of erosive acids are from exposure to
      <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
       chlorinated
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">
       swimming pool
      </a>
      water, and regurgitation of
      <a href="/wiki/Gastric_acid" title="Gastric acid">
       gastric acids
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
       Causes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_erosion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Extrinsic_acidic_sources">
       Extrinsic acidic sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_erosion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extrinsic acidic sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Acidic drinks and foods lower the pH level of the mouth so consuming them causes the teeth to demineralise. Drinks low in pH levels that cause dental erosion include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruit_juices" title="Fruit juices">
       fruit juices
      </a>
      , sports drinks,
      <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
       wine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">
       beer
      </a>
      and carbonated drinks . Orange and apple juices are common culprits among fruit juices. Carbonated drinks such as colas, lemonades are also very acidic, as are fruit-flavoured drinks and dilutables. Frequency rather than total intake of acidic juices is seen as the greater factor in dental erosion; infants using feeding bottles containing fruit juices (especially when used as a comforter) are therefore at greater risk of acid erosion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSullivan2008_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OSullivan2008-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">
       Saliva
      </a>
      acts as a buffer, regulating the pH when acidic drinks are ingested. Drinks vary in their resistance to the buffering effect of saliva. Studies show that fruit juices are the most resistant to saliva's buffering effect, followed by, in order: fruit-based carbonated drinks and flavoured mineral waters, non-fruit-based carbonated drinks, sparkling mineral waters;
      <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">
       Mineral water
      </a>
      being the least resistant. Because of this, fruit juices in particular, may prolong the drop in pH levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A number of medications such as vitamin C, aspirin and some iron preparations are acidic and may contribute towards acid erosion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSullivan2008_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OSullivan2008-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intrinsic_acidic_sources">
       Intrinsic acidic sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_erosion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Intrinsic acidic sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dental erosion can occur by non-extrinsic factors too. Intrinsic dental erosion is known as perimolysis, whereby
      <a href="/wiki/Gastric_acid" title="Gastric acid">
       gastric acid
      </a>
      from the stomach comes into contact with the teeth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandara1999_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gandara1999-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      People with diseases such as
      <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">
       anorexia nervosa
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulimia" title="Bulimia">
       bulimia
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">
       gastroesophageal reflux disease
      </a>
      (GERD) often suffer from this. GERD is quite common and an average of 7% of adults experience reflux daily.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandara1999_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gandara1999-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The main cause of GERD is increased acid production by the stomach.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandara1999_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gandara1999-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is not exclusive to adults, as GERD and other gastrointestinal disorders may cause dental erosions in children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Rumination_syndrome" title="Rumination syndrome">
       Rumination
      </a>
      also may cause acid erosion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">
       Behaviour
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_erosion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Acid erosion often coexists with
      <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(dental)" title="Abrasion (dental)">
       abrasion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Attrition_(dental)" title="Attrition (dental)">
       attrition
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSullivan2008_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-OSullivan2008-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Abrasion is most often caused by brushing teeth too hard.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dugnore2004_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dugnore2004-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Throthing or swishing acidic drinks around the mouth increases the risk of acid erosion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSullivan2008_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-OSullivan2008-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Signs">
       Signs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_erosion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Signs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dental_erosion_(hypoestrogenia)_no211.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="444" data-file-width="796" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Dental_erosion_%28hypoestrogenia%29_no211.jpg/220px-Dental_erosion_%28hypoestrogenia%29_no211.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Dental_erosion_%28hypoestrogenia%29_no211.jpg/330px-Dental_erosion_%28hypoestrogenia%29_no211.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Dental_erosion_%28hypoestrogenia%29_no211.jpg/440px-Dental_erosion_%28hypoestrogenia%29_no211.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dental_erosion_(hypoestrogenia)_no211.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <center>
         Dental erosion
        </center>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are many signs of dental erosion, including changes in appearance and sensitivity. One of the physical changes can be the color of teeth. There are two different colors teeth may turn if dental erosion is occurring, the first being a change of color that usually happens on the cutting edge of the central incisors. This causes the cutting edge of the tooth to become transparent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acid_attack_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-acid_attack-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A second sign is if the tooth has a yellowish tint. This occurs because the white enamel has eroded away to reveal the yellowish dentin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acid_attack_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-acid_attack-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A change in shape of the teeth is also a sign of dental erosion. Teeth will begin to appear with a broad rounded concavity, and the gaps between teeth will become larger. There can be evidence of wear on surfaces of teeth not expected to be in contact with one another.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acid_attack_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-acid_attack-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If dental erosion occurs in children, a loss of enamel surface characteristics can occur.
      <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(dentistry)" title="Amalgam (dentistry)">
       Amalgam
      </a>
      restorations in the mouth may be clean and non-tarnished. Fillings may also appear to be rising out of the tooth, the appearance being caused when the tooth is eroded away leaving only the filling. The teeth may form divots on the chewing surfaces when dental erosion is occurring. This mainly happens on the first, second, and third molars. One of the most severe signs of dental erosion is cracking,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where teeth begin to crack off and become coarse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acid_attack_12-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-acid_attack-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other signs include pain when eating hot, cold, or sweet foods. This pain is due to the enamel having been eroded away, exposing the sensitive dentin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention_and_management">
       Prevention and management
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_erosion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prevention and management">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Preventive and management strategies include the following:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Avoid sweet and acid foods. Even low sugar contained in fruit is bad for the teeth since it is the sugar/acid exposure time which erodes the teeth, not the amount of sugar.
      </li>
      <li>
       Modifying the pH of the food or beverage contributing to the problem, or changing lifestyle to avoid the food or beverage.
      </li>
      <li>
       Rinsing immediately after drinking or eating.
      </li>
      <li>
       Drinking through a straw
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards1998_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Edwards1998-16">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         16
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Avoid abrasive forces. Use a soft bristled toothbrush and brush gently. Avoid brushing immediately after consuming acidic food and drink as teeth will be softened. Leave at least half an hour of time inbetween. Rinsing with water is better than brushing after consuming acidic foods and drinks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSullivan2008_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-OSullivan2008-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gandara1999_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gandara1999-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Using a
       <a href="/wiki/Remineralisation_of_teeth" title="Remineralisation of teeth">
        remineralizing
       </a>
       agent, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">
        sodium fluoride
       </a>
       solution in the form of a fluoride mouthrinse, tablet, or lozenge, immediately before brushing teeth.
      </li>
      <li>
       Applying fluoride gels or varnishes to the teeth.
      </li>
      <li>
       Drinking milk or using other dairy products.
      </li>
      <li>
       Dentine bonding agents applied to areas of exposed dentin
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSullivan2008_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-OSullivan2008-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Use a neutralizing agent such as antacid tablets only as a last-resort. They have negative long-run effects.
      </li>
      <li>
       Treating the underlying medical disorder or disease.
      </li>
     </ul>
