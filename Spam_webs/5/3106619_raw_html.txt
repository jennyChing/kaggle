    <section class="content ">
     <div class="parsys editorsNote">
     </div>
     <div class="newbody body parsys">
      <div class="parbase image slate_image section">
       <div class=" none">
        <figure class="image inline " style="width: 590px;  display: block;margin: 0 auto;float: none;">
         <img alt="480548439-television-personality-adam-carolla-speaks-during-a" src="/content/dam/slate/blogs/future_tense/2014/08/01/adam_carolla_won_t_let_personal_audio_drop_podcast_patent_infringement_lawsuit/480548439-television-personality-adam-carolla-speaks-during-a.jpg.CROP.promo-mediumlarge.jpg" src_tag_name="src" title="480548439-television-personality-adam-carolla-speaks-during-a"/>
         <figcaption class="caption">
          <span>
           Adam Carolla.
          </span>
         </figcaption>
         <p class="credit">
          Photo by David Becker/Getty Images for Nightclub &amp; Bar Media Group
         </p>
        </figure>
       </div>
       <script>
        requirejs(["jquery"],function($){if($(window).width()&lt;640){$(".slate_image figure").width("100%");}});
       </script>
      </div>
      <div class="text text-1 parbase section">
       <p>
        As the fight against patent trolls grinds on, stymied by
        <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/future_tense/2014/05/29/leahy_patent_transparency_and_improvements_act_what_happens_now.html">
         government inability
        </a>
        (or unwillingness) to fix a malfunctioning patent system, anti-troll advocates could take cold comfort this week from a particularly amusing press release.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text-2 text parbase section">
       <p>
        Personal Audio has been demanding cash from broadcasters large and small for allegedly infringing its patent on delivering episodic online contentthe so-called podcasting patent. The company, which is based in Texas, makes no podcasts and does little but litigate against companies that it says are using its patents without a license, including major television video broadcasters like
        <a href="http://personalaudio.net/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/2013-04-12-Podcasting-Patent-Asserted-Against-NBC-and-CBS6.pdf">
         CBS and NBC
        </a>
        .
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="tablet-ad">
       Advertisement
       <br/>
       <div class="ad " data-ad-mode="tabletPortrait" data-ad-type="midarticleflex" id="slug_midarticleflex">
       </div>
       <div class="ad " data-ad-mode="mobile" data-ad-type="mob_bigbox" id="slug_mob_bigbox">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="text-3 text parbase section">
       <p>
        But this week, the company
        <a href="http://personalaudio.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Carolla-DIsmissal_7-29-2014.pdf">
         announced
        </a>
        that it wanted to drop a long-running case against comedian and podcaster Adam Carolla. Why? Well, he just isnt making that much money.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-4 section">
       <blockquote>
        When Personal Audio first began its litigation, it was under the impression that Carolla, the self-proclaimed largest podcaster in the world, as well as certain other podcasters, were making significant money from infringing Personal Audios patents. After the parties completed discovery, however, it became clear this was not the case. As a result, Personal Audio began to offer dismissals from the case to the podcasting companies involved, rather than to litigate over the smaller amounts of money at issue.
       </blockquote>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-5 section">
       <p>
        Earlier this year, Personal Audio also
        <a href="http://personalaudio.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Carolla-DIsmissal_7-29-2014.pdf">
         dismissed
        </a>
        podcasters Togi Net and How Stuff Works from the case.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text text-6 parbase section">
       <p>
        Carolla, for his part, refused Personal Audios offer to dismiss.
        <a href="https://fundanything.com/patenttroll?locale=en">
         A statement
        </a>
        , which appears on the page hes using to fundraise to defend the lawsuit, says that although its unable to comment on the proceedings, Carollas podcasting company Lotzi Digital Inc. intends to continue to vigorously defend Personal Audios lawsuit and to pursue its counterclaims against Personal Audio, which include a request that the Personal Audio patent be invalidated so that Personal Audio cannot sue other podcasters for infringement.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-7 section">
       <p>
        As Charles Duan, director of the Patent Reform Project at Public Knowledge,
        <a href="https://www.publicknowledge.org/news-blog/blogs/why-adam-corolla-should-keep-fighting-a-patent-troll">
         wrote
        </a>
        , if the case is simply dismissed, then the patent cannot be found by the courts to be invalid. Carolla is fighting on behalf of the whole public, everyone who makes, uses, and enjoys podcasts.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-8 section">
       <p>
        So how did a company that once released magazine articles on cassette tapes (kind of a crude form of podcasting, you might say, someone from Personal Audio once
        <a href="http://www.thisamericanlife.org/radio-archives/episode/496/transcript">
         suggested
        </a>
        ) get a patent on podcasting? Just by filing a claim to the Patent Office asserting that it came up with a 
        <a href="http://www.google.com/patents/US8112504?dq=8,112,504&amp;ei=FrkRUc63N8zWigKlt4DABg">
         system for disseminating media content representing episodes in a serialized sequence
        </a>
        . That patent was granted in 2012, and at the time, Personal Audio put out a
        <a href="http://personalaudio.net/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/2012-12-30-Podcasting-Patent-Issued-Press-Release1.pdf">
         statement
        </a>
        claiming that the company invented podcasting in 1996.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="topcomment section">
       <aside class="top-comment top-comment-link">
        <a class="primary" href="#lf_comment=195668671">
        </a>
        <p class="title">
         <span>
          Top Comment
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="quote">
         <p>
          However you feel about him, personally or politically, good for Carolla.
          <a>
           More...
          </a>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align:right; margin-bottom:-5px; margin-top:-18px;">
          -Return of LH
         </p>
        </div>
        <p class="comment-options">
         <a class="count labeled" href="#comments">
         </a>
         <a class="join-in" href="#comments">
          Join In
         </a>
        </p>
       </aside>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-9 section">
       <p>
        But in the Electronic Frontier Foundations
        <a href="https://www.eff.org/files/2013/10/16/_inter_partes_review_petition_of_us_pat._no._8112504.pdf">
         petition to have
        </a>
        the Patent Office review and invalidate the patent, the advocacy group suggests that the medium of podcasting had already been online about three years earlierCarl Malamud and his Geek of the Week podcast launched in 1993. As EFF Staff Attorney Daniel Nazer
        <a href="https://www.eff.org/press/releases/eff-files-challenge-patent-office-against-trolls-podcasting-patent">
         said
        </a>
        , "If you look into the history of podcasting, you won't see anything about Personal Audio." Early this year, the Patent Trial and Appeal Board
        <a href="https://www.eff.org/files/2014/04/21/21_-_institution_decision.pdf">
         said that
        </a>
        the EFFs petition had a "reasonable likelihood" of success.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-10 section">
       <p>
        Personal Audios lawsuit is more than an irritant to podcasters. It could also be highly damaginglaw suits are expensive, and as Personal Audio is discovering, podcasting is not often a high margins venture. That squares with broader trends55 percent of companies served by patent trolls make
        <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/future_tense/2014/05/29/leahy_patent_transparency_and_improvements_act_what_happens_now.html">
         $10 million per year or less
        </a>
        . For this reason, many of those targeted settle or
        <a href="http://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2146251">
         go out of business
        </a>
        rather than fight.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text-11 text parbase section">
       <p>
        Carollas case, as well as those of the TV broadcasters, should be held before an East Texas court in September, Joe Mullin
        <a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2014/07/podcasting-patent-troll-we-tried-to-drop-lawsuit-against-adam-carolla/">
         wrote
        </a>
        in
        <em>
         Ars Technica
        </em>
        . Unfortunately, the cases will likely be heard before the EFFs petition is considered by the patent officebut either way, Personal Audios lawsuits will hopefully find some real resolution this year.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text-12 text parbase section">
       <p>
        In the meantime, we can derive some amusement from Personal Audios quixotic quest. Mostly because, as Mike Masnick
        <a href="https://www.techdirt.com/articles/20140729/18073928049/podcasting-patent-troll-realizes-podcasters-dont-make-any-money-desperately-tries-to-escape-adam-carolla-lawsuit.shtml">
         points out
        </a>
        on
        <em>
         TechDirt
        </em>
        , representatives of Personal Audio show an uncommon willingness to talk to journalists.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text-13 text parbase section">
       <p>
        Take a 2013 episode of
        <em>
         This American Life
        </em>
        , 
        <a href="http://www.thisamericanlife.org/radio-archives/episode/496/transcript">
         When Patents Attack ... Part Two
        </a>
        ! Reporter Zoe Chace convinced Jim Logan and Richard Baker of Personal Audio to be interviewed. When she pointed out that
        <em>
         This American Life
        </em>
        had not paid them any license fee, this gem was the result:
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text text-14 parbase section">
       <blockquote>
        Zoe Chace: So are we doing something illegal right now, though?
       </blockquote>
      </div>
      <div class="text text-15 parbase section">
       <blockquote>
        Richard Baker: I wouldn't comment on that.
       </blockquote>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-16 section">
       <blockquote>
        Man: I wouldn't comment on it.
       </blockquote>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-17 section">
       <blockquote>
        Jim Logan: We don't--I don't comment on the legalities.
       </blockquote>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-18 section">
       <p>
        For a company that seems to exist solely to exercise the limits of the U.S. legal system, not commenting on the legalities of the case is like Kim Kardashian refusing to be photographed.
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
