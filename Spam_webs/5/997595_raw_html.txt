   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Nictitating membrane
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       nictitating membrane
      </b>
      (from Latin
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nicto" title="wiktionary:nicto">
        nictare
       </a>
      </i>
      , to blink) is a transparent or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent">
       translucent
      </a>
      third
      <a href="/wiki/Eyelid" title="Eyelid">
       eyelid
      </a>
      present in some animals that can be drawn across the
      <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">
       eye
      </a>
      for protection and to moisten it while maintaining visibility. Some reptiles, birds, and
      <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">
       sharks
      </a>
      have full nictitating membranes; in many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">
       mammals
      </a>
      , a small,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestigial" title="Vestigial">
       vestigial
      </a>
      portion of the membrane remains in the corner of the eye. Some mammals, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">
       camels
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">
       polar bears
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">
       seals
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Aardvark" title="Aardvark">
       aardvarks
      </a>
      , have full nictitating membranes. Often called a
      <b>
       third eyelid
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       haw
      </b>
      , it may be referred to in scientific terminology as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plica_semilunaris_of_the_conjunctiva" title="Plica semilunaris of the conjunctiva">
       <i>
        plica semilunaris
       </i>
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       membrana nictitans
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       palpebra tertia
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:17.23a.tif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1120" data-file-width="1680" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/17.23a.tif/lossy-page1-220px-17.23a.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/17.23a.tif/lossy-page1-330px-17.23a.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/17.23a.tif/lossy-page1-440px-17.23a.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:17.23a.tif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The nictitating membrane of a
        <a href="/wiki/Blue_shark" title="Blue shark">
         blue shark
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fully developed nictitating membranes are found in fish,
      <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">
       amphibians
      </a>
      , reptiles, birds and mammals, but are rare in
      <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">
       primates
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler05_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-butler05-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In humans, the plica semilunaris (also known as the semilunar fold) and its associated muscles are
      <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">
       homologous
      </a>
      to the nictitating membranes seen in some other mammals and other vertebrates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In most primate species, a plica semilunaris is present, although fully developed nictitating membranes can be found in
      <a href="/wiki/Lemur" title="Lemur">
       lemurs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lorisoidea" title="Lorisoidea">
       lorisoid
      </a>
      primates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Unlike the upper and lower eyelids, the nictitating membrane moves horizontally across the eyeball. It is normally translucent. In some diving animals, for example
      <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">
       beavers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Manatee" title="Manatee">
       manatees
      </a>
      , it moves across the eye to protect it while under water, and in these species it is transparent; in other diving animals, including
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">
       sea lions
      </a>
      , it is activated on land, to remove sand and other debris. This is its function in most animals. In birds of prey, it also serves to protect the parents' eyes from their chicks while they are feeding them, and when
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcons" title="Peregrine falcons">
       peregrine falcons
      </a>
      go into their 200-mile-per-hour (320km/h) dives, they will blink repeatedly with their nictitating membranes to clear debris and spread moisture across the eyes. In polar bears, it protects the eyes from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_blindness" title="Snow blindness">
       snow blindness
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Crocodilia" title="Crocodilia">
       crocodiles
      </a>
      , it protects their eyes from water but also hinders their focus under water. In sharks, it protects their eyes while the shark strikes at its prey.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodpeckers" title="Woodpeckers">
       Woodpeckers
      </a>
      tighten their nictitating membrane a millisecond prior to their beak impacting the trunk of a tree to prevent shaking-induced retinal injury.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </a>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nictitating_membrane_-_Nickhaut_-_Membrana_nittitante_-_(Balearica_pavonina_-_Weltvogelpark_2013).jpg" title="Enlarge">

        </div>
        The nictitating membrane (completely closed) of a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Crowned_Crane" title="Black Crowned Crane">

      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The nictitating membrane in cats and dogs does not have many muscle fibers, so consequently is not usually visible, and its being chronically visible should be taken as a sign of poor condition or ill health. It can, however, be seen clearly when gently opening the eye of the healthy animal when it is asleep, or pushing down/applying pressure on the eyeball, which will cause it to appear. In some breeds of dogs, the nictitating membrane can be prone to
      <a href="/wiki/Prolapse" title="Prolapse">
       prolapse
      </a>
      , resulting in a condition called
      <a href="/wiki/Cherry_eye" title="Cherry eye">
       cherry eye
      </a>
      . The cherry eye is due to a gland called the gland of the third eyelid (nictitans gland), which prolapses. This gland helps to produce up to 50% of the tear film in certain animals. Another gland, called
      <a href="/wiki/Harderian_gland" title="Harderian gland">
       Harder's gland
      </a>
      , is present in other animals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Birds can actively control their nictitating membrane.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In many species, any stimulus to the eyeball (such as a puff of air) will result in reflex nictitating membrane response. This reflex is widely used as the basis for experiments on
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_conditioning" title="Classical conditioning">
       classical conditioning
      </a>
      in rabbits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
