       <h1 class="article-title">
        There Are Ashes of Dead People Orbiting Earth
       </h1>
       <section class="motherboard-portlet motherboard-portlet-about" id="yw0">
        <div class="author-info image">
         <a href="/author/KaleighRogers">
          <div class="article-media-overlay">

        <div class="article-content rich-text">
         <p>
          
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           As New Horizons,
          </inline>
          <a href="http://motherboard.vice.com/read/after-nine-years-in-space-nasas-pluto-probe-is-about-to-wake-up" style="background-color: initial;" target="_blank">
           the first spacecraft NASAs sent to Pluto
          </a>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           , begins its encounter with the dwarf planet today, it carries with it some special cargo: the cremated body of Clyde Tombaugh. Tombaugh, who died in 1997, discovered Pluto in 1930, so its fitting that NASA decided to
          </inline>
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2006/01/20/science/space/20pluto.html?_r=0" style="background-color: initial;" target="_blank">
           included his remains
          </a>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           on the first mission to the dwarf planet when it launched the probe back in 2006, with the blessing of his family.
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          Still, this space burial is unusual for NASAand certainly for the rest of usbut its actually not all that unusual for the private space travel industry. In fact, the remains of dozens of men and women have been fired into space over the last 20 years, many of which are still orbiting us today, as I found out chatting with the man who sent them there.
         </p>
         <p>
          NASA generally doesnt perform space burials. Theyve only participated in three, not counting Tombaughs. The first was in 1992, when one of the astronauts aboard the space shuttle Columbia brought some of Star Trek creator Gene Roddenberrys ashes
          <a href="http://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=348&amp;dat=19940429&amp;id=bUMvAAAAIBAJ&amp;sjid=CjMDAAAAIBAJ&amp;pg=1725,8689414" target="_blank">
           aboard as a personal effect
          </a>
          before returning the remains to Earth.
         </p>
         <p>
          This past December, at the request of coworkers and family, NASA included the remains of Patrick OMalley, an aeronautical engineer, on
          <a href="http://motherboard.vice.com/read/heres-nasa-testing-orion-the-ship-that-will-take-us-to-mars" target="_blank">
           the test launch of Orion
          </a>
          the ship it hopes will one day take people to Mars. OMalley
          <a href="http://wtop.com/news/2014/12/mans-remains-travel-to-space-with-nasas-orion/" target="_blank">
           worked on Orion for more than a decade
          </a>
          but died of an undiagnosed brain illness before he could see the project to fruition. Since it was a test launch, his remains were also brought back to Earth.
         </p>
         <p>
          But thats nothing compared to the treatment given to Gene Shoemaker, the astrogeologist NASA helped to bury on the moon. Shoemakerwho discovered the
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Shoemaker%E2%80%93Levy_9" target="_blank">
           Shoemaker-Levy 9
          </a>
          cometdied in a car accident in 1997. After his death, one of his colleagues at the University of Arizona proposed the idea of having Shoemakers remains sent to space. With the exception of Roddenberry, NASA hadnt done anything like that before, so they turned to a company that had:
          <a href="http://www.celestis.com/" target="_blank">
           Celestis, Inc.
          </a>
         </p>
         <p class="has-image">
          <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="375" data-original-width="746" src="//motherboard-images.vice.com/content-images/contentimage/18333/1421358515679852.png"/>
         </p>
         <p class="photo-credit">
          Eugene Shoemaker. Image:
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Merle_Shoemaker#mediaviewer/File:Eugene_Shoemaker.jpg" target="_blank">
           WikiMedia Commons
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          The Texas-based company
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/1997/04/22/world/a-final-turn-on-lifts-timothy-leary-off.html" target="_blank">
           made headlines
          </a>
          when it launched the remains of 24 peopleincluding astrophysicist Gerard ONeill, author Timothy Leary, and some more of Roddenberrys ashesinto space.
         </p>
         <p>
          They wanted to make Gene Shoemaker the first man buried on the moon and NASAs response was well, we dont do that but we know these guys down in Texas that do, Charles Chafer, co-founder of Celestis, told me over the phone.
         </p>
         <p>
          Working with Celestis, NASA
          <a href="http://www2.jpl.nasa.gov/sl9/news82.html" target="_blank">
           brought Shoemakers remains
          </a>
          aboard the Lunar Prospector, which orbited the moon creating detailed surface maps before dropping down into a lunar crater at the end of the mission. Shoemakers ashes are still aboard the spacecraft, in the middle of a crater on the moon.
         </p>
         <p>
          Celestis has been doing space burialsor memorial spaceflights, as they call them, since the remains often are returned to Earthsince the mid-90s. Rather than pay for expensive launches themselves, Celestis hitches their passengers a ride on existing rocket launches that are doing things like sending satellites into orbit.
         </p>
         <p>
          A primary satellite purchases the rocketthats the guy that pays the $100 million to fly. But these rockets almost always over-perform and so theyve got secondary space available, which we purchase, Chafer explained to me.
         </p>
         <p>
          And yes, theyre available to the public. If youre interested in a space burial, for $1,000, you can send a loved ones ashes 100 km into space. The catch is that the cargo is then shed by the rocket and comes back to Earth via parachuteCelestis collects the capsule and gives the remains back to the families.
         </p>
         <p>
          For $5,000, you get a longer trip, riding on a satellite orbiting the Earth for anywhere from a few months to a few centuriesCelestis doesnt get much say in the matter, its up to the primary satellite.
         </p>
         <p>
          We go where the primary goes. Some of our early missions are scheduled to be up 700 years or so, Chafer said.
         </p>
         <p>
          But for $12,500, you can get the ultimate space burial: firing your remains into deep space, never to return to Earth.
         </p>
         <p>
          Over the years, Celestis has sent dozens of peoples earthly remains into space, from science fiction writers, to actorsthe remains of James Doohan, who played Scotty in the original Star Trek series, have been up three timesand ordinary people who simply had a special fascination with or love of space exploration.
         </p>
         <p>
          Chafer himself hopes to have his ashes rocketed off the planet after he dies. He jokes that its because hes a Baby Boomer, and that his generation wants to do everything differently, but theres a more humble goal behind all these dead men and women floating around in space. Celestis is just one arm of a larger corporation that seeks to increase the availability of private space travel. Along with seeing a market for this particular service, Chafer started Celestis because he saw it as a way to give people a somewhat affordable way to participate and contribute to space exploration.
         </p>
         <p>
          I like the idea that by me paying some money to have this service, Im increasing the demand for commercial space activity, he told me. Im broadening, in my little way, humanitys movement into the cosmos.
         </p>
        </div>
