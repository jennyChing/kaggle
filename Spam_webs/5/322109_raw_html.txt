       <h1 class="title">
        <a href="http://uxmovement.com/forms/9-things-designers-can-learn-from-targets-checkout-form/" rel="bookmark" title="9 Things Designers Can Learn from Targets Checkout Form">
         9 Things Designers Can Learn from Targets Checkout Form
        </a>
       </h1>
       <p class="post-details">
        by
        <a href="http://uxmovement.com/author/anthony/" rel="author" title="Posts by anthony">
         anthony
        </a>
        on 09/13/11 at 9:31 pm
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://target.com" target="_blank">
         Target
        </a>
        recently redesigned their website. While many have their ownopinions about it, there a few positive things designers can learn from Targets checkout form. At first glance it may not look like much, but once you dig deeper youll find a diamond in the rough.
       </p>
       <h2>
        1. Crystal Clear Page Headings
       </h2>
       <p>
        When the user gets to the checkout form, theres no question in their mind where they are. The large, bold and centered heading at the top makes it clear to users what page theyre on and what task theyre about to do. At the same time, its diminished with a gray color so that it doesnt take the focus away from the more important section headings in the form.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4787" height="94" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+heading.png" width="435"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        2. Input Mask for Phone Field
       </h2>
       <p>
        Phone numbers are one of the toughest things to get from a user. The user not only needs to know why youre asking them for their phone number, but they also need to know what format to type it in. Target tells their users why they need their phone number by adding a short note to the right of the field label. But the best part about their phone field is that they control the users format by using an input mask. This way theyre able to get the format they want from the user every time without confusing them.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Do it yourself:
         <a href="http://digitalbush.com/projects/masked-input-plugin/">
          Masked Input Plugin
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h2>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4788" height="90" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+input+mask.png" width="504"/>
       </h2>
       <h2>
        3. Instant Field Validation and Confirmation
       </h2>
       <p>
        Most forms that use instant field validation only validate for user errors. Thats not enough. Target goes the extra mile by also confirming success when the user corrects their error. This gives users the assurance they need to move forward to the next step on the form. Keeping users in the dark makes them wonder if they have corrected their error and if their information will go through. If you make the user think that after they fill out your form theres a chance it might not go through, the user wont want to fill out your form because theyll feel like theyre going to end up doing all that work for nothing. Instant field confirmation puts their worries to rest.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Learn how to do it the right way:
         <a href="http://uxmovement.com/forms/why-long-forms-need-instant-field-validation/" rel="bookmark" title="Why Long Forms Need Instant Field Validation">
          Why Long Forms Need Instant Field Validation
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4789" height="98" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+validation.png" width="405"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        4. Making Checkbox Questions That Users Often Ignore Unavoidable
       </h2>
       <p>
        Text fields and dropdown lists are widgets on forms that easily catch users attention. Theyre big, visual and shout at the user. Checkboxes, on other hand, are small, placed below the form and easy to ignore. Target makes their checkboxes unavoidable by using a large font to emphasize the questions in an elegant way. This not only makes the questions easier to read than plain text, but it also makes them impossible to miss as users move down the form.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4790" height="150" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+checkboxes.png" width="481"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        5. Unsaturated Button Color for Secondary Actions
       </h2>
       <p>
        Target treats their primary and secondary buttons differently. They use astrong, saturated red color on primary actions that submit the users information. But then they use aweak, unsaturated blue color onsecondary actions that modify the users information. This helps users differentiate between these two types of actions. Many sites make the mistake of using the same color on all of their form buttons. By using color contrast and saturation, Target makes their primary and secondary action buttons clear.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Learn more about primary and secondary actions:
         <a href="http://uxmovement.com/buttons/the-visual-weight-of-primary-and-secondary-action-buttons/" rel="bookmark" title="The Visual Weight of Primary and Secondary Action Buttons">
          The Visual Weight of Primary and Secondary Action Buttons
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4791" height="52" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+buttons.png" width="332"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        6. Fast Spinning Loading Icon
       </h2>
       <p>
        In any checkout, the user has to wait for their information to process. Target cleverly gives users a feeling offast load times by making their loading icons spin faster than normal. While this doesnt actually make the load times faster, the effect it has on the usersperceptionof time is whats impressive.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4792" height="61" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+loading+icon.png" width="79"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        7. Credit Card Icons to Illustrate What They Accept
       </h2>
       <p>
        Not every store accepts every credit card. Users need to know which ones they can use for their purchase. Target shows icons of the credit cards and gift cards they accept. This quickly tells users which cards they can use at a glance. Users can then select the one they want to use in the dropdown list with ease.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4793" height="33" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+credit+cards.png" width="203"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        8. Putting Promo Code Fields at the End of the Process
       </h2>
       <p>
        Promo codes are useful for giving customers special discounts. However, putting promo code field at the beginning of your form can hurt your form conversion rate. Target puts their promo code fields for customer and employee discounts at the end of the form process, which keeps users more committed to completing their checkout.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         A study on how promo codes affect conversion rates:
         <a href="http://www.conversiondoctor.com/conversion-blog/coupon-codes-increase-checkout-abandonment">
          Do Coupon Codes Increase Checkout Abandonment?
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4794" height="160" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+promocodes.png" width="501"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        9. Highlighting the Order Summary Box
       </h2>
       <p>
        Users will want to know how much theyre spending as the products in their cart add up. Targethighlightstheir order summary box with a light, unsaturated blue color fill that contrasts with the rest of the elements on the page, making it easy for users to see.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4795" height="240" src="http://uxmovement.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/checkout+order+summary.png" width="295"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        Effective Form Techniques
       </h2>
       <p>
        Targets site redesign isnt perfect in every way. However, they have managed to turn their checkout form into an interesting and productive user experience by using effective form techniques that most retail store sites would never think to use. These nine hidden gems in targets checkout form can offer designers new insight they can use to improve their checkout forms. The checkout form isnt the most exciting area of a site to design. But when designed right, its the one area that can reap the most rewards.
       </p>
       <p style="padding: 10px 0">
       </p>
