                 <img alt="Photobucket" border="0" src="http://i337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/stairsandemeraldshoes.jpg"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                 (show with our
                 <a href="http://joyfolie.com/_product_6069/Emerald_City_Heels">
                  Emerald City heels
                 </a>
                 )
                </span>
               </div>
               <br/>
               I have to say, of all the DIY posts that I have made so far, this one is my favorite! While the kiddos were little and learning to walk, we were quite happy to have carpeted stairs. However, imagine running up and down the stairs coffee in hand  or little kids carrying sippy cups full of chocolate milk and after awhile--the carpet on the stairs was in sad shape. We have wood floors through most of our home, so I wanted to replace the stairs with a solid surface and lose the carpet. However, to have pre-finished wood risers professionally installed would probably be around $6K, and we don't want to invest that much in this house, since we don't think we'll be in it much longer. Plus, if I wanted to spend that amount of $, it would be
               <a href="http://www.joyfolieblog.com/2011/06/dreaming-of-sintra.html">
                on a trip to Sinta, Portugal
               </a>
               .
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Pinning on Pinterest, I stumbled across this photo from
               <a href="http://www.lovelycraftyhome.com/2011/03/22/brown-paper-walnut-stain-mocha-love/">
                Lovely Crafty Home
               </a>
               of stairs refinished with paper bags--that's right--paper bags!
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://s337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/?action=view&amp;current=lovelycraftyhome.jpg" target="_blank">
                 <img alt="Photobucket" border="0" src="http://i337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/lovelycraftyhome.jpg"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               I tend to gravitate towards unusual things and was really taken with her paper bag floor finish. Anyway, the next Saturday came around and before I knew it, I was up to my elbows in torn out carpet. {That's sort of my modus operandi. One day, in our old home, Robert came home from work to discover I had knocked down half a wall.}
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Back to the paper floors, I am absolutely thrilled with the results! At one point in my 20's I worked at a flooring store doing marketing and interior design. I had always loved cork flooring from Portugal, and the brown paper steps looks quite a lot like that. First, I tore out the carpet, pad, tac strips, and all nails and staples. Next, I bought a gallon of elmer's glue, a big roll of brown paper, water-based polyurethane, a couple sticks of trim, liquid nails, and 3 rolls of white wallpaper border.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I brushed each step with a layer of straight glue. Then I dipped pieces of crumpled brown paper in a solution of 3 parts water to 1 part glue. Once they were crumpled, I would apply them to the step and smooth them out--a la paper mache. What is really important, I think, is to lay the paper on a floor brushed with straight glue. If you look at the brown paper floors at
               <a href="http://www.anoregoncottage.com/2010/08/video-diy-alternative-to-wood-floors.html">
                An Oregon Cottage
               </a>
               , the floors are really light and really look like paper. When I was putting the paper down, I was afraid it might come up if applied with only the watery glue mixture. As it turns out, laying down the paper on straight glue turned the floors much darker, which I really love. I thought I would have to stain them, but I actually love them just like how they turned out.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               After all the treads were covered with paper, I let them cure for a day. The risers of the subfloor had a lot of knicks and dings in them--and I wasn't too keen on having to apply 3 coats of white paint to get the risers looking good. So, I brushed a textured wallpaper border {called anaglypta} with more elmer's glue, and applied to to the risers. I love anaglypta because looks so much like vintage painted copper ceilings. It also has a really clean look that still has textural interest. It was a very fast and inexpensive way to give a clean look to the risers. To make the steps extra clean, I finished the bottom of each one with white trim. I am not sure what kind of trim exactly, but it was thinner than quarter round. I broke the chop saw trying to cut a pillar {really stupid on my part} and this trim was so light and thin, I was able to cut it with shears. I attached them with liquid nails. After that, I applied 4 coats of water-based polyurethane. I have to say--this project
               <u>
                way
               </u>
               exceeded my expectation! The steps look great--in fact, better than anything more traditional. It's a shame that I don't have the right equipment to take pictures that do them justice:
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://s337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/?action=view&amp;current=stairsone.jpg" target="_blank">
                 <img alt="Photobucket" border="0" src="http://i337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/stairsone.jpg"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://s337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/?action=view&amp;current=stairstop.jpg" target="_blank">
                 <img alt="Photobucket" border="0" src="http://i337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/stairstop.jpg"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                the up close pic again:
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://s337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/?action=view&amp;current=stairsandemeraldshoes.jpg" target="_blank">
                 <img alt="Photobucket" border="0" src="http://i337.photobucket.com/albums/n362/MiaJoieboutique/stairsandemeraldshoes.jpg"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                If you like this look, definitely visit
                <a href="http://www.anoregoncottage.com/2010/08/video-diy-alternative-to-wood-floors.html">
                 An Oregon Cottage
                </a>
                or
                <a href="http://www.lovelycraftyhome.com/2011/03/22/brown-paper-walnut-stain-mocha-love/">
                 Lovely Crafty Home
                </a>
                for lots more tips on this technique. I love it! I think it would even be interesting to do something non-traditional in a studio space... like sheet music or vintage book pages. Anyway, hope you enjoy!

IF YOU WANT TO SAVE A STEP then instead of glue, after your paper is crumbled (the more crumples, the more character the paper has), put it in the Polycrylic (the water based top coat you will be using anyway), squeeze it out and apply to wall or floor.
