       <div class="txt step-body">
        <p>
         The availability of 3D printers and printing services is making it easier for aspiring hobbyists to get involved in creating sculptures, toys, and gadgets. With access to these tools, designers and inventors can rapidly prototype and evolve their ideas.
        </p>
        <p>
         This tutorial is about creating articulated (movable) 3d printable figures, like my articulated crab, turtle, and crayfish. These models come right out of the 3D printer with moving parts - they dont even need to be assembled!
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uf0EdgbonaM" rel="nofollow">
          Here's a quick video
         </a>
         that shows how these figures are articulated.
        </p>
        <p>
         For the articulation to be successful, the printer you choose should be able to create nested parts (example: a two-part hinge with enough spacing between parts so they dont end up fused together). Currently, the best choices are laser-sintering printers that fuse layers of powder together, or SLA printers like the Formlabs Form 1+, that use lasers to selectively cure layers in a bath of resin. [I'm submitting this Instructable to the Instructables FormLabs contest, in hopes of winning their 3D printer so I can create more of my articulated figures. Being able to prototype all of my creations at home is something I've wanted for years!]
        </p>
        <p>
         However, even if you dont have a 3D printer, fear not! Shapeways is a great company that I use handle the 3D printing. After going through the steps of this tutorial, youll have a 3d model that can be sent to Shapeways for them to do the printing for you.
        </p>

       <h2 class="step-title">
        Step 1: Getting Started: Choosing a Subject
       </h2>
       <div class="photoset" data-entry-id="SIDQ9LWI3KCFTDV" data-entry-url="/id/How-to-Create-3d-Printable-Articulated-Sculptures/step1/Getting-Started-Choosing-a-Subject/" id="photoset-SIDQ9LWI3KCFTDV">
        <div class="row" style="width:600px;">
         <a class="photoset-link" data-fancybox-group="photoset-gallery-SIDQ9LWI3KCFTDV" data-fancybox-href="http://cdn.instructables.com/FAL/XT31/I3KCG2T3/FALXT31I3KCG2T3.LARGE.jpg" data-href="/file/FALXT31I3KCG2T3" data-notes="" href="/file/FALXT31I3KCG2T3" id="photoset-link-FK9UKJ6I3PRQD7O-0" style="">

           <img alt="902688_10100202805053848_1608809721_o.jpg" class="photoset-photo id_F149TSLI3KCG2UT lazyphoto" data-notes="" data-original="http://cdn.instructables.com/F14/9TSL/I3KCG2UT/F149TSLI3KCG2UT.MEDIUM.jpg" src="/static/img/pixel.png" style="height:600px;"/>
           <noscript>
            <img alt="902688_10100202805053848_1608809721_o.jpg" class="photoset-photo" src="http://cdn.instructables.com/F14/9TSL/I3KCG2UT/F149TSLI3KCG2UT.MEDIUM.jpg" style="height:600px;"/>

       <div class="txt step-body">
        <p>
         Theres really no limit to what you can model and print. To start off, I recommend designing something that is simple and small, just to get the hang of the process first. If youre like me, you might want to create insects or crustaceans. For this Instructable, I'll use the shore crab as an example. I used to catch these little crabs  in California whenever we visited the tide pools. A crab or spider might be a good starting point if you like small creatures: they have many pairs of similar legs, making it easier to model on the computer.
        </p>
