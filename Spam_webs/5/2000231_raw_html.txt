     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
       ancient Sparta
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       Krypteia
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Crypteia
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Greek
      </a>
      : 
      <i>
       kruptea
      </i>
      from 
      <i>
       krupts
      </i>
      , "hidden, secret things") was a state institution involving young Spartan men. Its goal and nature are still a matter of discussion and debate among historians, but some scholars (
      <a href="/wiki/Henri-Alexandre_Wallon" title="Henri-Alexandre Wallon">
       Wallon
      </a>
      ) consider the krypteia to be a kind of
      <a href="/wiki/Secret_police" title="Secret police">
       secret police
      </a>
      and state security force organized by the ruling classes of
      <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
       Sparta
      </a>
      whose purpose was to terrorize the servile
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helot" title="Helot">
       helot
      </a>
      population. Others (
      <a href="/wiki/Hermann_K%C3%B6chly" title="Hermann Kchly">
       Kchly
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wachsmuth" title="Wilhelm Wachsmuth">
       Wachsmuth
      </a>
      ) believe it to be a form of military training, similar to the Athenian
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephebia" title="Ephebia">
        ephebia
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_function">
       History and function
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crypteia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and function">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Certain young Spartan men who had completed their training at the
      <a href="/wiki/Agoge" title="Agoge">
       agoge
      </a>
      with such success that they were marked out as potential future leaders would be given the opportunity to test their skills and prove themselves worthy of the Spartan polity through participation in the
      <i>
       Krypteia
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Every autumn, according to
      <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
       Plutarch
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Life of Lycurgus
      </i>
      , 28, 37), the Spartan
      <a href="/wiki/Ephor" title="Ephor">
       ephors
      </a>
      would
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pro_forma" title="Pro forma">
        pro forma
       </a>
      </i>
      declare war on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helot" title="Helot">
       helot
      </a>
      population so that any Spartan citizen could kill a helot without fear of punishment. At night, the chosen
      <i>
       kryptes
      </i>
      (members of the Krypteia) were sent out into the
      <a href="/wiki/Laconia" title="Laconia">
       Laconian
      </a>
      countryside armed with knives with the instructions to kill any helot they encountered and to take any food they needed. They were specifically told to kill the strongest and best of the helots. This practice was instigated to prevent the threat of a rebellion by the helots and to keep their population in check.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Cartledge, Krypteia members stalked the helot villages and surrounding countryside, spying on the servile population. Their mission was to prevent and suppress unrest and rebellion. Troublesome helots could be summarily executed. Such brutal repression of the helots permitted the Spartan lite to successfully control the servile agrarian population and devote themselves to military practice. It may also have contributed to the Spartans' reputation for stealth since a
      <i>
       kryptes
      </i>
      who got caught was punished by whipping.
     </p>
     <p>
      Only Spartans who had served in the Krypteia as young men could expect to achieve the highest ranks in Spartan society and army. It was felt that only those Spartans who showed the willingness and ability to kill for the state at a young age were worthy to join the leadership in later years.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
       Plato
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_(Plato)" title="Laws (Plato)">
        Laws
       </a>
      </i>
      , I, 633), a scholiast to Plato, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heraclides_Lembos" title="Heraclides Lembos">
       Heraclides Lembos
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Fr. Hist. Gr.
      </i>
      , II, 210) also describe the krypteia.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Krypteia_on_the_battlefield">
       Krypteia on the battlefield
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crypteia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Krypteia on the battlefield">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In his
      <i>
       Cleomenes
      </i>
      , Plutarch describes Krypteia as being a unit of the Spartan army; during the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sellasia" title="Battle of Sellasia">
       battle of Sellasia
      </a>
      , the Spartan king Cleomenes "called Damoteles, the commander of the Krypteia, and ordered him to observe and find out how matters stood in the rear and on the flanks of his army".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Various scholars have speculated on the presence and function of the Krypteia on the battlefield, describing it as a reconnaissance,
      <a href="/wiki/Special_operations" title="Special operations">
       special operations
      </a>
      , or even
      <a href="/wiki/Military_police" title="Military police">
       military police
      </a>
      force.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Krypteia_as_rite_of_passage">
       Krypteia as rite of passage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crypteia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Krypteia as rite of passage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Jeanmaire points out that the
      <a href="/wiki/Bushranger" title="Bushranger">
       bushranger
      </a>
      life of the Krypteia has no common point with the disciplined and well-ordered communal life (see
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Homonia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Homonia (page does not exist)">
       Homonia
      </a>
      ) of the Spartan
      <a href="/wiki/Hoplite" title="Hoplite">
       hoplite
      </a>
      ; but as it is only a short part in a very long and thorough training, this could precisely fit an additional skill useful when separated from one's unit. Jeanmaire suggests that the krypteia was a
      <a href="/wiki/Rite_of_passage" title="Rite of passage">
       rite of passage
      </a>
      , possibly pre-dating the classical military organisation, and may have been preserved through Sparta's legendary religious conservatism. He draws comparison with the
      <a href="/wiki/Initiation" title="Initiation">
       initiation
      </a>
      rituals of some African secret societies (wolf-men and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopard_men" title="Leopard men">
       leopard men
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
