       <h1>
        Growing Carrots
       </h1>
       <hr class="solid"/>
       <p>
        Providing you keep to a few simple rules, growing carrots is fairly 
            easy. They are one of the root vegetables which nearly every kid will 
            like eating and hence be keen to plant, and digging them up is even 
            more fun than sowing them!
        <img align="right" alt="growing carrots clipart" height="87" hspace="10" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/carrots_growing_clipart.jpg" title="growing carrots clipart" vspace="10" width="100"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        While we tend to think of carrots as always being orange, there are actually 
            white,
        <strong>
         <font color="#990000">
          red
         </font>
        </strong>
        ,
        <strong>
         <font color="#FFCC00">
          yellow
         </font>
        </strong>
        and
        <strong>
         <font color="#330066">
          purple
         </font>
        </strong>
        carrots too! 
            Imagine a snowman with a purple nose ...
       </p>

       <h3 class="hbg">
        <a name="facts">
        </a>
        Some Basic Facts about Growing Carrots
       </h3>
       <p>
        Carrots varieties include short, medium and long-rooted types.
       </p>
       <p>
        The
        <em>
         <strong>
          short-rooted
         </strong>
        </em>
        <strong>
         carrots
        </strong>
        can 
            be suitable for growing in containers or deep window boxes and even 
            include small round ball-like types. They are the earliest to be sown 
            and the quickest to mature. Small, quick and tasty! Good varieties 
            to go for are the
        <a href="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/carrot_parmex" onclick="return FIX.track(this);" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         Parmex
        </a>
        (a round type) or the
        <a href="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/carrot_ideal" onclick="return FIX.track(this);" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         Ideal 
            Speedy Seeds
        </a>
        (a type of Nantes which is used as a baby vegetable 
            and ideal for growing in containers on patios).
       </p>
       <p>
        The
        <em>
         <strong>
          medium-rooted
         </strong>
        </em>
        <strong>
         types
        </strong>
        are 
            sown slightly later than the short-rooted carrots and are usually 
            the best bet for harvesting a successful crop.
       </p>
       <p>
        The
        <em>
         <strong>
          long-rooted
         </strong>
        </em>
        <strong>
         varieties
        </strong>
        take the longest to mature and are the most fussy about the quality 
            of the ground. Fine for showing off, but not the sweetest taste.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Carrots like:
        </strong>
        <strong>
         <img align="right" alt="happy carrot" height="120" hspace="10" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/carrot_happy.jpg" title="happy carrot" width="70"/>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li class="custom">
         cool, wet weather, and can be sown before the last frost has passed
        </li>
        <li class="custom">
         lots of sunshine - choose a sunny spot but keep them well watered
        </li>
        <li class="custom">
         fertile, sandy and well-draining soil, without loads of stones in 
          it,
        </li>
        <li class="custom">
         onions, chives, radishes, sage and rosemary.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Carrots dislike:
         <img align="right" alt="sad carrot" height="112" hspace="20" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/carrot_sad.jpg" title="sad carrot" width="70"/>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li class="custom">
         long, hot, dry spells which bakes the ground hard
        </li>
        <li class="custom">
         soil which is heavy, consists largely of clay or is full of stones
        </li>
        <li class="custom">
         ground that has been prepared with manure or compost
        </li>
        <li class="custom">
         too much nitrogen - it spoils their taste
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h3 class="hbg">
        <a name="companion">
        </a>
        Growing Carrots with Companion Plants
       </h3>
       <p>
        The main enemy of the carrot is the carrot fly, which is attracted to 
        the wonderful smell given off by the foliage. Onion, leeks and black salsify 
        help to repel carrot fly, as do the herbs rosemary, wormwood and sage.
       </p>
       <p>
        Carrots also grow well with tomatoes, chives,
        <a href="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/growing_lettuce.html" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">
         lettuce
        </a>
        , 
            and
        <a href="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/growing_radishes.html" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">
         radishes
        </a>
        - the latter is often 
            sown together in a row with carrots, as it germinates and grows quicker 
            and therefore indicates where the carrots are growing!
       </p>
       <h3 class="hbg">
        <a name="how_to">
        </a>
        How to Grow Carrots
       </h3>
       <p>
        Carrots can be sown outside in situ from around the middle of March 
            until the end of June or July. They
        <strong>
         germinate in 2 to 3 weeks
        </strong>
        , and take between 2 to 4 months to mature fully, depending on the 
            variety chosen. Chose a sunny spot. Check your seed packet for more 
            detailed information on that varieties prefered sowing time.
       </p>
       <p>
        Before sowing the carrots, ensure the ground has been well dug over 
            and is free of weeds and stones. If the ground is a bit hard or clayey, 
            dig in some sand or peat to lighten the soil up, but
        <strong>
         not fresh 
            compost or manure
        </strong>
        . In fact, carrots should not be grown on 
            a patch which has been manured during the previous year.
       </p>
       <table align="center" border="0" cellpadding="5" width="90%">
        <tr valign="top">
         <td>
          <img alt="preparing the row growing carrots" height="266" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/growing_carrots_1.jpg" title="preparing the row growing carrots" width="200"/>
         </td>
         <td>
          <p>
           Prepare the ground as described above, then, a couple of weeks 
              before sowing, rake some general fertilizer into the ground.
          </p>
          <p>
           Go over the plot with a rake until you have a fine tilth (die the 
              soil is in small grains not large clumps!).
          </p>
          <p>
           Use your
           <a href="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/markers_for_vegetable_rows.html" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">
            row markers
           </a>
           and some twine to mark out the line along which you are growing 
                  carrots, draw away about 1/2 inch of soild along the line with 
                  a garden rake or hoe, and
           <strong>
            water generously
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
         </td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
         <td>
          <img alt="carrot seeds" height="150" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/growing_carrots_2.jpg" title="carrot seeds" width="200"/>
         </td>
         <td>
          <p>
           Carrot seeds are tiny, and it is easy to sow too many and be 
              left with a large number to thin out later on. One way of avoiding 
              this is to mix the seed with sand before sowing.
          </p>
          <p>
           Alternatively use a carrot tape (eg
           <a href="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/groweasy_seed_tapes" onclick="return FIX.track(this);" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            Suttons 
                  Groweasy Seed Tapes
           </a>
           ), which spaces the seeds out for you 
                  and is quick and easy to put down.
          </p>
         </td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
         <td>
          <img alt="sowing carrots" height="266" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/growing_carrots_4.jpg" title="sowing carrots" width="200"/>
         </td>
         <td>
          <p>
           Carefully sprinkle the seeds along the line, trying to drop one 
              seed approximately every 1 1/2 to 2 inches.
          </p>
          <p>
           Once the seedlings are large enough to handle they can be thinned 
              out. But don't thin them out too much, as the denser the foliage 
              above them, the less likely weeds are to grow underneath and the 
              less weeding you need to do!
          </p>
          <p>
           Also try to avoid damaging the foliage when thinning out, as the 
              smell attracts carrot fly.
          </p>
         </td>
        </tr>
        <tr valign="top">
         <td>
          <img alt="covering carrot seeds" height="150" src="http://www.kiddiegardens.com/image-files/growing_carrots_5.jpg" title="covering carrot seeds" width="200"/>
         </td>
         <td>
          <p>
           Very gently cover the seeds with a tiny bit of soil. The seeds 
              barely need 1/2 inch of soil above them to germinate, and the most 
              frequent reason for a crop to fail to emerge is the seeds being 
              planted too deeply.
          </p>
          <p>
           Water generously.
          </p>
         </td>
        </tr>
       </table>
       <h3 class="hbg">
        <a name="tips">
        </a>
        Tips
       </h3>
       <p>
        If growing carrots in at least two
        <strong>
         adjacent rows
        </strong>
        , 
            leave a gap of about 6 inches between the rows.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Keep the carrots well watered
        </strong>
        during long, dry spells. 
            Carrot seedlings are quite weak, so keeping the ground damp while 
            they germinate and start to pop their heads through the ground is 
            a must. Radishes planted with them will also help by loosening the 
            ground.
       </p>
       <p>
        Try to grow some of the
        <strong>
         companion plants
        </strong>
        mentioned 
            above next to or at least near the carrots to help avoid them being 
            destroyed by the carrot fly.
       </p>
       <p>
        When the time comes to dig the carrots up, use a
        <strong>
         garden fork
        </strong>
        to loosen the ground around the carrots before pulling.
       </p>
