        <p>
         A massive cloud or plume has been spotted on Mars, and scientists aren't sure what it is or how it got there.
        </p>
        <p>
         The plume was first spotted by amateur astronomers in March 2012. Wayne Jaeschke, a patent attorney and member of the
         <a href="http://alpo-astronomy.org/" target="_hplink">
          Association of Lunar and Planetary Observers
         </a>
         , said the formation was so unusual he thought something was wrong with his camera.
        </p>
        <p>
         "I sent a couple of frames to some guys I know in Australia and asked, '
         <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2015/02/150216-mars-martian-plumes-astronomy-clouds-maven-mystery/" target="_hplink">
          Am I seeing things
         </a>
         ?'" Jaeschke said, according to National Geographic.
        </p>
        <p>
         There was nothing wrong with Jaeschke's camera or his eyesight because soon, other amateur Mars-watchers also spotted the formation and saw it grow and change shape for about 10 days. What started out as
         <a href="http://www.nature.com/articles/nature14162.epdf?referrer_access_token=Xy9CqxWNMT8BMacgBX9LotRgN0jAjWel9jnR3ZoTv0PQU3zoghsPsRCu5hOdcH3CHlh3R3ocEyvNcmGW_trVE7TqcdK86tns3LHmw_gAKAfuMwJzZ5sPsIjKyeHx4OEo" target="_hplink">
          "double blob protrusions" transformed into a "finger" pointing up into space
         </a>
         , according to a study on the formations published in Nature.
        </p>
        <p>
         Weeks later, a second plume appeared. Like the first, it formed on the planet's "terminator," or the line that divides night from day.
        </p>
        <p>
         These plumes reached an altitude of 125 to 155 miles, or twice as high as any other plume ever seen on the planet.
        </p>
        <p>
         At about 250 km (155 miles), the division between the atmosphere and outer space is very thin,
         <a href="http://www.esa.int/Our_Activities/Space_Science/Mystery_Mars_plume_baffles_scientists" target="_hplink">
          so the reported plumes are extremely unexpected
         </a>
         , Agustin Sanchez-Lavega of the Universidad del Pas Vasco in Spain, lead author of the paper, told the European Space Agency.
        </p>
        <p>
         Sanchez-Lavega's team came up with two possible explanations, although they don't appear to be particularly happy with either. One is that the plume is a reflective cloud made of particles of water-ice, carbon-dioxide ice or dust. The other is that the cloud is something like an aurora seen here on Earth -- although to be seen from here, it would have to be 1,000 times brighter than any earthly aurora.
        </p>
        <p>
         The researchers admit that these theories may be flawed.
        </p>
        <p>
         "
