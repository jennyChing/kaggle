     <h1>
      Top Ten Cyber Crime Skills in High Demand
     </h1>
     <div class="viewdate">
      Tuesday, May 10, 2011
     </div>
     <div class="viewavatar">
      <label>
       Contributed By:
      </label>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       <a href="/userprofile/2907.html">
        Headlines
       </a>
      </strong>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      <a href="/userprofile/2907.html">
       <img alt="69dafe8b58066478aea48f3d0f384820" src="/images/../uploads/avatar//69dafe8b58066478aea48f3d0f384820.jpg"/>
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="post_text">
      <p>
       With trillions of dollars in electronic funds available for the taking, cyber crime has become big business.
      </p>
      <p>
       Like any other rapidly expanding business sector, particular expertise and skills are increasingly in demand, presenting exceptional career opportunities for those with less than noble character dispositions.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        At the recent FOSE conference - a government IT trade show -
       </span>
       deputy assistant director in the FBI's cyber division Steven Chabinsky identified the top ten skill sets for the blossoming cyber crime industry.
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        "The cyber underground now consist of subject matter experts that can focus all their time and energy on improving their techniques, their goods and services,"
       </em>
       said Chabinsky.
      </p>
      <p>
       Expertise in high demand include:
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <strong>
         1. Coders/programmers,
        </strong>
        who write the exploits and malware used by the criminal enterprise. Contrary to popular belief, Chabinsky noted that coders who knowingly take part in a criminal enterprise are not protected by the First Amendment.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <strong>
         2. Distributors,
        </strong>
        who trade and sell stolen data and act as vouchers for the goods provided by other specialists.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <strong>
         3. Tech experts,
        </strong>
        who maintain the criminal enterprise's IT infrastructure, including servers, encryption technologies, databases, and the like.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <strong>
         4. Hackers,
        </strong>
        who search for and exploit applications, systems and network vulnerabilities.
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <span>
         <strong>
          5. Fraudsters,
         </strong>
         who create and deploy various social engineering schemes, such as phishing and spam.
        </span>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <span>
         <strong>
          6. Hosted systems providers,
         </strong>
         who offer safe hosting of illicit content servers and sites.
         <br/>
        </span>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <span>
         <strong>
          7. Cashiers,
         </strong>
         who control drop accounts and provide names and accounts to other criminals for a fee.
         <br/>
        </span>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <span>
         <strong>
          8. Money mules,
         </strong>
         who complete wire transfers between bank accounts. The money mules may use student and work visas to travel to the U.S. to open bank accounts.
         <br/>
        </span>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <span>
         <strong>
          9. Tellers,
         </strong>
         who are charged with transferring and laundering illicitly gained proceeds through digital currency services and different world currencies.
         <br/>
        </span>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <span>
         1
         <strong>
          0. Organization Leaders,
         </strong>
         often "people persons" without technical skills. The leaders assemble the team and choose the targets.
        </span>
       </em>
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        Source:
        <a href="http://www.amarjit.info/2010/03/top-10-posts-in-cybercriminal.html" target="_blank">
         http://www.amarjit.info/2010/03/top-10-posts-in-cybercriminal.html
        </a>
       </em>
      </p>
