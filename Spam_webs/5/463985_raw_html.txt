          <h1>
           Why Marvel Studios Succeeds (And How It Will Fail If It Doesnt Diversify)
          </h1>
          <div class="post_meta">
           <span class="the_author">
            <span>
             by
            </span>
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/author/andrew-wheeler/" title="Andrew Wheeler">
             Andrew Wheeler
            </a>
           </span>
           <span class="the_date">
            August 6, 2014 10:00 AM
           </span>
          </div>
          <div class="social-group-top large">
           <div class="social_icons size_large">
            <a class="facebook share_link" href="http://www.facebook.com/sharer.php?u=http%3A%2F%2Fcomicsalliance.com%2Fmarvel-studios-success-marvel-movies-diversity%2F%3Ftrackback%3Dfbshare_top&amp;t=Why+Marvel+Studios+Succeeds+%28And+How+It+Will+Fail+If+It+Doesn%26%238217%3Bt+Diversify%29" target="_blank" title="Share on Facebook">
             <div class="social_icon_large">
             </div>
             <span class="social_icon">
              Share on Facebook
             </span>
            </a>
            <a class="twitter share_link" href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text=Check+out+Why+Marvel+Studios+Succeeds+%28And+How+It+Will+Fail+If+It+Doesn%E2%80%99t+Diversify%29+http%3A%2F%2Fcomicsalliance.com%2Fmarvel-studios-success-marvel-movies-diversity%2F%3Ftrackback%3Dtwitter_top+%40comicsalliance" target="_blank" title="Share on Twitter">
             <div class="social_icon_large">
             </div>
             <span class="social_icon">
              Share on Twitter
             </span>
            </a>
            <span class="social-submenu">
            </span>
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="the_content clearfix">
           <p>
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-95537" height="420" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/mcu-hed.jpg" title="mcu-hed" width="630"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/tags/guardians-of-the-galaxy/">
             <em>
              Guardians Of The Galaxy
             </em>
            </a>
            just enjoyed a very successful weekend at movie theaters, taking home around $94m, far in excess of expectations. The movie also stands at 92% positive reviews on aggregator site Rotten Tomatoes, joining all previous
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/tags/marvel-studios/">
             <strong>
              Marvel Studios
             </strong>
            </a>
            movies in receiving predominantly favorable notices.
           </p>
           <p>
            Marvel Studios is doing very well. In six years and ten movies, it has avoided both critical and commercial disasters, and frustrated naysayers who hailed the demise of the superhero movie at every step. Marvels rivals at
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/tags/fox/">
             Fox
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/tags/sony/">
             Sony/Columbia
            </a>
            , and
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/tags/warner-bros/">
             Warner Bros
            </a>
            , have enjoyed commercial success as well  but not with the acclaim, consistency, or proliferation of Marvel.
            <strong>
             So how does Marvel do it, and can they keep doing it?
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            I think the secret to Marvels success owes much to necessity. Marvel simply cant afford to screw up the superhero movie business, because its the only business the studio is in. That sounds like a circular argument  it succeeds because it cant fail  but I think Marvels singular focus forces it to be smart, ambitious, and innovative in ways that its rivals are slow to understand. Marvel Studios succeeds because it goes all-in.
           </p>
           <p>
            Look at Marvels competitors and it becomes clear that superhero movies are not their sole priority. Fox has its
            <em>
             Fantastic Four
            </em>
            reboot and its
            <em>
             X-Men
            </em>
            franchise, but it makes plenty of other movies, including other franchises 
            <em>
             Assassins Creed
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             Ice Age
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             Planet Of The Apes
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             Taken
            </em>
            , etc. Sony/Columbia wants to build a universe around
            <em>
             Spider-Man
            </em>
            with
            <em>
             Sinister Six
            </em>
            , V
            <em>
             enom
            </em>
            , and perhaps
            <em>
             Black Cat
            </em>
            or
            <em>
             Spider-Woman
            </em>
            , but it also has the James Bond franchise,
            <em>
             Jump Street
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             The Smurfs
            </em>
            . DCs parent company Warner Bros. is a beast, and it didnt just make the
            <em>
             Dark Knight
            </em>
            movies; it also had
            <em>
             Harry Potter
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             The Hobbit
            </em>
            ,
            <em>
             The Hangover
            </em>
            , and much more, including the new
            <em>
             Godzilla
            </em>
            . There are people at Warner Bros. who
            <em>
             never
            </em>
            have to think about Batman.
           </p>
           <p>
            There is no-one at Marvel Studios who doesnt have to think about Captain America.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter wp-image-95317" height="337" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/captainamerica.jpg" title="captainamerica" width="504"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Marvel Studios, of course, is not a studio on the scale of Warner Bros. or Fox. Its a subsidiary of Disney, which also makes Disney-branded movies like
            <em>
             Frozen
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             Tangled
            </em>
            , Pixar movies, Muppet movies,
            <em>
             Pirates of the Caribbean
            </em>
            movies, and now
            <em>
             Star Wars
            </em>
            movies. But Marvel Studios appears to be  and by all accounts is  largely autonomous. It operates its own brand, and it puts out two movies a year under that brand  moving up to three in 2017. All of those movies are essentially one interconnected super-franchise.
           </p>
           <p>
            That makes Marvel very unusual. Liam Neesons character in
            <em>
             Taken
            </em>
            does not have to worry about his daughter being kidnapped by Caesar from
            <em>
             Planet Of The Apes
            </em>
            . James Bond is not going to take down Gargamel in the next
            <em>
             Smurfs
            </em>
            movie. But
            <em>
             every
            </em>
            Marvel movie has implications for the others, in-universe and out. Any stumble or sign of weakness has ramifications across the line. If the superhero movie renaissance were to fail apocalyptically, Marvel Studios would shutter its doors, because even if it makes a space movie or a magic movie, theyre all part of the same superhero franchise. Marvel doesnt have anything else. Even its horror comics are superhero comics. Even Patsy Walker is Hellcat.
           </p>
           <p>
            Marvel needs its superhero movies to succeed, and to keep succeeding. The studio cannot afford to go fallow. It cannot afford to take its heroes off the screen for a few years while the audience recovers its interest. It cannot afford to burn its audience out.
           </p>
           <p>
            For that reason, the studio maintains rigorous standards that seem designed to please as wide an audience as possible. Marvel strives to satisfy its core fan base while also entertaining general movie-goers. It doesnt pander to one section of the audience  for example, young white men, or Americans  in ways that alienate other sections of the audience  like women, or overseas viewers. Marvel tries to stay fresh by mixing superheroes with other flavors  fantasy, espionage, space opera. The studio created a franchise that is just episodic enough to build anticipation, build audience, and build brand loyalty between movies, but also sliced up into sub-franchises that wont overwhelm audiences or leave anyone behind. These strategies have helped Marvel establish a trustworthy brand and lure increasing numbers to take a chance on its pictures.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter wp-image-95319" height="336" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/gotg21.jpeg" title="gotg2" width="504"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Moving from two movies a year to three is going to be a big risk for the studio, because it brings the risk of wearing the audience down  but the expansion is both natural growth and a consequence of Marvels success.
           </p>
           <p>
            This is where success can be dangerous. Marvel is very tied to its existing franchises, and I dont think it expected to create as many successes as it has. Remember that
            <em>
             Iron Man
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             The Incredible Hulk
            </em>
            came out the same year. Even then, Marvel was laying the groundwork for its shared universe and its ambitious Avengers plans (in a way that the studio could easily walk away from if it all went south).
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             Iron Man
            </em>
            did very well and became a franchise.
            <em>
             Incredible Hulk
            </em>
            did less well, and the franchise stalled at one movie. (The earlier Ang Lee
            <em>
             Hulk
            </em>
            was not a Marvel Studios picture.) Both characters ended up in
            <em>
             Avengers
            </em>
            anyway, but Hulk/Bruce Banner was recast.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its easy to imagine that if
            <em>
             Thor
            </em>
            or
            <em>
             Captain America
            </em>
            had not met expectations, they too would have stalled at one movie, even if they both ended up in
            <em>
             Avengers
            </em>
            . This is how Marvel adapts to failure  or even under-performance. Failure is mixed back into the pot. Success is nurtured and rewarded. It all looks organic and unforced. So,
            <em>
             Captain America: The Winter Soldier
            </em>
            was a critical and commercial hit, and its sequel is scheduled for 2016.
            <em>
             Thor: The Dark World
            </em>
            didnt jump as high or earn as much acclaim, so its back-burnered. It probably will get a sequel, but its not on the calendar yet  not the public one, anyway.
           </p>
           <p>
            I dont think Marvel was counting on
            <em>
             Iron Man
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             Thor
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             Captain America
            </em>
            all being as successful as they were. I think Marvel had contingencies in place in case any of them failed.
           </p>
           <p>
            Similarly, I imagine this years
            <em>
             Guardians of the Galaxy
            </em>
            , next years
            <em>
             Ant-Man
            </em>
            , and the year afters
            <em>
             Doctor Strange
            </em>
            , are all properties that could be their own three-movie franchises  but they could just as easily stop at one movie apiece. Marvel already greenlit a sequel for
            <em>
             Guardians
            </em>
            based on advance buzz; it could have nudged that sequel off the schedule if the movie had bombed.
           </p>
           <p>
            Now imagine if Marvel still had the rights to
            <em>
             Spider-Man
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             X-Men
            </em>
            . It seems unlikely that wed be watching a
            <em>
             Guardians
            </em>
            movie in 2014. Of the ten movies Marvel has released, three would have been
            <em>
             Spider-Man
            </em>
            , and at least two would have been
            <em>
             X-Men
            </em>
            . Maybe wed have got to one
            <em>
             Hulk
            </em>
            and two
            <em>
             Iron Mans
            </em>
            , maybe a
            <em>
             Captain America
            </em>
            and a
            <em>
             Fantastic Four
            </em>
            .
            <em>
             Guardians Of The Galaxy
            </em>
            wouldnt even be on the maybe list.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter wp-image-95315" height="336" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/lost-marvel.jpg" title="lost-marvel" width="504"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Not having those brands is part of what forced Marvel to take risks  and really,
            <em>
             all
            </em>
            of their movies are bigger risks than
            <em>
             Spider-Man
            </em>
            or
            <em>
             X-Men
            </em>
            . Thats why those movies got made first  just at other studios.
           </p>
           <p>
            Not having the big names compelled Marvel to created a screenwriting hothouse to develop script treatments for lesser known properties, and
            <em>
             Guardians Of The Galaxy
            </em>
            emerged from that project. It might as easily have been
            <em>
             Black Panther
            </em>
            or
            <em>
             Captain Marvel
            </em>
            , or, who knows,
            <em>
             Power Pack
            </em>
            , or
            <em>
             Sleepwalker
            </em>
            , or
            <em>
             Darkhold: Pages From The Book Of Sin
            </em>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            This is the weird contradiction of Marvel Studios; it is both controlling and risk-taking. It cant play safe like Sony, Fox, and Warner Bros, because it doesnt have their more iconic licenses  although the nature of those icons is being redefined in real time by Marvel Studios success. But Spider-Man is Spider-Man and Rocket Raccoon is Rocket Raccoon, and that means Marvel cannot settle into the comfortable familiarity of making each movie a faded copy of the last success, whether in sequel, spin-off, or reboot format. It cant cavalierly disregard the source material and risk alienating the only people who already care about its weird fringes. It cant stick to a single grim or adolescent tone that predominantly appeals to one (big) audience segment. Those are conservative instincts that come from not wanting to ruin a sure thing like
            <em>
             Spider-Man
            </em>
            . Marvel cant be conservative if it wants to make audiences love Star-Lord the way they love Spidey.
           </p>
           <p>
            But
            <em>
             Guardians
            </em>
            is Marvels first movie with an all-new cast since 2011, and Marvel needs to establish new franchises like this as a precaution against old franchises failing. Thus far, Marvel sequels always outperform their predecessors worldwide. At some point diminishing returns must set in, and Marvel wants to be able to isolate any rot so that other branches can flourish, rather than let the whole tree  their whole business  die.
           </p>
           <p>
            The problem is that the success of existing franchises doesnt leave a lot of room in Marvels schedule for new franchises, even when the studio movies up to three pictures a year. Heres what the schedule looks like:
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             May 1st 2015:
             <strong>
              Avengers: Age of Ultron
             </strong>
             <br/>
             July 17th 2015:
             <strong>
              Ant-Man
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             May 6th, 2016:
             <strong>
              Captain America 3
             </strong>
             <br/>
             July 8th, 2016: Doctor Strange (unconfirmed)
            </p>
            <p>
             May 5th, 2017: Unknown, possibly Thor 3
             <br/>
             July 28th, 2017:
             <strong>
              Guardians of the Galaxy 2
             </strong>
             <br/>
             November 3rd 2017: Unknown
            </p>
            <p>
             May 4th, 2018: Unknown
             <br/>
             July 6th, 2018: Unknown
             <br/>
             November 2nd, 2018: Unknown
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            Counting
            <em>
             Avengers
            </em>
            as its own franchise, Marvel launched five franchises with its first ten movies, and only the
            <em>
             Iron Man
            </em>
            franchise isnt certain of another sequel. If
            <em>
             Ant-Man
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             Doctor Strange
            </em>
            repeat the success of Marvels previous properties, those might be the only franchises it launches among the next ten movies. One could well imagine a calendar that looks like this:
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             May 1st 2015: Avengers: Age of Ultron
             <br/>
             July 17th 2015: Ant-Man
            </p>
            <p>
             May 6th, 2016: Captain America 3
             <br/>
             July 8th, 2016:
             <strong>
              Doctor Strange
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             May 5th, 2017:
             <strong>
              Thor 3
             </strong>
             <br/>
             July 28th, 2017: Guardians of the Galaxy 2
             <br/>
             November 3rd 2017:
             <strong>
              Iron Man 4
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             May 4th, 2018:
             <strong>
              Avengers 3
             </strong>
             <br/>
             July 6th, 2018:
             <strong>
              Ant-Man 2
             </strong>
             <br/>
             November 2nd, 2018:
             <strong>
              Doctor Strange 2
             </strong>
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            Would this be a problem? Yes, if the old franchises start to wilt and Marvel doesnt have any new franchises set up to take their place. And the risk of old franchises wilting seems exacerbated by rapid changes in audience profiles and audience appetites.
           </p>
           <p>
            African-Americans and Asian Americans are
            <a href="http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/cinemacon-african-american-moviegoers-increase-691170">
             more enthusiastic moviegoers
            </a>
            than white American audiences. Hispanic audiences account for
            <a href="http://www.thewrap.com/how-hispanics-became-hollywoods-most-important-audience/">
             20% or more of opening weekend ticket sales
            </a>
            for blockbuster movies in the U.S. Women account for
            <a href="http://filmschoolrejects.com/features/film-movie-audience.php">
             52% of the moviegoing audience
            </a>
            , and major releases with female leads keep upsetting expectations  like Scarlet Johanssens
            <em>
             Lucy
            </em>
            , which smashed Dwayne Johnsons
            <em>
             Hercules
            </em>
            at the box office. On top of all that, the international audience for movies is more important than ever before, with
            <a href='http://www.bbc.com/culture/story/20130620-is-china-hollywoods-future"'>
             almost 70% of studio revenues
            </a>
            <a href="http://www.thewrap.com/how-hispanics-became-hollywoods-most-important-audience/">
             coming from foreign markets.
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            Disney is clearly switched on about all of this, and its easy to see that understanding reflected in the decision to cast black actors John Boyega and Lupita Nyongo in key roles in the next
            <em>
             Star Wars
            </em>
            movie, and Gwendoline Christie in a role reportedly written for a man. That all goes some way to addressing the sci-fi franchises historic shortage of women and people of color. Its also evident in the decision to adapt Marvels
            <em>
             Big Hero 6
            </em>
            with a diverse cast  though sadly at the expense of several Asian characters. Its notable that Disney Animation, rather than Marvel Studios, saw the potential in that property, and the feature carries no Marvel branding whatsoever.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-95316" height="420" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/bighero-gogo.jpg" title="bighero-gogo" width="630"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            This appreciation for a changing audience isnt limited to movies. One only need look at another Disney subsidiary to see the importance Disney attaches to female and non-white audiences.
           </p>
           <p>
            Disneys ABC television network is launching three sitcoms this fall with minority casts, as well as the first sitcom to feature an Asian American as the romantic lead (
            <em>
             Selfie
            </em>
            ). ABC has given a whole night of primetime programming to a black woman, writer/producer Shonda Rimes, with two of her three hours led by black female leads. Dramas with female leads have done very well for the network  with
            <em>
             Once Upon A Time
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             Scandal
            </em>
            as standout examples  and all the successful dramas launched by ABC in the last couple of years have featured black leading characters except
            <em>
             Nashville
             <em>
              and Marvels
              <em>
               Agents of SHIELD
               <em>
                .
               </em>
              </em>
             </em>
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
            Even ABC Family, once known as an evangelical Christian network, has weirdly become home to some of the most inclusive shows on television, like the queer-friendly
            <em>
             Pretty Little Liars
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             The Fosters
            </em>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            Come to that, another Disney-owned company has made impressive strides with diversity in just the past year, launching its highest ever number of series with female leads and people of color, and integrating significant LGBT content into its output. That company is of course Marvel. Marvel Comics, that is; not Marvel Studios.
           </p>
           <p>
            I dont know if all of this is the result of a Disney-wide initiative to improve diversity within its output, or if its simply writers and commissioning editors taking advantage of the fact that the changing market means less resistance at the top. I suspect its a little of both. Whats clear is that Marvel Studios has not taken advantage of that change with the same rapidity. It hasnt anticipated that change, and it hasnt left itself in a position to quickly adapt to that change.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter wp-image-95312" height="336" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/black-widow.jpg" title="black-widow" width="504"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Marvel Studios
            <em>
             has
            </em>
            made strides in acknowledging diversity, putting female heroes and heroes of color on the screen in productions that didnt require their presence. Sif, Black Widow, Heimdall, War Machine, Falcon, Gamora, Peggy Carter; these are all great characters. Marvel Studios own television division is giving a spotlight to Peggy Carter, Luke Cage and Jessica Jones. Rumors abound that Captain Marvel will cameo in
            <em>
             Avengers: Age of Ultron
            </em>
            (unfortunately under the Ms. Marvel moniker, and possibly
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/katee-sackhoff-ms-captain-marvel-avengers-tweets/">
             <em>
              not
             </em>
             played by Katee Sackhoff
            </a>
            ).
           </p>
           <p>
            But none of these characters has their own movie, and looking at the upcoming schedule, its easy to imagine that we wont see a movie with a female or non-white lead until 2019. The earliest available slot is three years away.
           </p>
           <p>
            If Marvel wants to be in a position to take advantage of changing audience appetites today, it should have announced a
            <em>
             Black Widow
            </em>
            movie at San Diego Comic-Con two years ago, when it announced
            <em>
             Guardians Of The Galaxy
            </em>
            . Marvel didnt even announce that movie
            <em>
             this
            </em>
            year, or a
            <em>
             Captain Marvel
            </em>
            movie, or a
            <em>
             Black Panther
            </em>
            movie. Instead it announced
            <em>
             Guardians Of The Galaxy 2
            </em>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            A studio that once looked bold and risk-taking is now in danger of looking conservative and cautious, weighed down by its own success  just like Warner Bros., Fox, and Sony. Except, of course, Sony is
            <a href="http://comicsalliance.com/report-sony-planning-a-woman-led-superhero-movie-in-the-spider-man-franchise/">
             reportedly planning a superhero movie with a female lead
            </a>
            for 2017. Even if Marvel now does likewise, its going to look like its following Sonys lead.
           </p>
           <p>
            On the subject of a movie with a female lead, Marvel Studios president Kevin Feige recently said to
            <a href="http://www.comicbookresources.com/?page=article&amp;id=54522">
             CBR
            </a>
            ; I hope we do it sooner rather than later. He is the
            <em>
             president of Marvel Studios
            </em>
            . He doesnt have to hope that they do it.
            <em>
             He has to do it.
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <img alt="" class="aligncenter wp-image-95313" height="336" src="http://wac.450F.edgecastcdn.net/80450F/comicsalliance.com/files/2014/08/chris-mass.jpg" title="chris-mass" width="504"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            As for a movie with a non-white lead; if Marvel makes
            <em>
             Thor 3
            </em>
            before it makes Black Panther, it will have made
            <em>
             ten
            </em>
            movies headlined by blond white men named Chris before it makes
            <em>
             one
            </em>
            movie headlined by someone who isnt even white. (They can cast a black actor named Chris. Thats totally OK.)
           </p>
           <p>
            They could of course circumvent that is if they cast a non-white actor as Doctor Strange, but no-one really expects that to happen  and therein lies the problem. Marvel now looks like its lagging behind audience expectations. Thats bad for a brand that built itself on giving audiences what they want. I dont think the female Thor or the black Captain America in the comics are part of the solution either. Weve been assured those ideas came from the comics writers. The movies can follow suit, but these story initiatives arent part of a stated Marvel Studios agenda.
           </p>
           <p>
            I do think that heroes like Captain Marvel and Black Panther are part of Marvel Studios agenda  and Im sure several factors conspired to keep those characters from our screens. Marvels unprecedented success is chief among them. Its plausible that
            <em>
             Black Panther
            </em>
            or
            <em>
             Captain Marvel
            </em>
            is the very next name on Marvels to do list.
           </p>
           <p>
            But maybe we have to wait for another franchise to fail, or for another slot to open up in 2019 or beyond, before Marvel gets that far down the list. And that simply isnt good enough. Audiences want to see these heroes now, and if Marvel wont provide them, maybe audiences will turn to Sony, or Fox, or Warner Bros, for the next phase of innovative superhero movie-making.
           </p>
           <p>
            The secret of Marvels success is that it took risks in order to grow. The danger of Marvels success is that it may stop taking risks just when it really needs to.
           </p>
           <p>
