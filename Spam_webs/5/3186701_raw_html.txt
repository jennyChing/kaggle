     <p>
      A
      <b>
       Trench knife
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Combat_knife" title="Combat knife">
       combat knife
      </a>
      designed to kill or gravely incapacitate an enemy soldier at close quarters, as might be encountered in a trenchline or other confined area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PET_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PET-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAT_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAT-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was developed in response to a need for a close combat weapon for soldiers conducting assaults and raids on enemy trenchlines during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">
       First World War
      </a>
      . An example of a World War I trench knife is the German Army's
      <i>
       Nahkampfmesser
      </i>
      (close combat knife).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUL_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BUL-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MIL_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MIL-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      With the outbreak of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
       Second World War
      </a>
      , the trench knife, by this time usually referred to as a
      <i>
       combat knife
      </i>
      , proved so useful that armies continued to develop and issue new designs. On the Axis side, the
      <i>
       Nahkampfmesser
      </i>
      and designs developed from it were again widely issued to the ordinary soldier as general-purpose fighting and utility knives, while Allied armies generally restricted issue of trench knives to elite infantry units and infantry not otherwise equipped with the
      <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">
       bayonet
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUL_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BUL-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_trench_knives">
       Early trench knives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early trench knives">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      With the exception of the German
      <i>
       Nahkampfmesser
      </i>
      or (combat knife),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUL_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BUL-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      most early trench knives were fabricated by hand by individual soldiers or ordnance blacksmiths for the purpose of silently eliminating sentries and other enemy personnel during trench raids.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These early "trench knives" were often nothing more than shortened and sharpened Army-issue bayonets. One type of stabbing weapon, the
      <i>
       French Nail
      </i>
      , was made by cutting and pointing the steel stakes used to support the ubiquitous barbed wire protecting the trench lines. All trench knives share one common characteristic: they were designed specifically for military use in
      <a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">
       close combat
      </a>
      encounters with enemy personnel. Some historians say that some trench knives models were inspired by the
      <a href="/wiki/Bowie_knife" title="Bowie knife">
       Bowie knife
      </a>
      for its design.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Soon afterwards, these fabricated trench knives were being used in defensive close-quarters trench warfare, and such fighting soon revealed certain limitations in existing designs.
     </p>
     <p>
      A more elegant form of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/French_Nail" title="French Nail">
        French Nail
       </a>
      </i>
      was the introduction of the
      <i>
       Poignard-Baonnette Lebel M1886/14
      </i>
      . Approved as a standard military infantry weapon after its development by Lt. Col. Coutrot
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the French Army, the
      <i>
       Poignard-Baonnette Lebel
      </i>
      consisted of a long, needle-pointed,
      <a href="/wiki/Stiletto" title="Stiletto">
       stiletto
      </a>
      -profile blade with wood handle and an integrated knuckle guard made of steel. Originally a conversion of the French
      <i>
       pe-Baonnette Modle 1886
      </i>
      (bayonet), and designed strictly as an offensive weapon, the
      <i>
       Poignard-Baonnette Lebel
      </i>
      used a section of the M1886 Lebel' long, narrow
      <a href="/wiki/Stiletto" title="Stiletto">
       stiletto
      </a>
      -type cruciform blade, designed to quickly kill a surprised enemy soldier with a single deep thrust. Up to three trench knives could be constructed from a single M1886 Lebel bayonet.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because French industry was working under wartime conditions with numerous material shortages, often using subcontracted labor, even officially sanctioned French Army trench knives tend to vary significantly from knife to knife. The need for knives was so great that already-understrength French Army formations were forced to demobilize hundreds of former cutlery workers so that they could return to their former jobs and begin quantity production of trench knives for the armed forces. As the war went on, newer and more versatile blade-type trench knife patterns such as the double-edged dagger
      <i>
       Couteau Poignard Mle 1916 dit Le Vengeur
      </i>
      began to replace the
      <i>
       French Nail
      </i>
      and earlier stiletto-style trench knives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The French lead in trench knife development was closely followed by the United States, which introduced three successive trench knife models - the M1917, M1918, and
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_I_trench_knife" title="Mark I trench knife">
       Mark I (1918)
      </a>
      - all based directly or indirectly upon previous French designs.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="German_Army_trench_knives">
       German Army trench knives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: German Army trench knives">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The German
      <i>
       Nahkampfmesser
      </i>
      was the standard issue German combat knife during the First World War.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUL_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-BUL-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It remained in service in modified form through the end of the Second World War.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAI_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAI-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most of these knives had slab wooden grips and metal sheaths and were sturdily made. According to one authoritative source, German-issued trench knives of World War I were "...conventional, general-purpose, cut-and-thrust knives..." with blades that were "...for the most part approximately six inches in length, single-edged with a top leading
      <a href="/wiki/Knife#Materials.2C_features.2C_and_construction" title="Knife">
       false edge
      </a>
      ...although double-edged blades are occasionally encountered."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the
      <i>
       French Nail
      </i>
      was employed against them, German forces on the Western Front also began to employ converted steel barbed-wire stakes into stabbing weapons for use by their own soldiers.
     </p>
     <p>
      German trench knives carried during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      were similar in design and are usually known today as
      <a href="/wiki/Boot_knife" title="Boot knife">
       boot knives
      </a>
      , although they seldom were carried in boots. Most also had steel sheaths with clips that could be attached to boots, webbing or clothing, and most were made by government contractors and issued as combat gear.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="British_and_Commonwealth_LASBVN_AFS_trench_knives">
       British and Commonwealth LASBVN AFS trench knives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: British and Commonwealth LASBVN AFS trench knives">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      and its
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth
      </a>
      allies (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      ) used a wide variety of trench knives during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      . Some were commercial models based on
      <a href="/wiki/Bowie_knife" title="Bowie knife">
       Bowie knives
      </a>
      . Others were more specialised types, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Push_dagger" title="Push dagger">
       push daggers
      </a>
      with a roughly cylindrical
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      grip which was shaped to fit comfortably when the user's hand made a fist. The attached 4in (10cm) blade protruded between the knuckles of the user. It was common
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       British
      </a>
      practice for trench knives to be used in combination with other "quiet" weapons, like
      <a href="/wiki/Trench_raiding_club" title="Trench raiding club">
       trench clubs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pickaxe#Pickaxe_handle" title="Pickaxe">
       pickaxe handles
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hatchet" title="Hatchet">
       hatchets
      </a>
      , during
      <a href="/wiki/Trench_raiding" title="Trench raiding">
       trench raiding
      </a>
      expeditions, backed up with
      <a href="/wiki/Revolver" title="Revolver">
       revolvers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hand_grenade" title="Hand grenade">
       hand grenades
      </a>
      . Many standardized versions were made by government contractors and officially issued. Most had slab wooden grips and metal sheaths and were sturdily made. During the
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       Second World War
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairbairn-Sykes_Fighting_Knife" title="Fairbairn-Sykes Fighting Knife">
       Fairbairn-Sykes Fighting Knife
      </a>
      was widely issued to forces such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">
       Royal Marines
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Parachute_Regiment_(United_Kingdom)" title="Parachute Regiment (United Kingdom)">
       Parachute Regiment
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Special_Boat_Service" title="Special Boat Service">
       Special Boat Service
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">
       Special Air Service
      </a>
      . Though not a true "trench knife" per se, the Fairbairn-Sykes was used for similar purposes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="US_trench_knives">
       US trench knives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trench_knife&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: US trench knives">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M1918_Trench_Knife.jpg">

        M1918 Trench Knife
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first official U.S. trench knife adopted for service issue was the U.S. M1917 trench knife designed by Henry Disston &amp; Sons, and based on examples of trench knives then in service with the French Army. The M1917 featured a triangular
      <a href="/wiki/Stiletto" title="Stiletto">
       stiletto
      </a>
      blade, wooden
      <a href="/wiki/Hilt#Grip" title="Hilt">
       grip
      </a>
      , metal knuckle guard, and a rounded pommel. The M1917 proved unsatisfactory in service, and a slightly improved version, the M1918, was adopted within months. Despite this, the M1918 is almost identical to the M1917, differing primarily in the construction and appearance of the knuckle guard. Usable only as stabbing weapons, the M1917 and M1918 frequently suffered broken blades. Their limited utility and general unpopularity caused the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Expeditionary_Forces" title="American Expeditionary Forces">
       AEF
      </a>
      to empanel a testing board in 1918 to test various trench knives and select a replacement.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next U.S. trench knife was the
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_I_trench_knife" title="Mark I trench knife">
       Mark I
      </a>
      , which was designed by a board of U.S. Army officers to remedy certain deficiencies of the M1917/18.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRO_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CRO-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Adopted in late 1918, with a blade profile patterned after the French
      <i>
       Couteau Poignard Mle 1916 dit Le Vengeur
      </i>
      , most Mark I knives were completed too late to see service in the trenches of World War I.
     </p>
     <p>
      During World War II, the Mark I was issued in 1942 and 1943 to
      <a href="/wiki/Paratrooper" title="Paratrooper">
       airborne troops
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Rangers" title="U.S. Army Rangers">
       Army Rangers
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Raider" title="Marine Raider">
       Marine Raiders
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPR_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SPR-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAN_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAN-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHA_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SHA-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Mark I featured a
      <a href="/wiki/Tang_(weaponry)" title="Tang (weaponry)">
       full-tang
      </a>
      design with a double-edged blade and a heavy
      <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">
       bronze
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hilt" title="Hilt">
       hilt
      </a>
      incorporating a
      <a href="/wiki/Hilt#Guard" title="Hilt">
       guard
      </a>
      ostensibly shaped as a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Brass_knuckles" title="Brass knuckles">
        knuckle duster
       </a>
      </i>
      , though the latter was intended to protect the fingers and prevent the knife from being knocked from the hand while in combat rather than for use as a weapon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRO_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CRO-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reflecting the savagery of hand-to-hand trench warfare, the Mark I's
      <a href="/wiki/Hilt#Pommel" title="Hilt">
       pommel
      </a>
      incorporated a so-called 'skull-crusher' cap extension, ostensibly designed to stun or kill an enemy soldier and to provide a secondary weapon in circumstances where the blade was damaged or broken. A special proprietary metal scabbard was issued with the Mark I, capable of accommodating the new knife and its over-sized knuckleduster grip handle.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1918, Captain
      <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Hughes" title="Rupert Hughes">
       Rupert Hughes
      </a>
      of the U.S. Army submitted a patent application for a specialized automatic-opening trench knife of his own design, the
      <i>
       Hughes Trench Knife
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUG_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HUG-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This was a curious device consisting of a folding spring-loaded knife blade attached to a handle which fastened to the back of the hand and was secured by a leather strap, leaving the palm and fingers free for grasping other objects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUG_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HUG-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pressing a button on the handle automatically extended a knife blade into an open position and locked position, allowing the knife to be used as a stabbing weapon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUG_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-HUG-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       Hughes Trench Knife
      </i>
      was evaluated as a potential military arm by a panel of U.S. Army officers from the American Expeditionary Force (AEF) in June 1918.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRO_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CRO-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unfortunately, after testing the board found the
      <i>
       Hughes
      </i>
      design to be of no value, and it was never adopted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hughes went on to patent his automatic trench knife in 1919, though it appears to have never interested any civilian manufacturers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUG_17-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-HUG-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1943, the U.S. Army adopted the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M3_Fighting_Knife" title="M3 Fighting Knife">
       M3 Trench Knife
      </a>
      as a replacement for the Mark I.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHIT_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHIT-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Mark I required strategic metals to produce and was too costly to place into mass production, and had been criticized as being unsuited to more modern styles of hand-to-hand knife fighting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPR_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SPR-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAN_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAN-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHIT_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHIT-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAS_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAS-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As the U.S.
      <i>
       Catalog of Standard Ordnance Items
      </i>
      of 1943 explained:
      <i>
       The Trench Knife M3 has been developed to fill the need in modern warfare for hand-to-hand fighting. While designated for issue to soldiers not armed with the bayonet, it was especially designed for such shock units as parachute troops and rangers.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAT_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAT-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAS_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAS-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The M3 was first issued to U.S. Army soldiers in 1943, with the first knives going to elite units such as airborne troops and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Rangers" title="U.S. Army Rangers">
       U.S. Army Rangers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAS_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAS-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite being designed for hand-to-hand warfare, the M3 did not receive universal praise as a fighting knife upon issue to combat units. While well-balanced (some paratroopers and rangers mastered the art of using the M3 as a throwing knife), the M3's blade was criticized as being too narrow for rough usage, particularly for utility tasks such as opening ammo crates and food tins, while its edge was found to be somewhat difficult to sharpen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAS_22-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAS-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The blade's secondary edge was also criticized as being too short, limiting the knife's utility when used for backhand slashing strokes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHIT_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHIT-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAS_22-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-CAS-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many features of the M3 were incorporated into the M4 and eventually
      <a href="/wiki/M5_bayonet" title="M5 bayonet">
       M5 bayonet
      </a>
      for the M1 Carbine and the M5A1 which was in production until 1960. The M5 series would lead directly to the M6-M8 bayonets for the M-16 Rifle. The M4/M5 bayonets had a matching scabbard, the M8A1, officially designated as the "Scabbard, Bayonet Knife, M8A1," with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Stock_Number" title="National Stock Number">
       National Stock Number
      </a>
      (NSN)1095-00-508-0339 and the scabbard could be used for either the M3 fighting knife or its M4/M5 bayonet cousins.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other fighting knives used by U.S. forces have sometimes been referred to as trench knives. These include the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Raider_stiletto" title="United States Marine Raider stiletto">
       stiletto-shaped daggers
      </a>
      carried by
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_Raiders" title="Marine Raiders">
       Marine Corps Raiders
      </a>
      in World War II, and fighting-utility knives made by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KA-BAR" title="KA-BAR">
       KA-BAR
      </a>
      and other manufacturers.
     </p>
