        &lt;p&gt;This season's signature look has more to do with decay than floral high-jinks.         <p>
         In central Melbourne, however, oaks, elms, zelkovas and more are luring visitors to the Royal Botanic Gardens, where, in one 30-minute session this week, I came across  three people expressly photographing red, orange and yellow leaves. Autumn foliage was also much photographed for a book published last year on high-end flower design around the world in which rusty and even dead-brown leaves were shown to be the perfect pairing with flawless blooms.
        </p>
        <!-- cT-imagePortrait -->
        <div class="cT-imagePortrait">
         <img alt="Autumn in Melbourne's Surrey Hills. " itemprop="image" src="http://images.theage.com.au/2015/04/20/6457294/Article%20Lead%20-%20narrow985027061mn9iyimage.related.articleLeadNarrow.353x0.1mn96a.png1429677645842.jpg-300x0.jpg"/>
         <p>
          Autumn in Melbourne's Surrey Hills.
          <em>
           Photo: Craig Abraham
          </em>
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         Although at least one blogger has lamented that the pigments revealed in a senescing leaf are "far too fleeting" for the work involved once it drops, many gardeners don't see it that way.
        </p>
        <p>
         Autumn leaves are not just transitory ornamentation but also mulch, soil enhancer, potting mix ingredient, compost booster, wildlife habitat, the list goes on. Just as the nutrients plants recover from their senescing leaves are recycled in surviving parts of the plant, the leaves themselves can be recycled in the garden.
        </p>
        <p>
         Australian art photographer John Kauffmann might have photographed
         <em>
          Burning the Autumn leaves
         </em>
         around 1900, but circa 2015, setting fire to the fallen foliage is not the way to go. In fact, some gardeners, far from trying to rid themselves of "leaf litter", are on a concerted mission to acquire it.
        </p>
        <!-- cT-imagePortrait -->
        <div class="cT-imagePortrait">
         <img alt="Yellow autumn leaves contrast prettily with the grey architecture." itemprop="image" src="http://images.theage.com.au/2015/04/20/6457291/Article%20Lead%20-%20narrow985027061mn9j4image.related.articleLeadNarrow.353x0.1mn96a.png1429677645842.jpg-300x0.jpg"/>
         <p>
          Yellow autumn leaves contrast prettily with the grey architecture.
          <em>
           Photo: Gary Medlicott
          </em>
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         In her book,
         <em>
          The Thrifty Gardener
         </em>
         , Millie Ross writes that come autumn she nearly always has a rake and a couple of bags on the back of her ute "for opportunistic collections". And, on most mornings for the past month Melbourne artist and author Leigh Hobbs has been loading his car with the fallen leaves of Williamstown's London plane trees and casting them about his garden until they form thick blankets all over his beds. He stomps on them and then leaves them to compost themselves while he stands back and admires how cohesive they make his garden look.
        </p>
        <p>
         Alternatively though, you can heap fallen leaves in a wire cage, compost bin or even a plastic bag with holes cut in it, then moisten them and let them slowly (for months, year even) rot. Although the resulting leaf mould isn't as high in nutrients as compost, it does provide great water-holding, soil-structure-improving organic matter.
        </p>
        <p>
         While different leaves vary in how long they take to decompose (with the soft leaves of the English oak a fast favourite), you can speed things up by shredding or mowing leaves first, or sprinkling them with blood and bone (the Royal Botanic Gardens in Sydney has made ready-to-use-leaf mold in three months by doing just that.)
        </p>
        <p>
         But happily, when it comes to rotting the bounty of autumn, you can be as active  or as inactive  as you like.
        </p>
       </li>
      </ul>
