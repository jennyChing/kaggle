         <h2 id="review-subtitle">
          A Dream Come True
         </h2>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="column-main column">
        <div id="review-body">
         <div id="article-content">
          <span class="review-date">
           <span class="icon-arrow">
            
           </span>
           December 23, 2014
          </span>
          <p>
           History students know Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. from his speeches, those first bellowed words of I Have a Dream universally logged in memory banks. So it makes sense that King's voice is the gateway to the man in Selma, a stirring account of the marches that provoked the 1965 Voting Rights Act's passing. Actor David Oyelowo summons the power and courage to recreate King's calls to action, preaching to the disenfranchised with a Biblical roar.
          </p>
          <p>
           A one-man-show centered on the master orator would satisfy. Oyelowo is that rousing. But director Ava Duvernay opens Selma up to the bigger picture, to the protesters who standing arm and arm with King, the steadfast Southerners who pushed back, and the fire that erupted out of their clash. Expanding the canvas creates a sense of deja vu. Duvernay's film chronicles events from 50 years ago, yet the film's simple, candid direction feels immersive and urgent. Shallow focused, nightmare images mimic in-deep protest videos one can find on YouTube, emerging from protests in Ferguson or New York City. There's reality to Selma that doesnt come easy to any docudrama. Oyelowo's performance is a keystone to a movie about American people.
          </p>
          <p>
           Selma opens with an emblematic juxtaposition: First we see King tying his tie, preparing for his Nobel Peace Prize speech. He's human, his wife Coretta (Carmen Ejogo) drawing out a playful, nervous side to the infallible figure. Moments later, Duvernay jumps halfway across the world to capture one of the African-American Civil Rights movement's darkest hours. The pressure is on, and King's pleas to President Lyndon Johnson (Tom Wilkinson) go unanswered.
          </p>
          <p>
           This sets the tone for Selma, swinging back and forth between stories of people and big picture issues on the table. The film comes to life in the hands of its stellar supporting cast. Duvernay has an eye for casting; Tessa Thompson (Dear White People), Colman Domingo (Lincoln), Wendell Pierce (The Wire), Andr Holland (The Knick) and rapper-turned-actor Common play the equivalent of King's cabinet, their down-time rapport paving the way for insight and wisdom as the figurehead guides followers into protest. A scene where the lively group sits down for a home-cooked meal before their campaign begins imbues Selma with a sense of community that shines through its most terrifying moments.
          </p>
          <p>
           Racism is difficult to depict on screen. Even at its most realistic, it can play cartoonish in modern context. As Alabama governor George Wallace, a staunch, white power oppressor if there ever was one, Tim Roth controls the vitriol with politics. Hate hides behind law, preventing Duvernay's version of Wallace from twirling his mustache in public or the police, who brutalize King's marchers, from exuding evil. They're servants of a mentality. When it starts to shatter, DuVernay is OK with indulging in rah-rah Hollywood moments. In one glorious payoff, Johnson wonders if Wallace really wants to die being the most hated man in America. Roth remains so calm and slimy, Wilkinson's blood boils so hot, that Johnson wonders aloud, Are you trying to f*** over your President? Sorry Schoolhouse Rock, that's how bills pass.
          </p>
          <p>
           Even with overly scripted notes, Selma never shies away from the harsh realities of its situation. The police pushback against King's early demonstrations get ugly, Oprah even taking one for the team as a bystander slammed into the ground by relentless authority. With an array of wideshots and gliding camerawork, DuVernay captures the increasing volume of King's movement and the militarized response to its growth.
          </p>

          <p>
           In an early scene, the civil rights leaders and brave protesters block an area in front of Selma's courthouse. Through cinematographer Bradford Young's lens, there's a 3D quality to this layered image. King's demonstration has dimension. When it's broken up, DuVernay takes us inside the chaos, like a stormchaser in the eye of a hurricane. Bodies hit the ground, smoke billows from canisters, and in one of the early marches towards Montgomery, a fleeing demonstrator is actually whipped by a man on horseback. It's unreal  until you remember it isn't.
          </p>
          <p>
           As the marches become more important, the movement swells, Selma loses track of King's personal hurdles. There may not be enough movie to contain the biopic-like elements, but hints of King's diminishing relationship with Coretta feel jammed in rather than tethered to the world outside. Knowing King only for his voice doesn't do justice to personal moments behind-closed-doors. King's compatriots feel closer than his wife  which may be true to the man  but Selma doesn't find ways to show those emotionally taxing choices.
          </p>

       <div class="column-main column">
        <h3>
         The Verdict
        </h3>
        <p>
         Selma pierces a Civil Rights milestone with honesty, avoiding damning accusations or easy schmaltz to reflect true human triumph. With expansive shots of protests in motion and intimate, shadowy close-ups of King locked in jail, DuVernay's direction brings to life what many only know in black-and-white snapshots. Sound is just as essential, gospel tunes adding history to the present, a melancholy score externalizing tension, and Oyelowo's powerhouse performance transcending the mere message. Know that feeling compelled to stand up in your chair and respond to his calls, even in the movie theater, is totally natural.
        </p>
