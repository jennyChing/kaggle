             <p>
              CENTRAL FALLS, Rhode Island  James Diossa was in college when the housing meltdown first hit his hometown. He was only an intern when Central Falls started careening towards bankruptcy. He was the council member ever when the citys mayor came under investigation for corruption.
             </p>
             <p>
              Now Central Falls is looking to Diossa for help. The 28-year-old Democrat has become its mayor, tasked with turning around the poorest city in Rhode Islandthe state with the highest unemployment rate in the country.
             </p>
             <div class="embed-right">
              <article class="node node-theplatform-video theplatform-video--embed theplatform-video--collapsed" id="node-341736">

                Obama calls surge of child migrants 'urgent'
               </h3>
               <div class="theplatform-video__summary theplatform-video__expanded-only">
                <div class="field field-name-field-short-summary field-type-text field-label-hidden">
                 On Monday, the Obama Administration ordered federal emergency authorities to respond to the growing number of unaccompanied immigrant children crossing the border. Jonathan Ryan and John Stanton discuss.
                </div>
               </div>
               <div class="theplatform-video__social theplatform-video__expanded-only">

             <p>
              The comeback city, Diossa said, Thats what weve been screaming, We are the comeback city!
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Hes turned it into a hashtag on Twitter, of course.
             </p>
             <p>
              Central Falls was the only Rhode Island town to go bankrupt. But its troubles reflect the economic and political dysfunction thats left communities across the country wondering whether America can pull itself together.
             </p>
             <p>
              Once the cradle of the nations industrial revolution, Central Falls had been slowly turning into its graveyard. Then the Great Recession made things even worsealong with unscrupulous leaders who wanted to profit from the citys suffering. And its fallen to Diossa to deal with the mess.
             </p>
             <p>
              Everyone looked at me as the different one, the young one, the new guy, says Diossa, who was first elected in 2012 after the former mayor, Charles Moreau, pled guilty to giving a friend a fat, no-bid contract to board up the citys foreclosed houses. In exchange for the favor, Moreau got a cheap furnace installed in his home.
             </p>
             <figure class="teaser__image--image embed embed-full">
              <img alt="" src="/sites/msnbc/files/styles/embedded_image/public/052714-rhode-island_unemployment.png?itok=zCaGQsPQ"/>
              <div class="media-meta">
              </div>
             </figure>
             <p>
              Meanwhile, Central Falls was hurtling towards bankruptcy, and Moreau, who was first elected in 2003, disavowed himself of any responsibility. I didnt create the problem, he said in late 2011.
             </p>
             <p>
              The scars still havent healed. Unemployment in Central Falls is still 10.4%, significantly above the
              <a href="http://bigstory.ap.org/article/rhode-island-struggles-highest-unemployment" rel="nofollow">
               statewide average
              </a>
              and the nations. Boarded-up buildings still pockmark the city, which spans just one square mile. And average home prices are only 55% of what they were during the peak of the housing boom.
             </p>
             <p>
              One resident, Manuel Perez, has been looking for work since he was laid off six months from his factory job. Originally from the Dominican Republic, hes been in Rhode Island for five years and feeling down on his prospects in America. I thought it was going to be better, but it was
              <em>
               feo
              </em>
              , he said. Ugly, nasty, foul.
             </p>
             <p>
              He surveys the street from the steps of a social services agency, surrounded by stores that promise to take food stamps and WIC. The last mayor took all the money and left, Perez said.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <figure class="teaser__image--image embed embed-right">
              <img alt="Fredy Garcia, 42, paints a building along Broad Street on May 26, 2014 in Central Falls, Rhode Island." src="/sites/msnbc/files/styles/embedded_image/public/cappy_20140526_0141.jpg?itok=mxTn72l5" title="Fredy Garcia, 42, paints a building along Broad Street on May 26, 2014 in Central Falls, Rhode Island."/>
              <div class="media-meta">
               <div class="media-meta__caption">
                Fredy Garcia, 42, paints a building along Broad Street on May 26, 2014 in Central Falls, Rhode Island.
               </div>
               <div class="media-meta__credit">
                Christopher Capozziello for MSNBC
               </div>
              </div>
             </figure>
             <p>
              I
              <span>
               ts a feeling that haunts old factory towns across the country: The sense that the U.S. has already seen its best days, and that our political leaders are more interested in helping themselves than anyone else. As a town of new immigrants, however, with a majority Hispanic population, Central Falls is also a glimpse into Americas future.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Unlike neighboring mill towns, where grey-haired retirees and veterans are the few to be found on Main Street, Central Fallss main drag is filled with backpack-strapped kids and giggling teenagers after school lets out.
             </p>
             <p>
              There are still a few relics from Central Fallss heyday: Stanleys has been serving hamburgers on Dexter Street since 1932. But theyre now surrounded by Guatemalan bakeries, Salvadorian pupuserias, Mexican taquerias, and Colombian restaurantsincluding the one Diossas great-uncle opened in the 1970s.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Diossa is not only towns youngest mayor, but also the first Hispanic to hold the office. He knows the symbolism of it all matters. With me being so young, its allowed people to say theres fresh, new young blood, he says.
             </p>
             <p>
              But there are decades of bad decisions to be undone.
             </p>
             <p style="text-align:center">
              <span>
               __________
              </span>
              <span>
               __________
              </span>
              <span>
               __________
              </span>
              <span>
               __________
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span>
               Rhode Island, the nations smallest state, has a long track record as the dark underbelly of capitalismand a reputation for corrupt leaders whove tried to profit from it.
              </span>
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Once the northern hub of the slave trade, the states mills and factories spawned a commercial aristocracy that corrupted the American stock in Rhode Island and laid the foundation of the present financial and political System of corruption in the State, muckraking journalist Lincoln Steffens
              <a href="mailto:blogs.wpri.com/2011/11/25/from-the-vault-lincoln-steffens-on-the-evils-of-rhode-island/%23more-37807" rel="nofollow">
               wrote
              </a>
              in 1905.
             </p>
             <p>
              Corruption scandals, big and small, have defined political life ever since. Former Gov. Ed DiPrete pled guilty to bribery and extortion in the late 1990s. Former Providence Mayor Buddy Cianci was forced out of office twice, most recently handed a five-year sentence for a racketeering conspiracy; hes now
              <a href="/73-buddy-cianci-considers-yet-another-run-mayor-providence" rel="nofollow">
               hinting at
              </a>
              another run for the office.
             </p>
             <p>
              In 2011, three North Providence council members pled guilty to accepting bribes to green-light a supermarket development. And just this year, Rhode Island state Rep. Gordon Fox
              <a href="http://www.providencejournal.com/breaking-news/content/20140322-gordon-fox-resigns-as-house-speaker-day-after-investigators-raid-home-office.ece" rel="nofollow">
               resigned
              </a>
              as House Speaker after the FBI raided his home and office.
             </p>
             <p>
              <figure class="teaser__image--image embed embed-full">
               <img alt="This view of Central Falls, Rhode Island can be seen from Jenks Park, a historical site in Central Falls." src="/sites/msnbc/files/styles/embedded_image/public/cappy_20140526_1148.jpg?itok=QeC0M8JY" title="This view of Central Falls, Rhode Island can be seen from Jenks Park, a historical site in Central Falls."/>
               <div class="media-meta">
                <div class="media-meta__caption">
                 Central Falls, Rhode Island is seen from Jenks Park.
                </div>
                <div class="media-meta__credit">
                 Christopher Capozziello for MSNBC
                </div>
               </div>
              </figure>
              Many insist Rhode Island isnt as crooked as people think, pointing to an
              <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2013-07-16/corruption-rocked-rhode-island-tops-public-integrity-list.html" rel="nofollow">
               analysis
              </a>
              last year showing the state has the countrys strongest ethics and transparency laws. When it comes to
              <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/govbeat/wp/2014/01/22/the-most-corrupt-states-in-america/" rel="nofollow">
               convictions
              </a>
              of public officials per capita, the state is almost on the
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2008/12/14/weekinreview/14marsh.html?_r=0" rel="nofollow">
               bottom of the list
              </a>
              in a 2008 study; a more recent
              <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/most-corrupt-states-and-territories-2013-9" rel="nofollow">
               analysis
              </a>
              put it in the middle of the pack.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              But word travels fast in this tiny state, where residents joke that a trip that takes more than 10 minutes means you should pack a bag. By reputation, Rhode Island ranked as the most corrupt state in a 2003
              <a href="http://spa.sagepub.com/content/3/4/420.short" rel="nofollow">
               survey
              </a>
              of statehouse reporters.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Theres a half a degree of separation in the whole statewere only one media market, said Scott Wolf, executive director of Grow Smart Rhode Island. That means that any scandal tends to be amplifiedand the players may seem all too familiar members of the old political machine.
             </p>
             <p>
              There seems to be a lot of our politics being personal and transactional, not ideological, a battle of ideas, says John Marion, president of Common Cause Rhode Island, a good government advocacy group. People seem to be drawing on the same recurring cast of charactersthe same person, the same deal.
             </p>
             <p>
              Both the perception and reality of a corrupt, self-dealing, dysfunctional government has made it even harder for Rhode Island to pull itself out of the hole.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Theres no question that corruption costs communities, says Rep. David Cicilline, a Democrat who represents Central Falls in Congress. It cost communities in terms of lost opportunities. It costs communities in terms of public moneys. And most importantly it has an impact on the investment environment.
             </p>
             <p>
              It makes somebody go hmmnobody wants to move their business to a den of iniquity, adds John Gregory, president of the Northern Rhode Island Chamber of Commerce.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>

             <p>
              Even when theyre genuinely trying to act in the states best interest, public officials have made it hard for residents to trust them with the purse. In 2010, then-Gov. Don Carcieri spearheaded a
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/04/21/business/curt-schilling-rhode-island-and-the-fall-of-38-studios.html?pagewanted=all" rel="nofollow">
               disastrous investment
              </a>
              in a video game company owned by baseball legend Curt Schilling, putting taxpayers on the hook for $75 million after the startup failed.
             </p>
             <p>
              It just reinforced the idea that we cant shoot straight, says Saul Kaplan, former executive director of the Rhode Island Economic Development Corporation.
             </p>
             <p>
              The biggest casualties of Central Fallss
              <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-08-02/central-falls-bankruptcy-driven-by-pensions-casts-shadow-over-rhode-island.html" rel="nofollow">
               bankruptcy
              </a>
              also believe failed leadership is to blame. By the time it declared insolvency, the city had racked up $80 million in unfunded pension and health-care liabilities. Under a court-approved plan, more than 130 retired city employees will have their pension cuts as much as 55%.
             </p>
             <p>
              Bruce Ogni, president of the Central Falls Police Retirees Association, said city workers had long worried about the state of their pensions, but City Hall refused to release the actuarial estimatesor to raise taxes to ensure benefits were funded adequately.
             </p>
             <p>
              <span>
               Every year they were in office, since they wanted to win their next term, they didnt want to [raise taxes], says Ogni. To save themselves, they hung us.
              </span>
             </p>

              Diossa was a member of the city council at the timea seat he decided to run for while interning for Democratic Sen. Sheldon Whitehouse and spotted a news story about the race. When Moreau sued the state for stripping him of power after the city entered receivership, Diossa was the sole council member who refused to back him.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              The prior administration didnt do a great job as far as keeping the community informed, and the community really lost faith, he
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/02/22/us/22mayor.html?pagewanted=all" rel="nofollow">

             </p>
             <p>
              In a town of newcomers, the scandal was especially disheartening. Theres a large Latino population that came over from different countries, they already had this notion of corruption, and seeing this here didnt help, Diossa says.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              But he also believes Central Fallss troubled years were a necessary, even inevitable step in helping it move forward.
             </p>
             <p>
              It had to happen in a city where transparency and access was so hard, he said.
             </p>
             <p>
              Diossa recalls struggling to get basic budget numbers from City Hall when he was a council member. It opened up the books, so we could really know what was on the books. It filtered out the corruption, he said.
             </p>
             <p>
              In the mean time, however, ordinary Rhode Islanders have continued to lose their jobs and their unemployment benefits. Despite months of
              <a href="mailto:http://www.msnbc.com/msnbc/congress-goes-home-leaves-unemployed-behind">
               lobbying
              </a>
              by Democratic Sen. Jack Reed, who frequently mentions his states economic woes, House Republicans have refused to renew the federal jobless benefits that expired in December.
             </p>
             <p>
              Soon Diossas own father, Bernando, will be joining their ranks: The factory in Central Falls where hes worked for thirty years is closing its doors in September.

             <p>
              Bernando Diossa moved from Medellin, Colombia to Central Falls in 1983, joining family members who had already come years earlier. In the early 1960s, workers from Medellin had been recruited to help fill a labor shortage at the citys mills, joining earlier waves of Irish, French-Canadian, Portuguese and Polish immigrants.
              <span>
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <figure class="teaser__image--image embed embed-right">

              Even after the factory jobs started to dry up, immigrants continued to come from all over Latin America to look for work and unite with their families in Central Falls. About 60% of the citys 19,000 residents are now Hispanic. But like so many other industrial American towns, the engine for Central Fallss economy has been slowing for decades.
             </p>
             <p>
              When giving directions in Rhode Island, Providence Mayor Angel Taveras explains, We talk about the places where things used to be.
             </p>
             <p>
              In Central Falls, theres the place in where they used to sell ice cream by the river; the defunct railway station where the trains used to stop; the abandoned mills that used to make chocolate, brooms, shoelaces, and toys; the shuttered VFW hall with two cannons left out front that were apparently too unwieldy to discard.
             </p>
             <p>
              For decades now, Diossas family has worried Bernando might lose his job at Osram Sylvania, the lighting manufacturing plant where hes worked as a machine operator since he first came to Central Falls. It was slowly dwindling downthere were always conversations that he wasnt going to have a job anymore, the mayor said.
             </p>
             <p>

              <a href="http://www.newhampshire.com/article/20140320/NEWS02/140329954" rel="nofollow">

               shuttering
              </a>
              three U.S. plants. That meant 88 workers laid off in Central Fallsincluding the Bernando Diossa.
             </p>
             <p>
              The mayor quickly jumped on the phone and convened a meeting at City Hall a few days later, gathering local business leaders and officials to come up with a game plan to help the laid-off workers and find another tenant for the factory.
             </p>
             <p>
              Diossas message: Lets take care of the people firstwe can take care of the building later, recalls Gregory, the local Chamber of Commerce president, who attended the meeting.
             </p>

              A few new employers have come to town since the bankruptcy, like ESS Solutions, a gun parts manufacturer. But with more advanced technology, such companies need fewer workers. And the remaining jobs havent been enough to convince the younger generation to stay here after theyve finished school. The hospitality jobs that are available and retail jobs, they arent enough to sustain the community, said Mario Bueno, executive director of Progreso Latino, a social services organization in Central Falls. We need to have a clear plan.
             </p>
             <p>
              When James Diossa was still in elementary school, the city thought it had found one solution to its woes: A privately-run jail, built on the site of a former junkyard. But the Wyatt Detention Center would undergo its own scandal decades later, dealing a blow to the citys imageand its pocketbook.
             </p>
             <p>
              In 2008, a detainee died in custody with a fractured spine and advanced cancer that had gone undiagnosed and untreated. An investigation
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/01/16/us/16detain.html" rel="nofollow">
               revealed
              </a>
              that employees at the jail denied him a wheelchair, dragged him from his cell while he was screaming in pain, and told him to stop whining. The case prompted federal officials to end its immigration detention contract with the facility, which had been producing $50,000 a month in revenue for the city.
             </p>
             <p>
              Like many of the residents in Central Falls, the detainee who died was an immigrant. He was a computer engineer who had overstayed his visa.
             </p>

              Hugo Figueroa, an IT specialist and father of two, sat on Wyatts board of directors, hoping to help overhaul the troubled facility.

             <p>
              But at Diossas urging, Figueroa gave up the seat to run for the city council last year. Stephanie Gonzalez, 27, whose family came from Colombia, and Shelby Maldonado, 26, were also elected to the council last year. Figueroa, 45, is now the Rhode Islands first Salvadoran-American elected official, and Maldonado is the states
              <a href="mailto:http://ripr.org/post/10-noteworthy-things-about-ris-2013-election" rel="nofollow">

              </a>
              Guatemalan-American.
             </p>
             <p>
              Voter turnout in 2013 was minuscule, and Diossa ran unopposed. But he was heartened to see more Latinos and young people running for office, and successfully ousting the old guard. In the 2012 special election, held after Moreau was arrested, Diossa handily
              <a href="http://www.providencejournal.com/breaking-news/content/20121220-central-falls-mayor-elect-diossa-ready-to-go-to-work.ece" rel="nofollow">
               beat
              </a>
              a former police chief with close ties to Moreau.

             <p>
              Diossa has kept only one official from Moreaus administration, replacing the rest with a mix of veteran staff (his chief of staff used to work for Cicilline) along with young blood; Theresa Agonia, his 22-year-old PR coordinator, graduated from college just last year.
             </p>
             <p>
              Every Monday after work, Diossa gathers young city employees to play pick-up soccer together. He keeps City Hall open late one day a month so more residents can drop by, and picks up the phone when hes not sure how to proceed on an issue. (When he doesnt know something, he asks a question, said Cicciline.)
             </p>
             <p>
              Up at 5:30 a.m. most days,Diossa tries to stay focused on the job.His main companion these days is his golden retriever, Ella. Im dating Central Falls, he joked.
             </p>

              <a href="/central-falls-high-school-three-years-after-mass-firing" rel="nofollow">
               leapt
              </a>
              <span>
