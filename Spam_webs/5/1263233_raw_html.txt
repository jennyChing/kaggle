     <p>
      <b>
       Dr. Benjamin Solomon Carson, Sr.
      </b>
      (born 1951) is an American retired pediatric neurosurgeon and 2016 presidential candidate best known for being the first to separate conjoined twins joined at the head.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a reward for this, a
      <span style="color:#477979;">
       made for TV movie
      </span>
      <sup>
       [
       <a class="extiw" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gifted_Hands" title="wp:Gifted Hands">
        wp
       </a>
       ]
      </sup>
      starring
      <s>
       Hollywood's generic inspirational black man
      </s>
      Cuba Gooding Jr was made about him.  Adding to the astounding nature of his achievement is his strong belief in
      <a href="/wiki/Creationism" title="Creationism">
       creationism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      From 2004 through 2013, Carson touted
      <a href="/wiki/Quackery" title="Quackery">
       quack
      </a>
      treatments in the form of "glyconutrient" products manufactured by a company called Mannatech. Mannatech claims that the
      <a href="/wiki/Nutritional_supplement" title="Nutritional supplement">
       supplements
      </a>
      can treat
      <a href="/wiki/Panacea" title="Panacea">
       everything
      </a>
      "from
      <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">
       cancer
      </a>
      to multiple sclerosis and
      <a href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">
       AIDS
      </a>
      ." Mannatech and its similar predecessor company have been the subject of a number of lawsuits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He attracted the attention of the
      <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party" title="Republican Party">
       Republican Party
      </a>
      after making a variety of
      <a href="/wiki/Conservative" title="Conservative">
       conservative
      </a>
      comments at the 2013
      <a href="/wiki/National_Prayer_Breakfast" title="National Prayer Breakfast">
       National Prayer Breakfast
      </a>
      hosted by
      <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
       President Obama
      </a>
      . Carson himself claimed no political affiliation, commenting that "If I were part of [a political party], it
      <a href="/wiki/Dunning-Kruger_effect" title="Dunning-Kruger effect">
       would be called the Logic party
      </a>
      , and it would be dedicated to
      <a href="/wiki/Common_sense" title="Common sense">
       commonsense
      </a>
      approaches we all should be able to see.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In November 2014, he joined the Republican Party, hinting at the possibility of running for president in 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On May 4th, 2015, Carson announced his candidacy for president.
     </p>
     <p>
      On May 10, 2015, Carson expressed his ignorance of the law on
      <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">
       Fox News
      </a>
      Sunday, saying that the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_Court" title="Supreme Court">
       Supreme Court
      </a>
      's power to review laws passed by Congress needs to be revisited.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
