           <p>
            Houston Jones nervously watched the cursor blink on his computer screen.
           </p>
           <p>
            Seconds passed. Then minutes.
           </p>
           <p>
            Still no reply.
           </p>
           <p>
            The 17-year-old glanced over his shoulder to the person on the other end of the Web chat, his roommate. The two were sitting with their backs to each other in their room in Hulen Hall on Texas Techs campus, each listening to earphones and exchanging instant messages.
           </p>
           <p>
            Or, rather, they had been before Jones last line stopped the exchange.
           </p>
           <p>
            I think I might be gay, it read.
           </p>
           <p>
            He regretted it instantly.
           </p>
           <p>
            When the Big Lake native arrived at Tech the previous week for his first semester, he convinced himself he was straight. He wanted to leave a childhood of scorn and confusion at home.
           </p>
           <p>
            In other words, he was determined to become somebody else.
           </p>
           <p>
            Growing up, Jones had never acknowledged he was gay. There were signs, he says, but he ignored them. Buried them. Flatly denied them.
           </p>
           <p>
            I would spend hours in front of the mirror trying to figure out what made me gay, he remembers. Was it my hair? Was it my weight? I was very much internalizing it. I kept telling myself I would never love a man.
           </p>
           <p>
            Everything the small-town native could piece together about the notion of gay was what he could glean from television and from his Baptist pastors choleric [filtered word]-hating sermons.
           </p>
           <p>
            Even as he began to grasp the concept in the years leading up to college, he continued to convince himself he was not gay.
           </p>
           <p>
            Before arriving at Tech two years ago for his first semester, Jones swore he would cast his true sexuality aside. He wanted to put the depression and the confusion behind him and, just like that, become a straight man on societys terms.
           </p>
           <p>
            I threw on the straight pants, Jones remembers. I threw on the straight voice, the straight mannerisms.
           </p>
           <p>
            This charade lasted a week before Jones, exhausted and lonely, broke down and sought a confidant.
           </p>
           <p>
            He had yet to make any friends, but he seemed to be getting along well enough with his roommate, so he made the gamble and typed it out that night.
           </p>
           <p>
            The roommate didnt stir or respond in any way.
           </p>
           <p>
            The following morning, the roommate left for class and spent the next three nights sleeping in his car.
           </p>
           <p>
            On the fourth day, Jones opened his door to find five large men were standing there with his roommate, who was packing his belongings  which, Jones added, consisted of little more than a suitcase and a laptop.
           </p>
           <p>
            Theres absolutely no reason it took five guys to move him out of his room, Jones says. His voice still shakes and the tension creeps into his shoulders as he recalls it. They all just stopped talking immediately and turned their heads in unison and stared at me.
           </p>
           <p>
            Jones, for the first time in his life, felt imperiled because of his sexuality. He grabbed a textbook and darted down the hall to a stairwell, where he fell apart.
           </p>
           <p>
            And that guilt overcame him once again.
           </p>
           <p>
            He had done nothing wrong, he now acknowledges, but yet there it was.
           </p>
           <p>
            Jones didnt know it at the time, but he was not alone.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Chilly climate
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            On Wednesday morning, police in New Jersey discovered the body of an 18-year-old Rutgers University freshman after it washed up on the banks of the Hudson River.
           </p>
           <p>
            The man, Tyler Clementi, had jumped off a nearby bridge the previous week, killing himself after two of his fellow students allegedly secretly videotaped him having an intimate encounter with another man and streamed the footage online, according to reports from The Associate Press.
           </p>
           <p>
            Both students have been charged with invasion of privacy.
           </p>
           <p>
            The local lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender (LGBT) advocacy group is calling the incident a hate crime.
           </p>
           <p>
            As extreme as it was, the incident in New Jersey may come as no surprise for those attuned to the LGBT community.
           </p>
           <p>
            Discrimination and bullying doesnt stop at high school, said Nonnie Ouchtaqalla, president of Techs Gay-Straight Alliance, I would love it if people could be that mature.
           </p>
           <p>
            She shook her head. It just doesnt happen. It still exists.
           </p>
           <p>
            According to an unprecedented study released less than a week before the body washed ashore in New Jersey, the alienation  and even fear  underscoring Jones and Clementis stories recurs daily in the lives of LGBT college students across the nation.
           </p>
           <p>
            The survey, sponsored by the national advocacy organization Campus Pride, compiled information from more than 5,000 college students, faculty and staff across the country.
           </p>
           <p>
            The 84 percent of the surveys respondents who identified themselves as something other than heterosexual were significantly more likely than their heterosexual counterparts to report discrimination, alienation and outright harassment from their broader campus communities.
           </p>
           <p>
            The report shows us we have a long way to go, said Shane Windmeyer, executive director of Campus Pride. It is alarming and striking to show that our nations colleges are still encountering a high level of discrimination. It should be a wake-up call for higher education to actually walk the walk.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Talking the talk
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            Windmeyer said fewer than 10 percent of the nations universities have LGBT support centers on campus, and Tech is no exception.
           </p>
           <p>
            The university is one of four schools in the Big 12 Conference without an institutional office specifically devoted to its LGBT students. The others are Baylor, the University of Oklahoma and Oklahoma State University.
           </p>
           <p>
            Until a few years ago, LGBT relations at Tech fell under the purview of the schools diversity center, though a reorganization eventually closed that particular office and transferred LGBT efforts to the Student Counseling Center.
           </p>
           <p>
            For Ouchtaqalla, the transfer smacked of insult, as if LGBT students needed mental help, not advocacy.
           </p>
           <p>
            Ouchtaqalla added she now sees Techs counseling staff as an invaluable advocate for LGBT students but still regards the move as a rude insinuation.
           </p>
           <p>
            Juan Munoz, Techs vice president for institutional diversity and equity, did not return several messages left with his office seeking comment.
           </p>
           <p>
            But Eileen Nathan, the director of Student Counseling Services, points out that many universities seek their counseling centers involvement in LGBT relations because their staff typically has diversity training.
           </p>
           <p>
            Beyond serving as the administrative liaison for the Gay-Straight Alliance and its roughly 350 members, the Student Counseling Center also oversees the schools Ally Program, which trains faculty about how they can offer a guidance and refuge for LGBT students who come to them with concerns.
           </p>
           <p>
            Other than the Ally Program and the discussions hosted by the counseling center, however, the only other on-campus sanctuary for LGBT students  whose numbers are estimated to be in the thousands  is the Gay-Straight Alliance, a wholly student-led effort.
           </p>
           <p>
            The groups membership, after years of dwindling, is booming today as more and more students come to college and embrace their sexuality.
           </p>
           <p>
            According to the recent study by Campus Pride, 75 percent of respondents said they had come out to their friends, though far fewer undergraduates than graduate students  46 percent, compared to 70 percent  said they had come out to their families.
           </p>
           <p>
            There seem to be fewer instances in recent years of students automatically moving elsewhere when they come out, Nathan wrote in an e-mail, speaking from her observations at the counseling center. This could be due to a relatively more accepting environment, or it could simply be because supportive resources available to LGBT students are more visible than in previous years.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             The stranger
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            Jones spent the hours after his roommate moved out that Friday afternoon in 2008 wracked by his own emotions.
           </p>
           <p>
            Betrayal. Confusion. Shame. Denial.
           </p>
           <p>
            But mostly just that familiar guilt.
           </p>
           <p>
            Then, when it seemed as if the world was collapsing around him, an elevator door opened, and there stood a man who Jones said he somehow knew was gay.
           </p>
           <p>
            Jones threw his arms around the stranger.
           </p>
           <p>
            The other man, Jones says, returned the gesture with a comforting smile, as if he instinctively understood. He then looked Jones in the eye and asked, Have you heard of the Gay-Straight Alliance?
           </p>
           <p>
            Within a week, Jones would attend his first GSA meeting and, for the first time in his life, publicly acknowledge he was gay.
           </p>
           <p>
            Merely saying it instantly lightened the burden he had carried for years, he now says, at least somewhat.
           </p>
           <p>
            But there were still two phone calls to make.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Three-letter words
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            The refuge offered by Techs GSA only goes so far.
           </p>
           <p>
            A group of its nature in conservative West Texas is bound to encounter at least a few dirty looks, or worse, Ouchtaqalla said.
           </p>
           <p>
            When the group promotes awareness outside of Techs Student Union Building, she said, its not uncommon for some passers-by to say things like, Fags go home, or fags should die.
           </p>
           <p>
            But Ouchtaqalla said thats usually the extent of the outright harassment on campus, and the alliance makes a point to shrug it off. The group is trying to distance itself from victimhood.
           </p>
           <p>
            Besides, she said, the majority of students show signs of support, not contempt.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its the more subtle clues that Ouchtaqalla worries about. The use of the word fag, for instance, is rampant among students. So, too, is the pejorative use of the word gay.
           </p>
           <p>
            Linda Larrabee, a sociology instructor and outspoken LGBT ally, laments how often her students, especially her male students, blithely express their homophobia, refusing to work with or sit next to gay classmates.
           </p>
           <p>
            She refuses to tolerate it in her classroom and tries to expand her students horizons to include more than what some of them were taught growing up.
           </p>
           <p>
            Prejudice is taught from the beginning, she said. And some religions promote a feeling of disgust about gays and lesbians, but its a whole segment of the population thats mistreated, misunderstood.
           </p>
           <p>
            And the administration?
           </p>
           <p>
            Ouchtaqalla seeks little and expects less from school officials. She said the group tries to rely on itself as much as possible, though it does hope to see a campus LGBT center open soon.
           </p>
           <p>
            Administrators, she said, may eventually realize they cannot achieve tier-one status without focusing on the diversity that will come with it.
           </p>
           <p>
            Tech already lacks diversity in general, she said, with whites comprising 71 percent of last falls enrollment.
           </p>
           <p>
            This all takes a toll on retention rates, at least to some degree. Ouchtaqalla has seen LGBT students reluctantly come to Tech after learning about its close-minded reputation.
           </p>
           <p>
            Although some are pleasantly surprised, she said, many who are not eventually withdraw.
           </p>
           <p>
            If we want to compare ourselves to the (Big 12) conference and talk about tier-one status, Ouchtaqalla said, Texas Tech is lagging. They really need to step it up.
           </p>
           <p>
            Windmeyer said Campus Prides recent study doesnt break down the numbers by region, but his experiences show the lack of LGBT awareness at Tech is symptomatic of the broader southern part of the country.
           </p>
           <p>
            We have witnessed a chilly, oftentimes frigid, campus climate from colleges in the South, he said. We receive less responses from our colleges in the South but also hear more from their LGBT students. Were hearing a lot of students asking for support but fewer administrators offering it.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Waterworks
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            During spring break in March 2009, Jones was in Lubbock when he finally picked up the phone and asked his mother if she was sitting down.
           </p>
           <p>
            She cried when he told her he was gay, Jones remembers.
           </p>
           <p>
            The first thing she said was, Why? She kept asking herself that over and over. I didnt have any answer. Thats just the way it is. You either are or you arent.
           </p>
           <p>
            She eventually collected herself and  Jones laughs about this now  asked him to help redecorate her apartment.
           </p>
           <p>
            He told his father a few days later, also over the phone.
           </p>
           <p>
            Jones says he told his father, an inveterately conservative oil field worker with whom Jones had always had a rocky relationship, as the two men yelled at each other.
           </p>
           <p>
            The line suddenly fell silent, and his father took a deep breath.
           </p>
           <p>
            This is going to be a long, hard path for you, he told his son. I just want you to pray about it.
           </p>
           <p>
            Then his father delivered the blow: With everything going on right now with my life, his father continued, and now this.
           </p>
           <p>
            That was what hurt, Jones says.
           </p>
           <p>
            Both parents have yet to grasp what he told them more than a year ago, he says, and they seem to waiver at times between acceptance and denial.
           </p>
           <p>
            But Jones, now 19, says theyre trying.
           </p>
           <p>
            People have a hard time understanding, he says, but theyre the ones who raised me. I dont want to have to go through my whole life without them knowing who I love.
           </p>
           <p>
            More generally, Jones is optimistic about what he sees as a growing acceptance and inclusion of LGBT students, both at Tech and more generally.
           </p>
           <p>
            We look at our generation and we see how far weve come, he continues. And then we look at our parents and their parents and we see how much theyre stuck in their ways. To me, thats hope.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
