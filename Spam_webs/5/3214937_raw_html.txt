              I think(hope?) this qualifies for a good discussion post.. ^_^;;
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Below is a list of the 50 most influential people in the world, of the last 1,000 years, as decided in the book
              <u>
               1,000 Years, 1,000 people: Ranking the Men and Women Who Shaped the Millennium.
              </u>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              My question is: Do you agree with the people, the ranking? Do they make sense? Is it fair, or unfairly western-centric? Do they deserve their place in history/posterity? I read the entire book, and to me, some of them seem oddly placed (I'd have placed Mao Ze Dong higher in the list, but...)
              <br/>
              <br/>
              1. Johannes Gutenberg- western inventor of the movable printing press
              <br/>
              2. Christopher Columbus- agent of Western Civilisation
              <br/>
              3. Martin Luther- the monk who divided Christendom
              <br/>
              4. Galileo Galilei- founder of modern science
              <br/>
              5. William Shakespeare- bard of the millennium
              <br/>
              6. Isaac Newton- founder of the theory of gravity and motion
              <br/>
              7. Charles Darwin- father of the theory of Evolution
              <br/>
              8. Thomas Aquinas- Christianity's Voice of REason
              <br/>
              9. Leonardo da Vinci- the ultimate Renaissance Man
              <br/>
              10. Ludwig Van Beethoven- music's titan
              <br/>
              <a name="cutid1">
              </a>
              11. John Locke- democracy's philosophiser
              <br/>
              12. Mohandas K. Ghandi- popularised passive resistance; peaceful slayer of an empire
              <br/>
              13. Michelangelo (Buonarroti)- first amoungst artists
              <br/>
              14. Karl Marx- founder of modern communism
              <br/>
              15. Sigmund Freud- sage of the subconscious, believed everyone wanted a penis
              <br/>
              16. Napoleon I- the human God of War
              <br/>
              17. Albert Einstein- the brain behind the revolution in physics, E=mc squared.
              <br/>
              18. Nicholas Copernicus- astronomer who dethroned man from the center of the universe
              <br/>
              19. Jean-Jacques Rousseau- spiritual father of just about everything
              <br/>
              20. Adolf Hitler- the millennium's most evil man
              <br/>
              21. Adam Smith- high priest of capitalism
              <br/>
              22. George Washington- father of our country (if you happen to be American)
              <br/>
              23 &amp; 24: Wilbur and Orville Wright- finally got the damn plane off the ground
              <br/>
              25. Rene Descartes- progenitor of modern philosophy and inventor of analytic geometry; 'I think, therefore I am.'
              <br/>
              26. Louis Pasteur- human scourge of the microorganisms
              <br/>
              27. Peter the Great- czar who built Russia into a great power by Westernising it
              <br/>
              28. Thomas Alva Edison- the man who lit the world; mostly known for light bulbs and such
              <br/>
              29. William I- conquerered England
              <br/>
              30. Dante Alighieri- divine poet of the vernacular verse; wrote in his native Italian rather than clerical Latin, paving the way for Europe's soon to come lay culture
              <br/>
              31. Elizabeth I- moulder of the modern British state
              <br/>
              32. Abraham Lincoln- saved the Union and managed to abolish slavery in the process
              <br/>
              33. Johannes Kepler- discoverer of the paths of the planets
              <br/>
              34. Leo Tolstoy- superman of letters
              <br/>
              35. Johann Sebastian Bach- inspiration for three centuries of musicians
              <br/>
              36. Voltaire- apostle of enlightenment; author of Candide
              <br/>
              37. Franklin Delano Roosevelt- protector of the American way of life
              <br/>
              38. Winston Churchill- hero of England's finest hour
              <br/>
              39. Francis of Assissi- instrument of peace
              <br/>
              40. Niccolo Machiavelli- first modern political scientist
              <br/>
              41. Vladimir Ilyich Lenin- primal revolutionary
              <br/>
              42. Ferdinand Magellan- leader of the first trip around the world
              <br/>
              43. Ghengis Khan- destroyer of Asia
              <br/>
              44. Miguel de Cervantes Saavedra- creator of the most beloved fool in literature
              <br/>
              45. Mary Wollstonecraft- beacon for women's rights
              <br/>
              46. Rembrandt (Harmenzoon van Rijm)- master of the Dutch masters
              <br/>
              47. Willian Harvey- physician who unlocked the secrets of blood circulation
              <br/>
              48. Simon Bolivar- liberator of South America
              <br/>
              49. Immanuel Kant- metaphysican of the modern world
              <br/>
              50. Mao Ze-Dong- China's unifier, at a horrendous cost

                <div class="comment-body">
                 Who was the publisher/author?  This sounds very similar to Time's attempt to figure out the "Person of the Century" in their December 31, 1999 issue.  They also tried to come up with "people of the centuries" for each of the centuries between the year 1000 and this one.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 I'd put Columbus much further down on the list.  Columbus doesn't strike me so much as being an active cause so much as being the lucky guy who happened to do the thing that someone was inevitably going to do anyway.  If he hadn't made the voyage from Europe to the New World, someone else would have sooner or later.  (Also, "Western Civilization had started well before then, by most accounts.)

                <div class="comment-body">
                 I think that lists like these are bound to be biased one way or another.  It's hard to compare the exact influence of people who were active in completely different fields - ie, a musician, a theologian, and a military leader.  People are also going to tend to emphasize great figures in different fields of activity depending on their personal tastes.  This list suggests to me a stronger interest in politics and the arts than in other fields like science and business.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 The list does have a western bias, but one could argue that great western figures have tended to be more influential overall in the last 500 years simply because western culture expanded to become the most influential and powerful culture in the world.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 They certainly are all influential figures, but one could probably come up with another list where they are all in a different order, or at least half of the historical figures were different.
