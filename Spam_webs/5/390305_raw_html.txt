&lt;div class="content-body"&gt;
&lt;p&gt;&lt;img class="userImageRight" src="http://cdn-ugc.cafemom.com/gen/resize/263/263/80/2010/12/29/10/5c/6z/po4ovri0gs3llp.jpg" width="263" height="263" /&gt;After the topic of &lt;a href="http://thestir.cafemom.com/search.php?keyword=car+seat+on+plane" target="_blank"&gt;car seats on airplanes&lt;/a&gt; being brought up constantly, and the legality of flying without one being a point of consideration, this news story confused me, and likely, really confused this poor mother.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;We're told our babies on airplanes, in turbulence, would be like a projectile and go flying, or our bodies would crush them if we tried to cling to them. So when &lt;strong&gt;Melissa Bradley took her infant car seat onboard a SkyWest airplane&lt;/strong&gt;, the last thing she expected was to be told she was not allowed to use it -- &lt;strong&gt;she could either hold her baby or get off the plane.&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;div class="ad-unit article-ad-unit"&gt;
&lt;div class="box-ad"&gt;
&lt;div id='div-gpt-ad-217156456-4'&gt; &lt;script type='text/javascript'&gt; googletag.display('div-gpt-ad-217156456-4');
      </script>
     </div>
     <script src="http://c.betrad.com/surly.js#;ad_w=300;ad_h=250;coid=2050;nid=16145;position=bottom-right;" type="text/javascript">
     </script>
    </div>
   </div>
   <p>
    Like we're told to do,
    <a href="http://www.thedenverchannel.com/news/26300622/detail.html" target="_blank">
     Bradley carried her infant car seat onto the plane
    </a>
    and installed it rear-facing into the plane's seat.
   </p>
   <p>
    The flight attendant came over and argued with her that she needed to turn the seat forward-facing, to which she replied, obviously, that infant seats can't go forward-facing -- they're not made to install that way, and it's not safe.
   </p>
   <p>
    Once the attendant dropped that argument, he told her that her Graco infant seat wasn't FAA-approved, which of course is equally ridiculous, and she showed him the sticker that proved him wrong. It's worth noting nowhere on the sticker does it say "FAA-Approved." It says "The restraint is certified for use in motor vehicles and aircraft." I'm surprised he didn't argue with her over that some more.
   </p>
   <p>
    Apparently incredibly determined, the attendant then
    <strong>
     gave her the ultimatum of holding her baby for the flight and not using the seat, or getting off the plane.
    </strong>
    Like many people when confronted with stressful situations, she caved and agreed, and spent the entire flight terrified for her daughter's safety -- not surprising with all the information about how unsafe it is being discussed daily.
   </p>
   <p>
    There's no question the attendant was in the wrong, and the airline has since apologized and said they've spoken with the attendant.
   </p>
   <p>
    I know car seats on airplanes can be a pain in the butt, so I can't imagine going through everything to get the seat there, only to have some seriously misinformed attendant start spewing fallacies at me about the seat itself. Poor Bradley!
   </p>
   <p>
    <em>
     (Edited to add: Mr. and Mrs. Bradley have commented on this post and yes, they did pay for the seat the baby, Sunshine's car seat was to be in.)
    </em>
   </p>
   <p>
    <strong>
     Have you ever had problems taking a car seat on a plane?
