     <p>
      Year
      <b>
       1
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">
        I
       </a>
      </b>
      ) was a
      <a href="/wiki/Common_year_starting_on_Saturday" title="Common year starting on Saturday">
       common year starting on Saturday
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Common_year_starting_on_Sunday" title="Common year starting on Sunday">
       Sunday
      </a>
      (link will display the full calendar) of the
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">
       Julian calendar
      </a>
      (the sources differ, see
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar#Leap_year_error" title="Julian calendar">
       leap year error
      </a>
      for further information) and a
      <a href="/wiki/Common_year_starting_on_Saturday" title="Common year starting on Saturday">
       common year starting on Saturday
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Proleptic_Julian_calendar" title="Proleptic Julian calendar">
       Proleptic Julian calendar
      </a>
      . It is a
      <a href="/wiki/Common_year_starting_on_Monday" title="Common year starting on Monday">
       Common year starting on Monday
      </a>
      , in the
      <a href="/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar" title="Proleptic Gregorian calendar">
       Proleptic Gregorian calendar
      </a>
      system. At the time, it was known as the
      <b>
       Year of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consulship" title="Consulship">
        Consulship
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Caesar" title="Gaius Caesar">
        Caesar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Aemilius_Paullus_(consul_1)" title="Lucius Aemilius Paullus (consul 1)">
        Paullus
       </a>
      </b>
      (or, less frequently,
      <b>
       year 754
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ab_urbe_condita" title="Ab urbe condita">
         Ab urbe condita
        </a>
       </i>
      </b>
      ). The denomination 1 for this year has been used since the early
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       medieval period
      </a>
      , when the
      <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">
       Anno Domini
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Calendar_era" title="Calendar era">
       calendar era
      </a>
      became the prevalent method in Europe for naming years. It was the first year of the
      <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">
       Christian
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">
       Common
      </a>
      era. The preceding year is
      <a href="/wiki/1_BC" title="1 BC">
       1 BC
      </a>
      in the widely used Julian calendar, which does not have a "
      <a href="/wiki/0_(year)" title="0 (year)">
       year zero
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
