          <p>
           <b>
            1. Lord of the Flies, William Golding (1954)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Are we human or are we savages? Or is the human essentially savage? Oh, the moral confusion! This good-boys-gone-bad tale of desert island adventure, cruelty and tragedy is a thriller when youre ten, but more of a chiller when youre older an can appreciate the true horror of murderous schoolkids. Its also the main reason why Goldings a household name, Nobel Prize notwithstanding.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="LordOfTheFliesBookCover" class="size-full wp-image-852 aligncenter" height="544" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/LordOfTheFliesBookCover.jpg" width="351"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            2. Charlottes Web, E.B. White (1952)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           A pig and a spider, friendship, love, loyalty, birth and death: were not being schmaltzy, but this childrens classic is a proper well of emotion, and its not getting any less moving as the decades pass. Plus, for all you lit-geeks, E.B. is the very same White you might recognize from Strunk &amp; Whites
           <em>
            The Elements of Style
           </em>
           , the dry-eyed compositional manual to beat all compositional manuals. Is there nothing this man couldnt do?
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="CharlotteWeb" class="size-full wp-image-855 aligncenter" height="505" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/CharlotteWeb.png" width="351"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            3. The Fellowship of the Ring, J.R.R. Tolkien (1954)
            <br/>
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Volume One of
           <em>
            The Lord of the Rings
           </em>
           trilogy. We hardly feel like we need to introduce this one  but if youve only seen the movies, its time you cracked the spine of this classic of British fantasy, if only to check out the unique musical stylings of Tom Bombadil. Also, if there had been no Fellowship, wed probably never have invented Dungeons &amp; Dragons! (Hmm)
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="FellowshipOfTheRing" class="size-full wp-image-857 aligncenter" height="559" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/FellowshipOfTheRing.jpg" width="352"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            4. East of Eden, John Steinbeck (1952)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Two families histories become entangled in the Californian Salinas Valley in the early twentieth century. Taking inspiration in part from the Cain and Abel story of the Old Testament, this is the novel that Steinbeck was said to consider his masterpiece. Its certainly stars one of literatures most interesting antagonists in the figure of Cathy Ames, a cruel and cold individual who leaves violence and mayhem in her wake. A brilliant family saga (but probably not one for the whole family to share, what with the murders and all).
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="EastOfEden" class="size-full wp-image-859 aligncenter" height="508" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/EastOfEden.jpg" width="352"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            5. Things Fall Apart, Chinua Achebe (1958)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           One of the most famous and historically significant African novels to be published in the English language, Achebes book tells the story of an Igbo mans downfall in the context of a white governments incursions onto his land. As brilliant as the book is, its also fascinating as regards its position at the intersection of language, colonialism and power. Given the still marginalized position of African literatures in the Western book-market today, Achebes work is still a central text for those who want to expand their literary horizons.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="ThingsFallApart" class="size-full wp-image-861 aligncenter" height="526" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/ThingsFallApart.jpg" width="352"/>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <center>
          </center>
          <p>
           <b>
            6. The Tin Drum, Gunter Grass (1959)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Oskar Matzerath is a German dwarf with questionable sanity who gets caught up with the Nazia, becomes a jazz drummer, gets falsely imprisoned for murder and writes his memoirs from jail. Well, no summary of
           <em>
            The Tin Drum
           </em>
           is likely to do Grasss huge and complex novel justice. Its been accused of being pornographic and blasphemous, like all the most famous books of the twentieth century  Lady Chatterley, please stand up!  and its nothing if not fascinating. Well worth a punt, we say.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="The_Tin_Drum_cover" class="size-full wp-image-863 aligncenter" height="501" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/The_Tin_Drum_cover.gif" width="349"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            7. The Borrowers, Mary Norton (1952)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           A race of diminutive people, struggling to stay alive under your floorboards  hey, its more likely than the Bilbo/dragon scenario. Nortons book began a series that spawned numerous TV shows and a 2010 Japanese animation, but the original is still the best, and we kind of wish Arrietty Clock would come borrowing
           <i>
            our
           </i>
           goods.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="TheBorrowers" class="size-full wp-image-865 aligncenter" height="484" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/TheBorrowers.jpg" width="351"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            8. The Grass Is Singing, Doris Lessing (1950)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Lessings first novel is, as you might expect, a book about racial politics in what was then Southern Rhodesia, and is now Zimbabwe  its set on a farm and stars an ill-suited white couple and their black servant, Moses. The 1940s setting made it very relevant to its contemporary readers, but its nonetheless got a kick to it today, as Marys treatment of Moses still makes for very uncomfortable reading.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="best50snovels" class="size-full wp-image-867 aligncenter" height="501" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/doris-tayler-may-lessing-1919-2013-L-9y9pNs.jpg" width="349"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            9. Under The Net, Iris Murdoch (1954)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Writers, philosophers and bookies rubbing shoulders in London: this picaresque gem was Murdochs first novel and it stands the test of time, not least because her prose, here as everywhere, is indubitably brilliant.
           <em>
            Under The Net
           </em>
           is slapstick existential madness of the highest order and were off to reread it right now.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="best50snovels" class="size-full wp-image-869 aligncenter" height="472" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/best50snovels1.jpg" width="350"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            10. Our Spoons Came From Woolworths, Barbara Comyns Carr (1950)
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           The least well-known novel on our list, well wager, this book by English writer Comyns Carr ought to be more widely loved, because, we cry, who
           <i>
            wouldnt
           </i>
           adore a book about an artists model in 1930s Bohemian London? Its witty and compelling, and we dare to whisper, an almost-forgotten classic.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="best50snovels2" class="size-full wp-image-871 aligncenter" height="534" src="//www.whizzpast.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/best50snovels2.jpg" width="347"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           These are some of the novels we think have improved in the last half a century, but theres doubtlessly plenty more worth a mention. If youve got some in mind, please let us know in the comments below.
          </p>
