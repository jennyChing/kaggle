   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Rookery (slum)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

     <div class="hatnote">
      For other uses, see
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rookery_(disambiguation)" title="Rookery (disambiguation)">
       Rookery (disambiguation)
      </a>

     <p>
      "
      <b>
       Rookery
      </b>
      " was a colloquial term given in the 18th and 19th centuries to a city
      <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">
       slum
      </a>
      occupied by
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       poor people
      </a>
      and frequently also by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal" title="Criminal">
       criminals
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostitute" title="Prostitute">
       prostitutes
      </a>
      . Such areas were overcrowded, with low-quality housing and little or no sanitation. Poorly constructed dwellings, built with multiple storeys and often crammed into any area of open ground, created densely-populated areas of gloomy, narrow streets and alleyways.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rookery_(slum)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The nickname
      <i>
       rookery
      </i>
      originated because of the perceived similarities between a city slum and the nesting habits of the
      <a href="/wiki/Rook_(bird)" title="Rook (bird)">
       rook
      </a>
      , a bird in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crow" title="Crow">
       crow
      </a>
      family. Rooks nest in large, noisy colonies consisting of multiple nests, often untidily crammed into a close group of treetops called a
      <a href="/wiki/Rookery" title="Rookery">
       rookery
      </a>
      . The word might also be linked to the
      <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">
       slang
      </a>
      expression
      <i>
       to rook
      </i>
      (meaning to cheat or steal), a verb well established in the 16th century and associated with the supposedly thieving nature of the rook bird. The term was first used in print by the
      <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">
       poet
      </a>
      George Galloway in 1792 to describe "a cluster of mean tenements densely populated by people of the lowest class".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="London_rookeries">
       London rookeries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rookery_(slum)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: London rookeries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="556" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg/220px-Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg/330px-Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg/440px-Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Poverty_map_old_nichol_1889.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Part of
        <a href="/wiki/Charles_Booth_(philanthropist)" title="Charles Booth (philanthropist)">
         Charles Booth
        </a>
        's
        <a href="/wiki/Poverty_map" title="Poverty map">
         poverty map
        </a>
        showing the
        <a href="/wiki/Old_Nichol" title="Old Nichol">
         Old Nichol
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/East_End_of_London" title="East End of London">
         East End of London
        </a>
        . Published 1889 in
        <a href="/wiki/Life_and_Labour_of_the_People_in_London" title="Life and Labour of the People in London">
         Life and Labour of the People in London
        </a>
        . The red areas are "middle class, well-to-do", light blue areas are poor, 18s to 21s a week for a moderate family, dark blue areas are very poor, casual, chronic want, and black areas are the "lowest class...occasional labourers, street sellers, loafers, criminals and semi-criminals".
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Famous rookeries include the
      <a href="/wiki/St_Giles,_London" title="St Giles, London">
       St Giles
      </a>
      area of central
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      , which existed from the 17th century and into Victorian times, an area described by
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mayhew" title="Henry Mayhew">
       Henry Mayhew
      </a>
      in about 1860 in
      <i>
       A Visit to the Rookery of St Giles and its Neighbourhood
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This,
      <a href="/wiki/Bermondsey" title="Bermondsey">
       Bermondsey
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob%27s_Island" title="Jacob's Island">
       Jacob's Island
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Nichol_Street_Rookery" title="Old Nichol Street Rookery">
       Old Nichol Street Rookery
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/East_End_of_London" title="East End of London">
       East End of London
      </a>
      were demolished as part of London
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slum_clearance" title="Slum clearance">
       slum clearance
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_redevelopment" title="Urban redevelopment">
       urban redevelopment
      </a>
      projects in the late 19th century. The Rookery of St Giles appears in
      <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">
       Neil Gaiman
      </a>
      's short story
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Study_in_Emerald" title="A Study in Emerald">
        A Study in Emerald
       </a>
      </i>
      as the place where the antagonist (Rache) and his sidekick (the limping doctor) reputedly take shelter after committing their (justifiable in their view) crime.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1850 the English novelist
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">
       Charles Dickens
      </a>
      was given a guided tour of several dangerous rookeries by "Inspector Field, the formidable chief detective of
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">
       Scotland Yard
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A party of sixDickens, Field, an assistant commissioner, and three lower ranks (probably armed)made their way into the Rat's Castle, backed by a squad of local police within whistling distance. The excursion started in the evening and lasted until dawn. They went through
      <a href="/wiki/St_Giles,_London" title="St Giles, London">
       St Giles
      </a>
      and even worse slums, in the Old Mint, along the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratcliffe_Highway" title="Ratcliffe Highway">
       Ratcliffe Highway
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petticoat_Lane" title="Petticoat Lane">
       Petticoat Lane
      </a>
      . The results of this and other investigations came out in novels, short stories, and straight journalism, of which Dickens wrote a great deal.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Twist" title="Oliver Twist">
        Oliver Twist
       </a>
      </i>
      (1838) features the rookery at
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob%27s_Island" title="Jacob's Island">
       Jacob's Island
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       ... crazy wooden galleries common to the backs of half a dozen houses, with holes from which to look upon the slime beneath; windows, broken and patched, with poles thrust out, on which to dry the linen that is never there; rooms so small, so filthy, so confined, that the air would seem to be too tainted even for the dirt and squalor which they shelter; wooden chambers thrusting themselves out above the mud and threatening to fall into itas some have done; dirt-besmeared walls and decaying foundations, every repulsive lineament of poverty, every loathsome indication of filth, rot, and garbage: all these ornament the banks of Jacob's Island.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sketches_by_Boz" title="Sketches by Boz">
        Sketches by Boz
       </a>
      </i>
      (1839), Dickens again described a rookery:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Wretched houses with broken windows patched with rags and paper: every room let out to a different family, and in many instances to two or even three... filth everywherea gutter before the houses and a drain behindclothes drying and slops emptying, from the windows; girls of fourteen or fifteen, with matted hair, walking about barefoot, and in white great-coats, almost their only covering; boys of all ages, in coats of all sizes and no coats at all; men and women, in every variety of scanty and dirty apparel, lounging, scolding, drinking, smoking, squabbling, fighting, and swearing.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Thomas Beame's
      <i>
       The Rookeries of London
      </i>
      (1850) also described one:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       The Rookery... was like an honeycomb, perforated by a number of courts and blind alleys, cul de sac, without any outlet other than the entrance. Here were the lowest lodging houses in London, inhabited by the various classes of thieves common to large cities were banded together Because all are taken in who can pay their footing, the thief and the prostitute are harboured among those whose only crime is poverty, and there is thus always a comparatively secure retreat for him who has outraged his country's laws. Sums here are paid, a tithe of which, if well laid out, would provide at once a decent and an ample lodging for the deserving poor; and that surplus, which might add to the comfort and better the condition of the industrious, finds its way into the pocket of the middleman....
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Kellow_Chesney" title="Kellow Chesney">
       Kellow Chesney
      </a>
      gives a whole chapter,
      <i>
       Citadels of the Underworld
      </i>
      , to the rookeries of London. At their zenith they were a problem that seemed impossible to solve, yet eventually they did decline. Changes in the law, the growing effectiveness of the police, slum clearances, and perhaps the growing prosperity of the economy gradually had their effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_rookeries">
       Other rookeries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rookery_(slum)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other rookeries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The King Street Rookery in
      <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">
       Southampton
      </a>
      was also notorious during the early 19th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term has also been used in other parts of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_(language)" title="English (language)">
       English
      </a>
      -speaking world, including the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
