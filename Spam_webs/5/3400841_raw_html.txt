   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    International Nonproprietary Name
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:35%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
      <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
       <div>
        <p>
         <b>
          Mandate
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         WHO has a constitutional mandate to
         <b>
          "develop, establish and promote international standards with respect to biological, pharmaceutical and similar products".
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         The World Health Organization collaborates closely with INN experts and national nomenclature committees to select a single name of worldwide acceptability for each active substance that is to be marketed as a pharmaceutical. To avoid confusion, which could jeopardize the safety of patients, trade-marks should neither be derived from INNs nor contain common stems used in INNs.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="text-align: left;">
       WHO
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WHO-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An
      <b>
       International Nonproprietary Name
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       INN
      </b>
      ) is an official
      <a href="/wiki/Trademark_distinctiveness#Generic_terms" title="Trademark distinctiveness">
       generic
      </a>
      and non
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proprietary_name" title="Proprietary name">
       proprietary
      </a>
      name given to a
      <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">
       pharmaceutical drug
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Active_ingredient" title="Active ingredient">
       active ingredient
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_innguidance_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHO_innguidance-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      INNs make communication more precise by providing a unique standard name for each active ingredient, to avoid
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_prescription" title="Medical prescription">
       prescribing
      </a>
      errors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHO-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The INN system has been coordinated by the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">
       World Health Organization
      </a>
      (WHO) since 1953.
     </p>
     <p>
      Having unambiguious standard names for each drug is important because a drug may be sold by many different brand names, or a branded medication may contain more than one drug. For example, the branded medications Celexa, Celapram and Citrol all contain the same active ingredient:
      <a href="/wiki/Citalopram" title="Citalopram">
       citalopram
      </a>
      ; and the branded preparation
      <a href="/wiki/Lemsip" title="Lemsip">
       Lemsip
      </a>
      contains two active ingredients:
      <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">
       paracetamol
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Phenylephrine" title="Phenylephrine">
       phenylephrine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Each drug's INN is unique but may contain a word stem that is shared with other drugs of the same
      <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug#Classification" title="Pharmaceutical drug">
       class
      </a>
      , for example the
      <a href="/wiki/Beta_blocker" title="Beta blocker">
       beta blocker
      </a>
      drugs
      <a href="/wiki/Propranolol" title="Propranolol">
       propranolol
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Atenolol" title="Atenolol">
       atenolol
      </a>
      share the
      <i>
       -olol
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
       suffix
      </a>
      , and the benzodiazepine drugs
      <a href="/wiki/Lorazepam" title="Lorazepam">
       lorazepam
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Diazepam" title="Diazepam">
       diazepam
      </a>
      share the
      <i>
       -azepam
      </i>
      suffix.
     </p>
     <p>
      The WHO issues INNs in
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
       Spanish
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
       Arabic
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      , and a drug's INNs are often
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognate
      </a>
      across most or all of the languages, with minor spelling or pronunciation differences, for example: "
      <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">
       paracetamol
      </a>
      " (
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       en
      </a>
      ) "paracetamolum" (
      <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">
       la
      </a>
      ), "paractamol" (
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       fr
      </a>
      ) and "" (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       ru
      </a>
      ). An established INN is known as an
      <b>
       rINN
      </b>
      (
      <i>
       recommended
      </i>
      INN), while a name that is still being considered is called a
      <b>
       pINN
      </b>
      (
      <i>
       proposed
      </i>
      INN).
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name_stems">
       Name stems
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Nonproprietary_Name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name stems">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Drugs from the same therapeutic or chemical class are usually given names with the same
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">
        stem
       </a>
      </i>
      . Stems are mostly placed word-finally, but in some cases word-initial stems are used. They are collected in a publication informally known as the
      <i>
       Stem Book
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StemBook_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-StemBook-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples are:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StemBook_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-StemBook-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        -anib
       </i>
       for angiogenesis inhibitors (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Pazopanib" title="Pazopanib">
        pazopanib
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -anserin
       </i>
       for serotonin receptor antagonists, especially
       <a href="/wiki/5-HT2_receptor" title="5-HT2 receptor">
        5-HT
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       antagonists (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Ritanserin" title="Ritanserin">
        ritanserin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mianserin" title="Mianserin">
        mianserin
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -arit
       </i>
       for antiarthritic agents (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Lobenzarit" title="Lobenzarit">
        lobenzarit
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -ase
       </i>
       for enzymes (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Asparaginase" title="Asparaginase">
        asparaginase
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -azepam
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzodiazepines" title="Benzodiazepines">
        benzodiazepines
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Diazepam" title="Diazepam">
        diazepam
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oxazepam" title="Oxazepam">
        oxazepam
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -caine
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_anaesthetics" title="Local anaesthetics">
        local anaesthetics
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Procaine" title="Procaine">
        procaine
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">
        cocaine
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -cain-
       </i>
       for class I
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiarrhythmics" title="Antiarrhythmics">
        antiarrhythmics
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Procainamide" title="Procainamide">
        procainamide
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -coxib
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclooxygenase-2" title="Cyclooxygenase-2">
        COX-2
       </a>
       inhibitors, a type of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatory_drug" title="Anti-inflammatory drug">
        anti-inflammatory drugs
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Celecoxib" title="Celecoxib">
        celecoxib
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -mab
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclonal_antibodies" title="Monoclonal antibodies">
        monoclonal antibodies
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Infliximab" title="Infliximab">
        infliximab
       </a>
       ); see
       <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_monoclonal_antibodies" title="Nomenclature of monoclonal antibodies">
        Nomenclature of monoclonal antibodies
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -navir
       </i>
       for antiretroviral
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protease_inhibitors" title="Protease inhibitors">
        protease inhibitors
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Darunavir" title="Darunavir">
        darunavir
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -olol
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_blocker" title="Beta blocker">
        beta blockers
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Atenolol" title="Atenolol">
        atenolol
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -pril
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACE_inhibitors" title="ACE inhibitors">
        ACE inhibitors
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Captopril" title="Captopril">
        captopril
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -sartan
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II_receptor_antagonist" title="Angiotensin II receptor antagonist">
        angiotensin II receptor antagonists
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Losartan" title="Losartan">

       </a>

      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -tinib
       </i>

        tyrosine kinase inhibitors
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Imatinib" title="Imatinib">
        imatinib
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -vastatin
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMG-CoA_reductase_inhibitor" title="HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor">
        HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors
       </a>
       , a group of cholesterol lowering agents (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Simvastatin" title="Simvastatin">
        simvastatin
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        -vir
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Antiviral_drug" title="Antiviral drug">
        antivirals
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Aciclovir" title="Aciclovir">

        ritonavir
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        arte-
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Artemisinin" title="Artemisinin">
        artemisinin
       </a>
       antimalarials (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Artemether" title="Artemether">
        artemether
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        cef-
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cefalosporin" title="Cefalosporin">
        cefalosporins
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Cefalexin" title="Cefalexin">
        cefalexin
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        io-
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">
        iodine
       </a>
       -containing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">
        radiopharmaceuticals
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Iobenguane" title="Iobenguane">
        iobenguane
       </a>
       )
      </li>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_discussion">
       Linguistic discussion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Nonproprietary_Name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Linguistic discussion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stems_and_roots">
       Stems and roots
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Nonproprietary_Name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stems and roots">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       stem
      </i>
      is not used consistently in
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      . It has been defined as a form to which
      <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
       affixes
      </a>
      (of any type) can be attached.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Under a different and apparently more common view, this is the definition of a
      <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
       root
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while a stem consists of the root plus optional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derivation_(linguistics)" title="Derivation (linguistics)">
       derivational
      </a>
      affixes, meaning that it is the part of a word to which
      <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
       inflectional
      </a>
      affixes are added.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      INN stems employ the first definition, while under the more common alternative they would be described as roots.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Translingual_communication">
       Translingual communication
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Nonproprietary_Name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Translingual communication">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">
       Pharmacology
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pharmacotherapy" title="Pharmacotherapy">
       pharmacotherapy
      </a>
      (like
      <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">
       health care
      </a>
      generally) are universally relevant around the world, making
      <a href="/wiki/Translingualism" title="Translingualism">
       translingual communication
      </a>
      about them an important goal. An
      <a href="/wiki/Interlinguistics" title="Interlinguistics">
       interlingual
      </a>
      perspective is thus useful in
      <a href="/wiki/Drug_nomenclature" title="Drug nomenclature">
       drug nomenclature
      </a>
      . The WHO issues INNs in English, Latin, French, Russian, Spanish, Arabic, and Chinese. A drug's INN names are often
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognate
      </a>
      across most or all of the languages, but they also allow small
      <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
       inflectional
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
       diacritic
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">
       transliterational
      </a>
      differences that are usually transparent and trivial for nonspeakers (as is true of most
      <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">
       international scientific vocabulary
      </a>
      ). For example, although "paracetamolum" (
      <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">
       la
      </a>
      ) has an inflectional difference from "
      <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">
       paracetamol
      </a>
      " (
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       en
      </a>
      ), and although "paractamol" (
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       fr
      </a>
      ) has a diacritic difference, the differences are trivial; users can easily recognize the "same word". And although "" (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       ru
      </a>
      ) and "paracetamol" (
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       en
      </a>
      ) have a transliterational difference, they sound similar, and for Russian speakers who can recognize
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
       Latin script
      </a>
      or English speakers who can recognize
      <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">
       Cyrillic script
      </a>
      , they look similar; users can recognize the "same word". Thus INNs make medicines bought anywhere in the world as easily identifiable as possible to people who do not speak that language. Notably, the "same word" principle allows health professionals and patients who do not speak the same language to communicate to some degree and to avoid potentially life-threatening confusions from drug interactions.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling_regularization">
       Spelling regularization
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Nonproprietary_Name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spelling regularization">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      A number of spelling changes are made to
      <a href="/wiki/British_Approved_Name" title="British Approved Name">
       British Approved Names
      </a>
      and other older nonproprietary names with an eye toward interlingual standardization of pronunciation across major languages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus a predictable spelling system, approximating
      <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_orthography" title="Phonemic orthography">
       phonemic orthography
      </a>
      , is used, as follows:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        ae
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        oe
       </i>
       is replaced by
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       (e.g. estradiol vs. oestradiol)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        ph
       </i>
       is replaced by
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       (e.g. amfetamine vs. amphetamine)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        th
       </i>
       is replaced by
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       (e.g. metamfetamine vs. methamphetamine)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       is replaced by
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       (e.g. aciclovir vs. acyclovir)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       are avoided where possible
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Names_for_radicals_and_groups_.28salts.2C_esters.2C_and_so_on.29">
       Names for radicals and groups (salts, esters, and so on)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Nonproprietary_Name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Names for radicals and groups (salts, esters, and so on)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many drugs are supplied as
      <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">
       salts
      </a>
      , with a cation and an anion. The way the INN system handles these is explained by the WHO at its "Guidance on INN" webpage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_innguidance_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHO_innguidance-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "For example, oxacillin and ibufenac are INN and their salts are named oxacillin sodium and ibufenac sodium. The latter are called modified INN (INNM)."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_innguidance_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHO_innguidance-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
