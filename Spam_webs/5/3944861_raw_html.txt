     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Psilocybe tampanensis
       </b>
      </i>
      is a very rare
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychedelic_mushroom" title="Psychedelic mushroom">
       psychedelic mushroom
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Strophariaceae" title="Strophariaceae">
       Strophariaceae
      </a>
      family. Originally collected in the wild in a sandy meadow near
      <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">
       Tampa, Florida
      </a>
      in 1977, the fungus has never again been reported in
      <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
       Florida
      </a>
      , but was later collected in
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">
       Mississippi
      </a>
      . The original Florida specimen was
      <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">
       cloned
      </a>
      , and descendants remain in wide circulation. The
      <a href="/wiki/Basidiocarp" title="Basidiocarp">
       fruit bodies
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">
       mushrooms
      </a>
      ) produced by the fungus are yellowish-brown in color with convex to conic
      <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(mycology)" title="Pileus (mycology)">
       caps
      </a>
      up to 2.4cm (0.9in) in diameter atop a thin
      <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">
       stem
      </a>
      up to 6cm (2.4in) long.
      <i>
       Psilocybe tampanensis
      </i>
      forms psychoactive
      <a href="/wiki/Truffle" title="Truffle">
       truffle
      </a>
      -like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclerotia" title="Sclerotia">
       sclerotia
      </a>
      that are known and sold under the nickname "philosopher's stones". The fruit bodies and sclerotia are consumed by some for
      <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">
       recreational
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Entheogen" title="Entheogen">
       entheogenic
      </a>
      purposes. In nature, sclerotia are produced by the fungus as a form of protection from
      <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">
       wildfires
      </a>
      and other natural disasters.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">
       Taxonomy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Psilocybe_tampanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The species was
      <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">
       described
      </a>
      scientifically by Steven H. Pollock and Mexican mycologist and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe" title="Psilocybe">
        Psilocybe
       </a>
      </i>
      authority
      <a href="/wiki/Gast%C3%B3n_Guzm%C3%A1n" title="Gastn Guzmn">
       Gastn Guzmn
      </a>
      in a 1978
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mycotaxon" title="Mycotaxon">
        Mycotaxon
       </a>
      </i>
      publication.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1978_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1978-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Stamets" title="Paul Stamets">
       Paul Stamets
      </a>
      , Pollock skipped a "boring taxonomic conference" near
      <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">
       Tampa, Florida
      </a>
      to go
      <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_hunting" title="Mushroom hunting">
       mushroom hunting
      </a>
      , and found a single specimen growing in a sand dune, which he did not recognize. Pollock later
      <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">
       cloned
      </a>
      the specimen and produced a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_culture" title="Pure culture">
       pure culture
      </a>
      , which remains widely distributed today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stamets1996_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stamets1996-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">
       type
      </a>
      specimen is kept at the
      <a href="/wiki/Herbarium" title="Herbarium">
       herbarium
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Polit%C3%A9cnico_Nacional" title="Instituto Politcnico Nacional">
       Instituto Politcnico Nacional
      </a>
      in Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1978_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1978-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Guzmn
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classification_(biology)" title="Classification (biology)">
       classified
      </a>
      <i>
       P.tampanensis
      </i>
      in his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Section_(biology)" title="Section (biology)">
       section
      </a>
      <i>
       Mexicanae
      </i>
      , a grouping of related
      <i>
       Psilocybe
      </i>
      species characterized primarily by having
      <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">
       spores
      </a>
      with lengths greater than 8
      <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">
       micrometers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1995_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1995-4">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Psilocybe_tampanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg/220px-Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg/330px-Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg/440px-Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Psilocybe.tampanensis.one.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Psilocybe tampanensis
        </i>
        fruit bodies and
        <a href="/wiki/Spore_print" title="Spore print">
         spore prints
        </a>
        of cultivated specimens
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(mycology)" title="Pileus (mycology)">
       cap
      </a>
      ranges in shape from
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convex" title="wikt:convex">
       convex
      </a>
      or conic with a slight
      <a href="/wiki/Umbo_(mycology)" title="Umbo (mycology)">
       umbo
      </a>
      , expanding in age to become flattened or with a slight central depression; it reaches diameters of 12.4cm (0.40.9in). The surface is smooth, not striate (grooved),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ochraceous" title="Ochraceous">
       ochraceous
      </a>
      brown to straw brown,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buff_(color)" title="Buff (color)">
       buff
      </a>
      to yellowish-grey when dry, with slight bluish tones at the margin,
      <a href="/wiki/Hygrophanous" title="Hygrophanous">
       hygrophanous
      </a>
      , and somewhat sticky when wet. The gills are more or less
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adnate" title="wikt:adnate">
       adnate
      </a>
      (broadly attached to the stem slightly above the bottom of the gill, with most of the gill fused to the stem) and brown to dark purple brown in color with lighter edges. The
      <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">
       stem
      </a>
      is 26cm (0.82.4in) long, 12mm (0.040.08in) thick, and equal in width throughout to slightly enlarged near the base. There are fibrils near the top of the stem. The
      <a href="/wiki/Partial_veil" title="Partial veil">
       partial veil
      </a>
      is cortinate (cobweb-like, similar to the partial veil of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cortinarius" title="Cortinarius">
        Cortinarius
       </a>
      </i>
      species), and soon disappears. The
      <a href="/wiki/Trama_(mycology)" title="Trama (mycology)">
       flesh
      </a>
      is whitish to yellowish, and bruises blue when injured. The taste and odor are slightly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farinaceous" title="Farinaceous">
       farinaceous
      </a>
      (similar to freshly ground flour).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1978_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1978-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1996_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1996-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="521" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg/220px-Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg/330px-Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg/440px-Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Psilocybe.tampanensis.spores.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Spores may appear somewhat rhombic to ellipsoid in shape, depending upon the angle from which they are viewed.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Spore_print" title="Spore print">
       spore print
      </a>
      is purple-brown. When viewed with a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_microscopy" title="Light microscopy">
       microscope
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">
       spores
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       P.tampanensis
      </i>
      are somewhat
      <a href="/wiki/Rhombus" title="Rhombus">
       rhombic
      </a>
      in face view and roughly elliptical in side view; they have dimensions of 8.89.9 by 88.8 by 5.56.6
      <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">
       m
      </a>
      . Spores appear brownish-yellow when mounted in a solution of
      <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">
       potassium hydroxide
      </a>
      , and have a thick, smooth wall, a distinct
      <a href="/wiki/Germ_pore" title="Germ pore">
       germ pore
      </a>
      , and a short appendage. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basidia" title="Basidia">
       basidia
      </a>
      (spore-bearing cells) are four-spored,
      <a href="/wiki/Hyaline" title="Hyaline">
       hyaline
      </a>
      (translucent), and measure 1422 by 810m. The cheilocystidia (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cystidia" title="Cystidia">
       cystidia
      </a>
      on the gill face) measure 1622 by 49m, and are
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lageniform" title="wikt:lageniform">
       lageniform
      </a>
      (flask-shaped) with
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Flexuous" title="wikt:Flexuous">
       flexous
      </a>
      thin necks that are 2.23m thick, and infrequently have irregular branches. There are no pleurocystidia (cystidia on the gill face).
      <a href="/wiki/Clamp_connection" title="Clamp connection">
       Clamp connections
      </a>
      are present in the
      <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">
       hyphae
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1978_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1978-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_species">
       Similar species
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Psilocybe_tampanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Similar species">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Guzmn considers
      <i>
       Psilocybe tampanensis
      </i>
      to be intermediate in form between
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe_mexicana" title="Psilocybe mexicana">
        P.mexicana
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psilocybe_caerulescens" title="Psilocybe caerulescens">
        P.caerulescens
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Psilocybe mexicana
      </i>
      has a more
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mycena" title="Mycena">
        Mycena
       </a>
      </i>
      -like fruit body shape, and longer basidia measuring 2224 by 7.711m. It is known only from Mexico and Guatemala.
      <i>
       Psilocybe caerulescens
      </i>
      , found in the USA and Venezuela, is also somewhat similar, but has a collybioid
      <a href="/wiki/Habit_(biology)" title="Habit (biology)">
       habit
      </a>
      (small to medium-sized mushrooms with a convex cap), with spores measuring 6.78 by 5.26.5 by 3.35.2m, and cheilocystidia that are 1522 by 4.45.5m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1978_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1978-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_distribution">
       Habitat and distribution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Psilocybe_tampanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Habitat and distribution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For almost two decades after its discovery,
      <i>
       Psilocybe tampanensis
      </i>
      was known only from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)" title="Type locality (biology)">
       type locality
      </a>
      , southeast of
      <a href="/wiki/Brandon,_Florida" title="Brandon, Florida">
       Brandon, Florida
      </a>
      . In 1996, Guzmn reported finding it in a meadow with sandy soil in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deciduous_forest" title="Deciduous forest">
       deciduous forest
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearl_River_County" title="Pearl River County">
       Pearl River County
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">
       Mississippi
      </a>
      , a habitat similar to that of the type location.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1996_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1996-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Due to its scarcity, however, its habitat preferences are not known with certainty.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beug2011_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beug2011-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Like all
      <i>
       Psilocybe
      </i>
      species, it is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saprobic" title="Saprobic">
       saprobic
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Like some other psychoactive grassland species such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe_semilanceata" title="Psilocybe semilanceata">
        Psilocybe semilanceata
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Conocybe_cyanopus" title="Conocybe cyanopus">
        Conocybe cyanopus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       P.tampanensis
      </i>
      can form
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclerotia" title="Sclerotia">
       sclerotia
      </a>
      a hardened mass of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycelia" title="Mycelia">
       mycelia
      </a>
      that is more resistant to adverse environmental conditions than normal mycelia. This
      <a href="/wiki/Truffle" title="Truffle">
       truffle
      </a>
      -like form gives the fungus some protection from wildfires and other natural disasters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stamets2000_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stamets2000-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other
      <i>
       Psilocybe
      </i>
      species known to produce sclerotia include
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe_mexicana" title="Psilocybe mexicana">
        Psilocybe mexicana
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psilocybe_caerulescens" title="Psilocybe caerulescens">
        Psilocybe caerulescens
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman1978_1-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guzman1978-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sclerotia are also produced when the species is grown in culture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stamets1983_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stamets1983-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recreational_use">
       Recreational use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Psilocybe_tampanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recreational use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Psilocybe tampanensis
      </i>
      contains the
      <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">
       psychedelic
      </a>
      compounds
      <a href="/wiki/Psilocin" title="Psilocin">
       psilocin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Psilocybin" title="Psilocybin">
       psilocybin
      </a>
      , and is consumed for
      <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">
       recreational
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Entheogen" title="Entheogen">
       entheogenic
      </a>
      purposes. The species was found to be one of the most popular psychoactive mushrooms confiscated by German authorities in a 2000 report, behind
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe_cubensis" title="Psilocybe cubensis">
        Psilocybe cubensis
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe_semilanceata" title="Psilocybe semilanceata">
        Psilocybe semilanceata
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Panaeolus_cyanescens" title="Panaeolus cyanescens">
        Panaeolus cyanescens
       </a>
      </i>
      . The alkaloid content in the confiscated samples ranged from not detectable to 0.19% psilocybin, and 0.01 to 0.03% psilocin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Musshoff2000_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Musshoff2000-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to mycologist Michael Beug, dried fruit bodies can contain up to 1% psilocybin and psilocin;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beug2011_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beug2011-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in terms of psychoactive
      <a href="/wiki/Potency_(pharmacology)" title="Potency (pharmacology)">
       potency
      </a>
      , Stamets considers the mushroom "moderately to highly active".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stamets1996_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stamets1996-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The psychoactive compounds are also present in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclerotia" title="Sclerotia">
       sclerotia
      </a>
      : in one analysis, the levels of psilocybin obtained from sclerotia ranged from 0.31% to 0.68% by
      <a href="/wiki/Dry_weight" title="Dry weight">
       dry weight
      </a>
      , and were dependent upon the composition of the
      <a href="/wiki/Growth_medium" title="Growth medium">
       growth medium
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gartz1994_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gartz1994-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sclerotia are sold under the nickname "philosopher's stones".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMCDDA_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EMCDDA-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They have been described as "resembling congealed
      <a href="/wiki/Muesli" title="Muesli">
       muesli
      </a>
      ", and having a somewhat bitter taste similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">
       walnut
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Honigsbaum2003_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Honigsbaum2003-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Strains existing as commercial cultivation kits sold originally in
      <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">
       countercultural
      </a>
      drug magazines are derived from the original fruit body found by Pollock in Florida.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ott1996_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ott1996-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Methods were originally developed by Pollock,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollock1979_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pollock1979-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and later extended by Stamets in the 1980s to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mushroom_cultivation" title="Mushroom cultivation">
       cultivate
      </a>
      the sclerotia on a
      <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(biology)" title="Substrate (biology)">
       substrate
      </a>
      of rye grass (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Lolium" title="Lolium">
        Lolium
       </a>
      </i>
      ), and on straw. Sclerotia prepared in this way take from 3 to 12weeks to develop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gartz1997_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gartz1997-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pollock was granted a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_patent_law" title="United States patent law">
       US patent
      </a>
      in 1981 for his method of producing sclerotia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollock1979_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pollock1979-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">
       Legal status
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Psilocybe_tampanensis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legal status">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Legal_status_of_psilocybin_mushrooms" title="Legal status of psilocybin mushrooms">
       Legal status of psilocybin mushrooms
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Psilocin and psilocybin are
      <a href="/wiki/Single_Convention_on_Narcotic_Drugs#Schedules_of_drugs" title="Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs">
       scheduled
      </a>
      drugs in many countries, and mushrooms containing them are prohibited by extension. In the United States,
      <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States#Levels_of_law" title="Law of the United States">
       Federal law
      </a>
      was passed in 1971 that put the psychoactive components into the most restricted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schedule_I_(US)#Schedule_I_drugs" title="Schedule I (US)">
       schedule I
      </a>
      category. For about three decades following this, several European countries remained relatively tolerant of mushroom use and possession. In the 2000s (decade), in response to increases in prevalence and availability, all European countries banned possession or sale of psychedelic mushrooms;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMCDDA_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EMCDDA-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Netherlands was the last country to enact such laws in 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marley2010_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Marley2010-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, they did not include psilocybin-containing sclerotia in the 2008 law, and thus, psilocybin-containing fungal compounds are available commercially in the Netherlands.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amsterdam2011_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Amsterdam2011-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In parallel legal developments in Asia,
      <i>
       P.tampanensis
      </i>
      was one of 13 psychoactive mushrooms specifically prohibited by law in Japan in 2002.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonmori2009_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gonmori2009-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
