      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Can Hedge Fund Billionaire Paul Tudor Jones Save America's Public Education System?
      </h1>
      <div class="magazine_issue_date">
       This story appears in the December 2, 2013 issue of Forbes.
      </div>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         If the folkloric character of Robin Hood actually existed, hed be hard-pressed to find any place better to ply his trade than Greenwich, Conn., perhaps the wealthiest town in the world. It happens to be the home of the
         <a href="https://www.tudorfunds.com/">
          Tudor Investment Corp.
         </a>
         , a $13 billion hedge fund run by the energetic 59-year-old
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/paul-tudor-jones-ii/">
          Paul Tudor Jones II
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         You can literally see the money being made. Sitting in his office, in a crisp white shirt and a tie flecked with navy blue and orange (the colors of his alma mater, the University of Virginia), Jones is surrounded by a series of Peter Beard photographs, yet to be hung, and a more interactive kind of art dancing above his head: His computer projects his funds market positions onto the wall, blinking when any share changes price, its overall performance channeled into a moving graph (which just so happens, as with most days for the past few decades, to be pointing up). I sit here and watch these all day, says Jones, describing his daily work routine.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 330px">
         <img alt="" class=" " src="http://b-i.forbesimg.com/monteburke/files/2013/11/1112_paul-tudor-jones-robin-hood_400x280.jpg"/>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Photo by Matthew Furman
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         But on this day, like many others before it, hes less concerned about making tens of millions of dollars than figuring out how to disperse them. Greenwich, with no little irony, houses a real-life, modern-day Prince of Thieves, whose
         <a href="http://www.robinhood.org/">
          Robin Hood Foundation
         </a>
         , in its quest to combat domestic poverty, has spent the past 25 years practicing a venture philanthropy philosophy that has reshaped charitable aid. Unlike the foundations namesake, Jones doesnt actually rob the rich nor merely hand over money and goods to the poor. Instead, with a traders intensity, Jones solicits donationsclose to $1.5 billion and countingfrom the financial elite, led by fellow hedge funders, and then deploys that cash on behalf of the poor of New York City in a quantitative, results-based manner. In essence, he runs his foundation like he runs his hedge fund.
        </p>
        <p>
         Jones, when he talks about Robin Hoodand when hes really getting to a pointhas the habit of closing his eyes, making him look like an evangelical minister getting to the good part of a sermon. When he was growing up in Memphis, his mother always told him that one day hed be a preacher. I think she said that because I talked so much, says Jones, in an accent that still carries a heavy Tennessee flavor.
        </p>
        <p>
         Jones has been clenching those eyes tighter lately. After a quarter-century combating poverty, he has decided to change Robin Hoods tactics. Weve been fighting poverty for 25 years through homeless shelters, food banks, job training, teenage pregnancy centers and charter schools, he says. But the solution to the problem was right there in front of us. The breakdown of our public education system is the single largest threatinternal or externalthat we have in this country.
        </p>
        <p>
         Its no small task for one man, through one organization, to undertake, even one with his wealth (currently estimated at $3.7 billion) and reputation in the charity world. Robin Hood will need to raise more money; it will need to influence tens of billions of dollars in public money; it will, after all these years, have to start getting involved in the public policy process. And Jones himselfno fan of media attentionwill be forced to become more of a public figure. With a preachers certainty of mission, he says he has no choice. The only reason Im working right now is so I can continue my philanthropy, so I can be financially empowered to make a difference, he says. I certainly dont want any more money for myself.
        </p>
        <p>
         **
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          JONES ALTRUISTIC STREAK
         </strong>
         stems from one of his earliest memories, an incident when he was 4 years old and got separated from his mother in an open-air market in Memphis. I was crying my eyes out when this very old black man came and took my hand and told me not to worry, says Jones. The two searched the market until they found his mother. She was crying from relief when she saw us. She offered the man five dollars, he says. The man refused and said, Id do that for anybody, and I know youd do that for my child, too.? In retrospectespecially since it occurred in the South during the racially charged 1950sJones says that moment shaped his view of philanthropy, which he defines as any act of kindness at any point during the day by anyone to everyone.
        </p>
        <p>
         Jones acts of kindness are focused on the city where he made his professional mark. An economics degree in hand, Jones came to New York in 1976. The college boxing champion gravitated to Wall Street trading, which was then still a physical game plied aggressively in the exchange pits. He began as a clerk, trading cotton commodities, and then proved something of a prodigy at E. F. Hutton. In 1980, at 26, he started Tudor Investment, which has grown to $13 billion under management, led by the flagship Tudor BVI Global Fund, which has had a 19% average annual return. Around that time Jones began visiting the Brooklyn neighborhood of Bedford-Stuyvesant, then among the poorest places in New York City. It was a very dangerous area, where there were homeless people, alcoholics, drug addicts all living in squalor in these burned-out buildings, says Jones. It was such a forgotten world, just a few miles away from the extraordinary wealth and power in Manhattan. The disparity is worse now but was more visible then.
        </p>
        <p>
         In 1986 Jones decided to try to help. Paul was a confirmed bachelor at the time, a weary bachelor really, says his friend and fellow hedgie
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/louis-bacon/">
          Louis Bacon
         </a>
         . I think he was really looking for meaning in his life. Jones, now married with four children, admits to a self-interested motivation, in the sense that it brings me so much joy, theres no doubt I get huge selfish pleasure from doing good. He cites Jesus as an inspiration. Im a Christian but a very poor one, he says. If you were judging me on the Ten Commandments, Id be a complete failure.
        </p>
        <p>
         That year Jones adopted an underfunded and underperforming school in Bedford-Stuyvesant as part of the
         <a href="http://www.ihaveadreamfoundation.org/html/">
          I Have A Dream Foundation
         </a>
         , an influential program started by Eugene M. Lang that aimed to motivate grade-schoolers (and their parents) by guaranteeing full college scholarships to any of them who graduated from high school, mentoring them along the way. Jones had hoped to graduate 90% of the kids, but the final number was closer to 33%. I had no idea what I was doing, he says. I vastly underestimated both the academic and social challenges facing these kids. I had the energy and desire and wallet to help them, but I was completely ill-equipped to do so in an efficient fashion.
        </p>
        <p>
         Then came 1987. By shorting the market before the October crash, Jones became a Wall Street legend, and the 201% gain for his fund put him on the path to billions. He actually believed the crash was just the beginning of something much worse. I thought we were going into a depression, and I thought, Oh, my God, if it gets bad here, it will be five times worse in Bed-Stuy. 
        </p>
        <p>
         So Jones hatched his idea for a foundation that fought poverty in New York City but in a different way than had ever been done before. It would treat the poverty problem like a business one, using due diligence, well-thought-out investments, constant monitoring of said investments, and strict accountability and measurements of outcomes. If expectations were not met, funding was discontinued. Every year Robin Hood drops around 10% of its grantees. Jones and the board pledged to cover all administrative costs, so that literally every penny donated by others would go to the fieldwork.
        </p>
        <p>
         It amounted to a completely novel way to approach charitable giving and one that also had special appeal to Jones hedge fund communitynot a group of folks known for charitable works. The Robin Hood board today consists of the likes of Barry Sternlicht of Starwood Capital Group (currently Robin Hoods chairman),
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/david-einhorn/">
          David Einhorn
         </a>
         of Greenlight Capital and the embattled
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/steve-cohen/">
          Steven A. Cohen
         </a>
         of SAC Capital Advisors. The hedge fund business is a copycat business in the first place, and I think a lot of younger guys, when they first make their money, look to Paul and pattern themselves after him, says Bacon, who has contributed nearly $1 million to Robin Hood. One of those younger guys is Lee Ainslie, the head of Maverick Capital, who has been on the Robin Hood board for 12 years. People donate to charity either for reasons of the heart or the head, he says. I think we appeal on both levels. We try to convince people that if you do want to help, if you think this cause is important, then the most effective use of your dollar is a donation to Robin Hood. The message has resonated. During the depth of the financial crisis in 2008, Robin Hoods annual gala raised $56 million in one night. This years galathe crowd of 4,200 at the Javits Center was entertained by such stars as Sting and Jerry Seinfeldhauled in $81 million. And its not just the wealthy. Some 300,000 individuals gave to the foundation last year.
        </p>
        <p>
         **
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          BY MOST MEASURES
         </strong>
         Robin Hood has been hugely successful. The foundation donates to more than 200 charities in New York City. Its helped build 62 libraries in public schools. In the late 1980s it started one of the citys first needle-exchange programsin the face of much controversywhich helped get the AIDS epidemic under control. The foundation organized relief efforts after Sept. 11 and Hurricane Sandy. And all of the afterschool programs, day care, pre-and postnatal-care educational classes have had positive effects on thousands who might not have otherwise ever been helped.
        </p>
        <p>
         But even with all of the successes, something nagged at Jones. He wondered: Could they do a better job? Leading up to Robin Hoods 25th anniversary, he organized a meeting for the entire staff and board. It was one of those kumbaya, touchy-feely exercises but something thats really important to do periodically, says Jones. They met at the offices of McKinsey &amp; Co. in midtown Manhattan and came up with three main goals for the next decade: to close the achievement gap between the poorest and the richest, to lift 1 million people out of poverty during that time and to impact $10 billion in public funding. Then they split into three different groups to try to figure out just how they could accomplish those goals.
        </p>
        <p>
         Thats when the lightbulb went on for Jones. All three groups came back and hit on the same thing: We need to be investing in the future, in kids today. And that means improving public education, at scale. Robin Hood has, of course, been involved in education from the start, including the 2005 funding of a charter school in Bedford-Stuyvesant that became the number-one-ranked elementary school in the city. But Jones says this realization marked a monumental shift for the foundation. Its important that we did those things, and we will continue to support them, he says. We proved with the charter school that the achievement gap was a myth, that with the right schools, kids from the poorest neighborhoods could do every bit as well as kids from the richest ones. But what I realized is that we werent having enough of an impact. The afterschool programs were Band-Aids. The charter schools were demonstration pieces. We needed to fix something that was completely broken: the public school system.
        </p>
        <p>
         And though he intends to begin with the New York City school system, Jones clearly had a bigger goal in mind. The U.S. is in the bottom quartile for developed countries in terms of educational outcomes for kids. Whats going on with low-income kids and education is about the highest expression of racism that weve ever probably experienced as a country, with the possible exception of slavery. For whatever reason, we are desensitized to it now.
        </p>
        <p>
         Jones likens this to John F. Kennedys call to land a man on the moon. 
         <a href="http://www.corestandards.org/">
          Common Core
         </a>
         [a set of national educational standards] was our Sputnik moment, when we found out that our kids arent measuring up to kids in other developed countries, he says. We are losing the space race. This time the space is between our kids ears. Jones says his goal is to get the U.S. educational system in the top quartile of developed countries in the next ten years. Twenty years from now he wants the U.S. at number one. (The irony: Jones says he himself got very little out of his own education, with the exception of a journalism class. My B.A. in economics was zero help in my profession today. Instead, he says that countless hours playing games during his school yearspoker, chess, backgammonwere the experiences that prepared me for what I do today.)
        </p>
        <p>
         Jones says that Robin Hood will bring its full force and weight financially to the public education fight. The foundation will do what it can on its own. It has seeded a school for New York City teacher training in which each teacher must run a classroom while pursuing a masters degree. True to Robin Hood form, the teachers earn no degree unless their students attain a certain level of academic achievement.
        </p>
        <p>
         But Jones knows that private philanthropy alone cant even begin to solve the problem. (The New York City public school system costs $22 billion a year.) Which means a new tactic for Robin Hood: leveraging its influence to effect public policy. Were not going to start a super PAC or anything, but we will be deeply involved, says Jones.
        </p>
        <p>
         Jones says this will entail a vertically integrated approach at getting all stakeholders in thisthe parents, students and teachersto acknowledge the problem, then get involved in this transformation, by working with schools, teachers and parent-teacher associations in applying the Robin Hood method of best practices. That means, among other things, longer school days and years, better teacher and principal training and true evaluation and accountability.
        </p>
        <p>
         The process for Robin Hood will begin with a battle that may be the most difficult: convincing the mayor-elect of New York City, Bill de Blasio, to buy in. I dont think he fully understands education. I dont think hes fully knowledgeable yet, says Jones. Our job will be to get him fully up to speed. There will be an unending stream of concerned citizens from every level of society going through his door demanding and ensuring that we implement best practices. The pressure will be unrelenting, never-ending, and there will be no compromise.
        </p>
        <p>
         The second front: teachers. One thing we are for is a complete change in the way teachers are viewed socially, politically and economically in this country, he says. We dont value the importance of teachers the way we have to in order to change our educational outcomes. One way to make better teachers, Jones argues, is to reform the way teachers unions work in education. Whats happened in the last 20 years in many of the large cities in the U.S. is that the educational system has been dictated by the unions, he says. They have failed. They get an F. What we have now is a failure in the way the teaching corps is organized, administered and deployed. Robin Hood will start this process in New York City during the teachers union contract negotiations next spring. We will be there, and we will be loud and demonstrative, he says. (Jones is not completely against teachers unions and prefers to work with them. He
         <a href="http://www.bizjournals.com/denver/news/2013/10/16/ednews-colorado-key-group-backing.html">
          recently gave
         </a>
         $125,000 to a union-led effort to increase school funding via a tax increase in Colorado.) The goals Paul and Robin Hood have set are incredibly ambitious, but theyre achievableif we confront the challenge head-on and dont shy away from the toughest issues, says
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/michael-bloomberg/">
          Michael Bloomberg
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         The new effort does come with some peril, at least to Jones personally. He will be forced to take a step out of his comfort zone. The most common refrain in any story about him is this single line: Jones was unavailable for comment. He is a media-shy man who has felt the heat at times when he has stepped into the spotlight. The first time was in the aftermath of the 1987 PBS documentary
         <em>
          Trader
         </em>
         , which featured a then 32-year-old Jones aggressively making trades and reveling in the high-octane world of trading of that era. Jones reportedly hated the way he was portrayed in the movie, and he effectively went underground for years afterwards. In the 1990s, when his
         <a href="http://www.evergladesfoundation.org/">
          Everglades Foundation
         </a>
         led the fight to protect the national park (Jones, an avid hunter and fisherman, has also been a leader in conservation philanthropy), his opponents brought to light Jones $2 million fine for filling in wetlands at his Chesapeake Bay estate. (Jones said he got erroneous advice from his contractor, who was eventually sent to prison.) Most recently, at a talk at the University of Virginia, Jones got into hot water when he argued that women lose their efficacy as traders after they have children (he has since apologized profusely).
        </p>
        <p>
         Now, of course, with Robin Hoods new initiative, everything will be fair game for his opponents. They will use the plutocrat angle to make sure he doesnt educate poor kids the way he educated his own kids, says Bacon, sardonically. Jones says hes ready: We will do whatever is necessary. This is too important. The rest of the country, the world, will be paying attention.
        </p>
