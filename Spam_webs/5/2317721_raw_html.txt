   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Plural form of words ending in -us
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English language
      </a>
      , the
      <b>
       plural form of words ending in
       <i>
        -us
       </i>
      </b>
      , especially those derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      , replaces
      <i>
       -us
      </i>
      with
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      . Many exceptions exist, some because the word does not derive from Latin, and others due to habit (for example,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">
        campus
       </a>
      </i>
      , plural
      <i>
       campuses
      </i>
      ). Conversely, some non-Latin words ending in
      <i>
       -us
      </i>
      and Latin words that did not have their Latin plurals with
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      form their English plurals with
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      . Between these extremes are words that do not justify a Latin plural on
      <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
       etymological
      </a>
      grounds, but that native English speakers commonly pluralize with
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      (for example,
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      as a plural for
      <a href="#Octopus">
       octopus
      </a>
      ). Whether to regard these alternative plural forms as incorrect depends on one's position in the ongoing debate over
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
       prescriptivism
      </a>
      versus
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_description" title="Linguistic description">
       descriptivism
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Language_education" title="Language education">
       language education
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The -us singular form with an -i plural comes from Latin. However, the morphology of Latin nouns is complex and not every Latin word ending in -us pluralized in -i. The ones that did largely were from the
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension#Second_declension_.28o.29" title="Latin declension">
       second declension
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
       masculine
      </a>
      . Briefly, a
      <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
       declension
      </a>
      is the way a noun changes to reflect facts about the object to which it refers (e.g., its gender or number) or the relationship that the noun has to other words in the sentence. Remnants of the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
       Old English
      </a>
      declension system can be seen in words like
      <i>
       I
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       me
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       we
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       us
      </i>
      in modern English, as well as (more distantly) in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/English_possessive" title="English possessive">
        's
       </a>
      </i>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enclitic" title="Enclitic">
       enclitic
      </a>
      . In Latin, just as in many languages spoken today, a word having several forms is the rule rather than the exception. Most words indicate their declension (including their
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
       number
      </a>
      , i.e. whether they are singular or plural) using an
      <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
       affix
      </a>
      , much as we use
      <i>
       's
      </i>
      today to indicate possession and (usually)
      <i>
       -s
      </i>
      to indicate plurality. Specifically, the
      <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
       nominative
      </a>
      singular form of second declension masculine nouns is marked with
      <i>
       -us
      </i>
      , and the nominative plural with
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      . More at
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_grammar" title="Latin grammar">
       Latin grammar
      </a>
      . Confusion arises because some Latin words ending in -us would not have pluralized with -i. Examples of regularly declined nouns include third declension neuters, such as
      <i>
       opus
      </i>
      , with the plural
      <i>
       opera
      </i>
      , and fourth declension masculine and feminine, such as
      <i>
       sinus
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       tribus
      </i>
      , with plurals
      <i>
       sins
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       tribs
      </i>
      . Some idiosyncratic instances are
      <i>
       bus
      </i>
      , a curtailed form of
      <i>
       omnibus
      </i>
      , a dative plural, and
      <i>
       ignoramus
      </i>
      , a verb.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Virus">
       Virus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Virus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The English plural of
      <i>
       virus
      </i>
      is
      <i>
       viruses
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In most speaking communities, this is non-controversial and speakers would not attempt to use the non-standard plural in -i. However, in computer enthusiast circles in the late 20th century and early 21st, the non-standard
      <i>
       viri
      </i>
      form (sometimes even
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      ) was well-attested, generally in the context of computer viruses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While the number of users employing these non-standard plural forms of
      <i>
       virus
      </i>
      was always a proportionally small percentage of the English-speaking population, the variation was notable because it coincided with the growth of the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
       Internet
      </a>
      , a medium on which users of
      <i>
       viri
      </i>
      were over-represented. As the distribution of Internet users shifted to be more representative of the population as a whole during the 2000s, the non-standard forms saw decline in usage. A tendency towards prescriptivism in the computer enthusiast community, combined with the growing awareness that
      <i>
       viri
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      are not etymologically supported plural forms, also played a part.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nonetheless, the question of what the Latin plural of
      <i>
       virus
      </i>
      would have been turns out not to be straightforward, as no plural form is attested in extant Latin literature. Furthermore, its unusual status as a neuter noun ending in -us apparently not of Greek origin obscures its morphology, making guesses about how it should have been declined difficult.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_noun_in_Latin">
       Mass noun in Latin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mass noun in Latin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Latin word
      <i>
       vrus
      </i>
      (the
      <i>
       
      </i>
      indicates a
      <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">
       long
      </a>
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      ) means "poison; venom", denoting the venom of a snake. This Latin word is probably related to the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       ios
      </i>
      ) meaning "venom" or "rust" and the
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       viam
      </i>
      meaning "toxic, poison".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since
      <i>
       vrus
      </i>
      in antiquity denoted something uncountable, it was a
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_noun" title="Mass noun">
       mass noun
      </a>
      . Mass nounssuch as
      <i>
       air
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       rice
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       helpfulness
      </i>
      in Englishpluralize only under special circumstances, hence the non-existence of plural forms in the texts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is unclear how a plural might have been formed under Latin grammar if the word had acquired a meaning requiring a plural form. In Latin
      <i>
       vrus
      </i>
      is generally regarded as a neuter of the second
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
       declension
      </a>
      , but neuter second declension nouns ending in
      <i>
       -us
      </i>
      (rather than
      <i>
       -um
      </i>
      ) are rare enough that inferring rules is difficult. (One of the rare attested plurals,
      <i>
       pelage
      </i>
      as a plural of
      <i>
       pelagus
      </i>
      , is borrowed from Greek, so does not give guidance for the
      <i>
       virus
      </i>
      .) Plural neuter nouns of other declensions always end in
      <i>
       -a
      </i>
      (in the nominative, accusative and vocative), but even if we were to apply this rule to
      <i>
       vrus
      </i>
      , it would be conjecture whether this should give us
      <i>
       vra
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       vrua
      </i>
      , or something else. There simply is no known plural for this word in
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
       Classical Latin
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Neo-Latin, a plural form is necessary in order to express the modern concept of viruses. Dictionaries such as
      <i>
       Whitaker's Words
      </i>
      therefore treat it as a second-declension noun with the following fairly ordinary forms:

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treating_v.C4.ABrus_as_2nd_declension_masculine">
       Treating
       <i>
        vrus
       </i>
       as 2nd declension masculine
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treating vrus as 2nd declension masculine">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      If
      <i>
       vrus
      </i>
      were a masculine second declension term like
      <i>
       alumnus
      </i>
      , it would be correct to use
      <i>
       vr
      </i>
      as its plural. However, it is not a masculine second declension term, as was explained.
     </p>
     <p>
      There does exist a Latin word
      <i>
       vir
      </i>
      , meaning "men" (the plural of
      <i>
       vir
      </i>
      , a second declension masculine noun), but it has a short
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      in the first syllable. The difference in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vowel_quantity" title="Vowel quantity">
       vowel quantity
      </a>
      is reflected in the different pronunciations of the (Latin-derived) English words
      <i>
       virile
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       viral
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The form
      <i>
       vri
      </i>
      is impossible as a plural of
      <i>
       vrus
      </i>
      since we only find the ending
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      in the plural form of masculine and feminine words ending in
      <i>
       -ius
      </i>
      . For instance,
      <i>
       radius
      </i>
      is pluralized by removing
      <i>
       -us
      </i>
      , to isolate the stem
      <i>
       radi
      </i>
      , and then adding the plural suffix
      <i>
       -
      </i>
      . Thus the
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      ending of the resulting word
      <i>
       radi
      </i>
      is not a suffix: it is simply the consequence of adding the actual suffix
      <i>
       
      </i>
      to a stem that has an
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      as its last letter. An example of this in English is adding the suffix
      <i>
       ing
      </i>
      to the word
      <i>
       ski
      </i>
      , resulting in
      <i>
       skiing
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Vri
      </i>
      would be the plural form of the putative, nonexistent word
      <i>
       vrius
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_nouns_in_English">
       Latin nouns in English
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Latin nouns in English">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Even if the Latin plural were known, English speakers would not be obliged to use it. Examples of Latin loanwords into English which have regular English plurals in -(e)s include
      <i>
       campus
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       bonus
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       anus
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       cancer
      </i>
      . These stand beside counterexamples such as
      <i>
       radius
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       radii
      </i>
      ) and
      <i>
       alumnus
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       alumni
      </i>
      ). Still other words are commonly used with either one:
      <i>
       corpus
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       corpora
      </i>
      , or sometimes
      <i>
       corpuses
      </i>
      ),
      <i>
       formula
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       formulae
      </i>
      in technical contexts,
      <i>
       formulas
      </i>
      in non-technical usage).
     </p>
     <p>
      As an indication of usage,
      <i>
       viruses
      </i>
      appears in the official Scrabble words list,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but neither
      <i>
       viri
      </i>
      nor
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      does. Similarly, the spellchecker built into the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" title="Mozilla Firefox">
       Mozilla Firefox
      </a>
      browser
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      accepts
      <i>
       viruses
      </i>
      but neither
      <i>
       viri
      </i>
      nor
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_form_virii">
       Use of the form
       <i>
        virii
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use of the form virii">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Usage of
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      within Internet communities has met with some resistance, most notably by Tom Christiansen, a figure in the
      <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">
       Perl
      </a>
      community, who researched the issue and wrote what eventually became referred to in various online discussions as the authoritative essay on the subject,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christiansen_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Christiansen-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      favoring
      <i>
       viruses
      </i>
      instead of
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      . The impetus of this discussion was the potential irony that the use of
      <i>
       virii
      </i>
      could be construed as a claim of superior knowledge of language when in fact more detailed research finds the naive
      <i>
       viruses
      </i>
      is actually more appropriate. (See
      <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
       hypercorrection
      </a>
      .)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">
       Biology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In life sciences, "viruses" generally refers to several distinct strains or species of virus. "Virus" is used in the original way as an uncountable mass noun, e.g. "a vial of virus". Individual, physical particles are called "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virion" title="Virion">
       virions
      </a>
      " or simply "virus particles".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Octopus">
       Octopus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Octopus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are three plural forms of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">
        octopus
       </a>
      </i>
      :
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [ktpsz]
      </span>
      ,
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [ktpa]
      </span>
      , and
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [ktpdiz]
      </span>
      . Currently,
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      is the most common form in the UK as well as the US;
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      is rare, and
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      is often objectionable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      lists
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      (in that order); it labels
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      "rare", and notes that
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      derives from the mistaken assumption that
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        octps
       </span>
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension#Second_declension_.28o.29" title="Latin declension">
       second declension Latin noun
      </a>
      , which it is not. Rather, it is (Latinized)
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Ancient Greek
      </a>
      , from
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        oktpous
       </span>
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      ), gender masculine, whose plural is
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        oktpodes
       </span>
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      ). If the word were native to Latin, it would be
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        octps
       </span>
      </i>
      ('eight-foot') and the plural
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        octpedes
       </span>
      </i>
      , analogous to
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        centipedes
       </span>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        mllipedes
       </span>
      </i>
      , as the plural form of
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        ps
       </span>
      </i>
      ('foot') is
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        pedes
       </span>
      </i>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">
       modern Greek
      </a>
      , it is called
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        khtapdi
       </span>
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      ), gender neuter, with plural form
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        khtapdia
       </span>
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Chambers 21st Century Dictionary
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <i>
       Compact Oxford Dictionary
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      list only
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      , although the latter notes that
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      is "still occasionally used"; the
      <a href="/wiki/British_National_Corpus" title="British National Corpus">
       British National Corpus
      </a>
      has 29 instances of
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      , 11 of
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      and 4 of
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Merriam-Webster 11th Collegiate Dictionary
      </i>
      lists
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      , in that order;
      <i>
       Webster's New World College Dictionary
      </i>
      lists
      <i>
       octopuses
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      (in that order).
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fowler%27s_Modern_English_Usage" title="Fowler's Modern English Usage">
        Fowler's Modern English Usage
       </a>
      </i>
      states that "the only acceptable plural in English is
      <i>
       octopuses,
      </i>
      " and that
      <i>
       octopi
      </i>
      is misconceived and
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedantic" title="Pedantic">
       pedantic
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       octopod
      </i>
      (plural
      <i>
       octopods
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       octopodes
      </i>
      ) is taken from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomic_order" title="Taxonomic order">
       taxonomic order
      </a>
      Octopoda but has no classical equivalent. The collective form
      <i>
       octopus
      </i>
      is usually reserved for animals consumed for food.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Platypus">
       Platypus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Platypus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The situation with the word
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">
        platypus
       </a>
      </i>
      is similar to that of
      <i>
       octopus
      </i>
      ; the word is etymologically Greek despite its Latinized ending, and so pluralizing it as if it were Latin (i.e. as
      <i>
       platypi
      </i>
      ) is ill-considered. As with
      <i>
       octopus
      </i>
      , importing Greek morphology into English would have
      <i>
       platypodes
      </i>
      as the plural, but in practice this form is not well-attested. In scientific contexts biologists often use
      <i>
       platypus
      </i>
      as both the singular and plural form of the word, in the tradition of
      <i>
       sheep
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       fish
      </i>
      , but laypersons and scientists alike often use the simple English plural
      <i>
       platypuses
      </i>
      . There is no consensus on which of these two is correct, with different dictionaries making different recommendations.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_Latin">
       Botanical Latin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Botanical Latin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As a word in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanical_Latin" title="Botanical Latin">
       Botanical Latin
      </a>
      (as distinct from
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
       Classical Latin
      </a>
      ),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">
        cactus
       </a>
      </i>
      follows standard Latin rules for pluralization and becomes
      <i>
       cacti
      </i>
      , which has become the prevalent usage in English. Regardless,
      <i>
       cactus
      </i>
      is popularly used as both singular and plural, and is cited as both singular and plural.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Cactuses
      </i>
      is also an acceptable plural in English.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Facetious_formations">
       Facetious formations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plural_form_of_words_ending_in_-us&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Facetious formations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Facetious mock-erudite plurals in
      <i>
       -i
      </i>
      or even
      <i>
       -ii
      </i>
      are sometimes found for words ending with a sound (vaguely) similar to
      <i>
       -us
      </i>
      . Examples are
      <i>
       stewardi
      </i>
      (supposed plural of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_Attendant" title="Flight Attendant">
        stewardess
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and
      <i>
       Elvii
      </i>
      (as a plural for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">
        Elvis
       </a>
      </i>
      imitators). The Toyota corporation has determined that their
      <i>
       Prius
      </i>
      model should have the plural form
      <i>
       Prii
      </i>
      , even though the Latin word
      <i>
       prius
      </i>
      has a plural
      <i>
       priora
      </i>
      , the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Lada_Priora" title="Lada Priora">
        Lada Priora
       </a>
      </i>
      having prior claim to that namethough the common plural is "Priuses".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Winklevoss_twins" title="Winklevoss twins">
       Winklevoss twins
      </a>
      are sometimes collectively referred to as "the Winklevii".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>

        </span>
