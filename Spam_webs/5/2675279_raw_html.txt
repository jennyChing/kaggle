        <h1 class="entry-title">
         FTSE 100 NOT at a new high. And, if it was, I really would not care
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <a class="author" href="http://moneystepper.com/author/admin/" rel="author">
          moneystepper
         </a>
         /
         <a class="entry-date" href="http://moneystepper.com/investing/the-markets-not-new-high-after-inflation/">
          February 28, 2014
         </a>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">

        <h1 style="margin: 0px 0px 30px 0px; border-bottom: 1px solid black; font-size: 22px;">
         <strong>
          The FTSE 100 is not at a new high
         </strong>
        </h1>
        <p>
         All of the mainstream media reported on Monday 24
         <sup>
          th
         </sup>
         February that the FTSE 100 has reached a 14 year high:
        </p>
        <p>
         FTSE 100 hits 14-year closing high after flurry of takeover action
         <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/business/blog/2014/feb/24/g20s-2tn-growth-pledge-fails-to-cheer-markets-business-live">
           guardian
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         FTSE 100 set to hit new record as investors await 14bn boost from Vodafone deal 
         <a href="http://www.thisismoney.co.uk/money/markets/article-2565602/FTSE-100-set-hit-new-record.html">
          thisismoney
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         FTSE 100 climbs to 14-year high
         <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/finance/markets/10658771/FTSE-100-climbs-to-14-year-high.html">
           The Telegraph
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Strong Vodafone puts FTSE in grasp of all-time high
         <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2014/02/21/markets-britain-stocks-idUSL6N0LQ12420140221">
           Reuters
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Not one of these articles mentions inflation or the fact that these are nominal highs. As ever, reporting half of the facts makes it much easier to grab the interest of the British public. However, dont fear geeks, Im here to bore you with the truth!!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h2 style="margin: 0px 0px 30px 0px; border-bottom: 1px solid black; font-size: 18px;">
         <strong>
          The FTSE 100 hits 14-year NOMINAL high
         </strong>
        </h2>
        <p>
         First of all, lets examine the claim:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high.jpg">
          <img alt="FTSE 100 hits a new high" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1727" height="535" src="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high.jpg" width="1018"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         So, the market closed on Monday 24
         <sup>
          th
         </sup>
         February at 6865.86. Indeed, this is its highest closing value since December 30, 1999, when it closed at 6930.20.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, this doesnt include inflation. One share of the FTSE 100 would have cost 68.66 in Feb 2014, and 69.30 in Dec 1999. However, 69 in 2014 is certainly not worth the same as it was in 1999 due to inflation.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h2 style="margin: 0px 0px 30px 0px; border-bottom: 1px solid black; font-size: 18px;">
         <strong>
          FTSE 100 inflation adjusted from today
         </strong>
        </h2>
        <p>
         If we take today (Feb 2014) and work backwards with inflation, we obtain the following inflation adjusted graph:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-2.jpg">
          <img alt="FTSE 100 hits a new high 2" height="351" src="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-2.jpg" width="580"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Mondays price of 6865.30 was a 14-year
         <b>
          NOMINAL
         </b>
         high. However, it was only a 5-year inflation adjusted (or
         <b>
          REAL)
         </b>
         high. Its called Real for a reason Mr. Media, because its real
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-3.jpg">
          <img alt="FTSE 100 hits a new high 3" height="351" src="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-3.jpg" width="580"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          The FTSE 100 is currently 26% below the REAL high from 1999
         </b>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Not really as sexy for a headline, is it?
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h2 style="margin: 0px 0px 30px 0px; border-bottom: 1px solid black; font-size: 18px;">
         <strong>
          Do I care if the FTSE 100 has hit a nominal high?
         </strong>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Im not sure I do. As you can see from the graph, the nominal price of the market (red line) goes upwards! Imagine that I invested 10,000 at new highs between April 1984 and today.
        </p>
        <p>
         Since April 1984, the market has reached new highs in 66 months.
        </p>
        <p>
         For example purposes, I will choose 5 of these dates haphazardly to show how much you would have today if you invested 10,000 at each of these nominal highs:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-4.jpg">
          <img alt="FTSE 100 hits a new high 4" height="163" src="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-4.jpg" width="552"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         As you can see, in the long-term, the market moves from bottom left to top right. In all my examples, other than 1999, the annual returns you receive after investing at the new high are over 5.5%. Therefore, they outperform inflation in the long-term and they outperform cash returns.
        </p>
        <p>
         Even if you bought 100% of your investment on 30
         <sup>
          th
         </sup>
         December 1999 (which you wouldnt have done because you are a fan of diversification and sensible investing), your returns would still have been better than the current savings rates across Britains banks.
        </p>
        <p>
         Yes, ideally, you want to buy low and sell high. However, if you have 10,000 to invest at a new high, you would still be making an investment which outperforms holding cash.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/financial-planning/timing-the-market/">
          See my post on timing the market for further analysis of this.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         But, couldnt I wait until a big fall in the market? Well, as you can see from the graph, that might not come for many years and you would be losing out on dividends over this period.
        </p>
        <p>
         Again, Ive written about 
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/financial-planning/best-time-to-invest">
          the best time to invest in the market
         </a>
         . I suggest you check it out if you are wondering about this.
        </p>
        <p>
         <script type="text/javascript">
          // &lt;![CDATA[
google_ad_client = "ca-pub-3293672126344120"; /* moneystepper 4 */ google_ad_slot = "7418964697"; google_ad_width = 710; google_ad_height = 90;
// ]]&gt;
         </script>
         <br/>
         <script src="http://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/show_ads.js" type="text/javascript">
          // &lt;![CDATA[&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;// ]]&gt;
         </script>
        </p>
        <h2 style="margin: 0px 0px 30px 0px; border-bottom: 1px solid black; font-size: 18px;">
         <strong>
          The longer-term trend in nominal prices
         </strong>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Before I go, I thought Id share one other pretty graph:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-5.jpg">
          <img alt="FTSE 100 hits a new high 5" height="351" src="http://moneystepper.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/FTSE-100-hits-a-new-high-5.jpg" width="580"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         This graph shows the inflation adjusted FTSE 100 (as before), with three trend lines added:
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          Red  a polynomial trend line showing that the making is slightly under trend
         </li>
         <li>
          Green  a linear trend line tracking the full data showing that the market is under trend
         </li>
         <li>
          Blue  a linear trend line based on 1984-1996 extrapolated. This trend line is used to show that if the 1999 craziness had never happened, where would we be today? The answer is that the market is a long way below what many people consider to be the norms set during this period.
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         Yes, mainstream media, the market is high. However, it is only NOMINALLY high. We would never say that milk is over-priced because it almost the same price that it was nominally in 1999. We certainly wouldnt say that it is overpriced if it had fell compared to inflation over that period.
        </p>
        <p>
         I understand that milk prices dont bring in the readers. The stock markets and housing markets do. However, in order to not mislead people, we should view the stock market
         <a href="http://moneystepper.com/financial-planning/housing-bubble-in-the-uk/">
          (and indeed the housing market)
         </a>
         in the same light. Rant over with sensationalist headlines (until next week when I shall destroy mainstream media headlines with record levels of fury).
        </p>
