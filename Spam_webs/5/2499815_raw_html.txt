        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          It's scratchy, lasts only 78 seconds and features the world's first recorded blooper.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          The modern masses can now listen to what experts say is the oldest playable recording of an American voice and the first-ever capturing of a musical performance, thanks to digital advances that allowed the sound to be transferred from flimsy tinfoil to computer.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          The recording was originally made on a Thomas Edison-invented phonograph in St. Louis in 1878.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p style="font-weight: bold;">
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Scroll down for video
         </font>

        <div class="floatRHS art-ins sciencetech">
         <h3 class="wocc">
          <span style="font-weight: bold;">
           HOW IT WORKED
          </span>
          <br/>
         </h3>
         <div class="ins cleared xolcc bdrcc">
          <p>
           <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
            The recording was made on a sheet of
 tinfoil, 5 inches wide by 15 inches long, placed on the cylinder of the
 phonograph Edison invented in 1877 and began selling the following 
year.
           </font>
           <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
            <br/>
           </font>
          </p>
          <p>
           <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
            A
 hand crank turned the cylinder under a stylus that would move up and 
down over the foil, recording the sound waves created by the operator's 
voice.
            <br/>
           </font>
          </p>
          <p>
           <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
            The stylus 
would eventually tear the foil after just a few playbacks, and the 
person demonstrating the technology would typically tear up the tinfoil 
and hand the pieces out as souvenirs, according to museum curator Chris 
Hunter.
           </font>
           <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
            <br/>
           </font>
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          At a time when music lovers can carry thousands of digital songs on a player the size of a pack of gum, Edison's tinfoil playback seems prehistoric.
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          But that dinosaur opens a key window into the development of recorded sound.
         </font>
        </p>

          <h3 class="wocc">
           <span style="font-weight: bold;">
            EDISON'S ACHIEVEMENTS
           </span>
           <br/>
          </h3>
          <div class="ins cleared xolcc bdrcc">
           <p>
            <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
             Thomas Edison was issued 1,093 patents.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
             Some of his best known include:
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
             The Electric Light Bulb, Storage Battery, Kinetoscope, Electric generators, Sound microphone, and the Phonograph.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
             He also made contributions to the telephone and telegraph.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
             He originated the concept and implementation of electric-power generation and the distribution of it to homes, businesses, and factories.
            </font>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
           The recording was made on a sheet of tinfoil, 5 inches wide by 15 inches long, placed on the cylinder of the phonograph Edison invented in 1877 and began selling the following year.
          </font>
          <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          A hand crank turned the cylinder under a stylus that would move up and down over the foil, recording the sound waves created by the operator's voice.
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          The stylus would eventually tear the foil after just a few playbacks, and the person demonstrating the technology would typically tear up the tinfoil and hand the pieces out as souvenirs, according to museum curator Chris Hunter.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Popping noises heard on this recording are likely from scars left from where the foil was folded up for more than a century.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          'Realistically, once you played it a couple of times, the stylus would tear through it and destroy it,' he said.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Only a handful of the tinfoil recording sheets are known to known to survive, and of those, only two are playable: the Schenectady museum's and an 1880 recording owned by The Henry Ford museum in Michigan.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Hunter said he was able to determine just this week that the man's voice on the museum's 1878 tinfoil recording is believed to be that of Thomas Mason, a St. Louis newspaper political writer who also went by the pen name I.X. Peck.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Edison company records show that one of his newly invented tinfoil phonographs, serial No. 8, was sold to Mason for $95.50 in April 1878, and a search of old newspapers revealed a listing for a public phonograph program being offered by Peck on June 22, 1878, in St. Louis, the curator said.
         </font>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
        <div class="artSplitter">
         <img alt="The recording was made on this tinfoil sheet  in St. Louis in 1878" class="blkBorder" height="465" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2012/10/25/article-0-15AC7405000005DC-28_634x465.jpg" width="634"/>
         <p class="imageCaption">
          The recording was made on this tinfoil sheet  in St. Louis in 1878
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
        </p>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
        <div class="artSplitter">
         <img alt="The tinfoil sheets could only be played a few times before the needle used for playback destroyed them" class="blkBorder" height="337" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2012/10/25/article-0-15AC7411000005DC-231_634x337.jpg" width="634"/>
         <p class="imageCaption">
          The tinfoil sheets could only be played a few times before the needle used for playback destroyed them
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          A woman's voice says the words 'Old Mother Hubbard,' but her identity remains a mystery, he said.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Three weeks after making the recording, Mason died of sunstroke, Hunter said.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          A Connecticut woman donated the tinfoil to the Schenectady museum in 1978 for an exhibit on the 100th anniversary of the founding of the Edison company that later merged with another to form GE.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          The woman's father had been an antiques dealer in the Midwest and counted the item among his favorites, Hunter said.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          In July, Hunter brought the Edison tinfoil recording to California's Berkeley Lab, where researchers such as Carl Haber have had success in recent years restoring some of the earliest audio recordings.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Haber's projects include recovering a snippet of a folk song recorded a capella in 1860 on paper by Edouard-Leon Scott de Martinville, a French printer credited with inventing the earliest known sound recording device.
         </font>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
        <div class="artSplitter">
         <img alt="John Schneiter, a trustee at the Museum of Innovation and Science, with the tinfoil phonographs" class="blkBorder" height="427" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2012/10/25/article-0-15AC74CE000005DC-841_634x427.jpg" width="634"/>
         <p class="imageCaption">
          John Schneiter, a trustee at the Museum of Innovation and Science, with the tinfoil phonographs
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Haber and his team used optical scanning technology to replicate the action of the phonograph's stylus, reading the grooves in the foil and creating a 3D image, which was then analyzed by a computer program that recovered the original recorded sound.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          The achievement restores a vital link in the evolution of recorded sound, Haber said.
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          The artifact represents Edison's first step in his efforts to record sound and have the capability to play it back, even if it was just once or twice, he said.
         </font>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          <br/>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          'It really completes a technology story,' Haber said. 'He was on the right track from the get-go to record and play it back.'
         </font>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
        <div class="artSplitter">

         <p class="imageCaption">
          Thomas Edison listening to a wax cylinder phonograph similar to the one used with tinfoil
         </p>
