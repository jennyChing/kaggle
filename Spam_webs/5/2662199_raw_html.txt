      <p>
       <b>
        Theater review
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       After seeing "The Book of Mormon" in Dallas last summer, I was left with just one question  what on earth will the good people of Nashville make of this Tony Award-winning musical?
      </p>
      <p>
       I received my answer Wednesday evening, with the thunderous ovation at a packed performance at TPAC's Jackson Hall. It seems that despite the outrageous humor and explicit language, the clean-cut lads of this touring production had us at "Hello."
      </p>
      <p>
       The brainchild of Trey Parker and Matt Stone (creators of the Emmy Award-winning animated series "South Park") and Robert Lopez (co-creator of the Tony Award-winning musical "Avenue Q"), "The Book of Mormon" follows the irreverent adventures of a woefully mismatched pair of young missionaries sent to a remote village in Uganda. Elder Price is disappointed in his assignment (he had his heart set on Orlando), but remains confident in his ability to do "something incredible." Meanwhile, the nebbishy Elder Cunningham is just happy to have a new friend. He hasn't exactly read the Mormon scripture, but totally identifies with fantasy adventures such as "Star Wars" and "The Lord of the Rings."
      </p>
      <p>
       Anyone familiar with the "South Park" brand of comedy can tell you that "Mormon" is not for those easily offended by profanity, crass humor or religious blasphemy. The gags are designed to shock and offend, hitting on everything from AIDS to African warlords and even female genital mutilation.
      </p>
      <p>
       So how do they get away with it? Perhaps it's because the piece's musical structure is so sound, so whip smart and downright traditional. Broadway fans will no doubt recognize cheeky nods to "The Lion King," "Wicked" and even "The Sound of Music." And because the lead characters are treated with such earnest affection, the story feels more like a send-up rather than a put-down.
      </p>
      <p>
       Indeed, Parker, Lopez and Stone raise some interesting questions with their religious satire. What would happen if these fresh-faced missionaries set aside personal belief and focused on the harsh realities of poverty and violence, rather than simply trying to convert the masses? What if the overall message of love and service was more important than the specific mission of bringing nonbelievers to the church?
      </p>
      <p>
       Pretty heady stuff, if you can get past the more in-your-face elements. (Interestingly enough, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints seems intent on having the last laugh  taking out ads in the playbill and having real-life missionaries distribute literature in TPAC's lobby.) But, of course, none of that would register without the enthusiastic performances of this super-talented touring company.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Read more:
       </b>
       <a href="/story/news/religion/2014/11/18/missionaries-use-book-mormon-tpac-recruit/19256413/" title="http://www.tennessean.com/story/news/religion/2014/11/18/missionaries-use-book-mormon-tpac-recruit/19256413/">
        Missionaries use 'Book of Mormon' at TPAC to recruit
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       David Larsen is sensational as Elder Price  an all-American (if somewhat conceited) Mormon who appears to have all the answers. Flashing a toothy smile that would make Donny Osmond jealous, Larsen works the crowd with big numbers such as "You and Me (But Mostly Me)" and "I Believe." "Spooky Mormon Hell Dream" (an unholy mash-up of serial killers and dancing cups of coffee) is a devilish delight.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cody Jamison Strand also is perfectly cast as the eternally awkward Elder Cunningham, lending great physicality to the goofy role. His rocking rendition of "Man Up" is hilarious, but he reveals his character in more quiet moments ("I Am Here for You").
      </p>
      <p>
       As trusting Nabulungi, Dene Benton shows offer some stellar vocals  particularly in "Sal Tlay Ka Siti." And her comical duet with Strand ("Baptize Me") is surprisingly endearing. Eric Huffman also scores as Elder McKinley, a sexually repressed man who knows just how to handle unsettling feelings ("Turn It Off")
      </p>
      <p>
       Scott Pask's excellent scenic design (complemented by Brian MacDevitt's terrific lighting) keeps the action moving along. And Ann Roth's costumes add to the fun. But it's Casey Nicholaw's fabulous choreography  perfectly executed by the tireless ensemble  that really takes this production to the next level.
      </p>
      <p>
       It's certainly not for the faint of heart. But despite some controversial methods, you may find yourself unable to resist the message behind this "Book of Mormon."
      </p>
