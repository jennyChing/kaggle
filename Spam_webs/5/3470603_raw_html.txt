     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Giver
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1993 American
      <a href="/wiki/Young-adult_fiction" title="Young-adult fiction">
       Young-adult fiction
      </a>
      -
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dystopian_novel" title="Dystopian novel">
       Dystopian novel
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Lois_Lowry" title="Lois Lowry">
       Lois Lowry
      </a>
      . It is set in a society which at first appears as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utopian_society" title="Utopian society">
       utopian society
      </a>
      but then later revealed to be a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dystopian_fiction" title="Dystopian fiction">
       dystopian
      </a>
      one as the story progresses. The novel follows a boy named Jonas through the twelfth and thirteenth years of his life. The society has eliminated pain and strife by converting to "Sameness," a plan that has also eradicated
      <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">
       emotional
      </a>
      depth from their lives. Jonas is selected to inherit the position of Receiver of Memory, the person who stores all the past memories of the time before Sameness, as there may be times where one must draw upon the wisdom gained from history to aid the community's decision making. Jonas struggles with concepts of all of the new emotions, and things being introduced to him, and whether they are inherently good, evil, in-between, and if it's even possible to have one without the other. The Community lacks any color, memory, climate and
      <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">
       terrain
      </a>
      whatsoever, all in effort to preserve structure, order, and a true sense of equality beyond personal individuality.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Giver
      </i>
      won the 1994
      <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">
       Newbery Medal
      </a>
      and has sold more than 10 million copies.

      <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">
       middle school
      </a>
      reading lists, but it is also on many
      <a href="/wiki/Challenge_(literature)" title="Challenge (literature)">
       challenged
      </a>
      book lists and appeared on the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">
       American Library Association
      </a>
      's list of most challenged books of the 1990s.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Jonas, who is eleven years old, is apprehensive about the upcoming Ceremony of Twelve, where he will be assigned his career or his "assignment in the community". In his society, little privacy is allowed; even private houses have two-way intercoms which can be used to listen in for infractions of the rules. However, the rules appear to be readily accepted by all, including Jonas. So it is without real protest that he initially accepts his selection as the Receiver of Memory, a vocation he is told will be filled with pain and the training for which will isolate him from his family and friends forever.
     </p>
     <p>
      Yet, under the guidance of the present Receiver, a surprisingly kind man who has the same rare, pale eyes as Jonas, the boy absorbs memories that induce for the first time feelings of true happiness and love. Also, for the first time, Jonas knows what it is to see a
      <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">
       rainbow
      </a>
      , and to experience
      <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">
       snow
      </a>
      and the thrill of riding a
      <a href="/wiki/Sled" title="Sled">
       sled
      </a>
      down a hill. But then he is given the painful memories:
      <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">
       war
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">
       pain
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">
       death
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">
       starvation
      </a>
      . These are memories of the Community's deep past. Jonas learns that the Community engineered a society of "sameness" to protect its people against this past, yet he begins to understand the tremendous loss he and his people have endured by giving their memories away, embracing "sameness", and using "climate control".
     </p>
     <p>
      In his "community", which is under extreme control, there is no suffering, hunger, war, and also no color, music, or love. Everything is controlled by "the Elders," who are looked upon in a very positive light, though they control who will marry whom, where children are placed, and what everyone will be "assigned" as a career. The people in the community do not have the freedom to choose. Jonas aches with this new found wisdom and his desire for a life Elsewhere blossoms. But the final blow for Jonas comes when he asks the Receiver (who now calls himself "The Giver") what "release" is. The Giver says that he could show him, and allows Jonas to watch a present-day tape of his own father, a seemingly kind and loving man, "releasing" a baby twin by giving him a
      <a href="/wiki/Lethal_injection" title="Lethal injection">
       lethal injection
      </a>
      . Like any other "aberration" from sameness, identical twins are against the rules, so the smaller of the two is dispatched like garbage, without the one who conducted the release understanding the true meaning of the action. Together, Jonas and the Giver come to the understanding that the time for change is now, that the Community has lost its way and must have its memories returned. The only way to make this happen is if Jonas leaves the Community, at which time the memories he has been given will flood back into the people. Jonas wants the Giver to escape with him, but the Giver insists that he will be needed to help the people manage the memories, or they will destroy themselves. The Giver also wants to remain behind so that when his work is done, he can be with his daughter, Rosemary, a girl with pale eyes who ten years earlier had failed in her training to become the new Receiver of Memories and who had asked to be released (the memories of pain and loneliness having overwhelmed her).
     </p>
     <p>
      The Giver devises a plot in which Jonas will escape to Elsewhere, an unknown land that exists beyond the boundaries of the Communities. The Giver will make it appear as if Jonas drowned in the river so that the search for him will be limited. In the meantime, the Giver will give Jonas memories of strength and courage to sustain him and save up his meals as Jonas' food and water supply for his journey.
     </p>
     <p>
      Their plan is changed when Jonas learns that Gabriel, the baby staying with his family unit, will be "released" the following morning. Jonas has become attached to the baby, who also has unusual pale eyes, and feels he has no choice but to escape with the infant. Without the memories of strength and courage promised by the Giver, Jonas steals his father's bike and leaves with Gabriel to find the Elsewhere. Their escape ride is fraught with dangers, and the two are near death from cold and starvation when they reach the border of what Jonas believes must be Elsewhere. Using his ability to "see beyond," a gift that he does not quite understand, he finds a sled waiting for him at the top of a snowy hill. He and Gabriel ride the sled down towards a house filled with colored lights and warmth and love and a Christmas tree, and for the first time he hears something he believes must be music. The ending is ambiguous, with Jonas depicted as experiencing symptoms of
      <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">
       hypothermia
      </a>
      . This leaves his and Gabriel's future unresolved. However, their fate is revealed in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Messenger_(novel)" title="Messenger (novel)">
        Messenger
       </a>
      </i>
      , a companion novel written much later.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, at the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Festival" title="National Book Festival">
       National Book Festival
      </a>
      , the author
      <a href="/wiki/Lois_Lowry" title="Lois Lowry">
       Lois Lowry
      </a>
      joked during a Q&amp;A, "Jonas is alive, by the way. You don't need to ask that question."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_significance_and_criticism">
       Literary significance and criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Giver&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary significance and criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While critical reception of
      <i>
       The Giver
      </i>
      has been mixed, the novel has found a home in "City Reads" programs, library-sponsored reading clubs on citywide or larger scales.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some reviewers have commented that the story lacks originality and is not likely to stand up to the sort of probing literary criticism used in "serious" circles, while others argue that books appealing to a young-adult audience are critical for building a developing reader's appetite for reading.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Karen Ray, writing in
      <i>
       The New York Times
      </i>
      , detects "occasional logical lapses", but adds that the book "is sure to keep older children reading".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">
       Young adult fiction
      </a>
      author
      <a href="/wiki/Debra_Doyle" title="Debra Doyle">
       Debra Doyle
      </a>
      was more critical stating that "Personal taste aside,
      <i>
       The Giver
      </i>
      fails the Plausibility Test", and that "Things are the way they are (in the novel) because The Author is Making A Point; things work out the way they do because The Author's Point Requires It".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Babbitt" title="Natalie Babbitt">
       Natalie Babbitt
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       The Washington Post
      </i>
      was more forgiving, calling Lowry's work "a warning in narrative form", saying:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The story has been told before in a variety of forms
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">
        Ray Bradbury
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_451" title="Fahrenheit 451">
         Fahrenheit 451
        </a>
       </i>
       comes to mindbut not, to my knowledge, for children. It's well worth telling, especially by a writer of Lowry's great skill. If it is exceedingly fragileif, in other words, some situations do not survive that well-known
       <a href="/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief" title="Suspension of disbelief">
        suspension of disbelief
       </a>
       well, so be it.
       <i>
        The Giver
       </i>
       has things to say that cannot be said too often, and I hope there will be many, many young people who will be willing to listen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Awards.2C_nominations.2C_and_recognition">
       Awards, nominations, and recognition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     <p>
      <span id="Awards">
      </span>

      Lowry won many awards for her work on
      <i>
       The Giver
      </i>
      , including the following:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The 1994
       <a href="/wiki/Newbery_Medal" title="Newbery Medal">
        Newbery Medal
       </a>
        The John Newbery award (Medal) is given by the
       <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Library_Service_to_Children" title="Association for Library Service to Children">
        Association for Library Service to Children
       </a>
       .The award is given for the most distinguished contribution to American literature for children.
      </li>
      <li>
       The 1994
       <a href="/wiki/Regina_Medal" title="Regina Medal">
        Regina Medal
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The 1996
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Allen_White_Award" title="William Allen White Award">
        William Allen White Award
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">
        American Library Association
       </a>
       listings for "Best Book for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_adult_literature" title="Young adult literature">
        Young Adults
       </a>
       ", "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALA_Notable_Children%27s_Book" title="ALA Notable Children's Book">
        ALA Notable Children's Book
       </a>
       ", and "100 Most Frequently Challenged Books of 19902000."
      </li>
      <li>
       A Boston Globe-Horn Book Honor Book
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Booklist" title="Booklist">
        Booklist
       </a>
       Editors' Choice
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/School_Library_Journal" title="School Library Journal">
         School Library Journal
        </a>
       </i>
       Best Book of the Year
      </li>

     <p>
      A 2004 study found that it was a common read-aloud book for sixth-graders in schools in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_County,_California" title="San Diego County, California">
       San Diego County, California
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Based on a 2007 online poll, the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">
       National Education Association
      </a>
      named the book one of its "Teachers' Top 100 Books for Children."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEA2007_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEA2007-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was one of the "Top 100 Chapter Books" of all time in a 2012 poll by
      <i>
       School Library Journal
      </i>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLJChapter2012_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SLJChapter2012-16">
        <span>

      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">
       Adaptations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cosby" title="Bill Cosby">

      </a>

      <i>
       The Giver
      </i>
      to film. In the years following, members of the partnership changed and the production team grew in size, but little motion was seen toward making the film. At one point, screenwriter Ed Neumeier was signed to create the screenplay. Later, Neumeier was replaced by
      <a href="/wiki/Todd_Alcott" title="Todd Alcott">
       Todd Alcott
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">

      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Walden_Media" title="Walden Media">
       Walden Media
      </a>
      became the central production company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>

        </span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Prime_Stage_Theatre" title="Prime Stage Theatre">
       Prime Stage Theatre
      </a>
      produced in 2006.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      Actor
      <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rifkin" title="Ron Rifkin">

      <a href="/wiki/Audiobook" title="Audiobook">
       audiobook
      </a>
      edition.

      <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8derbund" title="Brderbund">

      in 1995, however it was plagued by delays. Eventually the game was scrapped following the company's sale to
      <a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">

      <a href="/wiki/Ry_Cooder" title="Ry Cooder">

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Film">

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <a href="/wiki/Green-light" title="Green-light">
       green light
      </a>
      in December 2012. Jeff Bridges had wanted to film the futuristic novel for several years, but encountered obstacles when Warner Bros. bought the rights in 2007.
      <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges" title="Jeff Bridges">
       Jeff Bridges
      </a>
      plays the title character
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">

      <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">
       Meryl Streep
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Katie_Holmes" title="Katie Holmes">
       Katie Holmes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Odeya_Rush" title="Odeya Rush">
       Odeya Rush
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Monaghan" title="Cameron Monaghan">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Skarsg%C3%A5rd" title="Alexander Skarsgrd">
       Alexander Skarsgrd
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Swift" title="Taylor Swift">
       Taylor Swift
      </a>
      round out the rest of the main cast.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was released in North America on August 15, 2014. Jeff Bridges has said he has wanted to make the film for nearly 20 years, but originally wanted to direct it with his father
      <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Bridges" title="Lloyd Bridges">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Development_hell" title="Development hell">
