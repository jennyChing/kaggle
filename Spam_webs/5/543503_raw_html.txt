     <p>
      <br/>
      <i>
       <b>
        <span lang="de">
         Die Walkre
        </span>
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       <b>
        The Valkyrie
       </b>
      </i>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Wagner-Werk-Verzeichnis" title="Wagner-Werk-Verzeichnis">
       WWV
      </a>
      86B, is an
      <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">
       opera
      </a>
      in three acts by
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">
       Richard Wagner
      </a>
      with a German
      <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">
       libretto
      </a>
      by the composer. It is the second of the four operas that form Wagner's cycle
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">
        Der Ring des Nibelungen
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       The Ring of the Nibelung
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      The story of the opera is based on the
      <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
       Norse mythology
      </a>
      told in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lsunga_saga" title="Vlsunga saga">
        Volsunga Saga
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">
        Poetic Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Norse mythology, a
      <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">
       valkyrie
      </a>
      is one in a group of female figures who decide which soldiers die in battle and which live.
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Die Walkre
       </span>
       '
      </i>
      s best-known excerpt is the "
      <a href="/wiki/Ride_of_the_Valkyries" title="Ride of the Valkyries">
       Ride of the Valkyries
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      It received its premiere at the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_Munich" title="National Theatre Munich">
       Knigliches Hof- und National-Theater
      </a>
      in Munich on 26 June 1870. Wagner originally intended the opera to be premiered as part of the entire cycle, but was forced to allow the performance at the insistence of his patron King
      <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_II_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig II of Bavaria">
       Ludwig II of Bavaria
      </a>
      . It was first presented as part of the complete cycle on 14 August 1876 at Wagner's
      <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival" title="Bayreuth Festival">
       Bayreuth Festival
      </a>
      . The opera made its United States premiere at the
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Music_(New_York_City)" title="Academy of Music (New York City)">
       Academy of Music
      </a>
      in New York on 2 April 1877.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
       Composition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Die_Walk%C3%BCre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main articles:
      <a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen:_Composition_of_the_poem" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen: Composition of the poem">
       Der Ring des Nibelungen: Composition of the poem
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen:_Composition_of_the_music" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen: Composition of the music">
       Der Ring des Nibelungen: Composition of the music
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Die Walkre
       </span>
      </i>
      is the second of the
      <i>
       Ring
      </i>
      operas, it was the third in order of conception. Wagner worked backwards from planning an opera about
      <a href="/wiki/Sigurd" title="Sigurd">
       Siegfried
      </a>
      's death, then deciding he needed another opera to tell of Siegfried's youth, then deciding he needed to tell the tale of Siegfried's conception and of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C3%BCnnhilde" title="Brnnhilde">
       Brnnhilde
      </a>
      's attempts to save Siegfried's parents, and finally deciding he also needed a prelude that told of the original theft of the Rheingold and the creation of the ring.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wagner intermingled development of the text of these last two planned operas, i.e.
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Die Walkre
       </span>
      </i>
      , originally entitled
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Siegmund und Sieglinde: der Walkre Bestrafung
       </span>
      </i>
      ("Siegmund and Sieglinde: the Valkyrie's Punishment") and what became
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Das_Rheingold" title="Das Rheingold">
        Das Rheingold
       </a>
      </i>
      . Wagner had first written of his intention to create a trilogy of operas in the August 1851 draft of "
      <span lang="de">
       Eine Mittheilung an meine Freunde
      </span>
      " (
      <a href="/wiki/A_Communication_to_My_Friends" title="A Communication to My Friends">
       A Communication to My Friends
      </a>
      ), but did not produce any sketches of the plot of
      <i>
       Siegmund and Sieglinde
      </i>
      until November. The following summer, Wagner and his wife rented the Pension Rinderknecht, a
      <a href="/wiki/Pied-%C3%A0-terre" title="Pied--terre">
       pied--terre
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrichberg" title="Zrichberg">
       Zrichberg
      </a>
      (now Hochstrasse 5658 in Zrich). There he worked on the prose draft of
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Die Walkre
       </span>
      </i>
      , an extended description of the story including dialogue between 17 and 26 May 1852 and the verse draft between 1 June and 1 July. It was between these drafts that Wagner made the decision not to introduce
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W%C5%8Dden" title="Wden">
       Wotan
      </a>
      in act 1, instead leaving the sword the god had been going to bring on stage already embedded in the tree before the action starts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fair_copy" title="Fair copy">
       fair copy
      </a>
      of the text was completed by 15 December 1852.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even before the text of the
      <i>
       Ring
      </i>
      was finalised, Wagner had begun to sketch some of the music. On 23 July 1851 he wrote down on a loose sheet of paper what was to become the best-known
      <a href="/wiki/Leitmotif" title="Leitmotif">
       leitmotif
      </a>
      in the entire cycle: the theme from the "
      <a href="/wiki/Ride_of_the_Valkyries" title="Ride of the Valkyries">
       Ride of the Valkyries
      </a>
      " (
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Walkrenritt
       </span>
      </i>
      ). Other early sketches for
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Die Walkre
       </span>
      </i>
      were made in the summer of 1852. But it was not until 28 June 1854 that Wagner began to transform these into a complete draft of all three acts of the opera. This preliminary draft (
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Gesamtentwurf
       </span>
      </i>
      ) was completed by 27 December 1854. Much of the work of this stage of development of the opera overlapped with work on the final orchestral version of
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Das Rheingold
       </span>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As Wagner had included some indication of the orchestration in the draft, he decided to move straight on to developing a full orchestral score in January 1855 without bothering to write an intermediate instrumentation draft as he had done for
      <i>
       <span lang="de">
        Das Rheingold
       </span>
      </i>
      . This was a decision he was soon to regret, as numerous interruptions including a four-month visit to London made the task of orchestrating more difficult than he had expected. If he allowed too much time to elapse between the initial drafting of a passage and its later elaboration, he found that he could not remember how he had intended to orchestrate the draft. Consequently some passages had to be composed again from scratch. Wagner, nevertheless, persevered with the task and the full score was finally completed on 20 March 1856. The fair copy was begun on 14 July 1855 in the Swiss resort of
      <a href="/wiki/Seelisberg" title="Seelisberg">
       Seelisberg
      </a>
      , where Wagner and his wife spent a month. It was completed in Zrich on 23 March 1856, just three days after the completion of the full score.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
       Synopsis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Die_Walk%C3%BCre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Act_1">
       Act 1
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Die_Walk%C3%BCre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Act 1">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="1490" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg/170px-Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg/255px-Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg/340px-Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhinegold_and_the_Valkyries_p_080.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sieglinde brings the exhausted Siegmund some-thing to drink, illustration by
        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rackham" title="Arthur Rackham">
         Arthur Rackham
        </a>
        , 1910
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      During a raging storm, Siegmund seeks shelter at the house of the warrior
      <a href="/wiki/Hundings" title="Hundings">
       Hunding
      </a>
      . Hunding is not present, and Siegmund is greeted by Sieglinde, Hunding's unhappy wife. Siegmund tells her that he is fleeing from enemies. After taking a drink of mead, he moves to leave, claiming to be cursed by misfortune. But Sieglinde bids him stay, saying he can bring no misfortune to the "house where ill luck lives".
     </p>
     <p>
      Returning, Hunding reluctantly offers Siegmund the hospitality demanded by custom. Sieglinde, increasingly fascinated by the visitor, urges him to tell his tale. Siegmund describes returning home with his father one day to find his mother dead and his twin sister abducted. He then wandered with his father until he was parted from him as well. One day he found a girl being forced into marriage and fought with the girl's relatives. His weapons were broken and the bride was killed, and he was forced to flee to Hunding's home. Initially Siegmund does not reveal his name, choosing to call himself Wehwalt, 'filled with woe'.
     </p>
     <p>
      When Siegmund finishes, Hunding reveals that he is one of Siegmund's pursuers. He grants Siegmund a night's stay, but they are to do battle in the morning. Hunding leaves the room with Sieglinde, ignoring his wife's distress. Siegmund laments his misfortune, recalling his father's promise that he would find a sword when he most needed it.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1236" data-file-width="1803" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG/220px-B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG/330px-B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG/440px-B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:B%C3%BChnenbildentwurf_Walk%C3%BCre.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The end of act 1 in the 1876 production
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sieglinde returns, having drugged Hunding's drink to send him into a deep sleep. She reveals that she was forced into a marriage with Hunding. During their wedding feast, an old man appeared and plunged a sword into the trunk of the ash tree in the center of the room, which neither Hunding nor any of his companions could remove. She expresses her longing for the hero who could draw the sword and save her. Siegmund expresses his love for her, which she reciprocates, and as she strives to understand her recognition of him, she realises it is in the echo of her own voice, and reflection of her image, that she already knows him. When he speaks the name of his father,
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lsung" title="Vlsung">
       Wlse
      </a>
      , she declares that he is Siegmund, and that the Wanderer left the sword for him.
     </p>
     <p>
      Siegmund now easily draws the sword forth, and she tells him she is Sieglinde, his twin sister. He names the blade "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nothung" title="Nothung">
       Nothung
      </a>
      " (or
      <i>
       needful
      </i>
      , for this is the weapon that he needs for his forthcoming fight with Hunding). As the act closes he calls her "bride and sister", and draws her to him with passionate fervour.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Act_2">
       Act 2
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Die_Walk%C3%BCre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Act 2">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Wotan is standing on a rocky mountainside with Brnnhilde, his
      <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">
       Valkyrie
      </a>
      daughter. He instructs Brnnhilde to protect Siegmund in his coming fight with Hunding.
      <a href="/wiki/Frijj%C5%8D" title="Frijj">
       Fricka
      </a>
      , Wotan's wife and the guardian of wedlock, arrives demanding the punishment of Siegmund and Sieglinde, who have committed adultery and incest. She knows that Wotan, disguised as the mortal man Wlse, fathered Siegmund and Sieglinde. Wotan protests that he requires a free hero (i.e., one not ruled by him) to aid his plans, but Fricka retorts that Siegmund is not a free hero but Wotan's creature and unwitting pawn. Backed into a corner, Wotan promises Fricka that Siegmund will die.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="685" data-file-width="500" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg/170px-Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg/255px-Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg/340px-Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hunding_Siegmund_fight.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hunding kills Siegmund
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fricka exits, leaving Brnnhilde with a despairing Wotan. Wotan explains his problems: troubled by the warning delivered by
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Jr">
       Erda
      </a>
      (at the end of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Das_Rheingold" title="Das Rheingold">
        Das Rheingold
       </a>
      </i>
      ), he had seduced the earth-goddess to learn more of the prophesied doom; Brnnhilde was born to him by Erda. He raised Brnnhilde and eight other daughters as the Valkyries, warrior maidens who gather the souls of fallen heroes to form an army against
      <a href="/wiki/Alberich" title="Alberich">
       Alberich
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Valhalla" title="Valhalla">
       Valhalla's
      </a>
      army will fail if Alberich should ever wield the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andvarinaut" title="Andvarinaut">
       ring
      </a>
      , which is in
      <a href="/wiki/Fafnir" title="Fafnir">
       Fafner
      </a>
      's possession. The giant has transformed himself into a dragon, lurking in a forest with the
      <a href="/wiki/Nibelung" title="Nibelung">
       Nibelung
      </a>
      treasure. Wotan cannot wrest the ring from Fafner, who is bound to him by contract; he needs a free hero to defeat Fafner in his stead. But as Fricka pointed out, he can create only thralls (i.e. servants) to himself. Bitterly, Wotan orders Brnnhilde to obey Fricka and ensure the death of his beloved child Siegmund.
     </p>
     <p>
      Having fled Hunding's hall, Siegmund and Sieglinde enter the mountain pass, where Sieglinde faints in guilt and exhaustion. Brnnhilde approaches Siegmund and tells him of his impending death. Siegmund refuses to follow Brnnhilde to Valhalla when she tells him Sieglinde cannot accompany him there. Siegmund dismisses Brnnhilde's warning since he has Wlse's sword, which his father assured him would win victory for him, but Brnnhilde tells him it has lost its power. Siegmund draws his sword and threatens to kill both Sieglinde and himself. Impressed by his passion, Brnnhilde relents and agrees to grant victory to Siegmund instead of Hunding.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hunding arrives and attacks Siegmund. Blessed by Brnnhilde, Siegmund begins to overpower Hunding, but Wotan appears and shatters Nothung (Siegmund's sword) with his spear. While Siegmund is thus disarmed and helpless, Hunding stabs him to death. Wotan looks down on Siegmund's body, grieving, and Brnnhilde gathers up the fragments of Nothung and flees on horseback with Sieglinde. Wotan strikes Hunding dead with a contemptuous gesture, and angrily sets out in pursuit of his disobedient daughter.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Act_3">
       Act 3
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Die_Walk%C3%BCre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Act 3">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The other
      <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">
       Valkyries
      </a>
      assemble on the summit of a mountain, each with a dead hero in her saddlebag. They are astonished when Brnnhilde arrives with Sieglinde, a living woman. She begs them to help, but they dare not defy Wotan. Brnnhilde decides to delay Wotan as Sieglinde flees. She also reveals that Sieglinde is pregnant by Siegmund, and names the unborn son
      <a href="/wiki/Sigurd" title="Sigurd">
       Siegfried
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Wotan arrives in wrath and passes judgement on Brnnhilde: she is to be stripped of her Valkyrie status and become a mortal woman, to be held in a magic sleep on the mountain, prey to any man who happens by. Dismayed, the other Valkyries flee. Brnnhilde begs mercy of Wotan for herself, his favorite child. She recounts the courage of Siegmund and her decision to protect him, knowing that was Wotan's true desire. With the words 'Der diese Liebe mir ins Herz gehaucht' (
      <i>
       He who breathed this love into me
      </i>
      ), introducing the key of
      <a href="/wiki/E_major" title="E major">
       E major
      </a>
      , she identifies her actions as Wotan's true will. Wotan consents to her last request: to encircle the mountaintop with magic flame, which will deter all but the bravest of heroes (who, as shown through the leitmotif, they both know will be the yet unborn Siegfried). Wotan lays Brnnhilde down on a rock and, in a long embrace, kisses her eyes closed into an enchanted sleep. He summons
      <a href="/wiki/Loki" title="Loki">
       Loge
      </a>
      (the Norse demigod of fire) to ignite the circle of flame that will protect her, then slowly departs in sorrow, after pronouncing: "Whosoever fears the point of my spear shall not pass through the fire." The curtain falls as the Magic Fire Music again resolves into E major.
     </p>
