     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Doors of Perception
       </b>
      </i>
      is a short book by
      <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">
       Aldous Huxley
      </a>
      , first published in 1954, detailing his experiences when taking
      <a href="/wiki/Mescaline" title="Mescaline">
       mescaline
      </a>
      . The book takes the form of Huxley's recollection of a mescaline trip that took place over the course of an afternoon, and takes its title from a phrase in
      <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">
       William Blake
      </a>
      's 1793 poem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Heaven_and_Hell" title="The Marriage of Heaven and Hell">
        The Marriage of Heaven and Hell
       </a>
      </i>
      . Huxley recalls the insights he experienced, which range from the "purely aesthetic" to "sacramental vision".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also incorporates later reflections on the experience and its meaning for art and religion.
     </p>

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mescaline_.28Peyote_and_San_Pedro_Cactus.29">
       Mescaline (Peyote and San Pedro Cactus)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mescaline (Peyote and San Pedro Cactus)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Mescaline" title="Mescaline">
       Mescaline
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mescaline is the principal agent of the
      <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">
       psychedelic
      </a>
      cactus
      <a href="/wiki/Peyote" title="Peyote">
       peyote
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Pedro_cactus" title="San Pedro cactus">
       San Pedro cactus
      </a>
      , which has been used in
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
       Native American
      </a>
      religious ceremonies for thousands of years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A German pharmacologist,
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Heffter" title="Arthur Heffter">
       Arthur Heffter
      </a>
      , isolated the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaloids" title="Alkaloids">
       alkaloids
      </a>
      in the peyote cactus in 1891. These included mescaline, which he showed through a combination of
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_testing" title="Animal testing">
       animal
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Self-experimentation" title="Self-experimentation">
       self-experiments
      </a>
      was the compound responsible for the
      <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">
       psychoactive
      </a>
      properties of the plant. In 1919,
      <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Sp%C3%A4th" title="Ernst Spth">
       Ernst Spth
      </a>
      , another German chemist, synthesised the drug.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although personal accounts of taking the cactus had been written by psychologists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Silas_Weir_Mitchell" title="Silas Weir Mitchell">
       Weir Mitchell
      </a>
      in the US and
      <a href="/wiki/Havelock_Ellis" title="Havelock Ellis">
       Havelock Ellis
      </a>
      in the UK during the 1890s, the German-American
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Kluver" title="Heinrich Kluver">
       Heinrich Kluver
      </a>
      was the first to systematically study its psychological effects in a small book called
      <i>
       Mescal and Mechanisms of Hallucinations
      </i>
      published in 1928. The book stated that the drug could be used to research the unconscious mind.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Peyote_as_entheogen_drug">
       Peyote as entheogen drug
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peyote as entheogen drug">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the 1930s, an American anthropologist
      <a href="/wiki/Weston_La_Barre" title="Weston La Barre">
       Weston La Barre
      </a>
      , published
      <i>
       The Peyote Cult
      </i>
      , the first study of the ritual use of peyote as an
      <a href="/wiki/Entheogen" title="Entheogen">
       entheogen
      </a>
      drug amongst the
      <a href="/wiki/Huichol_people" title="Huichol people">
       Huichol
      </a>
      people of western Mexico. La Barre noted that the Indian users of the cactus took it to obtain visions for prophecy, healing and inner strength.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most psychiatric research projects into the drug in the 1930s and early 1940s tended to look at the role of the drug in mimicking psychosis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1947 however, the US Navy undertook
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Chatter" title="Project Chatter">
       Project Chatter
      </a>
      , which examined the potential for the drug as a truth revealing agent. In the early 1950s, when Huxley wrote his book, mescaline was still regarded as a research chemical rather than a drug and was listed in the
      <a href="/wiki/Parke-Davis" title="Parke-Davis">
       Parke-Davis
      </a>
      catalogue with no controls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peyote_Cactus.jpg">
        <img alt="Close up of a peyote cactus growing in the wild." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="697" data-file-width="1050" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Peyote_Cactus.jpg/220px-Peyote_Cactus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Peyote_Cactus.jpg/330px-Peyote_Cactus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Peyote_Cactus.jpg/440px-Peyote_Cactus.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peyote_Cactus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Peyote" title="Peyote">
         peyote
        </a>
        cactus, from which
        <a href="/wiki/Mescaline" title="Mescaline">
         mescaline
        </a>
        is derived.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Huxley had been interested in spiritual matters and had used alternative therapies for some time. In 1936 he told
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TS_Eliot" title="TS Eliot">
       TS Eliot
      </a>
      that he was starting to meditate,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and he used other therapies too; the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Technique" title="Alexander Technique">
       Alexander Technique
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bates_Method" title="Bates Method">
       Bates Method
      </a>
      of seeing had particular importance in guiding him through personal crises.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the late Thirties he had become interested in the spiritual teaching of
      <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">
       Vedanta
      </a>
      and in 1945 he published
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Perennial_Philosophy" title="The Perennial Philosophy">
        The Perennial Philosophy
       </a>
      </i>
      , which set out a philosophy that he believed was found amongst mystics of all religions. He had known for some time of visionary experience achieved by taking drugs in certain non-Christian religions.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research_by_Humphry_Osmond">
       Research by Humphry Osmond
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research by Humphry Osmond">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Huxley had first heard of peyote use in ceremonies of the
      <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">
       Native American Church
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
       New Mexico
      </a>
      soon after coming to the United States in 1937.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He first became aware of the cactuss active ingredient, mescaline, after reading an academic paper written by
      <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Osmond" title="Humphry Osmond">
       Humphry Osmond
      </a>
      , a British psychiatrist working at
      <a href="/wiki/Souris_Valley_Mental_Health_Hospital" title="Souris Valley Mental Health Hospital">
       Weyburn Mental Hospital
      </a>
      , Saskatchewan in early 1952. Osmond's paper set out results from his research into schizophrenia using mescaline that he had been undertaking with colleagues, doctors Abram Hoffer and John Smythies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the epilogue to his novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Devils_of_Loudun" title="The Devils of Loudun">
        The Devils of Loudun
       </a>
      </i>
      published earlier that year, Huxley had written that drugs were "toxic short cuts to self-transcendence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For the Canadian writer
      <a href="/wiki/George_Woodcock" title="George Woodcock">
       George Woodcock
      </a>
      , Huxley had changed his opinion because mescaline was not addictive and appeared to be without unpleasant physical or mental side-effects, further he had found that hypnosis, autohypnosis and meditation had apparently failed to produce the results he wanted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Huxley.27s_experience_with_mescaline">
       Huxley's experience with mescaline
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Huxley's experience with mescaline">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After reading
      <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Osmond" title="Humphry Osmond">
       Osmond
      </a>
      's paper, Huxley sent him a letter on 10 April 1952, expressing interest in the research and putting himself forward as an experimental subject. His letter explained his motivations as being rooted in an idea that the brain is a reducing valve that restricts consciousness and hoping mescaline might help access a greater degree of awareness, (an idea he later included in the book).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bedford_1974.2C_p._144_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bedford_1974.2C_p._144-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reflecting on his stated motivations, Woodcock wrote that Huxley had realised that the ways to enlightenment were many, including prayer and meditation. He hoped drugs might also break down the barriers of the ego, and both draw him closer to spiritual enlightenment and satisfy his quest as a seeker of knowledge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a second letter on 19 April, Huxley invited Osmond to stay while he was visiting Los Angeles to attend the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association" title="American Psychiatric Association">
       American Psychiatric Association
      </a>
      convention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bedford_1974.2C_p._142_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bedford_1974.2C_p._142-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also wrote that he looked forward to the mescaline experience and reassured Osmond that his doctor did not object to his taking it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bedford_1974.2C_p._144_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bedford_1974.2C_p._144-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Huxley had invited his friend, the writer
      <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Heard" title="Gerald Heard">
       Gerald Heard
      </a>
      , to participate in the experiment; although Heard was too busy this time, he did join him for a session in November of that year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Day_of_the_experiment">
       Day of the experiment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Day of the experiment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Osmond arrived at Huxley's house in West Hollywood on 3 May 1953, and recorded his impressions of the famous author as a tolerant and kind man, although he had expected otherwise. The psychiatrist had misgivings about giving the drug to Huxley, and wrote that "I did not relish the possibility, however remote, of being the man who drove Aldous Huxley mad," but instead found him an ideal subject. Huxley was shrewd, matter-of-fact and to the point" and his wife Maria "eminently sensible".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Overall, they all liked each other, which was very important when administering the drug. The mescaline was slow to take effect, but Osmond saw that after two and a half hours the drug was working and after three hours Huxley was responding well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The experience lasted eight hours and both Osmond and Maria remained with him throughout.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The experience started in Huxley's study before the party made a seven block trip to The Owl Drug (
      <a href="/wiki/Rexall" title="Rexall">
       Rexall
      </a>
      ) store, known as World's Biggest Drugstore, at the corner of Beverly and La Cienega Boulevards. Huxley was particularly fond of the shop and the large variety of products available there, (in stark contrast to the much smaller selection in English
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dispensing_chemist" title="Dispensing chemist">
       chemist's shops
      </a>
      ). There he considered a variety of paintings in art books. For one of his friends, Huxley's poor eyesight manifested in both a great desire to see and a strong interest in painting, which influenced the strong visual and artistic nature of his experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After returning home to listen to music, eat, and walk in the garden, a friend drove the threesome to the hills overlooking the city. Photographs show Huxley standing, alternately arms on hips and outstretched with a grin on his face. Finally, they returned home and to ordinary consciousness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of Huxleys friends who met him on the day said that despite writing about wearing
      <a href="/wiki/Flannel" title="Flannel">
       flannel
      </a>
      trousers, he was actually wearing blue jeans. Huxley admitted to having changed the fabric as Maria thought he should be better dressed for his readers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Osmond later said he had a photo of the day that showed Huxley wearing flannels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray_2003.2C_p._401_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Murray_2003.2C_p._401-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Compilation_of_the_book">
       Compilation of the book
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Compilation of the book">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mhh.h.p14.300.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mhh.h.p14.300.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        One of the copies of
        <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">
         William Blake
        </a>
        's unique hand painted editions, created for the original printing of the poem. The line from which Huxley draws the title is in the second to last paragraph. This image represents Copy H, Plate 14 of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Heaven_and_Hell" title="The Marriage of Heaven and Hell">
          The Marriage of Heaven and Hell
         </a>
        </i>
        which is currently held at
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fitzwilliam_Museum" title="The Fitzwilliam Museum">
         The Fitzwilliam Museum
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          24
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After Osmond's departure, Huxley and Maria left to go on a three-week, 5,000-mile car trip around the national parks of the North West of the USA. After returning to Los Angeles, he took a month to write the book.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      was the first book Huxley dedicated to his wife Maria.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Harold Raymond, at his publisher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chatto_and_Windus" title="Chatto and Windus">
       Chatto and Windus
      </a>
      , said of the manuscript, "You are the most articulate guinea pig that any scientist could hope to engage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray_2003.2C_p._401_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Murray_2003.2C_p._401-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The title was taken from
      <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">
       William Blake
      </a>
      's poem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Marriage_of_Heaven_and_Hell" title="The Marriage of Heaven and Hell">
        The Marriage of Heaven and Hell
       </a>
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       If the doors of perception were cleansed every thing would appear to man as it is, Infinite. For man has closed himself up, till he sees all things thro' narrow chinks of his cavern.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         27
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Huxley had used Blake's metaphor in
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      while discussing the paintings of Vermeer and the Nain brothers, and previously in
      <i>
       The Perennial Philosophy
      </i>
      , once in relation to the use of mortification as a means to remove persistent spiritual myopia and secondly to refer to the absence of separation in spiritual vision.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the early 1950s, Huxley had suffered a debilitating attack of the eye condition
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iritis" title="Iritis">
       iritis
      </a>
      . This increased his concern for his already poor eyesight and much of his work in the early part of the decade had featured metaphors of vision and sight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
       Synopsis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After a brief overview of research into mescaline, Huxley recounts that he was given 4/10 of a gram at 11:00am one day in May 1953. Huxley writes that he hoped to gain insight into extraordinary states of mind and expected to see brightly coloured visionary landscapes. When he only sees lights and shapes, he puts this down to being a bad visualiser; however, he experiences a great change in his perception of the external world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 12:30pm, a vase of flowers becomes the "miracle, moment by moment, of naked existence". The experience, he asserts, is neither agreeable nor disagreeable, but simply "is". He likens it to
      <a href="/wiki/Meister_Eckhart" title="Meister Eckhart">
       Meister Eckhart
      </a>
      's "istigheit" or "is-ness", and
      <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
       Plato
      </a>
      's "Being" but not separated from "Becoming". He feels he understands the
      <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">
       Hindu
      </a>
      concept of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satchitananda" title="Satchitananda">
       Satchitananda
      </a>
      , as well as the
      <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">
       Zen
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koan" title="Koan">
       koan
      </a>
      that "the dharma body of the Buddha is in the hedge" and Buddhist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tath%C4%81t%C4%81/Dharmat%C4%81" title="Tatht/Dharmat">
       suchness
      </a>
      . In this state, Huxley explains he didn't have an "I", but instead a "not-I". Meaning and existence, pattern and colour become more significant than spatial relationships and time. Duration is replaced by a perpetual present.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Reflecting on the experience afterwards, Huxley finds himself in agreement with philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/C._D._Broad" title="C. D. Broad">
       C. D. Broad
      </a>
      that to enable us to live, the brain and nervous system eliminate unessential information from the totality of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mind_at_Large" title="Mind at Large">
       Mind at Large
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg">
        <img alt="Vermeer's The Milkmaid." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4485" data-file-width="4000" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/170px-Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/255px-Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/340px-Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Vermeer_-_Het_melkmeisje_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Milkmaid_(Vermeer)" title="The Milkmaid (Vermeer)">
          The Milkmaid
         </a>
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Vermeer" title="Johannes Vermeer">
         Johannes Vermeer
        </a>
        . "That mysterious artist was truly gifted with the vision that perceives the Dharma-Body as the hedge at the bottom of the garden", reflected Huxley.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In summary, Huxley writes that the ability to think straight is not reduced while under the influence of mescaline, visual impressions are intensified, and the human experimenter will see no reason for action because the experience is so fascinating.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Temporarily leaving the chronological flow, he mentions that four or five hours into the experience he was taken to the World's Biggest Drug Store (WBDS), where he was presented with books on art. In one book, the dress in
      <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">
       Botticelli
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Judith
      </i>
      provokes a reflection on drapery as a major artistic theme as it allows painters to include the abstract in representational art, to create mood, and also to represent the mystery of pure being.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Huxley feels that human affairs are somewhat irrelevant whilst on mescaline and attempts to shed light on this by reflecting on paintings featuring people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Czanne">
       Czanne
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Self-portrait with a straw hat
      </i>
      seems incredibly pretentious, while
      <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Vermeer" title="Johannes Vermeer">
       Vermeer
      </a>
      's human still lifes (also, the
      <a href="/wiki/Le_Nain" title="Le Nain">
       Le Nain brothers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Vuillard" title="douard Vuillard">
       Vuillard
      </a>
      ) are the nearest to reflecting this not-self state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For Huxley, the reconciliation of these cleansed perceptions with humanity reflects the age old debate between active and contemplative life, known as the way of
      <a href="/wiki/Martha" title="Martha">
       Martha
      </a>
      and the way of
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">
       Mary
      </a>
      . As Huxley believes that contemplation should also include action and charity, he concludes that the experience represents contemplation at its height, but not its fullness. Correct behaviour and alertness are needed. Nonetheless, Huxley maintains that even
      <a href="/wiki/Quietism_(Christian_philosophy)" title="Quietism (Christian philosophy)">
       quietistic
      </a>
      contemplation has an ethical value, because it is concerned with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_and_negative_value" title="Positive and negative value">
       negative virtues
      </a>
      and acts to channel the transcendent into the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kniphofia02.jpg">
        <img alt="Red Hot Poker or Kniphofia flowers." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1067" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Kniphofia02.jpg/170px-Kniphofia02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Kniphofia02.jpg/255px-Kniphofia02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Kniphofia02.jpg/340px-Kniphofia02.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kniphofia02.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Kniphofia" title="Kniphofia">
         Red Hot Poker
        </a>
        flowers in Huxley's garden were "so passionately alive that they seemed to be standing on the very brink of utterance".
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After listening to
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">
       Mozart
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Piano_Concerto_No._24_(Mozart)" title="Piano Concerto No. 24 (Mozart)">
       C-Minor Piano Concerto
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Gesualdo" title="Carlo Gesualdo">
       Gesualdo
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrigal_(music)" title="Madrigal (music)">
       madrigals
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">
       Alban Berg
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyric_Suite" title="Lyric Suite">
       Lyric Suite
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Huxley heads into the garden. Outside, the garden chairs take on such an immense intensity that he fears being overwhelmed; this gives him an insight into madness. He reflects that spiritual literature, including the works of
      <a href="/wiki/Jakob_B%C3%B6hme" title="Jakob Bhme">
       Jakob Bhme
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/William_Law" title="William Law">
       William Law
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Bardo_Thodol" title="Bardo Thodol">
       Tibetan Book of the Dead
      </a>
      , talk of these pains and terrors. Huxley speculates that schizophrenia is the inability to escape from this reality into the world of common sense and thus help would be essential.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After lunch and the drive to the WBDS he returns home and to his ordinary state of mind. His final insight is taken from Buddhist scripture: that within sameness there is difference, although that difference is not different from sameness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The book finishes with Huxley's final reflections on the meaning of his experience. Firstly, the urge to transcend one's self is universal through times and cultures (and was characterised by
      <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
       H. G. Wells
      </a>
      as The Door in the Wall).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He reasons that better, healthier "doors" are needed than alcohol and tobacco. Mescaline has the advantage of not provoking violence in takers, but its effects last an inconveniently long time and some users can have negative reactions. Ideally, self-transcendence would be found in religion, but Huxley feels that it is unlikely that this will ever happen. Christianity and mescaline seem well-suited for each other; the
      <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">
       Native American Church
      </a>
      for instance uses the drug as a sacrament, where its use combines religious feeling with decorum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Huxley concludes that mescaline is not
      <a href="/wiki/Enlightenment_(spiritual)" title="Enlightenment (spiritual)">
       enlightenment
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Beatific_vision" title="Beatific vision">
       Beatific vision
      </a>
      , but a "gratuitous grace" (a term taken from
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">
       Thomas Aquinas
      </a>
      '
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Summa_Theologica" title="Summa Theologica">
        Summa Theologica
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is not necessary but helpful, especially so for the intellectual, who can become the victim of words and symbols. Although systematic reasoning is important, direct perception has intrinsic value too. Finally, Huxley maintains that the person who has this experience will be transformed for the better.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
       Reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The book met with a variety of responses, both positive and negative,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bedford_1974.2C_p._142_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bedford_1974.2C_p._142-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      from writers in the field of literature, psychiatry, philosophy and religion. These included a symposium published in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_Review_(US_magazine)" title="Saturday Review (US magazine)">
       The Saturday Review
      </a>
      magazine with the unlikely title of,
      <i>
       Mescalin  An Answer to Cigarettes
      </i>
      , including contributions from Huxley; J.S. Slotkin, a professor of Anthropology; and a physician, Dr. W.C. Cutting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Literature
      </b>
      : For the Scottish poet,
      <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Muir" title="Edwin Muir">
       Edwin Muir
      </a>
      Mr. Huxley's experiment is extraordinary, and is beautifully described.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">
       Thomas Mann
      </a>
      , the author and friend of Huxley, believed the book demonstrated Huxley's escapism. He thought that while escapism found in mysticism might be honourable, drugs were not. Huxley's 'aesthetic self-indulgence' and indifference to humanity would lead to suffering or stupidity, and he concluded the book was irresponsible, if not quite immoral, to encourage young people to try the drug.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For Huxley's biographer and friend, the author
      <a href="/wiki/Sybille_Bedford" title="Sybille Bedford">
       Sybille Bedford
      </a>
      , the book combined sincerity with simplicity, passion with detachment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "It reflects the heart and mind open to meet the given, ready, even longing, to accept the wonderful.
      <i>
       The Doors
      </i>
      is a quiet book. It is also one that postulates a goodwill  the choice once more of the nobler hypothesis. It turned out, for certain temperaments, a seductive book.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For biographer David King Dunaway,
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      , along with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_Seeing" title="The Art of Seeing">
        The Art of Seeing
       </a>
      </i>
      , can be seen as the closest Huxley ever came to autobiographical writing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Psychiatric responses
      </b>
      included those of
      <a href="/wiki/William_Sargant" title="William Sargant">
       William Sargant
      </a>
      , the controversial British psychiatrist, who reviewed the book for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_British_Medical_Journal" title="The British Medical Journal">
       The British Medical Journal
      </a>
      and particularly focused on Huxley's reflections on schizophrenia. He wrote that the book brought to life the mental suffering of schizophrenics, which should make psychiatrists uneasy about their failure to relieve this. Also, he hoped that the book would encourage the investigation of the physiological, rather than psychological, aspects of psychiatry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other medical researchers questioned the validity of Huxley's account. The book contained "99 percent Aldous Huxley and only one half gram mescaline" according to Ronald Fisher.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Joost A.M. Meerloo found Huxley's reactions "not necessarily the same as... other people's experiences."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For Steven J. Novak,
      <i>
       The Doors Of Perception
      </i>
      (and
      <i>
       Heaven and Hell
      </i>
      ) redefined taking mescaline (and
      <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid_diethylamide" title="Lysergic acid diethylamide">
       LSD
      </a>
      , although Huxley had not taken it until after he had written both books) as a mystical experience with possible psychotherapeutic benefits, where physicians had previously thought of the drug in terms of mimicking a psychotic episode, known as
      <a href="/wiki/Psychotomimetic" title="Psychotomimetic">
       psychotomimetic
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The popularity of the book also affected research into these drugs, because researchers needed a random sample of subjects with no preconceptions about the drug to conduct experiments, and these became very difficult to find.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the field of
      <b>
       religion
      </b>
      , Huxleys friend and spiritual mentor, the Vedantic monk
      <a href="/wiki/Swami_Prabhavananda" title="Swami Prabhavananda">
       Swami Prabhavananda
      </a>
      , thought that mescaline was an illegitimate path to enlightenment, a "deadly heresy" as
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Isherwood" title="Christopher Isherwood">
       Christopher Isherwood
      </a>
      put it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray_2003.2C_p._401_23-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Murray_2003.2C_p._401-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Buber" title="Martin Buber">
       Martin Buber
      </a>
      , the Jewish religious philosopher, attacked Huxley's notion that mescaline allowed a person to participate in "common being", and held that the drug ushered users "merely into a strictly private sphere".
      <b>
       Philosophically
      </b>
      , Buber believed the drug experiences to be holidays "from the person participating in the community of
      <a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">
       logos
      </a>
      and cosmosholidays from the very uncomfortable reminder to verify oneself as such a person." For Buber man must master, withstand and alter his situation, or even leave it, "but the fugitive flight out of the claim of the situation into situationlessness is no legitimate affair of man."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_Charles_Zaehner">
       Robert Charles Zaehner
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Robert Charles Zaehner">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It was probably the criticisms of
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      put forward by
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Charles_Zaehner" title="Robert Charles Zaehner">
       Robert Charles Zaehner
      </a>
      , a professor at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">
       Oxford University
      </a>
      , that formed the fullest and earliest critiques from a religious and philosophical perspective. In 1954, Zaehner published an article called
      <i>
       The Menace of Mescaline
      </i>
      , in which he asserted that "artificial interference with consciousness" could have nothing to do with the Christian "Beatific Vision".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zaehner expanded on these criticisms in his book
      <i>
       Mysticism Sacred and Profane
      </i>
      (1957), which also acts as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theistic" title="Theistic">
       theistic
      </a>
      riposte to what he sees as the
      <a href="/wiki/Monism" title="Monism">
       monism
      </a>
      of Huxley's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Perennial_Philosophy" title="The Perennial Philosophy">
        The Perennial Philosophy
       </a>
      </i>
      . Although he acknowledged the importance of
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      as a challenge to people interested in religious experience,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      he pointed out what he saw as inconsistencies and self-contradictions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaehner_p._25_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zaehner_p._25-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zaehner concludes that Huxley's apprehensions under mescaline are affected by his deep familiarity with
      <a href="/wiki/Vedanta" title="Vedanta">
       Vedanta
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhism" title="Mahayana Buddhism">
       Mahayana Buddhism
      </a>
      . So the experience may not be the same for others who take the drug and do not have this background, although they will undoubtedly experience a transformation of sensation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zaehner himself was a convert to
      <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">
       Catholicism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      That the longing to transcend oneself is "one of the principal appetites of the soul"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is questioned by Zaehner. There are still people who do not feel this desire to escape themselves,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and religion itself need not mean escaping from the ego.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zaehner criticises what he sees as Huxley's apparent call for all religious people to use drugs (including alcohol) as part of their practices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Quoting St Paul's proscriptions against drunkenness in church, in
      <a href="/wiki/First_Epistle_to_the_Corinthians" title="First Epistle to the Corinthians">
       1 Corinthians
      </a>
      xi, Zaehner makes the point that artificial ecstatic states and spiritual union with God are not the same.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaehner_p._25_58-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zaehner_p._25-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Holding that there are similarities between the experience on mescaline, the
      <a href="/wiki/Mania" title="Mania">
       mania
      </a>
      in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manic-depressive_psychosis" title="Manic-depressive psychosis">
       manic-depressive psychosis
      </a>
      and the visions of God of a mystical saint suggests, for Zaehner, that the saint's visions must be the same as those of a lunatic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The personality is dissipated into the world, for Huxley on mescaline and people in a manic state, which is similar to the experience of nature mystics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, this experience is different from the theistic mystic who is absorbed into a God, who is quite different from the objective world. The appendices to
      <i>
       Mysticism Sacred and Profane
      </i>
      include three accounts of mescaline experiences, including those of Zaehner himself. He writes that he was transported into a world of farcical meaninglessness and notes that the experience was interesting and funny, but not religious.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Huston_Smith">
       Huston Smith
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Huston Smith">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Soon after the publication of his book, Huxley wrote to Harold Raymond at Chatto and Windus that he thought it strange that when
      <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Belloc" title="Hilaire Belloc">
       Hilaire Belloc
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">
       G. K. Chesterton
      </a>
      wrote the praises of alcohol they were still considered good Christians, while anyone who suggested other routes to self-transcendence was accused of being a drug addict and perverter of mankind.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later Huxley responded to Zaehner in an article published in 1961: "For most of those to whom the experiences have been vouchsafed, their value is self-evident. By Dr. Zaehner, the author of
      <i>
       Mysticism, Sacred and Profane
      </i>
      , their deliberate induction is regarded as immoral. To which his colleague,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.H._Price" title="H.H. Price">
       Professor Price
      </a>
      , retorts in effect, 'Speak for yourself!'".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Professor of religion and philosophy
      <a href="/wiki/Huston_Smith" title="Huston Smith">
       Huston Smith
      </a>
      took issue with the belief that
      <i>
       Mysticism Sacred and Profane
      </i>
      had fully examined and refuted Huxley's claims made in
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huston_Smith_1964_68-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Huston_Smith_1964-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Smith claims that consciousness-changing substances have been linked with religion both throughout history and across the world, and further it is possible that many religious perspectives had their origins in them, which were later forgotten. Acknowledging that personality, preparation and environment all play a role in the effects of the drugs, Huston Smith draws attention to evidence that suggests that a religious outcome of the experience may not be restricted to one of Huxley's temperament. Further, because Zaehner's experience was not religious, does not prove that none will be. Contrary to Zaehner, Huston Smith draws attention to evidence suggesting that these drugs can facilitate theistic mystical experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huston_Smith_1964_68-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Huston_Smith_1964-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As the descriptions of naturally occurring and drug-stimulated mystical experiences cannot be distinguished phenomenologically, Huston Smith regards Zaehner's position in
      <i>
       Mysticism Sacred and Profane
      </i>
      , as a product of the conflict between science and religion  that religion tends to ignore the findings of science. Nonetheless, although these drugs may produce a religious experience, they need not produce a religious life, unless set within a context of faith and discipline. Finally, he concludes that psychedelic drugs should not be forgotten in relation to religion because the phenomenon of religious
      <a href="/wiki/Awe" title="Awe">
       awe
      </a>
      , or the encounter with the
      <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">
       holy
      </a>
      , is declining and religion cannot survive long in its absence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huston_Smith_1964_68-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Huston_Smith_1964-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_experience">
       Later experience
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Later experience">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Huxley continued to take these substances several times a year until his death,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but with a serious and temperate frame of mind.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He refused to talk about the substances outside scientific meetings,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      turned down an invitation to talk about them on TV
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and refused the leadership of a foundation devoted to the study of psychedelics, explaining that they were only one of his diverse number of interests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For Philip Thody, a professor of French literature, Huxley's revelations made him conscious of the objections that had been put forward to his theory of mysticism set out in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eyeless_in_Gaza_(novel)" title="Eyeless in Gaza (novel)">
        Eyeless in Gaza
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grey_Eminence" title="Grey Eminence">
        Grey Eminence
       </a>
      </i>
      , and consequently
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Island_(Huxley_novel)" title="Island (Huxley novel)">
        Island
       </a>
      </i>
      reveals a more humane philosophy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        74
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, this change in perspective may lie elsewhere. In October 1955, Huxley had an experience while on LSD that he considered more profound than those detailed in
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      . Huxley was overwhelmed to the point where he decided his previous experiments, the ones detailed in
      <i>
       Doors
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Heaven_and_Hell_(essay)" title="Heaven and Hell (essay)">
        Heaven and Hell
       </a>
      </i>
      , had been nothing but entertaining sideshows.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens.2C_Jay_1998_75-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stevens.2C_Jay_1998-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He wrote in a letter to
      <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Osmond" title="Humphry Osmond">
       Humphry Osmond
      </a>
      , that he experienced "the direct, total awareness, from the inside, so to say, of Love as the primary and fundamental cosmic fact. ... I was this fact; or perhaps it would be more accurate to say that this fact occupied the place where I had been. ... And the things which had entirely filled my attention on that first occasion, I now perceived to be temptations  temptations to escape from the central reality into a false, or at least imperfect and partial Nirvanas of beauty and mere knowledge."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The experience made its way into the final chapter of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Island_(Huxley_novel)" title="Island (Huxley novel)">
        Island
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This raised a troublesome point. Was it better to pursue a course of careful psychological experimentation.... or was the real value of these drugs to "stimulate the most basic kind of religious ecstasy"?
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens.2C_Jay_1998_75-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stevens.2C_Jay_1998-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
       Influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doors_of_Perception&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Influence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A variety of influences have been claimed for the book. The psychedelic proselytiser,
      <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Leary" title="Timothy Leary">
       Timothy Leary
      </a>
      , was given the book by a colleague soon after returning from Mexico where he had first taken
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psilocybin_mushrooms" title="Psilocybin mushrooms">
       psilocybin mushrooms
      </a>
      in the summer of 1960. He found that
      <i>
       The Doors of Perception
      </i>
      corroborated what he had experienced 'and more too'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Leary soon set up a meeting with Huxley and the two became friendly. The book can also be seen as a part of the history of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entheogenic" title="Entheogenic">
       entheogenic
      </a>
      model of understanding these drugs, that sees them within a spiritual context.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        79
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Looking to broader culture, Huxley's experiment can be seen, alongside the work of other artists such as
      <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">
       John Cage
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Pollock" title="Jackson Pollock">
       Jackson Pollock
      </a>
      , as proposing a model of the imagination opposite to the symbolic, representational structures that had governed Western thought for centuries. Although this new direction cannot be attributed entirely to mescaline or Huxley, it had made a strong impact on politics, art and religion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
       <a href="#cite_note-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        80
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
