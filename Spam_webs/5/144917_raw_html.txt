          Picturebook Study: Wolf Comes To Town by Denis Manton
         </a>
        </h2>
        <div class="post-meta">
         <span class="post-date">
          <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/picturebook-study-wolf-comes-to-town-by-denis-manton/" title="Picturebook Study: Wolf Comes To Town by Denis Manton">

       <div class="post-content">
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Wolf-Comes-To-Town-Cover.jpg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Cover" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4061" height="472" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Wolf-Comes-To-Town-Cover.jpg" width="367"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Wolf Comes To Town
         </em>
         mustbe one of the most underrated childrens book on the Internet. I was genuinely astonished to check out what others have said about this picturebook on Amazon and Goodreads. Both sites show a 1.5 star average rating at time of writing. Can you guess what reviewers dont like about this book?
        </p>
        <p>
         A. The story is poorly written and edited.
        </p>
        <p>
         B. The illustrations are amateurish.
        </p>
        <p>
         C. Not suitable for children due to the main characterbehaving badly and notgoing punished.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-4060">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         If you guessed C, youre right. Anyone would think we still lived in the year 1697, which is when Charles Perrault published
         <i>
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Perrault">
           Tales and Stories of the Past with Morals
          </a>
          :
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <em>
           This book is terrible, it should not be in print. The wolf eats the dogs and cats and then
           <strong>
            the little boy in it is praised for suggesting the wolf should be burnt and have his head cut off.
           </strong>
           He is promptly told what a Brave little boy he is! Then it is indicated that the wolf has eaten the little boy when the boys mother finds his trousers and is sobbing!
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           <strong>
            There is no good, it will do nothing but corrupt young children.
           </strong>
           It was given to my young son from his school and this was for me to read to him, but it was a horror story and Im disgusted that I did just that. I would suggest this book is thrown away!
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
            Amazon 1 Star Review
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           As the blurb says, its a book about a wolf who dresses up to fool the towns people..he manages to steal all sorts of things, eat the pets and eventually a little boy (caught whilst dressed as the vicar!). I thought it was funny for a bit- my daughter had some fun spotting the wolf dressed up, but
           <strong>
            there is no moral come back at all- he runs off with his dressing up clothes to terrorise the next town, and so the story ends on a bit of a down.
           </strong>
           The language and style suits 3 years plus, but
           <strong>
            some young children would be upset by it.
           </strong>
           My copy is in the bin (not the charity shop!). Also- are there no books out there where the wolf isnt so bad?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
            Amazon 1 Star Review
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           I could not believe it when my 3 year old brought this horrible story home from school.
          </em>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           <em>
            The wolf steals, eats pet cats, eats pet dogs, and eats a little boy.
           </em>
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <em>
           <strong>
            There is no comeuppance, no moral.
           </strong>
           <strong>
            There is nothing it indicate to children that his behaviour is wrong. He gets away with it with no punishment.
           </strong>
          </em>
          <br/>
          <em>
           My son was upset by the boy getting eaten.
          </em>
          <br/>
          <em>
           Dreadful book.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
            Amazon 1 Star Review
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           <strong>
            Because there is no real ending to this book.
           </strong>
           Im a Childrens Librarian, and I ran across this while looking for books about stealing for a patron. The book starts out well enough; the big bad wolf disguises himself in order to steal whatever he pleases from stores &amp; shops. The townspeople finally find the wolfs home (while searching for brave little Bernard, a young boy the wolf takes), and see all their belongings inside. Everyone claims their things, Bernards mother finds her sons trousers, and the wolfs clothes are packed up to be given to charity.
           <strong>
            Only the nurse doing the packing is really the wolf in disguise, and he makes off with the clothes to a new town. Thats the end of the story. Theres no conclusion, the wolf doesnt get punished for his bad behavior, and the reader doesnt know if little Bernard is alive or dead.
           </strong>
           Cute beginning, terrible ending. I would not choose this book for storytime, as a learning tool, or anything else.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
            Amazon 2 Star Review
          </em>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <h3>
         PLOT/STORY
        </h3>
        <p>
         A wolf lives on the outskirts of a town. He dresses up in all sorts of disguises, then visits the town to steal things he likes and needs.
        </p>
        <p>
         He tends to dress up as trustworthy people: policemen, priests.
        </p>
        <p>
         One day he eats a little boy at a garden party.
        </p>
        <p>
         The townspeople go out looking for Bernard  the wolf among them  and happen across his house, chock full of all the things he has stolen.
        </p>
        <p>
         Wolf manages to prevent his clothes from being thrown out. He takes them in a trolley to the next town, where he will presumably do exactly as he did in this one.
        </p>
        <h3>
         WONDERFULNESS
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          A Fun Catch Phrase
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Possibly my favourite aspect of this book is the phrase, I like it, I want it and ILL TAKE IT! In oral retellings of Little Red Riding Hood, too, the teller of the story would pounce in playful fashion at the child when the grandma wolf pounced at Little Red. This book is equally well suited to a dramatic retelling between parent and child.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_4067">
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2726.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Final Image" class="size-large wp-image-4067" height="653" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2726-768x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Wolf Comes To Town Final Image
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Frisson Of Fear In Relation To Real Life
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Whose town is the wolf in now? Roald Dahl makes use of this kind of fear in his book The Witches, in which the reader is told that even the teacher who is reading this book to you right now might be a witch. I was read The Witches by my teacher when I was six, and I do remember looking at her very closely, concluding she couldnt be because she wasnt wearing gloves.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Modern Morals For Modern Readers
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In common with various famous fairytales 
         <em>
          Little Red Riding Hood
         </em>
         springs first to mind  readers are warned not to take people at face value. The beautiful young woman is really a wolf. The policeman is really a wolf. The vicar is really a wolf. Although the classic tales have been watered down for modern children  starting with Grimm and completed by Disney  this story harks back to the pre-Grimm days, in which villains were not punished.
        </p>
        <p>
         In these days of Occupy Wall Street, which is the more important lesson for young readers? In real life, villains most often
         <em>
          do
         </em>
         go unpunished. They dress in respectable clothes and walk among us.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the picture below, an unsuspecting art gallery owner gives too much information to a criminal. When identities are hidden online, this is not a bad lesson for the rest of us:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2720.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Policeman In Art Gallery" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4068" height="653" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2720-768x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Other people are locked out of certain establishments based on what they look like. Here, a man has been excluded from a fancy restaurant simply for having grown fat and a beard. Use this to open up a discussion about all sorts of discrimination, including throughout history:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2719.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Fancy Restaurant" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4069" height="653" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2719-768x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         This is the double page spread where the story takes a particularly sinister turn:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2723.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Denis Manton Garden Party" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4072" height="336" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2723-1024x703.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         But again, is it so wrong to introduce the idea that even priests at garden parties cannot necessarily be trusted? In traditional fairytale fashion, Little Bernard does not survive this story:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2724.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Little Bernard's Little Trousers" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4073" height="653" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2724-768x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Especially disturbing, against the comic line Little Bernards little trousers! is the art gallery owner who is far more interested in his stolen painting than in this mothers missing son. In storybooks as in real life, sometimes we get our priorities wrong.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h3>
         NOTES ON THE ILLUSTRATION
        </h3>
        <p>
         The main challenge for Denis Manton in illustrating this work would have been to depict a wolf who looked sufficiently like a person when dressed up like one. He has risen to this challenge by turning the snout into a large nose.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_4066">
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2721.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Denis Manton Milkman" class="size-large wp-image-4066" height="653" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2721-768x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          The milkman (wolf) has a larger than average nose.
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         Because of this challenge, the loose, sketchy style works best. When the style is loose like this, the wolf looks different every time we see him, but the reader can put this down to the style, and accept its the same creature. The sketchy style is also fitting for the generally chaotic scenes: the wolfs messy abode, scenes of people screaming and running away, scenes with lots of movement etc.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2714.jpeg">
          <img alt="wolf dresses up in front of mirror" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4065" height="323" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2714-1024x674.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         The opposite choice  a photorealisticstyle of illustration  would result in a storytoo scary for a young audience. This art, with its black outlines and comic style, tells the audience that this isa comic tale. In keeping with comic book style, the facial expressions in this tale are marvellous:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2717.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Teeth Bared" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4070" height="635" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2717-790x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2715.jpeg">
          <img alt="Wolf Comes To Town Scary Teeth" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4074" height="653" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_2715-768x1024.jpeg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <h3>
         STORY SPECS
        </h3>
        <p>
         Written and illustrated by Australian Denis Manton in 1994, this book is now hard to get, possibly helped along its way to obscurityto the large numbers of adult gatekeepers who refused to buy or recommend this story.
        </p>
        <p>
         Manton seems to have illustrated more books than he wrote, and the books that he wrote seem to have fallen out of print, including
         <em>
          Anastasia
         </em>
         , published 1979.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h3>
         COMPARE WITH
        </h3>
        <p>
         Another picturebook which has certain adult readers up in arms is
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/picturebook-study-this-is-not-my-hat-by-jon-klassen/">
          <strong>
           This Is Not My Hat
          </strong>
         </a>
         by Jon Klassen, also accused of having no repercussion for stealing and no definitive ending.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://picturebooksblogger.wordpress.com/2015/02/08/red/">
          <strong>
           This retelling of Little Red Riding Hood
          </strong>
         </a>
         garners some similar comments regarding its suitability for children.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/PYB_LittleRedHood_Cover1websize3.jpg">
          <img alt="Little Red Hood Cover" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-4075" height="300" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/PYB_LittleRedHood_Cover1websize3-1024x627.jpg" width="490"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Some of my own favourite childhood books have catch-phrases which heighten the excitement. Enid Blytons Thirteen OClock is memorable for its repeated phrase,
         <em>
          The witches are coming! The witches are coming!
         </em>
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_4071">
         <a href="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/thirteen-o-clock-enid-blyton.jpg">
          <img alt="Thirteen O'Clock by Enid Blyton, 1970s edition" class="size-full wp-image-4071" height="290" src="http://www.slaphappylarry.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/thirteen-o-clock-enid-blyton.jpg" width="222"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Thirteen OClock by Enid Blyton, 1970s edition
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
