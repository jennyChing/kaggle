     <p>
      After
      <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">
       the Beatles
      </a>
      released the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rubber_Soul" title="Rubber Soul">
        Rubber Soul
       </a>
      </i>
      album at the end of 1965, they recorded their follow up album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Revolver_(Beatles_album)" title="Revolver (Beatles album)">
        Revolver
       </a>
      </i>
      then embarked on a world tour. In making these two albums, the group recorded music that had become increasingly difficult to reproduce on stage. But that problem only scratched the surface of problems the band would face in 1966, which included risking their lives in
      <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">
       Manila
      </a>
      at the hands of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marcos" title="Ferdinand Marcos">
       Marcos
      </a>
      family.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">
       Japan
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Japan">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="3578" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg/200px-Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg/300px-Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg/400px-Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nippon_Budokan_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Nippon Budokan Hall Main entrance
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Asian leg of the tour began with controversy as right-wing Japanese nationalists protested at the staging of their concerts in
      <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">
       Tokyo
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Budokan" title="Nippon Budokan">
       Nippon Budokan
      </a>
      in June and July which was built for the staging of the
      <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">
       martial arts
      </a>
      rather than musical performances.
     </p>
     <p>
      The performances went ahead but there was heavy security to protect the group from both fans and protesters,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShowdownatBudokan_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ShowdownatBudokan-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      meaning the group were forced to stay in their suite at the Tokyo Hilton, although Lennon escaped for a short time.
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Epstein" title="Brian Epstein">
       NEMS
      </a>
      staff photographer,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Whitaker_(photographer)" title="Robert Whitaker (photographer)">
       Robert Whitaker
      </a>
      , remembered: "He [Lennon] snuck out using my name, on the basis, I think, that all white people probably looked the same to the Japanese. He got away with it for a while, but then somebody recognised him and he was dragged back to the hotel." The foreign artists who have played the Budokan since then (with no protests against their appearances) include
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">
       Bob Dylan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">
       Led Zeppelin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Oasis_(band)" title="Oasis (band)">
       Oasis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kiss_(band)" title="Kiss (band)">
       KISS
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cheap_Trick" title="Cheap Trick">
       Cheap Trick
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ozzy_Osbourne" title="Ozzy Osbourne">
       Ozzy Osbourne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dream_Theater" title="Dream Theater">
       Dream Theater
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Beyonc%C3%A9" title="Beyonc">
       Beyonc
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magicalmiserytour_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Magicalmiserytour-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Philippines.2C_and_the_Height_of_The_Beatles.27_Turmoil">
       The Philippines, and the Height of The Beatles' Turmoil
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Philippines, and the Height of The Beatles' Turmoil">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Later in July when the Beatles toured the
      <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
       Philippines
      </a>
      , they unintentionally snubbed the nation's first lady,
      <a href="/wiki/Imelda_Marcos" title="Imelda Marcos">
       Imelda Marcos
      </a>
      , who had expected the group to attend a breakfast reception at the Presidential Palace.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitz2005619_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitz2005619-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When presented with the invitation, Brian Epstein politely declined on behalf of the group, as it had never been the group's policy to accept such "official" invitations, but the group soon found out that the Marcos regime was unaccustomed to accepting "no" for an answer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitz2005620_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitz2005620-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Imelda Marcos was infuriated when finding out that one of her frequent grand parties of 200 guests did not include the Beatles.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the snub was broadcast on Philippine television and radio, all of their police protection disappeared. Epstein tried to make an apology on Channel 5 in the
      <a href="/wiki/Manila_Hotel" title="Manila Hotel">
       Manila Hotel
      </a>
      , but the state-controlled channel blacked out when it reached his interview.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The group and their entourage had to make their way to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manila_International_Airport" title="Manila International Airport">
       Manila International Airport
      </a>
      on their own. The hotel was being attacked and everyone had to make way to the airport. The halls of the hotel were dark and lined with staff who shouted at them in Spanish and English.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the Beatles moved through the terminal, little bands of demonstrators appeared, grabbing at them and trying to hit them. They checked in for their flight as quickly as possible then were herded into a lounge "where an abusive crowd and police with guns had also gathered." At the airport, road manager
      <a href="/wiki/Mal_Evans" title="Mal Evans">
       Mal Evans
      </a>
      was beaten and kicked, and the band members were pushed and jostled about by a hostile crowd.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitz2005623_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitz2005623-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ringo Starr would quip, "they started spitting at us, spitting on us."
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The Beatles hid among a group of nuns and monks huddled by an alcove. Other members of their entourage, though, were kicked and beaten. They could not even differentiate between the angry mob or the cops carrying guns trying to maul them. Lennon described hearing people yell "You're treated like ordinary passengers!" as they pushed them around. He then quipped that "ordinary passengers" were not treated as badly.
     </p>
     <p>
      Paul McCartney said, "When we got on the plane, we were all kissing the seats. It was feeling as if wed found sanctuary. We had definitely been in a foreign country where all the rules had changed and they carried guns. So we werent too gung-ho about it at all."
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Ringo remembered being afraid of going to jail. Once the group boarded the plane, Epstein and Evans were ordered off, and Evans (fearing he would be imprisoned or executed) said, "Tell [my wife] that I love her."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitz2005624_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitz2005624-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Epstein was forced to give the tax authorities 6,800 worth of Philippine peso notes from the Manila shows, and had to sign the tax bond verifying the exchange before being allowed back on the plane.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitz2005625_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitz2005625-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon leaving the country, band members publicly expressed resentment at their treatment, with John Lennon saying "If we go back, it will be with an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">
       H-bomb
      </a>
      . I won't even fly over the place."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This also caused the band to end their Asia tour in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
       Religion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/More_popular_than_Jesus" title="More popular than Jesus">
       More popular than Jesus
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Almost as soon as they returned from the Philippines, an earlier comment by Lennon made in March that year launched a backlash against the Beatles from religious and social conservatives in the United States. In an interview with British reporter
      <a href="/wiki/Maureen_Cleave" title="Maureen Cleave">
       Maureen Cleave
      </a>
      published in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evening_Standard" title="Evening Standard">
       Evening Standard
      </a>
      on 4 March,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lennon had offered his opinion that
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      was dying and that the Beatles were "more popular than
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      now".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnLennonRemembered_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JohnLennonRemembered-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As the British public were used to Lennon's often caustic comments, it did not create a stir. The controversy began when the interview was reprinted in the American teenage fan magazine
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datebook" title="Datebook">
       DATEbook
      </a>
      with a front cover story.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheBeatlesAreBigger_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TheBeatlesAreBigger-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JohnLennonforgiven_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JohnLennonforgiven-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Afterwards, radio station
      <a href="/wiki/WAYE" title="WAYE">
       WAQY
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">
       Birmingham, Alabama
      </a>
      announced it was banning Beatles music and would hold a bonfire to burn Beatles records. Dozens of other radio stations followed suit and several stations also held bonfires. Attempting to make light of the incident, Harrison said, "They've got to buy them before they can burn them."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blecha181_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blecha181-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Epstein held a press conference at the Sheraton Hotel in New York City on 6 August 1966, reading a statement by Lennon, and then answering questions from the press. Epstein: The quote which John Lennon made to a London columnist nearly three months ago has been quoted and misrepresented entirely out of context... Lennon didn't mean to boast about the Beatles' fame. He meant to point out that The Beatles' effect appeared to be a more immediate one upon, certainly, the younger generation. John is deeply concerned and regrets that people with certain religious beliefs should have been offended.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Under further pressure from the American media, Lennon continued to explain his remarks at press conferences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilliland1969show_39.2C_track_2_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilliland1969show_39.2C_track_2-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Chicago on 11 August 1966, on the eve of the first performance of what turned out to be their final tour,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MilesPage293-295_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MilesPage293-295-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      his comments were more explanation than apology. Lennon was expressing his opinion that religion, in England, was losing its influence with young people; and, he was using the popularity of the Beatles as an example because the group's success was an analogy with which he could identify. He lamented that if he had said "television was more popular than Jesus" rather than naming his group, there would not have been any controversy at all. The controversy was on everyone's mind when the tour went through the region. In Memphis, a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">
       Ku Klux Klan
      </a>
      promised a few "surprises" for the group for their upcoming concert. Indeed, there was minor panic when a firecracker was set off on stage at a concert at the
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-South_Coliseum" title="Mid-South Coliseum">
       Mid-South Coliseum
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">
       Memphis, Tennessee
      </a>
      on 19 August.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeatlesInterviewMemphisTennessee_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BeatlesInterviewMemphisTennessee-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After the controversy and fallout surrounding Lennon's remarks, his cynicism with the media grew and he remained guarded in interviews the rest of his life. In subsequent interviews of Lennon before the break-up of the group, his responses to questions were often sardonic and cutting.
     </p>
     <p>
      In November, 2008, the Vatican publicly announced that it had "forgiven" John Lennon for his remarks, saying it was a "boast" by a young man grappling with sudden fame.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopeforgivesJohnLennon_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PopeforgivesJohnLennon-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Yesterday_and_Today_cover">
       The
       <i>
        Yesterday and Today
       </i>
       cover
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Yesterday and Today cover">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Beatles_-_Butcher_Cover.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/The_Beatles_-_Butcher_Cover.jpg/200px-The_Beatles_-_Butcher_Cover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/55/The_Beatles_-_Butcher_Cover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/55/The_Beatles_-_Butcher_Cover.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Beatles_-_Butcher_Cover.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <i>
         Yesterday and Today
        </i>
        "butcher cover"
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      On 25 March 1966, the group took part in a photo session in a London studio (1 The Vale, Chelsea). The studio was owned by Oluf Nissen, but Whitaker took the photographs.
     </p>
     <p>
      The publicity shots were used for the American
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Yesterday_and_Today" title="Yesterday and Today">
        Yesterday and Today
       </a>
      </i>
      album and a poster promoting the UK release of "
      <a href="/wiki/Paperback_Writer" title="Paperback Writer">
       Paperback Writer
      </a>
      ". The photograph created an uproar, as it featured the band dressed in butchers' overalls, draped in meat and mutilated plastic dolls. A popular, though
      <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">
       apocryphal
      </a>
      , rumour said that this was meant as a response to the way Capitol had "butchered" their albums.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheBeatlesStory_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TheBeatlesStory-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WhoButcheredTheBeatles_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WhoButcheredTheBeatles-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Capitol parent
      <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">
       EMI
      </a>
      chairman
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Joseph_Lockwood" title="Sir Joseph Lockwood">
       Sir Joseph Lockwood
      </a>
      ordered the butcher cover withdrawn.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thousands of copies of the album had a new cover pasted over.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MilesPage293-295_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MilesPage293-295-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Whitaker later explained that he was trying to create a satirical commentary on their fame, and that the images of dismembered doll and mannequin parts had been inspired by the German surrealist
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bellmer" title="Hans Bellmer">
       Hans Bellmer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Uncensored copies of
      <i>
       Yesterday and Today
      </i>
      command a high price today, with one copy selling for $10,500 at a December 2005 auction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_BeatlesButcherCover_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_BeatlesButcherCover-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unpeeled copies of the pasted over sleeve are also collectable.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion">
       Fashion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fashion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      1966 would mark the last year that the Beatles would be tied to their classic "Mop-Top" image (although their hair was noticeably longer by this time) and all-together unified look. The individual members took a three-month hiatus between their final concert and work on their next album,
      <i>
       Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band.
      </i>
      During this hiatus, each member of the group coincidentally grew a moustache without consulting the others. Lennon began wearing his signature "granny" glasses during the filming of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/How_I_Won_The_War" title="How I Won The War">
        How I Won The War
       </a>
      </i>
      , cut his hair (he played the role of a soldier in that movie) and switched to wearing colourful paisley suits. They were never again filmed performing with a unified look, with the exception of a mocking scene in the promo for the 1967 song, Hello Goodbye.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Touring">
       Touring
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Touring">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Beatles played their last concert at
      <a href="/wiki/Candlestick_Park" title="Candlestick Park">
       Candlestick Park
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      on 29 August 1966.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Immediately after the tour, the band took a three-month break from one another to follow individual pursuits. Lennon accepted a supporting role in the film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/How_I_Won_the_War" title="How I Won the War">
        How I Won the War
       </a>
      </i>
      , and it was during filming that he wrote "
      <a href="/wiki/Strawberry_Fields_Forever" title="Strawberry Fields Forever">
       Strawberry Fields Forever
      </a>
      ". McCartney wrote the score for the film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Family_Way" title="The Family Way">
        The Family Way
       </a>
      </i>
      . Harrison and his wife
      <a href="/wiki/Pattie_Boyd" title="Pattie Boyd">
       Pattie
      </a>
      , whom he had married in January, travelled to
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      , where he met and took
      <a href="/wiki/Sitar" title="Sitar">
       sitar
      </a>
      lessons from
      <a href="/wiki/Ravi_Shankar" title="Ravi Shankar">
       Ravi Shankar
      </a>
      and immersed himself in the local culture and religion. Starr mostly stayed at home with his family.
     </p>
     <p>
      A visit by Lennon to the
      <a href="/wiki/Indica_Gallery" title="Indica Gallery">
       Indica Gallery
      </a>
      in London on 9 November would shape the future of The Beatles as it was there that Lennon met
      <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Ono" title="Yoko Ono">
       Yoko Ono
      </a>
      for the first time. The group reconvened on 24 November to begin the sessions for what would become
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band">
        Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Because the group did not record a new album for the Christmas season as they did the previous three years,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parlophone_Records" title="Parlophone Records">
       Parlophone Records
      </a>
      issued the group's first compilation album, titled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Collection_of_Beatles_Oldies" title="A Collection of Beatles Oldies">
        A Collection of Beatles Oldies
       </a>
      </i>
      , on 10 December.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recordings">
       Recordings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Beatles_in_1966&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recordings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Beatles recorded 18 songs during calendar year 1966, the fewest recordings in any year for the group between 1963-1969. Fourteen of the songs were released on the UK version of their album,
      <i>
       Revolver
      </i>
      . They recorded "Paperback Writer" and "Rain" which were issued as the A and B sides, respectively, of a single record release. Two songs, "Strawberry Fields Forever" and "When I'm Sixty-Four" were recorded late in the year. Originally intended as the first sessions for the next album
      <i>
       Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band
      </i>
      , the former was issued as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">
       double A-side
      </a>
      single that also included "Penny Lane". "When I'm Sixty-Four" was included on the album released in 1967.
     </p>
