(Photograph by Leica Passionara, Flickr)" height="442" src="http://intelligenttravel.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/08/socca-nice-france.jpg" title="Socca - Nice, France" width="590"/>
          <div class="caption" style="width:590px;">
           Socca at Nissa Socca, a restaurant near the Cours Saleya in Nice. (Photograph by Leica Passionara, Flickr)
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="main">
          <div class="content entry-content">
           <p>
            You have to eat
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farinata" target="_blank" title="Wikipedia site - Farinata (socca)">
             <em>
              socca
             </em>
            </a>
            to understand it, says Caterina Prochilo, cradling a piping hot slice fresh from Theresas copper pan. Socca is a cross between a crepe and a pancake, a thin disc made with chickpea flour, and its a specialty of Nice, the unofficial capital of Frances Cote dAzur.
           </p>
           <p>
            Prochilo, a resident of Nice, takes visitors to
            <a href="http://www.tripadvisor.com/Attraction_Review-g187234-d255251-Reviews-Cours_Saleya-Nice_French_Riviera_Cote_d_Azur_Provence.html" target="_blank" title="TripAdvisor site - Cours Saleya review">
             <strong>
              Cours Saleya
             </strong>
            </a>
            , Old Towns open-air market, to sample the socca at Theresas stand, where the behatted grande dame has presided for decades.
           </p>
           <p>
            While socca shares culinary roots with Genoas
            <em>
             farinata
            </em>
            and Tuscanys
            <em>
             cecina
            </em>
            , it has developed its own distinctive style and flavor. Chickpea flour is combined with a mixture of water and olive oil, forming a batter, then baked on the pie-plate-like
            <em>
             plaque
            </em>
            in a wood-fired brick oven.The golden, dimpled cake that emerges  tender on the inside and crisp on the edges  balances the nutty taste of chickpeas with the tang of ground pepper.
           </p>
           <p>
            The fast food first gained favor in the mid-19th century as a snack for fishermen who would wolf down slices from wagons with charcoal ovens.True to tradition, socca is served in a paper cone, no utensils needed.
           </p>
           <p>
            Amble toward the port and
            <a href="http://www.chezpipo.fr/" target="_blank" title="Chez Pipo site">
             <strong>
              Chez Pipo
             </strong>
            </a>
            , which has specialized in socca since 1923. Sitting on the terrace with friendly locals, eating a savory slice of socca with a glass of chilled rose, offers an unforgettable taste of old, and abiding, Nice.
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             This piece, written by
             <a href="http://intelligenttravel.nationalgeographic.com/author/dgeorge/" target="_blank" title="Intelligent Travel site - Don George">
              Don George
             </a>
             , appeared in the June/July 2013 issue of
            </em>
            National Geographic Traveler
            <em>
             magazine. Follow Don on Twitter
             <a href="https://twitter.com/don_george" target="_blank" title="Twitter site - Don George profile">
              @don_george
             </a>
             .
            </em>
           </p>
