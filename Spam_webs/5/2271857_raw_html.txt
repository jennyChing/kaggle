           <p>
            <i>
             Here in the United States, ski season will come to a close in a few weeks. Theres time left to squeeze in one last run, and if you ask Contributing Blogger
             <a href="http://www.newstorytelling.org/Home/Welcome.html">
              Cathy Healy,
             </a>
            </i>
            <i>
             shell recommend it be a long one.
            </i>
            <br/>
            <span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image">
             <a href="http://blogs.nationalgeographic.com/blogs/intelligenttravel/longrun.jpg">
              <img alt="longrun.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="360px" src="http://intelligenttravel.nationalgeographic.com/files/2011/04/longrun-thumb-625x469-480x360.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0pt auto 20px;" width="480px"/>
             </a>
            </span>
            Engineers dont approach the mountains like we reporters, which means you can count on an engineer to give you memorable stories. With huge dumps of snow in the United States this year, I asked an engineer pal in Buenos Aires if he planned to come skiing up here. Last August, he had emailed me: Just returned from
            <a href="http://www.laslenas.com/eng/">
             Las Leas
            </a>
            in
            <b>
             Argentina
            </b>
            , 93 miles of skiing accomplished, with zero access to Internet.
           </p>
           <p>
            Cmon. How could he know that? Pedometers cant translate glides into steps.
           </p>
           <p>
            Yet I discovered his method was quite primitive:
           </p>
           <p>
            	Get a large trail map.
           </p>
           <p>
            	Steal a ruler from your son.
           </p>
           <p>
            	Get pen and paper to note calculated distances.
           </p>
           <p>
            	Pull out your record pad and carefully record number of trails and number of runs.
           </p>
           <p>
            	Figure out the length of the trails using the simple rule of three, based on the known length of the three successive runs at Las Leas, which are advertised as the longest runs in South America at 14.9 miles.
           </p>
           <p>
            Meanwhile, Im armchair skiing and wondering how to log 100 miles in a week. Consider one of these ski equations after the jump:
           </p>
           <p>
            <span id="more-8406">
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image">
             <a href="http://blogs.nationalgeographic.com/blogs/intelligenttravel/rivaridge.jpg">
              <img alt="rivaridge.jpg" class="mt-image-left" height="449px" src="http://intelligenttravel.nationalgeographic.com/files/2011/04/rivaridge-thumb-300x449.jpg" style="float: left; margin: 0pt 20px 20px 0pt;" width="299px"/>
             </a>
            </span>
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Youll have to ski the 13.7 miles of
             <a href="http://www.chamonix.net/english/skiing/valleeblanche/valleeblanche.htm">
              Chamonixs Valle Blanche
             </a>
             glacier in
             <b>
              France
             </b>
             7.3 times to hit the 100 mile mark. Perhaps the longest run in the world through the French wilderness, it all starts with a 45-minute, adrenaline climb down an icy ridge led by a mountain guide, wearing
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crampons">
              crampons
             </a>
             while you carry your skis on your shoulders.  Can you do the Valle every day to hit the magic 100 in a week?
             <p>
              A nameless, ex-army Brit told Trip Advisor: I was extremely fit at the timebut because my technique was poor, I found the Valle Blanchethe most physically demanding thing I have ever done. You could have fried eggs on my thighs at the bottom!
             </p>
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Jump in a cable car and ride to the top of
             <a href="http://www.ski-zermatt.com/winter.html">
              Zermatt
             </a>
             s Klein Matterhorn in
             <b>
              Switzerland
             </b>
             . Twenty-nine peaks rise to an elevation of 13,000 feet, providing stunning panoramic views of Europe. An 8-mile run from summit to village means youll have to altitude adjust 12.5 times to clock 100 miles.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Do 22.3 runs down
             <a href="http://www.vail.com/mountain/explore-mountain/explore-mountain.aspx">
              Vail
             </a>
             s Riva Ridge in
             <b>
              Colorado
             </b>
             , one of the longest U.S. runs at 4.5 miles. Its fabulous! Its nice and long; it has a friendly pitch and they groom it all the time, said my brothers brother-in-law, John Bailey, from a Vail chairlift. Added John, 68, a retired engineer: Its an easy black diamond.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Meanwhile my favorite long run is Keystone, Colorados Schoolmarm, an extreme bunny slope where I have floated from the top of the mountain to the bottom for 3.5 miles of zen bliss. Umm, I could make 28.6 runs and Id have my 100 miles. The last laughs on me: Engineers ski; reporters report.
            <br/>
            <i>
             <br/>
             SKI REPORT: Vail Resorts close on Sunday, April 24. Keystone closes Sunday, April 10. Chamonix closes around the end of April. Dont fret, Las Leas opens on June 18 and Zermatts Klein Matterhorn is open year-round.
            </i>
           </p>
           <p>
            Photo: Above, Chamonixs Valle Blanche by
            <i>
             <a href="http://ngm.nationalgeographic.com/myshot/gallery/127635#/gallery/403868/">
              Pierce Currid
             </a>
             /My Shot; Below, Vails Riva Ridge by Jack Affleck courtesy of Vail Resorts.
            </i>
           </p>
