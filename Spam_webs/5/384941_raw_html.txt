     <p>
      <b>
       Shadrach Minkins
      </b>
      (1814?December 13, 1875) was an African-American
      <a href="/wiki/Fugitive_slaves_in_the_United_States" title="Fugitive slaves in the United States">
       fugitive slave
      </a>
      from Virginia who escaped in 1850 and reached Boston. He is known for being freed from a courtroom in Boston after being captured by United States marshals under the
      <a href="/wiki/Fugitive_Slave_Act_of_1850" title="Fugitive Slave Act of 1850">
       Fugitive Slave Act of 1850
      </a>
      . White and black members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Boston_Vigilance_Committee" title="Boston Vigilance Committee">
       Boston Vigilance Committee
      </a>
      freed and hid him, helping him get to Canada via the
      <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">
       Underground Railroad
      </a>
      . Minkins settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      where he raised a family. Two men were prosecuted in Boston for helping free him, but they were acquitted by the jury.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadrach_Minkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Minkins was born into slavery about 1814 in
      <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">
       Norfolk, Virginia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Escape_and_capture">
       Escape and capture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadrach_Minkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Escape and capture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      He escaped from slavery as a young man in 1850 and reached
      <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">
       Boston
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">
       Massachusetts
      </a>
      , where he became a waiter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later that year, Congress enacted the
      <a href="/wiki/Fugitive_Slave_Act_of_1850" title="Fugitive Slave Act of 1850">
       Fugitive Slave Act
      </a>
      , which allowed federal agents to seize escaped slaves living in
      <a href="/wiki/Slave_and_free_states" title="Slave and free states">
       free states
      </a>
      and return them to their owners. It required law enforcement in all states to cooperate in enforcing this federal law.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service" title="United States Marshals Service">
       United States marshals
      </a>
      , who posed as customers at Tafts Cornhill Coffee House where Minkins worked, arrested him on February 15, 1851.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_History_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mass_History-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writ_of_habeas_corpus">
       Writ of habeas corpus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadrach_Minkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writ of habeas corpus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Minkins was taken to a hearing at the Boston federal courthouse. Attorneys, including Samuel E. Sewall, Ellis Gray Loring,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_(lawyer)" title="Robert Morris (lawyer)">
       Robert Morris
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Henry_Dana,_Jr." title="Richard Henry Dana, Jr.">
       Richard Henry Dana, Jr.
      </a>
      , offered their services to defend Minkins. Seeking to have Minkins released from police custody, they filed a petition for writ of
      <a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">
       habeas corpus
      </a>
      with the Supreme Judicial Court, which was refused by Chief Justice Lemuel Shaw.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_History_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mass_History-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_G._Walker" title="Edward G. Walker">
       Edward G. Walker
      </a>
      , Morris and activist
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Hayden" title="Lewis Hayden">
       Lewis Hayden
      </a>
      collaborated to obtain Shadrach's release.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_Past_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Black_Past-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      White and black members of the anti-slavery
      <a href="/wiki/Boston_Vigilance_Committee" title="Boston Vigilance Committee">
       Boston Vigilance Committee
      </a>
      , including Hayden, entered the courtroom and used force to take Minkins from the marshals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_History_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mass_History-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_History_FL_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mass_History_FL-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was hidden in an attic in
      <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Hill,_Boston" title="Beacon Hill, Boston">
       Beacon Hill
      </a>
      . Minkins escaped Massachusetts with the help of John J. Smith, Lewis Hayden and others. Nine abolitionists were indicted in the affair, but charges were dismissed for some of the individuals. Morris and Hayden were tried and acquitted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_History_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mass_History-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Resulting_trials">
       Resulting trials
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadrach_Minkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Resulting trials">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The rescue of Minkins brought calls for President
      <a href="/wiki/Millard_Fillmore" title="Millard Fillmore">
       Millard Fillmore
      </a>
      to use federal troops to help marshals enforce the Fugitive Slave Law. Fillmore cautiously called on the citizens of Boston to respect the law and aid in recapturing Minkins. Fillmore ordered Minkins' liberators to be prosecuted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/John_P._Hale" title="John P. Hale">
       John P. Hale
      </a>
      served as defense counsel in the resulting trials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This incident in his home state deeply embarrassed Secretary of State
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Webster" title="Daniel Webster">
       Daniel Webster
      </a>
      , who hoped to be elected President in 1852 with Southern support.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">
        <span>

        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">
       Canada
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadrach_Minkins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      From Boston, activists helped Minkins reach Canada via stops on the
      <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">
       Underground Railroad
      </a>
      . He settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      , in the section of the city known as
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Montreal" title="Old Montreal">
       Old Montreal
      </a>
      . There he made a living first as a waiter, then operating restaurants of his own and, finally, as a
      <a href="/wiki/Barber" title="Barber">
       barber
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_History_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mass_History-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He married in 1853 or 1854.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Minkins died in Montreal in December 1875. He is buried in an unmarked grave near two of his children in
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Royal_Cemetery" title="Mount Royal Cemetery">
       Mount Royal Cemetery
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
