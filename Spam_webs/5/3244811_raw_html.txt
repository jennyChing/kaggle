     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Planet Earth
       </b>
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/2006_in_television" title="2006 in television">
       2006
      </a>
      British television series produced by the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Natural_History_Unit" title="BBC Natural History Unit">
       BBC Natural History Unit
      </a>
      . Five years in the making, it was the most expensive
      <a href="/wiki/Nature_documentary" title="Nature documentary">
       nature documentary
      </a>
      series ever commissioned by the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      and also the first to be filmed in
      <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">
       high definition
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      premiered on 5 March 2006 in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
       BBC One
      </a>
      , and by June 2007 had been shown in 130 countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      The series comprises eleven episodes, each of which features a global overview of a different
      <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">
       biome
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">
       habitat
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      . At the end of each fifty-minute episode, a ten-minute featurette takes a behind-the-scenes look at the challenges of filming the series.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/2001_in_television" title="2001 in television">
       2001
      </a>
      the BBC broadcast
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Planet" title="The Blue Planet">
        The Blue Planet
       </a>
      </i>
      , a landmark series on the natural history of the world's oceans. It received critical acclaim, high viewing figures, audience appreciation ratings, and many awards. It also became a hugely profitable global brand, eventually being sold to 150 countries worldwide. Feedback showed that audiences particularly liked the epic scale, the scenes of new and unusual species and the cinematic quality of the series. Programme commissioners were keen for a follow-up, so
      <a href="/wiki/Alastair_Fothergill" title="Alastair Fothergill">
       Alastair Fothergill
      </a>
      decided that the Natural History Unit should repeat the formula with a series looking at the whole planet. The idea for
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      was born, and the series was commissioned by
      <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_Heggessey" title="Lorraine Heggessey">
       Lorraine Heggessey
      </a>
      , then Controller of BBC One, in January 2002.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNL_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DNL-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">
       feature film
      </a>
      version of
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      was commissioned alongside the television series, repeating the successful model established with
      <i>
       The Blue Planet
      </i>
      and its companion film,
      <i>
       Deep Blue
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_(2007_film)" title="Earth (2007 film)">
        Earth
       </a>
      </i>
      was released around the world from 2007 to 2009. There was also another accompanying television series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Planet_Earth:_The_Future" title="Planet Earth: The Future">
        Planet Earth: The Future
       </a>
      </i>
      , which looked at the
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">
       environmental problems
      </a>
      facing some of the
      <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">
       species
      </a>
      and habitats featured in the main series in more detail.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcast_details">
       Broadcast details
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Broadcast details">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="British_television">
       British television
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: British television">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The episodes are each an hour in length, comprising the main programme and a 10-minute featurette called
      <i>
       Planet Earth Diaries
      </i>
      , which details the filming of a particular event. In the UK,
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      was split into two parts, broadcast in spring and autumn 2006. The first five episodes premiered on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
       BBC One
      </a>
      at 9:00pm on Sundays, beginning on 5 March 2006. The programmes were repeated the following Saturday in an early evening slot on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">
       BBC Two
      </a>
      . Along with its 2005 dramatisation of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bleak_House" title="Bleak House">
        Bleak House
       </a>
      </i>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      selected
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      for its trial of
      <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">
       high-definition
      </a>
      broadcasts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The opening episode was its first-ever scheduled programme in the format, shown 27 May 2006 on the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_HD" title="BBC HD">
       BBC HD
      </a>
      channel.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first episode in the autumn series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">
        Great Plains
       </a>
      </i>
      , received its first public showing at the
      <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_International_Television_Festival" title="Edinburgh International Television Festival">
       Edinburgh International Television Festival
      </a>
      on 26 August 2006. It was shown on a giant screen in Conference Square.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The remaining episodes were broadcast from 5 November 2006 in the same primetime
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
       BBC One
      </a>
      slot, following a further repeat run of the spring programmes on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">
       BBC Four
      </a>
      . The autumn episodes were broadcast simultaneously on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_HD" title="BBC HD">
       BBC HD
      </a>
      and were repeated on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">
       BBC Four
      </a>
      the following week.
     </p>
     <p>
      Besides being
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
       BBC One
      </a>
      's featured
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_to_Watch" title="One to Watch">
        One to Watch
       </a>
      </i>
      programme of the day,
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      was heavily trailed on the BBC's television and radio channels both before and during its run. The music that was featured in the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      trailers for the series is the track "
      <a href="/wiki/Hopp%C3%ADpolla" title="Hopppolla">
       Hopppolla
      </a>
      " from the album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Takk..." title="Takk...">
        Takk...
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Icelanders" title="Icelanders">
       Icelandic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Post-rock" title="Post-rock">
       post-rock
      </a>
      band
      <a href="/wiki/Sigur_R%C3%B3s" title="Sigur Rs">
       Sigur Rs
      </a>
      . Following the advertisements, interest was so widespread that the single was re-released.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA" title="USA">
       United States
      </a>
      , the series was promoted using "The Time Has Come" from
      <a href="/wiki/Trailer_music" title="Trailer music">
       trailer music
      </a>
      company Epic Score,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      composed by Gabriel Shadid and Tobias Marberger.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian" title="Australian">
       Australian
      </a>
      trailers initially used
      <i>
       Jupiter: The Bringer of Jollity
      </i>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">
       Gustav Holst
      </a>
      's orchestral suite
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Planets" title="The Planets">
        The Planets
       </a>
      </i>
      , but later reverted to "
      <a href="/wiki/Hopp%C3%ADpolla" title="Hopppolla">
       Hopppolla
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International">
       International
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      pre-sold the series to several overseas broadcasters,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      including the
      <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">
       Discovery Channel
      </a>
      for the United States, the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Australian Broadcasting Corporation">
       Australian Broadcasting Corporation
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">
       Canadian Broadcasting Corporation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/China_Central_Television" title="China Central Television">
       China Central Television
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Westdeutscher_Rundfunk" title="Westdeutscher Rundfunk">
       WDR
      </a>
      for Germany, Discovery Channel for India,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Television" title="Prime Television">
       Prime Television
      </a>
      for New Zealand, and
      <a href="/wiki/C1R" title="C1R">
       C1R
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russian
      </a>
      broadcasts. The series was eventually sold to 130 countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radiotimes_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-radiotimes-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 25 March 2007, the series began its run on American television on the
      <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Communications" title="Discovery Communications">
       Discovery
      </a>
      network, premiering on the
      <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">
       Discovery Channel
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_HD_Theater" title="Discovery HD Theater">
       Discovery HD Theater
      </a>
      . There were a number of revisions to the original British programme. Actress and conservationist
      <a href="/wiki/Sigourney_Weaver" title="Sigourney Weaver">
       Sigourney Weaver
      </a>
      was brought in to replace
      <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">
       David Attenborough
      </a>
      as narrator, as it was thought her familiarity to American audiences would attract more viewers. The Discovery programmes also used a slightly different script to the British original. The series was broadcast on Sundays in one 3-hour block followed by four 2-hour blocks. The
      <i>
       Planet Earth Diaries
      </i>
      segments were not shown immediately after each episode, but collectively in
      <i>
       Planet Earth: The Filmmakers' Story
      </i>
      , a two-hour special which was broadcast after the series had finished its initial network run. Edited versions were later broadcast on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Science_Channel" title="The Science Channel">
       The Science Channel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_Planet" title="Animal Planet">
       Animal Planet
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planet_Green" title="Planet Green">
       Planet Green
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      , the series did not air on the Canadian Discovery Channel, as it is owned by
      <a href="/wiki/CTV_Television_Network" title="CTV Television Network">
       CTV
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian" title="Canadian">
       Canadian
      </a>
      rights were exclusively sold to the
      <a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">
       CBC
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1._.22From_Pole_to_Pole.22">
       1. "From Pole to Pole"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: 1. "From Pole to Pole"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        UK broadcast 5 March 2006, 9.41 million viewers (34% audience share
       </i>
       );
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BARB_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BARB-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        U.S. broadcast 25 March 2007
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The first episode illustrates a journey around the globe and reveals the effect of gradual climatic change and seasonal transitions en route. During
      <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
       Antarctica
      </a>
      's winter,
      <a href="/wiki/Emperor_penguin" title="Emperor penguin">
       emperor penguins
      </a>
      endure four months of darkness, with no food, in temperatures of 70
      <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">
       C
      </a>
      (94
      <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">
       F
      </a>
      ). Meanwhile, as spring arrives in the
      <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
       Arctic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">
       polar bear
      </a>
      cubs take their first steps into a world of rapidly thawing ice. In northern Canada, 3 million
      <a href="/wiki/Caribou" title="Caribou">
       caribou
      </a>
      complete an overland migration of 3,200 kilometres (2,000mi), longer than that of any animal, and are hunted by wolves during its journey. The forests of eastern
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      are home to the
      <a href="/wiki/Amur_leopard" title="Amur leopard">
       Amur leopard
      </a>
      ; with a population of just 40 individuals in the wild, it is now the world's rarest cat. This is primarily because of the destruction of its habitat, and Attenborough states that it "symbolises the fragility of our natural heritage". However, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
       tropics
      </a>
      , the jungle that covers 3% of the planet's surface supports 50% of its species. Other species shown include
      <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">
       New Guinea
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds_of_paradise" title="Birds of paradise">
       birds of paradise
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/African_wild_dog" title="African wild dog">
       African hunting dogs
      </a>
      in their efficient pursuit of
      <a href="/wiki/Impala" title="Impala">
       impala
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/African_elephant" title="African elephant">
       elephants
      </a>
      in Africa migrating towards the waters of the
      <a href="/wiki/Okavango_Delta" title="Okavango Delta">
       Okavango Delta
      </a>
      , a seasonal bloom of life in the otherwise arid
      <a href="/wiki/Kalahari_Desert" title="Kalahari Desert">
       Kalahari Desert
      </a>
      , and 300,000 migrating
      <a href="/wiki/Baikal_teal" title="Baikal teal">
       Baikal teal
      </a>
      , containing the world's entire population of the species in one flock. The
      <i>
       Planet Earth Diaries
      </i>
      segment shows how the wild dog hunt was filmed unobtrusively with the aid of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Heligimbal" title="Heligimbal">
        Heligimbal
       </a>
      </i>
      , a powerful,
      <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">
       gyro
      </a>
      -stabilised camera mounted beneath a
      <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">
       helicopter
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2._.22Mountains.22">
       2. "Mountains"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: 2. "Mountains"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2136" data-file-width="2848" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg/250px-Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg/375px-Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg/500px-Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baltoro_glacier_from_air.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Baltoro Glacier in the Karakoram, Pakistan
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        UK broadcast 12 March 2006, 8.57 million viewers (30% audience share)
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BARB_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-BARB-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>

         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        U.S. broadcast 25 March 2007
       </i>

     <p>
      The second instalment focuses on the
      <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
       mountains
      </a>
      . All the main ranges are explored with extensive aerial photography.
      <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">
       Ethiopia
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Erta_Ale" title="Erta Ale">
       Erta Ale
      </a>
      is the longest continually erupting
      <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
       volcano
      </a>
      for over 100 years. On the nearby highlands,
      <a href="/wiki/Gelada" title="Gelada">
       geladas
      </a>
      (the only primate whose diet is almost entirely
      <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">
       grass
      </a>
      ) inhabit precipitous slopes nearly five kilometres (3mi) up, in troops that are 800-strong: the most numerous of their kind. Alongside them live the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critically_endangered_species" title="Critically endangered species">
       critically endangered
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Walia_ibex" title="Walia ibex">
       walia ibex
      </a>
      , and both species take turns to act as lookout for predatory
      <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_wolf" title="Ethiopian wolf">
       Ethiopian wolves
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
       Andes
      </a>
      have the most volatile weather and
      <a href="/wiki/Guanaco" title="Guanaco">
       guanacos
      </a>
      are shown enduring a flash blizzard, along with an exceptional group sighting of the normally solitary
      <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">
       puma
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">
       Alpine
      </a>
      summits are always
      <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">
       snow
      </a>
      -covered, apart from that of the
      <a href="/wiki/Matterhorn" title="Matterhorn">
       Matterhorn
      </a>
      , which is too sheer to allow it to settle.
      <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">
       Grizzly bear
      </a>
      cubs emerge from their den for the first time in the
      <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">
       Rockies
      </a>
      , while
      <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">
       Himalayan
      </a>
      inhabitants include
      <a href="/wiki/Rut_(mammalian_reproduction)" title="Rut (mammalian reproduction)">
       rutting
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Markhor" title="Markhor">
       markhor
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Golden_eagle" title="Golden eagle">
       golden eagles
      </a>
      that hunt migrating
      <a href="/wiki/Demoiselle_crane" title="Demoiselle crane">
       demoiselle cranes
      </a>
      , and the rare
      <a href="/wiki/Snow_leopard" title="Snow leopard">
       snow leopard
      </a>
      . At the eastern end of the range, the
      <a href="/wiki/Giant_panda" title="Giant panda">
       giant panda
      </a>
      cannot hibernate due to its poor nutriment of
      <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">
       bamboo
      </a>
      and one of them cradles its week-old cub. Also shown is the Earth's biggest mountain
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
       glacier
      </a>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Baltoro_Glacier" title="Baltoro Glacier">
       Baltoro
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
       Pakistan
      </a>
      , which is 70 kilometres (43mi) long and visible from space.
      <i>
       Planet Earth Diaries
      </i>
      explains how difficult it was to get close-up footage of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_leopards" title="Snow leopards">
       snow leopards
      </a>
      ; it was a three-year process and is the world's first-ever video footage of snow leopards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>

      <i>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n,_Spain" title="San Sebastin, Spain">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Audience_response">
       Audience response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Audience response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The credentials of the filmmakers, the size of the production, a high-profile marketing campaign and a primetime BBC One timeslot all resulted in
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      attracting large audiences when it debuted in the UK in March 2006. The first episode, "From Pole to Pole", was watched by more people than any natural history programme since Attenborough and Fothergill's previous series,
      <i>
       The Blue Planet
      </i>
      , in 2001.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first five episodes drew an average audience of 11.4 million viewers, including the early evening repeats, outperforming even
      <i>
       The Blue Planet
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the series returned to British screens after a six-month break, it remained popular but viewing figures did not reach the same levels. The final six episodes attracted an average audience of 6.8 million viewers, appreciably lower than the spring episodes, but still higher than BBC One's average for the timeslot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The BBC's 2007 Annual Report revealed that the series "received the highest audience appreciation score of any British programme on TV this year".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States,
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      drew equally impressive ratings when it premiered on Discovery and Discovery HD Theater on 25 March 2007. The first three episodes (screened back to back) averaged 5.72 million viewers with a peak of 6.07 million viewers, giving the network its third highest audience ever. It was also the most watched Discovery programme since
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Flight_That_Fought_Back" title="The Flight That Fought Back">
        The Flight That Fought Back
       </a>
      </i>
      in 2005.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      went on to break more records during its American broadcast run, reaching more than 100 million viewers in total and becoming "the most watched cable event of all time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholastic_48-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-scholastic-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>

        </span>

      (

       NTSC
      </a>
      ) DVD on 24 April 2007. The
      <a href="/wiki/Warner_Home_Video" title="Warner Home Video">
       BBC Warner
      </a>
      release retained David Attenborough's narration from the original British television broadcasts, but the Discovery Channel edition used the alternative Sigourney Weaver voiceover.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="HD_DVD_and_Blu-ray">
       HD DVD and Blu-ray
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: HD DVD and Blu-ray">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Except for a small amount of extremely hard-to-obtain footage,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Planet Earth
      </i>
      was filmed entirely in
      <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">
       high-definition
      </a>
      , and consequently became one of the first television series to take advantage of the new HD disc formats.
     </p>
     <p>
      The series was released in both
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">
       Blu-ray
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD" title="HD DVD">
       HD DVD
      </a>
      formats as a five-disc
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc#Region_codes" title="Blu-ray Disc">
       Region B
      </a>
      box set on 12 November 2007. On the fifth disc, the bonus features from the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard-definition_television" title="Standard-definition television">
       standard-definition
      </a>
      DVD set were replaced by two episodes from the BBC's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_World_(BBC_TV_series)" title="Natural World (BBC TV series)">
        Natural World
       </a>
      </i>
      series, "Desert Lions" and "Snow Leopard: Beyond the Myth", both also presented in high-definition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States, the series was released as a four-disc set in both high-definition formats,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvdtimes_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dvdtimes-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Blu-ray version on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc#Technical_specifications" title="Blu-ray Disc">
       single-layer BD-25
      </a>
      discs
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the HD DVD set on
      <a href="/wiki/HD_DVD#Disc_structure" title="HD DVD">
       dual-layer HD DVD-30
      </a>
      discs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first U.S. high-definition releases omitted the extra disc of bonus features from the standard-definition boxed set, though these extras were included with new material in a special edition Blu-ray released in 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Books">
       Books
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planet_Earth_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Books">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Four official tie-in volumes were published by
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Books" title="BBC Books">
       BBC Books
      </a>
      in 2006 and 2007:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Planet Earth: As You've Never Seen It Before
       </i>
       , written by Alastair Fothergill with a foreword by David Attenborough, was published in hardback on 5 October 2006 (
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780563522126">
        ISBN 978-0563522126
       </a>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       The paperback title
       <i>
        Planet Earth: The Future
       </i>
       was also published on 5 October 2006 (
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780563539056">
        ISBN 978-0563539056
       </a>
       ). It was edited by Fergus Beeley and Rosamund Kidman Cox with a foreword by Jonathon Porritt.
      </li>
      <li>
       A second paperback volume revealed some of the tales from the field during filming expeditions.
       <i>
        Planet Earth: The Making of an Epic Series
       </i>
       was written by David Nicholson-Lord and published on 9 March 2006 (
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780563493587">
        ISBN 978-0563493587
       </a>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       A collection of still images from the series was published in a hardcover volume as
       <i>
        Planet Earth: The Photographs
       </i>
       on 7 October 2007 (
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9781846073465">
        ISBN 978-1846073465
       </a>
       ).
      </li>
     </ul>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtrack_album">
       Soundtrack album
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

      <a href="/wiki/Planet_Earth_(soundtrack)" title="Planet Earth (soundtrack)">

     <p>
      On 20 November 2006, a two-disc
      <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack" title="Soundtrack">
       soundtrack
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">

      </a>
