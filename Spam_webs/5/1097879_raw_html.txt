     <p>
      A
      <b>
       hot toddy
      </b>
      , also
      <b>
       hot totty
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       hot tottie
      </b>
      as well as
      <b>
       hot whiskey
      </b>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
       Ireland
      </a>
      , is typically a
      <a href="/wiki/Mixed_drink" title="Mixed drink">
       mixed drink
      </a>
      made of
      <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">
       liquor
      </a>
      and water with honey (or, in some recipes, sugar) and spices and served hot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hot toddy recipes vary and are traditionally drank before going to bed, or in wet or cold weather. Some believe the drink relieves the symptoms of the
      <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">
       cold
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">
       flu
      </a>
       in
      <i>
       How to Drink
      </i>
      , Victoria Moore describes the drink as "the
      <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">
       vitamin C
      </a>
      for health, the honey to soothe, the alcohol to numb."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">
       Preparation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_toddy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A hot toddy is a mixture of a spirit (usually
      <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">
       whisky
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">
       rum
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">
       brandy
      </a>
      ), boiling water, and honey (or, in some recipes, sugar). Additional ingredients such as
      <a href="/wiki/Clove" title="Clove">
       cloves
      </a>
      , a lemon slice or
      <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">
       cinnamon
      </a>
      (in stick or ground form) are often also added. The drink can also be made with
      <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">
       tea
      </a>
      instead of water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poister_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-poister-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A common version in the
      <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">
       Midwestern United States
      </a>
      uses golden
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ginger_Ale" title="Ginger Ale">
       Ginger Ale
      </a>
      , lemon, honey and
      <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_whiskey" title="Bourbon whiskey">
       Bourbon whiskey
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">
       Wisconsin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">
       brandy
      </a>
      is often used instead of bourbon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A common version in
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      typically consists of heated ginger-ale, honey, and either whiskey or brandy. It is often recommended to heat the ginger-ale before adding the whiskey or brandy, otherwise the heating process will reduce the alcoholic effects of the liquor.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_toddy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      It has been suggested that the name comes from the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Palm_wine" title="Palm wine">
        toddy
       </a>
      </i>
      drink in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      produced by fermenting the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sap" title="Plant sap">
       sap
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">
       palm trees
      </a>
      . The term could have been introduced into Scotland by a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">
       East India Company
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
