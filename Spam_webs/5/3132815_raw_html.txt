         <h1 class="wsj-article-headline" itemprop="headline">
          New York, the World's Greatest Wine City
         </h1>
         <h2 class="sub-head" itemprop="description">
          Swaggering sommeliers, intrepid importers, sophisticated consumerswhy even French Champagne producers agree New York is the greatest town for grape
         </h2>
        </div>
       </div>
       <!-- data-module-name="article.app/lib/module/articleHeadline" -->
      </header>
      <div class="column at8-col8 at12-col7 at16-col9 at16-offset1">

          <p>
           <strong>
            NEW YORK HAS INSPIRED
           </strong>
           hundreds, perhaps even thousands of songs, and the double-barreled anthem that Frank Sinatra famously belted (and that's belted, not sang) is probably the best known of all. "New York, New York" celebrates the city as the ultimate destination for anyone who is driven, ambitious and perhaps just a little bit over the top. It also captures the essence of what makes New York the greatest wine city in the world.
          </p>
          <!--

          <div class=" media-object wrap " data-layout="

             </span>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <!--

          <p>
           New York has the size and the swagger (and the money, of course) to foster a fierce style of competition. Strivers come to the city to be the best in their field, and wine salesmen, who include importers, sommeliers and merchants, are part of this tribe. "I can sell anything to anyone," said Josh Nadel, exhibiting typical Big Apple assurance. Mr. Nadel is the wine director of the Andrew Carmellini restaurant group, which includes Lafayette, Locanda Verde, the Dutch and many others. Australian-born Gordon Little voiced a similar confidence, saying he planned to fill what he saw as the city's one unmet need: Australian wine. Mr. Little lived all over the world before settling in New York. "You can find anything that you want in New York, except a great selection of Australian wine," said Mr. Little, the consummate New York salesman.
          </p>
          <p>
           Mr. Nadel also points to the adventurous clientele in New York to explain the city's stature as the wine capital of the world. "Even the most trepidatious wine drinker in New York will have an open mind," he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The well-traveled Valenti Llagostera, proprietor of Mas Doix, a top winery in the Priorat region of Spain, had been in New Orleans, Dallas, San Francisco and Seattle the week I reached him via email. Mr. Llagostera puts the liberality of New York's wine drinkers thus: "In New York, wines are decontextualized," he said. In other words, everything has an equal shotwhether it is a bottle from Bordeaux, Rioja or the Priorat. There is no regional bias to overcome. As Mr. Little stated, "It's the most egalitarian city in the world."
          </p>
          <p>
           This sort of openness enables Mr. Nadel and his Big Apple peers to take risks with their lists that sommeliers in smaller, more conservative cities might be unwilling or unable to do. Consequently, the wine lists of New York feature some of the most exciting and obscure wines in the world, as well as crowd-pleasers such as Pinot Grigio and Sancerre.
          </p>
          <p>
           These rare wines are often acquired by the city's many small importers. New York importers, another crucial component of the city's greatness, search for wines from all over the world produced in regions that few people have heard of or know exist, such as Italy's Molise, Germany's Franken and the French Minervois. They are motivated more often by passion than moneyhow else to explain an importer who specializes in the wines of Moravia or the Canary Islands of Spain?
          </p>
          <p>
           The great wine merchants of New York also possess a collective depth of knowledge and passionand a power of access that is practically unparalleled. Very few wines that I've sought couldn't be found in two days by a New York retailer.
          </p>
          <p>
           Not only are the wine sellers in the city knowledgeable, the drinkers are as well. "I have customers who know more than I do, so I have to stay up on my game," said Tim Finch, manager of K&amp;D Wines, who came to New York from New Hampshire on a bus 13 years ago, seeking his own place in the city's wine world.
          </p>

          <p>
           And this very high bar sets up a challenge for wine producers (the "If I can make it there, I'll make it anywhere" part of Sinatra's song). "It's all about credibility," explained
           <!--
-->
           Doug Shafer,
           <!--
-->
           of Napa's famed Shafer Vineyards. In other words, selling your wine in New York puts your winery on the map. Mr. Shafer recalled his early attempts to break into the New York market almost three decades ago. When the winery couldn't get traction with its Cabernet, they "busted in" with its Merlot, recalled Mr. Shafer, employing a very New York sort of verb to describe their moment of validation.
          </p>
          <p>
           The city is the grail even for great winemakers from Europe, such as Jean-Baptiste Lcaillon, director of Louis Roederer Champagne. Although Roederer Champagnes are sold all over the world and he himself owns an apartment in Paris, Mr. Lcaillon did not hesitate in naming New York the world's greatest wine city. "Nowhere else do you find such a diversity of everything."
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, many other cities are stocked with great wine shops, smart sommeliers and well-heeled oenophiles. I would consider four strong runners-up to New York.
          </p>
          <p>
           First is London, which has a much longer history of wine drinking and buying than New York. The merchants of London not only sold the greatest wines in the world but helped popularize them as well. I'm sure no one would be drinking Sherry or Port today if it weren't for those early London wine merchants.
          </p>
          <!-- T1:M -->
          <p>
           But London's consumers aren't nearly as adventurous as those of New York, and they do seem to have an inordinate attachment to Bordeaux, which they still insist on calling Claretnever mind that no one else does. Londoners also spend a great deal of time shopping for wine in supermarkets, something I don't think New Yorkers would do. (The state hasn't legalized the sale of wine in grocery stores, but New Yorkers probably wouldn't want to buy there anyway, as there are so many great small merchants to choose from.)
          </p>
          <p>
           Paris is unquestionably the
           <a class="icon none" href="http://online.wsj.com/news/articles/SB10001424052970204468004577168880090462596" target="_self">
            greatest city for French wine
           </a>
           . If you want a terrific bottle of Champagne or Chinon or a grand cru Burgundy, you can find it in Paris at a much better price than in New York. But it is virtually impossible to find the equivalent wine from Spain, Italy or Australia on a wine list or in a shop. And every wine-bar manager in Paris seems to be unnaturally
           <a class="icon none" href="http://online.wsj.com/news/articles/SB10001424127887324436104578579650208883968" target="_self">
            obsessed with natural wine
           </a>
           made without the addition of
           <a class="icon none" href="http://online.wsj.com/articles/are-sulfites-in-wine-bad-for-you-1408056869" target="_self">
            sulfur dioxide or additives
           </a>
           of any sort, the wines can be delicious or downright unstable. (Sulfur dioxide acts as a preservative and keeps the wine from oxidizing.) If you don't like potentially cloudy, oxidative wine, a Paris wine bar isn't for you.
          </p>
          <p>
           San Francisco is just south of some of the greatest wine regions in America, if not the world (Napa, Sonoma, Mendocino), so naturally they get a lot of great California juice. And yet just about any California producer of note ends up in New York. The West Coast city has a thriving food culture and is home to many excellent restaurants, but their numbers are much smaller than New York's, as is the size of the town. There are no major wine auction houses and very few importers and distributors of note in San Francisco; its most famous importers, Kermit Lynch and Martine Saunier, are both located across the bay.
          </p>
          <p>
           Hong Kong may be New York's closest contender for the title of world's greatest wine city. It has the size, the money and a huge chunk of the wine-auction scene, but it's still up-and-coming. Besides, it is home to a great deal of fraud. Because many wine buyers in Hong Kong are mainland Chinese, who may be new to fine wine and not as sensitive to counterfeiting (it's commonplace in China), Hong Kong has become the wine fraudsters' city of choice.
          </p>
          <p>
           And not all the fake wine there is pretending to be great wine; a Hong Kong firm was found selling thousands of bottles of counterfeit 1995 Mouton Cadet. No New York fraudster worth his or her salt would fake a mass-market wine like Mouton Cadet. The city's most famous convicted counterfeiter, Rudy Kurniawan, specialized in pirating very rare and very pricey Burgundies and Bordeaux.
          </p>
          <p>
           For now at least, New York is still "A-number one, top of the list, king of the hill" for winemakers, sommeliers, merchants, importers and distributors, and most important, for the wine drinkers who have made it the greatest place in the world to raise a glass of Sancerre or Moravian muskatelleror possibly both.
          </p>

           </strong>
          </p>
