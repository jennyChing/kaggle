    <h1>
     perfect number
    </h1>
   </div>
  </div>
  <div class="display-centre" style="text-align: justify">
   <p>
    A perfect number \(P\) is equal to the sum of its divisors (where
      the divisors include \(1\), but not \(P\) itself).
   </p>
   <ul>
    <li>
     <b>
      Euclid
     </b>
     : If \(2^n-1\) is
     <a href="prime.htm">
      prime
     </a>
     then \(2^{n-1}(2^n-1)\)  is perfect
    </li>
   </ul>
   <ul>
    <li>
     <b>
      Euler
     </b>
     :
     <i>
      all
     </i>
     even perfect numbers are of the form \(2^{p-1}(2^p-1)\),
        where \(2^p-1\) is a
     <a href="../m/mersenne.htm">
      Mersenne
          prime
     </a>
     (and so \(p\) is
     <a href="prime.htm">
      prime
     </a>
     ).

    <li>
     Every even perfect number ends in a '6' or an '8'.

    <li>

      </li>

    </li>

   <ul>
    <li>
     No
     <i>
      odd
     </i>
     perfect numbers are known, but if one does exist, a
        lot is known about it:
     <ul>
      <li>
       it is a perfect square multiplied by an odd power of a single
            prime
      </li>
      <li>
       it has at least 8 distinct prime factors
      </li>
      <li>
       it has at least 75 prime factors (not necessarily distinct)
      </li>
      <li>
       its largest prime factor greater that 10
       <sup>
        7
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       its second largest prime factor is greater that 10
       <sup>
        4
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       its third largest prime factor is greater that 10
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       it is divisible by a
       <a href="prime.htm#component">
        prime
              component
       </a>
       greater that 10
       <sup>
        20
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     Exhaustive computer search has shown that there are no odd perfect
        numbers less than 10
     <sup>
      300
     </sup>
     .
