     <p>
      <b>
       Open-source intelligence
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       OSINT
      </b>
      ) is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligence_(information_gathering)" title="Intelligence (information gathering)">
       intelligence
      </a>
      collected from publicly available sources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">
       intelligence community
      </a>
      (IC), the term "open" refers to overt, publicly available sources (as opposed to covert or clandestine sources); it is not related to
      <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">
       open-source software
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Collective_intelligence" title="Collective intelligence">
       public intelligence
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Open_sources_for_intelligence">
       Open sources for intelligence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Open sources for intelligence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      OSINT includes a wide variety of information and sources:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">
        Media
       </a>
       : newspapers, magazines, radio, television, and computer-based information.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">
        Web-based communities
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/User-generated_content" title="User-generated content">
        user-generated content
       </a>
       : social-networking sites,
       <a href="/wiki/Video_hosting_service" title="Video hosting service">
        video sharing sites
       </a>
       , wikis, blogs, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folksonomies" title="Folksonomies">
        folksonomies
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_data" title="Public data">
        Public data
       </a>
       : government reports, official data such as budgets, demographics, hearings, legislative debates, press conferences, speeches, marine and aeronautical safety warnings, environmental impact statements and contract awards.
      </li>
      <li>
       Observation and reporting: amateur airplane spotters, radio monitors and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_satellite_observer" title="Amateur satellite observer">
        satellite observers
       </a>
       among many others have provided significant information not otherwise available. The availability of worldwide satellite photography, often of high resolution, on the Web (e.g., Google Earth) has expanded open-source capabilities into areas formerly available only to major intelligence services.
      </li>
      <li>
       Professional and academic (including
       <a href="/wiki/Grey_literature" title="Grey literature">
        grey literature
       </a>
       ): conferences, symposia, professional associations, academic papers, and subject matter experts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Most information has geospatial dimensions, but many often overlook the geospatial side of OSINT: not all open-source data is unstructured text. Examples of geospatial open source include hard and softcopy maps, atlases, gazetteers, port plans, gravity data, aeronautical data, navigation data, geodetic data, human terrain data (cultural and economic), environmental data, commercial imagery,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LIDAR" title="LIDAR">
        LIDAR
       </a>
       , hyper and multi-spectral data, airborne imagery, geo-names, geo-features, urban terrain, vertical obstruction data, boundary marker data, geospatial
       <a href="/wiki/Mashup_(web_application_hybrid)" title="Mashup (web application hybrid)">
        mashups
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spatial_database" title="Spatial database">
        spatial databases
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_services" title="Web services">
        web services
       </a>
       . Most of the geospatial data mentioned above is integrated, analyzed, and syndicated using geospatial software like a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GIS" title="GIS">
        Geographic Information System
       </a>
       (GIS) not a browser
       <i>
        per se
       </i>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      OSINT is distinguished from research in that it applies the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Process_of_intelligence" title="Process of intelligence">
       process of intelligence
      </a>
      to create tailored knowledge supportive of a specific decision by a specific individual or group.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definers_for_OSINT">
       Definers for OSINT
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definers for OSINT">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      OSINT is defined by both the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Director_of_National_Intelligence" title="Director of National Intelligence">
       Director of National Intelligence
      </a>
      and the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">
       Department of Defense
      </a>
      (DoD), as "produced from publicly available information that is collected, exploited, and disseminated in a timely manner to an appropriate audience for the purpose of addressing a specific intelligence requirement."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      OSINT is, as of 2005
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , defined by the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">
       Office of Management and Budget
      </a>
      under the category of "Forces And Direct Support" and specifically for the DoD under Commercial Code M320 as
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Open-source intelligence (OSINT) collection/processing
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      A wide variety of vendors sell information products specifically within this category.
     </p>
     <p>
      Open-source intelligence under one name or another has been around for hundreds of years. The significance today of OSINT in the USA is the conflict between military, government, and the private sector as to how the bulk of intelligence should be obtained. With the Internet, instant communications, and advanced media search the bulk of actionable and predictive intelligence can be obtained from public, unclassified sources. Government agencies have been slow to embrace OSINT, or believe they already have suitable information feeds from the media, academia and public records.
     </p>
     <p>
      OSINT is especially helpful in addressing
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Global_coverage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Global coverage (page does not exist)">
       global coverage
      </a>
      , a term encompassing all of the countries and topics that are not considered by the secret or national security worlds to be "vital."
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Competitive_intelligence">
       Competitive intelligence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Competitive intelligence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the private sector,
      <a href="/wiki/Competitive_intelligence" title="Competitive intelligence">
       competitive intelligence
      </a>
      has become a tool for marketing strategies that focus on strategically prepared information under the direction of private companies or individuals who sell organized information to specific security, law enforcement and military industries, amongst other strategic applications, often on a contractual basis. Governments and civilians both use open source intelligence, both legitimately and illegitimately, the latter being the case with criminals who use information to gain an edge in planning and conducting criminal activities.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are still opportunities for small and medium businesses to compete in niche markets, but this too is being consolidated by major information providers (e.g.?). OSINT is not a novel concept in media where everyday operations of traditional newsroom methods of operations engage in useful strategies towards obtaining information for unique and original content through investigations of story leads, absent of reliance on formal methods of obtaining inside information through legal documents or basic interview techniques.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Investigative_journalist" title="Investigative journalist">
       Investigative journalists
      </a>
      use searches, databases, primary interviews, original sources, and leaks (informants/witnesses) who come forward either anonymously or openly, as direct contributors of inside information for journalists. Investigative journalists use specific strategies to obtain information. Sometimes informants come forward on their own to contribute original information that might not otherwise be made available, which often directly contributes to the publication of original feature stories. Such has been the case with regard to many whistle blowers in politics, government, law enforcement and also in commercial, financial and private sectors.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Risks_for_practitioners">
       Risks for practitioners
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Risks for practitioners">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Accredited journalists have some protection in asking questions, and researching for recognized media outlets. Even so they can be imprisoned, even executed, for seeking out OSINT. Private individuals illegally collecting data for a foreign military or intelligence agency is considered
      <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">
       espionage
      </a>
      in most countries. Of course, espionage that is not
      <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">
       treason
      </a>
      (i.e. betraying one's country of citizenship) has been a tool of statecraft since ancient times, is widely engaged in by nearly all countries, and is considered an honorable trade.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most countries recognize this, and if their counterintelligence agencies capture a foreign spy, that spy is usually unceremoniously deported or traded back to their homeland (for other spies) after a hostile debriefing; actual execution or refusal to trade back foreign spies with non-official cover would result in consequences in bilateral relations of the gravest possible magnitude, being an extraordinarily hostile act, even if those consequences were unofficially and extrajudicially imposed.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Value">
       Value
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Value">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commission_on_the_Intelligence_Capabilities_of_the_United_States_Regarding_Weapons_of_Mass_Destruction" title="Commission on the Intelligence Capabilities of the United States Regarding Weapons of Mass Destruction">
       Commission on the Intelligence Capabilities of the United States Regarding Weapons of Mass Destruction
      </a>
      report submitted in March 2005, OSINT must be included in the all-source intelligence process for the following reasons (as stated in the report):
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       The ever-shifting nature of our intelligence needs compels the IC to quickly and easily understand a wide range of foreign countries and cultures.   today's threats are rapidly changing and geographically diffuse; it is a fact of life that an intelligence analyst may be forced to shift rapidly from one topic to the next. Increasingly, IC professionals need to quickly assimilate social, economic, and cultural information about a countryinformation often detailed in open sources.
      </li>
      <li>
       Open-source information provides a base for understanding classified materials. Despite large quantities of classified material produced by the IC, the amount of classified information produced on any one topic can be quite limited, and may be taken out of context if viewed only from a classified-source perspective. Perhaps the most important example today relates to terrorism, where open-source information can fill gaps and create links that allow analysts to better understand fragmented intelligence, rumored terrorist plans, possible means of attack, and potential targets.
      </li>
      <li>
       Open-source materials can protect sources and methods. Sometimes an intelligence judgment that is actually informed with sensitive, classified information can be defended on the basis of open-source reporting. This can prove useful when policy-makers need to explain policy decisions or communicate with foreign officials without compromising classified sources.
      </li>
      <li>
       Only open source can
       <i>
        store history
       </i>
       . A robust open-source program can, in effect, gather data to monitor the world's cultures and how they change with time. This is difficult, if not impossible, using the
       <i>
        snapshots
       </i>
       provided by classified collection methods.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Process">
       Process
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Process">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Data" title="Big Data">
       Big Data
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Information collection in OSINT is generally a different problem from collection in other intelligence disciplines where obtaining the raw information to be analyzed may be the major difficulty, particularly if it is to be obtained from non-cooperative targets. In OSINT, the chief difficulty is in identifying relevant, reliable sources from the vast amount of publicly available information. However, this is not as great a challenge for those who know how to access local knowledge and how to leverage human experts who can create new tailored knowledge on the fly.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Broadcast_Information_Service" title="Foreign Broadcast Information Service">
       Foreign Broadcast Information Service
      </a>
      (FBIS) was created in 1941 to access and exploit OSINT in relation to World War II. A classic example of their value and success is reflected in the price of oranges in Paris as an indicator of whether railroad bridges had been bombed successfully.
     </p>
     <p>
      The recent history of OSINT began in 1988 when General
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_M._Gray,_Jr." title="Alfred M. Gray, Jr.">
       Alfred M. Gray, Jr.
      </a>
      , Commandant of the Marine Corps, called for a redirection of US intelligence away from the collapsing Soviet Union and toward non-state actors and Third World zones of instability. Additionally, he pointed out that most of the intelligence which needs to be known could be obtained via OSINT, and recommended a substantive increase in resources for this aspect of the intelligence collection spectrum of sources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the fall of 1992, Senator
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Boren" title="David Boren">
       David Boren
      </a>
      , then Chairman of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_Select_Committee_on_Intelligence" title="Senate Select Committee on Intelligence">
       Senate Select Committee on Intelligence
      </a>
      , sponsored the National Security Act of 1992, attempting to achieve modest reform in the U.S. Intelligence Community. His counterpart on the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Permanent_Select_Committee_on_Intelligence" title="United States House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence">
       House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence
      </a>
      was Congressman
      <a href="/wiki/Dave_McCurdy" title="Dave McCurdy">
       Dave McCurdy
      </a>
      . The House version of the legislation included a separate open-source office, at the suggestion of Larry Prior, a Marine Reservist familiar with the
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Intelligence_Command" title="Marine Corps Intelligence Command">
       MCIC
      </a>
      experience and then serving on the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence staff.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspin-Brown_Commission" title="Aspin-Brown Commission">
       Aspin-Brown Commission
      </a>
      stated in 1996 that US access to open sources was "severely deficient" and that this should be a "top priority" for both funding and DCI attention.
     </p>
     <p>
      In issuing its July 2004 report, the
      <a href="/wiki/9/11_Commission" title="9/11 Commission">
       9/11 Commission
      </a>
      recommended the creation of an open-source intelligence agency, but without further detail or comment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Subsequently, the
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq_Intelligence_Commission" title="Iraq Intelligence Commission">
       WMD Commission
      </a>
      (also known as the RobbSilberman Commission) report in March 2005 recommended the creation of an open-source directorate at the CIA.
     </p>
     <p>
      Following these recommendations, in November 2005 the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Director_of_National_Intelligence" title="United States Director of National Intelligence">
       Director of National Intelligence
      </a>
      announced the creation of the DNI
      <a href="/wiki/Open_Source_Center" title="Open Source Center">
       Open Source Center
      </a>
      . The Center was established to collect information available from "the Internet, databases, press, radio, television, video, geospatial data, photos and commercial imagery."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition to collecting openly available information, it would train analysts to make better use of this information. The Center absorbed the
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">
       CIA
      </a>
      's previously existing
      <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Broadcast_Information_Service" title="Foreign Broadcast Information Service">
       Foreign Broadcast Information Service
      </a>
      (FBIS), originally established in 1941, with FBIS head
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_Naquin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Douglas Naquin (page does not exist)">
       Douglas Naquin
      </a>
      named as director of the Center.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2005, the Director of National Intelligence appointed
      <a href="/wiki/Eliot_A._Jardines" title="Eliot A. Jardines">
       Eliot A. Jardines
      </a>
      as the
      <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Deputy_Director_of_National_Intelligence_for_Open_Source" title="Assistant Deputy Director of National Intelligence for Open Source">
       Assistant Deputy Director of National Intelligence for Open Source
      </a>
      to serve as the Intelligence Community's senior intelligence officer for open source and to provide strategy, guidance and oversight for the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Open_Source_Enterprise" title="National Open Source Enterprise">
       National Open Source Enterprise
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mr. Jardines has established the National Open Source Enterprise
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and authored Intelligence Community Directive 301. In 2008, Mr. Jardines returned to the private sector and was succeeded by
      <a href="/wiki/Dan_Butler_(civil_servant)" title="Dan Butler (civil servant)">
       Dan Butler
      </a>
      who is ADDNI/OS
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and previously Mr. Jardines' Senior Advisor for Policy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="OSINT_communities">
       OSINT communities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: OSINT communities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Government">
       Government
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Government">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are a large number of open-source activities taking place throughout the US Government. Frequently, these open-source activities are described as "media monitoring", "media analysis", "internet research" and "public surveys" but are open source nonetheless.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      sponsors the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Research_Division" title="Federal Research Division">
       Federal Research Division
      </a>
      (FRD) which conducts a great deal of tailored open-source research on a fee-for-service basis for the executive branch.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence">
       Intelligence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Intelligence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The US Intelligence Community's open-source activities (known as the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Open_Source_Enterprise" title="National Open Source Enterprise">
       National Open Source Enterprise
      </a>
      ) are dictated by Intelligence Community Directive 301 promulgated by the Director of National Intelligence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Directive establishes the authorities and responsibilities of the Assistant Deputy Director of National Intelligence for Open Source (ADDNI/OS), the DNI's Open Source Center and the National Open Source Committee.
     </p>
     <p>
      Prior to the establishment of the National Open Source Enterprise, the
      <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Broadcast_Information_Service" title="Foreign Broadcast Information Service">
       Foreign Broadcast Information Service
      </a>
      (FBIS), established in 1941, was the government's primary open-source unit, transcribing and translating foreign broadcasts. It absorbed the Defense Department's
      <a href="/wiki/Joint_Publications_Research_Service" title="Joint Publications Research Service">
       Joint Publications Research Service
      </a>
      (JPRS), which did a similar function with foreign printed materials, including newspapers, magazines, and technical journals.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Armed_Forces">
       Armed Forces
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Armed Forces">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The former
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Under-Secretary_of_Defense_for_Intelligence" title="Under-Secretary of Defense for Intelligence">
       Under-Secretary of Defense for Intelligence
      </a>
      , Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Cambone" title="Stephen Cambone">
       Stephen Cambone
      </a>
      encouraged in part by the
      <a href="/wiki/Defense_Science_Board" title="Defense Science Board">
       Defense Science Board
      </a>
      reports on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_Communication" title="Strategic Communication">
       Strategic Communication
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_to_and_From_Hostilities" title="Transition to and From Hostilities">
       Transition to and From Hostilities
      </a>
      , created the Defense Open Source Program (DOSP). The current Under-Secretary of Defense for Intelligence is assigned executive agency for this program to the
      <a href="/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Agency" title="Defense Intelligence Agency">
       Defense Intelligence Agency
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      U.S. military offices that engage in OSINT activities include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Agency" title="Defense Intelligence Agency">
        Defense Intelligence Agency
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/National_Geospatial-Intelligence_Agency" title="National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency">
        National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Military_Studies_Office" title="Foreign Military Studies Office">
        US Army Foreign Military Studies Office
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       US Army Asian Studies Detachment
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.fas.org/irp/agency/dod/eucom/jac/" rel="nofollow">
        EUCOM JAC Molesworth
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Foreign Media Monitoring in Support of Information Operations,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Strategic_Command" title="U.S. Strategic Command">
        U.S. Strategic Command
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      US armed forces are using fake online personas to influence social media opinions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fptlb.in.2Fiips.2F.3Fp.3D122_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fptlb.in.2Fiips.2F.3Fp.3D122-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      US Air Force had solicited private sector vendors for something called persona management software. Such a technology would allow single individuals to command virtual armies of fake, digital people across numerous social media portals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fperry4law.co.in.2Fcyber_security.2F.3Fp.3D40_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fperry4law.co.in.2Fcyber_security.2F.3Fp.3D40-18">
        <span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Homeland_Security">
       Homeland Security
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Homeland Security">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="Department of Homeland Security">
       Department of Homeland Security
      </a>
      has an active open-source intelligence unit. In congressional testimony before the House Homeland Security Committee's Intelligence, Information Sharing and Terrorism Risk Assessment Subcommittee the Undersecretary of Homeland Security Charles Allen indicated on February 14, 2007, that he had established the "Domestic Open Source Enterprise" to support the Department's OSINT needs and that of state, local and tribal partners.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement">
       Law enforcement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Law enforcement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The law enforcement OSINT community applies open-source intelligence (OSINT) to the prediction, prevention, investigation, and prosecution of criminals including terrorists.
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples of successful law enforcement OSINT include
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">
       Scotland Yard
      </a>
      OSINT;
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">
       Royal Canadian Mounted Police
      </a>
      (RCMP) OSINT.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INTERPOL" title="INTERPOL">
       INTERPOL
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EUROPOL" title="EUROPOL">
       EUROPOL
      </a>
      experimented with OSINT units for a time, but they appear to have atrophied with the departure of their individual champions.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Police_Department" title="New York Police Department">
       New York Police Department
      </a>
      (NYPD) is known to have an OSINT unit, as does the
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Sheriff%27s_Department" title="Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department">
       Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department
      </a>
      , housed within the Emergency Operations Bureau and affiliated with the LA Joint Regional Intelligence Center.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Business">
       Business
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open-source_intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Business">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Business OSINT encompasses
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_Intelligence" title="Commercial Intelligence">
       Commercial Intelligence
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competitor_Intelligence" title="Competitor Intelligence">
       Competitor Intelligence
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_Intelligence" title="Business Intelligence">
       Business Intelligence
      </a>
      , and is often a chief area of practice of
      <a href="/wiki/Private_intelligence_agency" title="Private intelligence agency">
       private intelligence agencies
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Businesses may use
      <a href="/wiki/Information_broker" title="Information broker">
       information brokers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Private_investigator" title="Private investigator">
       private investigators
      </a>
      to collect and analyze relevant information for business purposes which may include the
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">
       media
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Deep_Web_(search_indexing)" title="Deep Web (search indexing)">
       deep web
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">
       web 2.0
      </a>
      and commercial content.
     </p>
