      <h1 class="width100" role="heading">
       <span class="name internal pointer" data-internal-url="http://www.ranker.com/list/7-common-things-_that-were-pioneered-by-star-trek_/jf-sargent" itemprop="name">
        7 Ways Star Trek Changed the World
       </span>
      </h1>
      <div class="relative collapse desc init grey exclude" data-csst-arrow="true" data-csst-arrowclass="ui-icon ui-icon-carat-1-s" data-csst-bg="#dddee1" data-csst-color="#000" data-csst-height="17" data-csst-id="userDesc" itemprop="description">
       <p>
        <span class="edS-i">
         Star Trek
        </span>
        (the original series) was quite a progressive take on the future for a show filmed in a generation that wasn't as open minded as their future kin from a star date far, far away. In fact, Star Trek had an incredible cultural impact and many things people find common today actually originated on the classic sci-fi series.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        Why is
        <span class="edS-i">
         Star Trek
        </span>
        so popular? That's not a rhetorical question, we actually have the answer: accessibility. Despite its reputation as nerd fodder, the original show was actually a tightly paced, archetypal, and
        <span class="edS-i">
         cool
        </span>
        exploration of a possible future of the human race.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        Creator Gene Roddenberry was a visionary, and many of his predictions and ideas actually came true. What common place ideas and inventions that have changed the world came from
        <span class="edS-i">
         Star Trek
        </span>
        ? Read through the list below to find out.
       </p>

         <span id="pst_1000249191_c_13_19">
          Its no secret that miscegenation (interracial marriage) was kind of a big deal in the United States until very recently - as in, it was totally illegal for people of different races to marry until
         </span>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loving_v._Virginia">
         </a>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loving_v._Virginia">

         </a>

         <span class="edS-i">
          Star Trek
         </span>
         episode "Plato's Stepchildren" featured the
         <span id="pst_1000249191_c_10_4">
          first ever interracial kiss on scripted TV in the US
         </span>
         . It was so controversial, even on the set, that they had to shoot it twice - once where William Shatner (Kirk) and Nichelle Nichols (Uhura) kissed, and once where they didn't. But
         <span id="pst_1000249191_c_8_17">
          Shatner intentionally ruined the shot where they didnt kiss,
         </span>
         forcing the studio to use the other one.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         So what, right? It's not like the
         <span class="edS-i">
          Star Trek
         </span>
         cast was standing with the national guard outside Little Rock High School.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         But then again, consider that
         <span class="edS-i">
          this was the last great step forward for interracial relationships in media.
         </span>
         After that episode aired and the planet wasnt immediately consumed by whatever apocalypse the detractors assumed would consume it, the cabal of ancient immortals that run network TV figured "okay, so we can have white dudes boning black chicks -
         <span class="edS-i">
          but that's it.
         </span>
         " Forty five years later, and it's okay to have, say, Bob Thorton sleep with Halley Berry, but
         <a href="http://www.femalefirst.co.uk/celebrity/Will+Smith-2966.html">
          studio executives still think the country is terrified of black men and white women getting "intimate."
         </a>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Keep in mind, Uhura wasnt a one-off character - she was a major part of the cast and a ranking officer in Star Fleet. So, not only did
         <span class="edS-i">
          Star Trek
         </span>
         take a big step forward, but its still probably one the biggest steps any show or film has taken since.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         There's also the little fact that Nichelle Nichols considered quitting the show at one point, because being the only black actor on a huge show in the 1960s totally sucked. She decided to stay when Martin Luther King, Jr., a man you don't exactly say "no" to,
         <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DNTSV4Eh0R4">
          personally asked her to stay on the show
         </a>
         , because she was such a good role model for young black girls - including Whoopi Goldberg, who cited Nichelle as
         <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/cult/st/interviews/nichols/page4.shtml">
          her inspiration to get into acting
         </a>
         .
