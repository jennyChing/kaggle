         <div class="replytext bodytext" data-replyid="42438108" data-uname="J B L">
          <p>
           I've never ridden a bike with ABS, therefore I just relied on my skills &amp; senses to deal with the factors that affect traction (eg, wet roads, wet patches on the road, patches of sand/gravel, painted white lines, off-camber bends in the road, etc), as well as getting a feel for the point of brake lockup.
          </p>
          <p>
           Once you develop your skills, and learn how to 'read the road' (ie, what you see ahead tells you what to expect), then it becomes 2nd nature.
          </p>
          <p>
           I wouldn't like people to think that because their bike has ABS that they can 'switch off' their understanding of traction, braking distance, etc.  If you were to think that ABS will let you tail-gate other vehicles with less braking distance, then it wouldn't be a wise thing to do.  Think upon the ABS as something that you will never have to rely on, and don't brake to the max every time you want to come to a stop.
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, if you're in the process of purchasing a bike, then it makes sense to consider the features you want, so don't let me talk you out of it :)
          </p>
          <p>
           Ride safe &amp; ride another day! (do that everyday onwards).
          </p>

          <p>
           <span class="wcrep1">
            I'm over 50 and can afford another bike, any bike, but I'm not going to, far too dangerous. Seen the dopes on the road lately?
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           While its true there are lots of dopes on the road, it's important to remember that all risk is relative and life is full of other risks too.
          </p>
          <p>
           The risks with riding are higher than the risks of driving, but both are fairly safe activities in the grand scheme of things. Of course, its up to each person to decide if they want to take the risk, but the decision should be based on actual data, rather than gut impressions and anecdotes. IMHO.
          </p>
          <p>
           In Qld in 2011 there were 2.84 rider and pillion deaths per 10,000 registered motorcycles (fatality rate of 0.0284% per motorcycle). The equivalent death rate for light vehicles/cars in the same period was 0.72 deaths per 10,000 vehicles (0.0072% per light vehicle).
          </p>
          <p>
           The fatality rate per motorcycle is clearly 3.9 times higher than for light vehicles, which is a great sound bite for panic merchants, but in reality both figures are a tiny fraction of 1%.
          </p>
          <p>
           Source:
           <br/>
           <a class="external" href="http://www.tmr.qld.gov.au/~/media/Safety/Transport%2520and%2520road%2520statistics/Road%2520safety/Fatal_road_traffic_crashes_in_qld_2011.pdf" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            http://www.tmr.qld.g
            <wbr>
             ov.au/~/media/Safety/Transpo
             <wbr>
              rt%2520and%2520road%2520stat
              <wbr>
               istics/Road%2520safety/Fatal
               <wbr>
                _road_traffic_crashes_in_qld_2011.pdf
               </wbr>
              </wbr>
             </wbr>
            </wbr>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           The other metric commonly cited is the fatality rate per vehicle kms travelled. In WA between 2003 and 2012 there were 118.50 motorcyclist deaths per billion kms travelled (1 death per 8.4 million kms). In the same period there were 3.8 car deaths per billion car kms travelled (1 death per 263 million kms).
          </p>
          <p>
           263 million/8.4 million = 31.3, hence the oft cited claim motorcycling is '30 times more dangerous' than driving.
          </p>
          <p>
           This isnt the full story though, because Overall Risk = (Hazard x Exposure)  Mitigation Controls. The actual risk for most sensible riders is much lower because they travel fewer kms than the average car driver (lowering exposure) and they can apply simple mitigation controls such as:
          </p>
          <p>
           -	Dont speed (speeding contributed to 27.7% of bike fatalities in Qld in 2011)
           <br/>
           -	Dont drink and ride (drunk riding contributed to 19.1% of fatalities)
           <br/>
           -	Get properly licensed (unlicensed riding contributed to 19.1% of fatalities)
           <br/>
           -	Obey the road rules (illegal manoeuvres contributed to 19.1% of fatalities)
           <br/>
           -	Dont take drugs and ride (other drugs contributed to 10.6% of fatalities)
           <br/>
           -	Wear your helmet (not wearing a helmet contributed to 7% of fatalities)
          </p>
          <p>
           And to bring this back on topic, get ABS on your bike because:
          </p>
          <p>
           -	The Insurance Institute for Highway Safety (IIHS) concluded that motorcycles above 250cc equipped with ABS are 37% less likely to be involved in fatal crashes
           <br/>
           -	The Swedish Road Administration concluded that motorcycles above 125cc equipped with ABS would have avoided 48% of all severe and fatal motorcycle accidents.*
          </p>

         <div class="replytext bodytext" data-replyid="42451590" data-uname="J B L">
          <p>
           One of the problems in retrofitting an aftermarket ABS system to a vehicle (in this case, a motorcycle) is the ADR compliance &amp; vehicle modification issues.  Furthermore, when you consider compliance rules, likelihood of approval (or lack of), cost of lawsuits (existing ABS manufacturers always keen to sue), I can imagine that there really isn't a market for aftermarket ABS for motorcycles.
          </p>
          <p>
           Aftermarket ABS?  I don't think it's an option.
          </p>
          <p>
           Retrofitting AB$? (eg putting ABS on a bike as an option such as BMW R***GS models that were sold without it) ... whether that might not be allowed according to ADR, I'd have to find out.
          </p>
          <p>
           Bottom line: Everyone seems to think that it's best to get a bike with ABS as a standard fitment.
          </p>
