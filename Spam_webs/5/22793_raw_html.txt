       <h1>
        How to Keep Crushing On Your Long-Term Partner
       </h1>
       <div class="byline">
        <a class="author-link" href="http://hellogiggles.com/author/lyndsay-rush/">
         Lyndsay Rush
        </a>
        <span class="entry-date">
         / July 30, 2014 7:17 am
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-share">
        <a alt="Facebook" class="share-popup" href="http://www.facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?u=http://hellogiggles.com/keep-chemistry-alive-relationship/">
         <i class="icon-facebook">
         </i>
        </a>
        <a alt="Tweet" class="share-popup" href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http://hellogiggles.com/keep-chemistry-alive-relationship/&amp;text=How to Keep Crushing On Your Long-Term Partner&amp;via=hellogiggles">
         <i class="icon-twitter">
         </i>
        </a>
        <a alt="Tumblr" class="share-popup" href="http://tumblr.com/widgets/share/tool?canonicalUrl=http://hellogiggles.com/keep-chemistry-alive-relationship/&amp;title=How to Keep Crushing On Your Long-Term Partner">
         <i class="icon-tumblr">
         </i>
        </a>
        <a alt="E-Mail" href="mailto:?subject=How to Keep Crushing On Your Long-Term Partner&amp;body=Check out this story: %20%0D%0A%20%0D%0AHow to Keep Crushing On Your Long-Term Partner%20%0D%0Ahttp://hellogiggles.com/keep-chemistry-alive-relationship/%20%0D%0A%20%0D%0A via hellogiggles.com">
         <i class="icon-mail">
         </i>
        </a>
       </div>
      </header>
      <section class="entry-content">
       <div class="post-thumbnail post-thumbnail-medium wp-caption alignright">
        <img height="225" src="http://cdn2.hellogiggles.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/29/new-girl-jess-nick-embrace-w724-500x375c.jpg" title="How to Keep Crushing On Your Long-Term Partner" width="300"/>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        Love Nuggets may sound like your late-night relationship with fast food, but in the UK, its something else entirely: a government-funded campaign to help couples avoid a split.
       </p>
       <p>
        Thats right: the Department of Education is
        <a href="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-2707792/How-avoid-divorce-Run-hubbie-bath-making-cup-tea-won-t-hurt-either.html">
         funding a project
        </a>
        (run by charity,
        <a href="http://www.oneplusone.org.uk/">
         <span class="hiddenSpellError">
          OnePlusOne
         </span>
        </a>
        ) that encourages couples to do relationship-nurturing things like, 
        <a href="http://hellogiggles.com/drinking-tea-the-british-way">
         make cups of tea for each other
        </a>
        ; leave notes around the house for a spouse to find; or take their other half to the cinema even when they dont want to see the film themselves.The goal being to build into your relationship
        <i>
         before
        </i>
        it reaches its breaking point, instead of relying on crisis management. The campaign will offer a love nuggets generator which will provide couples with random suggestions to
        <a href="http://hellogiggles.com/10-rom-com-couples-best-chemistry">
         spice things up.
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        While some of their suggestions seem a little shallow (and mildly sexist?) the heart of the projectto uncover and celebrate the everyday things people do that make a happy relationshipis one worth thinking about.
       </p>
       <p>
        It can be be hard to keep that insane, butterfly-fluttering, crush feeling when youre in a long-term relationship. No matter how much you love a person, ruts happen. So what are some non-cheesy, totally simple ways to keep those so-called sparks sparking? We turned to a fewexperts for guidance, natch, and found some solid advice. Chemistry, ahoy!
       </p>
       <p>
        <b>
         1. You do you.
        </b>
       </p>
       <p>
        Yes, togetherness is the name of the game, but
        <i>
         separateness,
        </i>
        or individuality, is a key ingredient in keeping your relationship healthy and happy.
        <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/deborah-hecker-phd/relationship-success-bala_b_4776478.html">
         Deborah Hecker, Ph.D suggests ditching the you complete me idea
        </a>
        and instead asking yourself questions like, What is important to me? and What are the traditions/hobbies I want to keep? Couples who respect one anothers boundaries and independence have stronger relationships, so get back to taking those hip hop lessons or that solo trip you planned once upon a time; your relationship will be better for it.
       </p>
       <p>
        <b>
         2. Be spontaneous.
        </b>
       </p>
       <p>
        According to sex therapist
        <a href="http://www.ted.com/talks/esther_perel_the_secret_to_desire_in_a_long_term_relationship">
         Esther Perels Ted Talk,
        </a>
        long-term relationships draw out two conflicting needs in us: that for security and that for surprise. She suggests adding some new moves to your repertoire, whether thats in the bedroom, or in the everyday. Her research suggests that spontaneity and novelty are great ways of sustaining desire in a relationship. So when that predictable schedule or way of doing things rears its head, try switching it up and see what happens. Remember and foster your adventurous and curious side.
       </p>
       <p>
        <b>
         3. Celebrate good times COME ON
        </b>
       </p>
       <p>
        The pros have found that a big reason for divorce among couples is feeling underappreciated by their spouse. So the antidote? Simple: support, appreciate, and cheer on your partner in the big and little things. The reason this is so big, according to
        <a href="http://online.wsj.com/news/articles/SB10001424052702304640104579487452139791452">
         research by the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology
        </a>
        , is that couples whose spouses are supportive when things are going well, believe largely that they will then be supportive when things go poorly. Just the perception that there would be support during bad times increased their emotional intimacy, trust and marital satisfaction, said the co-author of the study, Shelly Gable. That feels like a winner to me!
       </p>
