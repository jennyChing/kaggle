           <h1 class="post-title">
            Road Bicycles for Sale: How to Change a Chain
           </h1>
           <p class="post-byline">
            by
            <a href="http://trainingfortriathlonswithmike.com/author/admin/" rel="author" title="Posts by Mike Rich">
             Mike Rich
            </a>
             December 12, 2013
           </p>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <div class="entry share">
            <div class="entry-inner">
             <h1>
              Thinking of buying used road bicycles for sale? Dont forget to change the chain after buying one. Read on to find out why and how!
             </h1>
             <p>
              There are a lot of people new to cycling who are not aware that a bike chain is considered a consumable, that is expected to be replaced periodically.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://images.trainingfortriathlonswithmike.com/meme/view/Road-Bicycles-for-Sale:-How-to-Change-a-Chain/52a9b1c215e71">
               <img alt="Road Bicycles for Sale: How to Change a Chain" data-caption="true" data-description="Changing the bicycle chain." data-memeid="52a9b1c215e71" height="250" src="http://images.trainingfortriathlonswithmike.com/uploads/full/52a9b1c215e71.png" title="" width="250"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              They usually find this out when their bike no longer shifts smoothly, or they experience chain slip while pedaling. (If this occurs while standing, it can be very painful as you come crashing down on the top-tube of the bike).
             </p>
             <p>
              Often, you can get away with ignoring your chain except for a little oil now and then, but you will come to regret this.
             </p>
             <p>
              When chains wear, there is often no easily visible indication that they are doing so. They seem to look just the same as when new, just a little dirtier. But chains wear in an odd way: they stretch.
             </p>
             <p>
              Now chains dont actually stretch in the normal sense of that word. If you measured an old and a new chain, each link of the old worn chain will be the same length as a link on the new chain, yet the old chain will be longer. You could painstakingly count the number of links to make sure that there were the same number of these same sized links. Yet, the old chain would still be longer.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               The secret:
              </strong>
              Chains wear out the connecting pins that hold the links together. The pins get ground down by all the grit your chain picks up from the road.
             </p>
             <p>
              This wear is hard to see because its hidden between the two outer chain side plates.
             </p>
             <p>
              The two outer side plates are friction fit to the chain pin, and wear does not generally occur there. The two inner plates are meant to swivel around the chain pin each time the chain goes around the gear. This repeated motion slowly grind the pin diameter down.
             </p>
             <p>
              Slowly, over time, the effective diameter of these pins gets smaller, and in some sever cases they get groves in them. In addition, the holes through the inner set of side plates (or the bushings in those holes) become slightly oval shaped.
             </p>
             <p>
              Chain stretch then, is simply the accumulation of a tiny bit of wear over many pins. Normally, a chain link is half an inch from pin to pin, and one full inch from the start of one link to the start of the following link (Yup, its measured in inches, not centimeters, its always been this way). When the pins wear, the overall distance between them gets further.
             </p>
             <p>
              So What?Who cares if the chain stretches? You do (or you should) and heres why.
             </p>
             <p>
              A worn chain measures just a tad longer between the pins, and does not mesh well with the gears on the bike, all of which are carefully made to mesh perfectly when the pins are half an inch from each other. As the pins move further from each other they come to rest on a different part of the gear tooth. Instead of nestling smoothly in the hollow between gear teeth, they land on the face of the following gear tooth, and start to wear that tooth down. More at
              <a href="http://mywheelsandmore.com/bicycleChains/bicycleChainLength.htm">
               <strong>
                Bicycle Chain Length
               </strong>
              </a>
             </p>
