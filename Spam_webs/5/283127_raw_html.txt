   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Tyranny of small decisions
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       tyranny of small decisions
      </b>
      is a phenomenon explored in an essay by that name, published in 1966 by the American economist
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Kahn" title="Alfred E. Kahn">
       Alfred E. Kahn
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kahn-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The article describes a situation where a number of decisions, individually small in size and time perspective, cumulatively result in an outcome which is not optimal nor desired. It is a situation where a series of small, individually rational decisions can negatively change the context of subsequent choices, even to the point where desired alternatives are irreversibly destroyed. Kahn described the problem as a common issue in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_economics" title="Market economics">
       market economics
      </a>
      which can lead to
      <a href="/wiki/Market_failure" title="Market failure">
       market failure
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kahn-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The concept has since been extended to areas other than economic ones, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">
       environmental degradation
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      political elections
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and health outcomes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A classic example of the tyranny of small decisions is the
      <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">
       tragedy of the commons
      </a>
      , described by
      <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Hardin" title="Garrett Hardin">
       Garrett Hardin
      </a>
      in 1968
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardin68_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hardin68-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as a situation where a number of herders graze cows on a commons. The herders each act independently in what they perceive to be their own rational self-interest, ultimately depleting their shared limited resource, even though it is clear that it is not in any herder's long-term interest for this to happen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ithaca_railroad">
       Ithaca railroad
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyranny_of_small_decisions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ithaca railroad">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1196" data-file-width="1227" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg/240px-Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg/360px-Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg/480px-Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Twin_Glens_abutment.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Abutment of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ithaca-Auburn_Short_Line" title="Ithaca-Auburn Short Line">
         Ithaca-Auburn Short Line
        </a>
        bridge
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The event that first suggested the tyranny of small decisions to Kahn was the withdrawal of passenger railway services in
      <a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">
       Ithaca, New York
      </a>
      . The railway was the only reliable way to get in and out of Ithaca. It provided services regardless of conditions, in fair weather and foul, during peak seasons and off-peak seasons. The local airline and bus company skimmed the traffic when conditions were favourable, leaving the trains to fill in when conditions were difficult. The railway service was eventually withdrawn, because the collective individual decisions made by travellers did not provide the railway with the revenue it needed to cover its incremental costs. According to Kahn, this suggests a hypothetical economic test of whether the service should have been withdrawn.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Suppose each person in the cities served were to ask himself how much he would have been willing to pledge regularly over some time period, say annually, by purchase of prepaid tickets, to keep rail passenger service available to his community. As long as the amount that he would have declared (to himself) would have exceeded what he actually paid on the periodand my own introspective experiment shows that it wouldthen to that extent the disappearance of the passenger service was an incident of market failure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn2_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kahn2-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The failure to reflect the full value to passengers of keeping the railroad service available had its origins in the discrepancy between the time perception within which the travellers were operating, and the time perception within which the railroad was operating. The travellers were making many short term decisions, deciding each particular trip whether to go by the railroad, or whether to go instead by car, bus or the local airline. Based on the cumulative effects of these small decisions, the railroad was making one major long run decision, "virtually all-or-nothing and once-and-for-all"; whether to retain or abandon its passenger service. Taken one at a time, each small travel decision made individually by the travellers had a negligible impact on the survivability of the railroad. It would not have been rational for a traveller to consider the survival of the railroad imperilled by any one of his particular decisions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn2_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kahn2-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The fact remains that each selection of x over y constitutes also a vote for eliminating the
       <i>
        possibility thereafter of choosing y
       </i>
       . If enough people vote for x, each time necessarily on the assumption that y will continue to be available, y may in fact disappear. And its disappearance may constitute a genuine deprivation, which customers might willingly have paid something to avoid. The only choice the market offered travellers to influence the longer-run decision of the railroad was thus shorter in its time perspective, and the sum total of our individual purchases of railroad tickets necessary added up to a smaller amount, than our actual combined interest in the continued availability of rail service. We were victims of the "tyranny of small decisions".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn2_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kahn2-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Earlier_references_to_the_idea">
       Earlier references to the idea
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyranny_of_small_decisions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earlier references to the idea">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">
       Thucydides
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">
       c.
      </a>
      460c. 395
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BCE" title="BCE">
       BC
      </a>
      ) stated:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       [T]hey devote a very small fraction of time to the consideration of any public object, most of it to the prosecution of their own objects. Meanwhile each fancies that no harm will come to his neglect, that it is the business of somebody else to look after this or that for him; and so, by the same notion being entertained by all separately, the common cause imperceptibly decays.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      (384322 BC) similarly argued against common goods of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">
        polis
       </a>
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Athens" title="History of Athens">
       Athens
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       For that which is common to the greatest number has the least care bestowed upon it. Every one thinks chiefly of his own, hardly at all of the common interest; and only when he is himself concerned as an individual. For besides other considerations, everybody is more inclined to neglect the duty which he expects another to fulfill; as in families many attendants are often less useful than a few.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aristotle_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Aristotle-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mun" title="Thomas Mun">
       Thomas Mun
      </a>
      (15711641), an English
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercantilist" title="Mercantilist">
       mercantilist
      </a>
      , commented about decisions made with a myopic, small time perspective:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       [T]hey search no further than the beginning of the work, which mis-informs their judgements, and leads them into error: For if we only behold the actions of the husbandman in the seed-time when he casteth away much good corn into the ground, we will rather account him a mad-man than a husbandman: but when we consider his labours in the harvest which is the end of his endeavours, we find the worth and plentiful increase of his actions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         10
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugen_von_B%C3%B6hm-Bawerk" title="Eugen von Bhm-Bawerk">
       Eugen von Bhm-Bawerk
      </a>
      (18511914), an Austrian economist, observed that decisions made with small time perspectives can have a seductive quality:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       It occurs frequently, I believe, that a person is faced with a choice between a present and a future satisfaction or dissatisfaction and that he decides in favor of lesser present pleasure even though he knows perfectly well, and is even explicitly aware at the moment he makes his choice, that the future disadvantage is the greater and that therefore his well-being, on the whole, suffers by reason of his choice. The "playboy" squanders his whole month's allowance in the first few days on frivolous dissipation. How clearly he anticipates his later embarrassment and deprivation! And yet he is unable to resist the temptations of the moment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_degradation">
       Environmental degradation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyranny_of_small_decisions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental degradation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG/220px-Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG/330px-Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG/440px-Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jones_River_marshland_near_mouth.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        As a result of many small decisions, and without the issue being directly addressed, nearly half the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshland" title="Marshland">
         marshlands
        </a>
        were destroyed along the coasts of Connecticut and Massachusetts.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1982, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuarine" title="Estuarine">
       estuarine
      </a>
      ecologist, William Odum, published a paper where he extended the notion of the tyranny of small decisions to environmental issues. According to Odum, "much of the current confusion and distress surrounding environmental issues can be traced to decisions that were never consciously made, but simply resulted from a series of small decisions."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Odum cites, as an example, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshland" title="Marshland">
       marshlands
      </a>
      along the coasts of Connecticut and Massachusetts. Between 1950 and 1970, almost 50 percent of these marshlands were destroyed. This was not purposely planned, and the public may well have supported preservation had they been asked. Instead, hundreds of small tracts of marshland were converted to other purposes through hundreds of small decisions, resulting in a major outcome without the overall issue ever being directly addressed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another example is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Everglades" title="Florida Everglades">
       Florida Everglades
      </a>
      . These have been threatened, not by a single unfavorable decision, but by many independent pin prick decisions, such as decisions to add this well, that drainage canal, one more retirement village, another roadway... No explicit decision was made to restrict the flow of surface water into the glades, or to encourage hot, destructive fires and intensify droughts, yet this has been the outcome.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      With few exceptions,
      <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">
       threatened
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">
       endangered species
      </a>
      owe their predicament to series of small decisions.
      <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">
       Polar bears
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">
       humpback whales
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bald_eagle" title="Bald eagle">
       bald eagles
      </a>
      have suffered from the cumulative effects of single decisions to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overexploit" title="Overexploit">
       overexploit
      </a>
      or convert habitats. The removal, one by one, of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_turtle" title="Green turtle">
       green turtle
      </a>
      nesting beaches for other uses parallels the decline in green turtle populations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cultural lake
      <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">
       eutrophication
      </a>
      is rarely the result of an intentional decision. Instead, lakes eutrophy gradually as a cumulative effect of small decisions; the addition of this domestic sewage outfall and then that industrial outfall, with a runoff that increases steadily as this housing development is added, then that highway and some more agricultural fields.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The insidious effects of small decisions marches on; productive land
      <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">
       turns to desert
      </a>
      , groundwater resources are
      <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">
       overexploited
      </a>
      to the point where they can't recover, persistent pesticides are used and
      <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">
       tropical forests are cleared
      </a>
      without factoring in the cumulative consequences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Counters">
       Counters
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyranny_of_small_decisions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Counters">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:260px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #ffffff;">
      <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
       <div>
        <p>
         Considering all of the pressures and short-term rewards that guide society toward simple solutions, it seems safe to assume that the "tyranny of small decisions" will be an integral part of environmental policy for a long time to come.  William Odum
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-7">
          <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           2
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="text-align: left;">
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An obvious counter to the tyranny of small decisions is to develop and protect appropriate upper levels of decision making. Depending on the issue, decision-making may be appropriate at a local, state, country or global level. However, organisations at these levels can entangle themselves in their own bureaucracy and politics, assigning decisions by default back to the lower levels. Political and scientific systems can encourage small decisions by rewarding specific problems and solutions. It is usually easier and more politic to make decision on individual tracts of land or single issues rather than implementing large-scale policies. The same pattern applies with academic science. Most scientists are more comfortable working on specific problems rather than systems. This reductionist tendency towards the small problems is reinforced in the way grant monies and academic tenure are assigned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Odum advocates that at least some scientists should study systems so the negative consequences that result when many small decisions are made from a limited perspective can be avoided. There is a similar need for politicians and planners to understand large scale perspectives.
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">
       Environmental science
      </a>
      teachers should include large scale processes in their courses, with examples of the problems that decision making at inappropriate levels can introduce.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum_2-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odum-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
