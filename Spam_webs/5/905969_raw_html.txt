      <h1>
       Cooking Pumpkins in the Community
      </h1>
      <span class="date">
       October 14, 2014
      </span>
     </div>
     <div class="post-entry">
      <p>
       <img alt="Cooking Pumpkins in the Community | greensideup.ie" class="aligncenter wp-image-11278 size-full" height="1024" src="http://greensideup.ie/media/Pumpkins-in-the-Community.jpg" width="903"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Not content with growing the vegetables, a couple of weeks ago I
       <del>
        rashly
       </del>
       gamely offered to demonstrate a few ways of cooking pumpkin flesh at Callan community garden as theres little point in growing food if we dont know how to prepare and eat it. Its the first year weve grown a pumpkin patch there and as the fruit have swollen nicely, it seemed a good idea to demonstrate that theres more to pumpkins than Halloween window decorations.Im sure many of us are, or have been guilty of discarding the flesh we scoop out and it seems such a waste of good food. In the shops and farmers markets, pumpkins are coming into season and are a vegetable/fruit that will store for months in a cool, dry environment, making them a fantastic winter staple.
      </p>
      <p>
       Not only do pumpkins make great decorations, they
       <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2014/09/04/pumpkin-health-benefits_n_1936919.html" target="_blank" title="Health Benefits of Pumpkins">
        are extremely good for us
       </a>
       , containing over 200% of our recommended daily allowance of vitamin A, the vitamin thats good for our eye sight, theyre rich in fibre, contain very few calories and are great for helping to lower cholesterol among other things.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Cookery Demonstration
      </h2>
      <p>
       <img alt="Cooking Pumpkins in the Community | greensideup.ie" class="alignright wp-image-11274 size-medium" height="300" src="http://greensideup.ie/media/Giant-Pumpkin-249x300.jpg" width="249"/>
       Im a family cook who likes a recipe in front of me (even though I stray from it quite regularly) which therefore resulted in a very informal cookery session at the family resource centre where everyone helped with the prepping and washing up, before gathering to share the food presented. I chose two safe, tried and tasted savoury pumpkin recipes using the flesh from one medium-sized pumpkin, as well as a roasted seed recipe that you can find below. I also demonstrated how to make courgette cake, a recipe Ive talked about on several occasions but gardeners had yet to try.The
       <a href="http://greensideup.ie/courgette-cake-recipe/" target="_blank">
        courgette cake recipe can be found here
       </a>
       and
       <a href="http://greensideup.ie/pumpkin-rice-pumpkin-soup-recipes/" target="_blank">
        the basic soup and delicately flavoured pumpkin rice recipes here
       </a>
       . Im afraid theres no photos as I was too busy cooking.
      </p>
      <p>
       I would have loved to have baked a pumpkin dessert for the group but simplydidnt have time to find a recipe that uses fresh pumpkin flesh  no matter where I looked, they all used tinned pumpkin pure. However, Ive since
       <a href="http://thatbloomingarden.wordpress.com/2011/11/02/cooking-with-fresh-pumpkin/" target="_blank" title="How to Make Pumpkin Puree">
        been given this recipe that shares how to make our own pure by Kristen who writes That Blooming Garden Blog
       </a>
       , so theyll be no stopping us.
      </p>
      <p>
       If youd like to try cooking pumpkins this year, as well as the recipes linked above that I cooked for the group, Ive added a few variations of soup at the bottom of the post from some fellow garden bloggers.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <img alt="Cooking Pumpkins in the Community | greensideup.ie" class="alignright wp-image-11276 size-thumbnail" height="150" src="http://greensideup.ie/media/roasted-pumpkin-seeds-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
       Roasted pumpkin seeds
      </h2>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Ingredients
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       225g pumpkin seeds
       <br/>
       450ml water
       <br/>
       2 tbls salt
       <br/>
       1 tblsp olive oil
      </p>
      <p>
       Heat oven to 20oC/Gas 6/400F
      </p>
      <p>
       Remove the lid of the pumpkin at the stalk end by cutting a disk shape around the top with a sharp knife. Scoop out the soft, seedy, fibrous flesh inside with a metal spoon and place into a colander, leaving the tougher flesh thats around the inside of the pumpkin to tackle later for another recipe.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pick out as many of the seeds as you can before sifting through the rest under a tap of running water.
       <em>
        (Tip: do this holding the colander over a bowl and use the drained water for the plants or flush the toilet with it.)
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       Add the seeds, water and salt to a saucepan, bring to the boil then simmer for ten minutes or so to allow the seeds to soften.
      </p>
      <p>
       Take off the heat, drain, pat the seeds dry with a clean tea towel then toss in the olive oil before placing on a baking tray lined with baking paper. Roast in the oven for around ten to twenty minutes, until the seeds brown.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Three Pumpkin Soup Recipes
      </h2>
      <p>
       Soup is such a versatile dish, quick to make and winter warming too. Here are some links to three variations of pumpkin soup you might like to try.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Cooking Pumpkin in the Community" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-11265" height="255" src="http://greensideup.ie/media/20141013_120013_000_1_resized-300x255.jpg" width="300"/>
       The first is from
       <a href="http://detoutcoeurlimousin.blogspot.fr/2014/10/roast-pumpkin-soup-with-sage-garlic.html" target="_blank">
        Emma from De Tout Coeur Limousin in France
       </a>
       where she adds sage, garlic and chilli to her pumpkin recipe .
      </p>
      <p>
       Secondly, from Kristin in British Columbia,
       <a href="http://thatbloomingarden.wordpress.com/2012/10/11/31-days-of-gardening-with-children-day-11/" target="_blank">
        a step by step guide to pumpkin soup with a nutmeg flavouring, very handy if youre new to soup making
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lastly (and these are in no particular order)
       <a href="http://www.newhousenewhomenewlife.com/#sthash.eBEvJBAP.dpbs" target="_blank">
        Heather from the New House New Home New Life blog
       </a>
       makes a curried soup and although has used pure as a base, the flavouring could easily be switched to a fresh pumpkin recipe.
      </p>

      <h2>
       History of Pumpkin Carving
      </h2>
      <p>
       If you prefer to carve your pumpkins,
       <a href="http://greensideup.ie/so-why-do-we-carve-out-pumpkins-at-halloween/" target="_blank" title="Why we carve pumpkins">
        heres an archived post on the blog that explains why
       </a>
       we do it. Did you know the tradition originated in Ireland?
      </p>
