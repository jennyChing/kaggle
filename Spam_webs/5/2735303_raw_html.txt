                <span class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text" id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" style="">
                 <p>
                  <img alt="dead-deals" src="http://blog.xoombi.com/hs-fs/hub/371955/file-2085158226-jpg/dead-deals.jpg?t=1436816192323&amp;width=700" style="width: 700px; margin: 5px auto 20px; display: block;" width="700"/>
                  We have a great product, just get them to the demo, and the software sells itself! Wouldnt that be nice? ...If only it were true.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Software rarely sells itself, but over and over again, I see software companies skipping critical steps in the sales process to get to the demo. Heres how the sales process plays out for companies who make this mistake
                 </p>
                 <!--more-->
                 <h3>
                  Killing the Deal
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  What is the first thing visitors see when they come to your website? Sign Up for a Free Trial or Schedule a Demo.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The prospect completes a form and gets an email from a business development rep who manages everyones demo schedule. After a few emails back-and-forth, they get something on the calendar.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Demo time comes two days later. An assigned salesperson meets the prospect for the first time, conducts a brief introduction, asks a few questions, and launches straight into the demo. The sales demos are all about how the software can do this, and it can do that feature, feature, feature.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Thats
                  <a href="http://blog.xoombi.com/how-an-egomaniac-cost-me-a-five-figure-commission-check-and-changed-my-sales-career">
                   when the deal starts to crumble
                  </a>
                  . The prospect is on the other line, distracted, multitasking, spraying their chair with WD-40 while we go on feature dumping in the background. Once in a while, we hit on something that triggers their interest. They ask, Can the software do this? and we respond, Yep, lets jump over to this screen and let me show you how that FEATURE works. Of course we think, this is going great, but at the end of the demo, the prospect isnt ready to buy, and they have no plans or thoughts about moving forward with next steps.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Sales spends the next two to three months following up with the same old question, Where are you guys in the process of making a decision to move forward? The prospect responds, Were tied up with other big projects right now. Call me in six months.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Six months?!!! How does that happen? It's simple. We dont REALLY KNOW WHY the prospect needs our product. We skipped the critical
                  <a href="http://blog.xoombi.com/5-questions-for-first-time-sales-appointments">
                   discovery and needs analysis stage
                  </a>
                  of the sales process. When we skip this step, it weakens our demo and kills our ability to follow up effectively.
                 </p>
                 <h3>
                  Winning the Deal
                 </h3>
                 <p>
                  When you speak with enough prospects, its common practice to lump needs into broad categories like saving time and money, improving processes and efficiency, building structure and accountability, etc.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Broad categories make it sound like you know whats going on, but its an illusion. Every client has their own story, and winning deals is about understanding the need behind the need.
                 </p>
                 <ul>
                  <li>
                   <p style="line-height: 1.25em;">
                    Why do they need to improve efficiency?
                   </p>
                  </li>
                  <li>
                   <p style="line-height: 1.25em;">
                    Who is responsible for results?
                   </p>
                  </li>
                  <li>
                   <p style="line-height: 1.25em;">
                    What specific problems can we pinpoint within the current business process?
                   </p>
                  </li>
                  <li>
                   <p style="line-height: 1.25em;">
                    What is the impact on the business?
                   </p>
                  </li>
                  <li>
                   <p style="line-height: 1.25em;">
                    How does it affect the people involved?
                   </p>
                  </li>
                  <li>
                   <p style="line-height: 1.25em;">
                    Whose job is on the line?
                   </p>
                  </li>
                 </ul>
                 <p>
                  Thats the kind of information you need going into a demo.
                  <span>
                   Stop sending deals to die, and
                   <a href="http://blog.roi-selling.com/never-lead-with-a-sales-demo" target="_blank">
                    never lead with a sales demo
                   </a>
                   without doing discovery.
                  </span>
                  Leverage insights, be relevant, sell in context, and share the advantages of using your platform to solve specific business problems. Youll build better engagement, your prospects will trust you, and youll win more new business.
                 </p>
