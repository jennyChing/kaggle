        <h1>
         Wheeler XCountry Ski - Kananaskis - Skiing Alberta
        </h1>
        <div class="region region-content">
         <div class="block block-system" id="block-system-main">
          <article class="node node--blog node--promoted node--full node--blog--full clearfix" id="node-430" role="article">
           <section class="node-submitted">
            <span class="submitted">
             By
             <span class="username">
              Barry
             </span>
            </span>
            <span class="date">
             23 / Jan / 2013
            </span>
           </section>
           <div class="node-content">
            <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
             <div class="field-items">
              <div class="field-item even">
               <p>
                <strong>
                 Cross-country skiing
                </strong>
                near
                <strong>
                 Kananaskis Lakes
                </strong>
                in
                <strong>
                 Kananaskis Country
                </strong>
                ,
                <strong>
                 Alberta
                </strong>
                ,
                <strong>
                 Canada
                </strong>
                .
               </p>
               <p>
                <img alt="" class="image-large" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/styles/large/public/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_20.jpg?itok=rsPVG3fT" style="height:640px; width:623px"/>
               </p>
               <p>
                Myobjective is to learn tocross-country ski.We have chosen
                <strong>
                 Elk Pass Trail
                </strong>
                near the end of
                <strong>
                 Kananaskis Lakes Road
                </strong>
                .After ahumiliating tumble trying toski the15 m (50 ft) from the parking lot to the trailhead, I am poised toenterthe groomed ski tracks. The day is crisp, with a breeze, but forest shelter makes the start comfortable. Following a shortdistance on rolling, uphill trail, and two more humbling spills, we retreat to find an easier trail.
               </p>
               <p>
                Learning a new sport is always humbling. I am strugglingto maintaina sense of humor whilealternatingmarginal skiingwithfallingonto the hard surface of the nicely groomed trail. Ironically, there are stick man pictures of me on the trail sign.
               </p>
               <p style="text-align: center;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16157" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_05.jpg" style="height:803px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16158" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_06.jpg" style="height:469px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
               </p>
               <p>
                Our second decision is to ski from the
                <strong>
                 Elkwood
                </strong>
                parking area, a bit further north on
                <strong>
                 Kananaskis Lakes Road
                </strong>
                .During a recent snowshoe of the
                <strong>
                 Elkwood Loop
                </strong>
                , I observed gentler trail, perhaps more suitable for an initialski experience. At trailhead, two skiers, who are very familiar with theprolific cross-country ski routes in this area,provide valuable insight and advice. We choose to ski south on the
                <strong>
                 Wheeler Trail
                </strong>
                .
               </p>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16159" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_07.jpg" style="height:344px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                The trailhead at the Elkwood parking area in Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada.
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16160" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_08.jpg" style="height:312px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                The beginning of the Wheeler Trail in Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada.
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16161" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_09.jpg" style="height:772px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                Again with the stick men! How do they know?
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <p style="text-align: left;">
                This is much more encouraging. I make it the entire 0.4 KM (1/4 mile) distance, to the
                <strong>
                 Amos Trail
                </strong>
                junction, without falling down. The trail is relatively flat and pine needles in the ski tracks are helpingto improve grip. Myfriend and workplace colleague,
                <strong>
                 Jen
                </strong>
                , is dutifully instructing me inuphill and downhill technique and doing everything humanly possible to assist me to keep myself vertical.
               </p>
               <p style="text-align: left;">
                Past the junction, I take a tumble on a slight downhill. Almost every movement is different from anything I have experienced before. Balance, and brain tomuscle communication, is compromised. Often my reaction is wrong, or too late, and I struggle to remain vertical. My falling down technique is improving. There is motivational sun on the trail ahead.
               </p>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16162" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_10.jpg" style="height:403px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                At the Wheeler Trail junction with the Amos Trail, a map rates the numerous cross-country ski trails for difficulty and distance. We continue straight towards Marl Lake.
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <p style="text-align: center;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16163" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_11.jpg" style="height:452px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
               </p>
               <p>
                Signs at trailside identify a more difficult trail component.
                <strong>
                 Jen
                </strong>
                is skiing forward trail reconnaissance and giving me the option to decide. I choose to walk the difficult component which drops more sharply into a hairpin turn near
                <strong>
                 Marl Lake
                </strong>
                , followed by a steep rise up to a forest-lined ridge, with flat trail, where I begin skiing again.
               </p>
               <p>
                There are picnic tables and viewing platforms to magnificent mountain views.2,973 m (9,754 ft)
                <strong>
                 Warrior Mountain
                </strong>
                looms above frozen
                <strong>
                 Fossil Falls
                </strong>
                , wedged between massive 3,174 m (10,414 ft)
                <strong>
                 Mount Sarrail
                </strong>
                and 3,082 m (10,112 ft)
                <strong>
                 Mount Lyautey
                </strong>
                .
               </p>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16164" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_12.jpg" style="height:532px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                Jen
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16165" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_14.jpg" style="height:554px; width:640px" title="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada"/>
                Views along the ridge top portion of the Wheeler Cross Country Ski Trail in Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada.
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <p style="text-align: center;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16166" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_15.jpg" style="height:343px; width:640px"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16167" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_16.jpg" style="height:467px; width:640px"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16168" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_17.jpg" style="height:534px; width:640px"/>
               </p>
               <p>
                One of the trail-side picnic tables along
                <strong>
                 Wheeler Trail
                </strong>
                isadjacent toan open, sunny area with amazing mountain views.Our stop, for a rest andsnack,is immediately attendedby a
                <strong>
                 Whiskey Jack
                </strong>
                <strong>
                 (Gray Jay).
                </strong>
                For the next few minutes we bond with nature.
               </p>
               <p>
                <strong>
                 Jen
                </strong>
                crumbles small portions of an energy bar into herpalm and extends her hand. It is only a few moments before the
                <strong>
                 Gray Jay
                </strong>
                flies to her hand and perches on her thumb to feed delicately from her palm. The
                <strong>
                 Gray Jay
                </strong>
                flies away into the forest,but returns in less than a minute with his mate, who is smaller, but equally comfortable, feedinggently from Jen's palm. The birdstake turns enjoying Jen's buffet lunch.Always a rewarding experience.
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16169" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_18.jpg" style="height:562px; width:640px"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16170" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_19.jpg" style="height:539px; width:640px"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16171" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_20.jpg" style="height:657px; width:640px"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16172" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_21.jpg" style="height:528px; width:640px"/>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16173" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_22.jpg" style="height:445px; width:640px"/>
                Ashort distance further south,we reach another difficult component prior to the second junction with
                <strong>
                 Amos Trail
                </strong>
                and decide to return the way we came. Our one-way distance is about 2 KM (1.25 miles). Once again Icarry my skis through the more difficult section of the routenear
                <strong>
                 Marl Lake
                </strong>
                . My attempts to ski uphill, even ongentle slopes are challenging. Mywaxless skis will simply not stick to the icy surface, even with the benefit of evergreen needles in the track. Iwonder ifmy naivet is compounded bythe camber on the ski.On the final section, which is flat followed by a gentle descent, I am beginning to establish good rhythm and I completemy first ski adventure witha satisfying finish.
               </p>
               <div style="margin: 0px auto; width: 650px; display: block;">
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="size-full wp-image-16174" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_23.jpg" style="height:433px; width:640px"/>
                Final views along the return ski of the Wheeler Cross Country Ski Trail in Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada.
                <p>
                </p>
               </div>
               <p>
                <img alt="Wheeler Trail Cross Country Ski - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-16175" src="/sites/hikingwithbarry.com/files/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wheeler_Ski_24.jpg" style="height:480px; width:640px"/>
                <strong>
                 Jen and I
                </strong>
                makethe very short drive to enjoy our lunch inside
                <strong>
                 William Watson Lodge
                </strong>
                <a href="http://www.albertaparks.ca/peter-lougheed/information-facilities/special-facilities/william-watson-lodge.aspx" target="_blank" title="William Watson Lodge - Kananaskis Country, Alberta, Canada">
                 <strong>
                  ,
                 </strong>
                </a>
                perched over
                <strong>
                 Lower Kananaskis Lake
                </strong>
                with
                <strong>
                 Mount Indefatigable
                </strong>
                consuming the skyline to the west.
               </p>
               <p>
                <strong>
                 William Watson Lodge
                </strong>

Although we do not cross paths at work often, Ialways enjoy hercompany.Jen is also a Registered Nurse.I figured there was agood chance Imight need medical attention.
