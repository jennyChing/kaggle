          <div class="article-body__content">
           <p>
            Aug. 7 (Bloomberg) -- The 1 percent is literally rich
beyond measure, depriving nations of billions in tax revenue and
obscuring shifts in global inequality.
           </p>
           <p>
            Research conducted separately by European Central Bank
economist Philip Vermeulen and London School of Economics
Gabriel Zucman show the wealth of the super-affluent -- hidden
by tax shelters and nonresponse to questionnaires -- is
undercounted. Correcting for similar lapses in income data
almost erases progress made from 1988 to 2008 in narrowing the
gap between the worlds rich and poor, World Bank research
found.
           </p>
           <p>
            We always suspected there was some low-balling of the top
1 percent, said Joseph Stiglitz, a Nobel-prize winning
economist and author of The Price of Inequality. Theres a
growing sense that our system is rigged and unfair.
           </p>
           <div data-view-uid="1|0_5_1_5">
            <div class="ad-v2 on-mobile" data-position="box1" id="1|0_5_1_5">
             <script type="text/javascript">

           <p>
            Failure to get a better handle on the actual amount of
wealth and income means economists and policy makers dont have
a proper understanding of the degree of disparity, which
represents a hurdle in addressing it. For instance, knowing that
earnings and assets are more concentrated could spur support for
changing the tax structure, Zucman said.
           </p>
           <p>
            If you dont have a good idea of what the world looks
like, its hard to determine what the effects of policies will
be, said Carter Price, senior mathematician at the Center for
Equitable Growth in Washington, which focuses on issues of
economic inequality. Looking retrospectively, its hard to
assess what the effects of a policy were.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Richest Rich
           </h2>
           <p>
            The richest of Americas rich -- the top 0.1 percent with
at least $20 million in net wealth -- held 23.5 percent of all
U.S. wealth in 2012 after adding in estimates of how much was
hidden in offshore tax havens, said Zucman, a visiting scholar
at the University of California at Berkeley. That compares with
his previous estimate of 21.5 percent.
           </p>
           <p>
            He collaborates with Thomas Piketty, author of the
bestseller Capital in the Twenty-First Century, and
University of California at Berkeley professor Emmanuel Saez in
trying to come up with more accurate tax-record figures.
           </p>
           <p>
            Survey data on all the ultra-rich also undershoot, foiled
in part by small sample sizes, ECB researcher Vermeulen writes
in a July paper. The 1 percent held 35 percent to 37 percent of
wealth in 2010, exceeding the 34 percent indicated in the
Federal Reserves Survey of Consumer Finances, Vermeulen found
in his paper.
           </p>
           <p>
            While the Fed already oversamples the rich to try to make
its numbers more accurate, Vermeulen readjusted the data and
supplemented it with Forbes Worlds Billionaires lists.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Greater Concentration
           </h2>
           <p>
            A greater concentration of income and wealth at the top
could help explain why consumer spending has been slow to
rebound from the recession that ended in June 2009, according to
Stiglitz.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some of the problems in the performance of the economic
system are related to the true degree of inequality, not the
measured degree of inequality, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            Since the 18-month slump ended, the Bloomberg Industries
Mass Merchant Index, which includes Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and
Dollar General, is up 80 percent, less than the 109 percent gain
in the Standard &amp; Poors 500 Index. Luxury retailers have
flourished, as evidenced by the 254 percent surge in the
Bloomberg Industries Global Luxury Goods Index, which includes
companies such as Coach Inc., Hermes International and Prada
Spa.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Not Surprised
           </h2>
           <p>
            Jeffrey Hollender, who is among the wealthiest 1 percent in
the U.S., isnt surprised the worlds richest have even more
than currently estimated.
           </p>
           <p>
            The more money that you have, the easier it becomes to
hide that and avoid taxes, said Hollender, 59, co-founder of
cleaning and personal-care products company Seventh Generation
Inc. He is a member of Responsible Wealth, a Boston-based
network that advocates for economic fairness.
           </p>
           <p>
            The measurement of assets for Europes super rich could be
even faultier, according to Zucman. About 10 percent of their
wealth is in offshore accounts compared with 4 percent in the
U.S., he estimates in a May paper. Very rich people also have
wealth in foundations and holding companies that make
calculations difficult, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            It is possible that some European countries, like the
U.K. in particular, are almost -- or even more -- unequal
than the U.S., Zucman said, a contrast with current data that
show they are more wealth-equal.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Sampling Bias
           </h2>
           <p>
            European surveys do less than U.S. surveys to counter
sampling bias and many miss the mark more, Vermeulen found. For
example, Austrias top 1 percent held as much as 36 percent of
that countrys wealth in 2013, if adjusted with Forbes data.
Thats 13 percentage points more than one survey estimate
suggests, which would make Austria almost as unequal as the U.S.
           </p>
           <p>
            Not understanding how much income and wealth the worlds
richest hold means theyre paying less in taxes. Financial
wealth held offshore costs the U.S. government $36 billion in
annual revenue from nonpayment of income, investment,
inheritance and estate taxes, according to Zucmans paper.
Thats enough to buy lunch for every student in New York City
public schools for more than a century. Europe is losing about
$75 billion.
           </p>
           <p>
            There are potential implications for tax policy, Zucman
said. If inequalities are higher than we thought, then maybe
it can change views on the extent to which marginal tax rates
should be increased on top incomes or the extent to which we
should use other tools, like a wealth tax.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Homeless Veteran
           </h2>
           <p>
            Governments could use the additional revenue to help
address imbalances. Carl Robits, 34, sleeps on London Wall, one
of the main streets through the City, Londons financial
district. Hes been homeless for about 18 months after a medical
discharge from the Royal Anglian Regiment Second Battalion.
           </p>
           <p>
            He was part of the Occupy London movement when it took over
Finsbury Square in 2011-2012. Since then, the systems not any
better, said Robits, who was back at Finsbury Square last
week. Theres too much bureaucracy. Unless you have a drug or
alcohol problem, youre not a high priority. Im not being
helped. Im not getting any form of advice or direction.
           </p>
           <p>
            The worldwide economy made some of the biggest strides in
globalization between the fall of the Berlin Wall and the start
of the past recession, causing living standards to improve for
millions of poor people, including in China and India. The World
Bank took a look at this period and found there actually was
very little progress in reducing global inequality once
adjustments were made for possibly undercounting the wealthy.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Gini Coefficient
           </h2>
           <p>
            In contrast with unadjusted figures that show a drop in
disparity, the World Banks Gini coefficient measuring the
extent of income inequality barely budged during those decades,
according to preliminary adjustments by economists Christoph
Lakner and Branko Milanovic.
           </p>
           <p>
            With a top heavy adjustment, the decrease in inequality
-- present when we use all other adjustments -- almost entirely
dissipates, they wrote in a December paper.
           </p>
           <p>
            Thats surprising for a period when poverty was falling
sharply: The number of people living on less than $1.25 a day
dropped to 1.22 billion in 2010 from 1.91 billion in 1990 after
adjusting for inflation, World Bank data show.
           </p>
           <p>
            If earning gaps havent narrowed around the world even as
the impoverished population declined, that could really change
the way economists think about the last 30 years, said
Lawrence Mishel, president of the Economic Policy Institute in
Washington, which advocates for workers rights.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Economic Policies
           </h2>
           <p>
            We really need to have a good sense on who is holding
income and wealth, and how thats changed over time, to get a
better sense of how economic policies are playing out, said
Heidi Shierholz, an economist at the institute.
           </p>
           <p>
            Tyler Cowen, an economics professor at George Mason
University in Fairfax, Virginia, and co-author of the economics
blog Marginal Revolution, thinks focusing on the top 1 percent
when looking at income distribution is misguided. It may matter
little if people such as Microsoft co-founder Bill Gates are
taking home a greater share of income, as long as the poor are
becoming better off in the process, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            People worry about the top 1 percent too much; the real
question is whether there is opportunity for everyone else,
Cowen said. Inequality in a meaningful sense has gone down.
           </p>
           <p>
            Plus, the way income is measured skews perceptions of
inequality. Tax data on earnings dont count transfer payments
made by the federal government through programs such as Social
Security. Those even out the distribution by shifting funds
toward poorer people, said Richard Burkhauser, a Cornell
University economist whose research focuses on how public
policies affect the economic behavior of vulnerable populations.
           </p>
           <p>
            Concentration at the top and a poor grasp on inequalitys
scope do worry Dal LaMagna, chief executive officer at Brooklyn,
New-York-based manufacturer IceStone LLC and a member of
Responsible Wealth.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its not good for the wealthy people sitting on Central
Park West or 5th Avenue to have so much money that everyone else
is hating them, said LaMagna, 68, citing preferred addresses
of New York Citys rich. Ignorance allows it to persist.
           </p>

Luxury retailers have\nflourished, as evidenced by the 254 percent surge in the\nBloomberg Industries Global Luxury Goods Index, which includes\ncompanies such as Coach Inc., Hermes International and Prada\nSpa. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Not Surprised &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Jeffrey Hollender, who is among the wealthiest 1 percent in\nthe U.S., isn&#x2019;t surprised the world&#x2019;s richest have even more\nthan currently estimated. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;The more money that you have, the easier it becomes to\nhide that and avoid taxes,&#x201D; said Hollender, 59, co-founder of\ncleaning and personal-care products company Seventh Generation\nInc. He is a member of Responsible Wealth, a Boston-based\nnetwork that advocates for economic fairness. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The measurement of assets for Europe&#x2019;s super rich could be\neven faultier, according to Zucman. About 10 percent of their\nwealth is in offshore accounts compared with 4 percent in the\nU.S., he estimates in a May paper. Very rich people also have\nwealth in foundations and holding companies that make\ncalculations difficult, he said. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;It is possible that some European countries, &#x201C;like the\nU.K. in particular,&#x201D; are &#x201C;almost -- or even more -- unequal\nthan the U.S.,&#x201D; Zucman said, a contrast with current data that\nshow they are more wealth-equal. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Sampling Bias &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;European surveys do less than U.S. surveys to counter\nsampling bias and many miss the mark more, Vermeulen found. For\nexample, Austria&#x2019;s top 1 percent held as much as 36 percent of\nthat country&#x2019;s wealth in 2013, if adjusted with Forbes&#x2019; data.\nThat&#x2019;s 13 percentage points more than one survey estimate\nsuggests, which would make Austria almost as unequal as the U.S. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Not understanding how much income and wealth the world&#x2019;s\nrichest hold means they&#x2019;re paying less in taxes. Financial\nwealth held offshore costs the U.S. government $36 billion in\nannual revenue from nonpayment of income, investment,\ninheritance and estate taxes, according to Zucman&#x2019;s paper.\nThat&#x2019;s enough to buy lunch for every student in New York City\npublic schools for more than a century. Europe is losing about\n$75 billion. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;There are potential implications for tax policy,&#x201D; Zucman\nsaid. &#x201C;If inequalities are higher than we thought, then maybe\nit can change views on the extent to which marginal tax rates\nshould be increased on top incomes or the extent to which we\nshould use other tools, like a wealth tax.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Homeless Veteran &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Governments could use the additional revenue to help\naddress imbalances. Carl Robits, 34, sleeps on London Wall, one\nof the main streets through the City, London&#x2019;s financial\ndistrict. He&#x2019;s been homeless for about 18 months after a medical\ndischarge from the Royal Anglian Regiment Second Battalion. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;He was part of the Occupy London movement when it took over\nFinsbury Square in 2011-2012. Since then, &#x201C;the system&#x2019;s not any\nbetter,&#x201D; said Robits, who was back at Finsbury Square last\nweek. &#x201C;There&#x2019;s too much bureaucracy. Unless you have a drug or\nalcohol problem, you&#x2019;re not a high priority. I&#x2019;m not being\nhelped. I&#x2019;m not getting any form of advice or direction.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The worldwide economy made some of the biggest strides in\nglobalization between the fall of the Berlin Wall and the start\nof the past recession, causing living standards to improve for\nmillions of poor people, including in China and India. The World\nBank took a look at this period and found there actually was\nvery little progress in reducing global inequality once\nadjustments were made for possibly undercounting the wealthy. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Gini Coefficient &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;In contrast with unadjusted figures that show a drop in\ndisparity, the World Bank&#x2019;s Gini coefficient measuring the\nextent of income inequality barely budged during those decades,\naccording to preliminary adjustments by economists Christoph\nLakner and Branko Milanovic. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;With a &#x2018;top heavy&#x2019; adjustment, the decrease in inequality\n-- present when we use all other adjustments -- almost entirely\ndissipates,&#x201D; they wrote in a December paper. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;That&#x2019;s surprising for a period when poverty was falling\nsharply: The number of people living on less than $1.25 a day\ndropped to 1.22 billion in 2010 from 1.91 billion in 1990 after\nadjusting for inflation, World Bank data show. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;If earning gaps haven&#x2019;t narrowed around the world even as\nthe impoverished population declined, that could &#x201C;really change\nthe way economists think about the last 30 years,&#x201D; said\nLawrence Mishel, president of the Economic Policy Institute in\nWashington, which advocates for workers&#x2019; rights. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Economic Policies &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;We really need to have a good sense on who is holding\nincome and wealth, and how that&#x2019;s changed over time, to get a\nbetter sense of how economic policies are playing out,&#x201D; said\nHeidi Shierholz, an economist at the institute. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Tyler Cowen, an economics professor at George Mason\nUniversity in Fairfax, Virginia, and co-author of the economics\nblog Marginal Revolution, thinks focusing on the top 1 percent\nwhen looking at income distribution is misguided. It may matter\nlittle if people such as Microsoft co-founder Bill Gates are\ntaking home a greater share of income, as long as the poor are\nbecoming better off in the process, he said. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;People worry about the top 1 percent too much; the real\nquestion is whether there is opportunity for everyone else,&#x201D;\nCowen said. &#x201C;Inequality in a meaningful sense has gone down.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Plus, the way income is measured skews perceptions of\ninequality. Tax data on earnings don&#x2019;t count transfer payments\nmade by the federal government through programs such as Social\nSecurity. Those even out the distribution by shifting funds\ntoward poorer people, said Richard Burkhauser, a Cornell\nUniversity economist whose research focuses on how public\npolicies affect the economic behavior of vulnerable populations. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Concentration at the top and a poor grasp on inequality&#x2019;s\nscope do worry Dal LaMagna, chief executive officer at Brooklyn,\nNew-York-based manufacturer IceStone LLC and a member of\nResponsible Wealth. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;It&#x2019;s not good for the wealthy people sitting on Central\nPark West or 5th Avenue to have so much money that everyone else\nis hating them,&#x201D; said LaMagna, 68, citing preferred addresses\nof New York City&#x2019;s rich. &#x201C;Ignorance allows it to persist.&#x201D;
