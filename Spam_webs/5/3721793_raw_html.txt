      Researching Online Social Roles by Structural Signature Visualization Method
      <br/>

        <a href="http://www.smartmobs.com/category/technologies-of-cooperation/" rel="category tag" title="Soft viagra vs viagra">

      <dt>
       June 20th, 2007
      </dt>
      <dd>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <h6>
          <a href="http://www.smartmobs.com/2007/06/20/researching-online-social-roles-by-structural-signature-visualization-method/#comments" title="Comment on Researching Online Social Roles by Structural Signature Visualization Method">
           2c
          </a>
         </h6>
         <span class="al">
          by
          <a href="http://www.smartmobs.com/author/admin/" rel="author" title="Posts by admin">
           admin

         </span>
        </li>

     <div class="entry">
      <div class="entrybody">
       <p>
        Howard T. Welser from Cornell University, Eric Gleave from University of Washington and Microsoft Research, and Danyel Fisher and Marc Smith from Microsoft Research are bringing to or attention
        <a href="http://www.cmu.edu/joss/content/articles/volume8/Welser/">
         a study of major importance on social roles in online discussion groups
        </a>
        . Based on
        <strong>
         a structural signature approach
        </strong>
        , with a thorough theoretical background, a complex methodology, and a rich bibliography, the study has the goal of researching and establishing strategies and techniques of deducing such
        <strong>
         social roles
        </strong>
        from
        <strong>
         structural and behavioral signatures
        </strong>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Given the multitude of possible social roles to be found within an online discussion group, the researchers limited their area of study and focused on only one role, that of the
        <strong>
         answer person
        </strong>
        in contrast with other kinds of roles, like the
        <strong>
         discussion person
        </strong>
        . The contributing behavior of a person who offers valuable information and advice in threaded online conversations can be explained by a
        <strong>
         desire to obtain social goods like status
        </strong>
        . The authors consider the
        <strong>
         patterns of contribution
        </strong>
        as
        <strong>
         structural and behavioral signatures
        </strong>
        , thus
        <strong>
         indicators of social roles
        </strong>
        and they develop
        <strong>
         visualization strategies
        </strong>
        for observing, recording and analyzing these signatures. Also, the authors consider that they can leverage this structural and behavioral data in order to identify other types of social roles in online discussion spaces and to construct a
        <strong>
         taxonomy
        </strong>
        of such roles.
       </p>
       <p>
        Figure 1: Authorline for an answer person
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
