          <p>
           Those of you who have an interest in
           <strong>
            building with cob
           </strong>
           as in the
           <a href="http://tinyhouseblog.com/earthcob/little-cob-cabin/" target="_blank" title="Little Cob Cabin">
            <strong>
             Little Cob Cabin
            </strong>
           </a>
           posted recently, will enjoy this site by Brian Liloia one of our readers.
          </p>
          <p>
           Brian is a 23 year old currently living at
           <a href="http://www.dancingrabbit.org/" target="_blank" title="Dancing Rabbit">
            <strong>
             Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage
            </strong>
           </a>
           , learning to fulfill his desires to live more sustainably and self-sufficiently.
          </p>
          <p>
           Theres a growing need to help shape a more eco-conscious, less destructive, and healthy culture, and he hopes to share some of his experiences living in a community that values these qualities.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://netdna.tinyhouseblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/06/cobfloorplan2.jpg">
            <img alt="cobfloorplan" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-8515" height="489" src="http://netdna.tinyhouseblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/06/cobfloorplan2-600x489.jpg" title="cobfloorplan" width="600"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Building With Cob
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Brian explains his cob building process that they have developed over the past few weeks.
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Mixing Cob
            </strong>
            <strong>
             :
            </strong>
            The first step is the burrito, which consists of the final cob mix of sand, clay, and straw. When the consistency is right the mixture will roll and shape into a burrito.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Making Cobs:
            </strong>
            The second step is to make cobs, or little loafs of the material. Kind of like kneading dough. Each loaf should only take a few seconds to make.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Cob Wall:
            </strong>
            The third step is moving the loaves to the wall and lining them up. The wall is soaked with water before applying the new loaves.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Cobbler Thumbs:
            </strong>
            The fourth step is taking cobbler thumbs ( a wooden tool) and stitching the cobs together, by working the straw into the layer below the new cobs and blending them together.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Cob Finishing:
            </strong>
            In the fifth step you use your hands to create a clean, plumb edge.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           Brians
           <strong>
            cob cottage
           </strong>
           footprint is 2016, and the structure is spiral-shaped, with an urbanite foundation and all cob walls. The house will feature a reciprocal living roof, and it will make maximum usage of passive solar and feature a rocket stove connected to a mass cob bed.
          </p>
