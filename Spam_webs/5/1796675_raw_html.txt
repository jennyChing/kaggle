          </li>
         </ul>
         <div class="body-content">
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad" id="tablet-ad-47801">
           </div>
           <div class="action-points">
            <div class="ap-heading">
             <h4>
              Action Points
             </h4>
            </div>
            <div class="ap-content">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Note that two studies modeling the West African Ebola crisis show that, if further measures are not taken, the disease may infect up to a million individuals by 2015.
              </li>
              <li>
               Be aware that dedicated national and international efforts appear to be curbing the tide of the disease in Nigeria and Senegal.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           It's a nightmare scenario: 1.4 million cases of Ebola by Jan. 20, 2015, in just two of the affected countries in West Africa.
          </p>
          <p>
           That's the worst case to come out of a new CDC mathematical model that evaluated the interplay of various factors driving the epidemic in Liberia and Sierra Leone, according to
           <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/about/cdcdirector/" target="_blank">
            CDC Director Tom Frieden, MD.
           </a>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           It's an "unsurprising finding  --  that if cases continue to increase exponentially, there will be a lot of them fairly soon," Frieden told reporters in a telephone briefing.
          </p>
          <p>
           To get that number, though, you have to assume that nothing more has been or will be done to control the epidemic between now and then, Frieden said, and that's not the case.
          </p>
          <p>
           Indeed, the models did not take into account recent increases in outside assistance, according to
           <a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/blog/author/Gayle%20Smith" target="_blank">
            Gayle Smith, special assistant to the president and senior director of the National Security Council.
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           "There is a regular and by now daily positive change in terms of what resources and capabilities countries are putting on the table," she told reporters.
          </p>
          <p>
           In addition to presenting a doomsday picture, Frieden said, the model also showed exactly what has to happen to control the outbreak: If 70% of patients are in care by late December  --  care that reduces their risk of transmitting the virus  --  the epidemic would be almost over Jan. 20.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "If you get enough people effectively isolated, the epidemic can be stopped," Frieden said.
          </p>
          <p>
           "When you reach a high enough number  --  and the higher you get, the better you do  --  the number of cases plummets rapidly, almost as rapidly as the exponential rise we are seeing now," he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            'The Enormous Cost of Delay'
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           "The cautionary finding of the modeling is the enormous cost of delay," Frieden said. "For each month of delay, there's a big increase in the number of cases, and it gets that much more difficult to control the epidemic."
          </p>
          <p>
           Details of the downloadable
           <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.15620/cdc.24900" target="_blank">
            EbolaResponse tool
           </a>
           are online in
           <em>
            Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report
           </em>
           .
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The model leaves out Guinea, where
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/InfectiousDisease/GeneralInfectiousDisease/45296" target="_blank">
            the outbreak apparently started last December
           </a>
           , because Ebola has come in waves in that country and CDC scientists were unable to build a model that would capture the "sawtooth" nature of the epidemic there, Frieden said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Short-term modeling of the outbreak in Liberia and Sierra Leone suggests the two countries could have 8,000 reported Ebola cases by Sept. 30, assuming that none of the promised international aid is in place.
          </p>
          <p>
           They would also have another 13,000 unreported cases, the model shows.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            WHO Analysis
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Those numbers are in the same ballpark as
           <a href="http://www.who.int" target="_blank">
            World Health Organization
           </a>
           (WHO) projections reported
           <a href="http://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1411100?query=featured_home" target="_blank">
            online in the
            <em>
             New England Journal of Medicine
            </em>
            .
           </a>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           By Sept. 14, 2014, 4,507 probable and confirmed cases, including 2,296 deaths, had been reported from Guinea, Liberia, Nigeria, Senegal, and Sierra Leone, according to the report by the WHO Ebola Response Team.
          </p>
          <p>
           The most recent figures are 5,843 probable, confirmed, and suspected cases, as well as 2,803 deaths.
          </p>
          <p>
           They analyzed data from a subset of those  --  3,343 confirmed and 667 probable cases collected in Guinea, Liberia, Nigeria, and Sierra Leone (Senegal's single case, an import from Guinea, has not resulted in any onward transmission or death).
          </p>
          <p>
           Based on those cases, the WHO analysts estimated that the reproduction numbers  --  the average number of new cases that arise when an infected person enters an uninfected population  --  are 1.81 for Guinea, 1.51 for Liberia, and 1.38 for Sierra Leone.
          </p>
          <p>
           A reproduction number greater than 1.0 implies the infection can spread.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Indeed, their reproduction numbers imply that the number of cases doubles every 15.7 days in Guinea, every 23.6 days in Liberia, and every 30.2 days for Sierra Leone, the researchers noted.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Doubling Times
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The CDC modelers also calculated doubling times and found that cases in Liberia are doubling every 15 to 20 days, compared with 30 to 40 days in Sierra Leone.
          </p>
          <p>
           Assuming, as the CDC model also does, that there will not be any change in control measures, the WHO projects a cumulative toll by Nov. 2, 2014, of 5,740 confirmed and probable cases in Guinea, compared with 9,890 in Liberia, and 5,000 in Sierra Leone.
          </p>
          <p>
           The WHO analysts did not adjust for under-reporting, but added that "the true case load, including suspected cases and undetected cases, will be higher still."
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           <strong>
            An Expanding Epidemic
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           While the data are uncertain, owing to the challenges of collecting them, they "provide a convincing case that the epidemic is still expanding," commented
           <a href="http://www.wellcome.ac.uk/about-us/organisation/governance/executive-board/index.htm" target="_blank">
            Jeremy Farrar, MD, PhD
           </a>
           , of the Wellcome Trust, and
           <a href="http://www.lshtm.ac.uk/aboutus/people/piot.peter" target="_blank">
            Peter Piot, MD, PhD,
           </a>
           of the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine, both in London.
          </p>
          <p>
           In an accompanying
           <em>
            NEJM
           </em>
           editorial, they said one risk of an expanding epidemic is that the virus might become endemic in West Africa, and a reservoir for spread elsewhere.
          </p>
          <p>
           The WHO analysis reported that many of the clinical characteristics of the epidemic are similar to earlier outbreaks, with an average incubation period of 11.4 days and serial interval  --  the time between disease onset in one patients and onset in a subsequently infected person  --  of 15.3 days.
          </p>
          <p>
           The signs and symptoms are also similar to earlier outbreaks, with the most common symptoms being fever (reported by 87.1% of patients), fatigue (76.4%), loss of appetite (64.5%), vomiting (67.6%), diarrhea (65.6%), headache (53.4%), and abdominal pain (44.3%).
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           One implication of that finding is the explosive nature of the epidemic probably can't be blamed on biological changes in the virus itself, argued Farrar and
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/InfectiousDisease/GeneralInfectiousDisease/46637" target="_blank">
            Piot, who was one of the co-discoverers of Ebola
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           More likely, they argued, is that the unprecedented epidemic arose from a toxic combination of "dysfunctional health systems, international indifference, high population mobility, local customs, densely populated capitals, and lack of trust in authorities."
          </p>
          <p>
           But the epidemic was an "avoidable crisis," they said.
          </p>
          <p>
           It took 3 months just to recognize that there was an outbreak, they noted, in contrast to a few days for authorities in the
           <a href="http://www.who.int/csr/don/2014_08_27_ebola/en/" target="_blank">
            Democratic Republic of Congo to recognize in August they had an outbreak of their own
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           After the outbreak was recognized, "it was not until 5 months and 1,000 deaths later that a public health emergency was declared, and it was nearly another 2 months before a humanitarian response began to be put in place," Farrar and Piot wrote.
          </p>
          <p>
           They argued that the world has been lucky so far, in that "slow, cumbersome, poorly funded, conservative, and ill prepared" responses to such things as SARS and the H1N1 pandemic did not cost more lives.
          </p>
          <p>
           "This Ebola epidemic shows what can happen when luck escapes us," they wrote. "With a different pathogen and a different transmission route, a similar crisis could strike in New York, Geneva, and Beijing as easily as this one has in West Africa."
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Lessons Learned
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           In a country-by-country assessment of the epidemic at 6 months, the WHO reports that two of the affected countries, Nigeria and Senegal, are stable, at least for the moment.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Nigerian case offers two important lessons, the agency said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The first is that the conventional tools used to control Ebola outbreaks in the past  --  early detection, contact tracing, isolation and monitoring of those exposed, good infection prevention and control  --  remain highly effective when they are used quickly and efficiently.
          </p>
          <p>
           In Nigeria, the index case was man who flew from Liberia to Lagos and died there. Given that Lagos is Africa's most populous city, with what the WHO report calls a "vastly inadequate and fragile infrastructure," the stage seemed set for a disaster.
          </p>
          <p>
           But swift action, including tracing nearly all exposed people and creating facilities to isolate them during the needed 21-day monitoring period, stopped the virus cold.
          </p>
          <p>
           Even when one of the exposed people escaped surveillance and went to Port Harcourt, leading to hundreds of new high-risk exposures, the same methods worked to prevent an explosion of cases, the WHO said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The second lesson is that "if Nigeria can control an outbreak caused by such a deadly and highly contagious virus, right from the start, any country in the world can do the same," the agency said.
          </p>
