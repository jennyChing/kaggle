     <p>
      "
      <b>
       The Parting Glass
      </b>
      " is a Scottish and Irish
      <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">
       traditional song
      </a>
      , often sung at the end of a gathering of friends. It was purportedly the most popular parting song sung in
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      before
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">
       Robert Burns
      </a>
      wrote "
      <a href="/wiki/Auld_Lang_Syne" title="Auld Lang Syne">
       Auld Lang Syne
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contemplator_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-contemplator-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The song is also hugely popular in
      <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
       Ireland
      </a>
      and amongst
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">
       Irish
      </a>
      communities.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Parting_Glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Text">
       Text
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Parting_Glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Text">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The earliest known printed version was as a
      <a href="/wiki/Broadside_(music)" title="Broadside (music)">
       broadside
      </a>
      in the 1770s and it first appeared in book form in "Scots Songs" by Herd.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contemplator_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-contemplator-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An early version is sometimes attributed to Sir Alex Boswell. The text is doubtless older than its 1770 appearance in broadside, as it was recorded in the
      <i>
       Skene Manuscript
      </i>
      , a collection of Scottish airs written at various dates between 1615 and 1635.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was known at least as early as 1605, when a portion of the first stanza was written in a farewell letter, as a poem now known as "Armstrong's Goodnight", by one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Border_Reivers" title="Border Reivers">
       Border Reivers
      </a>
      executed that year for the murder in 1600 of
      <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Carmichael" title="Sir John Carmichael">
       Sir John Carmichael
      </a>
      , Warden of the Scottish West March.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tune">
       Tune
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Parting_Glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tune">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The earliest known appearance of the tune today associated with this text is as a fiddle tune called "The Peacock", included in James Aird's
      <i>
       A Selection of Scots, English, Irish and Foreign Airs
      </i>
      , vol. 2 (1782).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Weston_Joyce" title="Patrick Weston Joyce">
       Patrick Weston Joyce
      </a>
      , in his
      <i>
       Old Irish Folk Music and Songs
      </i>
      (1909), gives the tune with a different text under the name "Sweet Cootehill Town," noting, "The air seems to have been used indeed as a general farewell tune, so thatfrom the words of another song of the same classit is often called 'Good night and joy be with you all.'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The celebrated Irish folk song collector
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colm_O_Lochlainn" title="Colm O Lochlainn">
       Colm O Lochlainn
      </a>
      has taken note of this identity of melodies between "The Parting Glass" and "Sweet Cootehill Town".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Sweet Cootehill Town" is another traditional farewell song, this time involving a man leaving Ireland to go to America.
     </p>
     <p>
      The tune appeared, with sacred lyrics, in 19th American tunebooks. "Shouting Hymn" in
      <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Ingalls" title="Jeremiah Ingalls">
       Jeremiah Ingalls
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Christian Harmony
      </i>
      (1805) is a related tune.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The tune achieved wider currency among
      <a href="/wiki/Shape_note" title="Shape note">
       shape note
      </a>
      singers with its publication, associated with a text first known in the 1814
      <i>
       Collection of Hymns and Spiritual Songs
      </i>
      , "Come Now Ye Lovely Social Band", in
      <a href="/wiki/William_Walker_(composer)" title="William Walker (composer)">
       William Walker
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Harmony" title="Southern Harmony">
        Southern Harmony
       </a>
      </i>
      (1835), and in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sacred_Harp" title="The Sacred Harp">
        The Sacred Harp
       </a>
      </i>
      (1844).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This form of the song is still widely sung by
      <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Harp" title="Sacred Harp">
       Sacred Harp
      </a>
      singers under the title "Clamanda".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_adaptations">
       Modern adaptations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Parting_Glass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern adaptations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The song
      <a href="/wiki/Restless_Farewell" title="Restless Farewell">
       Restless Farewell
      </a>
      written by
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">
       Bob Dylan
      </a>
      and featured on
      <a href="/wiki/The_Times_They_Are_a-Changin%27_(album)" title="The Times They Are a-Changin' (album)">
       The Times They Are a-Changin'
      </a>
      from 1964 is heavily based on The Parting Glass.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1998, the traditional words were set to a new, slightly different melody by Irish composer
      <a href="/wiki/Shaun_Davey" title="Shaun Davey">
       Shaun Davey
      </a>
      . In 2002, he orchestrated this version for orchestra, choir, pipes, fiddle, and percussion to commemorate the opening of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Helix_(Dublin)" title="The Helix (Dublin)">
       the Helix Concert Hall
      </a>
      , Dublin, Ireland.
     </p>
