       <h1>
        Supersymmetry running out of places to hide
       </h1>
       <div class="byline">
        By
        <a href="/news/michael-collett/166910">
         <span>
          Michael Collett
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p class="published">
        Updated
        <span class="timestamp">
         September 06, 2011 21:13:39
        </span>
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content photo full">
        <a href="/news/2011-09-01/large-hadron-collider/2866024">
         <img alt="Large Hadron Collider" height="467" src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/386646-3x2-700x467.jpg" title="Large Hadron Collider" width="700"/>
        </a>
        <a class="inline-caption" href="/news/2011-09-01/large-hadron-collider/2866024">
         <strong>
          Photo:
         </strong>
         The Large Hadron Collider (LHC).
         <span class="source">
          (www.cern.ch)
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="attached-content">
        <div class="inline-content story left">
         <a href="/news/2011-06-14/scientists-create-hottest-substance-on-earth/2757822">
          <strong>

          Scientists create hottest substance on Earth
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="inline-content left">
         <a href="http://www.abc.net.au/science/articles/2010/08/31/2997489.htm">
          <span>
           <strong>
            External Link:
           </strong>
           String theory ties us in knots
          </span>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="inline-content story left">
         <a href="/news/2010-11-16/big-bang-scientists-hunt-extra-dimensions/2338074">
          <strong>
           Related Story:
          </strong>
          Big Bang scientists hunt extra dimensions
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="inline-content story left">
         <a href="/news/2010-11-09/scientists-re-create-big-bang/2329144">
          <strong>
           Related Story:
          </strong>
          Scientists re-create big bang
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="inline-content map left">
         <div class="story-map">
         </div>
         <a class="inline-caption" href="http://maps.google.com/?q=-37.8099,144.9622(Melbourne%203000)&amp;z=5">
          <strong>
           Map:
          </strong>
          Melbourne 3000
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) has raised awkward questions for theoretical physicists, with experiments so far showing no evidence for the existence of supersymmetry.
       </p>
       <p>
        Professor Geoffrey Taylor from the University of Melbourne says while the theory is not dead yet, it is running out of places to hide.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It's a beautiful model in some theoretical senses. If it were a true representation of reality it would solve all sorts of problems the LHC is looking to solve," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "But there is not one skerrick of data yet which would suggest that we have to have supersymmetry. There's no experimental evidence for supersymmetry."
       </p>
       <p>
        Supersymmetry predicts the existence of a host of new superparticles, each partnered with a particle from the standard model of physics; for example, quarks may have a corresponding supersymmetric particle known as a squark.
       </p>
       <p>
        The LHC, the world's largest particle accelerator situated near Geneva in Switzerland, is smashing particles together to see and detect these superparticles.
       </p>
       <p>
        Though supersymmetry has existed in various forms since the 1960s, with many scientists hailing its aesthetic beauty and predictive powers, it has not been experimentally tested until now.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There are many variants of supersymmetry and the different variants will end up with different predictions," said Professor Taylor, director of Melbourne University's ARC Centre of Excellence for Particle Physics.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The LHC has already in its very short history eliminated quite a bit of territory from where supersymmetry might lie; it hasn't excluded all variants though."
       </p>
       <p>
        Supersymmetry, if correct, would help provide a "theory of everything" which reconciles general relativity, which explains physics on a large scale, with the standard model, which describes subatomic processes.
       </p>
       <p>
        It may also help explain the presence of dark matter  particles that appear to exist in the universe but which have not been found.
       </p>
       <h2>
        'Very sceptical'
       </h2>
       <p>
        Professor Taylor recently arrived back in Melbourne from Mumbai in India where the latest results from the LHC were revealed at a conference last week.
       </p>
       <p>
        He says it is the simplest models of supersymmetry that are currently being squeezed by the LHC experiments.
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content quote right">
        <blockquote class="pullquote">
         <div class="quote">
          <p>
           It is a beautiful theory, but I'm an experimentalist, and until we have a real reason for believing in some model ... then I will be very sceptical.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="cite">
          Professor Geoffrey Taylor
         </div>
        </blockquote>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        "It would be very nice to have the most minimal model represent reality if supersymmetry at all was going to say anything," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It is a beautiful theory, but I'm an experimentalist, and until we have a real reason for believing in some model having some basis in reality through experiment then I will be very sceptical of anything new."
       </p>
       <p>
        Professor Taylor says the reaction to the results was mixed, with theorists disappointed their promising ideas may not bear fruit, but experimentalists like himself are happy to upset the apple cart.
       </p>
       <p>
        "That's what we're supposed to be doing as experimentalists. We can only prove things wrong; we can't prove things right," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I think it is the sort of thing we want the LHC to be doing - to break some of these norms and force people to look at the data again, now that we have real data, and review their preconceptions."
       </p>
       <h2>
        Not in vain
       </h2>
       <p>
        But though many scientific careers have been dedicated to the pursuit of minimal models of supersymmetry, Professor Taylor says their efforts will not have been in vain if their theories are eventually disproven.
       </p>
       <p>
        "With any of these endeavours where you're trying to pick out the eyes of a particular model and pursue them as a representation of reality, you will learn a lot along the way in terms of techniques and in terms of where the certain shortcomings of certain types of theories are."
       </p>
       <p>
        Professor Taylor also notes there will be plenty of theorists who will continue to look for variants that are not excluded by current data.
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content quote right">
        <blockquote class="pullquote">
         <div class="quote">
          <p>
           If the Higgs exists, the LHC experiments will soon find it. If it does not, its absence will point the way to new physics.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="cite">
          Sergio Bertolucci
         </div>
        </blockquote>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Meanwhile, the LHC is closing in on the elusive Higgs boson - or God particle as it has been dubbed - with physicists expecting the LHC to prove or disprove its existence within the next year.
       </p>
       <p>
        "These are exciting times for particle physics," CERN research director Sergio Bertolucci said after results were announced in Mumbai.
       </p>
       <p>
        "If the Higgs exists, the LHC experiments will soon find it. If it does not, its absence will point the way to new physics."
       </p>
       <p>
        The Higgs boson, which is thought to have given matter its mass just after the Big Bang, is predicted under the standard model of physics but has not been verified.
       </p>
       <p>
        The LHC is expected to have doubled the amount of data produced so far by the end of this year.
       </p>
