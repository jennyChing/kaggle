     <p>
      <b>
       Gluons
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'g' in 'guy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
           l
          </span>
          <span title="/u/ long 'oo' in 'food'">
           u
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'o' in 'body'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="'z' in 'Zion'">
           z
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      are
      <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">
       elementary particles
      </a>
      that act as the exchange particles (or
      <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">
       gauge bosons
      </a>
      ) for the
      <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">
       strong force
      </a>
      between
      <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">
       quarks
      </a>
      , analogous to the exchange of
      <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
       photons
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_force" title="Electromagnetic force">
       electromagnetic force
      </a>
      between two
      <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">
       charged particles
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HyperPhysics_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HyperPhysics-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In technical terms, gluons are
      <a href="/wiki/Vector_boson" title="Vector boson">
       vector
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">
       gauge bosons
      </a>
      that mediate
      <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">
       strong interactions
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">
       quarks
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">
       quantum chromodynamics
      </a>
      (QCD). Gluons themselves carry the
      <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">
       color charge
      </a>
      of the strong interaction. This is unlike the
      <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
       photon
      </a>
      , which mediates the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_force" title="Electromagnetic force">
       electromagnetic interaction
      </a>
      but lacks an electric charge. Gluons therefore participate in the strong interaction in addition to mediating it, making QCD significantly harder to analyze than QED (
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">
       quantum electrodynamics
      </a>
      ).
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">
       Properties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Feynman_Diagram_Y-3g.PNG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="175" data-file-width="285" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Feynman_Diagram_Y-3g.PNG/240px-Feynman_Diagram_Y-3g.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Feynman_Diagram_Y-3g.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Feynman_Diagram_Y-3g.PNG 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Feynman_Diagram_Y-3g.PNG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Diagram 2: e
        <sup>
         +
        </sup>
        e
        <sup>
         
        </sup>
         (9.46)  3g
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The gluon is a
      <a href="/wiki/Vector_boson" title="Vector boson">
       vector boson
      </a>
      ; like the
      <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
       photon
      </a>
      , it has a
      <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">
       spin
      </a>
      of 1. While massive spin-1 particles have three polarization states, massless gauge bosons like the gluon have only two polarization states because
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauge_invariance" title="Gauge invariance">
       gauge invariance
      </a>
      requires the polarization to be transverse. In
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
       quantum field theory
      </a>
      , unbroken gauge invariance requires that gauge bosons have zero mass (experiment limits the gluon's rest mass to less than a few meV/c
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ). The gluon has negative intrinsic
      <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">
       parity
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Numerology_of_gluons">
       Numerology of gluons
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Numerology of gluons">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Unlike the single
      <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
       photon
      </a>
      of QED or the three
      <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons">
       W and Z bosons
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">
       weak interaction
      </a>
      , there are eight independent types of gluon in QCD.
     </p>
     <p>
      This may be difficult to understand intuitively.
      <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">
       Quarks
      </a>
      carry three types of
      <a href="/wiki/Color_charge" title="Color charge">
       color charge
      </a>
      ; antiquarks carry three types of anticolor. Gluons may be thought of as carrying both color and anticolor, but to correctly understand how they are combined, it is necessary to consider the mathematics of color charge in more detail.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Color_charge_and_superposition">
       Color charge and superposition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Color charge and superposition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
       quantum mechanics
      </a>
      , the states of particles may be added according to the
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">
       principle of superposition
      </a>
      ; that is, they may be in a "combined state" with a
      <i>
       probability
      </i>
      , if some particular quantity is measured, of giving several different outcomes. A relevant illustration in the case at hand would be a gluon with a color state described by:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="(r\bar{b}+b\bar{r})/\sqrt{2}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/0/270bd720f56bd93885f7eeddfc9761b5.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This is read as "redantiblue plus blueantired". (The factor of the square root of two is required for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normalisable_wave_function" title="Normalisable wave function">
       normalization
      </a>
      , a detail that is not crucial to understand in this discussion.) If one were somehow able to make a direct measurement of the color of a gluon in this state, there would be a 50% chance of it having red-antiblue color charge and a 50% chance of blue-antired color charge.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Color_singlet_states">
       Color singlet states
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Color singlet states">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It is often said that the stable strongly interacting particles (such as the proton and the neutron, i.e.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadrons" title="Hadrons">
       hadrons
      </a>
      ) observed in nature are "colorless", but more precisely they are in a "color singlet" state, which is mathematically analogous to a
      <a href="/wiki/Singlet_state" title="Singlet state">
       <i>
        spin
       </i>
       singlet state
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griff_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Griff-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Such states allow interaction with other color singlets, but not with other color states; because long-range gluon interactions do not exist, this illustrates that gluons in the singlet state do not exist either.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griff_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Griff-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The color singlet state is:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griff_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Griff-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="(r\bar{r}+b\bar{b}+g\bar{g})/\sqrt{3}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/2/c/32c8a68ec9eddcb265c41405ddc78394.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In words, if one could measure the color of the state, there would be equal probabilities of it being red-antired, blue-antiblue, or green-antigreen.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eight_gluon_colors">
       Eight gluon colors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eight gluon colors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are eight remaining independent color states, which correspond to the "eight types" or "eight colors" of gluons. Because states can be mixed together as discussed above, there are many ways of presenting these states, which are known as the "color octet". One commonly used list is:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griff_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Griff-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <table style="margin:auto;">
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="(r\bar{b}+b\bar{r})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/4/d/74ddf50e49714f21cd668556cb23d09e.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="-i(r\bar{b}-b\bar{r})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/3/d/73d949386e22dd9e1635c1dc809952f9.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="(r\bar{g}+g\bar{r})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/1/2710d72bbaf71d10e732ab3f8895899f.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="-i(r\bar{g}-g\bar{r})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/e/4/0e4b95854ff309dd25efc71b8c00f489.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="(b\bar{g}+g\bar{b})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/f/23f5baee6df6b41ec31fa685f5c2e101.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="-i(b\bar{g}-g\bar{b})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/3/3/b335ec92f821c98f76d1688ee88f59fe.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="(r\bar{r}-b\bar{b})/\sqrt{2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/f/27f23dd9920075d5ace5965f065fa16a.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="(r\bar{r}+b\bar{b}-2g\bar{g})/\sqrt{6}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/4/e/24e70db1138ccf5b2e1be7c1b69dc112.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      These are equivalent to the
      <a href="/wiki/Gell-Mann_matrices" title="Gell-Mann matrices">
       Gell-Mann matrices
      </a>
      ; the translation between the two is that red-antired is the upper-left matrix entry, red-antiblue is the upper middle entry, blue-antigreen is the middle right entry, and so on. The critical feature of these particular eight states is that they are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linearly_independent" title="Linearly independent">
       linearly independent
      </a>
      , and also independent of the singlet state; there is no way to add any combination of states to produce any other. (It is also impossible to add them to make r
      <span style="text-decoration:overline;">
       r
      </span>
      , g
      <span style="text-decoration:overline;">
       g
      </span>
      , or b
      <span style="text-decoration:overline;">
       b
      </span>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      otherwise the forbidden
      <a href="/wiki/Singlet_state" title="Singlet state">
       singlet state
      </a>
      could also be made.) There are many other possible choices, but all are mathematically equivalent, at least equally complex, and give the same physical results.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Group_theory_details">
       Group theory details
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Group theory details">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Technically, QCD is a
      <a href="/wiki/Gauge_theory" title="Gauge theory">
       gauge theory
      </a>
      with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SU(3)" title="SU(3)">
       SU(3)
      </a>
      gauge symmetry. Quarks are introduced as
      <a href="/wiki/Spinor" title="Spinor">
       spinor fields
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      <sub>
       f
      </sub>
      <a href="/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics)" title="Flavour (particle physics)">
       flavors
      </a>
      , each in the
      <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_representation" title="Fundamental representation">
       fundamental representation
      </a>
      (triplet, denoted
      <b>
       3
      </b>
      ) of the color gauge group, SU(3). The gluons are vector fields in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjoint_representation_of_a_Lie_group" title="Adjoint representation of a Lie group">
       adjoint representation
      </a>
      (octets, denoted
      <b>
       8
      </b>
      ) of color SU(3). For a general
      <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">
       gauge group
      </a>
      , the number of force-carriers (like photons or gluons) is always equal to the dimension of the adjoint representation. For the simple case of SU(
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      ), the dimension of this representation is
      <span class="nowrap">
       <i>
        N
       </i>
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
        1
      </span>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In terms of group theory, the assertion that there are no color singlet gluons is simply the statement that
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">
       quantum chromodynamics
      </a>
      has an SU(3) rather than a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U(N)" title="U(N)">
       U(3)
      </a>
      symmetry. There is no known
      <a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">
       <i>
        a priori
       </i>
      </a>
      reason for one group to be preferred over the other, but as discussed above, the experimental evidence supports SU(3).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griff_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Griff-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Confinement">
       Confinement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Confinement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">
       Color confinement
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Since gluons themselves carry color charge, they participate in strong interactions. These gluon-gluon interactions constrain color fields to string-like objects called "
      <a href="/wiki/Flux_tube" title="Flux tube">
       flux tubes
      </a>
      ", which exert constant force when stretched. Due to this force,
      <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">
       quarks
      </a>
      are
      <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">
       confined
      </a>
      within
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_particle" title="Composite particle">
       composite particles
      </a>
      called
      <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">
       hadrons
      </a>
      . This effectively limits the range of the strong interaction to
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
        6985100000000000000
       </span>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap">
        1
       </span>
       <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em">
        
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         15
        </span>
       </sup>
      </span>
      meters, roughly the size of an
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">
       atomic nucleus
      </a>
      . Beyond a certain distance, the energy of the flux tube binding two quarks increases linearly. At a large enough distance, it becomes energetically more favorable to pull a quark-antiquark pair out of the vacuum rather than increase the length of the flux tube.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gluons also share this property of being confined within hadrons. One consequence is that gluons are not directly involved in the
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">
       nuclear forces
      </a>
      between hadrons. The force mediators for these are other hadrons called
      <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">
       mesons
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Although in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_phase_of_QCD" title="Normal phase of QCD">
       normal phase of QCD
      </a>
      single gluons may not travel freely, it is predicted that there exist hadrons that are formed entirely of gluons  called
      <a href="/wiki/Glueball" title="Glueball">
       glueballs
      </a>
      . There are also conjectures about other
      <a href="/wiki/Exotic_hadron" title="Exotic hadron">
       exotic hadrons
      </a>
      in which real gluons (as opposed to
      <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">
       virtual
      </a>
      ones found in ordinary hadrons) would be primary constituents. Beyond the normal phase of QCD (at extreme temperatures and pressures),
      <a href="/wiki/Quark%E2%80%93gluon_plasma" title="Quarkgluon plasma">
       quarkgluon plasma
      </a>
      forms. In such a plasma there are no hadrons; quarks and gluons become free particles.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_observations">
       Experimental observations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gluon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Experimental observations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">
       Quarks
      </a>
      and gluons (colored) manifest themselves by fragmenting into more quarks and gluons, which in turn hadronize into normal (colorless) particles, correlated in jets. As shown in 1978 summer conferences
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMY_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SMY-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/PLUTO_detector" title="PLUTO detector">
       PLUTO detector
      </a>
      at the electron-positron collider DORIS (
      <a href="/wiki/DESY" title="DESY">
       DESY
      </a>
      ) produced the first evidence that the hadronic decays of the very narrow resonance (9.46) could be interpreted as
      <a href="/wiki/Three-jet_event" title="Three-jet event">
       three-jet event
      </a>
      topologies produced by three gluons. Later published analyses by the same experiment confirmed this interpretation and also the spin 1 nature of the gluon
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (see also the recollection
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMY_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-SMY-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PLUTO_experiments" title="PLUTO experiments">
       PLUTO experiments
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In summer 1979 at higher energies at the electron-positron collider
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETRA" title="PETRA">
       PETRA
      </a>
      (DESY) again three-jet topologies were observed, now interpreted as q
      <span style="text-decoration:overline;">
       q
      </span>
      gluon
      <a href="/wiki/Bremsstrahlung" title="Bremsstrahlung">
       bremsstrahlung
      </a>
      , now clearly visible, by
      <a href="/wiki/TASSO" title="TASSO">
       TASSO
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MARK-J&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MARK-J (page does not exist)">
       MARK-J
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and PLUTO experiments
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (later in 1980 also by
      <a href="/wiki/JADE_(particle_detector)" title="JADE (particle detector)">
       JADE
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ). The spin 1 of the gluon was confirmed in 1980 by TASSO
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and PLUTO experiments
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (see also the review
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOE_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SOE-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ). In 1991 a subsequent experiment at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LEP" title="LEP">
       LEP
      </a>
      storage ring at
      <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">
       CERN
      </a>
      again confirmed this result.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The gluons play an important role in the elementary strong interactions between
      <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">
       quarks
      </a>
      and gluons, described by QCD and studied particularly at the electron-proton collider
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HERA" title="HERA">
       HERA
      </a>
      at DESY. The number and momentum distribution of the gluons in the
      <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">
       proton
      </a>
      (gluon density) have been measured by two experiments,
      <a href="/wiki/H1_(particle_detector)" title="H1 (particle detector)">
       H1
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZEUS" title="ZEUS">
       ZEUS
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the years 1996 till today (2012). The gluon contribution to the proton spin has been studied by the
      <a href="/wiki/HERMES_experiment" title="HERMES experiment">
       HERMES experiment
      </a>
      at HERA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The gluon density in the photon (when behaving hadronically) also has been measured.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Color_confinement" title="Color confinement">
       Color confinement
      </a>
      is verified by the failure of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_quark" title="Free quark">
       free quark
      </a>
      searches (searches of fractional charges). Quarks are normally produced in pairs (quark + antiquark) to compensate the quantum color and flavor numbers; however at
      <a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">
       Fermilab
      </a>
      single production of
      <a href="/wiki/Top_quark" title="Top quark">
       top quarks
      </a>
      has been shown (technically this still involves a pair production, but quark and antiquark are of different flavor).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      No
      <a href="/wiki/Glueball" title="Glueball">
       glueball
      </a>
      has been demonstrated.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Deconfinement" title="Deconfinement">
       Deconfinement
      </a>
      was claimed in 2000 at CERN SPS
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy-ion_collisions" title="Heavy-ion collisions">
       heavy-ion collisions
      </a>
      , and it implies a new state of matter:
      <a href="/wiki/Quark%E2%80%93gluon_plasma" title="Quarkgluon plasma">
       quarkgluon plasma
      </a>
      , less interacting than in the
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">
       nucleus
      </a>
      , almost as in a liquid. It was found at the
      <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_Heavy_Ion_Collider" title="Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider">
       Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider
      </a>
      (RHIC) at Brookhaven in the years 20042010 by four contemporaneous experiments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A quarkgluon plasma state has been confirmed at the
      <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">
       CERN
      </a>
      Large Hadron Collider (LHC) by the three experiments
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Large_Ion_Collider_Experiment" title="A Large Ion Collider Experiment">
       ALICE
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATLAS" title="ATLAS">
       ATLAS
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Compact_Muon_Solenoid" title="Compact Muon Solenoid">
       CMS
      </a>
      in 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
