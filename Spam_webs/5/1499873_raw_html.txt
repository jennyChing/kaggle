            <h2>
             <u>
              How to Tell the Difference Between a Mock Charge and a Real Charge
              <br/>
             </u>
            </h2>
            <u>
             <b>
              Mock Charge
              <br/>
             </b>
            </u>
            -
            <u>
             Most charges are mock charges
            </u>
            . These are done to make a threat. The elephant is letting you know that you are making them uncomfortable.
            <i>
             "Back off."
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </i>
            - An elephant will mock charge to test you,
            <b>
             seeing if you are aggressive
            </b>
            or something that should not be considered a threat.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            -Sometimes an elephant may be unsure whether to charge or not. When this happens, they will twitch their trunk fr and swing it from side to side.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            - Biologist Dr. George Schallar discovered that elephants exhibit
            <i>
             "displacement activities."
            </i>
            Dr. Schallar found that the more elephants exhibited "displacement activities, the more likely the animal was
            <u>
             reacting out of fear
            </u>
            and was less likely to charge.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            -
            <b>
             Key Signs
            </b>
            : Ears will be fanned out. Trunk may be swinging from side to side.
           </div>

            <h2>
             <u>
              How to Avoid an Elephant Charge
              <br/>
             </u>
            </h2>
            It's important to remember,
            <b>
             we are in their territory!
            </b>
            Because of this, it pays to remember common courtesy. Elephants are animals with emotions just like humans. Here are a few things to keep in mind when coming into contact with wild elephants.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            - Elephants are not naturally aggressive. They do not want to harm you. Instead they just want to be left in peace.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            - Do not invade their space. Give them
            <b>
             approximately 30-40 metres of space
            </b>
            . Just like humans, they are uncomfortable and can feel threatened when you invade their personal space. Always allow them to have and open route and do not corner them.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            - Always give the elephant the right of way.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            - Always remain in your vehicle. and
            <u>
             do not rev your engine
            </u>
            or
            <u>
             try to drive past them at full speed
            </u>
            . This will only aggravate the animal and increase the likely hood of a charge.
           </div>
          </div>
