         <p>
          AUGUSTA, Ga.  Amy Mickelson paused, her short brown cowboy boots somewhere along the grounds near Augustas 13th fairway, not too far from the pine straw bed that once launched an iconic shot by her more famous husband. She literally pointed to her raised leg to prove the existence of the goose bumps shed just raised, the visceral response to talking about what it means to her and to Phil to make this yearly trek to the Masters.
         </p>
         <p>
          With three green jackets in his wardrobe and a potential fourth in play today  Phil heads into the final round in third place, five shots behind leader and runaway favorite Jordan Spieth  the long-term bond between the Mickelsons and the Masters is secure. But as the golfer arrived here seeking to relive the winning formula he last found in 2010, it was a different recent decision that should burnish his legacy just as much as any of the five major titles hes already won.
         </p>
         <p>
          By putting his name, and his acting skills, behind a powerful new ad campaign by KPMG, the accounting firm that is one of his longtime sponsors, Mickelson proved himself a loud, proud ambassador for womens golf, for a womens tour that is nowhere near the same financial or attention stratosphere as its male counterpart, trapped instead underneath the same glass ceiling so prevalent in corporate America.
         </p>
         <p>
          With KPMG serving as title sponsor for the inaugural KPMG Women's PGA Championship, an equivalentto the mens major that will be played June 11-14 at Westchester Country Club, Mickelsons willingness to show his support is more of what the game needs, more of what men like Masters chairman Billy Payne should be doing, rather than dismissing out of hand the notion of hosting a womens Masters, which Payne did at his annual state of the tournament address on Wednesday.
         </p>
         <p>
          Willing? I was happy to do it, Mickelson said Saturday after his stirring 5-under day, a day that included a miraculous 41-foot birdie putt on 16 that might  might  rival the line-drive laser he put through 13s pine trees back in 2010. Its a great thing to promote women in business. When you have two daughters, its even more important.  Im proud to be part of that.
         </p>
         <p>
          In the first of two commercials starring Mickelson and womens golfer Stacy Lewis, a fellow KPMG client and the onetime world No. 1, the two are shown approaching a tee, when Mickelson defers to Lewis to take the first shot. As her driver swings through the ball, it is suddenly flying above a corporate roundtable, where it hits and shatters a glass ceiling. Shards fly everywhere, some landing on the CEOs desk, others flying in front of a photo of an all-male board of directors, others swirling past a statue of Lady Justice. A final one magically comes to rest in Mickelsons tee box.
         </p>
         <p>
          A voice closes with the message, Heres to breaking more glass ceilings, in golf and everywhere else.
         </p>
         <p>
          Well, its obviously a very important cause. And Phil supports it, said Amy, mother of their three kids, Amanda, 15, Sophia, 13, and Evan, 12. Hes always felt that way. He comes from a very strong mother, has a strong wife and strong daughters. I think our daughters are very empowered as young women. We live in a home that supports them no matter what path they take. Phil was raised that way.
         </p>
         <p>
          Shouldnt we all be? But in a sports world that remains so skewed in favor of men, when the mens NCAA tournament champion Duke Blue Devils are lauded for surviving the ravages of March Madness while the three-time defending champion Connecticut women are derided for having it too easy, when the NFL is caught in an endless rinse-and-repeat cycle of atonement for failing to discipline misogynistic, abusive behavior among its players, public support from high-profile athletes like Mickelson is the exception not the norm.
         </p>
         <p>
          When this was over, Stacy said to me Anytime we have a man promoting womens golf it really is big for us, said Shawn Quill, the director of sports marketing and sports sponsorships for Montvale-based KPMG, standing under the shade of Augustas famous oak tree. She said, Looking back, I dont think weve ever had a male golfer do that.
         </p>
         <p>
          At 44, with the golf world already conquered, Mickelson has the earned the right to be selective, even with the corporate entities that have helped make him a millionaire many times over. He could have turned down the idea for these commercials, could have declined his role in the second one wherein he loses a bet to Lewis and has to serve as her caddie as payback, and no one would have ever known.
         </p>
         <p>
          He had no image to rescue, no mistake to atone for. All he had was a cause he believed in.
         </p>
         <p>
          We talked to him about this idea of a womens PGA and he said, Tell me how I can help, KPMG global chairman John Veihmeyer said near Augustas 14th green. We came up with the concept for the ads, with Stacy and Phil together, and theyre right in line with our goal of getting women on a level playing field.
         </p>
         <p>
          Caddying for her at the end  thats the kind of thing no world-class athlete is going to put himself in position for. I sent him the ads before they ran, just to check with him. He texted back This is GREAT!!!!!!!. Print em. I love them. Hes got a couple of daughters and he feels very passionate about them having the same opportunity as anybody else has.
         </p>
         <p>
          So often, so many of those business opportunities are conducted across 18 manicured holes, a tradition that has kept exclusion alive, leaving women on the wrong side of valuable networking opportunities. That only partly explains why an antiquated, outdated, exclusive, exclusionary place like Augusta didnt admit a female member until 2012, finally bowing to years of public pressure. Three women members do not an even playing field make.
         </p>
         <p>
          What Mickelson is doing matters so much more. With a gesture as simple as appearing in a television commercial that literally shatters a glass ceiling, Mickelson is helping break open the figurative one too.
         </p>
         <p>
          I loved it, Amy said. I thought it was fabulous.
         </p>
         <p>
          So did I.
         </p>
