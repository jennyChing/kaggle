     <p>
      <b>
       High cheekbones
      </b>
      refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/Zygomatic_bone" title="Zygomatic bone">
       zygomatic bones
      </a>
      in the face of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">
       primates
      </a>
      , which in certain individuals may be more pronounced than others, causing the upper part of the cheeks to jut out and form a line cut into the sides of the face. High cheekbones, forming a
      <a href="/wiki/Facial_symmetry" title="Facial symmetry">
       symmetrical face shape
      </a>
      , are very common in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_model" title="Fashion model">
       fashion models
      </a>
      and are considered a
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_attractiveness" title="Physical attractiveness">
       beauty trait
      </a>
      in both males and females.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_Society_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_Society-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      High cheekbones develop with maturity and are a sign a woman is old enough to be capable of reproduction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sex_and_Society_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sex_and_Society-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Cartwright, in males prominent facial features such as high cheekbones and a strong jaw and chin are a sign of a high level of
      <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">
       testosterone
      </a>
      and are considered attractive physical traits in many cultures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cartwright2000_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cartwright2000-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_cheekbones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cheek and
      <a href="/wiki/Zygomatic_bone" title="Zygomatic bone">
       zygomatic or malar bones
      </a>
      are the most prominent of the bone structure of facial features with malar bones contributing to the prominence of the cheeks. In the posterior part of the zygomatic bone the support is provided by the zygomatic arch, which acts as buttress support to the cheek bones) and is a part a "backward process of cheekbone and forward projection of the
      <a href="/wiki/Temporal_bone" title="Temporal bone">
       temporal bone
      </a>
      ." There is no flesh beneath this, and the hollowness extends up to the sides of the head in front of the ears. By placing a finger on the zygomatic arch and alternately opening and closing the mouth, one can discern the presence of the temporal
      <a href="/wiki/Fossa_(anatomy)" title="Fossa (anatomy)">
       fossa
      </a>
      (with its large
      <a href="/wiki/Temporal_muscle" title="Temporal muscle">
       temporal muscle
      </a>
      and its fibres passing below the Zygomatic arches).
     </p>
     <p>
      It is also noted that the prominence of cheek bones are identified with the
      <a href="/wiki/Race_(human_classification)" title="Race (human classification)">
       humans
      </a>
      (whether
      <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">
       Mongolian
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tatars" title="Tatars">
       Tartar
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_aborigines" title="Australian aborigines">
       Australian aborigines
      </a>
      who have broad features), but the fullness of
      <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">
       tissues
      </a>
      of the cheek on which the shape of the cheekbone is dependent, or the absence of tissues and lack of fat could result in prominence of malar bones causing high cheek bones; such a prominence is not real as it is purely on account of the "wasting of the surrounding tissues than undue projection of the bone itself." Such a feature is more prominent at old age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson1906_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Thomson1906-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The presence of high cheekbones as a beauty trait is evident in many sculptures and art from the ancient world. For instance Ancient
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       Chinese
      </a>
      sculptures of goddesses typically have a "broad forehead, raised
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyebrows" title="Eyebrows">
       eyebrows
      </a>
      , high cheekbones, and large, sensuous mouth".

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terracotta_Warriors" title="Terracotta Warriors">
       Terracotta Warriors
      </a>
      are depicted with "broad
      <a href="/wiki/Forehead" title="Forehead">
       foreheads
      </a>
      , high cheekbones, large eyes, thick eyebrows, and stiff
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beards" title="Beards">
       beards
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HowardLi2006_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HowardLi2006-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

        </span>

      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
       Native American
      </a>
      have been described as having high cheekbones apart from small, very sharp eyes, wide mouth and projecting and suspended lips.
