     <p>
      <b>
       Reformism
      </b>
      is the belief that gradual changes through and within existing institutions can ultimately change a
      <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">
       society
      </a>
      's fundamental
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">
       economic system
      </a>
      and political structures. This hypothesis of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">
       social change
      </a>
      grew out of opposition to
      <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_socialism" title="Revolutionary socialism">
       revolutionary socialism
      </a>
      , which contends that some form of
      <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">
       revolution
      </a>
      is necessary for fundamental structural changes to occur.
     </p>
     <p>
      Reformism is to be distinguished from pragmatic reforms: reformism is the assumption that an accumulation of reforms can lead to the emergence of an entirely different economic system and form of society than present-day
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       capitalism
      </a>
      , whereas pragmatic reforms represent attempts to safeguard the status quo against fundamental and structural changes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reformism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are two types of reformism. The first has no intention of bringing about socialism or fundamental economic change to society and is used to oppose such structural changes. The second type is based on the assumption that while reforms are not socialist in themselves, they can help rally supporters to the cause of revolution by popularizing the cause of socialism to the working class.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The debate on the ability for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_democratic" title="Social democratic">
       social democratic
      </a>
      reformism to lead to a socialist transformation of society is over a century old.
     </p>
     <p>
      Reformism is criticized for being paradoxical: it seeks to overcome the existing economic system of capitalism, but at the same time it tries to improve the conditions of capitalism thereby making it appear more tolerable to society. According to Rosa Luxembourg, under reformism "...(capitalism) is not overthrown, but is on the contrary strengthened by the development of social reforms."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a similar vein, Stan Parker of the
      <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Socialist Party of Great Britain">
       Socialist Party of Great Britain
      </a>
      argues that reforms are a diversion of energy for socialists and are limited because they must adhere to the logic of capitalism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reformism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Reformist socialism was first put forward by
      <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Bernstein" title="Eduard Bernstein">
       Eduard Bernstein
      </a>
      , who referred to the concept as
      <i>
       evolutionary socialism
      </i>
      . Bernstein was a leading
      <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">
       social democrat
      </a>
      in Germany. Reformism was quickly targeted by revolutionary socialists, with
      <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg" title="Rosa Luxemburg">
       Rosa Luxemburg
      </a>
      condemning Bernstein's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolutionary_Socialism" title="Evolutionary Socialism">
        Evolutionary Socialism
       </a>
      </i>
      in her 1900 essay
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reform_or_Revolution%3F" title="Reform or Revolution?">
        Reform or Revolution?
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While Luxemburg died in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Revolution" title="German Revolution">
       German Revolution
      </a>
      , the reformists soon found themselves contending with the
      <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">
       Bolsheviks
      </a>
      and their satellite
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       communist
      </a>
      parties for the support of intellectuals and the
      <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">
       working class
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1959, the
      <a href="/wiki/Godesberg_Program" title="Godesberg Program">
       Godesberg Program
      </a>
      (signed at a party convention in the West German capital of
      <a href="/wiki/Bad_Godesberg" title="Bad Godesberg">
       Bad Godesberg
      </a>
      ) marked the shift of the
      <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">
       Social Democratic Party of Germany
      </a>
      (SPD) from a Marxist program espousing an end to capitalism to a reformist one focused on social reform.
     </p>
     <p>
      After
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Josef_Stalin" title="Josef Stalin">
       Josef Stalin
      </a>
      consolidated power in the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comintern" title="Comintern">
       Comintern
      </a>
      launched a campaign against the Reformist movement by denouncing them as "
      <a href="/wiki/Social_fascism" title="Social fascism">
       social fascists
      </a>
      ". According to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_God_that_Failed" title="The God that Failed">
        The God that Failed
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Koestler" title="Arthur Koestler">
       Arthur Koestler
      </a>
      , a former member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Party of Germany">
       Communist Party of Germany
      </a>
      , the largest communist party in
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">
       Western Europe
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">
       Interwar period
      </a>
      , communists, aligned with the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      , continued to consider the "social fascist" Social Democratic Party of Germany to be the real enemy in
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      , even after the
      <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">
       Nazi Party
      </a>
      had gotten into power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In modern times, reformists are seen as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre-left" title="Centre-left">
       centre-left
      </a>
      . Some social democratic parties, such as the Canadian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_(Canada)" title="New Democratic Party (Canada)">
       NDP
      </a>
      and the Social Democratic Party of Germany, are still considered to be reformist.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="please give a reliable source for this assertion. (December 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reformism_in_the_British_Labour_Party">
       Reformism in the British Labour Party
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reformism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reformism in the British Labour Party">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term was applied to elements within the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       British
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">
       Labour Party
      </a>
      in the 1950s and subsequently, on the party's right.
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Crosland" title="Anthony Crosland">
       Anthony Crosland
      </a>
      wrote
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Future_of_Socialism" title="The Future of Socialism">
        The Future of Socialism
       </a>
      </i>
      (1956) as a personal manifesto arguing for a reformulation of the term. For Crosland, the relevance of
      <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">
       nationalization
      </a>
      (or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_ownership" title="Public ownership">
       public ownership
      </a>
      ) for socialists was much reduced as a consequence of contemporary
      <a href="/wiki/Full_employment" title="Full employment">
       full employment
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">
       Keynesian
      </a>
      management of the economy and reduced
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       capitalist
      </a>
      exploitation. In 1960, after the third successive defeat of his party in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1959" title="United Kingdom general election, 1959">
       1959 General Election
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gaitskell" title="Hugh Gaitskell">
       Hugh Gaitskell
      </a>
      attempted to reformulate the original wording of
      <a href="/wiki/Clause_IV" title="Clause IV">
       Clause IV
      </a>
      in the party's constitution, but proved unsuccessful.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of the younger followers of Gaitskell, principally
      <a href="/wiki/Roy_Jenkins" title="Roy Jenkins">
       Roy Jenkins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/William_Rodgers,_Baron_Rodgers_of_Quarry_Bank" title="William Rodgers, Baron Rodgers of Quarry Bank">
       Bill Rodgers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Williams" title="Shirley Williams">
       Shirley Williams
      </a>
      left the Labour Party in 1981 to found the
      <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">
       Social Democratic Party
      </a>
      , but the central objective of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gaitskellism" title="Gaitskellism">
       Gaitskellites
      </a>
      was eventually achieved by
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">
       Tony Blair
      </a>
      in his successful attempt to rewrite Clause IV in 1995.
     </p>
     <p>
      The use of the term is distinguished from the
      <a href="/wiki/Gradualism" title="Gradualism">
       gradualism
      </a>
      associated with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabianism" title="Fabianism">
       Fabianism
      </a>
      (the ideology of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Society" title="Fabian Society">
       Fabian Society
      </a>
      ), which itself should not be seen as being in parallel with the revisionism associated with Bernstein and the Social Democratic Party of Germany, as originally the Fabians had explicitly rejected
      <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">
       Marxism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
