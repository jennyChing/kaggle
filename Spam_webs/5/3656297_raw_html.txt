      <h1 class="headline">
       Grid Down Survival
      </h1>
      <div class="post_content">
       <p>
        A variety of natural and man-made disasters can impact your local or  regional power grids. Natural events like recent Hurricane Irene (that  disrupted power to 4 million customers), along with earthquakes,  tsunamis, tornadoes, snow storms, flooding, wildfires and  lightning strikes cause major impacts.
        <span id="more-5406">
        </span>
       </p>
       <h3>
        Prepping for a Prolonged Lights Out Scenario
       </h3>
       <p>
        <em>
         By Bama Bull, a reader of SurvivalCache.com
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Human caused disruptions can include ground vehicle and aircraft accidents hitting poles and lines  and acts of sabotage, terrorism and war. A low yield nuclear explosion on the surface or as an airburst a couple miles up would generate a debilitating
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_pulse" target="_blank" title="EMP">
         electro-magnetic pulse
        </a>
        (EMP). The EMP would literally fry most electronic devices and overload electrical distribution systems, knocking out power over a large scale area for miles. But there is one threat in the center of our universe that most people are not aware of  our sun.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Too Dependent?
       </h3>
       <p>
        Americans and most of the world are overly dependent upon electricity more so than oil,
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/coronal_mass_ejection.jpg">
         <img alt="Solar Flare" class="alignright size-full wp-image-5413" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/coronal_mass_ejection.jpg" title="coronal_mass_ejection" width="210"/>
        </a>
        especially in large urban areas with mass transit systems. Most folks never give it a second thought that electrical power could stop in a blink of an eye. And if it did, how would it impact you? How long could the juice be off before it was a major disruption to your life, your family, and your world? (Read 
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/book-review-lights-out/" target="_blank" title="Lights Out">
         Lights Out
        </a>
        ) Could you go a week or more like many of the people impacted by Hurricane Irene? Electricity is needed for nearly every aspect of our daily lives  from charging the batteries on our cell phones, laptops and mp3 players; to powering our coffee pots, elevators, subway cars, wireless and communications networks; to keeping the lights and TVs on, opening doors, controlling traffic lights, powering the local ATM machines, and running our water and gas pumps.
       </p>
       <p>
        There are three key components to the typical electrical system 
        <em>
         generation, transmission
        </em>
        and
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/power_plant_grid_down1.jpg">
         <img alt="Grid Down Survival " class="alignright size-full wp-image-5415" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/power_plant_grid_down1.jpg" title="power_plant_grid_down" width="210"/>
        </a>
        <em>
         distribution
        </em>
         and a problem in any one component stops the flow of electricity.
        <em>
         Generation
        </em>
        is the production of electricity from
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydro-electric_dam" target="_blank" title="Hydroelectricity">
         hydro-electric dams
        </a>
        , wind turbines, steam generators (from nuclear, coal powered and geo-thermal systems), and gasoline and diesel powered generators.
        <em>
         Transmission
        </em>
        is the movement of electricity from the production site on high voltage lines to substations. Then
        <em>
         distribution
        </em>
        is the passage from substations on local power lines to the end users. Usually it is in the distribution component that most disruptions occur.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Solar Threats
       </h3>
       <p>
        The first recorded event of the sun causing a disruption to electrical systems on Earth was in
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/telegraph-office.jpg">
         <img alt="Grid Down Survival" class="alignright size-full wp-image-5416" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/telegraph-office.jpg" title="telegraph-office" width="210"/>
        </a>
        1859. On September 1 at 11:18 a.m., British astronomer
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Carrington" target="_blank" title="Richard Carrington">
         Richard Carrington
        </a>
        was projecting an image of the sun on a screen and witnessed two brilliant threads of white light appear over sunspots he was studying at his observatory in England. The next day before dawn, the skies over the Earth began to erupt with brilliant auroras in red, green and purple from the Arctic Circle down to lower latitudes near Hawaii, Cuba and the Bahamas. The more significant phenomena that day was the effect upon the newly established telegraph systems in America and Europe. The Earth received the equivalent of an EMP burst from the Sun that manifested as electrical overloads in telegraph lines and discharges from telegraph equipment shocking operators and setting paper and other materials on fire.
       </p>
       <p>
        Carrington connected the observance of what was a
        <a href="http://helios.gsfc.nasa.gov/cme.html" target="_blank" title="CME">
         coronal mass ejection
        </a>
        (CME) or solar flare from the previous day and delayed impact electro-magnetic energy on the telegraph systems. When a CME occurs, it is basically an eruption or ejection of a massive cloud of charged plasma from a sunspot on the surface outward into space. On average, the distance between the sun and our planet is an estimated 93 million miles, or in light years about 8.3 light-minutes. While the CME can be seen in about 8 minutes after it occurs, the ejected plasma particles travel at a slower speed of between 500,000 to one million miles per hour  hence the delay in the observed event and any resulting effect on Earth a day or two later.
       </p>
       <p>
        A large CME that is in the direction of the Earth can affect our planets magnetic field, as well as
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/EMP_Solar_Flare.jpg">
         <img alt="Grid Down Survival " class="alignright size-full wp-image-5417" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/EMP_Solar_Flare.jpg" title="EMP_Solar_Flare" width="210"/>
        </a>
        have a severe impact on any satellites and spacecraft in orbit, and possibly disrupt electrical and communication girds on the ground. This is known as a 
        <a href="http://science.nasa.gov/science-news/science-at-nasa/2008/06may_carringtonflare/" target="_blank" title="Carrington Event">
         Carrington Event
        </a>
         and more information is available online from NASA, Wikipedia and other sources. Much like earthquakes and hurricanes are measured in increasing scales of severity, CMEs have a correspondence scale of magnitude; with X class being the highest.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Recent Impacts
       </h3>
       <p>
        In Carringtons day, no one knew flares existed until that September morning when one
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/ATT_Telegraph_Company.jpg">
         <img alt="Grid Down Survival " class="alignright size-full wp-image-5418" height="210" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/ATT_Telegraph_Company.jpg" title="ATT_Telegraph_Company" width="210"/>
        </a>
        super-flare produced enough light to reveal the explosive eruptions from the sun. Recently, as in last 40 years, there are a number of recorded events that have caused limited disruptions on Earth. On Aug. 4, 1972, a solar flare knocked out long distance telephone communications in Illinois, which caused AT&amp;T to redesign its power system for transatlantic cables. Another flare caused the Hydro Qubec generating station in Canada to shutdown on
        <a href="http://www.ips.gov.au/Educational/1/3/12" target="_blank" title="Solar Storm">
         March 13, 1989
        </a>
        , disrupted electric power transmission for 9 hours to 6 million people and blacking out most of the province. Geo-magnetic surges also caused melted power transformers in New Jersey. In December 2005, X-rays from another solar storm disrupted satellite-to-ground communications and Global Positioning System (GPS) navigation signals for about 10 minutes. Scientists know that solar flares can happen on a frequent basis during periods of solar sunspots maximum, which our sun is approaching in 2012 and 2013 as part of an 11-year cycle of peak flare activity.
       </p>
       <p>
        Hopefully, this information gives you cause to consider the what if aspect of a massive CME triggering a
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/shtf-vs-teotwawki/" target="_blank" title="SHTF vs TEOTWAWKI">
         SHTF or TEOTWAWKI
        </a>
        situation to some portion of our planet. What if a sizeable portion of North America was facing sunward when a CME solar wave reached Earth? The good news is that several U.S. Government agencies in 2010 and 2011 have started taking an interest in Carrington Events. The bad news is that most of our country  from the federal government down through state and local levels  is not adequately prepared to deal with the effects of wide-ranging power outages or have the necessary replacement stocks available to repair damages to transformers, generation and switching equipment, and monitoring systems. So the preparedness burden falls back on the prepper to figure out his or her short and long-term strategies for dealing with the loss of electricity.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Planning Ahead
       </h3>
       <p>
        A good plan starts with considering what you consider as essential or critical requirements for your home or shelter to aid in your survival, safety and comfort. Here are some basic categories to consider:
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Access to money
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Operation of medical and life support equipment.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Refrigeration of food and medicines.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Household septic systems and waste treatment.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Food preparation and cooking
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Home or shelter security systems
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Hot water for hygiene, laundry, cleaning and sanitation.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Air conditioning and heating
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Interior and exterior lighting.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         * Communications
        </strong>
       </p>
       <h3>
        Replacing Grid Power
       </h3>
       <p>
        There are several methods that can be used to produce electrical power if the gird goes down. The
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/generator_power_outage.jpg">
         <img alt="Grid Down Survival" class="alignright size-full wp-image-5419" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/generator_power_outage.jpg" title="generator_power_outage" width="210"/>
        </a>
        simplest solution is to obtain a portable fuel-powered generator that runs on gasoline, diesel or propane. Portable generators come in a variety sizes and the amount of wattage produced. Youll have to do your own homework to determine what type would be best suited to your specific needs. Prices can run from a couple hundred dollars up to several thousand. Other options are solar power systems, wind turbines, and water generated; however, cost-wise, they can be far more expensive for larger electrical requirements then fuel-powered generators. If you use diesel, gas or propane powered generators, youll need to have an adequate supply of fuel on hand to fill your tank to keep the generator operational. How long or how many hours a day will you run your generator? How much fuel will you initially need on hand for a week, two-weeks or a month of operation? How will your resupply our fuel stocks? Do you have spare parts or the necessary accessories (i.e., oil, filters, spark plugs, spare cables) to conduct maintenance or service your equipment?
       </p>
       <h3>
        Light Options
       </h3>
       <p>
        Generators are great for producing electricity for recharging batteries, running refrigerators and
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/candle_light_survival.jpg">
         <img alt="Grid Down Survival" class="alignright size-full wp-image-5420" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/candle_light_survival.jpg" title="candle_light_survival" width="210"/>
        </a>
        freezers, water pumps and septic systems, and powering other appliances. However, they are not as cost efficient for providing lighting. There are a variety of cost effective low tech and high tech options to provide light at night. New high tech light emitting diode (LED) based
        <a href="https://www.forgesurvivalsupply.com/store/flashlights-and-candles/74.html?redirected=1" target="_blank" title="Flashlights">
         flashlights
        </a>
        and
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/survival-gear-review-river-rock-nightfire-lantern/" target="_blank" title="LED Lanterns">
         lanterns
        </a>
        can provide over a hundred hours of light on a set of AA or AAA batteries. Low tech options are kerosene fuel lamps and hurricane lamps, Coleman-type gas lamps, and
        <a href="https://www.forgesurvivalsupply.com/my-cart/flashlights-and-candles/bees-wax-candles-12-to-15-burn-time/74-150.html?redirected=1" target="_blank" title="Beeswax Candles">
         long burning candles
        </a>
        . Each of these methods has their pros and cons, and what works best for you will depend on your situation. However, these give you options to consider.
       </p>
       <h3>
        A Stash of Emergency Cash
       </h3>
       <p>
        Financial guru Dave Ramsey stresses the importance of having a reserve of money  typically
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/ATM_Out_of_Cash.jpg">
         <img alt="ATM Out of Money" class="alignright size-full wp-image-5421" height="140" src="http://survivalcache.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/09/ATM_Out_of_Cash.jpg" title="ATM_Out_of_Cash" width="210"/>
        </a>
        about 6 months worth of your salary  set aside in your bank account for emergencies. As a prepper, you should take this same principle to the next level and have a
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/survival-money/" target="_blank" title="Reserve of Cash">
         reserve of cash
        </a>
        on hand for any emergency (see SurvivalCache article 
        <a href="http://survivalcache.com/a-real-emergency-survival-fund/" target="_blank" title="A Real Emergency Fund">
         A Real Emergency Fund
        </a>
        ). In the event of any loss of electrical power, the hardest thing to get your hands on will be your money from your account. Credit and debit cards, even checks, are worthless if there is no electricity to run verifications and process purchases. Cash (hard currency) will generally be the first choice for payment by most merchants during a crisis and a rush for goods. Do you a reserve of cash on hand if ATM machines are down and banks are closed?
       </p>
       <p>
        It is just not possible to cover all contingency in a short article. But hopefully this gives you something to think about, consider and include in your survival preparations. While the probability of a CME impacting the Earth maybe relatively low, the possibility still exists. Being aware of this threat should help you with your planning and preparedness. Do your own research and get smart.
       </p>
