     <h1 class="article-title" itemprop="headline">
      PM's Guide to the Ultimate DIY Thanksgiving: Build a Grill
     </h1>
     <h2 class="article-sub-title" itemprop="alternativeHeadline">
      Thanksgiving doesn't have to just be about all that time spent in the kitchen baking pie after pie, basting the turkey, eating for hours and then cleaning up the mess. Here at PM, straightforward cooking is uncreative and, frankly, a bit boringit's a project that needs a DIYer's touch. Mix it up this Turkey Day and flex your skills by
      <a href="/home_journal/workshop/4337260.html">
       frying a turkey
      </a>
      , turning extra food into
      <a href="/home_journal/workshop/4337382.html">
       Christmas gifts
      </a>
      , playing with new
      <a href="/technology/upgrade/4337394.html">
       hi-tech kitchen gadgets
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/home_journal/workshop/4337433.html">
       brewing an IPA
      </a>
      in three hours or an
      <a href="/home_journal/workshop/4337341.html">
       apfelwein in 30 minutes
      </a>
      , ready to comfort yourself when the cold months are in full force. Here, contractors Ronnie Lizana and Brian Pogue walk us through their plans that turned a 275-gallon underground gas tank into a giant charcoal grill.
     </h2>

        <p>
         <span>
          <strong>
           "It took us
          </strong>
         </span>
         a good week or two to figure out how to even cut into a gas tank," says one half of the Lizana-Pogue pair, a team of contractors who ponied up $75 to buy an excavated fuel tank that eventually became the Connecticut cookout sensation known as the Grilla. The two were concerned about the incision to create a hinged lid opening causing a spark, igniting flammable fumes lingering inside. "We ended up just using a leaf blower on one end to blow it out, and didn't smell any fumes." Then Pogue set to it with a Sawzall.
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-breaker-container">
         <div class="ad-gpt-breaker ad-article-breaker-mobile">
          <div class="ad-article-breaker-text">
           Advertisement - Continue Reading Below
          </div>
          <div class="gpt-tag" id="gpt-ad-vertical-top-mobile">
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         Before we go any further, a word of caution here from PM senior home editor Roy Berendsohn: "They're lucky they weren't killed." Berendsohn tells a story of a friend named Steve who, welding an old gas tank like this one, miraculously survived an explosion that popped the ends off the tank like a firecracker. "He says, `One minute I'm standing there welding the thing, the next, the tank ends are blown out and I've got this ringing in my ears. It happened so fast, I couldn't believe it. Guys were running around everywhere, asking me if I was alright.'" As for the tank-to-Grilla conversion, Berendsohn says, "It's suicide."
        </p>
        <div class="embedded-recirc--container norewrite">
         <a class="embedded-recirc--link link">
          <div class="embedded-recirc--image-container">
           <img class="embedded-recirc--image" nopin="nopin"/>
          </div>
          <div class="embedded-recirc--inner">
           <div class="embedded-recirc--label">
            We Recommend
           </div>
           <div class="embedded-recirc--title link-txt">
           </div>
          </div>
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         Despite the risk, Lizana and Pogue lived through the initial incision and went on to "grind the crap out of it" to remove years of paint and rust. For handles, they used "stainless-steel handicapped rails we got from the dump." One-inch angle irons became ledges for three mesh grates, which can hold about eight beer-can chickens. The grates open independently to stoke the fire, a task performed with a hoe or shovel. Salvaged pipe made the smokestack, scavenged door hinges became the lid hardware, and hood springs scrapped from a mid-1980s Chevy pickup support the lid, which was "pretty heavy" before the springs lightened the load by "a couple hundred pounds."
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         <div class="gpt-tag" id="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         The team worked a 40-hour week, on top of their day jobs installing solar panels and other GC work, to complete the Grilla for its inaugural
         <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/home_journal/workshop/4324487.html">
          home-brewed beer
         </a>
         and bluegrass festival back in 2005. Since then, it's been to "at least seven parties and three or four weddings," says Pogue. "It's been to every wedding I've been to." But Lazana cautions that the Grilla is "a blessing and a curse," adding that friends now invite them to events expecting the cooker in tow. The pair plan to someday weld the Grilla to a trailer to ease its mobility. But more immediately, perhaps this Thanksgiving, the grill's rotisserie feature--an as-yet-untested skewer represented only by unfilled knockout holes on opposite sides of the tank's edge--may get its shot at a beer-can Butterball.
        </p>
