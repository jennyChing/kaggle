        <h1>
         The 10 Best Animated Sci-fi Movies of All Time
        </h1>
        <div class="meta">
         <p>
          <span class="date">
           19 February 2015
          </span>
          <span class="no-display">
           |
          </span>
          <span class="categories">
           <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/category/features/" rel="category tag">
            Features
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/category/lists/film-lists/" rel="category tag">
            Film Lists
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="no-display">
           |
          </span>
          <span class="categories">
           by Charles Barnes
          </span>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="content">
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/best-animated-sci-fi.jpg">
           <img alt="best animated sci-fi" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-25330" height="390" src="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/best-animated-sci-fi.jpg" width="560"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Science Fiction and VFX have long been the marriage required to keep both running at their full potential and capacity, but what if there were another means of crafting the visual splendour and lack of physical realisation that comes with the genres territory?
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           There is, but it happens to be also wholly expensive, and possibly doubly time-consuming and tedious: animation.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Animation is a truly splendid art, one that allows unrestrained fulfilment of all imaginative extents. It presents the ordinary into the emotionally and tonally distinct, and unlike filming in real life, animation provides no constraints as there are no confinements to limit ones creativity.. Animation has long been considered, and resultantly dismissed, as a mere gimmick to attract children to appealing products, but the existence of worthy Disney titles rendered this belief inert.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Then came a point in twentieth century cinematic art wherein one man attempted to break some mould: the release of the first ever adult-oriented animated film for theatrical release. In 1972, Ralph Bakshis release of
           <em>
            Fritz the Cat
           </em>
           embraced, suffocated, and exploited its name as an adult animated feature as much as possible (and it was glorious), making the existence possible of descending mature animated content for the cinema, Bakshi or no, far more feasible. Therefore, windows began opening for cinephiles to take animation as yet another cinematic form that was also thoroughly worthy of being made by grown-ups for grown-ups.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Exposure to Japanese anime, primarily during the 1990s, established certain Eastern individuals and collectives as giants with whom to be reckoned on the animation field. The West unconsciously countered with Pixar, abandoning the blatant violations of moral decency and family-friendliness succumbed to by anime required for purely mature viewing, instead focusing once more on a time once wherein children and adults could enjoy their animation equally.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           An outline of some changing shifts regarding the production and producers of cinematic animation is key just for some background toward the different walks of life from which these animated sci-fi features originate. Perhaps as a weak means of tension as one reads the countdown, ponder which time and place, of which pencil-and-eraser filmic zeitgeist it a by-product?
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Find out now, in this particularly personal listing of the greatest animated films belonging to the science fiction brand.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 20px;">
           10. Futurama: Benders Big Score (Dwayne Carey-Hill, 2007)
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Benders-Big-Score.jpg">
           <img alt="Bender's Big Score" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-25331" height="303" src="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Benders-Big-Score.jpg" width="560"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Perhaps the peak of animated science fiction programs, certainly from the west,
           <em>
            Futurama
           </em>
           received a highly worthy comeback, following a dreaded four-year hiatus, in 2007: a direct-to-DVD paradox-spewing adventure known as
           <em>
            Benders Big Score
           </em>
           .
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Truly, few better comebacks exist for a television program
           <em>
            Benders Big Score
           </em>
           identifies and proves its knowledge of the
           <em>
            Futurama
           </em>
           cast within minutes, explores long-hinted arcs and recurring character developments, provides a worthy narrative for a feature-length product, and is very funny to boot.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Once more, the writers of this show prove their extreme worth in tackling the often-ridiculed and amateurishly handled time travel subgenre, following season threes classic,
           <em>
            Roswell That Ends Well
           </em>
           , to tell an intertwined, intricate tale of greed, love, determination, betrayal, stupidity, and growth. We wind up in one of the best stories the franchise has ever produced.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Three sequels were released, which collectively made up
           <em>
            Futurama
           </em>
           s fifth season, but none held the perfect balance and charming courage that
           <em>
            Benders Big Score
           </em>
           was heavily prided upon.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 20px;">
           9. Ghost in the Shell (Mamoru Oshii, 1995)
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Ghost-in-the-Shell.jpg">
           <img alt="Ghost in the Shell" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-25329" height="386" src="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Ghost-in-the-Shell.jpg" width="560"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           The first of two pivotal sci-fi anime films that is mentioned in most best of sci-fi or anime movie lists,
           <em>
            Ghost in the Shell
           </em>
           is a film that some reasonably could claim is mostly a series of complex ideas, philosophies, and theories presented against the backdrop of the gorgeous animated cyber-Metropolis: a digital labyrinth Japan.Regardless, this doesnt make
           <em>
            Ghost in the Shell
           </em>
           any less a unique, distinct piece of work that any fan of either sci-fi, anime, and especially (especially) both, ought to make mandatory, ASAP viewing.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Truth be told, while it is wholly true that the Wachowskis themselves garnered much inspiration from
           <em>
            Ghost in the Shell
           </em>
           when creating
           <em>
            The Matrix
           </em>
           , the existential, A.I. musing, crime thriller remains totally and utterly its own unique miracle in regard to sci-fi presentation and thematic substance for one of its brackets feature films.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           In addition, an eclectic, moving and heavily impactful soundtrack further merits Ghost in the Shell as a fairly essential viewing.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 20px;">
           8. Big Hero 6 (Don Hall / Chris Williams, 2014)
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Big-Hero-6-movie.jpg">
           <img alt="Big Hero 6 movie" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-25324" height="383" src="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Big-Hero-6-movie.jpg" width="560"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           The most recent entry on this list concerns a strongly Japan-influenced San Francisco as the engrossing setting with which to set a contemporary Disney rendition of the superhero summer blockbuster. Well, of course it would:
           <em>
            Big Hero 6
           </em>
           is based on a Marvel Comics property after all. It is less the utilization of superpowers and gadgets to quash an antagonists schemes, which makes the
           <em>
            Big Hero 6
           </em>
           brilliant viewing for sci-fi animation fanatics, but rather how Don Hall and Chris Williams celebrate the ability of animation to nurture concerns of what a live-action film is capable of mystifying an audience.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Through this means of story presentation, the sky (or a large Disney budget and more than several rooms brimming with exquisite computer clusters) is the limit, as far as imagination stimulation goes.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Without the innovative feats in animation that have allowed 3D modelling to prosper as the dominant force in childrens entertainment production, the world would never witness the liveliness, and motorized soul, of the adored Baymax: one of the finest Disney characters in years.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Exploring the scenarios that a medical A.I. might be abnormally thrust upon, and how his inflatable exterior sees practical application and, therefore, audience intrigue, upon witnessing its potential, is effective, back-to-basics Science Fiction 101. The hypothetical, fantastical possibilities of science and technology are presented and ingrained within a narrative (a phrasing under which one could practically blanket the entire genre).
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Also, anyone looking for an attempted merging point between Western and Eastern sci-fi animation may look no further. Disneys recent, blatant attempts to incorporate elements and influences of anime physiology (e.g. the enlarged eyes of Rapunzel and/or Elsa) are no more evident than with their 2014 outing: a city drowning in Eastern culture, young people attaining powers beyond their measure or apparent responsibility, and a typical incorporation toward the kind of loyalty and honour-based moral substance that underpins both Disney and anime productions alike.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 20px;">
           7. Wall-E (Andrew Stanton, 2008)
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/wall-e-2008.jpg">
           <img alt="wall-e-2008" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-11161" height="300" src="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/wall-e-2008.jpg" width="560"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Undoubtedly the most popular and well-known title on the list,
           <em>
            WALL-E
           </em>
           is the most mainstream slow, more artistically and intellectually inclined science fiction film ever created.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Well, at least for its first half.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Before it descends into an overuse of fat jokes clogging the full power of the pictures message,
           <em>
            WALL-E
           </em>
           is a moving, powerful, memorable piece of animation. Through no dialoguejust images and noisesthe establishment of the narrative is made perfectly sound and clear, plus, it progresses very smoothly to the extent where the films unique distinction of presentation gets taken for granted, forgotten. Considering how audiences gradually become used to
           <em>
            WALL-E
           </em>
           s more unconventional means of storytelling is a clear testament to its aesthetic ability, and at Pixars grasp at appeasing both the masses and the more artistically inclined.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           <em>
            WALL-E
           </em>
           is a special one, whether it is for its design of two of the most memorable movie robots, or for being upfront and unapologetic with its critique on detrimental human behaviour, or opening children a gateway, a shortcut, to eventually finding further appreciation in less flashy, fast-paced and over-the-top sci-fi.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Andrew Stanton is responsible for this, hopefully, eternal missing link between more sophisticated childrens sci-fi cinema, and the legendary sophisticated adult sci-fi cinephile repertoire involving the likes of
           <em>
            Metropolis
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            2001
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            Solaris
           </em>
           , and
           <em>
            Blade Runner
           </em>
           .
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 20px;">
           6. Wizards (Ralph Bakshi, 1977)
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Wizards.jpg">
           <img alt="Wizards" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-25328" height="404" src="http://www.tasteofcinema.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Wizards.jpg" width="560"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           To be sure, this is arguably more of an inherent fantasy narrative than a science fiction one, but its setting on a post-apocalyptic Earth, with themes from typical sci-fi fare, such as freedom, totalitarianism, faith, determination, and murderous androids, make
           <em>
            Wizards
           </em>
           a feasible entry in this list.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Plus, lets face it: the picture simply rocks!
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Also, surely it isnt any kind of stretch to deduce
           <em>
            Wizards
           </em>
           as the predecessor (intentional or otherwise) to two other, later, post-apocalyptic ventures on this list, accounting for some minute degree of importance to what isnt necessarily one of Bakshis most widely acclaimed works.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           <em>
            Wizards
           </em>
           offers some of the very best material one could ever gleefully expect from Ralph Bakashi, New York fiend: be it zany Jewish stereotypes, sickly comic attitudes toward sex, the lack of a protagonists morality, or, to top it all off, a hasty, rough means of animation to give the minor epic a surreal, engrossing flavour to call all its own.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: Helvetica; font-size: 14px;">
           Anyone who seeks to experience a strange, memorable, electrically entertaining, charming, humourous, and sadistic spin on the fantasy good vs. evil quest requires a regular dosage of
           <em>
            Wizards
           </em>
           .
          </span>
         </p>
