           <h1>
            With focus on wartime past, Japans global PR message could misfire
           </h1>

          <p>
           A push by Japan to correct perceived bias in accounts of the countrys wartime past is creating a row that risks muddling the positive message in a mammoth public relations campaign to win friends abroad.
          </p>
          <p>
           The PR campaign, which has a budget of over half a billion dollars, comes as Prime Minister Shinzo Abe aims to adopt a less apologetic stance on Japans actions before and during World War II and ease the fetters imposed on defense policy by Japans postwar, pacifist Constitution.
          </p>
          <p>
           History is hardly the sole focus of the PR program. Many of the funds will be used for soft-power initiatives to cultivate pro-Japan foreigners, such as supporting Japan studies at universities and setting up Japan House centers to promote the Japan Brand.
          </p>
          <p>
           But the government is also targeting wartime accounts by overseas textbook publishers and others that it sees as incorrect and damaging to Japans image.
          </p>
          <p>
           One such effort has already sparked a backlash.
          </p>
          <p>
           Nineteen historians from U.S. universities have written a letter of protest against a recent request by the Japanese government to publisher McGraw Hill Education to revise its account of comfort women, the term used in Japan for those forced to work in Japanese wartime military brothels.
          </p>
          <p>
           The request was rejected.
          </p>
          <p>
           We stand with the many historians in Japan and elsewhere who have worked to bring to light the facts about this and other atrocities of World War II. We practice and produce history to learn from the past, says the letter, which will be carried in the March edition of the American Historical Associations newsletter.
          </p>
          <p>
           We therefore oppose the efforts of states or special interests to pressure publishers or historians to alter the results of their research for political purposes, it added.
          </p>
          <p>
           Abe himself has signaled support for the more aggressive PR push. Being modest does not receive recognition in the international community, and we must argue points when necessary, he recently told a Diet panel.
          </p>
          <p>
           The effort comes at a touchy time as Asia marks the 70th anniversary of World War IIs end with bitter memories not yet laid to rest, especially in China and North and South Korea.
          </p>
          <p>
           After a decade of shrinking spending on public diplomacy, Japans Foreign Ministry won a total 70 billion ($590 million) for strategic communications in an extra budget for 2014-15 and the initial budget for the next year from April  up from just 20 billion in the initial 2014-15 budget.
          </p>
          <p>
           Many politicians and officials worry Japan has been outmaneuvered by the aggressive public diplomacy of regional rivals China and South Korea.
          </p>
          <p>
           Many countries are investing hugely in this field and we feel we were not investing enough, a Foreign Ministry official said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Conservatives have welcomed the bigger budget but want priority placed on correcting perceived errors about history.
          </p>
          <p>
           When we see lots of misunderstanding or prejudice against Japans history, wed like to at least set the record straight, said Yoshiko Sakurai, a journalist and head of the Japan Institute for National Fundamentals, a conservative think tank.
          </p>
          <p>
           We have already lost (the information war). Now we have to recover, she told Reuters in an interview.
          </p>
          <p>
           Aware of the danger of a backlash, diplomats seem to have mitigated pressure to make the Japan House centers  to be set up first in London, Los Angeles and Sao Paulo in late 2016  beachheads to market an official view of history. Instead, the facilities could provide what one bureaucrat called a platform for balanced discussion on controversial topics, for example, by sponsoring seminars.
          </p>
          <p>
           Conservative politicians however want bolder steps.
          </p>
          <p>
           We are half-satisfied. By mobilizing all means, we must strengthen Japans information strategy . . . so that in a real sense, we can have (others) properly understand what is good about Japan, said ruling Liberal Democratic Party lawmaker Yoshiaki Harada, who heads a party committee on improving the nations communication strategy.
          </p>
          <p>
           Experts said government efforts to seek changes in historical accounts would be counterproductive, since it would keep the issue of Japans wartime past in public focus.
          </p>
          <p>
           Dragging people into a long discussion about history . . . seems like they are going to brand Japan with that atrocity in terms of its image, said Dartmouth College professor Jennifer Lind. Its a losing battle.
          </p>

                <p>
                 Money spent in foreign affairs to polish up Japans image is wasted money. The attempt to straighten facts about WWII atrocities in school books reminds me of the protagonist in George Orwells novel 1984 whose job it was to rectify history according to the present political direction.
                 <br/>
                 The discussion whether the institution of comfort women actually existed or not is symptomatic in this aspect. Although every detail is important the review of the whole picture must not get lost over small-minded struggles. Of the many occurrences during WWII in East Asia and especially Japans role therein, this is only one mosaic stone of a huge image that remains to be discovered, made public, and analyzed. The goal is to come to terms with the past. Openness and acceptance of the reality are the key instruments to deal with this.
                 <br/>
                 The first step is to train the perception and awareness of the history inside of Japan. Therefore, Japans Foreign Ministry is not the organization which could take care of this enormous task. I believe that there is still a lot to do within the country otherwise the momentum of this discourse would be another. An education must be established that should have its focus on an unsparing disclosure of the facts of WWII. The goal cannot be a change of the
                 <br/>
                 perception in foreign countries as long as there is still so much to do inside the country.
                 <br/>
                 Half a billion dollars should not be spent to bend the reality following the tastes of the current government. A backlash is almost certain as constructs of half-truths and omissions will surely collapse like a house of cards if someone joggles a bit. The political situation in East Asia is not simple but Japan should avoid making it easy for its antagonists to destroy such fragile buildings.
                </p>
                <p>
                 As a German I started to learn at an age of about 10 years that the generation of my grandparents committed unsurpassed crimes against humanity in an unprecedented scale. As a young adult I visited the concentration camps of Auschwitz and Birkenau. I saw mountains of eyeglasses, shoes and suitcases of the victims in the exhibition. I saw the horse barracks used to keep innocent humans imprisoned during harsh winters. I saw the railways where train loads of people were released into death and remainders of factory-like crematoriums where thousands were burnt. I saw photographs of mountains of corpses of men and women who were famished to skin and bones. Every time I recall these impressions I am overwhelmed by a deep feeling of discomposure and sense of guilt. This is the inheritance of my ancestors and I have to endure it, have to come to terms with.
                 <br/>
                 Japan should learn from Germany and not try to shelter its people from historical facts and reality. Half a billion dollars should be spent for clearing up the history and to take control over the reality inside Japan. The government should give the people a chance to process their past based on facts instead of wishes.
                </p>
