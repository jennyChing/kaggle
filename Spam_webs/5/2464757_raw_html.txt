        <h1>
         Georgia Senate throws students under bus. House gets ready to run them over again.
        </h1>
        <div class="cm-story-meta">

         <span class="cm-timestamp">
         </span>
         4:30 p.m. Monday, March 3, 2014
                
                |  Filed in:

          <p>
           In capitulating to extremists who consider Common Core the work of the devil and/or Barack Obama, the state Senate passed a bill last week that isolates Georgia from the rest of the nation, sets our students up for failure and reverses the progress schools have made over the last eight years.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.legis.ga.gov/legislation/en-US/Display/20132014/SB/167">
            The main intent of Senate Bill 167 bill
           </a>
           was to ban the Common Core State Standards in Georgia.
          </p>
          <p>
           It no longer does that, which is good considering Georgia has already invested years into putting the standards into practice, training teachers and rewriting curriculum. In a compromise with the governor who didn't want a Common Core battle in an election year, the bill was changed so it doesn't ban Common Core, but sets up a review of the standards.
          </p>
          <p>
           That compromise explains why the bill passed the Senate so easily last week and with little debate. But senators should have read the 18-page bill a little more closely as it still contains plenty of bad stuff, including a prohibition on embracing
           <em>
            any
           </em>
           new content or tests that even smack of national standards. Let's hope that wiser minds prevail in the House and put the brakes on this bill, either killing it completely or rewriting it to get rid of all the mandates.
          </p>
          <p>
           The bill still states
           <strong>
           </strong>
           <strong>
            : On and after the effective date of this Code   section, the state shall not adopt any federally prescribed content   standards or any national content standards established by a consortium   of states or a third party, including, but not limited to, the Next   Generation Science Standards, the National Curriculum for Social   Studies, the National Health Education Standards, or the National   Sexuality Standards.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Imagine telling Georgia doctors they couldn't use any cancer   treatments developed by medical teams or labs outside the state. Patients would  riot in the streets. So should parents over this piece of legislation.
          </p>
          <p>
           I have  received emails from dozens of teachers frantic that parents are unaware  of what is about to hit their kids. It should scare all educators and the general public that such a  small number of legislators who have their own agendas and ideological  beliefs can undo all the hard work that has been put into implementing  the Common Core. If this passes it will be a setback and will make our  students less competitive and ill-prepared to face the challenges they  will face in the 21st century, wrote one.
          </p>
          <p>
           The bill comes before the House for a vote Wednesday and then goes to Gov. Nathan Deal. It has the blessing of the House education leaders. (I'm surprised that
           <strong>
            <a href="http://www.house.ga.gov/representatives/en-US/member.aspx?Member=84&amp;Session=23">
             House Education Chair Brooks Coleman, R-Duluth
            </a>
           </strong>
           and
           <strong>
            <a href="http://www.house.ga.gov/representatives/en-US/member.aspx?Member=759&amp;Session=23">
             Vice ChairmanMike Dudgeon, R-Johns Creek,
            </a>
           </strong>
           support SB 167 given how much their communities value education.)
          </p>
          <p>
           Lawmakers contend they can't oppose the bill because it has the stamp of approval of the governor and House leadership, both of which apparently want to appease the rancor some Georgians have toward anything to do with the White House or the President. (Did I mention this is an election year?)
          </p>
          <p>
           Angela Palm, director of policy and legislative services for the Georgia School Boards Association, says there are many dangers lurking in the language of the bill, but doubts that will prevent its passage because of the political pressures.
          </p>
          <p>
           It is a mess. It will take far more than strike this line or insert this to fix this bill," she said. "Unless the people who will be impacted really start speaking up on how this bill limits the work of moving education forward and how this bill contradicts what we have done thus far, I dont know that we are going to be able to stop this."
          </p>
          <p>
           In adopting Common Core State Standards, Georgia had reached consensus that it was not good enough for our students to do well on state tests while failing national assessments. We needed to teach to the same high standards as schools in Massachusetts and New Jersey. Parents understood their children would not only be competing with students from higher performing states for jobs and college admission; they would be vying with college graduates from New Delhi and Beijing as well.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Senate must believe parents dont need comparisons showing Georgia kids lagging many of their counterparts. Under the guise of protecting student privacy, SB 167 puts so many restraints on data access so no one will be able to tell how students are faring. The bill appears to prevent the release of student data to the press or researchers.
          </p>
          <p>
           Another unintended consequences of SB 167 could be a ban on critical national tests. The bill mandates all testing be controlled by the state. Some education experts worry that the broad language could prohibit the administration of International Baccalaureate exams, Advanced Placement tests, the SAT and the ACT -- tests students in Duluth and Johns Creek need to get into college.
          </p>
          <p>
           In my reading of the bill, it bans assessments that are not created  within or by Georgia, says Dr. Dana Rickman, policy and research  director for the Georgia Partnership for Excellence in Education and  former director of research and policy at the Annie E. Casey Foundation.
          </p>
          <p>
           What about AP, SAT and ACT? Are we not going to use them anymore?  The bill doesnt allow for assessments or standards based on any  multi-state consortiums, says Rickman. That means we are not allowed  to use the SAT or the ACT because they are based on Common Core.
          </p>
          <p>
           The bill states: All state-wide K-12 tests and assessments shall be controlled by the State of Georgia without obligation to other entities, states, consortia, or the federal government and shall not be designed to test national standards or rebranded national standards or be based on any standards established by a consortium of states or a third party, including but not limited to the Next Generation Science Standards, the National Curriculum for Social Studies, the National Health Education Standards, or the National Sexuality Standards. The state shall not relinquish any binding control over testing as a condition of receiving a grant. On and after the effective date of this Code section, criterion assessments developed pursuant to Code Section 20-2-281 shall be aligned with the content standards revised pursuant to Code Section 20-2-141 for each subject area. No official of the State of Georgia, whether elected or appointed or representing the state in any capacity, shall join, on behalf of the state or a state agency, any consortium, association, or entity or enter into a binding agreement, when such membership or agreement would relinquish any measure of control over standards and assessments, to any individual or entity outside the state.
          </p>
          <p>
           The state has spent millions of dollars overhauling its teacher evaluation system, linking teacher reviews to student growth on statewide assessments based on statewide standards. But now, every district can develop its own standards while a brand new review committee made up largely of parent and grandparents organizes and holds hearings. The bill states:
           <strong>
            Beginning September 24, 2014, a local school system shall have the flexibility to determine its curriculum and instruction without constraint, including returning to curriculum and instruction aligned to the former Georgia Performance Standards that were in effect in June 2010, until the completion of the revision process established pursuant to this part and the establishment of new standards pursuant to such process.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           It really delays implementation of current standards until this review is done. The reviews arent done until May of 2016, says Rickman. Districts can use whatever standards they want. If we are trying to do statewide assessments based on standards, this makes it impossible.
          </p>
          <p>
           Parent and Troup County schools curriculum coach Katie Brown sent this note that I wanted to share:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            This bill, if passed and signed into law, would essentially require the states schools to stop use of its current curriculum.
           </p>
           <p>
            Immediately.
           </p>
           <p>
            Setting aside the debate around the merits of Common Core, changing the plan midstream without a clear transition plan threatens the education of an entire generation of Georgia students. As you can imagine, any curriculum encompasses all grades, and depends on/builds from the prior years material.
           </p>
           <p>
            If the state reverts to its prior curriculum (a likely option), students will be left with large gaps on critical material, and may (another likely scenario) be forced to switch yet again in a few years after the panel has considered their options. What exactly do they expect the teachers to do the day after the bill passes? How confusing (or helpful) will this be for our students? This is a serious threat to the state of Georgia and merits detailed coverage in any serious publication.
           </p>
           <p>
            To compound the severity of what is unfolding, this bill does not appear to be driven by a regard for the quality of the Common Core curriculum, but from the view that the Common Core curriculum is a threat to states rights. The state is not required to use the Common Core (although it had already chosen to do so), but it will forgo large amounts of federal funding of the Race to the Top program if it does not ($400 million of which has ALREADY been earned in Phase 2 through the excellent efforts of Georgias educators).
           </p>
           <p>
            The premise of Senate Bill 167 is for Georgias educational system to be based solely on curriculum and testing developed in Georgia. Apparently Georgia could learn nothing from the collaboration with other states.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Common Core was developed by educators and governors from 48 states, including Georgia. It was developed to improve the effectiveness of the United States educational system and subsequent impact on the efficiency and competitiveness of our work force. You may or may not agree that the Common Core addresses the gaps (please read the standards before making your judgment), but numerous studies continue to show that the students of the United States compete poorly with other nations in the world (less than average in math!), and more alarmingly are losing ground.
           </p>
           <p>
            If Georgia has the funding and wherewithal to independently develop their own curriculum that is sufficiently robust that our students can complete favorably in with world, than go for it. But in the meantime, please dont throw the baby(ies) out with the bathwater.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
