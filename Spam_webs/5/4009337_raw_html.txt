     <h2 class="post-title">
      And Onto Insulin I Go
     </h2>
     <div class="post-meta">
      <span class="post-author">
       by
       <a href="http://www.mumstheword.me/author/admin/" rel="author" title="Posts by admin">
        admin
       </a>
      </span>
      <!-- .post-author -->
      <span class="post-date">
       on			September 26, 2013,
      </span>
      <!-- .post-author -->
      <span class="post-comments">
       <a href="http://www.mumstheword.me/2013/09/26/onto-insulin-go/#comments">
        7 comments
       </a>
      </span>
      <!-- .post-author -->
     </div>
     <!-- END .post-meta -->
     <div class="post-content">
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       <a href="http://www.mumstheword.me/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/IMG_15191.jpg" rel="image_group">
        <img alt="Humulin KwikPen Human Insulin" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-7806" height="716" src="http://www.mumstheword.me/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/IMG_15191-1024x768.jpg" width="955"/>
       </a>
       Since I fell pregnant, Ive known that my diabetes would be a complication. I had gestational diabetes whilst pregnant with Sausage, and it usually corrects itself after giving birth, but with a weight issue and family history of diabetes, mine never left me and Ive been managing it with tablets ever since.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       Once I started monitoring my blood sugars in this pregnancy, it became clear that the Metformin wasnt doing enough and although with a pretty strict diet I managed to control my daytime sugar levels, every morning Id wake up and the levels will have crept right up, despite having fasted over night. Ive been testing my sugars 5 times a day and tracking them in a special diary so I knew, at a glance, that my morning levels were not at all ideal and that eventually I could end up on insulin.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       I have to admit (and yes, I know, Im a big baby) that the prospect of using insulin scared me a bit. The thought of injecting myself was really daunting as I have a bit of a needle phobia and I built the whole thing up in my head to a point where I was genuinely really panicky about it.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       Yesterday, though, I had a moment of clarity.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       I thought about Jane, who writes
       <a href="http://www.northernmum.com/" target="_blank">
        Northern Mum
       </a>
       , and her daughter who, at the age of 7 is already dealing with Type I diabetes. I thought about
       <a href="http://www.northernmum.com/" target="_blank">
        this post
       </a>
       in particular and how Molly takes it all in her stride, a child of 22 years younger than me.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       I thought about the many millions of women in the developing world who arent so lucky and dont even know they have gestational diabetes until the worst happened.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       Most of all, I thought about how lucky I am to be able to walk into a hospital, free of charge, and be told Here you go, Mrs. Crammond. Here are the drugs you need to sustain yours and your babys life over the next 5 months. Do let us know when you need some more.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       After collecting my prescription from the hospital pharmacy, I went back to see my specialist diabetic midwife, who showed me how to set up my injection pen, what dosage I should be using and how to dispose of it all safely, after using it. My hands were shaking a ridiculous amount and I had to psych myself up to take the plunge and do it.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       But, do you know what? For all of my worrying, I literally didnt even feel the needle going in, its
       <em>
        that
       </em>
       thin. Im on a low dose of what they call background insulin for the time being to see if that will help to keep my fasting sugars down over night, but Ill be liasing regularly with my midwife to monitor how the insulin is affecting me.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       So, I no longer feel worried about the prospect of injecting or any of the other fears that Id allowed to build up. I just feel lucky.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       Very, very lucky.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       <em>
        (I want to give special thanks to Molly for being my inspiration and showing me how to man the heck up and stop being a great big baby. Theres a great video
        <a href="http://www.northernmum.com/2013/08/being-seven-and-living-with-type-1-diabetes/">
         here
        </a>
        where she shows you exactly how to a diabetic injects their insulin)
       </em>
      </p>
