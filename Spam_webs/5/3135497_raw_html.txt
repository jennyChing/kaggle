     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">
       Norse mythology
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       Freyja
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'f' in 'find'">
           f
          </span>
          <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
           r
          </span>
          <span title="/e/ long 'a' in 'base'">
           e
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      for "(the)
      <a href="/wiki/Lady" title="Lady">
       Lady
      </a>
      ") is a goddess associated with love, sexuality, beauty, fertility, gold,
      <a href="/wiki/Sei%C3%B0r" title="Seir">
       seir
      </a>
      , war, and death. Freyja is the owner of the necklace
      <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%ADsingamen" title="Brsingamen">
       Brsingamen
      </a>
      , rides a chariot pulled by two cats, keeps the boar
      <a href="/wiki/Hildisv%C3%ADni" title="Hildisvni">
       Hildisvni
      </a>
      by her side, possesses a cloak of
      <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">
       falcon
      </a>
      feathers, and, by her husband
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%93%C3%B0r" title="r">
       r
      </a>
      , is the mother of two daughters,
      <a href="/wiki/Hnoss" title="Hnoss">
       Hnoss
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gersemi" title="Gersemi">
       Gersemi
      </a>
      . Along with her brother
      <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">
       Freyr
      </a>
      (Old Norse the "
      <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">
       Lord
      </a>
      "), her father
      <a href="/wiki/Nj%C3%B6r%C3%B0r" title="Njrr">
       Njrr
      </a>
      , and her mother (
      <a href="/wiki/Sister-wife_of_Nj%C3%B6r%C3%B0r" title="Sister-wife of Njrr">
       Njrr's sister
      </a>
      , unnamed in sources), she is a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Vanir" title="Vanir">
       Vanir
      </a>
      . Stemming from Old Norse
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      , modern forms of the name include
      <b>
       Freya
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Freija
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Frejya
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Freyia
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Frja
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Frya
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Frjya
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Freia
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Freja
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Frua
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       Freiya
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Freyja rules over her heavenly afterlife field
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B3lkvangr" title="Flkvangr">
       Flkvangr
      </a>
      and there receives half of those that die in battle, whereas the other half go to the god
      <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">
       Odin
      </a>
      's hall,
      <a href="/wiki/Valhalla" title="Valhalla">
       Valhalla
      </a>
      . Within Flkvangr is her hall,
      <a href="/wiki/Sessr%C3%BAmnir" title="Sessrmnir">
       Sessrmnir
      </a>
      . Freyja assists other deities by allowing them to use her feathered cloak, is invoked in matters of fertility and love, and is frequently sought after by powerful
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="Jtunn">
       jtnar
      </a>
      who wish to make her their wife. Freyja's husband, the god r, is frequently absent. She cries tears of red gold for him, and searches for him under assumed names. Freyja has
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
       numerous names
      </a>
      , including
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Gefn
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Hrn
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Mardll
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Sr
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Valfreyja
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Vanads
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Freyja is attested in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Edda" title="Poetic Edda">
        Poetic Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      , compiled in the 13th century from earlier traditional sources; in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">
        Prose Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">
        Heimskringla
       </a>
      </i>
      , the two latter written by
      <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">
       Snorri Sturluson
      </a>
      in the 13th century; in several
      <a href="/wiki/Sagas_of_Icelanders" title="Sagas of Icelanders">
       Sagas of Icelanders
      </a>
      ; in the short story
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6rla_%C3%BE%C3%A1ttr" title="Srla ttr">
        Srla ttr
       </a>
      </i>
      ; in the poetry of
      <a href="/wiki/Skald" title="Skald">
       skalds
      </a>
      ; and into the modern age in
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_folklore" title="Scandinavian folklore">
       Scandinavian folklore
      </a>
      , as well as the name for
      <a href="/wiki/Friday" title="Friday">
       Friday
      </a>
      in many Germanic languages.
     </p>
     <p>
      Scholars have theorized about whether Freyja and the goddess
      <a href="/wiki/Frigg" title="Frigg">
       Frigg
      </a>
      ultimately stem from a single goddess common among the
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
       Germanic peoples
      </a>
      ; about her connection to the
      <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">
       valkyries
      </a>
      , female battlefield choosers of the slain; and her relation to other goddesses and figures in
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_paganism" title="Germanic paganism">
       Germanic mythology
      </a>
      , including the thrice-burnt and thrice-reborn
      <a href="/wiki/Gullveig" title="Gullveig">
       Gullveig/Heir
      </a>
      , the goddesses
      <a href="/wiki/Gefjon" title="Gefjon">
       Gefjon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skai">
       Skai
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Eorger%C3%B0r_H%C3%B6lgabr%C3%BA%C3%B0r_and_Irpa" title="orgerr Hlgabrr and Irpa">
       orgerr Hlgabrr and Irpa
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mengl%C3%B6%C3%B0" title="Mengl">
       Mengl
      </a>
      , and the 1st century CE
      <a href="/wiki/%22Isis%22_of_the_Suebi" title='"Isis" of the Suebi'>
       "Isis" of the Suebi
      </a>
      . Freyja's name appears in numerous place names in Scandinavia, with a high concentration in southern
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      . Various plants in Scandinavia once bore her name, but it was replaced with the name of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">
       Virgin Mary
      </a>
      during the process of
      <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">
       Christianization
      </a>
      . Rural Scandinavians continued to acknowledge Freyja as a supernatural figure into the 19th century, and Freyja has inspired various works of art.
     </p>

     <p>
      The name
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      is transparently "lady" and ultimately derives from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">
       Proto-Germanic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Asterisk#Historical_linguistics" title="Asterisk">
       *
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fraujaz" title="Fraujaz">
        fraw(j)n
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognate
      </a>
      with, for example,
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">
       Old Saxon
      </a>
      <i>
       fra
      </i>
      "lady, mistress" and
      <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
       Old High German
      </a>
      <i>
       frouwa
      </i>
      (compare modern German
      <i>
       Frau
      </i>
      "lady").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrel2003112_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrel2003112-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Theonym" title="Theonym">
       theonym
      </a>
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      is thus considered to have been an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epitheton" title="Epitheton">
       epithet
      </a>
      in origin, replacing a personal name that is now unattested.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrundy199855.E2.80.9356_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrundy199855.E2.80.9356-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result, either the original name became entirely
      <a href="/wiki/Word_taboo" title="Word taboo">
       taboo
      </a>
      or another process occurred in which the goddess is a duplicate or hypostasis of another known goddess; see "Relation to Frigg and other goddesses and figures" below.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prose_Edda">
       <i>
        Prose Edda
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Bauer-Freja.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2146" data-file-width="4158" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/John_Bauer-Freja.jpg/220px-John_Bauer-Freja.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/John_Bauer-Freja.jpg/330px-John_Bauer-Freja.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/John_Bauer-Freja.jpg/440px-John_Bauer-Freja.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Bauer-Freja.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Freja
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/John_Bauer_(illustrator)" title="John Bauer (illustrator)">
         John Bauer
        </a>
        (18821918)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Freyja appears in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">
        Prose Edda
       </a>
      </i>
      books
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">
        Gylfaginning
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1ldskaparm%C3%A1l" title="Skldskaparml">
        Skldskaparml
       </a>
      </i>
      . In chapter 24 of
      <i>
       Gylfaginning
      </i>
      , the enthroned figure of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High,_Just-As-High,_and_Third" title="High, Just-As-High, and Third">
       High
      </a>
      says that after the god
      <a href="/wiki/Nj%C3%B6r%C3%B0r" title="Njrr">
       Njrr
      </a>
      split with the goddess
      <a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skai">
       Skai
      </a>
      , he had two beautiful and mighty children (no partner is mentioned); a son,
      <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">
       Freyr
      </a>
      , and a daughter, Freyja. Freyr is "the most glorious" of the gods, and Freyja "the most glorious" of the goddesses. Freyja has a dwelling in the heavens,
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B3lkvangr" title="Flkvangr">
       Flkvangr
      </a>
      , and that whenever Freyja "rides into battle she gets half the slain, and the other half to Odin [...]." In support, High quotes the
      <i>
       Grmnisml
      </i>
      stanza mentioned in the
      <i>
       Poetic Edda
      </i>
      section above.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199524_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199524-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      High adds that Freyja has a large, beautiful hall called
      <a href="/wiki/Sessr%C3%BAmnir" title="Sessrmnir">
       Sessrmnir
      </a>
      , and that when Freyja travels she sits in a chariot and drives two cats, and that Freyja is "the most approachable one for people to pray to, and from her name is derived the honorific title whereby noble ladies are called
      <i>
       fruvor
      </i>
      [noble ladies]." High adds that Freyja has a particular fondness for love songs, and that "it is good to pray to her concerning love affairs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199524_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199524-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In chapter 29, High recounts the names and features of various goddesses, including Freyja. Regarding Freyja, High says that, next to Frigg, Freyja is highest in rank among them and that she owns the necklace Brsingamen. Freyja is married to r, who goes on long travels, and the two have a very fair daughter by the name of
      <a href="/wiki/Hnoss" title="Hnoss">
       Hnoss
      </a>
      . While r is absent, Freyja stays behind and in her sorrow she weeps tears of red gold. High notes that
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
       Freyja has many names
      </a>
      , and explains that this is because Freyja adopted them when looking for r and traveling "among strange peoples." These names include
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
       Gefn, Hrn, Mardll, Sr, and Vanads
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199529.E2.80.9330_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199529.E2.80.9330-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Freyja plays a part in the events leading to the birth of
      <a href="/wiki/Sleipnir" title="Sleipnir">
       Sleipnir
      </a>
      , the eight-legged horse. In chapter 42, High recounts that, soon after the gods built the hall
      <a href="/wiki/Valhalla" title="Valhalla">
       Valhalla
      </a>
      , a builder (unnamed) came to them and offered to build for them in three seasons a fortification so solid that no
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunn" title="Jtunn">
       jtunn
      </a>
      would be able to come in over from
      <a href="/wiki/Midgard" title="Midgard">
       Midgard
      </a>
      . In exchange, the builder wants Freyja for his bride, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%B3l_(Sun)" title="Sl (Sun)">
       sun
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1ni" title="Mni">
       moon
      </a>
      . After some debate the gods agree, but with added conditions. In time, just as he is about to complete his work, it is revealed that the builder is, in fact, himself a jtunn, and he is killed by Thor. In the mean time, Loki, in the form of a mare, has been impregnated by the jtunn's horse,
      <a href="/wiki/Sva%C3%B0ilfari" title="Svailfari">
       Svailfari
      </a>
      , and so gives birth to Sleipnir. In support, High quotes the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lusp%C3%A1" title="Vlusp">
        Vlusp
       </a>
      </i>
      stanza that mentions Freyja.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199535.E2.80.9336_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199535.E2.80.9336-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In chapter 49, High recalls the funeral of
      <a href="/wiki/Baldr" title="Baldr">
       Baldr
      </a>
      and says that Freyja attended the funeral and there drover her cat-chariot, the final reference to the goddess in
      <i>
       Gylfaginning
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199550_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199550-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the beginning of the book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1ldskaparm%C3%A1l" title="Skldskaparml">
        Skldskaparml
       </a>
      </i>
      , Freyja is mentioned among eight goddesses attending a banquet held for
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%86gir" title="gir">
       gir
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199559_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199559-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Chapter 56 details the abduction of the goddess
      <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B0unn" title="Iunn">
       Iunn
      </a>
      by the jtunn
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Ejazi" title="jazi">
       jazi
      </a>
      in the form of an eagle. Terrified at the prospect of death and torture due to his involvement in the abduction of Iunn, Loki asks if he may use Freyja's "falcon shape" to fly north to
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6tunheimr" title="Jtunheimr">
       Jtunheimr
      </a>
      and retrieve the missing goddess. Freyja allows it, and using her "falcon shape" and a furious chase by eagle-jazi, Loki successfully returns her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199560_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199560-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Freya_and_Heimdall_by_Blommer.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="406" data-file-width="302" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Freya_and_Heimdall_by_Blommer.jpg/220px-Freya_and_Heimdall_by_Blommer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4f/Freya_and_Heimdall_by_Blommer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4f/Freya_and_Heimdall_by_Blommer.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">

     <p>
      In chapter 6, a means of referring to Njrr is provided that refers to Frejya ("father of Freyr and Freyja"). In chapter 7, a means of referring to Freyr is provided that refers to the goddess ("brother of Freyja"). In chapter 8, ways of referring to the god
      <a href="/wiki/Heimdallr" title="Heimdallr">
       Heimdallr
      </a>
      are provided, including "Loki's enemy, recoverer of Freyja's necklace", inferring a myth involving Heimdallr recovering Freyja's necklace from Loki.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199575.E2.80.9376_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199575.E2.80.9376-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In chapter 17, the jtunn
      <a href="/wiki/Hrungnir" title="Hrungnir">
       Hrungnir
      </a>
      finds himself in Asgard, the realm of the gods, and becomes very drunk. Hrungnir boasts that he will move Valhalla to Jtunheimr, bury Asgard, and kill all of the godswith the exception of the goddesses Freyja and Sif, who he says he will take home with him. Freyja is the only one of them that dares to bring him more to drink. Hrungnir says that he will drink all of their ale. After a while, the gods grow bored of Hrungnir's antics and invoke the name of Thor. Thor immediately enters the hall, hammer raised. Thor is furious and demands to know who is responsible for letting a jtunn in to Asgard, who guaranteed Hrungnir safety, and why Freyja "should be serving him drink as if at the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir" title="sir">
       sir
      </a>
      's banquet."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199568_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199568-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In chapter 18, verses from the 10th century
      <a href="/wiki/Skald" title="Skald">
       skald
      </a>
      's composition
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3rsdr%C3%A1pa" title="rsdrpa">
        rsdrpa
       </a>
      </i>
      are quoted. A
      <a href="/wiki/Kenning" title="Kenning">
       kenning
      </a>
      used in the poem refers to Freyja.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199585_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199585-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In chapter 20, poetic ways to refer to Freyja are provided; "daughter of Njrr", "sister of Freyr", "wife of r", "mother of Hnoss", "possessor of the fallen slain and of Sessrumnir and tom-cats", possessor of Brsingamen, "Van-deity",
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
       Vanads
      </a>
      , and "fair-tear deity".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199586_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199586-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In chapter 32, poetic ways to refer to gold are provided, including "Freyja's weeping" and "rain or shower [...] from Freyja's eyes".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199595_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199595-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Chapter 33 tells that once the gods journeyed to visit
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%86gir" title="gir">
       gir
      </a>
      , one of whom was Freyja.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199595_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199595-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In chapter 49, a quote from a work by the skald
      <a href="/wiki/Einarr_Sk%C3%BAlason" title="Einarr Sklason">
       Einarr Sklason
      </a>
      employs the kenning "r's bedfellow's eye-rain", which refers to Freyja and means "gold".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes1995119_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFaulkes1995119-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Chapter 36 explains again that gold can be referring to as Freyja's weeping due to her red gold tears. In support, works by the skalds
      <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%BAli_%C3%9E%C3%B3rsteinsson" title="Skli rsteinsson">
       Skli rsteinsson
      </a>
      and Einarr Sklason are cited that use "Freyja's tears" or "Freyja's weepings" to represent "gold". The chapter features additional quotes from poetry by Einarr Sklason that references the goddess and her child Hnoss.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFaulkes199598_28-0">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heimskringla">
       <i>
        Heimskringla
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heimskringla">

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Freya_(1901)_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="988" data-file-width="700" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Freya_%281901%29_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg/220px-Freya_%281901%29_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Freya_%281901%29_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg/330px-Freya_%281901%29_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Freya_%281901%29_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg/440px-Freya_%281901%29_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Freya_(1901)_by_Anders_Zorn.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Freja
        </i>
        (1901) by
        <a href="/wiki/Anders_Zorn" title="Anders Zorn">
         Anders Zorn
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">
        Heimskringla
       </a>
      </i>
      book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ynglinga_saga" title="Ynglinga saga">
        Ynglinga saga
       </a>
      </i>
      provides an
      <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">
       euhemerized
      </a>
      account of the origin of the gods, including Freyja. In chapter 4, Freyja is introduced as a member of the Vanir, the sister of Freyr, and the daughter of Njrr and his sister (whose name is not provided). After the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir%E2%80%93Vanir_War" title="sirVanir War">
       sirVanir War
      </a>
      ends in a stalemate, Odin appoints Freyr and Njrr as priests over sacrifices. Freyja becomes the priestess of sacrificial offerings and it was she who introduced the practice of
      <a href="/wiki/Sei%C3%B0r" title="Seir">
       seir
      </a>
      to the sir, previously only practiced by the Vanir.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHollander20078_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHollander20078-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In chapter 10, Freyja's brother Freyr dies, and Freyja is the last survivor among the sir and Vanir. Freyja keeps up the sacrifices and becomes famous. The saga explains that, due to Freyja's fame, all women of rank become known by her name
      <i>
       frvor
      </i>
      ("ladies"), a woman who is the mistress of her property is referred to as
      <i>
       freyja
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       hsfreyja
      </i>
      ("lady of the house") for a woman who owns an estate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHollander200714_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHollander200714-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The chapter adds that not only was Freyja very clever, but that she and her husband
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%93%C3%B0r" title="r">
       r
      </a>
      had two immensely beautiful daughters,
      <a href="/wiki/Gersemi" title="Gersemi">
       Gersemi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hnoss" title="Hnoss">
       Hnoss
      </a>
      , "who gave their names to our most precious possessions."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHollander200714_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHollander200714-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     </p>

      <dl>
       <dd>
        Thorgerd replied in a loud voice, "I have had no evening meal, nor will I do so until I join Freyja. I know no better course of action than my father's. I do not want to live after my father and brother are dead."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScudder2001151_32-0">

      <a href="/wiki/Legendary_saga" title="Legendary saga">
       legendary saga
      </a>
      <i>
       Hlfs saga ok Hlfsrekka
      </i>
      , King Alrek has two wives, Geirhild and Signy, and cannot keep them both. He tells the two women that he would keep whichever of them that brews the better ale for him by the time he has returned home in the summer. The two compete and during the brewing process Signy prays to Freyja and Geirhild to Htt ("hood"), a man she had met earlier (earlier in the saga revealed to be Odin in disguise). Htt answers her prayer and spits on her yeast. Signy's brew wins the contest.

     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6rla_%C3%BE%C3%A1ttr" title="Srla ttr">
        Srla ttr
       </a>
      </i>
      , a short, late 14th century narrative from a later and extended version of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarbk">
        lfs saga Tryggvasonar
       </a>
      </i>
      found in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Flateyjarb%C3%B3k" title="Flateyjarbk">
        Flateyjarbk
       </a>
      </i>
      manuscript, an euhmerized account of the gods is provided. In the account, Freyja is described as having been a concubine of Odin, who bartered sex to four dwarfs for a golden necklace. In the work, the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir" title="sir">
       sir
      </a>
      once lived in a city called
      <a href="/wiki/Asgard" title="Asgard">
       Asgard
      </a>
      , located in a region called "Asialand or Asiahome". Odin was the king of the realm, and made
      <a href="/wiki/Nj%C3%B6r%C3%B0r" title="Njrr">
       Njrr
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Freyr" title="Freyr">
       Freyr
      </a>
      temple priests. Freyja was the daughter of Njrr, and was Odin's concubine. Odin deeply loved Freyja, and she was "the fairest of woman of that day." Freyja had a beautiful
      <a href="/wiki/Boudoir" title="Boudoir">
       bower
      </a>
      , and when the door was shut no one could enter without Freyja's permission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911127_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911127-34">

        </span>

         ]

     <p>
      Chapter 1 records that one day Freyja passed by an open stone where
      <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Germanic_mythology)" title="Dwarf (Germanic mythology)">
       dwarfs
      </a>
      lived. Four dwarfs were smithying a golden necklace, and it was nearly done. Looking at the necklace, the dwarfs thought Freyja to be most fair, and she the necklace. Freyja offered to buy the collar from them with silver and gold and other items of value. The dwarfs said that they had no lack of money, and that for the necklace the only thing she could offer them would be a night with each of them. "Whether she liked it better or worse", Freyja agreed to the conditions, and so spent a night with each of the four dwarfs. The conditions were fulfilled and the necklace was hers. Freyja went home to her bower as if nothing happened.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911128_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911128-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As related in chapter 2, Loki, under the service of Odin, found out about Freyja's actions and told Odin. Odin told Loki to get the necklace and bring it to him. Loki said that since no one could enter Freyja's bower against her will, this wouldn't be an easy task, yet Odin told him not to come back until he had found a way to get the necklace. Howling, Loki turned away and went to Freyja's bower but found it locked, and that he couldn't enter. So Loki transformed himself into a fly, and after having trouble finding even the tiniest of entrances, he managed to find a tiny hole at the gable-top, yet even here he had to squeeze through to enter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911128_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911128-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Having made his way into Freyja's chambers, Loki looked around to be sure that no one was awake, and found that Freyja was asleep. He landed on her bed and noticed that she was wearing the necklace, the clasp turned downward. Loki turned into a
      <a href="/wiki/Flea" title="Flea">
       flea
      </a>
      and jumped onto Freyja's cheek and there bit her. Freyja stirred, turning about, and then fell asleep again. Loki removed his flea's shape and undid her collar, opened the bower, and returned to Odin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911128.E2.80.93129_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911128.E2.80.93129-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The next morning Freyja woke and saw that the doors to her bower were open, yet unbroken, and that her precious necklace was gone. Freyja had an idea of who was responsible. She got dressed and went to Odin. She told Odin of the malice he had allowed against her and of the theft of her necklace, and that he should give her back her jewelry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911129_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911129-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Odin said that, given how she obtained it, she would never get it back. That is, with one exception: she could have it back if she could make two kings, themselves ruling twenty kings each, battle one another, and cast a spell so that each time one of their numbers falls in battle, they will again spring up and fight again. And that this must go on eternally, unless a
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christian
      </a>
      man of a particular stature goes into the battle and smites them, only then will they stay dead. Freyja agreed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrisMorris1911129_37-1">

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rye_field.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1497" data-file-width="1106" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Rye_field.jpg/220px-Rye_field.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Rye_field.jpg/330px-Rye_field.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Rye_field.jpg/440px-Rye_field.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

     <p>
      Although the
      <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Scandinavia" title="Christianization of Scandinavia">
       Christianization of Scandinavia
      </a>
      beheld a new institution in Scandinavia, the church, that sought to
      <a href="/wiki/Demonization" title="Demonization">
       demonize
      </a>
      the native gods, belief and reverence in the gods, including Freyja, remained into the modern period and melded into
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_folklore" title="Scandinavian folklore">
       Scandinavian folklore
      </a>
      .
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Britt-Mari_N%C3%A4sstr%C3%B6m&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Britt-Mari Nsstrm (page does not exist)">
       Britt-Mari Nsstrm
      </a>
      <span class="noprint" style="font-size: smaller; font-style: normal;">
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="//sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Britt-Mari_N%C3%A4sstr%C3%B6m" title="sv:Britt-Mari Nsstrm">
        sv
       </a>
       )
      </span>
      comments that Freyja became a particular target under Christianization:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Freyja's erotic qualities became an easy target for the new religion, in which an asexual virgin was the ideal woman [...] Freyja is called "a whore" and "a harlot" by the holy men and missionaries, whereas many of her functions in the everyday lives of men and women, such as protecting the vegetation and supplying assistance in childbirth were transferred to the Virgin Mary.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521_38-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521-38">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          38
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      However, Freyja did not disappear. In Iceland, Freyja was called upon for assistance by way of
      <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_magical_staves" title="Icelandic magical staves">
       Icelandic magical staves
      </a>
      as late as the 18th century, and as late as the 19th century, Freyja is recorded as retaining elements of her role as a fertility goddess among rural Swedes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREYJA-ICELAND-SWEDEN_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FREYJA-ICELAND-SWEDEN-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Old Norse poem
      <i>
       rymskvia
      </i>
      (or its source) continued into Scandinavian folk song tradition, where it was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euhemerized" title="Euhemerized">
       euhemerized
      </a>
      and otherwise transformed over time. In Iceland, the poem became known as
      <i>
       rylur
      </i>
      , whereas in Denmark the poem became
      <i>
       Thor af Havsgaard
      </i>
      and in Sweden it became
      <i>
       Torvisan
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Hammarhmtningen
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A section of the Swedish
      <i>
       Torvisan
      </i>
      , in which
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      has been transformed into "the fair" (
      <i>
       den vna
      </i>
      )
      <i>

      </i>

     </p>

         <dl>

           </small>
          </dd>

          </dd>
          <dd>

           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521_38-2">

          </dd>

           <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">

           demand]
          </dd>
          <dd>

          </dd>

           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521_38-3">

     <p>
      In the province of
      <a href="/wiki/Sm%C3%A5land" title="Smland">
       Smland
      </a>
      , Sweden, an account is recorded connecting Freyja with sheet lightning in this respect. Writer Johan Alfred Gth recalled a Sunday in 1880 where men were walking in fields and looking at nearly ripened
      <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">
       rye
      </a>
      , where Mns in Karryd said: "Now Freyja is out watching if the rye is ripe". Along with this, Gth recalls another mention of Freyja in the countryside:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        When as a boy I was visiting the old Proud-Katrina, I was afraid of lightning like all boys in those days. When the
        <a href="/wiki/Lightning#Observational_variations" title="Lightning">
         sheet lightning
        </a>
        flared at the night, Katrina said: "Don't be afraid little child, it is only Freyja who is out making fire with steel and flintstone to see if the rye is ripe. She is kind to people and she is only doing it to be of service, she is not like Thor, he slays both people and livestock, when he is in the mood" [...] I later heard several old folks talk of the same thing in the same way.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESch.C3.B6n2004227.E2.80.93228_40-0">

       plough
      </a>
      outdoors, because if Freyja sat on it, it would no longer be of any use.

      </a>
      .

      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
       constellation
      </a>
      was called either Frigg's distaff or Freyja's
      <a href="/wiki/Distaff" title="Distaff">
       distaff
      </a>
      (Swedish
      <i>
       Frejerock
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESch.C3.B6n2004228_41-1">

         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      reflect devotion to the goddess, including the Norwegian place name
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fr%C3%B8ihov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frihov (page does not exist)">
       Frihov
      </a>

      </i>
      , literally "Freyja's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heathen_hofs" title="Heathen hofs">
       hof
      </a>
      ") and Swedish place names such as
      <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%B6vi" title="Frvi">
       Frvi
      </a>
      (from *
      <i>
       Freyjuv
      </i>
      , literally "Freyja's
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9_(shrine)" title="V (shrine)">
       v
      </a>
      ").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREYJA-PLACE-NAMES_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FREYJA-PLACE-NAMES-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a survey of toponyms in Norway, M. Olsen tallies at least 20 to 30 location names compounded with
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      . Three of these place names appear to derive from *
      <i>
       Freyjuhof
      </i>
      ('Freyja's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heathen_hofs" title="Heathen hofs">
       hof
      </a>
      '), whereas the goddess's name is frequently otherwise compounded with words for 'meadow' (such as
      <i>
       -veit
      </i>
      , -
      <i>
       land
      </i>
      ) and similar land formations. These toponyms are attested most commonly on the west coast though a high frequency is found in the southeast.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETurville-Petre1964178_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETurville-Petre1964178-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Place names containing
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      are yet more numerous and varied in Sweden, where they are widely distributed. A particular concentration is recorded in Uppland, among which a number derive from the above-mentioned
      <i>
       *Freyjuv
      </i>
      and also *
      <i>
       Freyjulundr
      </i>
      ('Freyja's
      <a href="/wiki/Sacred_grove" title="Sacred grove">
       sacred grove
      </a>
      '), place names that indicate public worship of Freyja. In addition, a variety of place names (such as
      <i>
       Fral
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Frale
      </i>
      ) have been seen as containing an element cognate to Gothic
      <i>
       alhs
      </i>
      and Old English
      <i>
       ealh
      </i>
      ("temple"), although these place names may be otherwise interpreted. In addition,
      <i>
       Frejya
      </i>
      appears as a compound element with a variety of words for geographic features such as fields, meadows, lakes, and natural objects such as rocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETurville-Petre1964178.E2.80.93179_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETurville-Petre1964178.E2.80.93179-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Freyja name
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">
        Hrn
       </a>
      </i>
      appears in the Swedish place names
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A4rnevi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hrnevi (page does not exist)">
       Hrnevi
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A4rnevi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jrnevi (page does not exist)">
       Jrnevi
      </a>
      , stemming from the reconstructed Old Norse place name
      <i>
       *Hrnar-v
      </i>
      (meaning "Hrn's
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9_(shrine)" title="V (shrine)">
       v
      </a>
      ").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAARNEVI-OCH-JAARNEVI_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HAARNEVI-OCH-JAARNEVI-45">
        <span>
         [

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_record_and_historic_depictions">
       Archaeological record and historic depictions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Archaeological record and historic depictions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A 7th-century
      <a href="/wiki/Phalera_(military_decoration)" title="Phalera (military decoration)">
       phalara
      </a>
      found in a "warrior grave" in what is now
      <a href="/wiki/Eschwege" title="Eschwege">
       Eschwege
      </a>
      in northwestern Germany features a female figure with two large braids flanked by two "cat-like" beings and holding a staff-like object. This figure has been interpreted as Freyja.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGaimster199854.E2.80.9355_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGaimster199854.E2.80.9355-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This image may be connected to various B-type
      <a href="/wiki/Bracteate" title="Bracteate">
       bracteates
      </a>
      , referred to as the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=F%C3%BCrstenberg-type&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frstenberg-type (page does not exist)">
       Frstenberg-type
      </a>
      , that may also depict the goddess; they "show a female figure, in a short skirt and double-looped hair, holding a stave or sceptre in her right hand and a double-cross feature in the left".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGaimster199854.E2.80.9355_46-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGaimster199854.E2.80.9355-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 12th century depiction of a cloaked but otherwise nude woman riding a large cat appears on a wall in the
      <a href="/wiki/Schleswig_Cathedral" title="Schleswig Cathedral">
       Schleswig Cathedral
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">
       Schleswig-Holstein
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">
       Northern Germany
      </a>
      . Beside her is similarly a cloaked yet otherwise nude woman riding a
      <a href="/wiki/Distaff" title="Distaff">
       distaff
      </a>
      . Due to iconographic similarities to the literary record, these figures have been theorized as depictions of Freyja and Frigg respectively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesPennick1995144.E2.80.93145_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesPennick1995144.E2.80.93145-47">

     <h2>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Theories">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_Frigg_and_other_goddesses_and_figures">
       Relation to Frigg and other goddesses and figures
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Relation to Frigg and other goddesses and figures">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Due to numerous similarities, scholars have frequently connected Freyja with the goddess
      <a href="/wiki/Frigg" title="Frigg">
       Frigg
      </a>
      . The connection with Frigg and question of possible earlier identification of Freyja with Frigg in the
      <a href="/wiki/Common_Germanic_deities" title="Common Germanic deities">
       Proto-Germanic
      </a>
      period (
      <a href="/wiki/Frigg_and_Freyja_origin_hypothesis" title="Frigg and Freyja origin hypothesis">
       Frigg and Freyja origin hypothesis
      </a>
      ) remains a matter of scholarly discourse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrundy199856.E2.80.9366_48-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrundy199856.E2.80.9366-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Regarding a Freyja-Frigg common origin hypothesis, scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Grundy" title="Stephan Grundy">
       Stephan Grundy
      </a>
      comments that "the problem of whether Frigg or Freyja may have been a single goddess originally is a difficult one, made more so by the scantiness of pre-
      <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">
       Viking Age
      </a>
      references to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Germanic_deities_and_heroes" title="List of Germanic deities and heroes">
       Germanic goddesses
      </a>
      , and the diverse quality of the sources. The best that can be done is to survey the arguments for and against their identity, and to see how well each can be supported."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrundy199857_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrundy199857-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Like the name of the group of gods to which Freyja belongs, the
      <a href="/wiki/Vanir" title="Vanir">
       Vanir
      </a>
      , the name
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      is not attested outside of
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
       Scandinavia
      </a>
      , as opposed to the name of the goddess
      <i>
       Frigg
      </i>
      , who is attested as a goddess common among the Germanic peoples, and whose name is reconstructed as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">
       Proto-Germanic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Asterisk#Historical_linguistics" title="Asterisk">
       *
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Frijj%C5%8D" title="Frijj">
        Frijj
       </a>
      </i>
      . Similar proof for the existence of a common Germanic goddess from which
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      descends does not exist, but scholars have commented that this may simply be due to lack of evidence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrundy199856.E2.80.9366_48-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrundy199856.E2.80.9366-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <i>
       Poetic Edda
      </i>
      poem
      <i>
       Vlusp
      </i>
      , a figure by the name of
      <a href="/wiki/Gullveig" title="Gullveig">
       Gullveig
      </a>
      is burnt three times yet is three times reborn. After her third rebirth, she is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0r" title="Heir">
       Heir
      </a>
      . This event is generally accepted as precipitating the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir%E2%80%93Vanir_War" title="sirVanir War">
       sirVanir War
      </a>
      . Starting with scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Turville-Petre" title="Gabriel Turville-Petre">
       Gabriel Turville-Petre
      </a>
      , scholars such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Simek" title="Rudolf Simek">
       Rudolf Simek
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Andy_Orchard" title="Andy Orchard">
       Andy Orchard
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lindow" title="John Lindow">
       John Lindow
      </a>
      have theorized that Gullveig/Heir is the same figure as Freyja, and that her involvement with the sir somehow led to the events of the sirVanir War.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREYJA-GULLVEIG_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FREYJA-GULLVEIG-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Outside of theories connecting Freyja with the goddess Frigg, some scholars, such
      <a href="/wiki/Hilda_Ellis_Davidson" title="Hilda Ellis Davidson">
       Hilda Ellis Davidson
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Britt-Mari_N%C3%A4sstr%C3%B6m&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Britt-Mari Nsstrm (page does not exist)">
       Britt-Mari Nsstrm
      </a>
      <span class="noprint" style="font-size: smaller; font-style: normal;">
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="//sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Britt-Mari_N%C3%A4sstr%C3%B6m" title="sv:Britt-Mari Nsstrm">
        sv
       </a>
       )
      </span>
      , have theorized that other goddesses in Norse mythology, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gefjon" title="Gefjon">
       Gefjon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ger%C3%B0r" title="Gerr">
       Gerr
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ska%C3%B0i" title="Skai">
       Skai
      </a>
      , may be forms of Freyja in different roles and/or ages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson199885.E2.80.9386_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson199885.E2.80.9386-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Receiver_of_the_slain">
       Receiver of the slain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Receiver of the slain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Freyja and her afterlife field
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B3lkvangr" title="Flkvangr">
       Flkvangr
      </a>
      , where she receives half of the slain, has been theorized as connected to the valkyries. Scholar Britt-Mari Nsstrm points out the description in
      <i>
       Gylfaginning
      </i>
      where it is said of Freyja that "whenever she rides into battle she takes half of the slain," and interprets
      <i>
       Flkvangr
      </i>
      as "the field of the Warriors". Nsstrm notes that, just like Odin, Freyja receives slain heroes who have died on the battlefield, and that her house is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessrumnir" title="Sessrumnir">
       Sessrumnir
      </a>
      (which she translates as "filled with many seats"), a dwelling that Nsstrm posits likely fills the same function as Valhalla. Nsstrm comments that "still, we must ask why there are two heroic paradises in the Old Norse view of afterlife. It might possibly be a consequence of different forms of initiation of warriors, where one part seemed to have belonged to inn and the other to Freyja. These examples indicate that Freyja was a war-goddess, and she even appears as a valkyrie, literally 'the one who chooses the slain'."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199961_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199961-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Siegfried Andres Dobat comments that "in her mythological role as the chooser of half the fallen warriors for her death realm Flkvangr, the goddess Freyja, however, emerges as the mythological role model for the Valkyrjar  [
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
        sic
       </a>
      </i>
      ] and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%ADsir" title="Dsir">
       dsir
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDobat2006186_53-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDobat2006186-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22Oriental.22_hypothesis">
       The "Oriental" hypothesis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title='Edit section: The "Oriental" hypothesis'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Gustav Neckel, writing in 1920, connects Freyja to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phyrgia" title="Phyrgia">
       Phrygian
      </a>
      goddess
      <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">
       Cybele
      </a>
      . According to Neckel, both goddesses can be interpreted as "fertility goddesses" and other potential resemblances have been noted. Some scholars have suggested that the image of Cybele subsequently influenced the iconography of Freyja, the lions drawing the former's chariot becoming large cats. These observation became an extremely common observation in works regarding Old Norse religion until at least the early 1990s. In her book-length study of scholarship on the topic of Freyja, Britt-Mari Nsstrm (1995) is highly critical of this deduction; Nsstrm says that "these 'parallels' are due to sheer ignorance about the characteristics of Cybele; scholars have not troubled to look into the resemblances and differences between the two goddesses, if any, in support for their arguments for a common origin."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199523.E2.80.9324_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199523.E2.80.9324-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_influence">
       Modern influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freyja&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern influence">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ring5.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="791" data-file-width="600" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ring5.jpg/130px-Ring5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ring5.jpg/195px-Ring5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ring5.jpg/260px-Ring5.jpg 2x" width="130"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ring5.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Freiaa combination of Freyja and the goddess
        <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B0unn" title="Iunn">
         Iunn
        </a>
        from
        <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">
         Richard Wagner
        </a>
        's opera
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">

         </a>
        </i>

        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rackham" title="Arthur Rackham">

     <p>
      Into the modern period, Freyja was treated as a Scandinavian counterpart to the Roman Venus in, for example, Swedish literature, where the goddess may be associated with romantic love or, conversely, simply as a synonym for "lust and potency".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521.E2.80.9322_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521.E2.80.9322-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 18th century, Swedish poet
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Michael_Bellman" title="Carl Michael Bellman">
       Carl Michael Bellman
      </a>
      referred to
      <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">
       Stockholm
      </a>
      prostitutes as "the children of Frja".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521_38-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 19th century, Britt-Mari Nsstrm observes, Swedish Romanticism focused less on Freyja's erotic qualities and more on the image of "the pining goddess, weeping for her husband".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521_38-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199521-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Freyja is mentioned in the first stanza ("it is called old Denmark and it is Freja's hall") of the civil
      <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">
       national anthem
      </a>
      of Denmark,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Der_er_et_yndigt_land" title="Der er et yndigt land">
        Der er et yndigt land
       </a>
      </i>
      , written by 19th century Danish poet
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adam_Gottlob_Oehlenschl%C3%A4ger" title="Adam Gottlob Oehlenschlger">
       Adam Gottlob Oehlenschlger
      </a>
      in 1819.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndersen1899157_56-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndersen1899157-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, Oehlenschlger wrote a comedy entitled
      <i>
       Freyjas alter
      </i>
      (1818) and a poem
      <i>
       Freais sal
      </i>
      featuring the goddess.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek200791_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek200791-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 19th century German composer
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">
       Richard Wagner
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">
        Der Ring des Nibelungen
       </a>
      </i>
      opera cycle features
      <i>
       Freia
      </i>
      , the goddess Freyja combined with the apple-bearing goddess
      <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B0unn" title="Iunn">
       Iunn
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek200790_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek200790-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In late 19th century and early 20th century Northern Europe, Freyja was the subject of numerous works of art, including
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Ernst_Freund" title="Hermann Ernst Freund">
       H. E. Freund
      </a>
      (statue, 18211822),
      <i>
       Freja skande sin make
      </i>
      (painting, 1852) by
      <a href="/wiki/Nils_Blomm%C3%A9r" title="Nils Blommr">
       Nils Blommr
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Freyjas Aufnahme uner den Gttern
      </i>
      (charcoal drawing, 1881), and
      <i>
       Frigg; Freyja
      </i>
      (drawing, 1883) by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_Ehrenberg_(illustrator)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carl Ehrenberg (illustrator) (page does not exist)">
       Carl Ehrenberg
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      (1901) by
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Emil_Doepler" title="Carl Emil Doepler">
       Carl Emil Doepler d. J.
      </a>
      , and
      <i>
       Freyja and the Brisingamen
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/J._Doyle_Penrose" title="J. Doyle Penrose">
       J. Doyle Penrose
      </a>
      (painting, 18621932).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimek200791_57-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimek200791-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Like other Norse goddesses, her name was applied widely in Scandinavia to, for example, "sweetmeats or to stout carthorses".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199522_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199522-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Vanads
      </i>
      , one of Freyja's names, is the source of the name of the
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       chemical element
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">
       vanadium
      </a>
      , so named because of its many colored compounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIBERG-WIBERG-HOLLEMAN1345_60-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WIBERG-WIBERG-HOLLEMAN1345-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Starting in the early 1990s, derivatives of
      <i>
       Freyja
      </i>
      began to appear as a given name for girls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199522_59-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEN.C3.A4sstr.C3.B6m199522-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to the Norwegian name database from the Central Statistics Bureau, around 500 women have Frya (the modern, Norwegian spelling of the goddess's name) as a first name in the country. There are several similar names that likely have the same origin, such as the more widespread Frydis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Statistics_Norway_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Statistics_Norway-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Freyja is one of the incarnated goddesses in the New Zealand comedy/drama "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Almighty_Johnsons" title="The Almighty Johnsons">
       The Almighty Johnsons
      </a>
      ". The part of "Agnetha/Freyja" is played by
      <a href="/wiki/Alison_Bruce" title="Alison Bruce">
       Alison Bruce
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Almighty_Johnsons_62-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Almighty_Johnsons-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
