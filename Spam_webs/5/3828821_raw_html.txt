     <p>
      An
      <b>
       earth oven
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       ground oven
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       cooking pit
      </b>
      is one of the simplest and most ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">
       cooking
      </a>
      structures. At its most basic, an earth oven is a pit in the ground used to trap heat and bake, smoke, or steam food. Earth ovens have been used in many places and cultures in the past, and the presence of such cooking pits is a key sign of human settlement often sought by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologists" title="Archaeologists">
       archaeologists
      </a>
      . Earth ovens remain a common tool for cooking large quantities of food where no equipment is available. They have been used in various civilizations around the world and are still commonly found in the Pacific region to date.
     </p>
     <p>
      To bake food, the fire is built, then allowed to burn down to a
      <a href="/wiki/Smouldering" title="Smouldering">
       smoulder
      </a>
      . The food is then placed in the oven and covered. This covered area can be used to bake bread or other various items. Steaming food in an earth oven covers a similar process. Fire-heated rocks are put into a pit and are covered with green vegetation to add moisture, large quantities of food, more green vegetation and sometimes water, if more moisture is needed. Finally, a covering of earth is added over everything. The food in the pit can take up to several hours to a full day to cook, regardless of the dry or wet method used.
     </p>
     <p>
      Today, many communities still use cooking pits for ceremonial or celebratory occasions, including the indigenous
      <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">
       Fijian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lovo" title="Lovo">
       lovo
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
       Hawaiian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Luau" title="Luau">
       luau
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Mori people">
       Mori
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/H%C4%81ngi" title="Hngi">
       hngi
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/New_England_clam_bake" title="New England clam bake">
       New England clam bake
      </a>
      . The central Asian
      <a href="/wiki/Tandoor" title="Tandoor">
       tandoor
      </a>
      use the method primarily for uncovered, live-fire baking, which is a transitional design between the earth oven and the horizontal-plan
      <a href="/wiki/Masonry_oven" title="Masonry oven">
       masonry oven
      </a>
      . This method is essentially a permanent earth oven made out of clay or firebrick with a constantly burning, very hot fire in the bottom. In modern times, earth ovens are sometimes used for
      <a href="/wiki/Outdoor_cooking" title="Outdoor cooking">
       outdoor cooking
      </a>
      and recreational meals in lieu of an open
      <a href="/wiki/Campfire" title="Campfire">
       campfire
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">
       Americas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Americas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In many areas
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologists" title="Archaeologists">
       archaeologists
      </a>
      recognize "pit-hearths" as being in commonly used in the past. In Central
      <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
       Texas
      </a>
      there are large "burned-rock middens" speculated to be used for large-scale cooking of plants of various sorts, especially the bulbs of
      <a href="/wiki/Sotol" title="Sotol">
       sotol
      </a>
      . The Mayan
      <i>
       pib
      </i>
      and Andean
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Huatia" title="Huatia">
        watia
       </a>
      </i>
      are other examples.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/New_England_clam_bake" title="New England clam bake">
       clambake
      </a>
      , invented by
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       Native Americans
      </a>
      on the Atlantic seaboard and considered a traditional element of
      <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
       New England
      </a>
      cuisine, traditionally uses a type of ad hoc earth oven (usually built on the beach). A large enough hole is dug into the sand and heated rocks are added to the bottom of the hole, a layer of seaweed is laid on top to create moisture and steam, the food is placed on top of the seaweed and lastly another layer of seaweed is added to trap in the steam and cook the food which mainly consists of
      <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">
       shellfish
      </a>
      and vegetables.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Curanto" title="Curanto">
       Curanto
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Archipelago" title="Chilo Archipelago">
       Chilo Archipelago
      </a>
      consists of
      <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">
       shellfish
      </a>
      , meat, potatoes,
      <a href="/wiki/Milcao" title="Milcao">
       milcao
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Chapalele" title="Chapalele">
       chapaleles
      </a>
      and vegetables traditionally prepared in an earth oven. It has spread to the southern areas of
      <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
       Chile
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East_and_North_Africa">
       Middle East and North Africa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle East and North Africa">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Earth oven cooking is sometimes used for celebratory cooking in North Africa, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
       Morocco
      </a>
      : a whole lamb is cooked in an earth oven (called a
      <i>
       tandir
      </i>
      , etymologically related to the Central- and South Asian
      <a href="/wiki/Tandoor" title="Tandoor">
       tandoor
      </a>
      and possibly descended from an
      <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">
       Akkadian
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       tinuru
      </i>
      ) in a manner similar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
       Hawaiian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Kalua" title="Kalua">
       kalua
      </a>
      . Among
      <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">
       Bedouin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tuareg_people" title="Tuareg people">
       Tuareg
      </a>
      nomads a simple earth oven is used, often when men travel without family and kitchen equipment in the desert. The oven is mostly used to bake bread but is also used to cook
      <a href="/wiki/Venison" title="Venison">
       venison
      </a>
      such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_Monitor" title="Desert Monitor">
       waran
      </a>
      . When baking bread, the wheat or barley flour is mixed with water and some salt and then placed directly into the hot sands beneath the camp fire. It is then covered again by hot coal and left to bake. This kind of bread is eaten with
      <a href="/wiki/Black_tea" title="Black tea">
       black tea
      </a>
      (in the absence of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labneh" title="Labneh">
       labneh
      </a>
      ). The sand has to be knocked off carefully before consuming the bread. Sometimes this type of bread is also made when the family is together, because people like the taste of it. The bread is often mixed with molten fat (sometimes oil or butter) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labneh" title="Labneh">
       labneh
      </a>
      (goat milk yoghurt) and then formed into a dough before eating. This bread is known as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arbut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arbut (page does not exist)">
       Arbut
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-youtube_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-youtube-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but may be known under other local names.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pacific">
       The Pacific
       <span id="the_Pacific">
       </span>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Pacific">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maori_earth_oven.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="510" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Maori_earth_oven.svg/200px-Maori_earth_oven.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Maori_earth_oven.svg/300px-Maori_earth_oven.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Maori_earth_oven.svg/400px-Maori_earth_oven.svg.png 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maori_earth_oven.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A maori earth oven
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Earth oven cooking was very common in the past and continues into the present  particularly for special occasions, because of how labor-intensive the earth oven process was.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the closely related and some part-Melanesian
      <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_languages" title="Polynesian languages">
       Polynesian languages
      </a>
      the general term is "umu", from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Oceanic" title="Proto-Oceanic">
       Proto-Oceanic
      </a>
      root *qumun: e.g.;
      <a href="/wiki/Tongan_language" title="Tongan language">
       Tongan
      </a>
      <i>
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       umu
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Mori language">
       Mori
      </a>
      <i>
       umu
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/H%C4%81ngi" title="Hngi">
        hngi
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
       Hawaiian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiian_imu" title="Hawaiian imu">
       imu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Samoan_language" title="Samoan language">
       Smoan
      </a>
      <i>
       umu
      </i>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cook_Island_M%C4%81ori" title="Cook Island Mori">
       Cook Island Mori
      </a>
      umu. In some non-Polynesian, part-Polynesian and Micronesian parts of the Pacific, languages are more diverse so each language has its own term - in
      <a href="/wiki/Fijian_language" title="Fijian language">
       Fiji
      </a>
      it is a
      <i>
       lovo
      </i>
      and in
      <a href="/wiki/Rotuman_language" title="Rotuman language">
       Rotuman
      </a>
      it is a
      <i>
       koua
      </i>
      . (In Papua New Guinea, "mumu" - borrowed from Polynesian
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
         <span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (August 2011)">
          verification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , is used by
      <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">
       Tok Pisin
      </a>
      and English speakers, but each of the other
      <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">
       hundreds
      </a>
      of local languages has its own word.)
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite the similarities, there are many differences in the details of preparation, their cultural significance and current usage.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Samoan_Umu">
       Samoan Umu
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Samoan Umu">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1656" data-file-width="2406" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg/200px-Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg/300px-Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg/400px-Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pig_on_the_Samoan_Umu.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Samoan
        <i>
         umu
        </i>
        preparation with pig and
        <a href="/wiki/Taro" title="Taro">
         taro
        </a>
        on hot rocks above ground later covered by leaves for cooking.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Samoan
      <i>
       umu
      </i>
      uses the same method of cooking as many other earth ovens and is closely related to the Hawaiian earth oven, the
      <i>
       imu
      </i>
      , which is made underground by digging a pit (although generally the
      <i>
       umu
      </i>
      is done above ground rather than in a pit). It is a common day-to-day method of preparing roasted foods with modern ovens being restricted to western-style houses. In the traditional village house, gas burners will be used inside the house to cook some food in pots. The
      <i>
       umu
      </i>
      is sheltered by a roof in case of rain and is separate from the house. There are no walls, which allows the smoke from the cooking to escape.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Samoan
      <i>
       umu
      </i>
      starts with a fire to heat rocks which have been tested by fire and which have not exploded. These rocks are used repeatedly but eventually are discarded and replaced when it is felt that they no longer hold enough heat. Once the rocks are hot enough they are stacked around the parcels of food which are wrapped in banana leaves or aluminium foil. Leaves are then placed over the assembly and the food is left to cook for a few hours until it is cooked.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hawaiian_Imu">
       Hawaiian Imu
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hawaiian Imu">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Hawaiian
      <i>
       imu
      </i>
      was the easiest way to cook large quantities of food quickly and efficiently for the
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiians" title="Native Hawaiians">
       Hawaiians
      </a>
      . Because their creation was so labor-intensive,
      <i>
       imu
      </i>
      s were only created for special events or ceremonies where it would be worth the time and hard work. An
      <i>
       imu
      </i>
      is created by first digging a 24 foot hole in the ground. Porous rocks are heated and added to the bottom of the pit after they have been heating for a while; next, a layer of banana stumps is added over top along with the banana leaves. After the vegetation is laid down, the meat, fish and any other foods are placed on top and covered once again with more vegetation. Wet cocoa sacks are also sometimes added over top to add even more moisture and trap in more heat.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">
       Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_oven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although not believed to be common in Europe, earth ovens were used from the Neolithic period onwards with examples from this period found at the sites of Rinyo and Links of Notland on
      <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">
       Orkney
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but are more commonly known in the Bronze and Iron Ages from sites such as
      <a href="/wiki/Newquay" title="Newquay">
       Trethellan Farm
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Castle,_Dorset" title="Maiden Castle, Dorset">
       Maiden Castle, Dorset
      </a>
      . Examples from these periods vary in form but are generally bowl-shaped and shallow in depth (3045cm) with diameters between 0.5 and 2 metres. In
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_cuisine" title="Greek cuisine">
       Greek cuisine
      </a>
      , there is also a tradition of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klephts" title="Klephts">
        kleftiko
       </a>
      </i>
      ("thief style") dishes, ascribed to anti-Turkish partisans during the Greek war for independence, which involve wrapping the food in clay and cooking in a covered pit, allegedly at first to avoid detection by Turkish forces.
     </p>
