       <h1 id="title1">
        Technical FAQ
       </h1>
       <div id="content">
        <div id="ZONE-C">
         <div class="f-widget f-text_area" id="f-widget-133899">
          <h2>
           Technical FAQ
          </h2>
          <div class="article">
           <p>
            The
            <a href="http://www.arrl.org/technical-information-service">
             <strong>
              Technical Information Service
             </strong>
            </a>
            pages contain a brief overview of a particular topic, followed by links to ARRL and other downloadable articles about the subject and a list of other web pages with more information. Many of the pages also contain an FAQ section, where the ARRLLab staff provide an answer to the common questions we receive on the subject.
           </p>
           <p>
            Another excellent resource is the
            <a href="http://www.arrl.org/arrl-periodicals-archive-search">
             <strong>
              ARRLPeriodicals Archive and Search
             </strong>
            </a>
            , available to ARRL members.
           </p>
           <p>
            The ARRL Lab staff is happy to answer technical questions unanswered on our website.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Note:
            </strong>
            Lab consultation is an ARRL Members Only benefit.
            <a href="mailto:tis@arrl.org">
             <strong>
              Contact Us
             </strong>
            </a>
           </p>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
          </div>
          <script type="text/javascript">
           arrl_elements_text_area = 1;
          </script>
         </div>
         <div class="f-widget f-text_area" id="f-widget-133900">
          <h2>
           FAQ
          </h2>
          <div class="article">
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              Q)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             Why cant I get a recommendation about the best radio to buy?
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              A)
              <span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             <span>
              There may be a "best radio," but what is best for  one ham would not be best for another. Every ham needs to evaluate his  or her own needs, prefrences and budget against the new or used radios  that are available. ARRL answers this question by pointing members to  the informatiion we publish in each
              <em>
               QST
              </em>
              Product Reveiw, and to  the "Best Rig" FAQ page we have put on the ARRLweb site Technology  page, to help members answer the question for themselves. Just as one  example, to many members, a rig's features may be mroe important than  the "numbers" in the Product Review column that describe a rig's  measured performance. Just as one example, for hams with modest  antennnas, there are not usually a lot of big signals to overload the  receiver, so a receiver with a adequate dynamic range will sound just as  good as one with a top-drawer (and top price) dynamic range. The  articls on the Best Rig FAQ page describe all this and more.
             </span>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             -----
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              Q)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             I shelled out a lot of money for a top of the line radio, why dont I hear more signals than the economy radio I bought?
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              A)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             Modern economy HF radios are just as sensitive as the most  expensive radiosthe limitation isnt the radio, but the noise level at  your station.
             <span>
             </span>
             Sort of like trying to see better on a  bright sunny dayimage intensifiers work at night, but are useless when  there is plenty of light.
             <span>
             </span>
             What you get is the ability for the radio to handle stronger signals without overloading.
            </span>
            <span>
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </span>
            <span>
             <span>
              -----
             </span>
             <br/>
             <br/>
             <strong>
              Q)
              <span>
              </span>
             </strong>
             Where are those strong signals?
             <span>
             </span>
             I visited a ham with a beam at 100 feetdistant signals at his house are incredibly loud compared to the ones at my station.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              A)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             <span>
             </span>
             Strong signals are a combination of band conditions and antennas.
             <span>
             </span>
             At the bottom of the Sunspot cycle, high antennas have a huge advantage over low antennas, because they favor low angle paths.
             <span>
             </span>
             Low  ionization levels may only support low angle paths. When we move closer  to the top of the Sunspot cycle, this advantage will decrease. On  occasion, at the top of the cycle, low antennas will actually outperform  higher antennas on rare high angle paths, but at this time, we can only  dream.
            </span>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <span>
             -----
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              Q)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             <span>
             </span>
             Ive asked dozens of hams about the best antennaall the answers are too expensive or terribly impractical.
             <span>
             </span>
             Can you recommend the best antenna?
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              A)
              <span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             The "best" is rarely both cheap  and practical. Every ham decides on antenna performance capabiliites  based on their own antenna site and budget.
            </span>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <span>
             -----
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              Q)
              <span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             Which all band antenna will give me maximum efficiency with the convenience of a coax feed to the station?
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              A)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             <span>
             </span>
             Center feeding with open wire generally gives maximum efficiency.
             <span>
             </span>
             If cost isnt an issue, one can place an automatic antenna tuner between the open wire and the coaxial feed line.
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </span>
            <span>
             <span>
              -----
             </span>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              Q)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             Can I use my old ARRL software with Windows 7?
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <span>
              A)
             </span>
            </strong>
            <span>
             It depends on how old it is.
             <span>
             </span>
             Really old  software that is Windows 95 compatible software, designed to operate on  16 bit systems, wont run under your new 64 bit operating system, though  they will run under 32 bit versions of Windows 7.
             <span>
             </span>
             New systems with 4GB of RAM or more need 64 bits to access that much RAM.
             <span>
             </span>
             Some  old 4GB systems were shipped with 32 bit software, as there were issues  with getting drivers for 64 bit software at that time.
             <span>
             </span>
             Drivers interface hardware like printers with your computer software.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             The good news is, most of the newer software runs just fine, once you get past the security issues.
             <span>
             </span>
             Due  to issues with hackers writing malicious programs, Microsoft has  implemented tougher measures to prevent hackers from gaining control of  your computer.
             <span>
             </span>
             In order for programs like Yagi for Windows  to write and modify files, so you can save your work, you need to  explicitly grant YW Administrative Privileges, by clicking the  appropriate box in the security window.
             <span>
             </span>
             Merely operating the program from an administrative account wont allow the program to function properly.
             <span>
             </span>
             This  can be a little confusing, as the computer will flag the antenna file  as the problemchanging the settings on the file wont help.
             <span>
             </span>
             You need to change the settings on YW.
             <span>
              YW comes on the companion CD for the ARRL Antenna Book, 21st edition. I'd suggest downloading the
              <a href="http://www.arrl.org/files/file/Technology/software/ANT21softwareNotes.pdf">
               software notes
              </a>
              if you intend to use this CD.
             </span>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span>
             You may also run into problems with missing libraries that used  to be a part of older Windows software, but was left out of Windows 7.
             <span>
             </span>
             This  may not be an issue if you have a lot of software loaded, some programs  will supply the missing librariesyou may never notice this issue.
             <span>
             </span>
             But, with a fresh new system, you may
             <span>
             </span>
             run into this issue. A library known to be missing is COM32DLG.OCX. One can get this library installed by visiting Dan Maguire,
             <a href="http://www.ac6la.com/">
              AC6LAs page
             </a>
             and installing his Moxon antenna program.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            If you have difficulty understanding online help, you might consider buying
            <a href="http://oreilly.com/catalog/9780596806408">
             Windows 7: The Missing Manual
            </a>
            .
            <span>
            </span>
            It may not be obvious that you can use the Search box to run programsor that you can put Run back onto the Start menu.
           </p>
