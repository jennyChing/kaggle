           <p>
            These wolverines may not be the superhero variety found in the comic books.
           </p>
           <p>
            But rare footage of the elusive creature with attitude that live in B.C.s Glacier National Park shows they do have a playful side.
           </p>
           <p>
            As part of a study to determine the wolverines presence in relation to the TransCanada Hwy., Parks Canada researchers set up motion-sensitive cameras and captured one of the weasels behaving in a way seldom seen  it played with a rope.
           </p>
           <p>
            The cameras were set up at bait stations where trees were wrapped with barbed wire to collect hair samples as part of the study.
           </p>
           <p>
            Kelsey Furk, wildlife biologist for Mount Revelstoke and Glacier National Park, said what they captured on the camera at one site Feb. 3 was truly fascinating.
           </p>
           <p>
            In one of the sites in Glacier National Park we discovered the wolverine had pulled down one of my safety ropes that I use for climbing the tree and accidentally pulled it down and started wrestling with it, which was an interesting sight and not something I had seen on the videos previously, she said, adding he was there for three days.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its rare to see wolverine in the wild in general  they are a very elusive creatures and most people never get to see them in the wild so this is just a real interesting and different video ... in this particular situation he seemed to want to hang out and wrestle with the rope and rub himself around on the ground, Furk said.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its just a behaviour you dont see normally.
           </p>
           <p>
            Wolverines are listed as a species of special concern by the B.C. government and their status is being reassessed by the Committee on the Status of Endangered Wildlife in Canada.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its not known how many are in the wild but a density study in the 1990s estimated there were eight to 10 in both Mount Revelstoke and Glacier National Parks.
           </p>
           <p>
            Furk encourages anyone who comes across a wolverine to report the sighting at parkscanada.gc.ca/wolverine and highwaywilding.com.
           </p>
           <p>
            Both Alberta and British Columbia provincial governments recognize wolverine as a species that may be at risk and require special management considerations.
           </p>
