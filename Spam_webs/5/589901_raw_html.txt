         <div id="info">
          <div class="entry-content rich-content">
           <p>
            Take a (peacefully quiet) tour through
            <em>
             and beyond
            </em>
            our solar system to visit a neighboring
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf" target="_blank">
             brown dwarf
            </a>
             a sort of
            <a href="http://coolcosmos.ipac.caltech.edu/cosmic_classroom/cosmic_reference/brown_dwarfs.html" target="_blank">
             failed star thats too large to be called a planet
            </a>
             called W0855. A team of scientists, led by Carnegies
            <a href="http://home.dtm.ciw.edu/users/jfaherty/home.html" target="_blank">
             Jacqueline Faherty
            </a>
            , captured and combined 151 photos taken over three nights. Those images indicate that W0855 has
            <a href="http://carnegiescience.edu/news/first_evidence_water_ice_clouds_found_outside_solar_system" target="_blank">
             frozen clouds of sulfide and water
            </a>
            . This is a first:
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             Water ice clouds exist on our own gas giant planetsJupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptunebut have not been seen outside of the planets orbiting our Sun until now
            </p>
            <p>
             The object, named WISE J085510.83-071442.5, or W0855, was first seen by NASAs Wide-Field Infrared Explorer mission and published earlier this year. But it was not known if it could be detected by Earth-based facilities.
            </p>
            <p>
             This was a battle at the telescope to get the detection, said Faherty.
            </p>
            <p>
             Chris Tinney, an Astronomer at the Australian Centre for Astrobiology, UNSW Australia and co-author on the result stated: This is a great result. This object is so faint and its exciting to be the first people to detect it with a telescope on the ground.
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            What will the next generation of more powerful
            <a href="http://thekidshouldseethis.com/tagged/telescope">
             telescopes
            </a>
            reveal?
           </p>
           Related watching:
           <a href="http://thekidshouldseethis.com/post/64687825957">
            The Giant Magellan Telescope
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://thekidshouldseethis.com/post/laniakea-our-home-supercluster">
            Laniakea: Our home supercluster
           </a>
           and
           <a href="http://thekidshouldseethis.com/post/68989241369">
            How can we know anything about distant exoplanets
           </a>
           ?
           <p>
            via
            <a href="http://www.space.com/27100-water-ice-clouds-beyond-solar-system.html" target="_blank">
             Space.com
            </a>
            .
           </p>
