           <p>
            The
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/local/ohio/">
             Ohio
            </a>
            legislature, dominated by a Tea Party-controlled Republican supermajority, often seems to be creating more problems than it solves. And it could be creating a problem with international ramifications. In its lame duck session, the chamber passed HB 490 last week, a water quality bill which alters standards for withdrawing
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/water-news/">
             water
            </a>
            from Lake Erie and its tributaries, and sent it to the state Senate, where its expected to pass.
           </p>
           <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_351550" class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_351550" style="max-width: 610px">
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Toledo.jpg">
             <span class="image-container wp-image-351550 size-full" style="max-width:600px;max-height:350px;">
              <img alt="Toledo" class="wp-image-351550 size-full" height="350" src="//ecowatch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Toledo.jpg" width="600"/>
             </span>
            </a>
            <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_351550">
             Toledo is one of the Ohio cities dependent on Lake Erie for recreation, tourism and drinking water.
             <a href="http://www.shutterstock.com/pic-148467968/stock-photo-a-panoramic-view-of-downtown-toledo-ohio-s-skyline-reflecting-into-the-maumee-river-with-a-power.html?src=vjclGwW-ocn7msJUbw98BQ-1-4" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.shutterstock.com/pic-148467968/stock-photo-a-panoramic-view-of-downtown-toledo-ohio-s-skyline-reflecting-into-the-maumee-river-with-a-power.html?src=vjclGwW-ocn7msJUbw98BQ-1-4', 'Photo credit: Shutterstock');">
              Photo credit: Shutterstock
             </a>
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            The omnibus bill contains some good things, such as new restrictions on use of fertilizers on farmland, one of the causes of last summers algae bloom in Lake Erie that
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/2014/08/03/toxic-algae-bloom-500000-without-drinking-water-ohio/" target="_blank">
             shut off the water supply
            </a>
            to nearly 400,000 people in the Toledo area. But theres a lot of dissent about its new rules for the withdrawal of Lake Erie water by industrial and other heavy users. And when theres disagreement about the impact of something this legislature is doing, its wise to be wary especially when the fairly conservative Cleveland Plain Dealer
            <a href="http://www.cleveland.com/opinion/index.ssf/2014/11/kasich_should_urge_senate_to_p.html#incart_related_stories" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cleveland.com/opinion/index.ssf/2014/11/kasich_should_urge_senate_to_p.html#incart_related_stories', 'calls it');" target="_blank">
             calls it
            </a>
            a last-minute, larded-up mid-biennium agricultural bill with an industry-driven, water-withdrawal amendment that could condemn Lake Erie to death by a thousand straws.
           </p>
           <p>
            Among other things, the new language would consider only the impact of withdrawals on water level and not on wildlife or pollutant levels, potentially violating the 2008 Great Lakes Compact between the eight Great Lakes states and the international Great Lakes St. Lawrence River Basin Water Resources Compact with Ontario and Quebec. Joel Brammeier, president and CEO of the nonprofit environmental group Alliance for the Great Lakes,
            <a href="http://www.cleveland.com/open/index.ssf/2014/11/lake_erie_water_bill_would_set.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cleveland.com/open/index.ssf/2014/11/lake_erie_water_bill_would_set.html', 'told thePlain Dealer');" target="_blank">
             told the
             <em>
              Plain Dealer
             </em>
            </a>
            that if Ohio starts picking and choosing which parts of the compact it wants to follow other states might do the same.
           </p>
