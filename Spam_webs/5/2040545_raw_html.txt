       <div class="state_introduction">
        <h2>
         Welcome to the North Carolina solar power information page
        </h2>
        <img src="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/NorthCarolinaPaybackNo-Leasefixed.png.pagespeed.ce.QiGeoSplOL.png"/>
        <blockquote>
         <strong>
          Note:
         </strong>
         The numbers above are just estimates for a 5kW solar system, and your home is unique. The best way to know exactly how much money solar power can save you is to
         <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/see-your-solar-savings/">
          connect with one of our partners nearby
         </a>
         . A friendly solar expert we trust will give you a buzz and help you craft a personal plan to get the absolute most out of a solar power system for your home. It's 100% free (yes, thats right, 100% free) and you aren't obligated to buy anything.
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         North Carolinas been a regional leader encouraging solar power for years now, and thanks to a little help from our non-profit friends, things are continuing to look bright for renewable energy. While the current picture may be rosy indeed, things may not be as sunny in the future without further action.
        </p>
        <p>
         North Carolinas major energy suppliers have met their Renewables Portfolio Standard (RPS) goals (well get to exactly what an RPS is in just a second). The utilities have reined in their own solar incentives, and the states excellent solar tax credit is set to expire at the end of 2015.
        </p>
        <p>
         The tax credit and the remnants of utility programs are keeping things humming along for now, but without further action to extend those benefits, payback timeframes on solar power here are going to rise dramatically. The legislature should use this current window of opportunity to ensure an equally sunny future for solar by strengthening the states Renewables Portfolio Standard, and renewing the expiring state tax credit past 2015. With the tax credit, solar is a great deal in NC. Without it, the picture is changed significantly.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <strong>
          Questions?
         </strong>
         Our network of solar experts are on call to assist you. Simply
         <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/see-your-solar-savings/">
          sign up for personalized assistance
         </a>
         on our special solar deals page. You can get discounted on-grid pricing as low as $4,000/kW! This is paired with the North Carolina solar incentives you see below.
        </blockquote>

        <p>
         First, take a look at a typical electric bill before considering solar power. That's a nasty outlay of cash. Imagine what you could do with all that immediate savings above every month.
        </p>
        <p>
         As a result of what state legistlatures in leasing states have accomplished, you could instead save a bunch of cash. Imagine getting this bill in the mail instead. Whew!
        </p>
        <p>
         <img class="state_img" src="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/solar-power-leasing-economics-11.png.pagespeed.ce.rhdj0iIJKH.png"/>
         Now, while you have a drastically cut back power bill, you also have a solar lease payment. Essentially, you're renting out your rooftop to a company who then pimps it out with solar panels. Then, you pay a lease payment to them for the power it produces. In each case, this payment added to your existing power bill will be lower than your previous bill, netting you instant savings with nothing down out of pocket! How awesome is that?!
        </p>
        <div class="state_tooltip">
         <p>
          <strong>
           Leasing vs. Buying
          </strong>
          If you decide not to go with the leasing option, we've calculated the amount of time it would take for your home solar panel system to pay for itself if you put up the cost of the install out of pocket or financed it yourself. This calculation (see the bottom of the page under "5kw Solar System Purchase Payback Time") takes into account all the rest of the incentives below, and assumes you meet all the criteria to take advantage of them (e.g. - having a tax appetite, south facing roof with limited shade, etc.)
         </p>
        </div>
        <h3 class="rightheader">
         RPS
        </h3>
        <h3 class="value">
         12.5% by 2021
        </h3>
        <h3 class="rightheader bottom">
         Grade: D
        </h3>
        <div class="state_custom_info">
         <img src="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/NorthCarolinaRPSChart-01.png.pagespeed.ce.7_hd7B5dQY.png"/>
         <p>
          A Renewables Portfolio Standard (RPS) requires utilities in the state to eventually source at least a certain percentage of their electricity from clean, renewable sources like solar panels.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.google.com/url?q=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.ncuc.commerce.state.nc.us%2Freps%2Freps.htm&amp;sa=D&amp;sntz=1&amp;usg=AFQjCNHP1oYgwb0hCmZxfPD2O-Wlf0oT2Q">
           North Carolinas RPS
          </a>
          , first passed in 2007, requires all investor-owned utilities to produce 12.5% renewable energy by 2021. Municipal utilities and electric cooperatives have a lower goal of 10% renewables by 2018. The RPS also includes a small carve-out for solar power of 0.2% by 2018. That aint much, but a mandate is a mandate.
         </p>
         <p>
          An RPS is critical to strong renewable energy policy. Utility companies aren't really all that gung-ho about you producing your own power. After all, it costs them money when you use less of their electricity. They also naturally dont want to give you big payments for energy you're feeding back into the grid. The main reason the utilities have aided in the transition to lower electric bills and offered incentives to put solar on homeowners roofs is because the states force them to. In North Carolina, with the RPS goals set so low, the utilities have little incentive to help.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="state_tooltip">
         <p>
          <strong>
           What's an RPS?
          </strong>
          Your state legislature paves the way for strong solar energy incentives to flourish by setting standards for renewable energy generation within their territories. Those standards are called the states renewable portfolio standard (RPS). If utility companies do not meet these standards, they must pay alternative compliance fees directly to the state. Many utilities then determine the best ways to source their energy from renewable sources that are less expensive than this fee.
         </p>

The consequences are good, because utilities usually try to meet these RPS standards by creating solar power incentives for you, the homeowner.
