  <h1>
   4.1. Is
   <em class="jp">
    arigat
   </em>
   related to Portuguese "obrigado"?
  </h1>
  <div id="google-top-ad" style="position:relative;left=0px;top=0px">
   <script type="text/javascript">
    &lt;!--
google_ad_client = "pub-6052617510425031";

   </script>
   <script src="http://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/show_ads.js" type="text/javascript">
   </script>
  </div>
  <p>
   No.
   <em class="glossary">
    Arigat
   </em>
   (), the Japanese for "thank you", comes
from
   <em class="glossary">
    arigatai
   </em>
   (), a conjunction of the verb
   <a class="verb-link" href="http://www.sljfaq.org/cgi/verb-inflect.cgi?verb=aru">
    <span class="j">
     aru
    </span>
   </a>
   ,
"to have", with the ending
   <em class="glossary">
    gatai
   </em>
   () which means
"difficult". The "ou" ending comes from the conjunction of the
adjectival
   <span class="j">
    arigataku
   </span>
   with the polite verb
   <span class="j">
    gozaimasu
   </span>
   (originally from
   <a class="verb-link" href="http://www.sljfaq.org/cgi/verb-inflect.cgi?verb=gozaru">
    <span class="j">
     gozaru
    </span>
   </a>
   ).
  </p>
  <p>
   Other common examples of this type of conjugation include
   <span class="j">
    omedet gozaimasu
   </span>
   (congratulations) from
   <span class="j">
    medetaku
   </span>
   and
   <span class="j">
    ohay gozaimasu
   </span>
   (good morning) from
   <span class="j">
    hayaku
   </span>
   .  The word
   <span class="j">
    arigatai
   </span>
   existed in Japanese long before the Japanese ever encountered
Portuguese, and can be found in some of the earliest Japanese
literature. In the form
   <span class="j">
    arigatashi
   </span>
   it is found in the
   <span class="j">
    Manysh
   </span>
   poem number 4011, dating from circa 759 (See
   <a href="manyougana.html">
    1.3.10.
    <em>
     What is
     <em class="jp">
      man'ygana
     </em>
     ?
    </em>
   </a>
   ) and in
   <span class="j">
    Makura no Sshi
   </span>
   [The Pillow Book]
dating from 1002. Although Japanese does contain many loanwords from
Portuguese (see
   <a href="portuguese.html">
    4.2.
    <em>
     Which Japanese words come from Portuguese?
    </em>
   </a>
   ), the earliest recorded contact
between Japan and Portugal was in 1543.
  </p>
  <p>
   "Obrigado" in Portuguese comes from Latin "obligare".  The change from
l to r is typical of Latin-derived Portuguese words.
  </p>
