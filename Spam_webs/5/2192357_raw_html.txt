     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        A Night at the Opera
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1935 American comedy film starring the
      <a href="/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="Marx Brothers">
       Marx Brothers
      </a>
      , and featuring
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitty_Carlisle_Hart" title="Kitty Carlisle Hart">
       Kitty Carlisle
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Jones_(actor)" title="Allan Jones (actor)">
       Allan Jones
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Dumont" title="Margaret Dumont">
       Margaret Dumont
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sig_Ruman" title="Sig Ruman">
       Sig Ruman
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Woolf_King" title="Walter Woolf King">
       Walter Woolf King
      </a>
      . It was the first film the
      <a href="/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="Marx Brothers">
       Marx Brothers
      </a>
      made for
      <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">
       Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer
      </a>
      after their departure from
      <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
       Paramount Pictures
      </a>
      , and the first after
      <a href="/wiki/Zeppo_Marx" title="Zeppo Marx">
       Zeppo
      </a>
      left the act. The film was adapted by
      <a href="/wiki/George_S._Kaufman" title="George S. Kaufman">
       George S. Kaufman
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Morrie_Ryskind" title="Morrie Ryskind">
       Morrie Ryskind
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Al_Boasberg" title="Al Boasberg">
       Al Boasberg
      </a>
      (uncredited) from a story by
      <a href="/wiki/James_Kevin_McGuinness" title="James Kevin McGuinness">
       James Kevin McGuinness
      </a>
      . It was directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Wood" title="Sam Wood">
       Sam Wood
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A smash hit at the box office,
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      was selected in 1993 for preservation in the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">
       National Film Registry
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      as being "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-registry_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-registry-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also included in the 2007 update of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Movies" title="AFI's 100 Years... 100 Movies">
       AFI's 100 Years... 100 Movies
      </a>
      , at number 85; and previously in
      <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Laughs" title="AFI's 100 Years...100 Laughs">
       AFI's 100 Years...100 Laughs
      </a>
      2000 showing, at number 12.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Otis B. Driftwood (Groucho), business manager for the wealthy Mrs. Claypool (
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Dumont" title="Margaret Dumont">
       Margaret Dumont
      </a>
      ), is having dinner with another woman in the same restaurant. When they find each other at opposite tables, Driftwood joins Mrs. Claypool, and introduces her to Herman Gottlieb (
      <a href="/wiki/Sig_Ruman" title="Sig Ruman">
       Sig Ruman
      </a>
      ), director of the New York Opera Company, also dining at the restaurant. Driftwood has arranged for Mrs. Claypool to invest $200,000 in the opera company, allowing Gottlieb to engage Rodolfo Lassparri, (
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Woolf_King" title="Walter Woolf King">
       Walter Woolf King
      </a>
      ), the greatest tenor since Caruso. Meanwhile, opera chorister Ricardo Baroni (
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Jones_(actor)" title="Allan Jones (actor)">
       Alan Jones
      </a>
      ) hires his best friend Fiorello (Chico) to be his manager. Ricardo is in love with the soprano, Rosa Castaldi (
      <a href="/wiki/Kitty_Carlisle" title="Kitty Carlisle">
       Kitty Carlisle
      </a>
      ), who is also being pursued by Lassparri. Driftwood finds Lassparri attacking his dresser, Tomasso (Harpo), who knocks Lassparri unconscious by hitting him on the head. Fiorello enters and identifies himself as the manager of the "greatest tenor in the world". Driftwood, mistakenly thinking Fiorello is referring to Lassparri, signs Baroni to a contract. After saying goodbye to Rosa at the pier, Ricardo, Fiorello and Tomasso stowaway on board the ocean liner to New York inside Driftwood's trunk. After Driftwood discovers them, he tries to get the three of them to leave, as he is expecting a rendezvous with Mrs. Claypool. They refuse to go until they've eaten and eventually, Driftwood's tiny stateroom is crowded with an assortment of people. (see
      <i>
       <a href="#Stateroom_scene">
        Stateroom scene
       </a>
      </i>
      below) Lassparri later spots the stowaways among the immigrants on the ship and they are caught and thrown into the brig. They escape and are able to sneak into the country by assuming the identities of three famous bearded aviators, who are traveling aboard the ship. After a welcoming reception in New York, their true identities are discovered and they hide out in Driftwood's hotel room, pursued by police sergeant Henderson (
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmett_O%27Connor" title="Robert Emmett O'Connor">
       Robert Emmett O'Connor
      </a>
      ). Meanwhile, Ricardo is reunited with Rosa after climbing in the window of her hotel room. Ricardo has an altercation with Lassparri, which results in both Rosa and Driftwood being fired from the opera company by Gottlieb. The boys decide to seek revenge by sabotaging the opening night performance of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_Trovatore" title="Il Trovatore">
        Il Trovatore
       </a>
      </i>
      and they abduct Lassparri, forcing Gottlieb to substitute Ricardo and Rosa in his place. The audience clearly prefers Baroni, and Lassparri is booed and pelted with fruit after he attempts to return to the stage. The film ends with Driftwood and Fiorello attempting to negotiate another contract, as Rosa and Ricardo sing an encore.
     </p>

     </p>

     <p>
      This continues until Fiorello and Tomasso have ordered about a dozen hard-boiled eggs and Driftwood has ordered about everything elseincluding coffee to sober up some stewed prunes. However, this is just to set the viewer up for the famous "Stateroom Scene", which sees a total of 15 people in Driftwood's tiny ship's cabin, already containing a bed and a big wardrobe trunk.
     </p>
     <p>
      The scene starts with Driftwood finding out that Fiorello, Tomasso, and Riccardo Baroni managed to sneak onto the boat by stowing away in his steamer trunk. Fiorello and Tomasso have to hide out in the room while a parade of people walk in, asking to either use their cabin for something, or to perform their appointed tasks. Crammed into this little space at the end of the scene are: Driftwood, Fiorello, Tomasso, Baroni, two cleaning ladies who make up the bed, a manicurist, a ship's engineer and his assistant, a girl looking for her aunt, a maid ("I come to mop up." "You'll have to start on the ceiling.") and four waiters with trays of food (prompting Driftwood's classic line: "Is it my imagination, or is it getting crowded in here?"). The mass of humanity tumbles out into the hallway when Mrs. Claypool opens the door.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ANATOcontract.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="197" data-file-width="271" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/52/ANATOcontract.jpg/220px-ANATOcontract.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/52/ANATOcontract.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/52/ANATOcontract.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ANATOcontract.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The contract scene between
        <a href="/wiki/Chico_Marx" title="Chico Marx">
         Chico
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="Groucho Marx">
         Groucho
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In an interview with Richard J. Anobile in
      <i>
       The Marx Brothers Scrapbook
      </i>
      , Groucho said he was so appalled by an early draft of the scriptwhich was reportedly written by
      <a href="/wiki/Bert_Kalmar" title="Bert Kalmar">
       Bert Kalmar
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ruby" title="Harry Ruby">
       Harry Ruby
      </a>
      that he screamed, "Why fuck around with second-rate talent, get Kaufman and Ryskind [to write the screenplay]!"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the suggestion of producer
      <a href="/wiki/Irving_Thalberg" title="Irving Thalberg">
       Irving Thalberg
      </a>
      , the film marked a change of direction in the brothers' career. In their Paramount films, the brothers' characters were much more
      <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">
       anarchic
      </a>
      : they attacked anybody who was so unfortunate to cross their paths whether they deserved it or not, albeit comically. Thalberg, however, felt that this made the brothers unsympathetic, particularly to female filmgoers. So in the MGM films, the brothers were recast as more helpful characters, saving their comic attacks for the villains.
     </p>
     <p>
      Though some Marx Brothers fans were appalled at these changes, Thalberg was vindicated when the film became a solid hit. It helped that the film contained some of what fans consider to be the brothers' funniest routines. These routines were honed on
      <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">
       stage
      </a>
      , as the brothers performed the new material on the road before filming began.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, according to
      <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Levant" title="Oscar Levant">
       Oscar Levant
      </a>
      , the first preview was a "disaster", with "hardly a laugh" as was the second. Thalberg and
      <a href="/wiki/George_S._Kaufman" title="George S. Kaufman">
       George S. Kaufman
      </a>
      spent days in the editing room, adjusting the timing to match the rhythm of a stage performance. About nine minutes was cut from the running time, and the result was a hit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Opera">
       Opera
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Opera">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      True to its title, the film actually includes adaptations of some real
      <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">
       opera
      </a>
      scenes from
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Pagliacci" title="I Pagliacci">
        I Pagliacci
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_Trovatore" title="Il Trovatore">
        Il Trovatore
       </a>
      </i>
      , featuring the
      <i>
       Miserere
      </i>
      duet sung by
      <a href="/wiki/Kitty_Carlisle" title="Kitty Carlisle">
       Kitty Carlisle
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Jones_(actor)" title="Allan Jones (actor)">
       Allan Jones
      </a>
      . The opera setting also allowed MGM to add big production song numbers (which were one of this studio's specialties), such as the song
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alone_(1935_song)" title="Alone (1935 song)">
        Alone
       </a>
      </i>
      , with the departure of the steamship, and the song
      <i>
       Cosi Cosa
      </i>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">
       Italian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Buffet" title="Buffet">
       buffet
      </a>
      and dancing.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Kitty_Carlisle" title="Kitty Carlisle">
       Kitty Carlisle
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Jones_(actor)" title="Allan Jones (actor)">
       Allan Jones
      </a>
      , who were both trained in operatic singing, provided their own singing voices in the film.
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Woolf_King" title="Walter Woolf King">
       Walter Woolf King
      </a>
      was a trained
      <a href="/wiki/Baritone" title="Baritone">
       baritone
      </a>
      but he portrayed a
      <a href="/wiki/Tenor" title="Tenor">
       tenor
      </a>
      in the film. His singing was dubbed by
      <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera" title="Metropolitan Opera">
       Metropolitan Opera
      </a>
      tenor Tandy MacKenzie.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_re-editing">
       Subsequent re-editing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subsequent re-editing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The film originally was to have begun with each of the Marx Brothers taking turns roaring instead of
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_the_Lion_(MGM)" title="Leo the Lion (MGM)">
       Leo the Lion
      </a>
      (MGM's iconic mascot); Harpo Marx was to have honked his horn. This unique opening was created, but not used in the released film because MGM studio head
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_B_Mayer" title="Louis B Mayer">
       Louis B Mayer
      </a>
      felt the parody would cheapen the trademark. It would turn up years later, however, in a re-release trailer for the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDb_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IMDb-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louvish_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Louvish-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="400" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/64/Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg/220px-Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/64/Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg/330px-Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/64/Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Night_at_the_Opera_end.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="Marx Brothers">
         Marx Brothers
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Sig_Ruman" title="Sig Ruman">
         Sig Ruman
        </a>
        , with
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmett_O%27Connor" title="Robert Emmett O'Connor">
         Robert Emmett O'Connor
        </a>
        in the background, at the end of the film
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to MGM's dialogue cutting continuity and
      <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Maltin" title="Leonard Maltin">
       Leonard Maltin
      </a>
      's audio commentary on the current DVD release, the film originally began (after the opening credits) with the image of a "boat on canal". A superimposed title read, "ITALY - WHERE THEY SING ALL DAY AND GO TO THE OPERA AT NIGHT", and was followed by a musical number featuring bits and pieces from Leoncavallo's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pagliacci" title="Pagliacci">
        Pagliacci
       </a>
      </i>
      performed by "everyday" Italians: a street sweeper sings part of the prologue ("Un nido di memorie...") as he greets a man who then hands out opera tickets to a group of children emerging from a store; the children respond with "la-la-la-la-la, verso un paese strano" (from "Stridono lass"); a "captain" comes down a set of steps, salutes a sentry, then bursts into "
      <a href="/wiki/Vesti_la_giubba" title="Vesti la giubba">
       Vesti la giubba
      </a>
      "; then, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lap_dissolve" title="Lap dissolve">
       lap dissolve
      </a>
      into a hotel lobby, where a "baggage man" is rolling a trunk and crooning about "nettare divino" ("divine nectar"); a waiter joins the baggage man in song, enters the dining room, and sings as he serves a man who for a few notes also sings; the waiter then crosses the dining room to speak to Mrs. Claypool (Margaret Dumont), marking the beginning of the film in existing copies. Maltin
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adamson_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Adamson-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      stated the scene was cut during World War II to remove references to Italy, and unfortunately, the main negative was cut as well, so the scene is now lost.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This notable cut, with several other small ones made at about the same time, is why the stated running time of the film (95 minutes) was three minutes longer than that of existing prints.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDb_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IMDb-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A persistent rumor concerning
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      involves the presence of the Marx brothers' father
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Marx" title="Sam Marx">
       Sam Marx
      </a>
      (also known as "Frenchy") on the ship and on the dock, waving goodbye. Both Groucho and Harpo stated this as fact in their memoirs,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groucho_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Groucho-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harpo_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Harpo-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Leonard Maltin repeats it in the DVD commentary. But this could not have occurred, because Sam Marx had died in 1933, during pre-production of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Duck_Soup_(1933_film)" title="Duck Soup (1933 film)">
        Duck Soup
       </a>
      </i>
      , two years before
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      was released.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louvish_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Louvish-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The rumor arose because Frenchy had had such a cameo appearance in the Marx brothers' earlier
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Monkey_Business_(1931_film)" title="Monkey Business (1931 film)">
        Monkey Business
       </a>
      </i>
      . There is, however, a reference to the Marx brothers' mother,
      <a href="/wiki/Minnie_Marx" title="Minnie Marx">
       Minnie Marx
      </a>
      , during the stateroom scene, in which a woman asks, "Is my Aunt Minnie in here?"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDb_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-IMDb-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Part of the concept of casting the Marx brothers as stowaways on a ship was recycled from
      <i>
       Monkey Business
      </i>
      . As Groucho Marx's and Margaret Dumont's characters are boarding the ocean liner, Dumont asks Groucho, "Do you have everything, Otis?"; Groucho replies, "Well, I haven't had any complaints yet." In two different interviews with Dick Cavett on
      <i>
       The Dick Cavett Show - Comic Legends
      </i>
      DVD, Groucho claimed that that exchange of dialogue was banned in a majority of states when the film was released because it was too suggestive, although the number of states varied with different tellings of the story.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_rediscovery">
       Hungarian rediscovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hungarian rediscovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2008, a film student reported that the Hungarian National Film Archive possesses a longer print of the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungary_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hungary-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the print does not contain the opening musical number, it does contain several excised lines referencing Italy that had been cut upon the film's re-release in the 1940s. With the opening number still missing, it may be that this scene was cut after its original preview screenings during the 1930s rather than during its re-release, as previously thought. However, the discovery of the Hungarian print has not yet been independently verified, and Warner Brothers, who owns the rights to the film, has not indicated that any restoration is forthcoming.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Style_change">
       Style change
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Style change">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      began a new era for the Marx Brothers' style of comedy. Whereas their previous comedies at
      <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
       Paramount Pictures
      </a>
      consisted of a constant barrage of zany, free-for-all jokes sandwiched in between something resembling a plot,
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      was calculated comedy. Producer
      <a href="/wiki/Irving_Thalberg" title="Irving Thalberg">
       Irving Thalberg
      </a>
      insisted on a strong story structure, making the Brothers more sympathetic characters, interweaving their comedy with romantic plots and non-comic spectacular musical numbers. The targets of their mischief were largely confined to clear villains. Thalberg's logic was that the Marxes could get "twice the box office with half the laughs," believing their films would attract a wider audience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stables_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-stables-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Groucho himself agreed with Thalberg's rationale. In his autobiography,
      <i>
       Groucho and Me
      </i>
      , he wrote of the Marx Brothers' 13 films, Two were far above average. Some of the others were pretty good. Some were deplorable. The best two were made by Thalberg"a reference not only to
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      but
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Day_at_the_Races_(film)" title="A Day at the Races (film)">
        A Day at the Races
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another idea of Thalberg's was that before filming would commence on an upcoming picture, the Marx Brothers would try out its material on the vaudeville stage, working on comic timing and learning what earned a laugh and what did not. He was keen to plant gags accordingly so the laughs could be timed correctly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stables_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-stables-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Interestingly enough, the famous "stateroom" scene was nearly eliminated because it was not getting any laughs. One evening the Marx Brothers threw away the script and ad-libbed the whole thing. As a result, a weak scene was transformed into one of their all-time classics.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      , the Brothers' characters were refined. Groucho made more sense, and less trouble. Chico gained intelligence, and Harpo regressed into more of a child. The film dives straight into a plot and accompanying comedy, with every scene having a definitive beginning, middle, and end. The end consisted of a grand finale in traditional MGM musical fashion, something lacking from the Brothers' Paramount efforts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stables_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-stables-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Perhaps most notably,
      <i>
       A Night at the Opera
      </i>
      established a formula that would be used for every subsequent film the Marx Brothers made at MGM:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       a friendship existing between the romantic couple and Chico
      </li>
      <li>
       establishing sympathy for Harpo
      </li>
      <li>
       Chico and Groucho going through several verbal routines
      </li>
      <li>
       Harpo joining as Chico's partner (or brother)
      </li>
      <li>
       lush surroundings as a backdrop to the Brothers' lunacy
      </li>
      <li>
       a fall from grace
      </li>
      <li>
       a rebound on a grand scale in which everything is righted
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stables_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-stables-15">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         15
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
