     <h3 class="entry-header">
      How bold can Darth Vader be?
     </h3>
     <div class="entry-content">
      <div class="entry-body">
       <p>
        <img height="269" src="/images/darthbold.gif" width="424"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        The above word-cartoon is the end result of that quote rolling around in my head for weeks. Im not sure how successful it ended up being, but I know theres a font pun to be made somehow with that line.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img class="rightnoborder" height="200" src="/images/darthbold2.gif" width="114"/>
        I started out trying to literally depict that scene from Star Wars using letterforms, intending to use bold letters for Vader and light letters for Princess Leia. I loved the idea of the @ sign filling in for Leias hair bun. But after a few attempts I concluded that Im no
        <a href="http://epica-awards.com/pages/results/2006/winners/results2006-cat30.html">
         LIDA
        </a>
        when it comes to making art from letters. I just couldnt get the fabric to look like fabric without making the entire thing out of parentheses and tildes of various sizes. And that kind of misses the point. The letter M sort of did what I wanted, but not really. Its too rigid.
       </p>
       <p>
        Then I wondered if maybe I needed to stop trying to depict the scene from the movie, and just draw Darth Vader alone, made out of bold type. In many ways its the most visually striking of the attempts, but I felt like I overlapped the letterforms so much in his helmet that they were becoming mere shapes rather than letters. But heres how that ended up:
       </p>
       <p>
        <img height="388" src="/images/darthbold3.gif" width="424"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        So ultimately I decided to just use different fonts and weights to write their names (seen at the top of this post). But just floating in the frame without a sense of place, Im not convinced that works, either. And its less amazing than pictures made of letters.
       </p>
