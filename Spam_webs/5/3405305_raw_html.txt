  <h1 class="title">
   Proto-warblogger Kevin Sites returns to Afghanistan. Heres the reporting gear hes packing.
  </h1>
  <!-- Subhead -->
  <p class="subhead" style="text-align:center;margin-bottom:0px;">
   Xeni Jardin and Kevin Sites worked together to create one of the world's first "warblogs," when Kevin was a CNN correspondent headed into Iraq, and later, Afghanistan. Ten years later, Kevin is returning to Afghanistan to reconnect with the people whose lives he documented. He shares a snapshot of his gear bag, and the details on what's inside, as he prepares for what may be his last trip into a war zone.
  </p>
  <p class="byline">
   By
   <a href="http://boingboing.net/author/xeni_jardin">
    Xeni Jardin
   </a>
   <!--at 9:50 am Fri, Jun 28, 2013 -->

  </p>

   <article>
    <!-- The copy -->
    <div id="post">
     <p>
      Just before
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_War">
       the US-led Iraq war
      </a>
      began 10 years ago, an internet friend began forwarding emails from a CNN correspondent; emails this war reporter was sending home to friends and family as he tried to get from Jordan into Iraq.
     </p>
     <p>
      His personal stories were more raw and candid than any of the big-media coverage we were seeing and reading back home in the US. The stories felt real, and the voice behind them felt human, not newsman. This was back when there was no Twitter or Facebook, blogs were weblogs, and even when you said weblogs, no one knew what the hell you were talking about.
     </p>
     <p>
      This war correspondent was
      <a href="http://www.kevinsitesreports.com/">
       Kevin Sites
      </a>
      , now also the author of
      <em>
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0061990523/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0061990523&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=boingboing06-20">
        The Things They Cannot Say: Stories Soldiers Wont Tell You About What Theyve Seen, Done or Failed to Do in War
       </a>
       .
      </em>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2003, I was a
      <a href="http://articles.latimes.com/2005/apr/10/entertainment/ca-xeni10">
       newbie contributor to Boing Boing
      </a>
      , and we were a newbie blog; not even a business yet. Just friends having fun with the internet. I reached out to Kevin and asked if hed like me to help him convert these emails into a blog he could share with the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      Whats a blog?, he asked.
     </p>
     <!-- Middle ad -->
     <div id="gpt-ad-middle_1">
     </div>
     <span id="more-239256">
     </span>
     <p>
      I showed him, and we worked together with the help of other geek friends to produce one of the worlds first warblogs, which he filled with
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/2004/09/23/kevin-sites-blog-fro.html">
       stories
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/2003/11/05/latest-kevin-sites-b.html">
       photographs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/2004/10/12/kevin-sites-iraq-blo.html">
       odd anecdotes
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/2004/10/11/kevin-sites-iraq-blo.html">
       videos
      </a>
      from Iraq and Afghanistan as the conflict expanded.
     </p>
     <p>
      I will never forget
      <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kevin_Sites#Reporting_from_the_Middle_East">
       one day in April, 2003
      </a>
      , when I received a message hed been captured captured by Saddam Husseins Fedayeen militia, along with news colleagues. Their Kurdish translator negotiated their release one day later. What do you do with your warblog when your warblogger is abducted? These were new questions. We found new answers. Sometimes, they clashed with what the TV networks thought their correspondents should be doing.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kevin is returning to Afghanistan this week, to reconnect with some of the troops, fixers, and people in those blog posts. Hes doing this with the
      <a href="http://www.vice.com/tag/Kevin+Sites">
       support of
       <em>
        Vice.
       </em>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      It makes me feel like Im closing the loop on my war zone career blogging since it started with you, Kevin emailed recently.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>
     <p>
      Below,
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/?attachment_id=239258">
       his gear bag
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Its a shot of what is likely my last war zone kit, Kevin tells us.
     </p>
     <p>
      I think its minimalism at its best, author Robert Young Pelton (
      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0060011602/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0060011602&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=boingboing06-20">
       <em>
        Dangerous Places
       </em>
      </a>
      ) is making fun of me for taking three sets of pant (one to wear, one to wash, one in reserve). Says if hes sees hand sanitizer here, hes going to take away my man card.
     </p>
     <p>
      Im off today for Dubai, then to Tajikistan and then will cross the Amu Darya River into Afghanistan just like I did 12 years ago.
     </p>
     <p>
      More from Kevin below.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <p>
      <img alt="" class="alignright wp-image-239300" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/boingboing.net/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/spacer5.gif?resize=1%2C1" title="spacer"/>
     </p>
     <p class="caption">
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/2013/06/28/proto-warblogger-kevin-sites-r.html/afghan" rel="attachment wp-att-239258">
       <img alt="" class="bordered" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/boingboing.net/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Afghan.jpg?resize=600%2C535" style="width:100%" title="Afghan"/>
      </a>
      <br/>
      <a href="http://boingboing.net/?attachment_id=239258">
       Click to enlarge image
      </a>
      . What Kevin Sites is carrying on his return trip to Afghanistan this week. Photo: Kevin Sites.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>
     <p>
      <em>
       Ive been covering conflict for half of my 25 year career and every time Ive gone, my kit get smaller. The first time I went into Afghanistan in 2001, shortly after the 9/11 terror attach, I was working for NBC News as a producer and had to manage a crew of six and so much gear that we had to bribe a Tajik pilot to let us load it all on the plane (in retrospect, not the brightest move.)
       <p>
        Ive been working on my own for a long time now and this is what Im taking now, on my fifth and likely final trip into Afghanistan as a reporter:
       </p>
      </em>
      <em>
       For news gathering, Canon Vixia, Nikon D90, GoPro 2, Macbook Air, HyperDrive, collapsible, fold-flat tripod and assorted cables and other odds and ends (nothing top of the line, just reliable and functional.)
       <p>
        Personal maintenance, filter water bottle, some instant coffee packets and some anti-bacterial wipes, three-sets of quick-dry, insect repellant treated clothing (Robert Young Pelton, author of the Worlds Most Dangerous Place says hes going to take away my man-card for that.)
       </p>
       <p>
        Kevlar helmet and Type IIIA body armor required for military embeds but not particularly helpful or recommended when reporting in Afghan communities, unilaterally.
       </p>
       <p>
        To carry, a small, weatherproof North Face Duffle and High Sierra backpack. Think most independent war zone journalists have to be a little OCD because you only have yourself to rely on for everything you need.
       </p>
       <p>
        I pack and repack dozens of times, sometimes adding things, but since I have to carry and manage it all, mostly trying to subtract weightopting for only the absolute essentials.
       </p>
       <p>
        I used to have redundant everything, but my gear weighed 60-70 pounds.
       </p>
       <p>
        Now instead of carrying two of everyting, I carry duct tape to fix things and have descending redundancyif my Canon goes down I can shoot video on the Nikon DSLR, if the DSLR goes downI can shoot pix and video on my iPhonewhen all else fails theres my the GoPro (but then the whole world is wide angle.)
       </p>
       <p>
        My goal on this last trip? is to really explore whats changed in Afghanistan since my first trip there 12 years ago.
       </p>
       <p>
        How do Afghans feel about the U.S./NATO intervention?
       </p>
       <p>
        What do they think will happend when they leave in 2014?
       </p>
       <p>
        Im going to try and find some of the people I reported on back thenincluding the story of the child bride, Gulsoma, a girl married off as a childand beaten terribly until she escaped.
       </p>
       <p>
        I hope this trip ends up being about the people in a countrynot just the weapons in a war zone.
       </p>
       <p>
        It can be a challenge to keep peoples attention when its about something deeper than bombs and bullets.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
      </em>
     </p>
