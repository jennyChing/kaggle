          Shinji Mikamo, present day
         </div>
        </aside>
        <p>
         With the right intentions, she says, "our worst enemy of yesterday can be our best friend of tomorrow".
        </p>
        <p>
         Ten years ago Shinji himself had an experience that proved the truth of this. On the 60th anniversary of the Japanese surrender, 14 August 2005, he was visiting Akiko at her home in San Diego. They went down to the beach for a picnic, where they ran into two surf instructors Akiko knew  American twins, who insisted on giving her 79-year-old father a lesson "for peace".
        </p>
        <p>
         After Shinji successfully rode the waves, the surfers took him home to meet their father, a veteran of the war in the Pacific. His life, arguably, was saved by the atomic bombs, and the Japanese surrender that followed.
        </p>
        <p>
         The two old men were the same age. They shook hands and shared a drink, and then returned to the beach. Looking out to sea, they were looking directly towards Japan.
        </p>
        <p>
         We stood in the water of the Pacific Ocean with candles, says Akiko, and took a moment of silence.
        </p>
