                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/how-to-treat-heat-rash-naturally.html">
                      How To Treat Heat Rash - Naturally
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-7602279005187357432" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     It's been unseasonably cool for our area, and I guess I let that lull me into a false sense of security. Today was one of only a handful of days that we have seen mid to high 80 temperatures with very humid conditions, and I didn't prepare for it. I love the heat; I don't love heat rash.
                     <br/>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      The best way too treat heat rash is to prevent it. Cool, loose fitting cotton or moisture wicking "dry fit" clothing is the best. Tight or synthetic fabrics that don't breathe are not your best choice (like spandex riding pants with suede backside, inner thighs, and calves...)
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      If that ship has sailed and you're already suffering from heat rash, here are some natural remedies.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Cool bath or shower
                        </b>
                        Try to cool your body and especially the affected area with cool water in a bath or shower. Stay as long as you can, but at least 20 minutes, until you feel relief and/or see the rash or redness beginning to fade. Try to air dry if possible. Rubbing the rash while drying yourself will only make it worse.
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Baking Soda
                        </b>
                        A 1/4 to 1 cup of baking soda (depending on how much water you use) in the bath will make the bath more effective.
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Aloe
                        </b>
                        Aloe directly from the plant works to cool the skin and moisturize. I snip the tips of my aloe plants and place them in a baggy in the fridge or freezer. Once they are cool, I smear the cold gel on the affected areas and leave it for 15 to 20 minutes. Reapply as needed when the itchiness comes back until it heals. Topical gel aloe comes in a plastic bottle and can be kept in the fridge indefinitely.
                        <b>
                         Be sure it is pure aloe vera gel with no additives.
                        </b>
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <b>
                        <br/>
                       </b>
                      </div>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Ice
                        </b>
                        Wrap ice in a clean towel or cloth and place on the rash. The ice will cool the skin and alleviate the burning and itching.
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Cornstarch
                        </b>
                        Dust a fine layer of cornstarch over the affected area as if it were powder.
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Peppermint tea
                        </b>
                        Peppermint tea drank as an ice tea (or at least at room temperature for those who can't drink cold beverages) will help lower your internal temperature and balance your body. Drink a small tea cup's worth every couple hours until the rash begins to subside.
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <ul>
                        <li>
                         <b>
                          Tea bags
                         </b>
                         If you're already preparing a peppermint tea, use the tea bags as a poultice. Cool the bags in the fridge or freezer and then place them on the rash. Chamomile tea bags work wonderfully, too.
                        </li>
                       </ul>
                      </div>
                      <br/>
                      <ul>
                       <li>
                        <b>
                         Essential Oils
                        </b>
                        Lavender, Chamomile, Peppermint and Eucalyptus all have anti-itch and anti-inflammatory properties. For adults and kids over 12, add 4 drops of essential oil to the bath. Adding it with baking soda works even better. Youngsters need less, because you are using less water in their bath- 3 years and under, use about 2 drops; preschool through elementary age, use 3 drops.
                       </li>
                      </ul>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      I've already taken my cool bath, have put on light, cotton jammies, and am drinking my peppermint tea. By tomorrow I should be fine, but I'll have to be careful not to spend too much time in the heat and humidity so the rash doesn't come back. I'll still go riding (which is what started this nonsense), but I'll be sure to change into my riding breeches right before riding and change out of them immediately after finishing!
                     </div>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/spiced-applesauce.html">
                      Spiced Applesauce
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2802279267776454160" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-yXoHxr9bTdk/U_UHyrTyNOI/AAAAAAAAA5U/qB_OC_NyFbQ/s1600/applesauce.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                      <img border="0" height="320" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-yXoHxr9bTdk/U_UHyrTyNOI/AAAAAAAAA5U/qB_OC_NyFbQ/s1600/applesauce.jpg" width="192"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Applesauce has been one of my son's favorite foods since he was a baby. I hate that the store brands are full of corn syrup, so I make and preserve as many quarts as I can throughout apple season.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Summer varieties we love for applesauce include Lodi, Melrose, Mutsu, and Vista Bella. Later varieties include Akane, Macoun, Granny Smith, Northern Spy, and Rambo.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Use any of these to make your applesauce and you can't go wrong.
                     <br/>
                     Pictured are Lodi - left, Melrose - bottom right, Lodi/Melrose - top right.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      Spiced Applesauce
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      makes 1 quart. (I use a peck of apples and make 4 - 6 quarts)
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     3lbs apples
                     <br/>
                     water
                     <br/>
                     sugar (optional)
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/poudre-douce.html">
                       Poudre Douce
                      </a>
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/how-to-can-foods.html">
                       Prepare jars for canning.
                      </a>
                     </b>
                     Wash and drain apples. Core, slice, and peel. Add apples and a thin layer of water to a large pot. Cook apples until they are soft. Puree- do not liquify- using a food processor or blender. Return apple puree to the pot and add 1/4 cup of sugar per pound of apples or to taste.
                     <i>
                      Sugar is optional.
                     </i>
                     Stir regularly to prevent sticking while applesauce thickens just slightly. In the last 5 minutes of cooking, add Poudre Douce. Stir until completely mixed. Ladle hot applesauce into hot jars. Using a butter knife or bubble spatula remove air bubbles by running the utensil between the sauce and jar. Clean jar rims with a clean paper towel dipped in white vinegar. Place lids and bands on the jars and process in a boiling water canner for 20 minutes.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/crab-apple-butter.html">
                      Crab Apple Butter
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2159953224654232071" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-v9kfBgnj71s/U_TtHT6FdLI/AAAAAAAAA44/e9Dk5GBsZK4/s1600/crabapple.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                      <img border="0" height="200" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-v9kfBgnj71s/U_TtHT6FdLI/AAAAAAAAA44/e9Dk5GBsZK4/s1600/crabapple.jpg" width="185"/>
                     </a>
                     <span style="font-family: inherit;">
                      We have two beautiful crab apple trees on the property. Last year we had crab apples coming out of our ears. Unfortunately this year the late spring freeze killed most of the blossoms so I only got a couple pounds of apples.
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <span style="font-family: inherit;">
                      <br/>
                     </span>
                     <span style="font-family: inherit;">
                      I shrunk my original butter recipe in half on account of the lowered apple harvest, and it worked great! If you find yourself with only a small amount of apples (I had a cereal bowl full), this recipe works, but if you find yourself with oodles of crab apples, this recipe can safely be doubled or tripled.
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <span style="font-family: inherit;">
                      <br/>
                     </span>
                     <span style="background-color: white; line-height: 18.479999542236328px;">
                      <span style="font-family: inherit;">
                       You can stick the jars directly in the fridge and skip the canning process if you plan to use the apple butter quickly, but I always process my canned goods so I can preserve them for up to a year.
                      </span>
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <span style="background-color: white; font-family: 'Trebuchet MS', Trebuchet, Verdana, sans-serif; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18.479999542236328px;">
                      <br/>
                     </span>
                     <span style="background-color: white; font-family: 'Trebuchet MS', Trebuchet, Verdana, sans-serif; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18.479999542236328px;">
                      <br/>
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-gFe9lq3WC5k/U_TtMpUq6jI/AAAAAAAAA5E/-ebhj2N8dFk/s1600/crabapple%2Bbutter.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                      <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-gFe9lq3WC5k/U_TtMpUq6jI/AAAAAAAAA5E/-ebhj2N8dFk/s1600/crabapple%2Bbutter.jpg" width="204"/>
                     </a>
                     <b>
                      Crab Apple Butter
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      makes 3 pints
                     </i>
                     <br/>
                     <i>
                      <br/>
                     </i>
                     1-2 pounds (2 cups) crab apples
                     <br/>
                     2 cups sugar
                     <br/>
                     1 cup water
                     <br/>
                     1tbsp
                     <b>
                      <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/poudre-douce.html">
                       Poudre Douce
                      </a>
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/how-to-can-foods.html">
                       Prepare jars for processing.
                      </a>
                     </b>
                     <span style="background-color: white; line-height: 18.479999542236328px;">
                      <span style="font-family: inherit;">
                       This is important even if you choose to skip the canning process. The jars must be clean, and they must be hot when adding the hot apple butter to prevent shattering the jar.
                      </span>
                     </span>
                     <span style="background-color: white; font-family: 'Trebuchet MS', Trebuchet, Verdana, sans-serif; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18.479999542236328px;">
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Wash and drain apples. Slice apples in half. With the peels on, combine the apples and 1 cup water in a saucepot. Cook until the apples are soft. Pulp the apples and skins in a food processor, food mill, or blender. Completely mash and pulp the apples but do not liquify. Return the mashed apple pulp to the saucepot and add the 2 cups of sugar. Mix thoroughly. Add the Poudre Douce a little at a time, mixing completely and taste testing. Continue adding the spice mix until you reach a favorable taste. Cook mix on low to medium heat stirring regularly to prevent burning or sticking. Cook until the mix thickens to the consistency of applesauce or slightly thinner. Ladle hot sauce into jars leaving 1/2" headspace. Using a butter knife or a bubble spatula, remove air bubbles around the sides of the jar. Wipe rims of jars with a clean paper towel dipped in vinegar. Place lids and bands onto the jars. Process the jars in a boiling water canner for 10 minutes.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/easy-apple-butter.html">
                      Easy Apple Butter
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6692507557281872349" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                     </div>
                     <table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; text-align: center;">
                      <tbody>
                       <tr>
                        <td style="text-align: center;">
                         <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-hHyxCVtAdQ4/U_TYJeeSbgI/AAAAAAAAA30/rZ9TdUTOirA/s1600/applebutter.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                          <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-hHyxCVtAdQ4/U_TYJeeSbgI/AAAAAAAAA30/rZ9TdUTOirA/s1600/applebutter.jpg" width="138"/>
                         </a>
                        </td>
                       </tr>
                       <tr>
                        <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                         Apple Butter in two varieties. Top jar Lodi, Bottom jar Melrose
                        </td>
                       </tr>
                      </tbody>
                     </table>
                     <div>
                      Apple Butter is my favorite spread. I love when tart apples are paired with spices and turned into thick, textured apple butter. YUM! I
                      <i>
                       really
                      </i>
                      love an easy recipe for that apple butter.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      This recipe allows me to make my own apple butter in less than an hour using only a few ingredients and a few dishes. You can stick the jars directly in the fridge and skip the canning process, but I always process all of my canned goods so I can preserve them for up to a year.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      I made two different apple butters using Lodi and Melrose apples. Lodi apples are very, very tart and have a Red Delicious type of mealiness. For apple butter, the mealy texture of Lodi was okay but they needed watched carefully in the cooking process as they go from uncooked to mush in no time.Melrose are slightly tart and have a firm texture. Melrose is sweeter than Lodi and has a perfect texture for cooking without completely breaking down.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-BvApLaohRaM/U_TZF7Lu5cI/AAAAAAAAA4A/Do-jXuC40gE/s1600/melrose.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-BvApLaohRaM/U_TZF7Lu5cI/AAAAAAAAA4A/Do-jXuC40gE/s1600/melrose.jpg" width="179"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <b>
                       Easy Apple Butter
                      </b>
                      -
                      <i>
                       makes 1 quart. The recipe can be doubled or tripled.
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      8 - 10 medium apples
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      2 cups sugar
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      1 - 2 tbsp
                      <b>
                       <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/poudre-douce.html">
                        Poudre Douce
                       </a>
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <b>
                       <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/how-to-can-foods.html">
                        Prepare jars for canning.
                       </a>
                      </b>
                      This is important even if you choose to skip the canning process. The jars must be clean, and they must be hot when adding the hot apple butter to prevent shattering the jar.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      Wash apples. Core, peel, and quarter apples. Place apple slices and 2 cups of water in a large sauce pot. Simmer until the apples are soft. Puree using a food processor, mill, blender, or by hand with a potato masher. DO NOT liquify the pulp.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      Combine the apple pulp and sugar into the large saucepot. Add the Poudre Douce in small amounts, mixing thoroughly and taste testing as you go. Add the spice until you like the taste. Stir regularly to prevent sticking and burning until the mixture thickens to your liking. I like mine to be applesauce consistency. Ladle hot butter into hot jars leaving 1/4" headspace. Remove air bubbles with a butter knife or bubble spatula. Clean the rims of the jar with a clean paper towel dipped in white vinegar. Place lids and bands on the jars.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      If preserving, process the jars in a boiling water canner for 10 minutes. If not, allow jars to cool and place in the refrigerator.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://halfwayoak.blogspot.com/2014/08/poudre-douce.html">
                      Poudre Douce
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-7076427135005795857" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-XNb2uigJH84/U_Tk3Mo5SYI/AAAAAAAAA4U/6Jkv9ZU6pVM/s1600/20140820_140844%5B1%5D.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="400" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-XNb2uigJH84/U_Tk3Mo5SYI/AAAAAAAAA4U/6Jkv9ZU6pVM/s1600/20140820_140844%5B1%5D.jpg" width="225"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     Poudre Douce is a medieval spice mix that translates to "sweet powder", and sweet it is! Once you try this you'll never go back to pumpkin pie spice, pre-spiced canned pumpkin or using plain cinnamon on your cinnamon toast or in your cinnamon bread.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Mix together:
                     <br/>
                     1 tablespoon powdered ginger
                     <br/>
                     2 tablespoon good quality cinnamon
                     <br/>
                     1 teaspoon ground grains of paradise (or clove)
                     <br/>
                     1 teaspoon ground nutmeg
                     <br/>
                     1/4 cup raw sugar (or whatever sugar you wish to use)
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Store in a spice jar or glass canning jar. Use in any recipe calling for one or more of the ingredients.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     NOTE: Grains of paradise can be found in specialty stores like Whole Foods. You can also replace the grains with clove.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
