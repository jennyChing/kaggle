      <div class="h-entry Block" id="NewsContent">
       <h1 class="p-name">
        Smashed, not mashed: how to up your garlic potato game!
       </h1>
       <p>
        Posted by
        <span class="p-author h-card">
         Katelyn Willoughby / www.garlicshaker.com
        </span>
        on
        <time class="dt-published" datetime="2015-02-05T22:47:27+0000">
         February 05, 2015
        </time>
       </p>
       <div class="e-content BlockContent">
        <p>
         <img src="http://cdn3.bigcommerce.com/s-vtol2s/product_images/uploaded_images/smashed-not-mashed-how-to-up-your-garlic-potato-game.jpg?t=1423176426" style="width: 661px;"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          Ah,
potatoes: boiled, baked, or mashed, this hearty vegetable is a staple among
many dinner tables. Around this time of year, potatoes take prominence on our
plates with the planning of Thanksgiving, Christmas, and New Years
festivities. How could this comfort veggie get any better? By mashing, thats
how (and by adding garlic, of course!).
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          Mashed
potatoes are a favorite among many, and have a long history. Food historians
generally credit the French for their cultivation and spread of potatoes
throughout Europe. In fact, an 18
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          century army pharmacist,
Antoine-Auguste Parmentier, is acknowledged as the first European to promote
potatoes as a healthy and economical food staple. In the 1700s, France
experience widespread famine. Potatoes provided relief to starving populations-
they were easy to grow, harvest, and could be cooked in a variety of ways. More
importantly, they were cheap- not only to grow, but to purchase, becoming a
fast favorite across France and beyond. Before long, potatoes were embraced by
numerous other countries and assimilated into their cuisines. The oldest recipe
documented for mashed potatoes originates from 1747:
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          "Mashed
potatoes:Boil your Potatoes, peel them, and put them into a Sauce-pan,
mash them well: To two pounds of Potatoes put a Pint of Milk, a little Salt,
stir them well together, take care they don't stick to the Bottom, them take a
quarter of a Pound of Butter, stir in and serve it up." 
          <em>
           Some
          </em>
          <em>
           amazing
cook!
          </em>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          Flash
forward a few hundred years later, and potatoes are still one of the worlds
most popular foods. While the use of potatoes has varied over the span of
history and across cultures, people still love their mashed taters. More
recently, people are smashing instead of mashing. Whats the key difference?
Smashed potatoes are generally prepared with the skins on, creating a bit more
chunkiness to the finished dish.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          Another
distinct difference between smashed and mashed is the way in which the potatoes
are broken up. The majority of recipes advise hand mashing with either a fork
or handheld masher. Mashed potatoes are generally made with a handheld masher
and then whipped with an electric mixer, resulting in their familiar smooth and
creamy texture. Smashed potatoes are made chunkier by hand mashing, and usually
require less prep time. Just remember: if you decide to use your electric
mixer, your potatoes become mashed potatoes, regardless of whether the skins
are on or off!
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          What
will make your smashed potatoes eves better is, of course, a healthy heaping of
garlic. Here is one of our favorite recipes for simple smashed garlic potatoes.
Now grab your fork or masher and get smashin!
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Easy
Garlic Smashed Potatoes:
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Ingredients
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           6 pieces of medium sized
     potatoes
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           6 cloves of garlic
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           2 tablespoons of olive oil
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           1 teaspoon of rosemary,
     chopped
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
            teaspoon of table salt
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
            teaspoon of ground black
     pepper
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
            cup of milk, preferably 2%
     but you can substitute as desired
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Parsley
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Cooking
Instructions
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Preheat the oven to about 375
     degrees.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Scrub and clean the potatoes,
     do not peel skin.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Cut  to  inch off the top
     of your garlic cloves and drizzle olive oil on the head of each garlic
     clove and cover with aluminum foil.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Boil the potatoes for fifteen
     minutes and or until potatoes are soft. Set aside and let cool.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Using a skillet, pour olive
     oil and stir the rosemary for about 2 to 3 minutes, or until the rosemary
     is fragrant.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Add the roasted garlic and
     oil - rosemary mixture to the potatoes. Season with salt and pepper as
     desired.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Using a potato masher or
     fork, mash the potatoes. Add the milk little by little until desired
     consistency is achieved.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Transfer mashed potatoes into
     a serving dish and garnish with parsley. Serve warm.
          </span>
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          Number
of Servings: 6
         </span>
        </p>
