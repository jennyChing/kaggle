       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The Stephen King Drawer Method for Writing Better Copy
       </h1>
       <div class="post-meta">
        <span class="categories">
         Filed Under:
         <a href="http://www.problogger.net/archives/category/writing-content/" rel="category tag">
          Writing Content
         </a>
        </span>
        <a class="twitter-share-button" data-count="horizontal" href="http://twitter.com/share">
         Tweet
        </a>
        <script src="http://platform.twitter.com/widgets.js" type="text/javascript">
        </script>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <iframe allowtransparency="true" class="fbLikeContainer" frameborder="0" id="fbLikeIframe" name="fbLikeIframe" scrolling="no" src="http://www.facebook.com/plugins/like.php?href=http://www.problogger.net/archives/2014/03/26/the-stephen-king-drawer-method-for-writing-better-copy/&amp;layout=standard&amp;show_faces=true&amp;width=360&amp;height=25&amp;action=like&amp;font=arial&amp;colorscheme=light" style="border:none; overflow:hidden; width:360px; height:25px; display:inline;">
        </iframe>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_29079" style="width: 650px">
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/modomatic/">
          <img alt="Image by Flickr user Mo Riza" class="size-full wp-image-29079" height="428" src="http://www.problogger.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/2422802734_e8bbdd3122_z.jpg" width="640"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Image by Flickr user Mo Riza
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <em>
          This is a post from ProBlogger.net Managing Editor Stacey Roberts
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         When I was studying journalism, it was pointed out to us very early on that our first drafts of anything were never going to be printed. They just werent. They were to be edited by professionals with no emotional ties to the content, and we were to accept the final product as it passed through their experienced hands.
        </p>
        <p>
         If we were going to get precious about our words and our bylines,
         <strong>
          we were in the wrong profession
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         As a result, I learned to detach from my writing. To write well, but also to see it from anothers perspective, and to be able to take edits and cuts with no offence. The subs werent trying to be cruel, they were doing their job by making my copy better.
        </p>
        <p>
         When I began blogging, and had no editor or filter to pass through before I published my work, I still would read back over my work with a sharp eye to tidy it up a bit before launching it into cyberspace. What journalism taught me was to write cleanly, boldly, and in the least amount of words possible. I could no longer waffle, and I wasnt precious about cutting my copy where I thought it might be extraneous.
        </p>
        <h2>
         But what about blogging?
        </h2>
        <p>
         The nature of blogging and journalism means youre usually in a rush to get your content in the hands of readers while it is still relevant. Were staying on top of trends and were riding the waves while we can. But for more evergreen content, or things that arent time-sensitive, then
         <a href="http://contemporarylit.about.com/od/wordsandlanguage/fr/On-Writing.htmhttp://contemporarylit.about.com/od/wordsandlanguage/fr/On-Writing.htm">
          Stephen Kings editing method
         </a>
         is one of the most useful things Ive ever practised: the art of putting time and space between you and your words.
        </p>
        <p>
         In his book
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/On-Writing-Anniversary-Edition-Memoir/dp/1439156816" target="_blank">
          <i>
           On Writing
          </i>
         </a>
         , King describes the methods by which he creates fiction novels. A manuscript should take a season to write, he says. Then he will put a physical copy of it in a drawer and
         <b>
          forget about it for at least six weeks.
         </b>
        </p>
        <h2>
         What does that do?
        </h2>
        <ul>
         <li>
          It puts just enough time between you and your writing to ensure youve become somewhat unfamiliar with the words and can read it with less bias.
         </li>
         <li>
          It ensures youre looking at the work with fresh eyes, not in the heat of the moment where your brain
          <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/media/mind-your-language/2013/aug/09/mind-your-language-brain-mistakes">
           autocorrects the errors
          </a>
          it reads so they fail to register.
         </li>
         <li>
          You disassociate yourself somewhat from what you have written so it doesnt hurt to cut it.
         </li>
         <li>
          Your brain has had time to percolate on some of the ideas and thus can flesh them out more.
         </li>
         <li>
          You can immediately see simpler and clearer ways to convey your message.
         </li>
         <li>
          You can finally remember those things niggling at you in the back of your mind that you wanted to include but couldnt quite put your finger on what they were.
         </li>
         <li>
          You might have learned something new you could add.
         </li>
         <li>
          You might decide you hate it all and start over again.
         </li>
         <li>
          It means you have a deeper feel for what works and what might be received better by your readers.
         </li>
         <li>
          You can publish knowing youve produced the best work youre capable of.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Now, obviously there are small differences between a behemoth fiction manuscript and your blog post. You might not want to wait six weeks, and you dont think its necessary to print it out. Thats not important. What is important is that you are
         <strong>
          distancing yourself from your work in order to come back to it with a more professional attitude
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Your blog might be personal, and your words an extension of yourself. It is ok to feel a bit of emotional attachment to them  this method only ensures youre editing with a clear head as well as a full heart.
        </p>
        <h3>
         The takeaway:
        </h3>
        <p>
         Save your work and close your laptop. Forget about your writing as fully as you can, and put as much time as possible between you and it. Re-read your copy with an open mind and make quick notes about edits youd like to make as you go.
         <i>
          Then
         </i>
         you can go back and change. Dont be afraid  be
         <b>
          bold and decisive
         </b>
         . These are words to be molded, sentences to be crafted. Go with your gut and rearrange what you want until you feel it is right. Then hit publish.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Tell me  do you let your posts rest for a bit before going live? Or are you churn-and-publish kind of blogger?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         S

        </p>
