     <p>
      <b>
       Flarf poetry
      </b>
      is an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
        avant-garde
       </a>
      </i>
      poetry movement of the early 21st century. The term
      <b>
       Flarf
      </b>
      was coined by the poet Gary Sullivan, who also wrote and published the earliest Flarf poems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Publishers_Weekly_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Publishers_Weekly-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its first practitioners, working in loose collaboration on an email listserv, used an approach that rejected conventional standards of quality and explored subject matter and tonality not typically considered appropriate for poetry. One of their central methods, invented by Drew Gardner, was to mine the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
       Internet
      </a>
      with odd search terms then distill the results into often hilarious and sometimes disturbing poems, plays and other texts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pioneers of the movement include
      <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Davis_(poet)" title="Jordan Davis (poet)">
       Jordan Davis
      </a>
      , Katie Degentesh, Drew Gardner, Nada Gordon, Mitch Highfill,
      <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Koeneke" title="Rodney Koeneke">
       Rodney Koeneke
      </a>
      , Michael Magee,
      <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Mesmer" title="Sharon Mesmer">
       Sharon Mesmer
      </a>
      , Mel Nichols, Katie F-S,
      <a href="/wiki/K._Silem_Mohammad" title="K. Silem Mohammad">
       K. Silem Mohammad
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rod_Smith_(poet)" title="Rod Smith (poet)">
       Rod Smith
      </a>
      , Gary Sullivan and others.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flarf_poetry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Joyelle_McSweeney" title="Joyelle McSweeney">
       Joyelle McSweeney
      </a>
      wrote in the
      <i>
       Constant Critic
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       Jangly, cut-up textures, speediness, and bizarre trajectories  I love a movement thats willing to describe its texts as a kind of corrosive, cute, or cloying awfulness. This is utterly tonic in a poetry field crowded by would-be sincerists unwilling to own up to their poems self-aggrandizing, sentimental, bloviating, or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexist" title="Sexist">
        sexist
       </a>
       tendencies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Clover" title="Joshua Clover">
       Joshua Clover
      </a>
      wrote in the
      <i>
       The Claudius App
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       If both (conceptual poetry and flarf) are compelled by what we might term impoetic language, flarf seems interested in discovering the poetic within that field, finding the excess and alterity that once defined poetic language but now must be found elsewhere, within the circuits of ersatz fame and junkspeech, within the anonymized and reshuffled errancies of various machinic protocols (whether it is the Google search algorithm, or a purported human adapting herself to the imperatives of a virtual chatroom.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In 2007,
      <a href="/wiki/Barrett_Watten" title="Barrett Watten">
       Barrett Watten
      </a>
      , a poet and cultural critic, long associated with the so-called
      <a href="/wiki/Language_poets" title="Language poets">
       Language poets
      </a>
      observed that:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       It is precisely, however, to the degree that Flarf does something new performatively and with its use of the detritus of popular cultural and the internet, treading the high/low distinction until it breaks under the weight, that it reinvents the avant-garde. In a larger aesthetic economy, it seems, "the truth will out." Flarf's recent productivity shows how the injunction against the sentence, paragraph, narrative, and even discourse from some sectors of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_poetry" title="Language poetry">
        Language school
       </a>
       intersects with actual conditions of language use. Any such thing as stylistic norms in the avant-garde must inevitably intersect with "life."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Discussion about Flarf has been broadcast by the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">
       NPR
      </a>
      and published in magazines such as
      <a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">
       The Atlantic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bookforum" title="Bookforum">
       Bookforum
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       The Constant Critic
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Jacket
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">
       The Nation
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Rain Taxi
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       The Wall Street Journal
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">
       The Village Voice
      </a>
      . Further discussion has taken place on dozens of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blogs" title="Blogs">
       blogs
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Listservs" title="Listservs">
       listservs
      </a>
      across the United States, and in Australia, Denmark, Finland, Germany, Holland, Mexico, and elsewhere.
     </p>
