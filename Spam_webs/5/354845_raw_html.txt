--&gt;\n\n&lt;/p&gt;&lt;ul&gt;&lt;li&gt;&lt;strong&gt;&lt;span&gt;&lt;a href=\"http://www.bbc.com/travel/feature/20120127-sardinias-island-splendours\"&gt;Related article: Sardinia's island splendours&lt;/a&gt;&lt;br /&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/li&gt;&lt;/ul&gt;&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Immediately\nbelow, the sharp lines of the Supramonte mountains run like sharks fins as far\nas the eye can see through central-eastern Sardinia. Somewhere among them\npastori  shepherds  keep an eye on their herds of sheep or goats, maintaining\nthe solitary and demanding lives of their forebears.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;The history\nof Sardinia, Italys second largest island, has been one of conquest and\noccupation. The mysterious Nuraghic people came around 1800 BC, followed by the\nPhoenicians and Carthaginians, Romans, Germanic Vandals, Byzantines, Pisans,\nGenoese and Spanish, before the island was incorporated into a unified Italy in\n1861. While its size and strategic position in the Mediterranean may have\nattracted hosts of conquerors, the nature of its interior and the independent\nspirit of its inhabitants ensured that Sardinia was never a placid, compliant\ncolony. Both its food and its dialect are evidence of a strong sense of\nindividuality that lives on to this day.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;The work\nand produce of the fishermen and shepherds still give Sardinia an extraordinary\nculinary culture, one strictly divided into food from the sea and food from the\nland. On the coast you eat fish; inland you eat meat. There is very little\ncrossover. Fish stews beside the sea, roasted meats in the mountains  the\nqualities of the dishes are rooted in region, terrain and tradition, and the\nSardinians are proud of those distinctions. To explore Sardinias physical\nlandscape is to explore its culinary landscape at one and the same time. And\nwho could resist such a temptation? Certainly not me.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Tuh-tuh-tuh-tuh.\n5am. The engine of the Sparviero, a purposeful tub of a fishing boat, shudders\ninto life. The port of Villasimius is cloaked in darkness. Some 20 miles east\nof the capital Cagliari, at the southern end of the island, Villasimius was\nonce another of those small fishing villages that speckle the coast of\nSardinia, but its position and sheltered anchorage lured the yachts of the rich\nand the cars of the holidaymakers, and now its a flourishing centre of\ntourism. Lights from some of the moored boats send silver ribbons flickering\nover the gleaming black water. Even above the sound of the engine I can hear\nthe insistent high-pitched zither of crickets.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Silverio\nSandolo backs the Sparviero out into clear water and sends the boat nosing past\nthe sleeping mega-cruisers, trim yachts and other fishing boats not making the\ndawn run. As it moves beyond the protective arm of the harbour wall, the cool\nbreeze of very early morning picks up. Silverio turns east along the coast,\nwhich looms, a barely discernible shadow, pricked here and there by individual\npoints of light.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Silverio is\none of 10 or so fishermen still working out of Villasimius, selling their catch\neither in the fish market at Cagliari, or to restaurants in the town and others\nalong the coast. Between them they keep alive a tradition that goes back\ncenturies, a tradition celebrated on the plate in the form of stuffed mussels,\nmarinated anchovies, octopus salad, deep-fried sea anemones, prawns with\ncannellini beans, and linguine with spiny lobster. Dishes that shine with the\nlife and light of the brilliance of the raw materials.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;The first\nfaint glimmers of dawn appear at about 6am  a luminous strip along the line\nwhere the sea meets the sky. The Sparviero makes steady progress, heading for\nthe Isola Serpentara, a favourite fishing ground of Silverios. The lights of\nanother fishing boat flicker in the darkness away to the right. Silverio keeps\nup a constant bantering chatter with Antonio Loi, the mate.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;The colour\nof the sea changes as the light advances up the sky. The sharpness of stars\ndims. The sky turns pink, peach and apricot, shading into pale Prussian grey.\nSilverio heaves to in the lee of the Isola Serpentara, a small, rocky outcrop\nrising abruptly from the sea, as the sun breaks over the horizon  half a blood\norange rising silently up the sky. Silverio left a necklace of 150 pots for\noctopus here yesterday. He pulls up a pot, opens the door and brusquely yanks\none out, throwing it into a tub sloshing with water.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Its 8am\nnow, and the sun is full, gilding the sea. The air is warm, rich with the\nsmells of iodine, diesel, seaweed and salt. We move back to where Silverio has\nleft a line with hooks baited with sardines. The line stretches through the\nclear, aquamarine water. Far down something flutters whitely, like a\nhandkerchief. Its a skate, unhooked with matter-of-fact speed and tossed into\nanother tub. Its joined by a small conger eel, and another moray. And then,\ndrawn up from the depths, is a dentex  a magnificent, glittering, chain-mailed\nmonster, closely related to sea bream, with a tail like a propeller, and the\nface of a disappointed alderman. Silverio and Antonio grin with pleasure.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;They seem\nfaintly surprised by their good luck. And even more surprised when its twin is\nhauled aboard shortly afterwards.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Later, Ill\nfind samples of the days catch, including dentex, at the market in Cagliari.\nThe marble slabs fronting each stall shimmer with glittery, glimmery, spangled\nfish  sardines, sea bass, cod, grey mullet, red mullet, scarlet and gold as a\nbrocaded waistcoat. Theres a tray full of languorously squirming eels, as thin\nas bootlaces, and mounds of cardinal-red prawns. An octopus gloopily clambers\nover the bodies of some of its kind. These are the materials used to form\ndishes such as the warm salad of prawn tails, fish stew or pasta with sea\nurchin roe served in the restaurants of Villasimius.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;For now\nthough, Silverio secures his boat at the marina and prepares to take in his\nhaul. His movements are quick and purposeful, born of many years experience \nand he could go on for many years to come. There are five marine reserves\naround Sardinia that act as hatcheries and nurseries for breeding fish, which\nthen move out of the protected areas into those parts where the fishermen are\nlicensed to work. This network of reserves means that the fisheries around\nSardinia remain sustainable, feeding locals and providing a livelihood for the\nfishermen.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;My father\nwas a fisherman, Silverio says, and his father, too. Were a family of\nfishermen. Whether my son will be one as well  who knows? Its better to\nstudy. He shrugs and looks out to the flat blue line of the horizon for a\nmoment. But the sea, the sea. Its always in your blood.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;If the sea\nis one defining aspect of Sardinia, mountains are the other. Travel from\nVillasimius in the south to Dorgali in the northeast and youll pass through a\nforbidding landscape of razor-backed mountains clad with pines, holm oak and\ncork trees, between which grow the macchie, the fragrant scrub and shrubs so\ndistinctive of the Mediterranean. The area of mountains known as the Supramonte\nrises between Dorgali and Mamoiada to the west, with Orgosolo at its heart.\nThese peaks were once the kingdom of shepherds, charcoal burners, kidnappers\nand bandits. Now only the shepherds remain.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Antonio\nFronteddu is a 70-year-old retired shepherd and he has spent decades roving\namong these peaks. He still moves with an effortless steadiness over the\ntesting terrain, not a bead of sweat on his bald pate or smooth brow, both\ntanned a rich chestnut. Goats file through the scrub, an irregular column, with\ngoatee beards, horns like the handles of mountain bikes and mad, golden, dreamy\neyes, led by a ram with scimitar horns. The herd ambles round the edge of the\nslope at a deliberate pace and fades into the myrtle, cistus, holm oak and\npine. Gradually the musical tinkle of their bells disappears into the great\nsilence of the mountains.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Until quite\nrecently, there were 30 or 40 pastori and their herds on these particular\npeaks. Today, there are just three or four  and although there are more to be\nfound practising the old ways on the surrounding mountains that run to the\nhorizon, theirs is a way of life that is slowly disappearing.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Antonios\nson Lino carries on the family tradition, with about three hundred goats and\nthirty or so pigs. The pigs are smallish, curious mottled animals, which range\nfreely over the mountain, feeding on the acorns from the cork and holm oaks\nthat flourish in the parched soil. Lino comes up each morning between 6am and\n6.30am, checks on his goats, feeding and milking them, before returning home at\nabout 8pm or 9pm. Shepherds must do this every day because, as Antonio says\nwith a wry grin, the goats dont take holidays. But if the pattern for\nmanaging the goats hasnt changed, other parts of the shepherds life have.\nThese days, Antonio says, the pastori come up in their four-wheel drives.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;The ground\nbecomes increasingly treacherous as we climb  loose shale banked around crests\nof rock worn to flesh-slashing sharpness by wind and rain, but presently we\ncome down into a shaded enclosure. Places for lunch are laid at a long trestle\ntable in the cool shade of a large holm oak. Weve been walking for nearly five\nhours. Five hours! says Lino. You should have done it in one.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Lino is as\nthin as a whippet, all sinew and lean muscle, and dark with the sun. Dry humour\nlurks behind his taciturn manner and austere features. He busies himself at the\nbarbecue pit. The embers of a fire glow in the middle. Lunch is porceddu\n(suckling pig), the offspring of one of the families weve seen during the day,\nsplit down the middle and cooked in a metal frame leant against the stones and\nturned from time to time.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;But before\nthat comes moddizzosu  potato bread not unlike pitta bread, but lighter and\nfluffier, suggesting a small, warm bread duvet. It sits very happily with\nslices of dark, purple, dense salami; cured belly  pink reefs of meat in wide\nseas of white fat; and guanciale, cured pigs cheek. Theyve been thick cut,\nand the fat has a dense, silky delicacy. There are homegrown tomatoes of\npenetrating freshness and good wine from a jug, the kind of everyday food and\ndrink enjoyed by Sardinians of the mountains. Cooking is the culture of the\npeople, says Antonio.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Now its\ntime for the porceddu. Lino unclips the metal frame thats been holding it, and\nflips it into a huge wooden trough lined with paper. He takes a massive cleaver\nand hacks the tiny carcass into small chunks with casual force, and tosses them\ninto a wooden bowl. The skin of the suckling pig is as thin, as crisp as the\nlayer of caramel on top of a crme brle. The meat is sweet and delicate, and\nthe abundant fat carries hints of the herbs and roots foraged on the hillsides\naround. I suck the meat from the bones, wipe the grease from my chin and go\nback for another mouthful.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;We eat\nneat, narrow triangles of four-month-old clarissa cheese, made by hand by Lino.\nIt is firm, close-textured, with a lovely fresh, lactic tang to it. And casu\nmarzu, the pungent cheese famous for visibly containing tiny maggots. Its\nsquidgy and spreadable. There dont seem to be any maggots today, but the\ncheese is deliciously meaty and potent.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt;\n\n&lt;p class=\"MsoNormal\"&gt;&lt;span&gt;The life of\na shepherd is hard and solitary, and the financial rewards are slim. No\nholidays, little money, as Lino sums up his life choice. But he wouldnt\nchange it. I have freedom, he says simply. Im very independent, like the\ngoats. As with Silverio and his fishing boat, I wonder whether his children\nwill want to share the same life. It would be sad to think that one day the\npastori might fade like the carillon of their goats bells upon the bright air\nof the Supramonte mountains. But some traditions will not die out. Not up here\non the high pastures.
