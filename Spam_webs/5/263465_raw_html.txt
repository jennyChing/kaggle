             <h1>
              These Glasses Block Cameras From Recognizing Your Face
             </h1>
             <div class="field-subtitle">
              <p>
               And they don't look too bad
              </p>
             </div>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">

          <div class="content-main basic-sidebar">
           <div class="panel-pane pane-entity-field pane-node-body">
            <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="AVG privacy glasses" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/avg-privacy-glasses.jpg?itok=H4FTyGJg" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/avg-privacy-glasses.jpg?itok=GaPIVb2a" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/avg-privacy-glasses.jpg?itok=BRQqwYNh" height="515" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="AVG privacy glasses" class="full-width" height="309" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/avg-privacy-glasses.jpg?itok=GaPIVb2a" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                AVG
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              With cameras and
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/tags/facial-recognition">
               face-detection software
              </a>
              everywhere these days, what's a privacy-loving citizen supposed to do? Anti-virus software maker AVG has a proposal:
              <a href="http://now.avg.com/avg-reveals-invisibility-glasses-at-pepcom-barcelona/">
               eyeglasses that thwart those technologies
              </a>
              , thanks to infrared LEDs that are invisible to the human eye, but interfere with cameras' ability to see your face. (Want to see a simple version of this in action? Press a button on your TV's remote control while pointing it at your smartphone's camera.)
             </p>
             <p>
              Unfortunately, AVG's privacy glasses, which the company is demoing at this week's Mobile World Congress in Barcelona, are merely a concept at this point; AVG says not to expect them for sale anytime soon. Instead, the company hopes to start a conversation about the pervasiveness of these types of privacy-eroding developments, and how technology can help combat them. Just as well, since asking everybody to don weird-looking glasses probably isn't really a solution.
             </p>
             <p>
              On the upside, at least AVG's glasses are better looking than the pair developed by the National Institute of Informatics in Japan a couple years back. Those are specs that not even people
              <a href="http://www.diginfo.tv/v/13-0050-r-en.php">
               miniaturized and stuck inside a computer
              </a>
              could love.
             </p>
