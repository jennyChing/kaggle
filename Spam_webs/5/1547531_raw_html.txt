        <h1 class="article-title">
         This Robot Draws Like a Human
        </h1>
        <div class="article-meta">
         <span class="author">
          By
          <a href="/author/beckettmufson">
           Beckett Mufson
          </a>
         </span>
          Nov 7 2014
        </div>
        <div class="header-socials share-widget" data-media="" data-permalink="http://thecreatorsproject.vice.com/blog/this-robot-draws-like-a-human" data-title="The Creators Project" data-topic="" data-tweet="The Creators Project" data-twitter-username="CreatorsProject" data-url="http://thecreatorsproject.vice.com/blog/this-robot-draws-like-a-human" id="">

          </div>

          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="social plusone">

       <div class="article-content">
        <p>
         <em>
          <img alt="" src="https://s3.amazonaws.com/vice_asset_uploader/files/1415389123AncientPalatableCaimanlizard.gif" style="width: 640px; height: 360px;"/>
          Images courtesy of the artist,
          <a href="https://dl.dropboxusercontent.com/u/18451154/book_tresset.pdf" target="_blank">
           via
          </a>
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Can robots make art that comments on the human condition? That's a question artist and researcher
         <a href="http://doc.gold.ac.uk/~ma701pt/patricktresset/" target="_blank">
          Patrick Tresset
         </a>
         asks with
         <em>
          Paul-IX
         </em>
         , an automated sketch-bot that turns visual data into "hand drawn" pen sketches. If
         <a href="https://www.google.com/url?q=http://thecreatorsproject.vice.com/blog/installation-asks-could-you-empathize-with-a-robot&amp;sa=U&amp;ei=O_hcVOCaK8WsogSn2ICwDA&amp;ved=0CAYQFjAA&amp;client=internal-uds-cse&amp;usg=AFQjCNHMPz8ur-xSW4M7AKaz2gntUnDLNg" target="_blank">
          robots can be designed to make humans empathize with them
         </a>
         , might they be just a hop, skip, and a hydraulically enhanced jump away from empathizing with us?
        </p>
        <p>
         Tresset has been working with artmaking robots for years, an ongoing exploration
         <a href="http://thecreatorsproject.vice.com/blog/meet-paul-and-pete-the-sketching-robots--2" target="_blank">
          we covered in 2011 when robots
          <em>
           Paul
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Peter
          </em>
         </a>
         were on display at London's
         <a href="http://thecreatorsproject.vice.com/blog/meet-paul-and-pete-the-sketching-robots--2" target="_blank">
          Tenderpixel Gallery
         </a>
         . Back then,
         <em>
          Paul
         </em>
         was young, barely into Version I, but it was still captivating to see it use facial recognition technology to isolate a visitor's visage, then reproduce it in ink as a carefully calculated line drawing. Now the sketch-bot has matured into
         <em>
          Paul-IX
         </em>
         , learning all sorts of new art tricks due to Tresset's ongoing robotics research, which earned the automaton a place at the
         <a href="http://www.gold.ac.uk/news/homepage-news/creativemachinethedigitalrevolutionlivesoninanewartexhibitionatgoldsmiths.php" target="_blank">
          Goldsmiths University
          <em>
           Creative Machines
          </em>
          exhibition
         </a>
         from today through Nov. 14.
        </p>
        <p>
         Tresset's research combines innovations into actual robotic design and investigates the ways that people look at art. In
         <a href="https://dl.dropboxusercontent.com/u/18451154/book_tresset.pdf" target="_blank">
          his self-titled book
         </a>
         , he writes, "The technological element of my practice aims to develop computational systems capable of autonomously producing artifacts that stand as artworks." He evaluates the results based on his own artistic sensibilities, as well as through surveys from critics, curators, and fellow artists. What he has created with
         <em>
          Paul-IX
         </em>
         is a robot that can autonomouslyinterpret the world around, to create a unique artistic vision. "Their style is not a pastiche but rather an interpretation influenced by the robots' characteristics," Tresset tells The Creators Project.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" src="http://thecreatorsproject-images.vice.com/content-images/contentimage/no-slug/58b0acd2ea8dd9188ead5f3acaa5513d.jpg" style="width: 640px; height: 352px;"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         While he had developed young
         <em>
          Paul
         </em>
         as a "creative prosthetic" to help him overcome a debilitating case of painter's block, the most recent iteration "passes time by drawing a still life from observation," Tresset says, a distinct difference between a human-aiding machine and a machine capable of independent creativity. The artist asks, "What is the point for such a robot to dedicate its existence to drawings that comment on human existence, rather than be a utilitarian slave as expected of it?" But alas, Tresset doesn't giveor perhaps even
         <em>
          want
         </em>
         an answer to that question.
        </p>
        <p>
         See
         <em>
          Paul
         </em>
         pass time, and the product of his work, in the video and images below:
        </p>
