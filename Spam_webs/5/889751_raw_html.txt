     <p>
      <b>
       Carl Menger
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       German:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">
        [m]
       </a>
      </span>
      ; February 23, 1840  February 26, 1921) was the founder of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_School_of_economics" title="Austrian School of economics">
       Austrian School of economics
      </a>
      . Menger contributed to the development of the theory of marginalism, (
      <a href="/wiki/Marginal_utility" title="Marginal utility">
       marginal utility
      </a>
      ), which rejected the cost-of-production theories of value, such as were developed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">
       classical economists
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">
       Adam Smith
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">
       David Ricardo
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_Menger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Menger was born in the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Nowy_S%C4%85cz" title="Nowy Scz">
       Nowy Scz
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">
       Austrian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galicia_(Central_Europe)" title="Galicia (Central Europe)">
       Galicia
      </a>
      , which is now in
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      . He was the son of a wealthy family of minor nobility; his father, Anton, was a lawyer. His mother, Caroline, was the daughter of a wealthy
      <a href="/wiki/Bohemian" title="Bohemian">
       Bohemian
      </a>
      merchant. He had two brothers,
      <a href="/wiki/Anton_Menger" title="Anton Menger">
       Anton
      </a>
      and Max, both prominent as lawyers. His son,
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Menger" title="Karl Menger">
       Karl Menger
      </a>
      , was a mathematician who taught for many years at
      <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Institute_of_Technology" title="Illinois Institute of Technology">
       Illinois Institute of Technology
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After attending
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">
        Gymnasium
       </a>
      </i>
      he studied law at the Universities of
      <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">
       Prague
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">
       Vienna
      </a>
      and later received a doctorate in jurisprudence from the
      <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">
       Jagiellonian University
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Krakw">
       Krakw
      </a>
      . In the 1860s Menger left school and enjoyed a stint as a
      <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">
       journalist
      </a>
      reporting and analyzing market news, first at the
      <i>
       Lemberger Zeitung
      </i>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lw%C3%B3w" title="Lww">
       Lww
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      and later at the
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung
      </i>
      in Vienna.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the course of his newspaper work he noticed a discrepancy between what the classical economics he was taught in school said about
      <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">
       price determination
      </a>
      and what real world market participants believed. In 1867 Menger began a study of
      <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">
       political economy
      </a>
      which culminated in 1871 with the publication of his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Economics" title="Principles of Economics">
        Principles of Economics
       </a>
      </i>
      <i>
       (Grundstze der Volkswirtschaftslehre),
      </i>
      thus becoming the father of the
      <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" title="Austrian School">
       Austrian School
      </a>
      of economic thought. It was in this work that he challenged classical cost-based theories of value with his theory of marginality  that price is determined at the margin.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1872 Menger was enrolled into the law faculty at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">
       University of Vienna
      </a>
      and spent the next several years teaching finance and political economy both in seminars and lectures to a growing number of students. In 1873 he received the university's chair of economic theory at the very young age of 33.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1876 Menger began tutoring Archduke
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Prince_Rudolf_of_Austria" title="Crown Prince Rudolf of Austria">
       Rudolf von Habsburg
      </a>
      , the Crown Prince of
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austria
      </a>
      in political economy and
      <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
       statistics
      </a>
      . For two years Menger accompanied the prince in his travels, first through continental Europe and then later through the British Isles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is also thought to have assisted the crown prince in the composition of a pamphlet, published anonymously in 1878, which was highly critical of the higher Austrian aristocracy. His association with the prince would last until Rudolf's suicide in 1889 (see the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mayerling_Incident" title="The Mayerling Incident">
       Mayerling
      </a>
      Affair).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1878 Rudolf's father, Emperor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_of_Austria" title="Franz Josef of Austria">
       Franz Josef
      </a>
      , appointed Menger to the chair of political economy at Vienna. The title of
      <i>
       Hofrat
      </i>
      was conferred on him, and he was appointed to the Austrian
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Council_(Austria)" title="Imperial Council (Austria)">
        Herrenhaus
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1900.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ensconced in his professorship he set about refining and defending the positions he took and methods he utilized in
      <i>
       Principles,
      </i>
      the result of which was the 1883 publication of
      <i>
       Investigations into the Method of the Social Sciences with Special Reference to Economics (Untersuchungen ber die Methode der Socialwissenschaften und der politischen Oekonomie insbesondere).
      </i>
      The book caused a firestorm of debate, during which members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Historical_school_of_economics" title="Historical school of economics">
       Historical school of economics
      </a>
      began to derisively call Menger and his students the "Austrian School" to emphasize their departure from mainstream economic thought in
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
       the term was specifically used in an unfavorable review by
      <a href="/wiki/Gustav_von_Schmoller" title="Gustav von Schmoller">
       Gustav von Schmoller
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1884 Menger responded with the pamphlet
      <i>
       The Errors of Historicism in German Economics
      </i>
      and launched the infamous
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Methodenstreit" title="Methodenstreit">
        Methodenstreit
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      or methodological debate, between the Historical School and the Austrian School. During this time Menger began to attract like-minded disciples who would go on to make their own mark on the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      , most notably
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugen_von_B%C3%B6hm-Bawerk" title="Eugen von Bhm-Bawerk">
       Eugen von Bhm-Bawerk
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_von_Wieser" title="Friedrich von Wieser">
       Friedrich von Wieser
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the late 1880s Menger was appointed to head a commission to reform the Austrian monetary system. Over the course of the next decade he authored a plethora of articles which would revolutionize
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monetary_theory" title="Monetary theory">
       monetary theory
      </a>
      , including "The Theory of Capital" (1888) and "Money" (1892).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Largely due to his pessimism about the state of German scholarship, Menger resigned his professorship in 1903 to concentrate on study.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">
       Economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_Menger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere,_1933_-_5787924.tif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3233" data-file-width="1922" height="370" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere%2C_1933_-_5787924.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere%2C_1933_-_5787924.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere%2C_1933_-_5787924.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere%2C_1933_-_5787924.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere%2C_1933_-_5787924.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere%2C_1933_-_5787924.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Menger_-_Untersuchungen_%C3%BCber_das_Methode_der_socialwissenschaften_und_der_politischen_%C3%96konomie_insbesondere,_1933_-_5787924.tif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Untersuchungen ber das Methode der socialwissenschaften und der politischen konomie insbesondere
        </i>
        , 1933
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Menger used his Subjective Theory of Value to arrive at what he considered one of the most powerful insights in economics: both sides gain from exchange. Unlike
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Jevons" title="William Jevons">
       William Jevons
      </a>
      , Menger did not believe that goods provide utils, or units of utility. Rather, he wrote, goods are valuable because they serve various uses whose importance differs. Menger also came up with an explanation of how money develops that is still accepted by some schools of thought today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econlib_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-econlib-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
