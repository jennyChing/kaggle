     <h1 class="post-title">
      For Geckos  Even Dead Ones  Being Sticky Doesnt Require Any Effort
     </h1>
     <div class="post-author">
      <span>
       by editor
      </span>
     </div>
     <p>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://blogs.www.redorbit.com/author/flowers/" target="_blank">
        April Flowers
       </a>
       for redOrbit.com - Your Universe Online
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      Mankind has been fascinated with
      <a href="http://new.www.redorbit.com/topics/gecko/" target="_blank">
       geckos
      </a>
      for centuries. We have studied everything from how they reproduce in
      <a href="http://new.www.redorbit.com/news/space/1113224577/russian-space-sex-geckos-study-090214/" target="_blank">
       space
      </a>
      to how they are able to
      <a href="http://new.www.redorbit.com/news/science/1113211633/geckos-defy-gravity-thanks-to-seta-081314/" target="_blank">
       climb any surface
      </a>
      . We have even tried to replicate their ability to climb with a DARPA program called Z-Man. Although we have been successful in copying their ability, we still dont understand how the gecko achieves the adhesive strength with which they climb and cling to nearly any surface.
     </p>
     <p>
      A new
      <a href="http://rsbl.royalsocietypublishing.org/content/10/12/20140701" target="_blank">
       study
      </a>
      from the University of California, Riverside (UCR), published online in
      <em>
       Biology Letters
      </em>
      , investigates the question: Is the strength of this adhesion determined by the gecko or is it somehow intrinsic to the adhesive system? In other words, is this adhesion an active action initiated by the animal, or a passive action resulting from the way the toe pads work?
     </p>
     <p>
      The researchers, led by associate professor of biology
      <a href="http://new.www.redorbit.com/topics/timothy-higham/" target="_blank">
       Timothy E. Higham
      </a>
      , conducted laboratory experiments on live and dead geckos to determine the answer to whether this ability is active or passive. They were surprised to find that dead geckos have the exact same strength of adhesion as live geckos.
     </p>
     <p>
      "With regards to geckos, being sticky doesn't require effort,"
      <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/26045" target="_blank">
       said Higham
      </a>
      , who conducted the research alongside William J. Stewart, a postdoctoral researcher in Highams lab. "We found that dead geckos maintain the ability to adhere with the same force as living animals, eliminating the idea that strong adhesion requires active control. Death affects neither the motion nor the posture of clinging gecko feet. We found no difference in the adhesive force or the motion of clinging digits between our before- and after-death experiments."
     </p>
     <p>
      Scientific literature has, in the past, suggested that gecko adhesion at the organismal, or whole-animal, level would require active initiation by the animal. For example, does the gecko need to initiate a muscle activity to push the foot and toes onto the surface to enhance their adhesion? Despite years of speculation, this has never been tested.
     </p>
     <p>
      Higham and Stewart, who is now a postdoctoral scholar at the Whitney Laboratory for Marine Bioscience at the University of Florida, used a new technique that involved a controlled pulling system to test the hypothesis. The device applies repeatable and steadily-increasing pulling forces to the gecko foot in shear. In other words, the device measures clings by pulling the geckos foot in a highly controlled manner along a vertical acrylic sheet. At the same time, the device records shear adhesion with video cameras.
     </p>
     <p>
      The researchers found that the adhesive force or motion of a gecko foot when pulled along a vertical surface was high and variable when the gecko was alive. Surprisingly, they found that within 30 minutes of death, the adhesive force remained high and variable.
     </p>
     <p>
      Geckos, found in the warmer climates of the planet, can climb a wide variety of surfaces, including smooth glass. This ability has inspired climbing devices such as
      <a href="http://new.www.redorbit.com/news/science/1113164485/darpa-gecko-inspired-climbing-device-060614/" target="_blank">
       DARPAs Z-Man program
      </a>
      (developed by Draper Laboratory) or Stanfords Spider-Man Gloves to allow humans to climb with similar abilities.
     </p>
     <p>
      The toe pads of the gecko contain hair-like structures only microns wide called satae, which adhere to contacted surfaces through frictional forces, as well as forces between molecules (known as
      <a href="http://new.www.redorbit.com/news/science/1113289667/van-der-waals-force-re-measured-120114/" target="_blank">
       Van der Waals forces
      </a>
      ). Setae are so strong that a gecko can support 20 times its own body weight on one foot.
     </p>
     <p>
      The UCR study is the first to show that geckos retain their adhesion ability even after death. These results refute the notion that the ability is an active one, such as muscle recruitment or neural activity, initiated by the animal to generate forces.
     </p>
     <p>
      "The idea that adhesion can be entirely passive could apply to many different kinds of adhesion," said Higham, who has previously studied geckos and anole lizards. "This is clearly a cost-effective way of remaining stationary in a habitat. For example, geckos could perch on a smooth vertical surface and sleep for the night - or day - without using any energy."
     </p>
     <p>
      The researchers did find an active component to the geckos climbing/clinging ability: the reduction of adhesion force when the gecko hyperextends its toes, lifting them off the ground by curling only the tips of the toes while the rest of the foot remains on the surface.
     </p>
     <p>
      "We found that the dead animals were more likely to experience damage to their adhesive system, which suggests that the active control may actually prevent injury," Stewart aid. "In other words, when the forces become too high, the gecko likely releases the system using its muscles."
     </p>
