           {"contentUrl": "http://c0263062.cdn.cloudfiles.rackspacecloud.com/content/images/budget-home-remodel-living-room_7a16baa8ce313cf6d95f31209b20f55c_vvytQAV_3x2.jpg", "description": "A smart remodeler ballparks the costs and adds a 15% to 20% contingency.         <div class="article-overview">
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.houselogic.com/home-advice/contracting/home-remodeling-contractor-tips/">
           <img alt="Home remodeling tips navigation banner" src="http://c0263062.cdn.cloudfiles.rackspacecloud.com/content/images/home-remodeling-contractor-tips-navigation_c8ef0b40028ed82ca5ae8a8565c15ffd.png"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          When it comes to home improvements, knowing what you want is the easy part. The tougher question is figuring out how much you can afford. Follow this four-step plan to arrive at the answer.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="article-body">
         <span itemprop="articleBody">
          <p>
           <strong>
            1. Ballpark the costs.
           </strong>
           First, get a handle on how much your remodeling dreams will cost. In general, major upgrades, such as a bathroom remodel or a family-room addition, run $100 to $200 per square foot.
          </p>
          <p>
           Remodeling magazines 2015 
           <a href="http://www.costvsvalue.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            Cost vs. Value Report
           </a>
            gives national averages for 36 common projects. Youll find many of those project costs and other good info in our
           <a href="/home-topics/cost-vs-value/">
            Cost vs. Value section
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            2. Figure out how much you have to spend.
           </strong>
           Once youve zeroed in on a project, the next question is whether you have the money. If youre paying cash, thats easy to answer. But if youre borrowing, you need to assess how much a bank will lend you and what that loan will add to your monthly expenses.
          </p>
          <p>
           There are
           <a href="/home-advice/equity-loans/equity-loan-options/">
            three basic types of loan options
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            A cash-out refinance
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            A home equity loan
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            A home equity line of credit (HELOC)
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           For the vast majority of homeowners, the best way to borrow for a home improvement is a
           <a href="/home-advice/equity-loans/home-equity-line-tips/">
            home equity line of credit
           </a>
           . A HELOC is a loan thats secured by your home equity, which means that it qualifies for a lower rate than other loan types, and you can deduct the interest on your taxes.
          </p>
          <p>
           Because a HELOC is a line of credit rather than a lump-sum loan, it comes with a checkbook that you use to withdraw money as needed, up to the maximum amount of the loan.
          </p>
          <p>
           For help shopping for a HELOC,
           <a href="http://c0263062.cdn.cloudfiles.rackspacecloud.com/content/files/HELOC%20kwlg_8d867ead917b5bda9acab6cb898b4494.pdf" target="_blank">
            download our free worksheet
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           The catch is that the minimum payment on a HELOC is just that months interest; youre not required to pay back any principal. Like only paying the minimum due on a credit card, thats a recipe for getting stuck in debt.
          </p>
          <p>
           Instead, establish your own repayment schedule. You can do this by paying 1/60th of the principal (for a five-year pay down) or 1/120th (for 10 years) in addition to the monthly interest. If you cant afford that much, then you should reconsider your project.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Related:
           </strong>
           <a href="/home-advice/planning-your-remodel/how-to-pay-for-remodel/">
            6 Ways to Pay for a Remodel When You Cant Tap Home Equity
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            3. Get quotes from contractors.
           </strong>
           Before seeking bids, determine exactly what you want, right down to the kitchen countertop material and the type of faucet. By specifying these details up front, you ensure that prospective contractors are all pricing the same items.
          </p>
          <p>
           Get recommendations for at least three contractors from friends, neighbors, and other tradesmen who you trust. Give each one your project description and specific product lists and request an itemized bid. To
           <a href="/blog/contracting/hire-contractor-where-do-you-start/">
            find the right contractor
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Ask to see their recent work
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Check references
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Look at online sites that provide peer reviews of contractors
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Related:
           </strong>
           <a href="/home-advice/contracting/five-essential-questions-ask-before-hiring-contractor/">
            5 Essential Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Contractor
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Reality Check: Cost Overruns
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Take the winning contractors bid and add a 15% to 20% contingency for the unforeseen problems and changes that occur on every project. Is the total still within your ability to pay? If so, youre ready to get started. If not, its time to scale back your plans.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            4. Set priorities and trim the project to fit your budget.
           </strong>
           Dreams and budget not in alignment? Carefully scale down your dream  chances are youll end up satisfied and solvent. Enlist your contractor for suggestions on cutting costs  that way, hell be an ally in helping you stick to your budget.
          </p>
          <p>
           Possibilities include:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Low-cost alternatives.
            </strong>
            For example, specify laminate countertops instead of granite.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Keeping older items that are still in working condition.
            </strong>
            Appliances, furnaces, and lighting fixtures can be upgraded later.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Making the project smaller.
            </strong>
            Trim that bathroom addition from 100 square feet to 80 square feet.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             <a href="/home-advice/home-improvement/remodeling-on-a-budget/">
              Buy it yourself
             </a>
             .
            </strong>
            Youll save up to 20% on your project costs if you buy materials and appliances yourself. Be sure to coordinate your BIY efforts with your contractor.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.houselogic.com/home-advice/contracting/home-remodeling-contractor-tips/">

           </a>
          </p>
         </span>
        </div>
