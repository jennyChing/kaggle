     <p>
      <b>
       Alligator gar
      </b>
      (
      <i>
       Atractosteus spatula
      </i>
      ) are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray-finned" title="Ray-finned">
       ray-finned
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Euryhaline" title="Euryhaline">
       euryhaline
      </a>
      fishes related to
      <a href="/wiki/Bowfin" title="Bowfin">
       bowfin
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infraclass" title="Infraclass">
       infraclass
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Holostei" title="Holostei">
       Holostei
      </a>
      (ho'-las-te-i). The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">
       fossil record
      </a>
      traces the existence of alligator gars back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">
       Early Cretaceous
      </a>
      over a hundred million years ago. They are the largest in the gar family, and among the largest freshwater fishes in
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      . Gars are often referred to as "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primitive_fishes" title="Primitive fishes">
       primitive fishes
      </a>
      ", or "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_fossils" title="Living fossils">
       living fossils
      </a>
      " because they have retained some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphological_(biology)" title="Morphological (biology)">
       morphological
      </a>
      characters of their earliest ancestors, such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Spiral_valve" title="Spiral valve">
       spiral valve
      </a>
      intestine which is also common to the digestive system of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharks" title="Sharks">
       sharks
      </a>
      , and they can breathe both air and water. Their common name was derived from their resemblance to American
      <a href="/wiki/Alligator" title="Alligator">
       alligators
      </a>
      , particularly their broad snout and long sharp teeth. Anecdotal scientific reports suggest that alligator gars can grow up to 10ft (3.0m) in length and weigh as much as 300lb (140kg); however in 2011 the largest alligator gar ever caught and officially recorded was 8ft
      <span class="frac nowrap">
       5
       <span class="visualhide">
       </span>
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
      </span>
      in (2.572m) long, weighed 327lb (148kg), and was 47in (120cm) around the girth. Their bodies are torpedo shaped, usually brown or olive fading to a lighter gray or yellow
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">
       ventral
      </a>
      surface. They do not have scales like other fishes, rather they are armored for protection against predation with hard, enamel-like, jagged diamond-shaped
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganoid" title="Ganoid">
       ganoid scales
      </a>
      that are nearly impenetrable. Unlike other
      <a href="/wiki/Gar" title="Gar">
       gar
      </a>
      species, mature alligator gars have a dual row of large sharp teeth in the upper jaw which they use for impaling and holding prey. They are stalking, ambush
      <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">
       predators
      </a>
      that are primarily
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piscivores" title="Piscivores">
       piscivores
      </a>
      , but will also ambush and eat
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_fowl" title="Water fowl">
       water fowl
      </a>
      and small
      <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">
       mammals
      </a>
      that may be floating on the surface.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alligator gars have been
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extirpated" title="Extirpated">
       extirpated
      </a>
      from much of their historic range through habitat destruction, indiscriminate
      <a href="/wiki/Culling" title="Culling">
       culling
      </a>
      , and unrestricted harvests. Populations are now located primarily in the southern portions of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      extending into
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      . They are considered
      <a href="/wiki/Euryhaline" title="Euryhaline">
       euryhaline
      </a>
      because they can adapt to varying salinities ranging from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater_lake" title="Freshwater lake">
       freshwater lakes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">
       swamps
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Brackish_marsh" title="Brackish marsh">
       brackish marshes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">
       estuaries
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">
       bays
      </a>
      along the
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">
       Gulf of Mexico
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      For nearly a half-century, alligator gars were considered "trash fish",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FWS_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FWS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or a "nuisance species" that were detrimental to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportfish" title="Sportfish">
       sport fisheries
      </a>
      , and therefore targeted for elimination by state and federal authorities in the United States, but the last ten years has seen a greater emphasis placed on the importance of alligator gars to the
      <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">
       ecosystems
      </a>
      they inhabit. As a result, they were afforded protection by restricted licensing. They are also protected under the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacey_Act" title="Lacey Act">
       Lacey Act
      </a>
      which makes it illegal to transport fish in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_commerce" title="Interstate commerce">
       interstate commerce
      </a>
      when in violation of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_law" title="State law">
       state law
      </a>
      or regulation. Several state and federal resource agencies are monitoring populations in the wild, and have initiated outreach programs to educate the public. Alligator gars are being
      <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">
       cultured
      </a>
      in ponds, pools, raceways and tanks by federal
      <a href="/wiki/Hatchery" title="Hatchery">
       hatcheries
      </a>
      for mitigation stocking, by universities for research purposes, and in Mexico for consumption.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UAEX_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UAEX-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy_and_physiology">
       Anatomy and physiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anatomy and physiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Alligator gars are the largest species of gar, and among the largest freshwater fishes found in North America. Mature alligator gars commonly measure 6ft (1.8m) in length, and weigh over 100lbs. (45kg). However, anecdotal reports suggest they can grow up to 10ft (3m) in length, and weigh as much as 350lbs. (159kg).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLMNH_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FLMNH-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The largest alligator gar officially recorded was inadvertently caught in the net of fisherman Kenny Williams of
      <a href="/wiki/Vicksburg,_Mississippi" title="Vicksburg, Mississippi">
       Vicksburg, Mississippi
      </a>
      while he was fishing the
      <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">
       oxbow lakes
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">
       Mississippi River
      </a>
      on February 14, 2011. Williams was pulling up his net on Lake Chotard expecting to find
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffalo_fish" title="Buffalo fish">
       buffalo fish
      </a>
      , but instead discovered a large alligator gar tangled in his net. The gar was 8ft
      <span class="frac nowrap">
       5
       <span class="visualhide">
       </span>
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
      </span>
      in (2.572m) long, weighed 327lb (148kg), and its girth was 47in (120cm). According to wildlife officials, the fish was estimated to be somewhere between 50 and 70 years old. Williams donated it to the
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Museum_of_Natural_Science" title="Mississippi Museum of Natural Science">
       Mississippi Museum of Natural Science
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Mississippi" title="Jackson, Mississippi">
       Jackson
      </a>
      where it will remain on display.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GarGillCU.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/GarGillCU.jpg/250px-GarGillCU.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/GarGillCU.jpg/375px-GarGillCU.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/GarGillCU.jpg/500px-GarGillCU.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GarGillCU.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Gill of a juvenile gar
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      All gars have torpedo-shaped bodies, but some distinguishing characteristics of adult alligator gars include their large size, heavy bodies, broad heads, short broad snouts, large sharp teeth and double row of teeth on their upper jaw. They are usually brown or olive fading to a lighter gray or yellow
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">
       ventral
      </a>
      surface. The
      <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">
       dorsal
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_fin" title="Anal fin">
       anal fins
      </a>
      are positioned toward the back of their bodies, and their
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" title="Caudal fin">
       caudal fin
      </a>
      is abbreviate-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterocercal" title="Heterocercal">
       heterocercal
      </a>
      , or non-symmetrical.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLMNH_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FLMNH-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alligator gars have
      <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">
       gills
      </a>
      , but unlike other fishes they also have a highly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascularized" title="Vascularized">
       vascularized
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Swim_bladder" title="Swim bladder">
       swim bladder
      </a>
      lung that supplements gill respiration. The bladder not only provides buoyancy but also enables them to breathe in air which is why they are able to inhabit bodies of water in which most other fishes would die of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffocation" title="Suffocation">
       suffocation
      </a>
      . The swim bladder is connected to their foregut by a small
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic_duct" title="Pneumatic duct">
       pneumatic duct
      </a>
      which allows them to breathe or gulp air when they break the surface,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaEDU_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WaEDU-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      an action that is seen quite frequently on lakes in the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">
       southern United States
      </a>
      during the hot summer months. The scales of alligator gars are not like the scales of other fishes; their bodies are protected by overlapping, enamel-like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganoid" title="Ganoid">
       ganoid
      </a>
      scales that are diamond-shaped with jagged edges and composed of a hard inner layer of bone and an outer layer of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganoin" title="Ganoin">
       ganoin
      </a>
      that is nearly impenetrable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">
       Taxonomy and evolution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_Germain_%C3%89tienne_comte_de_La_Ville-sur-Illon_La_C%C3%A9p%C3%A8de" title="Bernard Germain tienne comte de La Ville-sur-Illon La Cpde">
       Lacpde
      </a>
      first described the alligator gar in 1803. The original name was
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lepisosteus_spatula" title="Lepisosteus spatula">
        Lepisosteus spatula
       </a>
      </i>
      , but was later changed by E.O. Wiley in 1976 to
      <i>
       Atractosteus spatula
      </i>
      in order to recognize two distinct
      <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">
       extant taxon
      </a>
      of gars. Synonyms of
      <i>
       Atractosteus spatula
      </i>
      include
      <i>
       Lesisosteus
      </i>
      [sic]
      <i>
       ferox
      </i>
      (Rafinesque 1820), and
      <i>
       Lepisosteus spatula
      </i>
      (Lacepede 1803). Fossils from the order
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lepisosteiformes" title="Lepisosteiformes">
       Lepisosteiformes
      </a>
      have been collected in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">
       Cretaceous
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">
       Oligocene
      </a>
      periods, in Africa and India from the Cretaceous, and in North America from the Cretaceous to recent times. Lepisosteidae is the only extant family of gar which has seven species all located in North and
      <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
       Central America
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLMNH_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FLMNH-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">
       fossil record
      </a>
      traces the existence of alligator gars back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">
       Early Cretaceous
      </a>
      over a hundred million years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ResearchGate_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ResearchGate-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naturalist_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Naturalist-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite being a highly evolved species, alligator gars are often referred to as "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primitive_fishes" title="Primitive fishes">
       primitive fishes
      </a>
      ", or "
      <a href="/wiki/Living_fossil" title="Living fossil">
       living fossils
      </a>
      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Warren-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fossils_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fossils-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      because they have retained a few
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphological_(biology)" title="Morphological (biology)">
       morphological
      </a>
      characteristics of their earliest ancestors with seemingly little to no apparent changes, such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Spiral_valve" title="Spiral valve">
       spiral valve
      </a>
      intestine which is also common to the digestive system of
      <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">
       sharks
      </a>
      , an abbreviate-heterocercal tail, and a swim bladder lung for breathing in both air and water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLMNH_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FLMNH-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBGraham_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JBGraham-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Alligator gars are relatively passive, seemingly sluggish solitary fish, but voracious
      <a href="/wiki/Ambush_predator" title="Ambush predator">
       ambush predators
      </a>
      . They are opportunistic night predators and are primarily
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piscivores" title="Piscivores">
       piscivores
      </a>
      , but they will also ambush and eat
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_fowl" title="Water fowl">
       water fowl
      </a>
      and small
      <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">
       mammals
      </a>
      that may be floating on the surface. Their method of ambush is to float a few feet below the surface, and wait for unsuspecting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prey" title="Prey">
       prey
      </a>
      to swim within reach. They lunge forward, and with a sweeping motion grab their prey, impaling it on their double rows of sharp teeth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Diet studies have shown alligator gars to be opportunistic piscivores, and even
      <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">
       scavengers
      </a>
      depending on the availability of their preferred food source. They occasionally ingest sport fishes, but the majority of stomach content studies suggest they feed predominately on
      <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">
       forage fishes
      </a>
      such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gizzard_shad" title="Gizzard shad">
       gizzard shad
      </a>
      as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">
       invertebrates
      </a>
      , and water fowl. Diet studies have also revealed fishing tackle and boat engine parts in their stomachs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDC_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MDC-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPWDgar_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TPWDgar-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spawning">
       Spawning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spawning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As with most ancestral species, alligator gars are long living, and sexually late maturing. Most females do not reach sexual maturity until after their first decade of life while males reach sexual maturity in half that time. The conditions must be precise for a successful spawning to occur. Preparation for spawning begins in the spring with the extended
      <a href="/wiki/Photoperiodism" title="Photoperiodism">
       photoperiod
      </a>
      and rising water temperatures, but flooding is also necessary to trigger the event. When rivers rise and spread over the floodplain, they create
      <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">
       oxbow lakes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Slough_(hydrology)" title="Slough (hydrology)">
       sloughs
      </a>
      , and inundate terrestrial vegetation which in turn provides protection and a nutrient rich habitat for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larval_fish" title="Larval fish">
       larval fishes
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_(fry)" title="Fish (fry)">
       fry
      </a>
      . Once the water temperature has reached 68 to 82F (20 to 28C), and all the other criteria are met, gars will move into the grassy, weed-laden shallows to spawn.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCEQ_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TCEQ-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chron-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Actual spawning occurs when a collection of males gather around
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravid" title="Gravid">
       gravid
      </a>
      females, and begin writhing, twisting, bumping into and slithering over the tops of females, an activity which triggers the release of eggs. Males release clouds of
      <a href="/wiki/Milt" title="Milt">
       milt
      </a>
      to fertilize the eggs as they are released into the water column.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCEQ_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TCEQ-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The sticky eggs then attach to submerged vegetation, and development begins. It takes only a few days for the eggs to hatch into larval fish, and another ten days or so for the larval fish to detach from the vegetation and start moving about as young fry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chron-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Egg production is variable, and believed to be dependent on the size of the female. A common formula used for predicting the volume of eggs a female can produce is 4.1 eggs/gram of body weight which gives an average of about 150,000 eggs per spawn. The eggs of alligator gars are bright red and poisonous to humans if ingested.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_range">
       Natural range
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Natural range">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Alligator gars inhabit a wide variety of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_habitat" title="Aquatic habitat">
       aquatic habitats
      </a>
      , but most are found in the Southern United States in reservoirs and lakes, in the backwaters of lowland rivers, and in the
      <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">
       brackish waters
      </a>
      of estuaries, bayous and bays. They have occasionally been seen in the Gulf of Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Texas and Louisiana it is common to see large gars breaking the surface in reservoirs, bayous, and brackish marshes. They are found throughout the lower Mississippi River Valley and Gulf Coast states of the Southern United States and Mexico as far south as
      <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">
       Veracruz
      </a>
      , encompassing the following states in the United States: Texas, Oklahoma, South Carolina, North Carolina, Virginia, Louisiana, Kentucky, Mississippi, Alabama, Tennessee, Arkansas, Missouri, Illinois, Florida, and Georgia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reports suggest alligator gars were once numerous throughout much of their northern range, however valid sightings today are rare, and may occur once every few years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLMNH_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-FLMNH-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Records of historical distribution indicate alligator gars once inhabited regions as far north as central Kansas, Nebraska, Ohio, Iowa, and west-central Illinois where they are now listed as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extirpated" title="Extirpated">
       extirpated
      </a>
      . The most northerly verified catch was in
      <a href="/wiki/Meredosia,_Illinois" title="Meredosia, Illinois">
       Meredosia, Illinois
      </a>
      in 1922.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-il-acad-sci.org_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-il-acad-sci.org-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_natural_range">
       Outside natural range
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Outside natural range">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A few notable sightings of alligator gars have been reported outside North America. In November 2008, an alligator gar measuring 5.2 to 6.4ft (1.6 to 2.0m) was caught in the north of
      <a href="/wiki/Esenguly" title="Esenguly">
       Esenguly
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">
       Turkmenistan
      </a>
      by two officials of Turkmenistan Fishery Protection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On September 4, 2009 a 3ft 3in (0.99m) alligator gar was found in Tak Wah Park in
      <a href="/wiki/Tsuen_Wan" title="Tsuen Wan">
       Tsuen Wan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
       Hong Kong
      </a>
      . Over the next two days, at least 16 other alligator gars, the largest measuring 4.9ft (1.5m), were found in ponds in public parks in Hong Kong.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nearby residents reported the alligator gars had been released into the ponds by
      <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">
       aquarium
      </a>
      hobbyists, and had lived there for several years. However, after a complaint made by a citizen who falsely identified alligator gars as
      <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">
       crocodiles
      </a>
      , the use of terms like "horrible man-eating fish" had begun appearing in the headlines of some major local newspapers. Officials with Leisure and Cultural Services in Tak Wah Park removed all the alligator gars from the ponds because they were concerned the large, carnivorous fish might harm children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCMP_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SCMP-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is not unusual for the large sharp teeth and outward appearance of alligator gars to precipitate unreasonable fear in those unfamiliar with the species. Sensationalized reports have contributed to the misconception of predatory attacks by alligator gars on humans even though none of the reports have been confirmed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMSUP_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SMSUP-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On January 21, 2011, an alligator gar measuring 4feet 11inches (1.50m) was caught in a canal in
      <a href="/wiki/Pasir_Ris" title="Pasir Ris">
       Pasir Ris
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
       Singapore
      </a>
      by two recreational fishermen. The fish was taken to a nearby pond where the owner confirmed it was an alligator gar rather than an
      <a href="/wiki/Arapaima" title="Arapaima">
       arapaima
      </a>
      as the men had initially thought.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Human utilization">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">
       Early history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Early history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       Native Americans
      </a>
      in the south, and
      <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_people" title="Caribbean people">
       Caribbean peoples
      </a>
      used the alligator gar's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ganoid" title="Ganoid">
       ganoid scales
      </a>
      for arrow heads, breastplates, and as shielding to cover plows.
      <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">
       Early settlers
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Tanning" title="Tanning">
       tanned
      </a>
      the skins to make a strong, durable leather to cover their wooden plows, make purses, and various other items. Gar oil was also used by the people of Arkansas as a repellent for buffalo-gnats.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDC_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MDC-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For nearly half a century, alligator gars were considered "trash fish",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FWS_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FWS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or a "nuisance species" by state and federal authorities who targeted them for elimination to protect game fish populations,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WarmSpringsNFH_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WarmSpringsNFH-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and to prevent alleged attacks on humans, a claim that remains unsubstantiated with the exception of occasional injuries sustained from captured alligator gars thrashing around on the decks of boats.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fishermen participated in the slaughter of thousands of alligator gars believing they were providing a great service. In 1995,
      <a href="/wiki/KUHT" title="KUHT">
       KUHT
      </a>
      channel 8, a member
      <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">
       PBS
      </a>
      television station located on the campus of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Houston" title="University of Houston">
       University of Houston
      </a>
      in Texas, distributed and broadcast the first video documentary ever produced on alligator gar. The documentary, "Alligator Gar:Predator or Prey?", debuted nationally in prime time during the July
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweeps" title="Sweeps">
       Sweeps
      </a>
      , and according to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nielsen_rating" title="Nielsen rating">
       Nielsen rating
      </a>
      report provided to KUHT, was the number one rated program of the evening.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The documentary focused on the physiology and life cycle of alligator gars, addressed the destruction of habitat, the unregulated culling and over harvesting of alligator gars from various lakes in Texas and Louisiana, and expressed concerns for the future of the species at a time when they were still considered a "trash fish".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A decade passed before any significant action was taken to protect and preserve the remaining populations of alligator gar in the United States. The
      <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Department_of_Conservation" title="Missouri Department of Conservation">
       Missouri Department of Conservation
      </a>
      has since partnered with Tennessee, Arkansas, Kentucky, Illinois, Alabama, Mississippi, Texas, Oklahoma, and Louisiana in restoration and management activities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDC_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-MDC-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_fish">
       Sport fish
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sport fish">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="385" data-file-width="640" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg/250px-Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg/375px-Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg/500px-Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alligator_Gar_6_Feet_129_lbs_Brazos_River_8_Nov_04a.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        6ft (1.8m) 129lb (59kg) alligator gar caught by Steve Zeug and Clint Robertson,
        <a href="/wiki/Brazos_River" title="Brazos River">
         Brazos River
        </a>
        , Texas, 2004
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The long time public perception of alligator gars as "trash fish", or a "nuisance species" has changed with increasing national and international attention on the species as a sport fish which some have attributed to features on popular television shows. Oklahoma, Texas, Arkansas, Mississippi, and Louisiana allow regulated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_fishing" title="Sport fishing">
       sport fishing
      </a>
      of alligator gars. Texas has one of the best remaining fisheries for alligator gars, and in concert with its efforts to maintain a viable fishery, imposed a one-per-day bag limit on them in 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPWD_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TPWD-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Texas state record, and world record for the largest alligator gar caught on rod and reel is 279lb (127kg), taken by Bill Valverde on January1,1951 on the Rio Grande in Texas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Alligator gars are also quite popular among
      <a href="/wiki/Bowfishing" title="Bowfishing">
       bowfishers
      </a>
      because of their large size, trophy potential, and fighting ability. The Texas state bowfishing record was set In 2001 by Marty McClellan with a 290 pounds (130kg) alligator gar from the Trinity River. The all-tackle record was a 302 pounds (137kg) alligator gar caught on a trotline in 1953 by T.C. Pierce, Jr. In 1991, fishing guide, Kirk Kirkland, anecdotally reported catching an alligator gar measuring 9feet 6inches (2.90m) on rod and line from the Trinity River.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPWDpress_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TPWDpress-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercialization_and_aquaculture">
       Commercialization and aquaculture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alligator_gar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commercialization and aquaculture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Declining populations of alligator gars throughout their historic range has resulted in the need to monitor wild populations and regulate commercial harvests. Alligator gars have a high yield of white meat fillets and a small percentage of waste relative to body weight. The meat is sold to wholesale distributors, and also sold retail by a few supermarkets with prices starting at around $3.00/lb. Fried gar balls, grilled fillets, and fillets boiled in water with
      <a href="/wiki/Crab_boil" title="Crab boil">
       crab boil
      </a>
      seasoning are popular dishes in the south. There is also a small
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cottage_industry" title="Cottage industry">
       cottage industry
      </a>
      that makes jewelry out of ganoid scales, and tans gar hides to produce leather for making lamp shades, purses, and a host of novelty items.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthwave_7-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthwave-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDC_13-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-MDC-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Atractosteus
      </i>
      gars, including alligator gars,
      <a href="/wiki/Tropical_gar" title="Tropical gar">
       tropical gars
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Cuban_gar" title="Cuban gar">
       Cuban gars
      </a>
      are considered good candidates for
      <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">
       aquaculture
      </a>
      particularly in developing regions where their rapid growth, disease resistance, easy adaptation to artificial feeds as juveniles, and ability to tolerate low water quality are essential. Their ability to breathe in both air and water eliminates the need for costly aeration systems and other technology commonly used in aquaculture. In the Southern United States, as well as in parts of Mexico and Cuba,
      <a href="/wiki/Broodstock" title="Broodstock">
       broodstocks
      </a>
      have already been established, and are being maintained in their respective regions where they already are a popular food fish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ecofisiologia_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ecofisiologia-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

       </a>
      </sup>

     <p>
      Despite the large size alligator gars can attain, they are kept as aquarium fish, though many fish labeled as "alligator gar" in the aquarium trade are actually smaller species. Alligator gars require a very large aquarium or pond, and ample resources in order for them to thrive in captivity. They are also a popular fish for public aquaria, and zoos. It is illegal in many areas to keep alligator gars as pets, but they will occasionally show up in fish stores. Alligator gars are highly prized and sought after for private aquaria, particularly in Japan. According to some reports, large alligator gars could fetch as much as US$40,000 in what some consider the "Japanese black market".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In June 2011, three men from Florida and Louisiana were indicted on charges of illegally removing wild alligator gars from the
      <a href="/wiki/Trinity_River_(Texas)" title="Trinity River (Texas)">
       Trinity River
      </a>
      in Texas, and attempting to ship them to Japan for private collectors. The
      <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">
       indictments
      </a>
      resulted from an undercover sting operation by
      <a href="/wiki/Special_agent" title="Special agent">
       special agents
      </a>
      with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Fish_%26_Wildlife_Service" title="U.S. Fish &amp; Wildlife Service">
       U.S. Fish &amp; Wildlife Service
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_Parks_and_Wildlife_Department" title="Texas Parks and Wildlife Department">
       Texas Parks and Wildlife Department
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Florida_Fish_and_Wildlife_Conservation_Commission" title="Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission">
       Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The charges included violations of three separate provisions of the Lacey Act, specifically conspiracy to submit a false label for fish transported in interstate commerce; conspiracy to transport fish in interstate commerce in violation of state law or regulation; and conspiracy to transport and sell fish in interstate commerce in violation of state law or regulation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two of the conspirators entered guilty pleas to one count, and the government dropped the other two charges against them. A third conspirator went to trial on all three counts, was acquitted on one count, and found guilty on two. The
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_district_court" title="United States district court">
       district court
      </a>
      sentenced him to serve nine months in prison followed by one year of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supervised_release" title="Supervised release">
       supervised release
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The case was appealed, and on April 15, 2014, the appellate court affirmed the judgment of the district court.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
