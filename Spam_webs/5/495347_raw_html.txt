    <h1>
     Environmentalists launch direct action education campaign
    </h1>
    <p class="publishing">
     Elizabeth Jackson reported this story on
     <a href="/worldtoday/archives.html">
      Friday, October 31, 2014
     </a>
     12:18:00
    </p>
    <div class="storyplayerpage">
     <a href="http://mpegmedia.abc.net.au/news/audio/twt/201410/20131031-twt03-cousinsiv.mp3" id="fullstoryplayer" title="click to play MP3 or right click to save it to your desktop">
      Listen to MP3 of this story (
      <em>
      </em>
      minutes)
     </a>
     <p class="alternateaudio">
      |
      <a href="http://mpegmedia.abc.net.au/news/audio/twt/201410/20131031-twt03-cousinsiv.mp3" title="click to play MP3 or right click to save it to your desktop">
       MP3 download
      </a>
     </p>

    <div id="article">
     <!-- &lt;p class="intro"&gt;Geoff Cousins has criticised the Business Council for supporting the Federal Government's direct action carbon emissions reduction plan, which passed through the Senate overnight. The businessman, environmental activist and former Howard government advisor says the BCA appears to be attracted by the financial handouts rather than recognise that it's poor policy to pay polluters who are free to continue to pollute.&lt;/p&gt; -->
     <p class="body">
      ELIZABETH JACKSON: The recently appointed head of the Australian Conservation Foundation (ACF) says the organisation will embark on a public education campaign in response to the Government's direct action legislation passing through Parliament.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Businessman, environmental activist and former Howard government advisor, Geoff Cousins says the legislation is shameful because it pays big polluters to keep polluting.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Mr Cousins has lashed out at the Business Council of Australia (BCA) for supporting the legislation which he says individual companies would have rejected.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      GEOFF COUSINS: If somebody had brought a business case to the boards of one of those public companies for this program, no responsible board would have given it the time of day.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      You would have asked first of all how cost efficient it was, you would have asked what was world's best practice in all of these areas, these sorts of questions, and none of them would have been able to be answered positively in regard to this program.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      The plan that they called direct action actually gives money to the big polluters; taxpayers' money is given out. They say of course that the aim of that is that it will be used then by these companies to reduce pollution but there's no guarantee that that will be the case and of course, there's no compulsion on the companies to do anything at all.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      It is really quite a shameful situation and to have the Prime Minister of Australia running around the world as he did a couple of weeks ago saying coal is our future. Goodness me. It's really just ridiculous. Not even the coal companies actually believe that.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ELIZABETH JACKSON: If the board is there to represent the members and the members, as you have said you believe would have thrown out direct action, why then did the BCA board support the legislation?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      GEOFF COUSINS: I suppose that's because in the short term there's money available but that's a very cynical attitude. For instance, you know, these so-called reverse auctions where companies are able to put forward to the Government a plan that they might have to have some positive impact on pollution.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      So let's say a company was wanting to replace the seals on all its refrigeration equipment. It puts forward a proposal; it puts a price on it. If it falls within the Government's range of pricing the Government approves it and gives the company the money. That may well have been something the company was going to do anyway in the normal course of business but now they get paid to do it.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      It may not have any particular impact on reduction of pollution, any particular impact on the major climate change issues but the Government isn't able to really estimate that so just parcels out money to the biggest polluters in the country.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      I mean business is actually a long way ahead of the Government on this issue and a very good example of that would be that all the major insurance companies in Australia quite some years ago now changed all their ratings for policies for homeowners policies based on climate change science so that they re-rated all the major areas in Australia for wind velocity and storm damage and that kind of thing based on climate change science.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      But the Prime Minister and the Government are still trying to figure out whether there's, in quotes, "any truth in it".
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ELIZABETH JACKSON: What did you make of Clive Palmer's change of heart?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      GEOFF COUSINS: (Laughs) Well, Palmer tries to look as if he's come out of this having engineered a great result and then he says the thing he's really won is there'll be an inquiry into whether an emissions trading scheme or some similar mechanism might be good for the country.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      There've been since 1992 over 30 inquiries, government inquiries in this country into this sort of climate change issue. What's been produced from them? Nothing.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      What's likely to be produced form this one? Nothing. Even the minister himself has sort of indicated that - it's just a token.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ELIZABETH JACKSON: Where does this deal leave Australia?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      GEOFF COUSINS: It leaves Australia so far behind the rest of the western world that it is shameful and that's why as the president of the Australian Conservation Foundation, a job I've only very recently taken on, we've decided that the ACF has got to be the leading voice in these issues because the politicians are failing us.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ELIZABETH JACKSON: Geoff Cousins, the president of the Australian Conservation Foundation.
     </p>
