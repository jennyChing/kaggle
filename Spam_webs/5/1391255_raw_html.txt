     <p>
      <b>
       Christopher Taylor Buckley
      </b>
      (born December 24, 1952)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is an American
      <a href="/wiki/Political_satire" title="Political satire">
       political satirist
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Author_of_novels" title="Author of novels">
       author of novels
      </a>
      including
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/God_is_my_broker" title="God is my broker">
        God Is My Broker
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Thank_You_for_Smoking_(novel)" title="Thank You for Smoking (novel)">
        Thank You for Smoking
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Little_Green_Men_(novel)" title="Little Green Men (novel)">
        Little Green Men
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       The White House Mess
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/No_Way_to_Treat_a_First_Lady" title="No Way to Treat a First Lady">
        No Way to Treat a First Lady
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Wet Work
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Florence_of_Arabia" title="Florence of Arabia">
        Florence of Arabia
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Boomsday_(novel)" title="Boomsday (novel)">
        Boomsday
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Courtship" title="Supreme Courtship">
        Supreme Courtship
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Losing Mum and Pup: A Memoir
      </i>
      and, most recently,
      <i>
       They Eat Puppies, Don't They?: A Novel
      </i>
      . He is the son of writer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_F._Buckley_Jr." title="William F. Buckley Jr.">
       William F. Buckley Jr.
      </a>
      and socialite
      <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Buckley" title="Patricia Buckley">
       Patricia Buckley
      </a>
      and inherited Canadian citizenship through his mother.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life">
       Life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Buckley_(novelist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After a classical education at the
      <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Abbey_School" title="Portsmouth Abbey School">
       Portsmouth Abbey School
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Buckley graduated from
      <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">
       Yale University
      </a>
      in 1975.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was a member of
      <a href="/wiki/Skull_and_Bones" title="Skull and Bones">
       Skull and Bones
      </a>
      like his father, living at
      <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Edwards_College" title="Jonathan Edwards College">
       Jonathan Edwards College
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robbins_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-robbins-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :173
      </sup>
      He became managing editor of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">
        Esquire
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1981, he moved to Washington, D.C., to work as chief
      <a href="/wiki/Speechwriter" title="Speechwriter">
       speechwriter
      </a>
      for Vice President
      <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
       George H. W. Bush
      </a>
      . This experience led to his novel
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_White_House_Mess&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The White House Mess (page does not exist)">
        The White House Mess
       </a>
      </i>
      , a satire on
      <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">
       White House
      </a>
      office politics and political memoirs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (The title refers to the White House lunchroom, which is known as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Mess" title="Mess">
       mess
      </a>
      " because the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       Navy
      </a>
      operates it.)
     </p>
     <p>
      Buckley's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Thank_You_for_Smoking_(novel)" title="Thank You for Smoking (novel)">
        Thank You for Smoking
       </a>
      </i>
      is another satire, its protagonist a lobbyist for the tobacco industry,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nick_Naylor" title="Nick Naylor">
       Nick Naylor
      </a>
      . He followed that with more humor about Washington in the form of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Little_Green_Men_(novel)" title="Little Green Men (novel)">
        Little Green Men
       </a>
      </i>
      , about the government agency investigating UFO sightings. His
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_Way_To_Treat_A_First_Lady" title="No Way To Treat A First Lady">
        No Way To Treat A First Lady
       </a>
      </i>
      has the president's wife on trial for assassinating her husband and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Florence_of_Arabia" title="Florence of Arabia">
        Florence of Arabia
       </a>
      </i>
      is about a do-gooding
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Department" title="State Department">
       State Department
      </a>
      bureaucrat in the Middle East. His one serious novel,
      <i>
       Wet Work
      </i>
      , is about a billionaire businessman avenging his granddaughter's death from drugs.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Thank You for Smoking
      </i>
      was adapted into
      <a href="/wiki/Thank_You_for_Smoking_(film)" title="Thank You for Smoking (film)">
       a movie
      </a>
      written and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Jason_Reitman" title="Jason Reitman">
       Jason Reitman
      </a>
      , and starring
      <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Eckhart" title="Aaron Eckhart">
       Aaron Eckhart
      </a>
      . It was released on 17 March 2006.
     </p>
     <p>
      Buckley also wrote the non-fiction
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Steaming_To_Bamboola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Steaming To Bamboola (page does not exist)">
        Steaming To Bamboola
       </a>
      </i>
      , about the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchant_marine" title="Merchant marine">
       merchant marine
      </a>
      , as well as contributed to an oral history of
      <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Connecticut" title="Milford, Connecticut">
       Milford, Connecticut
      </a>
      , and is an editor at
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">
        Forbes
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine. Buckley has written for many national newspapers and magazines, including
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">
        The Wall Street Journal
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">
        The Washington Post
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Monthly" title="The Atlantic Monthly">
        The Atlantic Monthly
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_(magazine)" title="Smithsonian (magazine)">
        Smithsonian
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_News_%26_World_Report" title="US News &amp; World Report">
        US News &amp; World Report
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">
        Vanity Fair
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vogue_(magazine)" title="Vogue (magazine)">
        Vogue
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conde_Nast_Traveler" title="Conde Nast Traveler">
        Conde Nast Traveler
       </a>
      </i>
      and numerous humorous essays in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">
        The New Yorker
       </a>
      </i>
      .

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Obama_endorsement">
       Obama endorsement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Buckley_(novelist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Obama endorsement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For a brief time in summer and fall 2008, Christopher Buckley also wrote the back-page column for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">
        National Review
       </a>
      </i>
      , the conservative magazine founded by his father. This came to an end after Buckley endorsed the 2008
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">
       Democratic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">
       presidential nominee
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
       Barack Obama
      </a>
      in October 2008. Buckley's endorsement, entitled "Sorry Dad, I'm Voting for Obama",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      appeared in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Beast" title="The Daily Beast">
        The Daily Beast
       </a>
      </i>
      . He chose
      <i>
       The Daily Beast
      </i>
      to avoid complications with
      <i>
       National Review
      </i>
      . After many readers and contributors expressed their displeasure, Buckley resigned from
      <i>
       National Review
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reader_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reader-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Buckley last wrote a column for
      <i>
       The Daily Beast
      </i>
      in April 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Family">
       Family
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      An only child, Buckley found his mother easier to talk to than his father because of her attitude toward religion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11

     <p>
      He first married Lucy Gregg, daughter of
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Gregg" title="Donald Gregg">
       Donald Gregg
      </a>
      , who served as assistant to Vice President Bush for national security affairs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They have two children, Caitlin and William (born in 1988 and 1991). He also has a son Jonathan (born 2000), from a relationship with former Random House publicist Irina Woelfle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Buckley and Gregg divorced in spring 2011.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [

          citation needed

        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">
        New York Post
       </a>
