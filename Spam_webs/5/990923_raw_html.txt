     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">
       Jainism
      </a>
      does not support belief in a
      <a href="/wiki/Creator_deity" title="Creator deity">
       creator deity
      </a>
      . According to Jain doctrine, the
      <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">
       universe
      </a>
      and its constituentssoul, matter, space, time, and principles of motionhave always existed. All the constituents and actions are governed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Law" title="Universal Law">
       universal
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_laws" title="Natural laws">
       natural laws
      </a>
      . It is not possible to create matter out of nothing and hence the sum total of matter in the universe remains the same (similar to law of
      <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">
       conservation of mass
      </a>
      ). Jain text claims that the universe consists of Jiva (life force or souls), and Ajiva (lifeless objects). Similarly, the
      <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">
       soul
      </a>
      of each living being is unique and uncreated and has existed since beginningless time.
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Anone">
       <a href="#endnote_Anone">
        [a]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Jain theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">
       causation
      </a>
      holds that a cause and its effect are always identical in nature and hence a conscious and immaterial entity like God cannot create a material entity like the universe. Furthermore, according to the Jain concept of divinity, any soul who destroys its karmas and desires, achieves liberation/Nirvana. A soul who destroys all its passions and desires has no desire to interfere in the working of the universe. Moral rewards and sufferings are not the work of a divine being, but a result of an innate moral order in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cosmos" title="Cosmos">
       cosmos
      </a>
      ; a self-regulating mechanism whereby the individual reaps the fruits of his own actions through the workings of the karmas.
     </p>
     <p>
      Through the ages,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jain_Philosophy#Jain_philosophers" title="Jain Philosophy">
       Jain philosophers
      </a>
      have rejected and opposed the concept of creator and omnipotent God and this has resulted in Jainism being labeled as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nastika" title="Nastika">
        nastika darsana
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">
       atheist philosophy
      </a>
      by the rival
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_philosophy" title="Indian philosophy">
       religious philosophies
      </a>
      . The theme of non-creationism and absence of omnipotent God and divine grace runs strongly in all the philosophical dimensions of Jainism, including its
      <a href="/wiki/Jain_cosmology" title="Jain cosmology">
       cosmology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karma_in_Jainism" title="Karma in Jainism">
       karma
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moksa_(Jainism)" title="Moksa (Jainism)">
       moksa
      </a>
      and its moral code of conduct. Jainism asserts a religious and virtuous life is possible without the idea of a creator god.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jaina_conception_of_the_Universe">
       Jaina conception of the Universe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Jaina conception of the Universe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2265" data-file-width="1006" height="450" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg/200px-Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg/300px-Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg/400px-Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lokapurusha_Cosmic_man.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Representation of Universe in Jain cosmology in form of a lokapurusa or cosmic man.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Jain_cosmology" title="Jain cosmology">
       Jain cosmology
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jain_universe.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="366" data-file-width="533" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Jain_universe.JPG/200px-Jain_universe.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Jain_universe.JPG/300px-Jain_universe.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Jain_universe.JPG/400px-Jain_universe.JPG 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jain_universe.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Structure of Universe as per the Jain Scriptures.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Jain scriptures reject God as the creator of universe. Jainism offers an elaborate cosmology, including Heavenly beings/Devas. These Heavenly beings are not viewed as creators, they are subject to suffering and change like all other living beings, and must eventually die. If godliness is defined as the state of having freed one's soul from karmas and the attainment of enlightenment/Nirvana and a God as one who exists in such a state, then those who have achieved such a state can be termed Gods/Tirthankara. Thus, Mahavira was God/Tirthankara.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Jains, this
      <i>
       loka
      </i>
      or universe is an entity, always existing in varying forms with no beginning or end. Jain texts describe the shape of the universe as similar to a man standing with legs apart and arms resting on his waist. Thus, the universe is narrow at top, widens above the middle, narrows towards the middle, and once again becomes broad at the bottom.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Bnone">
       <a href="#endnote_Bnone">
        [b]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wheel_of_time">
       Wheel of time
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wheel of time">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="798" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg/300px-Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg/450px-Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg/600px-Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jain_Cosmic_Time_Cycle.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Jain Cosmic Wheel of Time
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to Jainism, time is beginningless and eternal. The cosmic wheel of time rotates ceaselessly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schubring.2C_Walther_1995_pp._204.E2.80.93246_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Schubring.2C_Walther_1995_pp._204.E2.80.93246-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This cyclic nature eliminates the need for a creator, destroyer or external deity to maintain the universe.
     </p>
     <p>
      The wheel of time is divided into two half-rotations,
      <i>
       Utsarpi
      </i>
      or ascending time cycle and
      <i>
       Avasarpi
      </i>
      , the descending time cycle, occurring continuously after each other.
      <i>
       Utsarpi
      </i>
      is a period of progressive prosperity and happiness where the time spans and ages are at an increasing scale, while
      <i>
       Avsarpi
      </i>
      is a period of increasing sorrow and immorality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Concept_of_reality">
       Concept of reality
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Concept of reality">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This universe is made up of what Jainas call the six
      <i>
       dravyas
      </i>
      or substances classified as follows 
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <b>
         Jva
        </b>
       </i>
       -
       <b>
        The living substances
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Jains believe that
       <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">
        souls
       </a>
       (Jva) exist as a reality, with a separate existence from the body that houses it. It is characterised by
       <i>
        cetana
       </i>
       (consciousness) and
       <i>
        upayoga
       </i>
       (knowledge and perception).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though the soul experiences both birth and death, it is neither destroyed nor created. Decay and origin refer respectively to the disappearance of one state of soul and appearance of another, both merely various modes of the soul.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <b>
         Ajva
        </b>
       </i>
       -
       <b>
        Non-Living Substances
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <i>
          <b>
           Pudgala
          </b>
         </i>
         or
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
           Matter
          </a>
         </b>
         - Matter is solid, liquid, gas, energy, fine karmic materials and extra-fine matter or ultimate particles.
         <i>
          Paramnu
         </i>
         or ultimate particles are the basic building block of matter. One quality of
         <i>
          paramnu
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          pudgala
         </i>
         is permanence and indestructibility. It combines and changes its modes but its qualities remain the same. According to Jainism, it cannot be created nor destroyed.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          <b>
           Dharma-tattva
          </b>
         </i>
         or
         <b>
          Medium of
          <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">
           Motion
          </a>
         </b>
         and
         <i>
          <b>
           Adharma-tattva
          </b>
         </i>
         or
         <b>
          Medium of Rest
         </b>
         - Also known as
         <i>
          Dharmstikya
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          Adharmstikya
         </i>
         , they are distinct to Jain thought depicting motion and rest. They pervade the entire universe. Dharma-tattva and Adharma-tattva are by itself not motion or rest but mediate motion and rest in other bodies. Without
         <i>
          dharmstikya
         </i>
         motion is impossible and without
         <i>
          adharmstikya
         </i>
         rest is impossible in the Universe.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          <b>
           ka
          </b>
         </i>
         or
         <b>
          Space
         </b>
         -
         <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">
          Space
         </a>
         is a substance that accommodates living souls, matter, the principles of motion and rest, and time. It is all-pervading, infinite and made of infinite space-points.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          <b>
           Kla
          </b>
         </i>
         or
         <b>
          Time
         </b>
         -
         <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">
          Time
         </a>
         is a real entity according to Jainism and all activities, changes or modifications are achieved only in time. Time is like a wheel with twelve spokes divided into descending and ascending: half with six stages of immense durations, each estimated at billions of "ocean years" (sagaropama).
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
          <a href="#cite_note-8">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           8
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
         In each descending stage, sorrow increases and at each ascending stage, happiness and bliss increase.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      These uncreated constituents of the universe impart dynamics upon the universe by interacting with each other. These constituents behave according to natural laws without interference from external entities.
      <i>
       Dharma
      </i>
      or true religion according to Jainism is
      <i>
       vatthu sahvo dhammo
      </i>
      translated as "the intrinsic nature of a substance is its true dharma."
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Cnone">
       <a href="#endnote_Cnone">
        [c]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Material_cause_and_effect">
       Material cause and effect
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Material cause and effect">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to Jainism,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cause" title="Cause">
       causes
      </a>
      are of two types 
      <i>
       Updan krana
      </i>
      (substantial or material cause) and
      <i>
       Nimitta krana
      </i>
      (instrumental cause).
      <i>
       Updan krana
      </i>
      is always identical with its effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example out of clay, you can only produce a clay pot; hence the clay is the
      <i>
       updan krana
      </i>
      or material cause and clay pot its effect. Wherever the effect is present, the cause is present and vice versa. The effect is always present in latent form in the material cause. For transforming the clay to pot, the potter, the wheel, the stick and other operating agents are required that are merely nimitta or instrumental cause or catalysts in transformation. The material cause always remains the clay. Hence the cause and effect are always entirely identical in nature.
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Gnone">
       <a href="#endnote_Gnone">
        [g]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Potter cannot be the material cause of pot. If this were the case, then Potter might as well prepare the pot without any clay. But this is not so. Thus a clay pot can only be made from clay; gold ornaments can be made only from gold. Similarly the different modes of existence of a soul are a result of activities of soul itself. There cannot be any contradiction or exceptions.
     </p>
     <p>
      In such a scenario, Jains argue that the material cause of a living soul with
      <i>
       cetana
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conscious" title="Conscious">
       conscious
      </a>
      entity) is always the soul itself and cause of dead inert matter (non-cetana i.e. without any consciousness) is always the matter itself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If God is indeed the creator, then this is an impossible predication as the same cause will be responsible for two contradictory effects of
      <i>
       cetana
      </i>
      (life) and
      <i>
       acetana
      </i>
      (matter).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This logically precludes an immaterial God (a conscious entity) from creating this Universe, which is made up of material substances.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_soul">
       The soul
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The soul">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to Jainism, Soul is the master of its own
      <a href="/wiki/Destiny" title="Destiny">
       destiny
      </a>
      . One of the qualities of the soul is complete lordship of its own destiny.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The soul alone chooses its actions and soul alone reaps its consequences. No God or prophet or angel can interfere in the actions or the destiny of the soul. Furthermore, it is the soul alone who makes the necessary efforts to achieve liberation without any
      <a href="/wiki/Divine_grace" title="Divine grace">
       divine grace
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Jains frequently assert that we are alone in this world.
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Hnone">
       <a href="#endnote_Hnone">
        [h]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Amongst the twelve contemplations (anupreksas) of Jains, one of them is the loneliness of ones soul and nature of the universe and transmigration. Hence only by cleansing our soul by our own actions can we help ourselves.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anne_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Anne-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Jainism thus lays a strong emphasis on the efforts and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freewill" title="Freewill">
       freewill
      </a>
      of the soul to achieve the desired goal of liberation.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jaina_conception_of_divinity">
       Jaina conception of divinity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jaina conception of divinity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/God_in_Jainism" title="God in Jainism">
       God in Jainism
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Siddha.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="505" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Siddha.jpg/200px-Siddha.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Siddha.jpg/300px-Siddha.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Siddha.jpg/400px-Siddha.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Siddha.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Image of a
        <i>
         Siddha
        </i>
        : the soul who attains Moksa; although the
        <i>
         Siddhas
        </i>
        (the liberated beings) are formless and without a body, this is how the Jain temples often depict the
        <i>
         Siddhas
        </i>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to Jainism, gods can be categorized into
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">
        Trthankaras
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Arihant_(Jainism)" title="Arihant (Jainism)">
        Arihants
       </a>
      </i>
      or ordinary
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kevala_Jnana" title="Kevala Jnana">
        Kevalins
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Siddha" title="Siddha">
        Siddhas
       </a>
      </i>
      . Jainism considers the
      <i>
       Devs
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Devas
      </i>
      to be celestial beings who dwell in heavens owing to meritorious deeds in their past lives.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arhats">
       Arhats
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arhats">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <b>
       Arihants
      </b>
      , also known as Kevalins, are "Gods" (supreme souls) in embodied states who ultimately become Siddhas, or liberated souls, at the time of their
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nirvana_(Jainism)" title="Nirvana (Jainism)">
        nirvana
       </a>
      </i>
      . An Arihant is a soul who has destroyed all passions, is totally unattached and without any desire and hence has destroyed the four
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Karma_in_Jainism#Ghatiya_karmas" title="Karma in Jainism">
        ghtiy karmas
       </a>
      </i>
      and attain
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kevala_J%C3%B1%C4%81na" title="Kevala Jna">
        kevala Jna
       </a>
      </i>
      , or omniscience. Such a soul still has a body and four
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Karma_in_Jainism#Aghatiya_karmas" title="Karma in Jainism">
        aghtiy karmas
       </a>
      </i>
      . An
      <i>
       Arhata
      </i>
      , at the end of his lifespan, destroys his remaining
      <i>
       aghtiy karma
      </i>
      and becomes a
      <i>
       Siddha
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="T.C4.ABrthankaras">
       Trthankaras
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Trthankaras">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">
        Trthankaras
       </a>
      </b>
      (also known as "Jinas") are Arihants who are teachers and revivers of the Jain philosophy. There are 24 Trthankaras in each time cycle;
      <a href="/wiki/Mahavira" title="Mahavira">
       Mahvra
      </a>
      was the 24th and last Trthankara of the current time cycle. Trthankaras are literally the ford makers who have shown the way to cross the ocean of rebirth and transmigration and hence have become a focus of reverence and worship amongst Jains. However it would be a mistake to regard the Trthankaras as gods analogous to the gods of
      <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">
       Hindu
      </a>
      pantheon despite the superficial resemblances in Jain and Hindu way of worship.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Trthankaras
      </i>
      like
      <i>
       Arhatas
      </i>
      ultimately become
      <i>
       Siddhas
      </i>
      on liberation.
      <i>
       Trthankaras
      </i>
      , being liberated, are beyond any kind of transactions with the rest of the universe. They are not the beings who exercise any sort of creative activity or who have the capacity or ability to intervene in answers to prayers.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Siddhas">
       Siddhas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Siddhas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Siddha_Shila.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="207" data-file-width="576" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Siddha_Shila.svg/450px-Siddha_Shila.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Siddha_Shila.svg/675px-Siddha_Shila.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Siddha_Shila.svg/900px-Siddha_Shila.svg.png 2x" width="450"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Siddha_Shila.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Siddhashila" title="Siddhashila">
         Siddhashila
        </a>
        as per the
        <a href="/wiki/Jain_cosmology" title="Jain cosmology">
         Jain cosmology
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Ultimately all Arihants and Trthankaras become Siddhas. A Siddha is a soul who is permanently liberated from the transmigratory cycle of birth and death. Such a soul, having realized its true self, is free from all the Karmas and embodiment. They are formless and dwell in
      <a href="/wiki/Siddhashila" title="Siddhashila">
       Siddhashila
      </a>
      (the realm of the liberated beings) at the apex of the universe in infinite bliss, infinite perception, infinite knowledge and infinite energy. Siddhahood is the ultimate goal of all souls.
     </p>
     <p>
      Jains pray to these passionless Gods not for any favours or rewards but rather pray to the qualities of the God with the objective of destroying the
      <i>
       karmas
      </i>
      and achieving the Godhood. This is best understood by the term 
      <i>
       vandetadgunalabhdhaye
      </i>
      i.e. we pray to the attributes of such Gods to acquire such attributes
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Fnone">
       <a href="#endnote_Fnone">
        [f]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <i>

      or the divine preaching assembly of a
      <i>
       Trthankara
      </i>
      .
     </p>

      <tr>

        </cite>
        <p>

          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">

            <span>

     <p>
      Worship of such gods is considered as
      <i>
       mithytva
      </i>

     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_Karmas">
       Nature of Karmas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nature of Karmas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Karma_in_Jainism" title="Karma in Jainism">
       Karma in Jainism
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Zydendos" title="Robert Zydendos">
       Robert Zydendos
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karma_in_Jainism" title="Karma in Jainism">
       karma in Jainism
      </a>
      can be considered a kind of system of laws, but natural rather than moral laws. In Jainism, actions that carry moral significance are considered to cause certain consequences in just the same way as, for instance, physical actions that do not carry any special moral significance. When one holds an apple in one's hand and then let go of the apple, the apple will fall: this is only natural. There is no judge, and no moral judgment involved, since this is a mechanical consequence of the physical action.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robert_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-robert-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Hence in accordance with the natural karmic laws, consequences occur when one utters a lie, steals something, commits acts of senseless violence or leads the life of a debauchee. Rather than assume that moral rewards and retribution are the work of a divine judge, the Jains believe that there is an innate moral order to the
      <a href="/wiki/Cosmos" title="Cosmos">
       cosmos
      </a>
      , self-regulating through the workings of karma. Morality and ethics are important, not because of the personal whim of a fictional god, but because a life that is led in agreement with moral and ethical principles is beneficial: it leads to a decrease and finally to the total loss of karma, which means: to ever increasing happiness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robert_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-robert-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Karmas are often wrongly interpreted as a method for reward and punishment of a soul for its good and bad deeds. In Jainism, there is no question of there being any reward or punishment, as each soul is the master of its own destiny. The karmas can be said to represent a sum total of all unfulfilled desires of a soul. They enable the soul to experience the various themes of the lives that it desires to experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuhn_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kuhn-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They ultimately mature when the necessary supportive conditions required for maturity are fulfilled.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tat_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tat-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hence a soul may transmigrate from one life form to another for countless of years, taking with it the karmas that it has earned, until it finds conditions that bring about the fruits.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hence whatever suffering or pleasure that a soul may be experiencing now is on account of choices that it has made in past. That is why Jainism stresses pure thinking and moral behavior. Apart from
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      , perhaps Jainism is the only religion that does not invoke the fear of God as a reason for moral behavior.
     </p>
     <p>
      The karmic theory in Jainism operates endogenously.
      <a href="/wiki/Tirthankara" title="Tirthankara">
       Tirthankaras
      </a>
      are not attributed "absolute godhood" under Jainism. Thus, even the Tirthankaras themselves have to go through the stages of emanicipation, for attaining that state. While Buddhism does give a similar and to some extent a matching account for Shri
      <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">
       Gautama Buddha
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
       Hinduism
      </a>
      maintains a totally different theory where "divine grace" is needed for emanicipation.
     </p>
     <p>
      The following quote in
      <i>
       Bhagavat rdhan
      </i>
      (1616) sums up the predominance of karmas in Jain doctrine:-
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        <cite>
         There is nothing mightier in the world than karma;
        </cite>
        <p>
         <cite>
          karma tramples down all powers, as an elephant a clump of lotuses.
         </cite>
        </p>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Thus it is not the so-called all embracing omnipotent God, but the law of karma that is the all governing force responsible for the manifest differences in the status, attainments and happiness of all life forms. It operates as a self-sustaining mechanism as natural universal law, without any need of an external entity to manage them.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jain_opposition_to_creationism">
       Jain opposition to creationism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Jain scriptures reject God as the creator of universe. 12th century crya Hemacandra puts forth the Jain view of universe in Yogastra as thus
      <sup class="reference" id="ref_Inone">
       <a href="#endnote_Inone">
        [i]
       </a>
      </sup>
      
     </p>

      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Historical_Vedic_religion" title="Historical Vedic religion">
       vedics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Samkhya" title="Samkhya">
       smkhyas
      </a>
      , mimimsas, Buddhists and other school of thoughts were analysed, debated and repudiated by the various Jain cryas. However the most eloquent refutation of this view is provided by crya
      <a href="/wiki/Jinasena" title="Jinasena">
       Jinasena
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adipurana" title="Adipurana">
       Mahpurna
      </a>
      as thus

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_of_Jain_non-creationist_theory">
       Criticisms of Jain non-creationist theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jainism_and_non-creationism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticisms of Jain non-creationist theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">
       Jainism
      </a>
      along with
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      has been categorized as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atheist" title="Atheist">
       atheist
      </a>
      philosophy i.e.
      <i>
       Nstika darana
      </i>
      by the followers of
      <a href="/wiki/Historical_Vedic_religion" title="Historical Vedic religion">
       Vedic religion
      </a>
      . However, the word Nstika corresponds more to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterodox" title="Heterodox">
       heterodox
      </a>
      rather than atheism. Accordingly, those who did not believe in Vedas and rejected
      <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">
       Brahma
      </a>
      as the creator of Universe were labeled as Nstika.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mrs. Sinclair Stevenson, an Irish
      <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">
       missionary
      </a>
      , declared that the heart of Jainism is empty since it does not depend on beseeching an omnipotent God for salvation. While fervently appealing to accept
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      , she says Jains believe strongly in forgiving others, and yet have no hope of forgiveness by a higher power. Jains believe that liberation is by personal effort not an appeal for divine intervention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Heart of Jainism was written from her missionary point of view without respecting Jain sensibilities.
     </p>
     <p>
      If atheism is defined as disbelief in existence of a God, then Jainism cannot be labeled as atheistic, as it not only believes in existence of gods but also of the soul which can attain godhood. As
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dundas" title="Paul Dundas">
       Paul Dundas
      </a>
      puts it  while Jainism is, as we have seen, atheist in a limited sense of rejection of both the existence of a creator God and the possibility of intervention of such a being in human affairs, it nonetheless must be regarded as a theist religion in the more profound sense that it accepts the existence of divine principle, the paramtm i.e. God, existing in potential state within all beings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Jaina position on God and religion from a perspective of a non-jain can be summed up in the words of Anne Vallely.
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        <cite>
         Jainism is the most difficult religion. We get no help from any gods, or from anyone. We just have to cleanse our souls. In fact other religions are easy, but they are not very ambitious. In all other religions when you are in difficulty, you can pray to God for help and maybe, God comes down to help. But Jainism is not a religion of coming down. In Jainism it is we who must go up. We only have to help ourselves. In Jainism we have to become God. That is the only thing.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anne_15-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-Anne-15">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           15
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </td>
