                         <p>
                          ANDERSON - Front-porch stargazers will be disappointed later next month if they're looking for a large, glowing Mars in the night sky.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Contrary to a PowerPoint file widely forwarded in e-mails, Mars will not steal the moon's spotlight on Aug. 27. The e-mail urges stargazers to get out their telescopes at the beginning of August to see Mars as close to Earth as it has been in 5,000 years.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Yes, Mars is modestly brighter than usual, but it's nothing out of the ordinary, and Mars was closer to Earth as recently as January than it is projected to be at any time in August, said Mark Leising, professor of physics and astronomy at Clemson University.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "You will see Mars right after dark in the western sky just after sun sets," he said. "But it won't be anything special. It won't be spectacular."
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Venus, Saturn and Mars will be visible after the sun sets, with a sliver of moon illuminating the three planets, on Aug. 12-13, but that's a far cry from the PowerPoint's claims that Mars will seem as large as the moon to the naked eye, Leising said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "It's one of these forwards of e-mail addresses that aren't true, or aren't true anymore," Leising said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          The hoax originated seven years ago, another time when Mars and Earth got close, and has resurfaced multiple times.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "The closest approach Mars made was in 2003, and there was quite a bit of a deal made about it then," Leising said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          According to NASA, the distance between Mars and Earth depends on the positions of the two planets in their orbits, and the distance between the two can be as small as about 34 million miles.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Mars becomes brighter at this stage of its almost two-year orbit around the sun. A microscope is needed to notice a difference in the planet's brightness and its size compared with the moon, said Bob Fries, who taught astronomy at Anderson University for 35 years.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "Through a big enough telescope, Mars would have seemed about the same size to your unaided eye, which of course is comparing apples to oranges, isn't it?" Fries said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          He learned about the hoax from a person whose "intellect I respect," he said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "The person actually came back within a day and said, This is a hoax, isn't it?' I said, I don't know whether it's a hoax or a misunderstanding, but whatever it is, it's wrong.' "
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          The hoax has reached the upper levels of astronomy. Zolt Levay, imaging resource lead for HubbleSite.org, said people who received the e-mails called Hubble employees about them. He doesn't find it surprising that so many people trust the e-mail.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "Sometimes people want to believe in unusual things," he said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Entertainment only encourages the beliefs, he added. "I think that 2012' film fueled a lot of this stuff, too."
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          The Mars PowerPoint reminds Fries of another astronomy hoax a few years ago that a view from spacecraft showed all nine planets lined up "like meat and veggies on a barbecue skewer. That never happens," he said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          It doesn't really matter to Daisy Airline, 52, of Anderson whether Mars appears or disappears  planets aren't really her thing.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          The same goes for Scott Boucha, 41, of Anderson.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "It doesn't really interest me," he said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Jeremy Dockins, 30, also of Anderson, rarely gives forwarded e-mails a second look.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "If I get something like, that I delete it," he said. "But something like (the e-mail about Mars) I might believe because it sounds true.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "Ain't no telling what they can do on the Internet these days."
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          So far, the makers of the PowerPoint have a lot of people looking for a moon-size Mars next month.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          The distribution of the false information is not in vain, Leising said.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          "It's probably more a lesson in spam than it is in astronomy."
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Visit hubblesite.org to view more images of Mars.
                         </p>
