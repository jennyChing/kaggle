          What Will a Teen Do to Earn a Smartphone?

          <p>
           Eddie Grabovsky doesn't take his Android phone for granted.
          </p>
          <p>
           The 13-year-old eighth-grader in Monroe, Conn., pleaded with his parents last year for an upgrade to his bare-bones LG cellphone after seeing many of his classmates using fancier smartphones with email and Internet access.
          </p>
          <p>
           "He'd been bugging us about his friends having iPhones," says his father,
           <!--
-->
           Bazil Grabovsky,
           <!--
-->
           46, who works in sales for Melissa &amp; Doug, a Wilton, Conn., toy company. Mr. Grabovsky says he recognized that, for schoolwork, Eddie would be better off with a phone that offered Internet access because many of his tasks required research and data mining.
          </p>

          <p>
           Eddie's father and mother, Kristine, 45, told their son: "If that's what you want, then we'll take care of it for you, but you'll have added responsibilities."
          </p>
          <p>
           Last Christmas, the Grabovskys gave Eddie a Samsung Galaxy S3 phone, and Eddie's responsibilities swelled from occasional yard chores to more systematic weekly requirements. They include collecting trash around the house and putting bins out to the curb each Wednesday night for pickup the next dayhis least favorite chore. "It's dirty," he says.
          </p>

          <p>
           Every other day, he cleans out the cage belonging to family parrot Kiwi, a green Hahn's Macaw, and puts down fresh paper. Eddie is also expected to help with seasonal outdoor chores, whether it is mowing the lawn every week in the summertime, raking leaves in the fall, or shoveling snow in the wintertime.
          </p>
          <p>
           Eddie's parents pay him for extra tasks that are more involved. In the fall, he helped stack the firewood logs that his father split. For two days of stacking he was paid $30, says Mr. Grabovsky. He also recently trimmed and thinned out a few rows of Black Locust saplings around the house. That job, which took a day, netted him $20.
          </p>
          <p>
           This summer, Eddie amped up his earnings by about $35 a week with two jobs. One was planting pachysandra and sifting soil at a local wholesale plant nursery; the other involved pool maintenance at a neighbor's house.
          </p>
          <p>
           The money he earns goes into his own bank account. His parents expect him to save one-fourth, which they match, for his future college education.
          </p>

          <p>
           Mr. Grabovsky says the Samsung smartphone cost about $50. The cost of adding it to the family's unlimited talk-and-text plan is an extra $35 a month. The parents cover the additional cost in exchange for the added chores.
          </p>
          <p>
           The rules of engagement: The phone stays downstairs at night, and Eddie knows his parents can check it at any time to see what he's doing and who he's texting. Mr. Grabovsky says he and his wife have looked on occasion.
          </p>
          <p>
           So far, however, they haven't had to set any time limits on Eddie's phone use. "He is not addicted to texting or talking on it all the time," says Mr. Grabovsky. "That may change at some point, but for now we are thankful."
          </p>
