       <h1 class="entry-title">
        Fundraising Secret #26: You gotta do it yourself
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2008-11-04T09:53:21+00:00">
         November 4, 2008
        </span>
        by
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://fundraisingcoach.com/author/marc-a-pitman-2/" rel="author">
           Marc A. Pitman
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="post-comments">
         <a href="http://fundraisingcoach.com/2008/11/04/fundraising-secret-26-you-gotta-do-it-yourself/#comments">
          9 Comments
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         This week I've had two conversations with founders of nonprofits. I've had to tell both the unwelcome truth:
         <strong>
          you're going to have to do the fundraising yourself
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         This was a bitter pill for both of them. You see, they'd created their nonprofit because of their passion. They saw a need and they filled it. And they love what they do.
        </p>
        <p>
         But filling that need takes money.
        </p>
        <p>
         Both founders had hired people to do the fundraising for them. And both founders were pretty upset that neither were as effective as they had been. After all, they'd given their employee the same list they used, hadn't they? What was the problem.
        </p>
        <p>
         The biggest problem in each case was that the founders put off asking as long as they could. And they were a bit put off that they even needed to ask. Wasn't their cause self-evident? Neither had really factored fundraising into their original plans. So they only did it when the need was most pressing. They paid many of the costs out of their own resources.
        </p>
        <p>
         By the time they hired their new employees, they were behind the eight ball, desperately in need of money. And they had unrealistic expectations. They didn't realize how steep the learning curve would be for their new employees. They just thought the funding problem would be magically fixed.
        </p>
        <p>
         The reality is
         <strong>
          they still had to do the fundraising themselves
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         I see similar situations come up in the way some people hire consultants. They have a money problem, so they hire a consultant to come in and raise a lot of money to fix it. They have a hole that needs filling so they hire someone to fill it
         <strong>
          but they don't want to learn how to stop digging the hole
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         (That, by the way, is why I don't do consulting. There are a lot of
         <i>
          wonderful
         </i>
         consultants that are amazingly good at raising money. But when I've done it, it's been a thankless task. I'd prefer to work with people that want to learn. That is also why I wrote
         <a href="http://fundraisingcoach.com/ask-without-fear/" target="_blank">
          <i>
           Ask Without Fear
          </i>
         </a>
         , to give people a low-cost way to learn to do waht all nonprofits need to do.)
        </p>
        <p>
         If you're the founder of a nonprofit, or even a CEO or Executive Director,
         <strong>
          you
          <i>
           will
          </i>
          have to fundraise.
         </strong>
         No matter how wonderful your staff is, you have a unique way of telling the story. And donors want to hear from you.
        </p>
        <p>
         You can hire an amazing staff, and a great coach :), but
         <strong>
          you're still going to have to do some of the fundraising yourself
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         And you can have a really good time doing it. Honestly!
        </p>
