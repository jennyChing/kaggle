     <p>
      <b>
       tzi
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       German pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">
        [tsi]
       </a>
      </span>
      <small class="nowrap metadata">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/File:De-Oetzi-pronunciation.ogg" title="File:De-Oetzi-pronunciation.ogg">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/>
       </a>
       <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/De-Oetzi-pronunciation.ogg" title="De-Oetzi-pronunciation.ogg">
        listen
       </a>
       )
      </small>
      ; also called
      <b>
       tzi the Iceman
      </b>
      , the
      <b>
       Similaun Man
      </b>
      , the
      <b>
       Man from Hauslabjoch
      </b>
      ,
      <i>
       <b>
        Homo tyrolensis
       </b>
      </i>
      , and the
      <b>
       Hauslabjoch mummy
      </b>
      ) is a well-preserved
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_mummy" title="Natural mummy">
       natural mummy
      </a>
      of a man who lived around 3,300 BCE,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snowshoes_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-snowshoes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      more precisely between 3359 and 3105 BCE, with a 66% chance that he died between 3239 and 3105 BCE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radiocarbondating_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-radiocarbondating-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The mummy was found in September 1991 in the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%96tztal_Alps" title="tztal Alps">
       tztal Alps
      </a>
      , hence the nickname "tzi", near the
      <a href="/wiki/Similaun" title="Similaun">
       Similaun
      </a>
      mountain and Hauslabjoch on the border between Austria and Italy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is Europe's oldest known natural human mummy, and has offered an unprecedented view of
      <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">
       Chalcolithic
      </a>
      Europeans. His body and belongings are displayed in the
      <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol_Museum_of_Archaeology" title="South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology">
       South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">
       Bolzano
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol" title="South Tyrol">
       South Tyrol
      </a>
      , Italy.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
       Discovery
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg">
        <img alt="tzi the Iceman half uncovered, face down in a pool of water with iced banks" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="450" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg/250px-OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg/375px-OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/1e/OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OetzitheIceman-glacier-199109a.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        tzi the Iceman while still frozen in the glacier, photographed by Helmut Simon upon the discovery of the body in September 1991
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      tzi was found on 19 September 1991 by two German tourists, at an elevation of 3,210 metres (10,530ft) on the east ridge of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fineilspitze" title="Fineilspitze">
       Fineilspitze
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%96tztal_Alps" title="tztal Alps">
       tztal Alps
      </a>
      on the AustrianItalian border. The tourists, Helmut and Erika Simon, were walking off the path between the mountain passes Hauslabjoch and Tisenjoch. They believed that the body was of a recently deceased mountaineer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The next day, a mountain gendarme and the keeper of the nearby Similaunhtte first attempted to remove the body, which was frozen in ice below the torso, using a pneumatic drill and ice-axes, but had to give up due to bad weather. The next day, eight groups visited the site, amongst whom happened to be the famous mountaineers
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Kammerlander" title="Hans Kammerlander">
       Hans Kammerlander
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Messner" title="Reinhold Messner">
       Reinhold Messner
      </a>
      . The body was semi-officially extracted on 22 September and officially salvaged the following day. It was transported to the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Innsbruck" title="University of Innsbruck">
       University of Innsbruck
      </a>
      , where it was recognized to be
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/primeval" title="wikt:primeval">
       primeval
      </a>
      the same day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the
      <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Germain-en-Laye_(1919)" title="Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye (1919)">
       Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye (1919)
      </a>
      , the border between
      <a href="/wiki/North_Tyrol" title="North Tyrol">
       North
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol" title="South Tyrol">
       South Tyrol
      </a>
      was defined as the
      <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">
       watershed
      </a>
      of the rivers
      <a href="/wiki/Inn_(river)" title="Inn (river)">
       Inn
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etsch" title="Etsch">
       Etsch
      </a>
      . However, near Tisenjoch the (now withdrawn) glacier complicated establishing the watershed at the time and the border was established too far north. Therefore, although tzi's find site drains to the Austrian side, surveys in October 1991 showed that the body had been located 92.56 metres (101.22yd) inside Italian territory as delineated in 1919

      The province of South Tyrol therefore claimed property rights, but agreed to let Innsbruck University finish its scientific examinations. Since 1998, it has been on display at the South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology in

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_analyses">
       Scientific analyses
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The corpse has been extensively examined, measured,
      <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
       X-rayed
      </a>
      , and dated. Tissues and intestinal contents have been examined microscopically, as have the items found with the body. In August 2004, frozen bodies of three
      <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">
       Austro-Hungarian
      </a>
      soldiers killed during the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Matteo" title="Battle of San Matteo">
       Battle of San Matteo
      </a>
      (1918) were found on the mountain
      <a href="/wiki/Punta_San_Matteo" title="Punta San Matteo">
       Punta San Matteo
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Trentino" title="Trentino">
       Trentino
      </a>
      . One body was sent to a museum in the hope that research on how the environment affected its preservation would help unravel tzi's past.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Body">
       Body
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      By current estimates, at the time of his death tzi was approximately 1.65 metres (5ft 5in) tall,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Defrosted_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Defrosted-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      weighed about 50 kilograms (110lb; 7.9st)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mummy_Tombs:_Health_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mummy_Tombs:_Health-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was about 45 years of age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Defrosted_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Defrosted-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When his body was found, it weighed 13.750kg (30.25lb).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because the body was covered in ice shortly after his death, it had only partially deteriorated. Analysis of pollen, dust grains and the
      <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
       isotopic
      </a>
      composition of his
      <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">
       tooth enamel
      </a>
      indicates that he spent his childhood near the present village of
      <a href="/wiki/Feldthurns" title="Feldthurns">
       Feldthurns
      </a>
      , north of Bolzano, but later went to live in valleys about 50 kilometres farther north.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Analysis by Franco Rollo's group at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Camerino" title="University of Camerino">
       University of Camerino
      </a>
      has shown that tzi's
      <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">
       mitochondrial DNA
      </a>
      belongs to the K1 subcluster of the mitochondrial
      <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_K_(mtDNA)" title="Haplogroup K (mtDNA)">
       haplogroup K
      </a>
      , but that it cannot be categorized into any of the three modern branches of that subcluster.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rollo's group published tzi's complete mtDNA sequence in 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OetzitheIceman.jpg">
        <img alt="The Iceman from the chest up lying on stainless steel table, with his left arm across his body just between the top of his right shoulder and under his chin" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="179" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/OetzitheIceman.jpg/170px-OetzitheIceman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/39/OetzitheIceman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/39/OetzitheIceman.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OetzitheIceman.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        tzi the Iceman, now housed at the South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology in
        <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">
         Bolzano
        </a>
        , Italy
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Analysis of tzi's intestinal contents showed two meals (the last one consumed about eight hours before his death), one of
      <a href="/wiki/Chamois" title="Chamois">
       chamois
      </a>
      meat, the other of
      <a href="/wiki/Red_deer" title="Red deer">
       red deer
      </a>
      and herb bread. Both were eaten with grain as well as roots and fruits. The grain from both meals was a highly processed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einkorn" title="Einkorn">
       einkorn
      </a>
      wheat bran,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      quite possibly eaten in the form of bread. In the proximity of the body, and thus possibly originating from the Iceman's provisions, chaff and grains of einkorn and
      <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">
       barley
      </a>
      , and seeds of
      <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">
       flax
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Poppy" title="Poppy">
       poppy
      </a>
      were discovered, as well as kernels of
      <a href="/wiki/Prunus_spinosa" title="Prunus spinosa">
       sloes
      </a>
      (small plumlike fruits of the blackthorn tree) and various seeds of berries growing in the wild.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Hair_analysis" title="Hair analysis">
       Hair analysis
      </a>
      was used to examine his diet from several months before.
     </p>
     <p>
      Pollen in the first meal showed that it had been consumed in a mid-altitude
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">
       conifer
      </a>
      forest, and other pollens indicated the presence of wheat and
      <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">
       legumes
      </a>
      , which may have been domesticated crops. Pollen grains of
      <a href="/wiki/Ostrya" title="Ostrya">
       hop-hornbeam
      </a>
      were also discovered. The pollen was very well preserved, with the cells inside remaining intact, indicating that it had been fresh (a few hours old) at the time of tzi's death, which places the event in the spring. Einkorn wheat is harvested in the late summer, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sloe" title="Sloe">
       sloes
      </a>
      in the autumn; these must have been stored from the previous year.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_computed_tomography" title="X-ray computed tomography">
       CAT
      </a>
      scan revealed that the stomach had shifted upward to where his lower lung area would normally be. Analysis of the contents revealed the partly digested remains of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibex" title="Ibex">
       ibex
      </a>
      meat, confirmed by DNA analysis, suggesting he had a meal less than two hours before his death. Wheat grains were also found.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-natgeo-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      High levels of both copper particles and
      <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">
       arsenic
      </a>
      were found in tzi's hair. This, along with tzi's copper axe blade, which is 99.7% pure copper, has led scientists to speculate that tzi was involved in copper
      <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">
       smelting
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By examining the proportions of tzi's
      <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">
       tibia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">
       femur
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_pelvis" title="Human pelvis">
       pelvis
      </a>
      , Christopher Ruff has determined that tzi's lifestyle included long walks over hilly terrain. This degree of mobility is not characteristic of other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_Age" title="Copper Age">
       Copper Age
      </a>
      Europeans. Ruff proposes that this may indicate that tzi was a high-altitude shepherd.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Using modern 3-D technology, a facial reconstruction has been created for the South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology in Bolzano, Italy. It shows tzi looking old for his 45 years, with deep-set brown eyes, a beard, a furrowed face, and sunken cheeks. He is depicted looking tired and ungroomed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disco_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-disco-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Health">
       Health
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      tzi apparently had
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whipworm" title="Whipworm">
       whipworm
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Trichuris trichiura
      </i>
      ), an intestinal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">
       parasite
      </a>
      . During
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">
       CT
      </a>
      scans, it was observed that three or four of his right ribs had been cracked when he had been lying face down after death, or where the ice had crushed his body. One of his fingernails (of the two found) shows three
      <a href="/wiki/Beau%27s_lines" title="Beau's lines">
       Beau's lines
      </a>
      indicating he was sick three times in the six months before he died. The last incident, two months before he died, lasted about two weeks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, it was found that his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">
       epidermis
      </a>
      , the outer skin layer, was missing, a natural process from his mummification in ice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mummy_Tombs:_Health_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mummy_Tombs:_Health-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      tzi's teeth showed considerable internal deterioration from cavities. These oral pathologies may have been brought about by his grain-heavy, high carbohydrate diet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      DNA analysis in February 2012 revealed that tzi was
      <a href="/wiki/Lactose_intolerance" title="Lactose intolerance">
       lactose intolerant
      </a>
      , supporting the theory that lactose intolerance was still common at that time, despite the increasing spread of agriculture and dairying.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Skeletal_details_and_tattooing">
       Skeletal details and tattooing
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      tzi had several carbon
      <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">
       tattoos
      </a>
      including groups of short, parallel, vertical lines to both sides of the
      <a href="/wiki/Lumbar_vertebrae" title="Lumbar vertebrae">
       lumbar spine
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Cruciform" title="Cruciform">
       cruciform
      </a>
      mark behind the right knee, and various marks around both ankles.
      <a href="/wiki/Radiology" title="Radiology">
       Radiological
      </a>
      examination of his bones showed "age-conditioned or strain-induced degeneration" in these areas, including
      <a href="/wiki/Osteochondrosis" title="Osteochondrosis">
       osteochondrosis
      </a>
      and slight
      <a href="/wiki/Spondylosis" title="Spondylosis">
       spondylosis
      </a>
      in the lumbar spine and wear-and-tear degeneration in the knee and especially in the ankle joints.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been speculated that these tattoos may have been related to pain relief treatments similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Acupressure" title="Acupressure">
       acupressure
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Acupuncture" title="Acupuncture">
       acupuncture
      </a>
      . If so, this is at least 2000 years before their previously known earliest use in China (c. 1000 BCE).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clothes_and_shoes">
       Clothes and shoes
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg">
        <img alt="Line drawing of a right shoe" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1041" data-file-width="1722" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg/170px-%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg/255px-%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg/340px-%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%C3%96tzischuh_2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An artist's impression of tzi's right shoe
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      tzi's clothes were sophisticated. He wore a cloak made of woven grass
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a coat, a belt, a pair of leggings, a loincloth and shoes, all made of leather of different skins. He also wore a bearskin cap with a leather chin strap. The shoes were waterproof and wide, seemingly designed for walking across the snow; they were constructed using bearskin for the soles, deer hide for the top panels, and a netting made of tree bark. Soft grass went around the foot and in the shoe and functioned like modern socks. The coat, belt, leggings and loincloth were constructed of vertical strips of leather sewn together with sinew. His belt had a pouch sewn to it that contained a cache of useful items: a scraper, drill, flint flake, bone awl and a dried fungus.
     </p>
     <p>
      The shoes have since been reproduced by a Czech academic, who said that "because the shoes are actually quite complex, I'm convinced that even 5,300 years ago, people had the equivalent of a cobbler who made shoes for other people". The reproductions were found to constitute such excellent footwear that it was reported that a Czech company offered to purchase the rights to sell them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, a more recent hypothesis by British archaeologist
      <a href="/wiki/Jacqui_Wood" title="Jacqui Wood">
       Jacqui Wood
      </a>
      says that tzi's "shoes" were actually the upper part of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowshoes" title="Snowshoes">
       snowshoes
      </a>
      . According to this theory, the item currently interpreted as part of a "backpack" is actually the wood frame and netting of one snowshoe and animal hide to cover the face.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snowshoes_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-snowshoes-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_and_equipment">
       Tools and equipment
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3126" data-file-width="1980" height="347" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg/220px-ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg/330px-ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg/440px-ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ReconstructedOetziAxe.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A modern reconstructed replica of tzi's copper axe
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Other items found with the Iceman were a copper axe with a
      <a href="/wiki/Taxus_baccata" title="Taxus baccata">
       yew
      </a>
      handle, a
      <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">
       flint
      </a>
      -bladed
      <a href="/wiki/Knife" title="Knife">
       knife
      </a>
      with an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ash_tree" title="Ash tree">
       ash
      </a>
      handle and a
      <a href="/wiki/Quiver" title="Quiver">
       quiver
      </a>
      of 14 arrows with
      <a href="/wiki/Viburnum" title="Viburnum">
       viburnum
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogwood" title="Dogwood">
       dogwood
      </a>
      shafts. Two of the arrows, which were broken, were tipped with flint and had
      <a href="/wiki/Fletching" title="Fletching">
       fletching
      </a>
      (stabilizing fins), while the other 12 were unfinished and untipped. The arrows were found in a
      <a href="/wiki/Quiver" title="Quiver">
       quiver
      </a>
      with what is presumed to be a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_string" title="Bow string">
       bow string
      </a>
      , an unidentified tool, and an
      <a href="/wiki/Antler" title="Antler">
       antler
      </a>
      tool which might have been used for sharpening arrow points.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There was also an unfinished yew
      <a href="/wiki/Longbow" title="Longbow">
       longbow
      </a>
      that was 1.82 metres (72in) long.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Davies-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition, among tzi's possessions were
      <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">
       berries
      </a>
      , two
      <a href="/wiki/Birch_bark" title="Birch bark">
       birch bark
      </a>
      baskets, and two species of
      <a href="/wiki/Polypore" title="Polypore">
       polypore
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">
       mushrooms
      </a>
      with leather strings through them. One of these, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birch_bracket" title="Birch bracket">
       birch fungus
      </a>
      , is known to have antibacterial properties, and was probably used for medicinal purposes. The other was a type of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tinder_fungus" title="Tinder fungus">
       tinder fungus
      </a>
      , included with part of what appeared to be a complex firestarting kit. The kit featured pieces of over a dozen different plants, in addition to flint and
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">
       pyrite
      </a>
      for creating sparks.
     </p>
     <p>
      tzi's copper
      <a href="/wiki/Axe" title="Axe">
       axe
      </a>
      was of particular interest. The axe's
      <a href="/wiki/Axe#Parts_of_the_axe" title="Axe">
       haft
      </a>
      is 60 centimetres (24in) long and made from carefully worked
      <a href="/wiki/Taxus#Uses_and_traditions" title="Taxus">
       yew
      </a>
      with a right-angled crook at the shoulder, leading to the blade. The 9.5 centimetres (3.7in) long axe head is made of almost pure copper, produced by a combination of
      <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">
       casting
      </a>
      , cold
      <a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">
       forging
      </a>
      , polishing, and sharpening. It was let into the forked end of the crook and fixed there using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birch-tar" title="Birch-tar">
       birch-tar
      </a>
      and tight leather
      <a href="/wiki/Lashing_(ropework)" title="Lashing (ropework)">
       lashing
      </a>
      . The blade part of the head extends out of the lashing and shows clear signs of having been used to chop and cut. At the time, such an axe would have been a valuable possession, important both as a tool and as a
      <a href="/wiki/Status_symbol" title="Status symbol">
       status symbol
      </a>
      for the bearer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_analysis">
       Genetic analysis
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A group of scientists have sequenced tzi's full genome and the report was published on 28 February 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keller2012_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-keller2012-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y-DNA" title="Y-DNA">
       Y-DNA
      </a>
      of tzi belongs to a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haplogroup_G_(Y-DNA)" title="Haplogroup G (Y-DNA)">
       subclade of G
      </a>
      defined by the SNPs M201, P287, P15, L223 and L91 (G-L91, ISOGG G2a2b, former "G2a4"). He was not typed for any of the subclades downstreaming from G-L91. G-L91 is now mostly found in South
      <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
       Corsica
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Analysis of his
      <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">
       mitochondrial DNA
      </a>
      has shown that tzi belongs to the K1 subclade, but cannot be categorized into any of the three modern branches of that subclade (K1a, K1b or K1c). The new subclade has provisionally been named
      <i>
       K1
      </i>
      for
      <i>
       tzi
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Multiplex assay study was able to confirm that the Iceman's mtDNA belongs to a previously unknown European mtDNA clade with a very limited distribution amongst modern data sets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autosomal_DNA" title="Autosomal DNA">
       autosomal DNA
      </a>
      he is most closely related to southern Europeans, especially to the geographically isolated populations of the two
      <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
       Mediterranean
      </a>
      islands of
      <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
       Sardinia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
       Corsica
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      DNA analysis also showed him at high risk of
      <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">
       atherosclerosis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lactose_intolerance" title="Lactose intolerance">
       lactose intolerance
      </a>
      , and the presence of the
      <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">
       DNA sequence
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Borrelia_burgdorferi" title="Borrelia burgdorferi">
        Borrelia burgdorferi
       </a>
      </i>
      , possibly making him the earliest known human with
      <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">
       Lyme disease
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keller2012_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-keller2012-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngautopsy_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ngautopsy-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A later analysis suggested the sequence may have been a different
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Borrelia" title="Borrelia">
        Borrelia
       </a>
      </i>
      species.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2012 paper by
      <a href="/wiki/Paleoanthropology" title="Paleoanthropology">
       paleoanthropologist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/John_D._Hawks" title="John D. Hawks">
       John Hawks
      </a>
      suggests that tzi had a
      <a href="/wiki/Archaic_human_admixture_with_modern_humans" title="Archaic human admixture with modern humans">
       higher degree of Neanderthal ancestry
      </a>
      than modern Europeans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 2013, it was reported that 19 modern
      <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">
       Tyrolean
      </a>
      men were related to tzi. Scientists from the Institute of Legal Medicine at
      <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck_Medical_University" title="Innsbruck Medical University">
       Innsbruck Medical University
      </a>
      had analysed the DNA of over 3,700 Tyrolean male blood donors and found 19 who shared a particular genetic mutation with the 5,300 year old man, which led them to identify the link.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Blood">
       Blood
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In May 2012, scientists announced the discovery that tzi still had intact blood cells. These are the oldest complete human blood cells ever identified. In most bodies this old, the blood cells are either shrunken or mere remnants, but tzi's have the same dimensions as living red blood cells and resembled a modern-day sample.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cause_of_death">
       Cause of death
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oetzi_Memorial.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1155" data-file-width="768" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Oetzi_Memorial.jpg/170px-Oetzi_Memorial.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Oetzi_Memorial.jpg/255px-Oetzi_Memorial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Oetzi_Memorial.jpg/340px-Oetzi_Memorial.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_speculation">
       Initial speculation
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It was initially believed that tzi died from exposure during a winter storm. Later it was speculated that tzi may have been a victim of a
      <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">
       ritual
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">
       sacrifice
      </a>
      , perhaps for being a
      <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">
       chieftain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rollo_2002_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rollo_2002-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This explanation was inspired by theories previously advanced for the first millennium BCE
      <a href="/wiki/Bog_body" title="Bog body">
       bodies
      </a>
      recovered from
      <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">
       peat bogs
      </a>
      such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Tollund_Man" title="Tollund Man">
       Tollund Man
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Lindow_Man" title="Lindow Man">
       Lindow Man
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rollo_2002_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rollo_2002-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_involving_struggle_followed_by_cold_death">
       Theories involving struggle followed by cold death
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2001 X-rays and a CT scan revealed that tzi had an arrowhead lodged in his left shoulder when he died,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a matching small tear on his coat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mummy_Tombs:_Scientific_Studies_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mummy_Tombs:_Scientific_Studies-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The discovery of the arrowhead prompted researchers to theorize tzi died of
      <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">
       blood loss
      </a>
      from the wound, which would probably have been fatal even if modern medical techniques had been available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Further research found that the arrow's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrow_straightener" title="Arrow straightener">
       shaft
      </a>
      had been removed before death, and close examination of the body found
      <a href="/wiki/Bruise" title="Bruise">
       bruises
      </a>
      and cuts to the hands, wrists and chest and
      <a href="/wiki/Head_injury" title="Head injury">
       cerebral trauma
      </a>
      indicative of a blow to the head. One of the cuts was to the base of his thumb that reached down to the bone but had no time to heal before his death. Currently, it is believed that the cause of death was a blow to the head, however researchers are unsure of what inflicted the fatal injury.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unpublished and thus unconfirmed DNA analyses claim they revealed traces of blood from four other people on his gear: one from his knife, two from the same arrowhead, and a fourth from his coat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Interpretations of these findings were that tzi killed two people with the same arrow, and was able to retrieve it on both occasions, and the blood on his coat was from a wounded comrade he may have carried over his back.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mummy_Tombs:_Scientific_Studies_45-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mummy_Tombs:_Scientific_Studies-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      tzi's unnatural posture in death (frozen body, face down, left arm bent across the chest) suggests that the theory of a solitary death from blood loss, hunger, cold and weakness is untenable. Rather, before death occurred and
      <a href="/wiki/Rigor_mortis" title="Rigor mortis">
       rigor mortis
      </a>
      set in, the Iceman was turned on to his stomach in the effort to remove the arrow shaft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In May 2012, researchers using
      <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">
       Raman spectroscopy
      </a>
      and atomic force microscopy concluded that tzi did not die immediately from his shoulder wound. They detected dried blood cells and possibly fibrin in a state of degradation from maturity, suggesting an established blood clot of more than a few days' age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interface_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-interface-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
         <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2014)">
          not in citation given
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The DNA evidence suggests that he was assisted by companions who were also wounded; pollen and food analysis suggests that he was out of his home territory. The copper axe could not have been made by him alone. It would have required a group tribal effort to mine, smelt and cast the copper axe head. This may indicate that tzi was part of an armed raiding party involved in a skirmish, perhaps with a neighboring tribe, and this skirmish had gone badly.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When the Iceman's
      <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">
       mitochondrial DNA
      </a>
      was analyzed by Franco Rollo and his colleagues, it was discovered that he had genetic markers associated with reduced fertility. It has been speculated that this may have affected his social acceptance, or at least that his infertility could have had social implications within his tribal group, which could have played a role in the chain of events that led to the confrontation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Burial_theory">
       Burial theory
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2010, it was proposed that tzi died at a much lower altitude and was buried higher in the mountains, as posited by archaeologist Alessandro Vanzetti of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome" title="Sapienza University of Rome">
       Sapienza University of Rome
      </a>
      and his colleagues.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to their study of the items found near tzi and their locations, it is possible that the iceman may have been placed above what has been interpreted as a stone burial mound but was subsequently moved with each thaw cycle that created a flowing watery mix driven by gravity before being re-frozen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bower2010_53-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bower2010-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While archaeobotanist
      <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Oeggl" title="Klaus Oeggl">
       Klaus Oeggl
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Innsbruck" title="University of Innsbruck">
       University of Innsbruck
      </a>
      agrees that the natural process described probably caused the body to move from the ridge that includes the stone formation, he pointed out that the paper provided no compelling evidence to demonstrate that the scattered stones constituted a burial platform.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bower2010_53-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bower2010-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moreover, biological anthropologist Albert Zink argues that the iceman's bones display no dislocations that would have resulted from a downhill slide and that the intact blood clots in his arrow wound would show damage were the body carted up the mountain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bower2010_53-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-bower2010-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In either case, the burial theory does not contradict the possibility of a violent cause of death as stated in the preceding theories.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_dispute">
       Legal dispute
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Italian law entitled the Simons to a finders' fee from the South Tyrolean provincial government of 25% of the value of tzi. In 1994 the authorities offered a "symbolic" reward of 10 million
      <a href="/wiki/Italian_lira" title="Italian lira">
       lire
      </a>
      (5,200), which the Simons turned down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_20080929_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC_20080929-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2003, the Simons filed a lawsuit which asked a court in
      <a href="/wiki/Bolzano" title="Bolzano">
       Bolzano
      </a>
      to recognize their role in tzi's discovery and declare them his "official discoverers". The court decided in the Simons' favor in November 2003, and at the end of December that year the Simons announced that they were seeking US$300,000 as their fee. The provincial government decided to appeal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mummy_Tombs_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mummy_Tombs-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition, two people came forward to claim that they were part of the same mountaineering party that came across tzi and discovered the body first:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Magdalena Mohar Jarc, a retired
       <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">
        Slovenian
       </a>
       climber, who alleged that she discovered the corpse first after falling into a crevice, and shortly after returning to a mountain hut, asked Helmut Simon to take photographs of tzi. She cited Reinhold Messner, who was also present in the mountain hut, as the witness to this.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jarc_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jarc-56">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         56
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Sandra Nemeth, from Switzerland, who contended that she found the corpse before Helmut and Erika Simon, and that she spat on tzi to make sure that her
       <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">
        DNA
       </a>
       would be found on the body later. She asked for a DNA test on the remains, but experts believed that there was little chance of finding any trace.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph:_Cold_Case_57-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph:_Cold_Case-57">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         57
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      According to The Telegraph report in 2005 the rival claims were heard by a Bolzano court. The legal case angered Mrs. Simon, who alleged that neither woman was present on the mountain that day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph:_Cold_Case_57-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph:_Cold_Case-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2005, Mrs. Simon's lawyer said: "Mrs. Simon is very upset by all this and by the fact that these two new claimants have decided to appear 14 years after tzi was found."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph:_Cold_Case_57-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph:_Cold_Case-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2008, however, Jarc stated for a Slovene newspaper that she wrote two times to the Bolzano court in regard to her claim but received no reply whatsoever.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jarc_56-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jarc-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004, Helmut Simon died. Two years later, in June 2006, an appeals court affirmed that the Simons had indeed discovered the Iceman and were therefore entitled to a finder's fee. It also ruled that the provincial government had to pay the Simons' legal costs. After this ruling, Mrs. Erika Simon reduced her claim to 150,000. The provincial government's response was that the expenses it had incurred to establish a museum and the costs of preserving the Iceman should be considered in determining the finder's fee. It insisted it would pay no more than 50,000. In September 2006, the authorities appealed the case to Italy's highest court, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Cassation_(Italy)" title="Court of Cassation (Italy)">
       Court of Cassation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mummy_Tombs_55-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mummy_Tombs-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 29 September 2008 it was announced that the provincial government and Mrs. Simon had reached a settlement of the dispute, under which she would receive 150,000 in recognition of tzi's discovery by her and her late husband and the tourist income that it attracts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_20080929_54-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC_20080929-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22.C3.96tzi.27s_curse.22">
       "tzi's curse"
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Influenced by the "
      <a href="/wiki/Curse_of_the_pharaohs" title="Curse of the pharaohs">
       Curse of the pharaohs
      </a>
      " and the media theme of cursed mummies, claims have been made that tzi is
      <a href="/wiki/Curse" title="Curse">
       cursed
      </a>
      . The allegation revolves around the deaths of several people connected to the discovery, recovery and subsequent examination of tzi. It is alleged that they have died under mysterious circumstances. These persons include co-discoverer Helmut Simon,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Konrad Spindler, the first examiner of the mummy in Austria at a local morgue in 1991.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To date, the deaths of seven people, of which four were the result of some violence in the form of accidents, have been attributed to the alleged curse. In reality hundreds of people were involved in the recovery of tzi and are still involved in studying the body and the artifacts found with it. The fact that a small percentage of them have died over the years has not been shown to be statistically significant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
