         <p>
          Aside from stirring a sizable amount of controversy due to its horrible lyrical content, last year's monster hit 'Blurred Lines' also attracted the wrong sort of attention due to its musical similarities to Marvin Gaye track 'Got To Give It Up'.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           When Gaye's family questioned publicly why he Gaye wasn't credited as a composer,
          </span>
          <strong>
           <span style="font-size: 1em;">
            Robin
           </span>
           Thicke
          </strong>
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           filed a pre-emptive lawsuit regarding the copyright infringement allegations. The family counter-sued, and both
           <strong>
            Pharrell Williams
           </strong>
           and Thickegave their depositions in April.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          The tapes from this day in court have been made public, and they are hilarious.
         </p>
         <p>
          First of all, Thicke flatly denied he set out to make a track inspired by 'Got To Give It Up', despite the following quote
          <a href="http://www.gq.com/blogs/the-feed/2013/05/robin-thicke-interview-blurred-lines-music-video-collaborating-with-2-chainz-and-kendrick-lamar-mercy.html" target="_blank">
           given to a GQ journalist
          </a>
          , last May.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           <span style="font-size: 1em;">
            "
           </span>
           Pharrell
           <span style="font-size: 1em;">
            and I were in the studio and I told him that one of my favorite songs of all time was Marvin Gaye's 'Got to Give It Up.' I was like, 'Damn, we should make something like that, something with that groove.' Then he started playing a little something and we literally wrote the song in about a half hour and recorded it."
           </span>
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Quite a damning quote; almost impossible to argue with, really. Yet, when presented with
          <em>
           his own words
          </em>
          in the court of law, Thicke aimed to distance himself completely from the creative process. "I was jealous and I wanted some of the credit", he told the court. "I tried to take credit for it later because he wrote the whole thing pretty much by himself and I was envious of that."
         </p>
         <p>
          Thicke also claimed the entire rhythm track was basically complete when he arrived in the studio, high as the Hollywood sign.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           "I was high on vicodin
          </span>
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           and alcohol when I showed up at the studio. So my recollection is when we made the song, I thought I wanted - I - I wanted to be more involved than I actually was by the time, nine months later, it became a huge hit and I wanted credit. So I started kind of convincing myself that I was a little more part of it than I was and I - because I didn't want him - I wanted some credit for this big hit. But the reality is, is that
          </span>
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           Pharrell had the beat and he wrote almost every single part of the song."
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/CZNA8ExCdZA" width="853">
          </iframe>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Compare and contrast. It's like a sleazy, sleazy exam.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Drug-taking pops up frequently through Thicke's deposition. In fact, any quote that slipped from Thicke's mouth in the past year-and-a-half can be put down to drug and alcohol use - especially the ones where he is stating his appreciation for Gaye. Like this nugget from an interview with Oprah Winfrey, during which he was drunk and droopy on Norco, "which is like two Vicodin in one pill", Thicke informs the court.
         </p>
         <p>
          You know how weird it is to be in a legal battle with my idol, with the person who inspires almost half of my music?
         </p>
         <p>
          Almost half of his music. That's a lot of inspiration.Thicke goes on to whitewash his entire press history further, stating he had "a drug and alcohol problem for the year" and "didn't do an interview sober."
         </p>
         <p>
          <img src="http://www.vmusic.com.au/content/Screen shot 2014-09-16 at 10.44.01 AM.png"/>
          <br/>
          <em>
           <c>
            Oprah's 'disappointed face' would be crushing.
           </c>
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Williams backed up Thicke's limited involvement in the writing, adding that he himself wrote those terrible lyrics, and not Thicke, as widely believed/assumed/hoped. At times, Williams took on a dismissive, cosmic tone; when asked if Gaye had influenced him, he quipped, "He's an Aries. I respect him."
         </p>
         <p>
          By far the most interesting part of Williams' deposition (aside from the "ewww, those are Pharrell's rapeylyrics" revelation) is his belief that he alone couldn't have made the song a hit:"It wouldn't be what it was - what it is today." Thicke's inherent punchabilitycertainly makes him suit the role better, but this isn't what Williams is getting at.
         </p>
         <p>
          When pressed on why it isThicke'svoice, and not "the bass line and the keyboard" that holds the track together, Williams explained: "
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           Because it's the white man singing soulfully and we, unfortunately, in this country don't get enough - we don't get to hear that as often, so we get excited by it when the mainstream gives that a shot. But there's a lot of incredibly talented white folk with really soulful vocals, so when we're able to give them a shot - and when I say 'we,' I mean like as in the public gives them a shot to be heard, then you hear the Justin
          </span>
          Timberlakes
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           and you hear the Christina
          </span>
          Aguileras
          <span style="font-size: 1em;">
           and you hear, you know, all of these masterful voices that have just been given, you know, an opportunity to be heard because they're doing something different."
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          The lawsuit continues and is likely to for some time, so until there is a legal resolution, it's best to give Questlove the final word,
          <a href="http://www.vulture.com/2013/09/questlove-on-his-new-album-with-elvis-costello.html" target="_blank">
           who told Vulture
          </a>
          last September.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           "There's a thin line, but for the sake of hip-hop culture: Look, technically it's not plagarised. It's not the same chord progression. It's a feeling. Because there's a cowbell in it and a fender Rhodes as the main instrumentation - that still doesn't make it plagiarised. We all know it's derivative. That's how Pharrell works. Everything that Pharrell produces is derivative of another song - but it's an homage.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           "If it were a case of melodic plagiarism, I would definitely side with the estate [...]I know the history of the "Elvising" of music. But I'm still siding with Pharrell and Robin on this one."
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          So there.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <em>
            - NATHAN JOLLY
           </em>
          </strong>
         </p>
