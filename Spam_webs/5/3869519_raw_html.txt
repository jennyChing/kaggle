      <hgroup>
       <h1>
        Anti-Dilma protests in Brazil
       </h1>
       <span class="byline">
        April 13 2015 at 11:08am
        <br/>
        By AFP
       </span>
       <a class="comment_call_to_action" href="#comments_start">
        <span class="icon icon-comment-o fs0">
        </span>
        Comment on this story
       </a>
      </hgroup>
      <div class="ctx_content">
       <!-- C-ON-TEXT_CONTENT_START -->
       <div id="article_container">
        <div class="article_column">
         <figure>
          <!-- Main Editorial Image -->
          <img alt="iol wld brazil -protest" class="pics" src="/polopoly_fs/iol-wld-brazil-protest-1.1844119!/image/1571135107.jpg_gen/derivatives/box_300/1571135107.jpg" title=""/>
          <span class="captions_credit_article">
           EPA
          </span>
          <span class="captions">
           Thousands of people take part in a demonstration against the government of Brazilian President Dilma Rousseff in Sao Paulo. Picture: Sebastiao Moreira
          </span>
          <span class="captions">
          </span>
         </figure>
         <!--PSTYLE=WT Web Text-->
         <p>
          Rio de Janeiro - Hundreds of thousands of Brazilians took to the streets on Sunday, venting anger over government corruption and economic drift a month after larger protests gathered more than a million people.
         </p>
         <p>
          With cries of Dilma out and corrupt government, marchers - many wearing the yellow and green jerseys of the national football team - called for President Dilma Rousseff's ouster and an end to impunity for corruption.
         </p>
         <p>
          Police put turnout at 585,000 based on a count from 100 cities, while organisers gave an estimate of 582 000, albeit not including what appeared to be by far the largest gathering in business centre Sao Paulo.
         </p>
         <p>
          Beyond the widening graft scandal at state-owned oil giant Petrobras, protesters also expressed displeasure over rising inflation and soaring utility bills.
         </p>
         <p>
          Those pocketbook issues - as well as the perceived erosion of workers' rights - have fuelled growing opposition to the leftist president, re-elected in October.
         </p>
         <p>
          Rio hosted two modest protests at the tourist magnet of Copacabana beach, where media put participants at about 10 000.
         </p>
         <figure>
          <img alt="iol wld_BRAZIL-ROUSSEFF-PROTESTS_" class="pics" src="/polopoly_fs/iol-wld-brazil-rousseff-protests-1.1844121!/image/3535002506.jpg_gen/derivatives/box_300/3535002506.jpg" title=""/>
          <span class="captions">
           Demonstrators hold a banner with a drawing of Brazil's President Dilma Rousseff during an anti-government demonstration in Copacabana in Rio de Janeiro. Picture: Pilar Olivares
          </span>
          <span class="captions">
           <span class="captions_credit">
            REUTERS
           </span>
          </span>
         </figure>
         <p>
          But an afternoon march in business hub Sao Paulo drew a much larger crowd of about 275 000 according to police.
         </p>
         <p>
          organisers of that march gave unofficial estimates of between 600 000 and 1.2 million, which would make the national total comparable with that of a month ago.
         </p>
         <p>
          It is very difficult to draw the masses out onto the streets every month, political analyst Andre Cesar told AFP, citing a lack of central leadership to galvanise a diffuse protest movement.
         </p>
         <p>
          Dianara Loubet, a 75-year-old yoga instructor, said we have come to show what is going on in Brazil - this government is doing nothing. The people must show their dissatisfaction.
         </p>
         <p>
          Marchers converged on the capital Brasilia, where some protesters hung a banner calling for the army to intervene.
         </p>
         <p>
          Similar protests on March 15 brought out more than 1.7 million people, according to police, although pollsters Datafolha questioned Sao Paulo figures seen as inflated.
         </p>
         <figure>
          <img alt="iol wld fora dilma-brazil" class="pics" src="/polopoly_fs/iol-wld-fora-dilma-brazil-1.1844123!/image/2262132389.jpg_gen/derivatives/box_300/2262132389.jpg" title=""/>
          <span class="captions">
           A man sells T-shirts with the sentence 'Dilma out', during a demonstration against the government of Brazilian President Dilma Rousseff in Sao Paulo. Picture: Sebastiao Moreira
          </span>
          <span class="captions">
           <span class="captions_credit">
            EPA
           </span>
          </span>
         </figure>
         <p>
          We want all of Brazil to come out into the streets (but) we are not here to break records, said Rizzia Arreiro, a 35-year-old Rio protester from the Vem Pra Rua (take to the streets) protest group.
         </p>
         <p>
          Public confidence in Brazil's political class has slumped with the detention or questioning of dozens of lawmakers and officials, including the treasurer of the ruling Workers Party over an alleged multibillion-dollar kickback scheme at Petrobras.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thomaz Albuquerque, a 38-year-old attending a Copacabana rally, pointed to political and legal reasons to call for Dilma's impeachment.
         </p>
         <p>
          She was the president of the Petrobras board during the worst phase of the 'Petrolao' graft scandal. That is reason enough, Albuquerque said.
         </p>
         <p>
          Rousseff is herself not under investigation, despite her former ties to the company, but the widening probe has fingered a swath of her party colleagues and close allies.
         </p>
         <p>
          A Datafolha poll out Saturday found that 63 percent of a 2 800-strong sample said Rousseff should personally be investigated but 64 percent said she would still retain office even under investigation.
         </p>
         <p>
          Eight in 10 Brazilians said Rousseff was aware of the dealings at Petrobras, a claim she vehemently denies.
         </p>
         <p>
          Rousseff's popularity ratings have crashed below 20 percent, not just because of the Petrobras affair but also as a result of a staggering economy after four years of anaemic growth.
         </p>
         <p>
          Rousseff has responded with budget cuts, but promised to protect welfare programs that form the foundation of support for her party.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           AFP
          </strong>
         </p>
