     <p>
      The
      <b>
       scene subculture
      </b>
      is a contemporary
      <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">
       subculture
      </a>
      which has been common in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      , Asia, and
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
       Latin America
      </a>
      from the late 2000s until the mid 2010s. People (most often in their teens to 20s) involved in this style are called "scene people," "scene kids," "
      <a href="/wiki/Fad" title="Fad">
       trendies
      </a>
      " or sometimes "scenesters"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-smh-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the US, "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosh" title="Mosh">
       moshers
      </a>
      ," "chavmos," or "chemos" in the UK, "coloridos" in Latin America, and "shamate" in China.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buzzfeed1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-buzzfeed1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_evolution_of_fashion">
       History and evolution of fashion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scene_(subculture)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and evolution of fashion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_.28late_1990s_to_early_2000s.29">
       Origins (late 1990s to early 2000s)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scene_(subculture)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins (late 1990s to early 2000s)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scene_kid2008.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1937" data-file-width="782" height="545" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Scene_kid2008.jpg/220px-Scene_kid2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Scene_kid2008.jpg/330px-Scene_kid2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Scene_kid2008.jpg/440px-Scene_kid2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scene_kid2008.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        German girl wearing
        <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">
         pop punk
        </a>
        fashion, associated with the early European scene subculture
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The scene subculture began in United Kingdom during the late 1990s and early 2000s when some members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Chav" title="Chav">
       chav
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      subculture began to experiment with
      <a href="/wiki/Alternative_fashion" title="Alternative fashion">
       alternative fashion
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finding_emos_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Finding_emos-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and took fashionable characteristics of
      <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">
       indie pop
      </a>
      , emo,
      <a href="/wiki/Rave_music" title="Rave music">
       rave music
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Punk_fashion" title="Punk fashion">
       punk fashions
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fashion originally included typical
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">
       pop punk
      </a>
      and skater clothing like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tripp_pants" title="Tripp pants">
       tripp pants
      </a>
      , stripes, tartan,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiky_hair" title="Spiky hair">
       spiky hair
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chucks" title="Chucks">
       Chucks
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vans" title="Vans">
       Vans
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Trucker_hat" title="Trucker hat">
       trucker hats
      </a>
      derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">
       grunge
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Skate_punk" title="Skate punk">
       skate punk
      </a>
      fashion. Older punks and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skater_subculture" title="Skater subculture">
       skaters
      </a>
      , however, looked down on these young trendies (as they were then called) for their inauthenticity and inability to skate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the internet, especially fashion and makeup tutorials on
      <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">
       YouTube
      </a>
      , scene fashion had spread to
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       America
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      by the mid-2000s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Typical clothing of this period included
      <a href="/wiki/Miniskirt" title="Miniskirt">
       miniskirts
      </a>
      , striped tights,
      <a href="/wiki/Hoodie" title="Hoodie">
       hoodies
      </a>
      , androgynous hairstyles and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skinny_jeans" title="Skinny jeans">
       skinny jeans
      </a>
      . Before 2005, these outfits were in predominantly darker colors like black or grey, but accented with the occasional brightly colored garment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finding_emos_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Finding_emos-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The name was originally derived from "scene queen", a derogatory term within the 1970s
      <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">
       glam rock
      </a>
      scene for any heterosexual musician who pretended to be gay and later applied to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poseurs" title="Poseurs">
       poseurs
      </a>
      within the UK
      <a href="/wiki/Goth_subculture" title="Goth subculture">
       goth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">
       heavy metal
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">
       punk subcultures
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the 2000s, this term was replaced with "scene kid", later shortened to "scene." It was widely used by members of the American
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skater_subculture" title="Skater subculture">
       skater subculture
      </a>
      to put down younger hangers-on viewed as being more interested in alternative or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Designer_clothes" title="Designer clothes">
       designer clothes
      </a>
      than
      <a href="/wiki/Skateboarding" title="Skateboarding">
       skateboarding
      </a>
      .

     <p>
      By 2008, the scene subculture had become a common sight in Britain and the US, superseding the earlier
      <a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">
       emos
      </a>
      , goths,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skater_subculture" title="Skater subculture">
       skater subculture
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosh" title="Mosh">
       Moshers
      </a>

       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>

        </span>

        <span>

       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buzzfeed1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-buzzfeed1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drainpipe_jeans" title="Drainpipe jeans">
       drainpipe jeans
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      cartoon print hoodies,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shutter_shades" title="Shutter shades">
       shutter shades
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pre-engagement_ring" title="Pre-engagement ring">
       promise rings
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      checked shirts, and lots of very bright colors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-smh-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>

        </span>
        1
        <span>

      <a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">

      <a href="/wiki/Preppy" title="Preppy">
       preppy
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">
       glam
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Users from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Network" title="Social Network">
       Social Network
      </a>
      site
      <a href="/wiki/Myspace" title="Myspace">
       Myspace
      </a>
      started to upload
      <a href="/wiki/Selfie" title="Selfie">
       Selfie
      </a>
      photos showing hairstyles with dyed hair and spikes, black skinny jeans, heavy silver necklaces, heavy makeup and flashy or black T-Shirts. At first this new trend was attributed to singer and fashion designer
      <a href="/wiki/Jeffree_Star" title="Jeffree Star">
       Jeffree Star
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Black_Veil_Brides" title="Black Veil Brides">
       Black Veil Brides

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falling_In_Reverse" title="Falling In Reverse">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escape_The_Fate" title="Escape The Fate">
       Escape The Fate
      </a>
      .

     <p>
      Often the scene subculture has been associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">
       emo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Post-hardcore" title="Post-hardcore">
       post-hardcore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Metalcore" title="Metalcore">
       metalcore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Deathcore" title="Deathcore">
       deathcore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">
       electronica
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">
       pop punk
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">
       indie rock
      </a>
      . Some groups and artists in music associated with the scene subculture include X Blake Freeman X, Never Shout Never!, K-oZZ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brokencyde" title="Brokencyde">
       Brokencyde
      </a>

       Blood on the Dance Floor
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

     <p>
      Much of scene's popularity and rise to notability is due to scene music uploaded to
      <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">
       YouTube
      </a>
      . Music videos, lyric videos, and sometimes fan uploaded videos, helped bring the Scene to what it is today. Either because the songs were loved or hated, they were shared, which helped bring it into the public's eyes. Songs such as Dot Dot Curve's "Scene Kid", X Blake Freeman X's "The Scene Is Dying", and Geoffrey Paris' "Scene Kids" are examples of songs that brought scene to what it is today.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Latin America, scene clothing and androgynous hairstyles are often worn by teenage fans of
      <a href="/wiki/Bubblegum_pop" title="Bubblegum pop">
       bubblegum pop
      </a>
      groups like
      <a href="/wiki/Restart_(band)" title="Restart (band)">
       Restart
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In response to the (sometimes violent) backlash against
      <a href="/wiki/Emo" title="Emo">
       emo
      </a>
      kids, especially in Mexico, teenagers of both genders began wearing bright colors and cartoon prints, rather than black.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From the late 2000s until the mid 2010s these fans, known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorido" title="Colorido">
       Coloridos
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      were a common sight in northern Brazilian towns like
      <a href="/wiki/Fortaleza" title="Fortaleza">
       Fortaleza
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 2015, however, bands and fans alike had moved away from the bright colors in favor of a darker look inspired by 1990s grunge and the 2010s
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hipster_subculture" title="Hipster subculture">
       hipster subculture
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
