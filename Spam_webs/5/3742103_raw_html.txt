In addition to being a hell of a lot of fun, the 1970s Euro-disco sensations known as Boney M. are an academic paper on gender and ethnicity in popular music&#8212;or three&#8212;waiting to happen. Boney M.&#8216;s best years were from 1974 to the early 1980s, a pretty healthy run for a genre that often favored one-hit wonders. 

Operating out of Germany, Boney M. were an outfit consisting of one man and three women, all four of whom were from the Caribbean and read as &#8220;exotic&#8221; in the lily-white Vaterland. (Liz Mitchell and Marcia Barrett were from Jamaica; Maizie Williams was from Montserrat; and Bobby Farrell was from Aruba.) As their producer, Frank Farian, later attested, Farrell made almost no vocal contributions to the group&#8217;s studio output, while Farian himself performed the male parts for the recordings. Farrell&#8217;s primary functions were to look awesome and (just as with the three women) to dance his ass off, often in that synchronized Spinners sort of way. The vocal hooks were often quite infectious, and the busy beat gave people something to dance to. When Boney M. were good, they were very, very good.

     <div id="entrytitle2">
      Appreciating the peculiar Euro-disco genius of Boney M.
     </div>
     <div id="clear">
     </div>
     <div id="catinfo">
      <strong>
       10.24.2014
      </strong>
      <br/>
      01:17 pm
      <br/>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Topics:
      </strong>
      <br/>
      <a href="http://dangerousminds.net/categories/category/dance">
       Dance
      </a>
      <br/>
      <a href="http://dangerousminds.net/categories/category/music">
       Music
      </a>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Tags:
      </strong>
      <br/>
      <a href="http://dangerousminds.net/tag/Boney-M.">
       Boney M.
      </a>
      <br/>
      <span class="socialImageFallback">
       <img alt="Appreciating the peculiar Euro-disco genius of Boney M." class="socialImage" src="http://dangerousminds.net/content/uploads/images/BoneyMsocialssdsdsds.jpg"/>
      </span>
     </div>
     <div id="text">
      <p>
       <img alt="" height="465" src="http://dangerousminds.net/content/uploads/images/BoneyMrope.jpg" width="465"/>
       <br/>
       <br/>
       In addition to being a hell of a lot of fun, the 1970s Euro-disco sensations known as
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/search/ref=as_li_qf_sp_sr_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;index=aps&amp;keywords=boney%20m&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=boxoffbof-20&amp;linkId=CYHTY3YD2BNFUJTZ">
        Boney M.
       </a>
       are an academic paper on gender and ethnicity in popular musicor threewaiting to happen. Boney M.s best years were from 1974 to the early 1980s, a pretty healthy run for a genre that often favored one-hit wonders.
      </p>
      <p>
       Operating out of Germany, Boney M. were an outfit consisting of one man and three women, all four of whom were from the Caribbean and read as exotic in the lily-white Vaterland. (Liz Mitchell and Marcia Barrett were from Jamaica; Maizie Williams was from Montserrat; and Bobby Farrell was from Aruba.) As their producer, Frank Farian, later attested, Farrell made almost no vocal contributions to the groups studio output, while Farian himself performed the male parts for the recordings. Farrells primary functions were to look awesome and (just as with the three women) to dance his ass off, often in that synchronized Spinners sort of way. The vocal hooks were often quite infectious, and the busy beat gave people something to dance to. When Boney M. were good, they were very, very good.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       <img alt="" height="550" src="http://dangerousminds.net/content/uploads/images/BoneyM4_96466c.jpg" width="465"/>
       <br/>
       <br/>
       They never did much damage in the U.S., but Boney M. were a force in Europe. Farian had a sense for how to get the most out of unlikely combinations of talents. His most notorious act (by far) was Milli Vanilli, who if you notice, followed a very similar template to Boney M., attractive black people pretending to sing vocal tracks
       <em>
        they had not sung in the studio
       </em>
       (to be fair, Boney M. generally
       <em>
        did
       </em>
       sing their own vocals in live settings). We encountered Farian a few months ago when we wrote about
       <a href="http://dangerousminds.net/comments/wow_milli_vanilli_the_opera">
        Wow, the Milli Vanilli opera
       </a>
       . As
       <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Farian">
        Wikipedia
       </a>
       blandly says of Farian, His tendency to create bands with a visual image distinct from the recorded musical performances led to controversy in the case of Milli Vanilli.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       <img alt="" height="408" src="http://dangerousminds.net/content/uploads/images/boneyMUntitled.jpg" width="465"/>
       <br/>
       In any case, 1975 wasnt 1990, so the media police were quite willing to let Boney M. persevere with their quasi-lip-synched presentationof course, Boney M. never won any Grammys. Their first hit, 
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/search/ref=as_li_qf_sp_sr_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;index=aps&amp;keywords=boney%20m%20bump&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=boxoffbof-20&amp;linkId=COWZ4USKFS26IWNG">
        Baby Do You Wanna Bump?
       </a>
        was inventive disco to be sure (ripping off the horn riff from Prince Busters 1964 ska hit Al Caponea song also homaged in The Specials Gangsters) but generic in terms of subject matter. With 
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/search/ref=as_li_qf_sp_sr_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;index=aps&amp;keywords=boney%20m%20baker&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=boxoffbof-20&amp;linkId=TRWSFKIYIYLVPR2F">
        Ma Baker
       </a>
        and 
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/search/ref=as_li_qf_sp_sr_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;index=aps&amp;keywords=boney%20m%20rasputin&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=boxoffbof-20&amp;linkId=2D3W2XTR3DQZJILB">
        Rasputin
       </a>
       , Boney M. cashed in on the exoticism implied in their groups concept.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       <img alt="" height="462" src="http://dangerousminds.net/content/uploads/images/Boney_M._-_Ma_Baker_(1977_single).jpg" width="465"/>
       <br/>
       <br/>
       The story of Ma Baker is likely the most interesting in Boney M.s catalog. The birth name of Ma Barker (not Baker) was Arizona Donnie Clark, and in the early 20th century her four sons committed enough violent crimes to be called the Barker gangMa Barker traveled with them as they terrorized the midwest. She was killed in a shootout with the FBI in 1935, and of all possible people J. Edgar Hoover called her the most vicious, dangerous, and resourceful criminal brain of the last decade. Now
       <em>
        thats
       </em>
       a resume! For whatever reason Farian felt that Baker sounded better than Barker (not that it matters, but I think he was wrong about this). So this track about a legendary American female crime lord was recorded by four black people from the Caribbean and overseen by a Germancalling the music ethnologists, there are monographs to be written here. (Probably worth pointing out right here that the B-side was a discofied take on the Yardbirds Still Im Sad which was
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X9fmleT7mxs">
        practically a Gregorian chant
       </a>
       in the gloomy original!)
       <br/>
       <br/>
       <img alt="" height="454" src="http://dangerousminds.net/content/uploads/images/BONEYMloveforsale.jpg" width="465"/>
       <br/>
       <br/>
       The exotic concept continued with Rasputin, which likely has the most hilarious lyrics in the Boney M. catalogfor instance, get this: Rasputin! Lover of the Russian queen, there was a cat that really was gone. Rasputin! Russias greatest love machine, it was a shame how he carried on! Alas, the Soviet Union banned the song, which probably didnt bother Boney M. too much.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       <em>
        Rasputin
       </em>
      </p>
      <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="349" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/9_T3x8qBoic?rel=0" width="465">
      </iframe>
      <p>
       <br/>
       Boney M. reached the height of their fame in 1978 when they released the double A-side single, Rivers of Babylon/
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GiX2PbrBXCQ">
        Brown Girl in the Ring
       </a>
       , a #1 in several European countries. To this day Rivers of Babylon remains one of the biggest selling singles of all time in UK chart history.
       <br/>
      </p>
