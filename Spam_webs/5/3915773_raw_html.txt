                                    Mexican official: CIA 'manages' drug trade
                                   </h1>
                                   <div>
                                    <h2 class="standfirst">
                                     Spokesman for Chihuahua state says US agencies don't want to end drug trade, a claim denied by other Mexican officials.
                                    </h2>
                                    <p class="meta">
                                     <span class="oryx-heading-profile">
                                      <a href="/profile/chris-arsenault.html">
                                       Chris Arsenault
                                      </a>
                                      |

                                      </a>
                                     </span>
                                    </p>

                                 </div>

                                  </figcaption>
                                 </figure>
                                 <div class="article-body" id="article-body">
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    <strong>
                                     <br/>
                                     Juarez, Mexico
                                    </strong>
                                    - The US Central Intelligence Agency and other international security forces "don't fight drug traffickers", a spokesman for the
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Chihuahua state government
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    in northern Mexico has told Al Jazeera, instead "they try to manage the drug trade".
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   A
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    llegations about official complicity in the drug business are nothing new when they come from activists, professors, campaigners or even former officials. However, an official spokesman for the authorities in one of Mexico's most violent states - one which directly borders Texas - going on the record with such accusations is unique.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p style="text-align: left;">
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    "It's like pest control companies,
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    they only control,
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    "
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Guillermo Terrazas Villanueva, the Chihuahua spokesman, told Al Jazeera last month at his office in Juarez. "If you finish off the pests, you are out of a job. If they finish the drug business, they finish their jobs."
                                   </span>
                                  </p>

                                   <tbody>

                                      </a>
                                     </td>
                                    </tr>
                                   </tbody>
                                  </table>
                                  <p style="text-align: left;">
                                   A spokesman for the CIA in Washington wouldn't comment on the accusations directly, instead he referred Al Jazeera to an official website.
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <strong>
                                    <span style="color: black;">
                                     Accusations are 'baloney'
                                    </span>
                                   </strong>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Villanueva is not a high ranking official and his views do not represent Mexico's foreign policy establishment.
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Other more senior officials in Chihuahua State, including the mayor of Juarez, dismissed the claims as "baloney".
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    "I think the CIA and DEA [US Drug Enforcement Agency] are on the same side as us in fighting drug gangs," Hector Murguia, the mayor of Juarez, told Al Jazeera during an interview inside his SUV. "We have excellent collaboration with the US."
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Under the Merida Initiative, the US Congress has approved more than $1.4bn in drug war aid for Mexico, providing attack helicopters, weapons and training for police and judges.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    More than
                                    <a class="internallink" href="http://www.aljazeera.com/indepth/interactive/2012/06/201262591438842304.html" target="_blank">
                                     55,000 people have died in drug related violence
                                    </a>
                                    in Mexico since December 2006. Privately, residents and officials across Mexico's political spectrum often blame the lethal cocktail of US drug consumption and the flow of high-powered weapons smuggled south of the border for causing much of the carnage.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>

                                      </p>
                                      <p style="text-align: center;">
                                       <span style="font-size: 10pt; color: white;">
                                        <strong>
                                         Meeting the Juarez cartel
                                        </strong>
                                       </span>
                                      </p>
                                     </td>
                                    </tr>
                                   </tbody>
                                  </table>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    <!-- PAGELOADEDSUCCESSFULLY-->
                                    "The war on drugs is an illusion," Hugo Almada Mireles,
                                    <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                     professor at the Autonomous University of Juarez and author of several books, told Al Jazeera. "It's a reason to intervene in Latin America."
                                    </span>
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; color: black; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                    "The CIA wants to control the population; they don't want to stop arms trafficking to Mexico, look at [Operation] Fast and Furious, he said, referencing a
                                    <a class="internallink" href="http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2012/06/21/what-is-operation-fast-and-furious-11-questions-answers.html">
                                     botched US exercise
                                    </a>
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; color: black; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                    where automatic weapons were sold to criminals in the hope that security forces could trace where the guns ended up.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; color: black; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                    The Bureau of Alcohol Tobacco and Firearms lost track of 1,700 guns as part of the operation, including an AK-47 used in 2010 the murder of
                                    <a class="internallink" href="http://www.tucsonnewsnow.com/story/14190801/n">
                                     Brian Terry
                                    </a>
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; color: black; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                    , a Customs and Border Protection Agent.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Blaming the gringos for Mexico's problems has been a popular sport south of the Rio Grande ever since the Mexican-American war of the 1840s, when the US conquered most of present day California, Utah, Nevada, Arizona and New Mexico from its southern neighbour. But operations such as Fast and Furious show that reality can be stranger than fiction when it comes to the drug war and relations between the US and Mexico. If the case hadn't been proven, the idea that US agents were actively putting weapons into the hands of Mexican gangsters would sound absurd to many.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <strong>
                                    <span style="color: black;">
                                     'Conspiracy theories'
                                    </span>
                                   </strong>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    "I think it's easy to become cynical about American and other countries' involvement in Latin America around drugs," Kevin Sabet, a former senior adviser to the White House on drug control policy, told Al Jazeera. "Statements [accusing the CIA of managing the drug trade] should be backed up with evidence I dont put much stake in it."
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Villanueva's accusations "might be a way to get some attention to his region, which is understandable but not productive or grounded in reality", Sabet said. "We have sort of 'been there done that' with CIA conspiracy theories."
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    In 1996, the
                                    <em>
                                     San Jose Mercury News
                                    </em>
                                    published
                                    <em>
                                     <a class="internallink" href="http://articles.latimes.com/2006/aug/18/opinion/oe-schou18">
                                      Dark Alliance
                                     </a>
                                    </em>
                                    ,
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    a series of investigative reports linking CIA missions in Nicaragua with the explosion of crack cocaine consumption in America's ghettos.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    In order to fund Contra rebels fighting Nicaragua's socialist government, the CIA partnered with Colombian cartels to move drugs into Los Angeles, sending profits back to Central America, the series alleged.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    "There is no question in my mind that people affiliated with, or on the payroll of, the CIA were involved in drug trafficking," US Senator John Kerry said at the time, in response to the series.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Other newspapers, including the
                                    <em>
                                     Washington Post
                                    </em>
                                    and the
                                    <em>
                                     Los Angeles Times,
                                    </em>
                                    slammed
                                    <em>
                                     Dark Alliance,
                                    </em>
                                    and the editor of the
                                    <em>
                                     Mercury News
                                    </em>
                                    eventually wrote that the paper had over-stated some elements in the story and made mistakes in the journalistic process, but that he stood by many of the key conclusions.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <strong>
                                    <span style="color: black;">
                                     Widespread rumours
                                    </span>
                                   </strong>
                                  </p>

                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    <!-- PAGELOADEDSUCCESSFULLY-->
                                    "It's true, they want to control it," a mid-level official with the
                                    <em>
                                     Secretariat Gobernacion
                                    </em>
                                    in Juarez, Mexico's equivalent to the US Department of Homeland Security, told Al Jazeera of the CIA and DEA's policing of the drug trade. The officer, speaking on the condition of anonymity, said he knew the allegations to be correct, based on discussions he had with US officials working in Juarez.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Acceptance of these claims within some elements of Mexico's government and security services shows the difficulty in pursuing effective international action against the drug trade.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; color: black; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                    Jess Zambada Niebla
                                   </span>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    , a leading trafficker from the Sinaloa cartel currently awaiting trial in Chicago, has said he was working for the US Drug Enforcement Agency during his days as a trafficker, and was promised immunity from prosecution.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="background-image: initial; background-attachment: initial; background-origin: initial; background-clip: initial; background-color: white; color: black; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;">
                                    "Under that agreement, the Sinaloa Cartel under the leadership of [Jesus Zambada's] father, Ismael Zambada and 'Chapo' Guzmn were given
                                    <em>
                                     carte blanche
                                    </em>
                                    to continue to smuggle tonnes of illicit drugs... into... the United States, and were protected by the United States government from arrest and prosecution in return for providing information against rival cartels," Zambada's lawyers wrote as part of his defence. "Indeed, the Unites States government agents aided the leaders of the Sinaloa Cartel."
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    The Sinaloa cartel is Mexico's oldest and most powerful trafficking organisation, and some analysts believe security forces in the US and Mexico favour the group over its rivals.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    Joaquin "El Chapo", the cartel's billionaire leader and one of the world's most wanted men, escaped from a Mexican prison in 2001 by sneaking into a laundry truck - likely with collaboration from guards - further stoking rumours that leading traffickers have complicit friends in high places.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    "It would be easy for the Mexican army to capture El Chapo," Mireles said. "But this is not the objective." He thinks the authorities on both sides of the border are happy to have El Chapo on the loose, as his cartel is easier to manage and his drug money is recycled back into the broader economy. Other analysts consider this viewpoint a conspiracy theory and blame ineptitude and low level corruption for El Chapo's escape, rather than a broader plan from government agencies.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>

                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    After an election hit by reported irregularities, Enrique Pena Nieto from the Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI) is set to be sworn in as Mexico's president on December 1.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   <span style="color: black;">
                                    He wants to open a high-level dialogue with the US about the drug war, but has said legalisation of some drugs is not an option. Some hardliners in the US worry that Nieto will make a deal with some cartels, in order to reduce violence.
                                   </span>
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   "I am hopeful that he will not return to the PRI party of the past which was corrupt and had a history of turning a blind eye to the drug cartels," said Michael McCaul, a Republican Congressman from Texas.
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   Regardless of what position a new administration takes in order to calm the violence and restore order, it is likely many Mexicans - including government officials such as Chihuahua spokesman Guillermo Villanueva - will believe outside forces want the drug trade to continue.
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   The widespread view linking the CIA to the drug trade - whether or not the allegations are true - speaks volumes about officials' mutual mistrust amid ongoing killings and the destruction of civic life in Mexico.
                                  </p>
                                  <p>
                                   "We have good soldiers and policemen," Villanueva said. "But you won't resolve this problem with bullets. We need education and jobs."
                                  </p>
