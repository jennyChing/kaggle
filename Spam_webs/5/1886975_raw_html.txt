        </a>
        in resource constrained environments such as the Internet of Things (IoT) and the day begins with Homenet exploring how we create better home networks based on IPv6.  And in the midst of that will be the IDR working group working to improve the Internets routing infrastruture! Heres what today looks like for us
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         NOTE:
        </strong>
        If you are unable to attend IETF 92 in person,
        <a href="http://www.ietf.org/live/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         there are multiple ways to participate remotely
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        We start in the 0900-1130 CDT block in the International Room where the Homenet working group will be meeting.
        <a href="https://www.internetsociety.org/blog/tech-matters/2015/03/rough-guide-ietf-92-all-about-ipv6" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         As Phil Roberts explained in his Rough Guide to IETF 92 post about IPv6
        </a>
        :
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          the
          <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/homenet/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
           Homenet working group
          </a>
          is doing a lot of interesting work producing open standards for protocols to implement robust networks in homes of the future, all based on IPv6. The topics include routing, addressing, naming, and security. Its exciting to see new standards work for such a potentially huge area for extending the reach of open standards in networks that matter to people around the world.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Beyond IPv6, were also monitoring Homenet for possibilities where DNSSEC and TLS can help improve the security of those home networks.
       </p>
       <p>
        As
        <a href="http://www.internetsociety.org/deploy360/blog/2015/03/deploy360ietf92-day-1/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         was curiously the case yesterday
        </a>
        , the 1300-1500 CDT session block does not contain any of the regular groups we follow, but you might find us in
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/httpbis/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         HTTPBIS
        </a>
        hearing about the next version of HTTP, in
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/netconf/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         NETCONF
        </a>
        learning about network configuration proposals (the
        <a href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/draft-ietf-netconf-zerotouch-02" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         zero touch provisioning draft
        </a>
        looks interesting), or over in
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/ace/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         ACE
        </a>
        understanding new ideas to make the Internet of Things (IoT) more secure.
       </p>
       <p>
        Speaking of IoT, the 1520-1720 CDT session block is one in which well be split across
        <em>
         three
        </em>
        different working group sessions, one of which will be IoT focused.  The 6LO working group, formally known as the
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/charter-ietf-6lo/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         IPv6 over Networks of Resource Constrained Nodes WG
        </a>
        , has
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/6lo/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         a packed agenda
        </a>
        looking at how IPv6 works in IoT environments.  Transmitting IPv6 packets over near field communications (NFC), security and privacy, multicast technologies and multiple discussions of the IoT bootstrapping process it all should make for an interesting discussion for those folks looking to get IP everywhere!
       </p>
       <p>
        Simultaneously over in the Far East Room,
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/idr/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         the Inter-Domain Routing (IDR) working group will be looking at ways to improve the Internets routing infrastructure
        </a>
        .  Andrei
        <a href="https://www.internetsociety.org/blog/tech-matters/2015/03/rough-guide-ietf-92-routing-resilience-and-security" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         wrote more about some of the routing discussions
        </a>
        happening at IETF 92. Im interested in the draft here about route leaks, as I find that area fascinating.
       </p>
       <p>
        However, Ill be over in the Gold Room (virtually, as I am remote for this meeting) for the DNS Operations (DNSOP) working group that
        <a rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         has a VERY packed agenda
        </a>
        looking at how to improve the operations of the Domain Name System (DNS).
        <a href="https://www.internetsociety.org/blog/tech-matters/2015/03/rough-guide-ietf-92-dnssec-dane-and-dns-security" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         As I wrote in my Rough Guide to IETF 92 post
        </a>
        , this session has a good number of drafts related to DNS security in general.  I expect there to be some vigorous discussion around the restriction of meta queries such as the ANY query.  There are multiple drafts on the agenda about reserving new top-level domains (TLDs) such as .onion, which inevitably gets discussion.  The QNAME minimization is important for DNS privacy/confidentiality and there are a range of other discussions that will be had related to making DNS work better, faster and be more secure.
       </p>
       <p>
        Well end the day in the 1730-1830 CDT block with the DANE Working Group focused on
        <a rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         the DANE protocol
        </a>
        and how it can be used to add a layer of trust to TLS and SSL certificates.   This is incredibly important work and while
        <a href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/92/agenda/dane/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         the agenda for toda
        </a>
        y has only one presentation about DANE and S/MIME, I expect
        <a href="http://www.ietf.org/mail-archive/web/dane/current/maillist.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         based on the strong activity on the DANE mailing list
        </a>
        that other topics will be brought up.
       </p>
       <p>
        When the sessions are all over, Chris and the many folks in Dallas will no doubt head to the IETF Social Event, while those of us who are remote will have a bit of break before heading into Day 3.  Speaking of attending remotely, please do remember that multiple options to participate are available at
        <a href="http://www.ietf.org/live/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         http://www.ietf.org/live/
        </a>
       </p>
