       <h2 class="entry-title">
        <a href="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/diagnosing-electric-power-tools/" rel="bookmark">
         Diagnosing Electric Power Tools
        </a>
       </h2>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="posted">
      <span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">
       Posted by
      </span>

     <div class="entry-content">
      <div addthis:title="Diagnosing Electric Power Tools " addthis:url="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/diagnosing-electric-power-tools/" class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style ">
       <a class="addthis_button_facebook_like" fb:like:layout="button_count">
       </a>
       <a class="addthis_button_tweet">
       </a>
       <a class="addthis_button_pinterest_pinit">

       <a class="addthis_counter addthis_pill_style">
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        This troubleshooting article is a companion article to
        <a href="/article/1049/Electric_Power_Tool_Parts_101.html">
         Electric Power Tool Parts 101
        </a>
        .Electric Tool Parts 101explains how the major parts of a power tool work.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Click the link above to visit the101article for additional information about electric power tool parts and what causes them to break down. This article will
        <strong>
         focus
        </strong>
        on
        <strong>
         diagnosing
        </strong>
        power tool service and performance problems.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="How to Diagnose Electric Power Tools" class="aligncenter" height="244" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2011/04/diagnosetrans.gif" width="270"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Once the cause of the malfunction or drop in tool performance is worked out, most of them can be fixed right on site.
        <strong>
         Visualizing
        </strong>
        the
        <strong>
         path
        </strong>
        that electrical energy follows through a power tool is the key to knowing why a tool only starts after being shook, why it doesnt start at all, or why sparks are flying out the dust vents.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <a name="powerpath">
         </a>
         Power-Path Logic
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Since electricity
        <strong>
         follows
        </strong>
        a physical logic through a power tool, it makes sense that most
        <strong>
         malfunctions
        </strong>
        in a power tool follow the
        <strong>
         same
        </strong>
        <strong>
         path
        </strong>
        . Most electrical tool problems are caused by heat, shorting, or wear
        <strong>
         along
        </strong>
        the path of electrical power in the tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Electrical power enters the tool at one end, follows the path of wiring and connections to the tools motor, is converted into physical energy, and leaves the tool at its working end.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Diagnosing most problems in an electric power tool means
        <strong>
         following
        </strong>
        <strong>
         the
        </strong>
        <strong>
         path
        </strong>
        that electrical power follows through the tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        For the sake of this troubleshooting guide, we call this way of thinking about tool diagnosis 
        <em>
         power
        </em>
        
        <em>
         path
        </em>
        <em>
         logic
        </em>
        .
        <strong>
         Power-path logic
        </strong>
        is really just simple, mechanical
        <strong>
         common
        </strong>
        <strong>
         sense
        </strong>
        , and it is the key idea needed to get to the root of most tool break downs.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Power-path logic goes like this:
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="more-10000">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         1.
        </strong>
        Power
        <strong>
         enters
        </strong>
        a power tool at its
        <strong>
         power end
        </strong>
        through an outlet or battery. From there it follows a path through the different parts of the tool. The
        <strong>
         order
        </strong>
        of the major
        <strong>
         parts
        </strong>
        along the power path is:
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        a) the
        <strong>
         power
        </strong>
        <strong>
         cord
        </strong>
        ,
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        b) the
        <strong>
         power switch
        </strong>
        ,
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        c) the
        <strong>
         carbon brushes
        </strong>
        ,
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        d) the commutator/
        <strong>
         armature
        </strong>
        , and
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        e) the
        <strong>
         field
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        For additional information on these tool parts, visit the article 
        <a href="/article/1049/Electric_Power_Tool_Parts_101.html">
         Electric Power Tool Parts 101
        </a>
        <a href="/articles/1049/Electric%20Power%20Tool%20Parts%20101" target="_blank">
         .
        </a>
        
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         2.
        </strong>
        Power tool
        <strong>
         problems
        </strong>
        that happen closer to the
        <strong>
         power end
        </strong>
        of the tool (the end where the electrical energy enters from an outlet or battery) are
        <strong>
         more
        </strong>
        <strong>
         common
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        This is because the parts closer to the power source are more
        <strong>
         likely
        </strong>
        to take the brunt of a power surge or heat
        <strong>
         damage
        </strong>
        . Also, parts farther down the path, like armatures and fields, are generally tougher than switches and brushes.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        So, diagnosis for most problems starts at the entry end and works its way up to the exit end.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         3.
        </strong>
        Many symptoms of a tool failure
        <em>
         do not
        </em>
        point to just one problem. Starting inspection at the entry end is always best if the source of the problem is unknown for two reasons.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        a) If the source of the problem is unknown,
        <strong>
         starting at the power end is best
        </strong>
        because the problems that occur towards the power end are more common.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        b) If the source of the problem is unknown, starting at the power end is best because it requires
        <strong>
         less
        </strong>
        <strong>
         dismantling
        </strong>
        of the tool, making the repair
        <strong>
         faster
        </strong>
        and
        <strong>
         easier
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         4.
        </strong>
        Some symptoms point to specific problems in the tool, in which case power-path logic is
        <strong>
         not
        </strong>
        needed for diagnosis.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        As might be expected, the steps for diagnosing most symptoms will include following the tools power path, but every problem is different.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Before that though, an explanation about power cords and this guide is needed.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="#top">
         [Back to top]
         <br/>
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <a name="powercords">
         </a>
         Power Cords
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Power cords are the point of entry of electrical energy for power tools that dont use a battery, but they are
        <strong>
         excluded
        </strong>
        from the bulk of this guide for two reasons.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1.
        <strong>
         Diagnosing
        </strong>
        a damaged or worn power cord is usually
        <strong>
         easy
        </strong>
        to do just by looking at the cord, and
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2. Most
        <strong>
         problems
        </strong>
        caused by power cords in a tool can be
        <strong>
         prevented
        </strong>
        by using the correct power cord and power source.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        The major problem caused by power cords is that they can cause heat in the tools motor by not supplying enough voltage.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        There are three major reasons why a power cord will make a tool overheat by supplying less power:
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1. The cord is
        <strong>
         damaged
        </strong>
        , cut or worn. Damaged, cut, or worn cords are easy to spot and replace.3
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Damaged Power Cord" class="aligncenter" height="221" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/images/worncord.jpg" title="Damaged Power Cord" width="221"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2
        <strong>
         .
        </strong>
        The
        <strong>
         wiring
        </strong>
        in the power outlet does
        <strong>
         not match
        </strong>
        the requirements of the tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Electric power tools should always be operated on outlets that
        <strong>
         match
        </strong>
        their
        <strong>
         specifications
        </strong>
        . A tools
        <strong>
         amperage rating
        </strong>
        indicates how many amps a tool can handle without overheating and what kind of outlet to use for a tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Or,
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        3. Because there is
        <strong>
         too much extension
        </strong>
        <strong>
         cord
        </strong>
        in use.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If
        <strong>
         too much extension
        </strong>
        cord is being used, the power supply can be very
        <strong>
         weak
        </strong>
        by the time the electricity gets to the tool. Luckily, charts like the one below show exactly how much extension cord should be used for each tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a name="chart">
        </a>
        Using a tools
        <strong>
         amperage rating
        </strong>
        and the
        <strong>
         gauge
        </strong>
        of its cord with this chart will show the length limits for that tools cord:
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Recommended Extension Cord Sizes
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Recommended Extension Cord Sizes" class="aligncenter" height="204" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/extension-cord-sizes2.jpg" title="Recommended Extension Cord Sizes" width="483"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="/article/1049/Electric_Power_Tool_Parts_101.html#powercords">
         For more information on Power CordsClick here
        </a>
        .
        <a href="/article/1049/Electric_Power_Tool_Parts_101.html#powercords">
         <br/>
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="/article/913/Replacing_Bad_Cut_or_Damaged_Power_Cords_on_Your_Power_Tools.html">
         For a Power Cord Replacement articleClick here
        </a>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        The remainder of this troubleshooting guide
        <strong>
         assumes
        </strong>
        that:
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1. Users are using the
        <strong>
         correct
        </strong>
        <strong>
         gauge
        </strong>
        of power cord for the power tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2. Users are operating power tools on outlets that
        <strong>
         match
        </strong>
        the tools
        <strong>
         amperage
        </strong>
        <strong>
         rating
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        3. Users are using the
        <strong>
         right
        </strong>
        <strong>
         length
        </strong>
        of power cord.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        With power cord issues ruled out from the beginning, this guide can focus on what might go wrong inside a tool
        <em>
         past
        </em>
        the cord.
       </span>
      </p>

      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a name="troubleshooting">
        </a>
        <strong>
         Troubleshooting By Symptom
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        These are the most
        <strong>
         common
        </strong>
        <strong>
         red
        </strong>
        <strong>
         flags
        </strong>
        that a power tool needs inspection, maintenance or repair. Following each are steps to diagnose the problem and check the
        <strong>
         mostly
        </strong>
        
        <strong>
         likely
        </strong>
        causes
        <strong>
         first
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        The symptoms listed in this troubleshooting guide are:
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="#offon">
         Off-And-On Use
        </a>

        </a>

      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

         The Tool Wont Start At All

      </p>

       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

         Sparks And/Or Smoke Out The Dust Vents

      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="smokeswitch">
         Sparks And/Or Smoke In The Switch
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="#shakingtwisting">
         Shaking And/Or Twisting The Tool Helps It Start
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <em>
          Note:
         </em>
        </strong>
        These symptoms are
        <em>

        listed in any particular order because their character and frequency can vary from tool to tool. You can
        <em>

        to jump to the section of this article that you need.

      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        (
        <strong>
         <em>
          Hint:
         </em>
        </strong>
        Most of the symptoms below will follow power path logic to some degree, but some symptoms will call for starting halfway down the power path or will point to just one cause, etc. Remember, power path logic is just a general way to think about how electric power tools work and why they might malfunction.)
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="#top">
         [Back to top]
        </a>

      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <a name="offon">
         </a>
        </strong>
        Off-And-On Use
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        There are a few things to check if a power tool
        <strong>
         works
        </strong>
        <strong>
         fine
        </strong>
        one minute and
        <strong>
         then
        </strong>
        <strong>
         doesn
        </strong>
        
        <strong>
         t
        </strong>
        even
        <strong>
         start
        </strong>
        up the next. The most likely source of this problem is the first part (after the cord) along the power path, the power
        <strong>
         switch
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1.
        <strong>
         Take a look at the switch assembly and check for damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as melted or discolored wiring, or melted plastic.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Switch Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="226" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110236c.jpg" title="Switch Heat Damage" width="339"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Power switches often wear out with use and just have to be replaced sometimes before they can be eliminated as the cause of off-and-on use. Shaking and twisting a tool will not help a tool start if its power switch has failed.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        The best way to be sure about a power switch if there is no visible damage is to use amultitester.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="/article/1071/Multitesters_and_Multimeters_101.html">
         For a Multitesters 101 articleClick here
        </a>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="/article/948/Power_Switch_Repair_101.html">
         For a Power Switch Repair 101 articleClick here
        </a>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If youre confident that the power switch is good and cant see any visible damage on it, then checking the brushes is next.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2.
        <strong>
         Check the brushes for heavy wear, heat damage, chips, and burrs.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heavy wearing will be close to or past the brushs wear lines.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as dark areas on the brushes or discoloration of the wires/springs around the brushes.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Chips will be clearly visible.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Burrs will be small formations around the brushs sides or edges which can restrict a brushs movement in its holders and prevent it from contacting the commutator.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check that the brush springs are still springy and applying enough pressure.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Chipped Brush" class="aligncenter" height="216" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110218c.jpg" title="Chipped Brush" width="324"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Brush Wire Discoloration" class="aligncenter" height="225" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110210c1.jpg" title="Brush Wire Discoloration" width="338"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Chips, heavy wear and burrs all cause
        <strong>
         off-and-on use
        </strong>
        in a power tool because they create
        <strong>
         gaps
        </strong>
        in conductivity where the brushes meet the armature.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Burrs should be filed down, but brushes should be replaced in all other cases.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        It is especially important to continue along the power path and check the armatures commutator for damage if chips are found in brushes. Chipped brushes are often cause by worn or
        <strong>
         damaged
        </strong>
        <strong>
         commutators
        </strong>
        , so replacing damaged brushes with new ones wont always be a lasting fix.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        In any case, even if no damage to the brushes is found, checking the commutator is next on the list.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        3.
        <strong>
         Take a look at the commutator and check it for damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Commutators should be perfectly round. High points, low points and other warping on a commutator can cause off-and-on use.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for missing commutator bars. Missing pads or bars will force the electrical current to arc between gaps, causing off-and-on use.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Look for discoloration on the commutator caused by heat damage.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for buildup in the grooves between commutator bars.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Damaged Commutator" class="aligncenter" height="281" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110194c.jpg" title="Damaged Commutator" width="422"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Sometimes a damaged commutator is the problem all along and it takes getting this far into the tool to discover it. This could happen if the damage to the commutator is recent and hasnt had the chance to chip or wear the brushes yet.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        A damaged commutator should be replaced quickly, before it damages other components of the power tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Also remember that these problems can appear in any
        <strong>
         combination
        </strong>
        and all show up
        <strong>
         together
        </strong>
        as a single off-and-on use issue, so checking the whole tool is often the best thing to do.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="#top">
         [Back to top]
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <a name="decrease">
         </a>
        </strong>
        A Decrease In Power Or Performance
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        A decrease in power or performance in a power tool is caused by either a weak or damaged
        <strong>
         connection
        </strong>
        somewhere along the power path, by
        <strong>
         heat
        </strong>
        <strong>
         damage
        </strong>
        to one or more of the tools components, or by
        <strong>
         overloading
        </strong>
        the tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        (
        <strong>
         <em>
          Hint:
         </em>
        </strong>
        Remember, this is assuming that power cord issues and external power source issues have already been checked out and
        <strong>
         eliminated
        </strong>
        .)
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Checking the power switch is the first step for many symptoms, but a failing or failed power switch will not be able to start the tool at all or only sometimes.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If a tool is simply
        <strong>
         not
        </strong>
        <strong>
         performing
        </strong>
        with the same power as it once did and the power switch is working normally, its probably not the power switch and starting with the brushes is the first step.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1
        <em>
         .
        </em>
        <strong>
         Check the carbon brushes for heavy wearing, chips, or signs of heat damage
         <em>
          .
         </em>
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heavy wearing will be close to or past the brushs wear lines.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as dark areas on the brushes or discoloration of the wires/springs around the brushes.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Chips will be clearly visible.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check that the brush springs are still springy and applying enough pressure.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Chipped Brush" class="aligncenter" height="231" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110218c.jpg" title="Chipped Brush" width="347"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Brush Wire Discoloration" class="aligncenter" height="235" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110210c1.jpg" title="Brush Wire Discoloration" width="354"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        When brushes are heavily worn, chipped, or damaged like this, the tool user will often experience off-and-on use, like when power switches act up from wear or damage.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        But
        <strong>
         heat
        </strong>
        damage to brushes
        <em>
         especially
        </em>
        can
        <strong>
         reduce
        </strong>
        the overall
        <strong>
         conductivity
        </strong>
        of the brush, causing less power to reach the motor of the tool. Bushes should be replaced in all of the above cases.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If you check the brushes and they are free of wear or damage, the next step is checking the
        <strong>
         commutator
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2
        <em>
         <strong>
          .
         </strong>
        </em>
        <strong>
         <em>
         </em>
         Take a look at the commutator and check it for damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Commutators should be perfectly round. High points, low points and other warping on a commutator can cause a decrease in tool performance.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for missing commutator bars. Missing pads or bars will force the electrical current to arc between gaps, reducing overall performance.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Look for discoloration on the commutator caused by heat damage.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for buildup in the grooves between commutator bars.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Damaged Commutator" class="aligncenter" height="301" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110194c.jpg" title="Damaged Commutator" width="453"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Damaged commutators can cause off-and-on damage too, but they can also cause a general
        <strong>
         decrease
        </strong>
        in
        <strong>
         performance
        </strong>
        . A damaged commutator calls for replacing the armature, but it also calls for checking the rest of motor.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        3.
        <strong>
         Check the rest of the armature assembly for signs of heat damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Heat damage will appear as melted insulation around the wires surrounding the armature assembly, or discoloration of the wires and/or insulation.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="239" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110229c.jpg" title="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" width="359"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Lack of power from any source along the power path before the armature can cause the armature and motor to
        <strong>
         heat
        </strong>
        up and suffer damage. If the problem is caught before the tool sees too much low-power use, then damaging the field and other parts of the tool can be avoided.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        An armature assembly that has been damaged by heat will cause a sharp decrease in tool performance.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If heat damage is found on the armature, then taking a look at the field is also a good idea. Although a damaged field will cause low performance in a tool all on its own.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        4.
        <strong>
         Look for heat damage on the field assembly
        </strong>
        
        <strong>
         s wires or wire insulation.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage to a field will also appear as melted wire insulation or wire discoloration.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="250" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110229c.jpg" title="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" width="375"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Fields can also fail from being
        <strong>
         overloaded
        </strong>
        if a tool is used for work that exceeds its
        <strong>
         specifications
        </strong>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Most field and armature damage is obvious by other signs (like sparks or smoke coming out the dust vents) but sometimes the power decrease caused by the damage isnt noticeable before it gets too bad.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        However, if you already know that the tool has been overloaded or has overheated, then you might want to plan on dismantling the tool all the way to the motor from the beginning, checking each part on the way.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Also remember that these problems can appear in any
        <strong>
         combination
        </strong>
        and all show up
        <strong>
         together
        </strong>
        as a single performance decrease issue, so checking the whole tool is often the best thing to do.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="#top">
         [Back to top]
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <a name="won'tstart">
         </a>
        </strong>
        The Tool Wont Start At All
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If a power tool wont start at all, then the steps for diagnosing the problem are a lot like the steps for diagnosing off-and-on use. This is because the problem could be caused by damage or wear to any tool part along the power path (like with off-and-on use), but a tool not starting at all could also be caused by a damaged armature or field.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1
        <strong>
         . Take a look at the switch assembly and check for damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as melted or discolored wiring, or melted plastic.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Switch Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="225" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110236c.jpg" title="Switch Heat Damage" width="338"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Power switches often wear out with use and just have to be replaced sometimes before they can be eliminated as the cause of a tool not starting. You can be especially sure that it is the switch though if you know that the switch has suffered
        <strong>
         water
        </strong>
        or
        <strong>
         moisture
        </strong>
        damage.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Power switch failure is the most common reason for a tool not to start at all.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        The best way to be sure about a power switch if there is no visible damage is to use amultitester.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="/article/1071/Multitesters_and_Multimeters_101.html">
         For a Multitesters 101 articleClick here
        </a>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <a href="/article/948/Power_Switch_Repair_101.html">
         For a Power Switch Repair 101 articleClick here
        </a>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If youre confident that the power switch is good and cant see any visible damage on it, then checking the brushes is next.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2.
        <strong>
         Check the brushes for heavy wear, heat damage, chips, and burrs.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heavy wearing will be close to or past the brushs wear lines.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as dark areas on the brushes or discoloration of the wires/springs around the brushes.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Chips will be clearly visible.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Burrs will be small formations around the brushs sides or edges which can restrict a brushs movement in its holders and prevent it from contacting the commutator.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check that the brush springs are still springy and applying enough pressure.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Chipped Brush" class="aligncenter" height="216" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110218c.jpg" title="Chipped Brush" width="324"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Brush Wire Discoloration" class="aligncenter" height="227" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110210c1.jpg" title="Brush Wire Discoloration" width="341"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Sometimes the
        <strong>
         wear
        </strong>
        or damage to a carbon brush is
        <strong>
         bad
        </strong>
        enough that power isnt getting to the electric motor at all. This will keep the tool from turning on, even if the switch is fine.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        This is especially true if a burr has formed on the brush. Burrs can sometimes
        <strong>
         prevent
        </strong>
        a brush from making
        <strong>
         contact
        </strong>
        with the armature at all, providing zero power to the tool motor.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Any kind of heavy damage or chipping to the brushes merits taking a look at the armature as well. If there isnt any problem with the brushes, then the armature is still the next stop. So,
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        3.
        <strong>
         Take a look at the commutator and check it for damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Commutators should be perfectly round. High points, low points and other warping on a commutator can cause a decrease in tool performance.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for missing commutator bars. Missing pads or bars will force the electrical current to arc between gaps, reducing overall performance.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Look for discoloration on the commutator caused by heat damage.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for buildup in the grooves between commutator bars.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Damaged Commutator" class="aligncenter" height="290" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110194c.jpg" title="Damaged Commutator" width="436"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Commutator damage can be heavy enough to prevent the tool from starting at all, which means that the armature will have to be replaced, as well as any brushes damaged by the commutator.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        A damaged commutator should be replaced quickly, before it damages other components of the power tool.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If the brushes were fine, then it still might be
        <strong>
         heat
        </strong>
        damage to the armature that has completely
        <strong>
         disabled
        </strong>
        the tool. It would be unlikely that heat damage could
        <strong>
         fry
        </strong>
        an
        <strong>
         armature
        </strong>
        without damaging other parts of the tool, but it could still happen.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        4.
        <strong>
         Check the rest of the armature assembly for signs of heat damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as melted insulation around the wires surrounding the armature assembly, or discoloration of the wires and/or insulation.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="265" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110229c.jpg" title="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" width="398"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        A damaged armature will have to be replaced to fix the power tool and prevent further damage.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        It could still be the field, even if everything else up to this point has checked out fine. Its unlikely that a field could completely fail like this without
        <strong>
         obvious
        </strong>
        <strong>
         signs
        </strong>
        or damage to other parts of the tool, but it can still happen.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        5.
        <strong>
         Look for heat damage on the field assembly
        </strong>
        
        <strong>
         s wires and wire insulation.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage to a field will also appear as melted wire insulation or wire discoloration.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="273" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110229c.jpg" title="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" width="412"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Fields fail most often from being
        <strong>
         overloaded
        </strong>
        if a tool is used for work that exceeds its specifications. Damaged fields should be replaced quickly.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Also remember that these problems can appear in any
        <strong>
         combination
        </strong>
        and can all show up
        <strong>
         together
        </strong>
        as a single electrical connection issue, so checking the whole tool is often the best thing to do.
       </span>
      </p>

       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        This is one where power-path logic
        <em>
         isn
        </em>
        
        <em>
         t
        </em>
        needed. If
        <strong>
         sparks
        </strong>
        or
        <strong>
         smoke
        </strong>
        are coming out of a tools
        <strong>
         dust
        </strong>
        <strong>
         vents
        </strong>
        , either the tools
        <strong>
         armature
        </strong>
        or
        <strong>
         field
        </strong>
        <strong>
         or
        </strong>
        <strong>
         both
        </strong>
        .is damaged and failing,
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

        </span>
       </li>

       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        A damaged commutator can make the carbon brushes bounce and spark as the armature spins. Severe sparks and smoke is usually because of more severe heat damage to the rest of the armature though.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2.
        <strong>

       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as melted insulation around the wires surrounding the armature assembly, or discoloration of the wires and/or insulation.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">

        Even if the armature checks out fine, it could still be the field. Its unlikely that a field could completely fail like this without

        <strong>
         s wires or wire insulation.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage to a field will also appear as melted wire insulation or wire discoloration.

      <p>

        <img alt="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" class="aligncenter" height="256" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110229c.jpg" title="Motor Wiring Heat Damage" width="384"/>

      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <strong>
         <a name="shakingtwisting">
         </a>
        </strong>
        Shaking And/Or Twisting The Tool Helps It Start
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If a tool wont start up but then
        <strong>
         shaking
        </strong>
        or twisting it seems to
        <strong>
         help
        </strong>
        it start, then its probably either a chipped brush, a burr formation on a brush, or a damaged commutator.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Shaking or striking a power tool will make its armature
        <strong>
         spin
        </strong>
        inside the tool. If either the commutator or the brushes have chips or damage, spinning the armature can help the remaining contact areas
        <strong>
         line
        </strong>
        <strong>
         up
        </strong>
        better and complete the electrical circuit.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        If this is the case, then checking the power switch isnt necessary.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        1.
        <strong>
         Check the brushes for heavy wear, heat damage, chips, and burrs.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heavy wearing will be close to or past the brushs wear lines.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Heat damage will appear as dark areas on the brushes or discoloration of the wires/springs around the brushes.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Chips will be clearly visible.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Burrs will be small formations around the brushs sides or edges which can restrict a brushs movement in its holders and prevent it from contacting the commutator.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check that the brush springs are still springy and applying enough pressure.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Chipped Brush" class="aligncenter" height="230" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110218c.jpg" title="Chipped Brush" width="345"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Brush Wire Discoloration" class="aligncenter" height="246" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110210c1.jpg" title="Brush Wire Discoloration" width="369"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Its possible for all of the above brush issues to cause a tool not to start until shaken, but the most likely are chips, burrs and worn-out springs.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Burrs should be filed down, but brushes should be replaced in all other cases. As wear or damage to the carbon brushes increases, shaking and twisting the tool wont help to start the tool eventually.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        It is especially important to continue along the power path and check the armatures commutator for damage if chips are found in brushes. Chipped brushes are often cause by worn or
        <strong>
         damaged
        </strong>
        <strong>
         commutators
        </strong>
        , so replacing damaged brushes with new ones wont always be a lasting fix.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        In any case, even if no damage to the brushes is found, checking the commutator is next on the list.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        2.
        <strong>
         Take a look at the commutator and check it for damage.
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Commutators should be perfectly round. High points, low points and other warping on a commutator can cause a decrease in tool performance.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for missing commutator bars. Missing pads or bars will force the electrical current to arc between gaps, reducing overall performance.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Look for discoloration on the commutator caused by heat damage.
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
         Check for buildup in the grooves between commutator bars.
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        <img alt="Damaged Commutator" class="aligncenter" height="305" src="http://www.ereplacementparts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2009/09/P1110194c.jpg" title="Damaged Commutator" width="458"/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        When it comes to commutator damage, shaking or twisting the tool will eventually do nothing to help the tool start up as the damage worsens.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        A damaged armature will have to be replaced to fix the power tool and prevent further damage.
       </span>
      </p>

        <strong>
         Conclusion
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        Understanding how electric power tools work is the first step towards diagnosing the problems they have over time. In general, following the power path through a tool is the best way to narrow down the possibilities.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        The great news is that if you can figure out the problem, youve usually cleared the highest hurdle. Finding and ordering the right parts for power tools is fast and easy, and speedy shipping options are usually available.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size: small; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
        From there, repair guides and a little well-spent time on the part of the tool user can seal the deal and get the tool working like new again.
       </span>
