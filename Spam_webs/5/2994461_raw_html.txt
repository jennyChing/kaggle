    Sociolect
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics" title="Sociolinguistics">
       sociolinguistics
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       sociolect
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       social dialect
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
       variety of language
      </a>
      (a
      <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
       register
      </a>
      ) associated with a social group such as a socioeconomic class, an ethnic group (precisely termed
      <a href="/wiki/Ethnolect" title="Ethnolect">
       ethnolect
      </a>
      ), an age group, etc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sociolects involve both passive acquisition of particular communicative practices through association with a local community, as well as active learning and choice among speech or writing forms to demonstrate
      <a href="/wiki/Identification_(psychology)" title="Identification (psychology)">
       identification
      </a>
      with particular groups.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Individuals who study sociolects are called sociolinguists. Sociolinguists study language variation. Sociolinguists define a sociolect by examining the social distribution of specific linguistic terms. For example, a sociolinguist would examine the use of the second person pronoun "you" for its use within the population. If one distinct social group used 'yous' as the plural form of the pronoun then this could indicate the existence of a sociolect. A sociolect is distinct from a dialect because social class rather than geographical subdivision substantiates the unique linguistic features.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A sociolect, defined by Peter Trudgill, a leading sociolinguist and
      <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">
       philosopher
      </a>
      , is a variety or lect which is thought of as being related to its speakers social background rather than geographical background.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This idea of sociolect began with the commencement of
      <a href="/wiki/Dialectology" title="Dialectology">
       dialectology
      </a>
      , the study of different dialects in relation to social society, which has been established in countries such as England for many years, but only recently has the field garnered more attention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, as opposed to dialect, the basic concept of a sociolect is that a person speaks in accordance with their social group whether it is with regard to ones ethnicity, age, gender, etc. As William Labov once said, the sociolinguistic viewis that we are programmed to learn to speak in ways that fit the general pattern of our communities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, what we are surrounded with in unison with our environment determines how we speak; hence, our actions and associations.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_difference_between_sociolects_and_dialects">
       The difference between sociolects and dialects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The difference between sociolects and dialects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The main distinction between a sociolect and dialect, which are continually confused, are the settings they are created in. A dialects main identifier is geography where a certain region uses specific phonological, morphosyntactic, or lexical rules.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Asif Agha expands and specializes this concept by stating that, the case where the demographic dimension marked by speech are matters of geographic provenance along, such as speakers birth locale, extended residence and the like.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the opposite side, a sociolects main identifier are things such as socioeconomic class, age, gender, and ethnicity spoken in a certain speech community.
     </p>
     <p>
      An example of a dialectal difference  based on region  is the use of the words soda or pop and coke in different parts of the United States. As Thomas E. Murray states, coke is used generically by thousands of people, especially in the southern half of the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Contrastively, pop is known to be a term that is used by many citizens in the northern half of the country.
     </p>
     <p>
      An example of a sociolect difference  based on social grouping  is the
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_copula" title="Zero copula">
       deletion of copulas
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/African_American_Vernacular_English" title="African American Vernacular English">
       African American Vernacular English
      </a>
      . The deletion occurs in a specific ethnic group, but in all areas of the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As William Labov gives an example, he here instead of hes here.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
       Definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_switching" title="Code switching">
        Code switching
       </a>
      </b>
      is "the process whereby bilingual or bidialectal speakers switch back and forth between one language or dialect and another within the same conversation."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Diglossia" title="Diglossia">
        Diglossia
       </a>
      </b>
      , associated with the American linguist
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Ferguson" title="Charles A. Ferguson">
       Charles A. Ferguson
      </a>
      , which describes sociolinguistic situation such as those that obtain in Arabic-speaking countries and in German-speaking Switzerland. In such a diglossic community, the prestigious standard of High(or H) variety, which is linguistically related to but significantly different from the vernacular or Low (or L) varieties, has no native speakers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Domain
      </b>
      is "Different language, dialects, or styles are used in different social contexts."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Language Attitudes
      </b>
      are "social in origin, but that they may have important effects on language behavior, being involved in acts of identity, and on linguistic change."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_variable" title="Linguistic variable">
        Linguistic variable
       </a>
      </b>
      is "a linguistic unitinitially developedin order to be able to handle linguistics variation. Variables may be lexical and grammatical, but are most often phonological. Example of British English (h) which is sometimes present and sometimes not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">
        Pragmatics
       </a>
      </b>
      is the meaning of a word in social context, while
      <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
       semantics
      </a>
      has "purely linguistic meaning."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Register
      </b>
      is "a language variety that is associated with a particular topic, subject, or activity...." Usually it is defined by vocabulary, but has grammatical features as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tamil_Caste_System">
       Tamil Caste System
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tamil Caste System">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The following is an example of the lexical distinction between the Mudaliyar and the Iyengar groups of the Tamil-speaking caste in India. The Iyengar group is part of the Brahmin caste which is scholarly and higher in the caste hierarchy than the non-Brahmin, or Mudaliyar, caste.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Mudaliyars use many of the same words for things that are differentiated within the Iyengars speech. For example, as you can see below, the difference between drinking water, water in general, and non-potable water is used by one word in the non-Brahmin caste and three separate words in the Brahmin caste. Furthermore, Agha references how the use of different speech reflects a departure from a group-internal norm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, if the non-Brahmin caste uses Brahmin terms in their mode of speech it is seen as self-raising, where as if people within the Brahmin caste use non-Brahmin speech it is seen as pejoratives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, depending on which castes use certain words the pragmatics change. Hence, this speech system is determined by socioeconomic class and social context.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Code-switching">
       Code-switching
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Code-switching">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Many times within communities that contain sociolects that separate groups linguistically it is necessary to have a process where the independent speech communities can communicate in the same register; even if the change is as simple as different pronunciation. Therefore, the act of codeswitching becomes essential. Codeswitching is defined as, the process whereby bilingual or bidialectal speakers switch back and forth between one language or dialect and another within the same conversation
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At times codeswitching can be situational, depending on the situation or topical, depending on the topic. Halliday terms this the best when he defines the role of discourse stating that, it is this that determines, or rather correlates with, the role played by the language activity in the situation
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, meaning that which register is used depends on the situation and lays out the social context of the situation, because if the wrong register is used, then the wrong context is placed on the words. Furthermore, referring back to the diglossia expressed in the Arab-speaking world and the Tamil caste system in India, which words are used must be appropriate to not only the social class of the speaker, but the situation, the topic, and the need for courtesy. A more comprehensive definition is stated, Code-switching is not only a definition of situation but an expression of social hierarchy
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For examples of the use of speech within certain situation refer back to the chart on Classical and Colloquial Arabic.
     </p>
     <p>
      For an example of dialect selection based on topic refer below:
     </p>
     <p>
      As Trudgill defines it the Aravanikita is, the name given in Greece given to the language of the indigenous Albanian-speaking linguistic minority in that country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This community is different linguistically than the surrounding area and must use their language according. For example, now in days, Arvanitika is only used in the home and other situations, such as in school during games, on the playground, or for chatting up girls, while, only Greek is spoken in class.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, it is both topical and situational in context.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discrimination">
       Discrimination
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociolect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Discrimination">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Arvanitika community also suffers from discrimination because they are cast under stereotypes by the use of their native language. As Garrett writes, a number of Arvanites had suffered from what they regarded as discrimination, particularly during military service, and at school".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even though, the language is the only thing that differentiates them from the surrounding Greeks, it still defines them as a distinct class and places them within a social hierarchy. Furthermore, within societies that maintain a diglossic state, the High (H) and Low (L) forms serve as a basis for discrimination. As Mesthrie writes, Since the H form is learned via formal education, diglossia can be a means of excluding people from access to full participation in society.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
