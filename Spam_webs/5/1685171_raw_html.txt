     <h1>
      <!-- InstanceBeginEditable name="heading" -->
      Carlsbad Hat
      <!-- InstanceEndEditable -->
     </h1>
     <div id="content">
      <div id="patterndetails_wide">
       <!-- InstanceBeginEditable name="content" -->
       <p>
        Copyright Marnie MacLean 2005
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Click on Images for Larger View
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="images/hat2.jpg" target="_blank">
         <img align="left" border="0" height="154" src="thumbnails/hat2.jpg" width="200"/>
        </a>
        Carlsbad is a city in the San Diego area of California. Mild temperatures and beautiful beaches can be found year round. Sometimes, the ocean breeze is just cool enough to warrant a hat, but one that is light and breathable.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Carlsbad hat is constructed largely as the
        <a href="../Pismo/index.html" target="_blank">
         Pismo Hat
        </a>
        is. It's a tight fitting beanie knit with fine gauge cotton yarn and using paired decreases for a professional finish. Where the Carlsbad hat differs is in it's weight. Knit in stockinette instead of ribbing, it's a hat you can forget you are wearing. The fabric is light, thin and cool and being cotton, it won't cause sensitive skin to itch.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Supplies
       </h2>
       <p>
        1 skein of Idena Bambino Bomull or another baby cotton in color of choice.
        <br/>
        Optional: scraps of contrasting color for stripe(s)
        <br/>
        US #1 needles (flat or circular)
        <br/>
        6 stitch markers (SM)
        <br/>
        Tapestry needles
       </p>
       <h2>
        Gauge
       </h2>
       <p>
        In stockinette
        <br/>
        32 stitches x 48 rows = 4"
       </p>
       <h2>
        Sizes
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="images/schematic.jpg" target="_blank">
         <img align="right" border="0" height="267" src="thumbnails/schematic.jpg" width="300"/>
        </a>
        S [M, L]
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Finished circumference:
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        18" [19.5", 21"]
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         NOTE:
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        If you wish to modify for a different size, add or subtract stitches in multiples of 6.
       </p>
       <p>
        To determine best size, measure circumference of head. If in between, go down a size.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Hat
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="images/ribbing.jpg" target="_blank">
         <img align="left" border="0" height="120" hspace="5" src="thumbnails/ribbing.jpg" vspace="5" width="200"/>
        </a>
        CO 145 [157, 169] stitches
       </p>
       <p>
        Place a stitch marker after the 12th [13th, 14th] stitch and then after every 24th [26th, 28th] stitch after that. 6 stitch markers total
       </p>
       <p>
        First row: K2,
        <strong>
         *p1, k1, p1, k3*
        </strong>
        repeat from
        <strong>
         * to *
        </strong>
        to last 5 stitches. P1, k1, p1, k2
       </p>
       <p>
        Work in pattern established for a total of 5 rows
       </p>
       <p>
        Next row: P
       </p>
       <p>
        OPTIONAL: Knit 1 row in contrasting color
       </p>
       <p>
        Work in stockinette until piece measures 3"
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <a href="images/hattop.jpg" target="_blank">
          <img align="right" border="0" height="210" src="thumbnails/hattop.jpg" width="200"/>
         </a>
         Decrease row
        </strong>
        : Knit to within 2 stitches of first stitch marker, ssk, slip marker, k2tog.
        <strong>
         133 [145, 157] stitches remaining
        </strong>
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          Note: Reverse the decreases to get a slightly different looking effect. The decrease indicated will give a sort of feathered looking line of decreases. Reversing them will make a smoother looking line of decreases. Alternately, work a vertical double decrease (slip 2 stitches as if to k2tog, knit the next stitch, pass the two slipped stitches over the knit stitch) to make a raised chain of decrease.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Knit 5 rows straight.
       </p>
       <p>
        Repeat the decrease row and 5 rows straight 1 [2, 3] times more.
        <strong>
         121 stitches remaining
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="images/hat1.jpg" target="_blank">
         <img align="left" border="0" height="200" src="thumbnails/hat1.jpg" width="164"/>
        </a>
        *Work decrease row, then work 3 rows straight* 6 times.
        <strong>
         49 stitches remaining
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Work decrease row, every other row two times.
        <strong>
         25 stitches remaining
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Cut a long tail and pull through remaining stitches twice. Cinch tightly.
       </p>
       <p>
        Use tail to sew up hat from crown to hem.
       </p>
       <p>
        Weave in all ends.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Blocking
       </h2>
       <p>
        While not entirely necessary, blocking helps the hat to fit a little better and makes it less likely to migrate upwards. Find a head sized object or stuff a plastic grocery bag to head size. Wet the hat and place upon said object. Smooth out the hat.
       </p>
       <p>
        Allow to dry in place.
       </p>
