     <p>
      <b>
       Agnosia
      </b>
      is the inability to process sensory information. Often there is a loss of ability to recognize objects, persons, sounds, shapes, or smells while the specific sense is not defective nor is there any significant memory loss.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is usually associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Acquired_brain_injury" title="Acquired brain injury">
       brain injury
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurological_illness" title="Neurological illness">
       neurological illness
      </a>
      , particularly after damage to the
      <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_37" title="Brodmann area 37">
       occipitotemporal border
      </a>
      , which is part of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral_stream" title="Ventral stream">
       ventral stream
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KolbWhishaw2003_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KolbWhishaw2003-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Agnosia only affects a single modality,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      such as vision or hearing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NINDS_Agnosia_Information_Page_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NINDS_Agnosia_Information_Page-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     </h2>
     <table class="wikitable">
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         Name
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <b>
         Description
        </b>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Akinetopsia" title="Akinetopsia">
         Akinetopsia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Also known as Cerebral akinetopsia is associated with the inability to perceive visual motion. One cause of cerebral akinetopsia is lesions outside the striate cortex.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          5
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Anosognosia" title="Anosognosia">
         Anosognosia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        This is the inability to gain feedback about one's own condition and can be confused with lack of insight but is caused by problems in the feedback mechanisms in the brain. It is caused by neurological damage and can occur in connection with a range of neurological impairments but is most commonly referred to in cases of paralysis following stroke. Those with Anosognosia with multiple impairments may even be aware of some of their impairments but completely unable to perceive others.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Apperceptive_agnosia#Visual_apperceptive_agnosia" title="Apperceptive agnosia">
         Apperceptive visual agnosia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Patients are unable to distinguish visual shapes and so have trouble recognizing, copying, or discriminating between different visual stimuli. Unlike patients suffering from associative agnosia, those with apperceptive agnosia are unable to copy images.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          6
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Associative_visual_agnosia" title="Associative visual agnosia">
         Associative visual agnosia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Patients can describe visual scenes and classes of objects but still fail to recognize them. They may, for example, know that a fork is something you eat with but may mistake it for a spoon. Patients suffering from associative agnosia are still able to reproduce an image through copying.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Astereognosis" title="Astereognosis">
         Astereognosis
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Also known as Somatosensory agnosia is connected to tactile sensethat is, touch. Patient finds it difficult to recognize objects by touch based on its texture, size and weight. However, they may be able to describe it verbally or recognize same kind of objects from pictures or draw pictures of them. Thought to be connected to lesions or damage in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex" title="Somatosensory cortex">
         somatosensory cortex
        </a>
        .
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Auditory_agnosia" title="Auditory agnosia">
         Auditory agnosia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Auditory agnosia has been recognized since 1877.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein1974_7-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein1974-7">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          7
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
        With Auditory Agnosia there is difficulty distinguishing environmental and non-verbal auditory cues including difficulty distinguishing speech from non-speech sounds even though hearing is usually normal. There are two types of auditory agnosia: semantic associative and discriminative agnosia. Semantic associative agnosia is associated with lesions to the left hemisphere, whereas discriminative agnosia is associated with lesions to the right hemisphere.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          8
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Auditory_verbal_agnosia" title="Auditory verbal agnosia">
         Auditory verbal agnosia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Also known as Pure Word Deafness (PWD) This presents as a form of meaning 'deafness' in which hearing is intact but there is significant difficulty recognising spoken words as semantically meaningful.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Autotopagnosia" title="Autotopagnosia">
         Autotopagnosia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Is associated with the inability to orient parts of the body, and is often caused by a
        <a href="/wiki/Lesion" title="Lesion">
         lesion
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">
         parietal part
        </a>
        of the posterior thalmic radiations.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_achromatopsia" title="Cerebral achromatopsia">
         Cerebral achromatopsia
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Also known as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_agnosia" title="Color agnosia">
         Color agnosia
        </a>
        involves having difficulty categorizing colours, as well as recognizing colours. Cerebral achromatopsia is usually caused by neurological damage.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          9
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          10
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
        There are two regions of the brain which specialize for color recognition, areas V4 and V8. If there is a unilateral lesion to area V4, a loss of color perception will result known as hemiachromatopsia.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          3
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Cortical_deafness" title="Cortical deafness">
         Cortical deafness
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        Refers to people who do not perceive any auditory information but whose hearing is intact.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Environmental agnosia
       </td>
       <td>
        It is the inability to locate a specific room or building that one is familiar with, as well as the inability to provide directions for how to arrive at a particular location. These individuals experience difficulty with learning routes. This form of agnosia is often associated with lesions to the bilateral or right hemisphere posterior regions. It is also associated with prosopagnosia and Parkinson's disease.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_agnosia">
       Visual agnosia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Visual agnosia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_agnosia" title="Visual agnosia">
       Visual agnosia
      </a>
      is a broad category that refers to a deficiency in the ability to recognize visual objects. Visual agnosia can be further subdivided into two different subtypes:
      <a href="/wiki/Apperceptive_agnosia" title="Apperceptive agnosia">
       apperceptive visual agnosia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Associative_visual_agnosia" title="Associative visual agnosia">
       associative visual agnosia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cognitive_Psychology_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cognitive_Psychology-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Individuals with apperceptive visual agnosia display the ability to see contours and outlines when shown an object, but they experience difficulty if asked to categorize objects. Apperceptive visual agnosia is associated with damage to one hemisphere, specifically damage to the posterior sections of the right hemisphere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cognitive_Psychology_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cognitive_Psychology-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In contrast, individuals with associative visual agnosia experience difficulty when asked to name objects. Associative agnosia is associated with damage to both the right and left hemispheres at the occipitotemporal border.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cognitive_Psychology_20-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cognitive_Psychology-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A specific form of associative visual agnosia is known as prosopagnosia.
      <a href="/wiki/Prosopagnosia" title="Prosopagnosia">
       Prosopagnosia
      </a>
      is the inability to recognize faces. For example, these individuals have difficulty recognizing friends, family and coworkers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cognitive_Psychology_20-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cognitive_Psychology-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, individuals with prosopagnosia can recognize all other types of visual stimuli.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
       Causes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Causes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Agnosia can result from
      <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">
       strokes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">
       dementia
      </a>
      , or other
      <a href="/wiki/Neurological_disorder" title="Neurological disorder">
       neurological disorders
      </a>
      . It may also be trauma-induced by a head injury, brain infection, or hereditary. Additionally, some forms of agnosia may be the result of developmental disorders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NINDS_Agnosia_Information_Page_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NINDS_Agnosia_Information_Page-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Damage causing agnosia usually occurs in either the occipital or parietal lobes of the brain. Although one modality may be affected, cognitive abilities in other areas are preserved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NINDS_Agnosia_Information_Page_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NINDS_Agnosia_Information_Page-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Patients who experience dramatic
      <a href="/wiki/Recovery_from_blindness" title="Recovery from blindness">
       recovery from blindness
      </a>
      experience significant to total Agnosia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">
       Diagnosis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In order to assess an individual for agnosia, it must be verified that the individual is not suffering from a loss of sensation, and that both their language abilities and intelligence are intact. In order for an individual to be diagnosed with agnosia, they must only be experiencing a sensory deficit in a single modality. To make a diagnosis, the distinction between apperceptive and associative agnosia must be made. This distinction can be made by having the individual complete copying and matching tasks. If the individual is suffering from a form of apperceptive agnosia they will not be able to match two stimuli that are identical in appearance. In contrast, if an individual is suffering from a form of associative agnosia, they will not be able to match different examples of a stimulus. For example, an individual who has been diagnosed with associative agnosia in the visual modality would not be able to match pictures of a laptop that is open with a laptop that is closed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_alexia">
       Pure alexia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pure alexia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Individuals with pure alexia usually have difficulty reading words as well as difficulty with identifying letters. In order to assess whether an individual has pure alexia, tests of copying and recognition must be performed. An individual with pure alexia should be able to copy a set of words, and should be able to recognize letters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prosopagnosia">
       Prosopagnosia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prosopagnosia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Individuals are usually shown pictures of human faces that may be familiar to them such as famous actors, singers, politicians or family members. The pictures shown to the patient are selected to be age and culture appropriate. The task involves the examiner asking the individual to name each face. If the individual cannot name whose face appears in the picture, the examiner may ask a question that would help to recognize the face in the picture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
       Treatment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Treatment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For all practical purposes, there is no direct cure. Patients may improve if information is presented in other modalities than the damaged one. Different types of therapies can help to reverse the effects of Agnosia. In some cases,
      <a href="/wiki/Occupational_therapy" title="Occupational therapy">
       occupational therapy
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_therapy" title="Speech therapy">
       speech therapy
      </a>
      can improve agnosia, depending on its
      <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">
       etiology
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Initially many individuals with a form of agnosia are unaware of the extent to which they have either a perceptual or recognition deficit. This may be caused by anosognosia which is the lack of awareness of a deficit. This lack of awareness usually leads to a form of denial and resistance to any form of help or treatment. There are various methods that can be used which can help the individual recognize the impairment in
      <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">
       perception
      </a>
      or recognition that they may have. A patient can be presented with a stimulus to the impaired modality only to help increase their awareness of their deficit. Alternatively, a task can be broken down into its component parts so that the individual can see each part of the problem caused by the deficit. Once the individual acknowledges their perceptual or recognition deficit, a form of treatment may be recommended. There are various forms of treatment such as compensatory strategies with alternate modalities, verbal strategies, alternate cues and organizational strategies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_strategies">
       Verbal strategies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Verbal strategies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Using verbal descriptions may be helpful for individuals with certain types of agnosia. Individuals such as prosopagnosics may find it useful to listen to a description of their friend or family member and recognize them based on this description more easily than through visual cues.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_cues">
       Alternate cues
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alternate cues">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Alternate cues may be particularly useful to an individual with environmental agnosia or prosopagnosia. Alternate cues for an individual with environmental agnosia may include color cues or
      <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">
       tactile
      </a>
      markers to symbolize a new room or to remember an area by. Prosopagnosics may use alternate cues such as a scar on an individual's face or crooked teeth in order to recognize the individual.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Organizational_strategies">
       Organizational strategies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Organizational strategies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Organizational strategies may be extremely helpful for an individual with visual agnosia. For example organizing clothes according to different hangers provides tactile cues for the individual, making it easier to identify certain forms of clothing as opposed to relying solely on visual cues.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_medicine">
       Alternative medicine
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alternative medicine">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      These strategies elicit the use of an unaffected modality. For example visual agnosics can use tactile information in replacement of visual information. Alternatively, an individual with prosopagnosia can use
      <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">
       auditory information
      </a>
      in order to replace
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">
       visual information
      </a>
      . For example, an individual with prosopagnosia can wait for someone to speak, and will usually recognize the individual from their speech.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agnosia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term 'agnosia' comes from the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Ancient Greek
      </a>
       (
      <i>
       agnosia
      </i>
      ), "ignorance", "absence of knowledge". It was introduced by
      <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">
       Sigmund Freud
      </a>
      in 1891:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "For disturbances in the recognition of objects, which Finkelnburg classes as asymbolia, I should like to propose the term agnosia." Prior to Freud's introduction of the term, some of the first ideas about agnosia came from
      <a href="/wiki/Wernicke" title="Wernicke">
       Wernicke
      </a>
      who created theories about
      <a href="/wiki/Receptive_aphasia" title="Receptive aphasia">
       receptive aphasia
      </a>
      in 1874. He noted that individuals with receptive aphasia did not possess the ability to understand speech or repeat words. He believed that receptive aphasia was due to lesions of the posterior third of the left
      <a href="/wiki/Superior_temporal_gyrus" title="Superior temporal gyrus">
       superior temporal gyrus
      </a>
      . Due to these lesions, Wernicke believed that individuals with receptive aphasia had a limited deafness for certain sounds and frequencies in
      <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">
       speech
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein1974_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein1974-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After Wernicke, came Kussmaul in 1877 who attempted to explain why
      <a href="/wiki/Auditory_verbal_agnosia" title="Auditory verbal agnosia">
       Auditory verbal agnosia
      </a>
      , also known as word deafness, occurs. Contrary to Wernicke's explanations, Kussmaul believed Auditory verbal agnosia was the result of major destruction to the first left temporal gyrus. Kussmaul also posited about the origins of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexia_(acquired_dyslexia)" title="Alexia (acquired dyslexia)">
       Alexia (acquired dyslexia)
      </a>
      also known as word blindness. He believed that word blindness was the result of lesions to the left angular and
      <a href="/wiki/Supramarginal_gyrus" title="Supramarginal gyrus">
       supramarginal gyri
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein1974_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein1974-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Heinrich Lissauer shared his ideas about agnosia after Wernicke and Kussmaul.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein1974_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein1974-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1890, he theorized that there were two ways in which object recognition impairment could occur. One way in which impairment could occur was if there was damage to early perceptual processing or if there was damage to the actual object representation. If the actual object representation was damaged, this would not allow the object to be stored in visual memory, and therefore the individual would not be able to recognize the object.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the time of Wernicke, Kussmaul and Lissauer there was little known about the
      <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">
       cerebral cortex
      </a>
      . Today with new
      <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">
       neuroimaging
      </a>
      techniques we have been able to expand our knowledge on agnosia greatly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.S.Burns_.282004.29_3-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-M.S.Burns_.282004.29-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
