     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
       Terminology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period_spear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The pre-migration term reported by
      <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">
       Tacitus
      </a>
      is
      <i>
       <b>
        framea
       </b>
      </i>
      , who identifies it as "
      <a href="/wiki/Hasta_(spear)" title="Hasta (spear)">
       hasta
      </a>
      "; The native term for "
      <a href="/wiki/Javelin" title="Javelin">
       javelin
      </a>
      , spear" was
      <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
       Old High German
      </a>
      <i>
       <b>
        gr
       </b>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
       Old English
      </a>
      <i>
       <b>
        gr
       </b>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      <i>
       <b>
        geirr
       </b>
      </i>
      , apparently from
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">
       proto-Germanic
      </a>
      <i>
       *gaizaz
      </i>
      . The names
      <a href="/wiki/Genseric" title="Genseric">
       Genseric
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Radagaisus" title="Radagaisus">
       Radagaisus
      </a>
      indicate
      <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
       Gothic
      </a>
      <i>
       gaisu
      </i>
      besides
      <i>
       gairu
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Latin
      <i>
       gaesum
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       gaesus
      </i>
      , Greek
      <span lang="gk" xml:lang="gk">
       
      </span>
      was the term for the lance of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">
       Gauls
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Avestan_language" title="Avestan language">
       Avestan language
      </a>
      has
      <i>
       gau
      </i>
      "lance bearer" as a likely cognate. The Celtic word is found e.g. in the name of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gaesatae" title="Gaesatae">
       Gaesatae
      </a>
      . Old Irish has
      <i>
       gae
      </i>
      "spear". Proto-Germanic
      <i>
       *gaizaz
      </i>
      would derive from
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
       proto-Indo-European language
      </a>
      <i>
       *ghaisos
      </i>
      , although loan from Celtic has also been considered, in which case the PIE form would be
      <i>
       *gaisos
      </i>
      . The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Indogermanisches_etymologisches_W%C3%B6rterbuch" title="Indogermanisches etymologisches Wrterbuch">
        Indogermanisches etymologisches Wrterbuch
       </a>
      </i>
      has
      <i>
       *g'haisos
      </i>
      (with a palatal velar aspirate), discounting the Avestan form in favour of (tentatively) comparing
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        has-
       </span>
      </i>
      "projectile".
     </p>
     <p>
      <span id="Mos_spearhead">
      </span>
      The form
      <i>
       gaois
      </i>
      is read in an early
      <a href="/wiki/Runic_inscriptions" title="Runic inscriptions">
       Runic inscriptions
      </a>
      on the so-called Mos spearhead, dated to the 3rd century, found in
      <a href="/wiki/Stenkyrka" title="Stenkyrka">
       Stenkyrka
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">
       Gotland
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The etymon of English
      <i>
       spear
      </i>
      , from Germanic
      <i>
       *speri
      </i>
      (Old English
      <i>
       spere
      </i>
      , Old Frisian
      <i>
       sper
      </i>
      , Old High German
      <i>
       sper
      </i>
      , Old Norse
      <i>
       spjr
      </i>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in origin also denoted a throwing spear or lance (
      <i>
       hasta
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ger">
       Ger
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period_spear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ger">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       kr
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       gr
      </i>
      is attested since the 8th century (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lay_of_Hildebrand" title="Lay of Hildebrand">
       Lay of Hildebrand
      </a>
      37,
      <a href="/wiki/Heliand" title="Heliand">
       Heliand
      </a>
      3089).
      <i>
       Gar
      </i>
      and cognates is a frequent element in
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_name" title="Germanic name">
       Germanic names
      </a>
      , both male and female.
      <a href="/wiki/Lupo_II_of_Gascony" title="Lupo II of Gascony">
       Garseand
      </a>
      survived in
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_France" title="Southern France">
       Southern France
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Gar%C3%A7on" title="Garon">
        garon
       </a>
      </i>
      to designate either all male youth, or a spear bearing arm.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term survives into
      <a href="/wiki/New_High_German" title="New High German">
       New High German
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       Ger
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Gehr
      </i>
      (Grimm 1854) with a generalized meaning of "
      <a href="/wiki/Gusset" title="Gusset">
       gusset
      </a>
      " besides "spear". In contemporary German, the word is used exclusively in antiquated or poetic context, and a feminine
      <i>
       Gehre
      </i>
      is used in the sense of "gusset".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Framea">
       Framea
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Migration_Period_spear&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Framea">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">
       Tacitus
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Germania_(book)" title="Germania (book)">
        Germania
       </a>
      </i>
      6) describes the equipment of the Germanic warrior as follows:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Even iron is not plentiful with them, as we infer from the character of their weapons. But few use swords or long lances. They carry a spear [
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hasta_(spear)" title="Hasta (spear)">
        hasta
       </a>
       <i>
        ] (
       </i>
       framea
       <i>
        is their name for it), with a narrow and short head, but so sharp and easy to wield that the same weapon serves, according to circumstances, for close or distant conflict. As for the horse-soldier, he is satisfied with a shield and spear; the foot-soldiers also scatter showers of missiles each man having several and hurling them to an immense distance, and being naked or lightly clad with a little cloak.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The term is also used by
      <a href="/wiki/Eucherius_of_Lyon" title="Eucherius of Lyon">
       Eucherius of Lyon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">
       Gregory of Tours
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">
       Isidore
      </a>
      . By the time of Isidore (7th century),
      <i>
       framea
      </i>
      referred to a
      <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">
       sword
      </a>
      , not a
      <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">
       spear
      </a>
      . Since Tacitus reports that the word is natively Germanic, various Germanic etymologies of a Proto-Germanic
      <i>
       *framja
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       *framj
      </i>
      or similar have been suggested, but remain speculative. Must (1958) suggests
      <i>
       *ramja
      </i>
      , cognate to
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      <i>
       remjar
      </i>
      "edges, sword blades",
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">
       Old Saxon
      </a>
      <i>
       thrumi
      </i>
      "point of a spear".
     </p>
     <p>
      The word reappears on the title page of the 15th-century witch-finding book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Malleus_Maleficarum" title="Malleus Maleficarum">
        Malleus Maleficarum
       </a>
      </i>
      : "MALLEUS MALEFICARUM, Maleficas, &amp; earum hresim, ut
      <i>
       phramea
      </i>
      potentissima conterens." ("The Hammer of Witches, which destroys witches and their heresy as with a very mighty sword".
     </p>
     <p>
      Icelandic, the modern language as well as the language of the Sagas, has the wordframi(distinction, renown, fame). This word was in earlier times strongly connected with warfare, but its use as a name of a weapon is not known.
     </p>
     <h2>

     <p>
      <i>
       Gar
      </i>
      "spear" is also the name of
      <span class="script-runic" lang="gem-Runr" style="font-family: Junicode, 'Segoe UI Symbol', 'Free Monospaced', Code2000, Hnias, 'Noto Sans Runic';" title="runes" xml:lang="gem-Runr">
       
      </span>
      , an
      <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_runes" title="Anglo-Saxon runes">
       Anglo-Saxon rune
      </a>
      . It is not attested epigraphically, and first appears in 11th-century manuscript tradition. Phonetically,
      <i>
       gar
      </i>
      represents the /g/ sound. It is a modification of the plain
      <a href="/wiki/Gyfu" title="Gyfu">
       gyfu
      </a>
      rune
      <span class="script-runic" lang="gem-Runr" style="font-family: Junicode, 'Segoe UI Symbol', 'Free Monospaced', Code2000, Hnias, 'Noto Sans Runic';" title="runes" xml:lang="gem-Runr">
       
      </span>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Old English
      <i>
       gr
      </i>
      means "spear", but the name of the rune likely echoes the rune names
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/J%C4%93ran" title="Jran">
        ger
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ear_(rune)" title="Ear (rune)">
        ear
       </a>
       , ior
      </i>
      : due to palatalization in Old English, the original
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      rune (
      <a href="/wiki/Gyfu" title="Gyfu">
       gyfu
      </a>
      ) could express either /j/ or /g/ (see
      <a href="/wiki/Yogh" title="Yogh">
       yogh
      </a>
      ). The
      <i>
       ger
      </i>
      unambiguously expressed /j/, and the newly introduced
      <i>
       gar
      </i>
      rune had the purpose of unambiguously expressing /g/.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Gar
      </i>
      is the 33rd and final rune in the row as given in
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_manuscripts_in_the_Cotton_library#Domitian" title="List of manuscripts in the Cotton library">
       Cotton Domitian
      </a>
      A.ix.
     </p>
