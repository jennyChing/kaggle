        <div class="article-copy copy-text" id="article-copy" itemprop="articleBody">
         <p>
          Cliff Lee has $37.5 million guaranteed dollars left on his
          <a href="http://www.axs.com/tag/philadelphia-phillies">
           Philadelphia Phillies
          </a>
          ' contract. Understandable surgery risks aside, money could be considered the reason why this pitcher has opted for continued rehabilitation of the flexor tendon in his left elbow. But, is that the main truth to be told?
         </p>
         <p>
          Clearly, Lee's agent and additional legal representation surely performed their due diligence. The suits previously advised the 36-year-old veteran of his employee obligations and his employer's rights.
         </p>
         <p>
          All professional baseball players are expected to honestly pursue the profession while under contract. Generally comparable to how most workplace injuries are handled in the United States, ongoing proof must be provided that documents return-to-work pursuits.
         </p>
         <p>
          Attorneys aren't likely to have mentioned the moral merits of having his arm surgically repaired. Instead, they would have presented a legal baseline that defined what a player with a guaranteed contract was required to do in order to keep his coin intact.
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course, it's easy to think someone else should have their body cut open. Let Lee assume the health risks and subsequent months of rehabilitation, which might have allowed him to fully recover sometime this calendar year. A public song, with loud refrains that cite the $105.8 million he's earned during a 13-year career, ranks high on the social media hit parade.
         </p>
         <p>
          General manager Ruben Amaro, Junior initially acquired Lee from the Cleveland Indians in the summer of 2009. One year removed from an American League Cy Young season, his addition helped the Phillies return to the World Series.
         </p>
         <p>
          Lee's memorable postseason performances that fall inexplicably resulted in a December trade to the Seattle Mariners on the same day that Roy Halladay was obtained from the Toronto Blue Jays. It's fair to argue that the Phillies' dynastic hopes died on December 16, 2009.
         </p>
         <p>
          A massive free agent deal shocked the baseball world one day short of a year later, when Amaro pushed the New York Yankees' overtures aside. In 106 regular season games since Lee put red pinstripes on again he's posted 41 wins, a 1.085 WHIP and a 2.89 ERA.
         </p>
         <p>
          A lone playoff start, in his second Phillies' go-round, wasn't good. Yielding five earned runs, in six-plus innings, to the St. Louis Cardinals prevented Philadelphia from taking what would have been a commanding 2-0 lead in the 2011 National League Division Series. That postseason game, along with many other missed organizational opportunities that have followed, symbolizes overall frustrations.
         </p>
         <p>
          Lee must be considering the entirety of his professional career where medical decisions are concerned. He also may be looking forward to his post-baseball life.
         </p>
         <p>
          Currently, one of the best starting pitchers of this era sits on the 60-day DL and waits. As Lee continues to earn $25 million in 2015, maybe he'll eventually opt for surgery, accept the $12.5 million buyout when the season ends and try to pitch somewhere else next year. Then again, he might cash out, tell his agent to file retirement papers after some other team wins the World Series and wish for what might have been.
         </p>
