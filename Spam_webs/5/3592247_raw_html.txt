         <div class="entry-content">
          <div class="ndn_embed" data-config-distributor-id="90301" id="ndn-video-player-1">
          </div>
          <p>
           Scientists agree that
           <a href="http://www.spaceweatherlive.com/en">
            a solar flare Monday and an even larger one Wednesday
           </a>
           on the sun will increase the chance for spectacular displays of the northern lights Friday night.
          </p>
          <p>
           The
           <a href="http://www.oregonlive.com/pacific-northwest-news/index.ssf/2014/09/solar_activity_expected_to_pro.html">
            forecast for viewing in Oregon, however
           </a>
           , and for others in the mid-latitudes of North America, has dimmed, but not totally diminished.
          </p>
          <p>
           "As of now according to
           <a href="http://www.swpc.noaa.gov/ovation/#">
            the Ovation website
           </a>
           and the
           <a href="http://www.swpc.noaa.gov/rt_plots/kp_3d.html">
            KP index,
           </a>
           there is slim chance of visible auroras for the Portland area," said Jim Todd, director of space science education at OMSI. "In order for the auroras to be visible, the KP index needs to be 6 or higher for our latitude. This can change by the hour depending on the strength and timing of the solar impact, but it's likely the best viewing will be in the higher latitudes."
          </p>
          <p>
           Todd said if the
           <a href="http://www.spaceweatherlive.com/en/auroral-activity/kp">
            KP index (a measure of geomagnetic activity)
           </a>
           does reach 6, the northern lights would be best seen away from city lights.
          </p>
          <p>
           But all is not lost, Todd insisted. Not only can t
           <a href="http://www.space.com/27132-solar-storms-bombarding-earth-now.html">
            he forecast change by the hour
           </a>
           , a
           <a href="http://www.pcmag.com/encyclopedia/term/42047/dslr">
            DSLR
           </a>
           (digital single-lens reflex) camera may be able to capture an image of the lights even ifthe naked eye may not.
          </p>
          <p>
           While the aurora is often bright red and green in the higher latitudes of Canada and Alaska, the lights will be far more subtle and muted if they reach Oregon, he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           "We're just right on the line," Todd said. "People could be looking right at the lights and not even know it because it's very faint and ... not all that obvious."
          </p>
          <p>
           In addition to city lights obscuring the auroras, a waning
           <a href="http://www.universetoday.com/20324/gibbous-moon/">
            gibbous moon
           </a>
           that rises at 9:39 p.m. Friday will further hamper the view. Skies will be clear.
          </p>
          <p>
           "It's either going to happen or not," Todd said.
          </p>
          <p>
           For the best viewing, Todd said, go to some of the popular star-gazing spots around northwest Oregon and southwest Washington including
           <a href="http://www.oregonstateparks.org/index.cfm?do=parkPage.dsp_parkPage&amp;parkId=75">
            L.L. Stub Stewart State Park,
           </a>
           <a href="http://www.oregonstateparks.org/index.cfm?do=parkPage.dsp_parkPage&amp;parkId=108">
            Vista House at Crown Point
           </a>
           in the Columbia River Gorge or the Cascades of southern Oregon. In any case, look for an unobscured view of the northern horizon.
          </p>
          <p>
           -- Stuart Tomlinson
          </p>
