     <p>
      <b>
       Phonetics
      </b>
      (pronounced
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="'f' in 'find'">
           f
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'e' in 'bed'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
           t
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'i' in 'bid'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      , from the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%86%CF%89%CE%BD%CE%AE" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      ,
      <i>
       phn
      </i>
      , 'sound, voice') is a branch of
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      that comprises the study of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">
       sounds
      </a>
      of human
      <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">
       speech
      </a>
      , orin the case of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sign_languages" title="Sign languages">
       sign languages
      </a>
      the equivalent aspects of sign.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is concerned with the physical properties of speech sounds or signs (
      <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
       phones
      </a>
      ): their physiological production, acoustic properties, auditory perception, and neurophysiological status.
      <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
       Phonology
      </a>
      , on the other hand, is concerned with the abstract, grammatical characterization of systems of sounds or signs.
     </p>
     <p>
      The field of phonetics is a multilayered subject of
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      that focuses on speech. In the case of oral languages there are three basic areas of study:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Articulatory_phonetics" title="Articulatory phonetics">
        Articulatory phonetics
       </a>
       : the study of the production of speech sounds by the articulatory and vocal tract by the speaker.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_phonetics" title="Acoustic phonetics">
        Acoustic phonetics
       </a>
       : the study of the physical transmission of speech sounds from the speaker to the listener.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Auditory_phonetics" title="Auditory phonetics">
        Auditory phonetics
       </a>
       : the study of the reception and perception of speech sounds by the listener.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      These areas are inter-connected through the common mechanism of sound, such as wavelength (
      <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">
       pitch
      </a>
      ), amplitude, and harmonics.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Phonetics was studied as early as the 3rd century BC in the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
       Indian subcontinent
      </a>
      , with
      <span class="Unicode">
       <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pini">
        Pini
       </a>
       's
      </span>
      account of the
      <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
       place
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
       manner of articulation
      </a>
      of consonants in his treatise on
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      . The major
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmic_family" title="Brahmic family">
       Indic alphabets
      </a>
      today order their consonants according to
      <span class="Unicode">
       Pini's
      </span>
      classification.
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern phonetics begins with attemptssuch as those of Joshua Steele (in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prosodia_Rationalis" title="Prosodia Rationalis">
        Prosodia Rationalis
       </a>
      </i>
      , 1779) and
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Melville_Bell" title="Alexander Melville Bell">
       Alexander Melville Bell
      </a>
      (in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_Speech" title="Visible Speech">
        Visible Speech
       </a>
      </i>
      , 1867)to introduce systems of precise notation for speech sounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The study of phonetics grew quickly in the late 19th century partly due to the invention of phonograph, which allowed the speech signal to be recorded. Phoneticians were able to replay the speech signal several times and apply acoustic filters to the signal. In doing so, one was able to more carefully deduce the acoustic nature of the speech signal.
     </p>
     <p>
      Using an Edison phonograph,
      <a href="/wiki/Ludimar_Hermann" title="Ludimar Hermann">
       Ludimar Hermann
      </a>
      investigated the spectral properties of vowels and consonants. It was in these papers that the term
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Formant" title="Formant">
        formant
       </a>
      </i>
      was first introduced. Hermann also played vowel recordings made with the Edison phonograph at different speeds in order to test
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilfrid_Willis" title="Wilfrid Willis">
       Willis'
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Charles_Wheatstone" title="Sir Charles Wheatstone">
       Wheatstone's
      </a>
      theories of vowel production.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_phonology">
       Relation to phonology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to phonology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In contrast to phonetics,
      <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
       phonology
      </a>
      is the study of how sounds and gestures pattern in and across languages, relating such concerns with other levels and aspects of language. Phonetics deals with the articulatory and acoustic properties of speech sounds, how they are produced, and how they are perceived. As part of this investigation, phoneticians may concern themselves with the physical properties of meaningful sound contrasts or the social meaning encoded in the speech signal (
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Socio-phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Socio-phonetics (page does not exist)">
       socio-phonetics
      </a>
      ) (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">
       gender
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">
       sexuality
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">
       ethnicity
      </a>
      , etc.). However, a substantial portion of research in phonetics is not concerned with the meaningful elements in the speech signal.
     </p>
     <p>
      While it is widely agreed that phonology is grounded in phonetics, phonology is a distinct branch of linguistics, concerned with sounds and gestures as abstract units (e.g.,
      <a href="/wiki/Distinctive_feature" title="Distinctive feature">
       distinctive features
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
       phonemes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">
       mora
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllables" title="Syllables">
       syllables
      </a>
      , etc.) and their conditioned variation (via, e.g.,
      <a href="/wiki/Allophonic_rule" title="Allophonic rule">
       allophonic rules
      </a>
      , constraints, or
      <a href="/wiki/Phonological_rule" title="Phonological rule">
       derivational rules
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Phonology relates to phonetics via the set of
      <a href="/wiki/Distinctive_feature" title="Distinctive feature">
       distinctive features
      </a>
      , which map the abstract representations of speech units to articulatory gestures, acoustic signals, and/or perceptual representations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Subfields">
       Subfields
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subfields">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <b>
       Phonetics
      </b>
      as a research discipline has three main branches:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Articulatory_phonetics" title="Articulatory phonetics">
        Articulatory phonetics
       </a>
       is concerned with the articulation of speech: The position, shape, and movement of
       <i>
        articulators
       </i>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Speech_organ" title="Speech organ">
        speech organs
       </a>
       , such as the lips, tongue, and
       <a href="/wiki/Vocal_folds" title="Vocal folds">
        vocal folds
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_phonetics" title="Acoustic phonetics">
        Acoustic phonetics
       </a>
       is concerned with
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">
        acoustics
       </a>
       of speech: The spectro-temporal properties of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">
        sound waves
       </a>
       produced by speech, such as their
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        frequency
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">
        amplitude
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmonic_structure" title="Harmonic structure">
        harmonic structure
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Auditory_phonetics" title="Auditory phonetics">
        Auditory phonetics
       </a>
       is concerned with
       <a href="/wiki/Speech_perception" title="Speech perception">
        speech perception
       </a>
       : the
       <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">
        perception
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Categorization" title="Categorization">
        categorization
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Recall_(memory)" title="Recall (memory)">
        recognition
       </a>
       of speech sounds and the role of the
       <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">
        auditory system
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">
        brain
       </a>
       in the same.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transcription">
       Transcription
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transcription">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_transcription" title="Phonetic transcription">
       Phonetic transcription
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_transcription" title="Phonetic transcription">
       Phonetic transcription
      </a>
      is a system for transcribing sounds that occur in a language, whether
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oral_language" title="Oral language">
       oral
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
       sign
      </a>
      . The most widely known system of phonetic transcription, the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
       International Phonetic Alphabet
      </a>
      (IPA), provides a standardized set of symbols for oral phones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Grady-p17_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-O.27Grady-p17-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The standardized nature of the IPA enables its users to transcribe accurately and consistently the phones of different languages,
      <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
       dialects
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Idiolect" title="Idiolect">
       idiolects
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Grady-p17_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-O.27Grady-p17-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladefoged.2C_Peter_1975_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ladefoged.2C_Peter_1975-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The IPA is a useful tool not only for the study of phonetics, but also for language teaching, professional acting, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_pathology" title="Speech pathology">
       speech pathology
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladefoged.2C_Peter_1975_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ladefoged.2C_Peter_1975-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Applications of phonetics include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Forensic_linguistics" title="Forensic linguistics">
        Forensic phonetics
       </a>
       : the use of phonetics (the science of speech) for forensic (legal) purposes.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Speech_recognition" title="Speech recognition">
        Speech recognition
       </a>
       : the analysis and transcription of recorded speech by a computer system.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_phonetic_training">
       Practical phonetic training
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonetics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Practical phonetic training">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Studying phonetics involves not only learning theoretical material but also undergoing training in the production and perception of speech sounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The latter is often known as
      <i>
       ear-training
      </i>
      . Students must learn control of
      <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
       articulatory variables
      </a>
      and develop their ability to recognize fine differences between different vowels and consonants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As part of the training, they must become expert in using phonetic symbols, usually those of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
       International Phonetic Alphabet
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
