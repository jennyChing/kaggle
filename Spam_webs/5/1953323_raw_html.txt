     <p>
      The
      <b>
       categorical imperative
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
       <i>
        kategorischer Imperativ
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is the central
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">
       philosophical
      </a>
      concept in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deontological" title="Deontological">
       deontological
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_philosophy#Deontology" title="Moral philosophy">
       moral philosophy
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
       Immanuel Kant
      </a>
      . Introduced in Kant's 1785
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grounding_for_the_Metaphysics_of_Morals" title="Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals">
        Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
       </a>
      </i>
      , it may be defined as a way of evaluating
      <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">
       motivations
      </a>
      for action.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Kant, human beings occupy a special place in creation, and morality can be summed up in an imperative, or ultimate commandment of reason, from which all duties and obligations derive. He defined an imperative as any proposition declaring a certain action (or inaction) to be
      <a href="/wiki/Logical_possibility" title="Logical possibility">
       necessary
      </a>
      .
      <br/>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Hypothetical_imperative" title="Hypothetical imperative">
       Hypothetical imperatives
      </a>
      apply to someone who wishes to attain certain ends. For example:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       if I wish to quench my thirst, I must drink something;
      </li>
      <li>
       if I wish to acquire knowledge, I must learn.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A categorical imperative, on the other hand, denotes an absolute, unconditional requirement that must be obeyed in all circumstances and is justified as an end in itself. It is best known in its first formulation:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Act only according to that
       <a href="/wiki/Maxim_(philosophy)" title="Maxim (philosophy)">
        maxim
       </a>
       whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellington_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ellington-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Kant expressed extreme dissatisfaction with the popular moral philosophy of his day, believing that it could never surpass the level of hypothetical imperatives: a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilitarian" title="Utilitarian">
       utilitarian
      </a>
      says that murder is wrong because it does not maximize good for those involved, but this is irrelevant to people who are concerned only with maximizing the positive outcome for themselves. Consequently, Kant argued, hypothetical moral systems cannot persuade moral action or be regarded as bases for moral judgments against others, because the imperatives on which they are based rely too heavily on subjective considerations. He presented a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deontological" title="Deontological">
       deontological
      </a>
      moral system, based on the demands of the categorical imperative, as an alternative.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_the_concept">
       Nature of the concept
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nature of the concept">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The capacity that underlies deciding what is moral is called
      <a href="/wiki/Pure_practical_reason" title="Pure practical reason">
       pure practical reason
      </a>
      , which is contrasted with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_reason" title="Pure reason">
       pure reason
      </a>
      (the capacity to know without having been shown) and mere
      <a href="/wiki/Practical_reason" title="Practical reason">
       practical reason
      </a>
      (which allows us to interact with the world in experience).
      <a href="/wiki/Hypothetical_imperative" title="Hypothetical imperative">
       Hypothetical imperatives
      </a>
      tell us which means best achieve our ends. They do not, however, tell us which ends we should choose. The typical dichotomy in choosing ends is between ends that are "right" (e.g., helping someone) and those that are "good" (e.g., enriching oneself). Kant considered the "right" superior to the "good"; to him, the "good" was morally irrelevant. In Kant's view, a person cannot decide whether conduct is "right," or moral, through empirical means. Such judgments must be reached
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_priori_knowledge" title="A priori knowledge">
       <i>
        a priori
       </i>
      </a>
      , using pure practical reason.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Reason, separate from all empirical experience, can determine the principle according to which all ends can be determined as moral. It is this fundamental principle of moral reason that is known as the categorical imperative. Pure practical reason is the process of determining what ought to be done without reference to empirical contingent factors. Moral questions are determined independent of reference to the particular subject posing them. It is because morality is determined by pure practical reason rather than particular empirical or sensuous factors that morality is universally valid. This
      <a href="/wiki/Moral_universalism" title="Moral universalism">
       moral universalism
      </a>
      has come to be seen as the distinctive aspect of Kant's moral philosophy and has had wide social impact in the legal and political concepts of
      <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">
       human rights
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">
       equality
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Freedom_and_autonomy">
       Freedom and autonomy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Freedom and autonomy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kant viewed the human individual as a rationally self-conscious being with "impure" freedom of choice:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The faculty of desire in accordance with concepts, in-so-far as the ground determining it to action lies within itself and not in its object, is called a faculty to "do or to refrain from doing as one pleases". Insofar as it is joined with one's consciousness of the ability to bring about its object by one's action it is called
       <i>
        choice
       </i>
       (Willkr); if it is not joined with this consciousness its act is called a
       <i>
        wish
       </i>
       . The faculty of desire whose inner determining ground, hence even what pleases it, lies within the subject's reason is called the
       <i>
        will
       </i>
       (Wille). The will is therefore the faculty of desire considered not so much in relation to action (as choice is) but rather in relation to the ground determining choice in action. The will itself, strictly speaking, has no determining ground; insofar as it can determine choice, it is instead practical reason itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       Insofar as reason can determine the faculty of desire as such, not only
       <i>
        choice
       </i>
       but also mere
       <i>
        wish
       </i>
       can be included under the will. That choice which can be determined by
       <i>
        pure reason
       </i>
       is called free choice. That which can be determined only by
       <i>
        inclination
       </i>
       (sensible impulse,
       <i>
        stimulus
       </i>
       ) would be animal choice (
       <i>
        arbitrium brutum
       </i>
       ). Human choice, however, is a choice that can indeed be
       <i>
        affected
       </i>
       but not
       <i>
        determined
       </i>
       by impulses, and is therefore of itself (apart from an acquired proficiency of reason) not pure but can still be determined to actions by pure will.
      </p>
      <p>
       - Immanuel Kant, Metaphysics of Morals, 6:213-4
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      For a will to be considered "free", we must understand it as capable of affecting causal power without being caused to do so. But the idea of lawless
      <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">
       free will
      </a>
      , that is, a will acting without any causal structure, is incomprehensible. Therefore, a free will must be acting under laws that it gives to
      <i>
       itself
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Although Kant conceded that there could be no conceivable example of free will, because any example would only show us a will as it
      <i>
       appears
      </i>
      to us  as a subject of natural laws  he nevertheless argued against
      <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">
       determinism
      </a>
      . He proposed that determinism is logically inconsistent: The determinist claims that because
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      caused
      <i>
       B
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       B
      </i>
      caused
      <i>
       C
      </i>
      , that
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      is the true cause of
      <i>
       C
      </i>
      . Applied to a case of the human will, a determinist would argue that the will does not have causal power and that something outside the will causes the will to act as it does. But this argument merely assumes what it sets out to prove: viz. that the human will is part of the causal chain.
     </p>
     <p>
      Secondly, Kant remarks that free will is
      <i>
       inherently unknowable
      </i>
      . Since even a free person could not possibly have knowledge of their own freedom, we cannot use our failure to find a proof for freedom as evidence for a lack of it. The observable world could never contain an example of freedom because it would never show us a will as it appears to
      <i>
       itself
      </i>
      , but only a will that is subject to natural laws imposed on it. But we do appear to ourselves as free. Therefore he argued for the idea of transcendental freedom  that is, freedom as a presupposition of the question "what
      <i>
       ought
      </i>
      I to do?" This is what gives us sufficient basis for ascribing moral responsibility: the rational and self-actualizing power of a person, which he calls moral
      <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">
       autonomy
      </a>
      : "the property the will has of being a law unto itself."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Good_will.2C_duty.2C_and_the_categorical_imperative">
       Good will, duty, and the categorical imperative
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Good will, duty, and the categorical imperative">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Since considerations of the physical details of actions are necessarily bound up with a person's subjective preferences, and could have been brought about without the action of a rational will, Kant concluded that the expected
      <a href="/wiki/Consequentialism" title="Consequentialism">
       consequences
      </a>
      of an act are themselves morally neutral, and therefore irrelevant to moral deliberation. The only objective basis for moral value would be the rationality of the good will, expressed in recognition of
      <a href="/wiki/Moral" title="Moral">
       moral
      </a>
      duty.
     </p>
     <p>
      Duty is the necessity to act out of reverence for the moral law set by the categorical imperative. Only a categorical imperative, then, can be the supreme principle of morality.
     </p>
     <p>
      An absolute right.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_First_Formulation">
       The First Formulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The First Formulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div style="font-size:115%">
      <table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
       <tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
        <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
         
        </td>
        <td style="padding:0 10px;">
         Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law without contradiction.
        </td>
        <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
         
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px;">
         <p style="font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right">
          
          <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
           Immanuel Kant
          </a>
          , Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellington_1-1">
           <a href="#cite_note-Ellington-1">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            1
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p>
      From this step, Kant concludes that a moral proposition that is true must be one that is not tied to any particular conditions, including the identity of the person making the moral deliberation. A moral maxim must imply absolute necessity, which is to say that it must be disconnected from the particular physical details surrounding the proposition, and could be applied to any rational being. This leads to the first formulation of the categorical imperative, sometimes called the "universalizability principle":
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law without contradiction."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellington_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ellington-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Kant divides the duties imposed by this formulation into two subsets:
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Perfect_duty">
       Perfect duty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Perfect duty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to his reasoning, we first have a perfect duty not to act by maxims that result in logical contradictions when we attempt to universalize them. The moral proposition
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      : "It is permissible to steal" would result in a contradiction upon universalisation. The notion of stealing presupposes the existence of property, but were
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      universalized, then there could be no property, and so the proposition has logically negated itself.
     </p>
     <p>
      In general, perfect duties are those that are blameworthy if not met, as they are a basic required duty for a human being.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Imperfect_duty">
       Imperfect duty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Imperfect duty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Second, we have imperfect duties, which are still based on pure reason, but which allow for desires in how they are carried out in practice. Because these depend somewhat on the subjective preferences of humankind, this duty is not as strong as a perfect duty, but it is still morally binding. As such, unlike perfect duties, you do not attract blame should you not complete an imperfect duty but you shall receive praise for it should you complete it, as you have gone beyond the basic duties and taken duty upon yourself. Imperfect duties are circumstantial, meaning simply that you could not reasonably exist in a constant state of performing that duty. This is what truly differentiates between perfect and imperfect duties, because imperfect duties are those duties that are never truly completed. A particular example provided by Kant is the imperfect duty to cultivate one's own talents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Second_Formulation">
       The Second Formulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Second Formulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div style="font-size:115%">
      <table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
       <tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
        <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
         
        </td>
        <td style="padding:0 10px;">
         Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end.
        </td>
        <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
         
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px;">
         <p style="font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right">
          
          <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
           Immanuel Kant
          </a>
          , Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellington_1-3">
           <a href="#cite_note-Ellington-1">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            1
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p>
      Every rational action must set before itself not only a principle, but also an end. Most ends are of a subjective kind, because they need only be pursued if they are in line with some particular hypothetical imperative that a person may choose to adopt. For an end to be objective, it would be necessary that we categorically pursue it.
     </p>
     <p>
      The free will is the source of all rational action. But to treat it as a subjective end is to deny the possibility of freedom in general. Because the autonomous will is the one and only source of moral action, it would contradict the first formulation to claim that a person is merely a means to some other end, rather than always an end in themselves.
     </p>
     <p>
      On this basis, Kant derives the second formulation of the categorical imperative from the first.
     </p>
     <p>
      By combining this formulation with the first, we learn that a person has
      <i>
       perfect duty
      </i>
      not to use the humanity of themselves or others merely as a means to some other end. As a
      <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">
       slaveowner
      </a>
      would be effectively asserting a moral right to own a person as a slave, they would be asserting a property right in another person. But this would violate the categorical imperative because it denies the basis for there to be free rational action at all; it denies the status of a person as an end in themselves. One cannot, on Kant's account, ever suppose a right to treat another person as a mere means to an end.
     </p>
     <p>
      The second formulation also leads to the
      <i>
       imperfect duty
      </i>
      to further the ends of ourselves and others. If any person desires perfection in themselves or others, it would be their moral duty to seek that end for all people equally, so long as that end does not contradict perfect duty.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Third_Formulation">
       The Third Formulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Third Formulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div style="font-size:115%">
      <table class="rquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">
       <tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
        <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
         
        </td>
        <td style="padding:0 10px;">
         Therefore, every rational being must so act as if he were through his maxim always a legislating member in the universal kingdom of ends.
        </td>
        <td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">
         
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px;">
         <p style="font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right">
          
          <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
           Immanuel Kant
          </a>
          , Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellington_1-4">
           <a href="#cite_note-Ellington-1">
            <span>
             [
            </span>
            1
            <span>
             ]
            </span>
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p>
      Because a truly autonomous will would not be subjugated to any interest, it would only be subject to those laws it makes for itself  but it must also regard those laws as if they would be bound to others, or they would not be universalizable, and hence they would not be laws of conduct at all. Thus Kant presents the notion of the hypothetical
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ends" title="Kingdom of Ends">
       Kingdom of Ends
      </a>
      of which he suggests all people should consider themselves both means and ends.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always at the same time as an end and never merely as a means."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      We ought to act only by maxims that would harmonize with a possible kingdom of ends. We have
      <i>
       perfect duty
      </i>
      not to act by maxims that create incoherent or impossible states of natural affairs when we attempt to universalize them, and we have
      <i>
       imperfect duty
      </i>
      not to act by maxims that lead to unstable or greatly undesirable states of affairs.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Normative_interpretation">
       Normative interpretation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Normative interpretation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although Kant was intensely critical of the use of examples as moral yardsticks, because they tend to rely on our moral intuitions (feelings) rather than our rational powers, this section will explore some interpretations of the categorical imperative for illustrative purposes.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Deception">
       Deception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_mental_reservation" title="Doctrine of mental reservation">
       Doctrine of mental reservation
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Kant asserted that lying, or deception of any kind, would be forbidden under any interpretation and in any circumstance. In
      <i>
       Grounding
      </i>
      , Kant gives the example of a person who seeks to borrow money without intending to pay it back. This is a contradiction because if it were a universal action, no person would lend money anymore as he knows that he will never be paid back. The maxim of this action, says Kant, results in a contradiction in conceivability (and thus contradicts perfect duty). With lying, it would logically contradict the reliability of language. If it were universally acceptable to lie, then no one would believe anyone and all truths would be assumed to be lies. The right to deceive could also not be claimed because it would deny the status of the person deceived as an end in itself. The theft would be incompatible with a possible kingdom of ends. Therefore, Kant denied the right to lie or deceive for any reason, regardless of context or anticipated consequences.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theft">
       Theft
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Theft">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kant argued that any action taken against another person to which he or she
      <i>
       could not possibly
      </i>
      consent is a violation of perfect duty interpreted through the second formulation. If a thief were to steal a book from an unknowing victim, it may have been that the victim would have agreed, had the thief simply asked. However, no person can consent to theft, because the presence of consent would mean that the transfer was not a theft. Because the victim could not have consented to the action, it could not be instituted as a universal law of nature, and theft contradicts perfect duty.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Suicide">
       Suicide
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Suicide">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kant applied his categorical imperative to the issue of suicide in
      <i>
       Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      writing that:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       A man reduced to despair by a series of misfortunes feels sick of life, but is still so far in possession of his reason that he can ask himself whether taking his own life would not be contrary to his duty to himself. Now he asks whether the maxim of his action could become a universal law of nature. But his maxim is this: from self-love I make as my principle to shorten my life when its continued duration threatens more evil than it promises satisfaction. There only remains the question as to whether this principle of self-love can become a universal law of nature. One sees at once that a contradiction in a system of nature whose law would destroy life by means of the very same feeling that acts so as to stimulate the furtherance of life, and hence there could be no existence as a system of nature. Therefore, such a maxim cannot possibly hold as a universal law of nature and is, consequently, wholly opposed to the supreme principle of all duty.
       <p style="text-align: right;">
         Immanuel Kant,
        <i>
         Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals, p30-31
        </i>
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Laziness">
       Laziness
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Laziness">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kant also applies the categorical imperative in
      <i>
       Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
      </i>
      on the subject of "failing to cultivate one's talents." He proposes a man who if he cultivated his talents could bring many goods, but he has everything he wants and would prefer to enjoy the pleasures of life instead. The man asks himself how the universality of such a thing works. While Kant agrees that a society could subsist if everyone did nothing, he notes that the man would have no pleasures to enjoy, for if everyone let their talents go to waste, there would be no one to create luxuries that created this theoretical situation in the first place. Not only that, but cultivating one's talents is a duty to oneself. Thus, it is not willed to make laziness universal, and a rational being has imperfect duty to cultivate its talents. Kant concludes in
      <i>
       Grounding
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       ...he cannot possibly will that this should become a universal law of nature or be implanted in us as such a law by a natural instinct. For as a rational being he necessarily wills that all his faculties should be developed, inasmuch as they are given him for all sorts of possible purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Charity">
       Charity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Charity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kant's last application of the categorical imperative in
      <i>
       Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
      </i>
      is of charity. He proposes a fourth man who finds his own life fine but sees other people struggling with life and who ponders the outcome of doing nothing to help those in need (while not envying them or accepting anything from them). While Kant admits that humanity could subsist (and admits it could possibly perform better) if this were universal, he states in
      <i>
       Grounding
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       But even though it is possible that a universal law of nature could subsist in accordance with that maxim, still it is impossible to will that such a principle should hold everywhere as a law of nature. For a will that resolved in this way would contradict itself, inasmuch as cases might often arise in which one would have need of the love and sympathy of others and in which he would deprive himself, by such a law of nature springing from his own will, of all hope of the aid he wants for himself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cruelty_to_animals">
       Cruelty to animals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cruelty to animals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kant derived a prohibition against
      <a href="/wiki/Cruelty_to_animals" title="Cruelty to animals">
       cruelty to animals
      </a>
      as a violation of a duty in relation to oneself. According to Kant, man has the imperfect duty to strengthen the feeling of compassion, since this feeling promotes morality in relation to other human beings. But, cruelty to animals deadens the feeling of compassion in man. Therefore, man is obliged not to treat animals brutally (Kant, Metaphysics of Morals,  17).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Normative_criticism">
       Normative criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Normative criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Golden_Rule">
       The Golden Rule
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The Golden Rule">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first formulation of the Categorical Imperative appears similar to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethic_of_reciprocity" title="Ethic of reciprocity">
       The Golden Rule
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The 'Golden Rule' (in its negative form) says: "Do not impose on others what you do not wish for yourself."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 'Golden Rule' (in its positive form) says: "Treat others how you wish to be treated".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Kant's first formulation of CI says: "Act only in accordance with that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law."
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to this similarity, some have thought the two are identical.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Corning" title="Peter Corning">
       Peter Corning
      </a>
      suggests that, "Kant's objection to the Golden Rule is especially suspect because the categorical imperative (CI) sounds a lot like a paraphrase, or perhaps a close cousin, of the same fundamental idea. In effect, it says that you should act toward others in ways that you would want everyone else to act toward others, yourself included (presumably). Calling it a universal law does not materially improve on the basic concept."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Corning claims that Ken Binmore thought so as well.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kant himself did not think so in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grounding_for_the_Metaphysics_of_Morals" title="Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals">
        Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      Rather, the categorical imperative is an attempt to identify
      <i>
       a purely formal
      </i>
      and necessarily universally binding rule on all rational agents. The Golden Rule, on the other hand, is neither purely formal nor necessarily universally binding. It is "empirical" in the sense that applying it depends on providing content, like "If you don't want others to hit you, then don't hit them." Also, it is a hypothetical imperative in the sense that it can be formulated, "If you want X done to you, then do X to others." Kant feared that the hypothetical clause, "if you want X done to you," remains open to dispute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He wanted an imperative that was categorical: "Do X to others." And this he thinks he discovered and formulated. Kant thought, therefore, that the Golden Rule (insofar as it is accurate) is derived from the categorical imperative .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inquiring_murderer">
       Inquiring murderer
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Inquiring murderer">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One of the first major challenges to Kant's reasoning came from the
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       French
      </a>
      philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Constant" title="Benjamin Constant">
       Benjamin Constant
      </a>
      , who asserted that since truth telling must be universal, according to Kant's theories, one must (if asked) tell a known murderer the location of his prey. This challenge occurred while Kant was still alive, and his response was the essay
      <i>
       On a Supposed Right to Tell Lies from Benevolent Motives
      </i>
      (sometimes translated
      <i>
       On a Supposed Right to Lie because of Philanthropic Concerns
      </i>
      ). In this reply, Kant agreed with Constant's inference, that from Kant's premises one must infer a moral duty not to lie to a murderer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kant denied that such an inference indicates any weakness in his premises: not lying to the murderer is required because moral actions do not derive their worth from the expected consequences. He claimed that because lying to the murderer would treat him as a mere means to another end, the lie denies the rationality of another person, and therefore denies the possibility of there being free rational action at all. This lie results in a contradiction in conceivably and therefore the lie is in conflict with duty.
     </p>
     <p>
      Constant and Kant agree that refusing to answer the murderer's question is consistent with the categorical imperative, but assume for the purposes of argument that refusing to answer would not be an option, perhaps because of duress such as kidnapping, torture, or extortion. Neither of them explain why such duress would cause a truthful answer to be more consistent with the categorical imperative than refusing to answer. Subsequent commentators on the challenge often point out that there is likely no such reason.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Questioning_autonomy">
       Questioning autonomy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Questioning autonomy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schopenhauer%27s_criticism_of_the_Kantian_philosophy#Categorical_Imperative" title="Schopenhauer's criticism of the Kantian philosophy">
       Schopenhauer's criticism of the Kantian philosophy
      </a>
      expresses doubt concerning the absence of
      <a href="/wiki/Egotism" title="Egotism">
       egoism
      </a>
      in the Categorical Imperative.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schopenhauer" title="Schopenhauer">
       Schopenhauer
      </a>
      claimed that the Categorical Imperative is actually
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypothetical" title="Hypothetical">
       hypothetical
      </a>
      and egotistical, not categorical.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kierkegaard" title="Kierkegaard">
       Kierkegaard
      </a>
      believed Kantian autonomy was insufficient and that, if unchecked, people tend to be lenient in their own case, either by not exercising the full rigor of the moral law or by not properly disciplining themselves of moral transgressions:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Kant was of the opinion that man is his own law (autonomy) - that is, he binds himself under the law which he himself gives himself. Actually, in a profounder sense, this is how lawlessness or experimentation are established. This is not being rigorously earnest any more than
       <a href="/wiki/Sancho_Panza" title="Sancho Panza">
        Sancho Panza
       </a>
       's self-administered blows to his own bottom were vigorous. ... Now if a man is never even once willing in his lifetime to act so decisively that [a lawgiver] can get hold of him, well, then it happens, then the man is allowed to live on in self-complacent illusion and make-believe and experimentation, but this also means: utterly without grace.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       
       <i>
        Papers and Journals
       </i>
      </div>
     </blockquote>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kant.27s_categorical_imperative_and_the_trial_of_Adolf_Eichmann">
       Kant's categorical imperative and the trial of Adolf Eichmann
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_imperative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Kant's categorical imperative and the trial of Adolf Eichmann">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1961, discussion of Kant's categorical imperative included even the trial of the infamous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">
       SS
      </a>
      Lieutenant Colonel
      <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Eichmann" title="Adolf Eichmann">
       Adolf Eichmann
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">
       Jerusalem
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As
      <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Arendt" title="Hannah Arendt">
       Hannah Arendt
      </a>
      wrote in her book on the trial, Eichmann declared "with great emphasis that he had lived his whole life ... according to a Kantian definition of duty". Arendt considered this so "incomprehensible on the face of it" that it confirmed her sense that he wasn't really thinking at all, just mouthing accepted formulae, thereby establishing his banality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arendt_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Arendt-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Judge Raveh indeed had asked Eichmann whether he thought he had really lived according to the categorical imperative during the war. Eichmann acknowledged he did not "live entirely according to it, although I would like to do so."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arendt_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Arendt-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Lipstadt" title="Deborah Lipstadt">
       Deborah Lipstadt
      </a>
      , in her book on the trial, takes this as evidence that evil is not
      <i>
       banal
      </i>
      , but is in fact
      <i>
       self-aware
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     </p>
