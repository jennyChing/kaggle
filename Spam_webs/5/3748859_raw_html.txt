   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Juggling robot
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       juggling robot
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">
       robot
      </a>
      designed to be able to successfully carry out
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bounce_juggling" title="Bounce juggling">
       bounce
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Toss_juggling" title="Toss juggling">
       toss juggling
      </a>
      . Robots capable of juggling are designed and built both to increase and test understanding and theories of human movement, juggling, and
      <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">
       robotics
      </a>
      . Juggling robots may include sensors to guide arm/hand movement or may rely on physical methods such as tracks or funnels to guide prop movement. Since true juggling requires more
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juggling_prop" title="Juggling prop">
       props
      </a>
      than hands, many robots described as capable of juggling are not.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bounce_juggling">
       Bounce juggling
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juggling_robot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bounce juggling">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A toss juggling robot that can do more than a two ball
      <a href="/wiki/Columns_(juggling)" title="Columns (juggling)">
       column
      </a>
      has only recently been built. However,
      <a href="/wiki/Claude_Shannon" title="Claude Shannon">
       Claude Shannon
      </a>
      built the first juggling robot, a 3-ball bounce juggler, from an
      <a href="/wiki/Erector_Set" title="Erector Set">
       Erector Set
      </a>
      , in the 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B.26L_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-B.26L-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 1992,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Christopher G. Atkeson and Stefan K. Schaal of the
      <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">
       Georgia Institute of Technology
      </a>
      built a similar 5-ball bounce juggling robot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B.26L_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-B.26L-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Decorated as and named
      <a href="/wiki/W._C._Fields" title="W. C. Fields">
       W. C. Fields
      </a>
      , Shannon's machine used
      <a href="/wiki/Groove_(engineering)" title="Groove (engineering)">
       grooved
      </a>
      cups/tracks instead of sensors or feedback.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shannon also devised a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juggling_theorem" title="Juggling theorem">
       juggling theorem
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1989 Martin Bhler and Daniel E. Koditschek produced a juggler with one rotating bar, moving one way then the other, that bounces two-props in a
      <a href="/wiki/Fountain_(juggling)" title="Fountain (juggling)">
       fountain
      </a>
      of indefinite length.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B.26L_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-B.26L-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Toss_juggling">
       Toss juggling
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juggling_robot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toss juggling">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sakaguchi et. al. (1991) and Miyazaki (1993) produced a one-armed two-ball fountain juggler with a funnel-shaped hand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Kizaki and Namiki (2012) developed a fingered robot that does the same.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 2011 students at the Department of Control Engineering at Prague's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_Technical_University" title="Czech Technical University">
       Czech Technical University
      </a>
      built a 5-ball cascade juggling robot whose arms have both vertical and horizontal motion, whose hands are ring-shaped, and which contains a basket that provides the initial throws and relaunches any failed catches.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Disney_Research" title="Disney Research">
       Disney Research
      </a>
      is developing a robot capable of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pass_juggling" title="Pass juggling">
       pass juggling
      </a>
      with the goal of being able to provide more
      <a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Humancomputer interaction">
       physical interaction
      </a>
      between visitors and mechanized characters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_juggling">
       Contact juggling
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juggling_robot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contact juggling">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Contact_juggling" title="Contact juggling">
       Contact juggling
      </a>
      appears to be less common among robots, as it is with people. However, in 2010 undergraduates at
      <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">
       Northwestern University
      </a>
      developed a robot capable of rolling a grooved disk from the center, over the edge, and to the center of the other side of a figure-eight shaped track capable of rotation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
