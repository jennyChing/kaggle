     <p>
      <b>
       Unitarianism
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">
       Christian theological
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociological_classifications_of_religious_movements" title="Sociological classifications of religious movements">
       movement
      </a>
      named for the affirmation that
      <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">
       God
      </a>
      is one entity, in direct contrast to
      <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">
       Trinitarianism
      </a>
      , which defines God as three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(philosophy)" title="Hypostasis (philosophy)">
       persons
      </a>
      coexisting
      <a href="/wiki/Consubstantiality" title="Consubstantiality">
       consubstantially
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Homoousian" title="Homoousian">
       one being
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Knight-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unitarians maintain that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth" title="Jesus of Nazareth">
       Jesus of Nazareth
      </a>
      is in some sense the "son" of God (as all humans are children of the Creator), but that he is not the one God himself. (Bryan Dao, God Balance)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miano_2003_15_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Miano_2003_15-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They may believe that he was
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inspiration" title="Biblical inspiration">
       inspired
      </a>
      by God in his moral teachings and can be considered a savior,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but all Unitarians perceive Christ as human rather than divine. Unitarianism is also known for the rejection of several other
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">
       Western Christian
      </a>
      doctrines,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      including the
      <a href="/wiki/Soteriology" title="Soteriology">
       soteriological
      </a>
      doctrines of
      <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">
       original sin
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predestination_in_Christianity" title="Predestination in Christianity">
       predestination
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and, in more recent history,
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inerrancy" title="Biblical inerrancy">
       biblical inerrancy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unitarians in previous centuries accepted the doctrine of punishment in an eternal
      <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">
       hell
      </a>
      , but few do today.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Unitarian movement was not called "Unitarian" initially. It began almost simultaneously in Poland-Lithuania and
      <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Transylvania_(1570%E2%80%931711)" title="Principality of Transylvania (15701711)">
       Transylvania
      </a>
      in the mid-16th century. Among the adherents were a significant number of Italians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In England, the first Unitarian Church was established in 1774 on Essex Street,
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      , where today's British Unitarian headquarters are still located.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since the theology was also perceived as
      <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">
       deistic
      </a>
      , it began to attract many people from wealthy and educated backgrounds,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although it was only at the late second half of the 18th century that it started to gain some wider traction within
      <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">
       Christendom
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the United States, it spread first in
      <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
       New England
      </a>
      , and the first official acceptance of the Unitarian faith on the part of a congregation in America was by
      <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Chapel" title="King's Chapel">
       King's Chapel
      </a>
      in Boston, from where
      <a href="/wiki/James_Freeman_(clergyman)" title="James Freeman (clergyman)">
       James Freeman
      </a>
      began teaching Unitarian doctrine in 1784, and was appointed rector and revised the Prayer Book according to Unitarian doctrines in 1786.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/J._Gordon_Melton" title="J. Gordon Melton">
       J. Gordon Melton
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Encyclopedia of American Religions
      </i>
      , it is classified among "the '
      <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Christianity" title="Liberal Christianity">
       liberal
      </a>
      ' family of churches".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
       Terminology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "Unitarianism" is a
      <a href="/wiki/Proper_noun" title="Proper noun">
       proper noun
      </a>
      and follows the same English usage as other theologies that have developed within a religious movement (
      <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">
       Calvinism
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptism" title="Anabaptism">
       Anabaptism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Adventism" title="Adventism">
       Adventism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wesleyanism" title="Wesleyanism">
       Wesleyanism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">
       Lutheranism
      </a>
      , etc.).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term existed shortly before it became the name of a religious movement, and thus occasionally it is used as a common noun that would describe any
      <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">
       understanding of Jesus Christ
      </a>
      that denies the Trinity or which believes that God is only one person. In that case it would be a
      <a href="/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">
       nontrinitarian
      </a>
      belief system not necessarily associated with the Unitarian religious movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, the Unitarian movement has never accepted the Godhood of Jesus, and therefore does not include those
      <i>
       nontrinitarian
      </i>
      belief systems that dosuch as
      <a href="/wiki/Oneness_Pentecostalism" title="Oneness Pentecostalism">
       Oneness Pentecostalism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/United_Pentecostal_Church_International" title="United Pentecostal Church International">
       United Pentecostal Church International
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/True_Jesus_Church" title="True Jesus Church">
       True Jesus Church
      </a>
      and the writings of
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">
       Michael Servetus
      </a>
      and which maintain that Jesus is God as a single person. Although these groups are unitarians in the common sense, they are not in the proper sense. To avoid confusion, this article is about Unitarianism as a religious movement (proper noun). For the generic form of unitarianism (the
      <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">
       Christology
      </a>
      ), see
      <a href="/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">
       Nontrinitarianism
      </a>
      . Recently some religious groups have adopted the 19th-century term "
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Unitarianism" title="Biblical Unitarianism">
       biblical unitarianism
      </a>
      " to distinguish their theology from Unitarianism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These likewise have no direct relation to the Unitarian movement.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       Unitarian
      </i>
      is sometimes applied today to those who belong to a Unitarian church but who do not hold a Unitarian theological belief.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the past, the vast majority of members of Unitarian churches were Unitarians also in theology. Over time, however, some Unitarians and Unitarian Universalists moved away from the traditional Christian roots of Unitarianism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, in the 1890s the American Unitarian Association began to allow non-Christian and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-theism" title="Non-theism">
       non-theistic
      </a>
      churches and individuals to be part of their fellowship.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result, people who held no Unitarian belief began to be called "Unitarians" because they were members of churches that belonged to the American Unitarian Association. After several decades, the non-theistic members outnumbered the theological Unitarians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A similar, though proportionally much smaller, phenomenon has taken place in the Unitarian churches in the United Kingdom, Canada, and other countries, which remain more theistically based. Unitarian theology, therefore, is distinguishable from the belief system of modern Unitarian and
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalism" title="Unitarian Universalism">
       Unitarian Universalist
      </a>
      churches and fellowships. This article includes information about Unitarianism as a theology and about the development of theologically Unitarian churches. For a more specific discussion of Unitarianism as it evolved into a pluralistic
      <a href="/wiki/Liberal_religion" title="Liberal religion">
       liberal religious
      </a>
      movement, see
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalism" title="Unitarian Universalism">
       Unitarian Universalism
      </a>
      (and its national groups the
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">
       Unitarian Universalist Association
      </a>
      in the United States, the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Unitarian_Council" title="Canadian Unitarian Council">
       Canadian Unitarian Council
      </a>
      in Canada, the
      <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_Unitarian_and_Free_Christian_Churches" title="General Assembly of Unitarian and Free Christian Churches">
       General Assembly of Unitarian and Free Christian Churches
      </a>
      in the United Kingdom, and the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Unitarians_and_Universalists" title="International Council of Unitarians and Universalists">
       International Council of Unitarians and Universalists
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Unitarianism" title="History of Unitarianism">
       History of Unitarianism
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Unitarianism, both as a theology and as a
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">
       denominational
      </a>
      family of churches, was defined and developed in five countries: Poland, Transylvania, England, Wales and America. Although there were common beliefs among Unitarians in each of these regions, they initially grew independently from each other. Only later did they influence one another and accumulate more similarities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Ecclesia minor
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Minor Reformed Church of Poland
      </i>
      , better known today as the
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_Brethren" title="Polish Brethren">
       Polish Brethren
      </a>
      , was born as the result of a controversy that started on January 22, 1556, when
      <a href="/wiki/Piotr_of_Goni%C4%85dz" title="Piotr of Gonidz">
       Piotr of Gonidz
      </a>
      (Peter Gonesius), a Polish student, spoke out against the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_the_Trinity" title="Doctrine of the Trinity">
       doctrine of the Trinity
      </a>
      during the general synod of the Reformed (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvinist" title="Calvinist">
       Calvinist
      </a>
      ) churches of Poland held in the village of
      <a href="/wiki/Secemin" title="Secemin">
       Secemin
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After nine years of debate, in 1565, the anti-Trinitarians were excluded from the existing synod of the
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_Reformed_Church" title="Polish Reformed Church">
       Polish Reformed Church
      </a>
      (henceforth the
      <i>
       Ecclesia maior
      </i>
      ) and they began to hold their own synods as the
      <i>
       Ecclesia minor
      </i>
      . Though frequently called "Arians" by those on the outside, the views of
      <a href="/wiki/Fausto_Sozzini" title="Fausto Sozzini">
       Fausto Sozzini
      </a>
      became the standard in the church, and these doctrines were quite removed from
      <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">
       Arianism
      </a>
      . So important was Sozzini to the formulation of their beliefs that those outside Poland usually referred to them as
      <a href="/wiki/Socinianism" title="Socinianism">
       Socinians
      </a>
      . The Polish Brethren were disbanded in 1658 by the
      <a href="/wiki/Sejm" title="Sejm">
       Sejm
      </a>
      (Polish Parliament). They were ordered to convert to Roman Catholicism or leave Poland. Most of them went to Transylvania or Holland, where they embraced the name "Unitarian." Sozzini's grandson
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrzej_Wiszowaty_Sr." title="Andrzej Wiszowaty Sr.">
       Andrzej Wiszowaty Sr.
      </a>
      in 16651668 published
      <i>
       Bibliotheca Fratrum Polonorum quos Unitarios vocant
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Library of the Polish Brethren who are called Unitarians
      </i>
      4 vols. 166569).
     </p>
     <p>
      The Unitarian Church in Transylvania was first recognized by the
      <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Torda" title="Edict of Torda">
       Edict of Torda
      </a>
      , issued by the
      <a href="/wiki/Transylvanian_Diet" title="Transylvanian Diet">
       Transylvanian Diet
      </a>
      under
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rulers_of_Transylvania" title="Rulers of Transylvania">
       Prince
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_II_Sigismund_Z%C3%A1polya" title="John II Sigismund Zpolya">
       John II Sigismund Zpolya
      </a>
      (January 1568),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poperamet_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-poperamet-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was first led by
      <a href="/wiki/Ferenc_D%C3%A1vid" title="Ferenc Dvid">
       Ferenc Dvid
      </a>
      (a former
      <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">
       Calvinist
      </a>
      bishop, who had begun preaching the new doctrine in 1566). The term "Unitarian" first appeared as
      <i>
       unitaria religio
      </i>
      in a document of the Diet of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le%C8%9B" title="Le">
       Lcfalva
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">
       Transylvania
      </a>
      , on 25 October 1600, though it was not widely used in Transylvania until 1638, when the formal
      <i>
       recepta Unitaria Religio
      </i>
      was published.
     </p>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       Unitarian
      </i>
      had been circulating in private letters in England, in reference to imported copies of such publications as the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_Fratrum_Polonorum" title="Bibliotheca Fratrum Polonorum">
        Library of the Polish Brethren who are called Unitarians
       </a>
      </i>
      (1665).
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hedworth" title="Henry Hedworth">
       Henry Hedworth
      </a>
      was the first to use the word "Unitarian" in print in English (1673), and the word first appears in a title in
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Nye" title="Stephen Nye">
       Stephen Nye
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       A brief history of the Unitarians, called also Socinians
      </i>
      (1687). The movement gained popularity in England in the wake of the
      <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">
       Enlightenment
      </a>
      and began to become a formal denomination in 1774 when
      <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_Lindsey" title="Theophilus Lindsey">
       Theophilus Lindsey
      </a>
      organised meetings with
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">
       Joseph Priestley
      </a>
      , founding the first avowedly Unitarian congregation in the country, at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essex_Street_Church" title="Essex Street Church">
       Essex Street Church
      </a>
      in London.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first official acceptance of the Unitarian faith on the part of a congregation in America was by
      <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Chapel" title="King's Chapel">
       King's Chapel
      </a>
      in Boston, which settled
      <a href="/wiki/James_Freeman_(clergyman)" title="James Freeman (clergyman)">
       James Freeman
      </a>
      (17591835) in 1782, and revised the Prayer Book into a mild Unitarian liturgy in 1785. In 1800,
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stevens_Buckminster" title="Joseph Stevens Buckminster">
       Joseph Stevens Buckminster
      </a>
      became minister of the
      <a href="/wiki/Brattle_Street_Church" title="Brattle Street Church">
       Brattle Street Church
      </a>
      in Boston, where his brilliant sermons, literary activities, and academic attention to the German "New Criticism" helped shape the subsequent growth of Unitarianism in New England. Unitarian
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ware_(Unitarian)" title="Henry Ware (Unitarian)">
       Henry Ware
      </a>
      (17641845) was appointed as the Hollis professor of divinity at Harvard College, in 1805. Harvard Divinity school then shifted from its conservative roots to teach Unitarian theology. See:
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Divinity_School#Harvard_Divinity_School_and_Unitarianism" title="Harvard Divinity School">
       Harvard &amp; Unitarianism
      </a>
      . Buckminster's close associate
      <a href="/wiki/William_Ellery_Channing" title="William Ellery Channing">
       William Ellery Channing
      </a>
      (17801842) was settled over the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Street_Church_(Boston)" title="Federal Street Church (Boston)">
       Federal Street Church
      </a>
      in Boston, 1803, and in a few years he became the leader of the Unitarian movement. A theological battle with the Congregational Churches resulted in the formation of the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Association" title="American Unitarian Association">
       American Unitarian Association
      </a>
      at Boston in 1825.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">
       Beliefs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beliefs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Christology">
       Christology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Christology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Unitarians believe that mainline Christianity does not adhere to strict
      <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">
       monotheism
      </a>
      but that they do by maintaining that Jesus was a great man and a
      <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">
       prophet
      </a>
      of God, perhaps even a
      <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">
       supernatural
      </a>
      being, but not God himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miano_2003_15_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Miano_2003_15-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They believe Jesus did not claim to be God and that his teachings did not suggest the existence of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triune_God" title="Triune God">
       triune God
      </a>
      . Unitarians believe in the
      <a href="/wiki/Moral_authority" title="Moral authority">
       moral authority
      </a>
      but not necessarily the
      <a href="/wiki/Divinity" title="Divinity">
       divinity
      </a>
      of Jesus. Their theology is thus opposed to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitarianism" title="Trinitarianism">
       trinitarian
      </a>
      theology of other
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">
       Christian denominations
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Unitarian Christology can be divided according to whether Jesus is believed to have had a pre-human existence. Both forms maintain that
      <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
       God
      </a>
      is one being and one "person" and that Jesus is the (or a)
      <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">
       Son of God
      </a>
      , but generally not God himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 19th century, Unitarian
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wallace_(Unitarian)" title="Robert Wallace (Unitarian)">
       Robert Wallace
      </a>
      identified three particular classes of Unitarian doctrines in history:
      <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">
       Arians
      </a>
      , which believed in a pre-existence of
      <a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">
       the divine spirit
      </a>
      , but maintained that Jesus was created and lived as human only; "
      <a href="/wiki/Socinianism" title="Socinianism">
       Socinians
      </a>
      ", which, denied his original divinity, but agreed that Christ should be
      <a href="/wiki/Worship" title="Worship">
       worshipped
      </a>
      ; and "Strict unitarians", which, believing in an "incommunicable divinity of God", denied both the existence of the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">
       Holy Spirit
      </a>
      and the worship of "the man Christ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unitarianism is considered a factor in the decline of classical deism because there were people who increasingly preferred to identify themselves as Unitarians rather than deists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several tenets of unitarianism overlap with the beliefs of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muwahhid_Muslims" title="Muwahhid Muslims">
       Muwahhid Muslims
      </a>
      ,.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Socinian.22_Christology">
       "Socinian" Christology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: "Socinian" Christology'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Christology commonly called "
      <a href="/wiki/Socinianism" title="Socinianism">
       Socinian
      </a>
      " (after
      <a href="/wiki/Fausto_Sozzini" title="Fausto Sozzini">
       Fausto Sozzini
      </a>
      , one of the founders of Unitarian theology) refers to the belief that Jesus Christ began his life when he was born as a human. In other words, the teaching that Jesus
      <a href="/wiki/Pre-existence_of_Christ" title="Pre-existence of Christ">
       pre-existed
      </a>
      his human body is rejected. There are various views ranging from the belief that Jesus was simply a human (
      <a href="/wiki/Psilanthropism" title="Psilanthropism">
       psilanthropism
      </a>
      ) who, because of his greatness, was adopted by God as his Son (
      <a href="/wiki/Adoptionism" title="Adoptionism">
       adoptionism
      </a>
      ) to the belief that Jesus literally became the
      <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">
       son of God
      </a>
      when he was conceived by the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">
       Holy Spirit
      </a>
      (see
      <a href="/wiki/Virgin_birth_of_Jesus" title="Virgin birth of Jesus">
       Virgin birth of Jesus
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      This Christology existed in some form or another prior to Sozzini.
      <a href="/wiki/Theodotus_of_Byzantium" title="Theodotus of Byzantium">
       Theodotus of Byzantium
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Artemon" title="Artemon">
       Artemon
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Samosata" title="Paul of Samosata">
       Paul of Samosata
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      denied the pre-existence of Christ. These ideas were continued by
      <a href="/wiki/Marcellus_of_Ancyra" title="Marcellus of Ancyra">
       Marcellus of Ancyra
      </a>
      and his pupil
      <a href="/wiki/Photinus" title="Photinus">
       Photinus
      </a>
      in the 4th century AD.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Radical_Reformation" title="Radical Reformation">
       Radical Reformation
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptist" title="Anabaptist">
       Anabaptist
      </a>
      movements of the 16th century this idea resurfaced with Sozzini's uncle,
      <a href="/wiki/Lelio_Sozzini" title="Lelio Sozzini">
       Lelio Sozzini
      </a>
      . Having influenced the
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_Brethren" title="Polish Brethren">
       Polish Brethren
      </a>
      to a formal declaration of this belief in the
      <a href="/wiki/Racovian_Catechism" title="Racovian Catechism">
       Racovian Catechism
      </a>
      , Fausto Sozzini involuntarily ended up giving his name to this Christological position,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which continued with English Unitarians such as
      <a href="/wiki/John_Biddle_(Unitarian)" title="John Biddle (Unitarian)">
       John Biddle
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Belsham" title="Thomas Belsham">
       Thomas Belsham
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_Lindsey" title="Theophilus Lindsey">
       Theophilus Lindsey
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">
       Joseph Priestley
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/James_Martineau" title="James Martineau">
       James Martineau
      </a>
      . In America, most of the early Unitarians were "Arian" in Christology (see below), but among those who held to a "Socinian" view was
      <a href="/wiki/James_Freeman_(clergyman)" title="James Freeman (clergyman)">
       James Freeman
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Regarding the virgin birth of Jesus among those who denied the preexistence of Christ, some held to it and others did not. Its denial is sometimes ascribed to the
      <a href="/wiki/Ebionites" title="Ebionites">
       Ebionites
      </a>
      ; however,
      <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">
       Origen
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Contra Celsum
      </i>
      v.61) and
      <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">
       Eusebius
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       HE
      </i>
      iii.27) both indicate that some Ebionites did accept the virgin birth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the other hand,
      <a href="/wiki/Theodotus_of_Byzantium" title="Theodotus of Byzantium">
       Theodotus of Byzantium
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Artemon" title="Artemon">
       Artemon
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Samosata" title="Paul of Samosata">
       Paul of Samosata
      </a>
      all accepted the virgin birth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stead_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stead-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the early days of Unitarianism, the stories of the virgin birth were accepted by most. The
      <i>
       Chambers Biographical Dictionary
      </i>
      (1897) incorrectly ascribes denial of the virgin birth to
      <a href="/wiki/Ferenc_D%C3%A1vid" title="Ferenc Dvid">
       Ferenc Dvid
      </a>
      , leader of the Transylvanian Unitarians.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      However, there were a number of Unitarians who questioned the historical accuracy of the Bible (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Symon_Budny" title="Symon Budny">
       Symon Budny
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Palaeologus" title="Jacob Palaeologus">
       Jacob Palaeologus
      </a>
      , Thomas Belsham, and
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wright_(Unitarian)" title="Richard Wright (Unitarian)">
       Richard Wright
      </a>
      ), and this made them question the virgin birth story.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Beginning in England and America in the 1830s, and manifesting itself primarily in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcendentalist_Unitarianism" title="Transcendentalist Unitarianism">
       Transcendentalist Unitarianism
      </a>
      , which emerged from the German liberal theology associated primarily with
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schleiermacher" title="Friedrich Schleiermacher">
       Friedrich Schleiermacher
      </a>
      , the psilanthropist view increased in popularity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its proponents took an intellectual and humanistic approach to religion. They embraced evolutionary concepts, asserted the "inherent goodness of man", and abandoned the doctrine of biblical infallibility, rejecting most of the miraculous events in the Bible (including the virgin birth). Notable examples are
      <a href="/wiki/James_Martineau" title="James Martineau">
       James Martineau
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Parker" title="Theodore Parker">
       Theodore Parker
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">
       Ralph Waldo Emerson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Henry_Hedge" title="Frederic Henry Hedge">
       Frederic Henry Hedge
      </a>
      . Famous American Unitarian
      <a href="/wiki/William_Ellery_Channing" title="William Ellery Channing">
       William Ellery Channing
      </a>
      was a believer in the virgin birth until later in his life, after he had begun his association with the Transcendentalists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Arian.22_Christology">
       "Arian" Christology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "Arian" Christology'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Christology commonly called "
      <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">
       Arian
      </a>
      " holds that Jesus, before his human life, existed as the
      <a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">
       Logos
      </a>
      , a being created by God, who dwelt with God in heaven. There are many varieties of this form of Unitarianism, ranging from the belief that the Son was a divine spirit of the same nature as God before coming to earth, to the belief that he was an angel or other lesser spirit creature of a wholly different nature from God.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Not all of these views necessarily were held by
      <a href="/wiki/Arius" title="Arius">
       Arius
      </a>
      , the namesake of this Christology. It is still Nontrinitarian because, according to this belief system, Jesus has always been beneath God, though higher than humans. Arian Christology was not a majority view among Unitarians in Poland, Transylvania or England. It was only with the advent of American Unitarianism that it gained a foothold in the Unitarian movement.
     </p>
     <p>
      Among early Christian theologians who believed in a pre-existent Jesus who was subordinate to God the Father were
      <a href="/wiki/Lucian_of_Antioch" title="Lucian of Antioch">
       Lucian of Antioch
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">
       Eusebius of Caesarea
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arius" title="Arius">
       Arius
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Nicomedia" title="Eusebius of Nicomedia">
       Eusebius of Nicomedia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Asterius_the_Sophist" title="Asterius the Sophist">
       Asterius the Sophist
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eunomius" title="Eunomius">
       Eunomius
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ulfilas" title="Ulfilas">
       Ulfilas
      </a>
      , as well as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felix,_Bishop_of_Urgell" title="Felix, Bishop of Urgell">
       Felix, Bishop of Urgell
      </a>
      . Proponents of this Christology also associate it (more controversially) with
      <a href="/wiki/Justin_Martyr" title="Justin Martyr">
       Justin Martyr
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">
       Hippolytus of Rome
      </a>
      . Antitrinitarian
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">
       Michael Servetus
      </a>
      did not deny the pre-existence of Christ, so he may have believed in it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (March 2013)">
          unreliable source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      (In his "Treatise Concerning the Divine Trinity" Servetus taught that the Logos (Word) was the reflection of Christ, and "that reflection of Christ was 'the Word with God" that consisted of God Himself, shining brightly in heaven, "and it was God Himself"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Servetus_1553_75_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Servetus_1553_75-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and that "the Word was the very essence of God or the manifestation of God's essence, and there was in God no other substance or hypostasis than His Word, in a bright cloud where God then seemed to subsist. And in that very spot the face and personality of Christ shone bright."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Servetus_1553_75_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Servetus_1553_75-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      )
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
       Isaac Newton
      </a>
      had Arian beliefs as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Famous 19th-century Arian Unitarians include
      <a href="/wiki/Andrews_Norton" title="Andrews Norton">
       Andrews Norton

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">

      and Dr.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_beliefs">
       Other beliefs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other beliefs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although there is no specific authority on convictions of Unitarian belief aside from rejection of the Trinity, the following beliefs are generally accepted:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       One God and the oneness or unity of God.
      </li>
      <li>
       The life and teachings of Jesus Christ constitute the exemplar model for living one's own life.
      </li>
      <li>
       Reason, rational thought, science, and philosophy coexist with faith in God.
      </li>
      <li>
       Humans have the ability to exercise
       <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">
        free will
       </a>
       in a responsible, constructive and ethical manner with the assistance of religion.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Human_nature" title="Human nature">
        Human nature
       </a>
       in its present condition is neither inherently corrupt nor depraved (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Original_Sin" title="Original Sin">
        original Sin
       </a>
       ) but capable of both good and evil, as God intended.
      </li>
      <li>
       No religion can claim an absolute monopoly on the Holy Spirit or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theological" title="Theological">
        theological
       </a>
       truth.
      </li>
      <li>
       Though the authors of the Bible were inspired by God, they were humans and therefore subject to human error.
      </li>
      <li>
       The traditional
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctrines" title="Doctrines">
        doctrines
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Predestination" title="Predestination">
        predestination
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hell_in_Christian_beliefs" title="Hell in Christian beliefs">
        eternal damnation
       </a>
       , and the vicarious sacrifice and satisfaction theories of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atonement_in_Christianity" title="Atonement in Christianity">
        Atonement
       </a>
       are invalid because they malign God's character and veil the true nature and mission of Jesus Christ.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
        <a href="#cite_note-63">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         63
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Unitarians have
      <a href="/wiki/Liberal_religion" title="Liberal religion">
       liberal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Conceptions_of_God" title="Conceptions of God">
       views of God
      </a>
      , Jesus, the
      <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">
       world
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Meaning_of_life" title="Meaning of life">
       purpose of life
      </a>
      as revealed through
      <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">
       reason
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Scholarship" title="Scholarship">
       scholarship
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
       science
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">
       philosophy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Religious_text" title="Religious text">
       scripture
      </a>
      and other prophets and
      <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">
       religions
      </a>
      . They believe that reason and belief are complementary and that
      <a href="/wiki/Relationship_between_religion_and_science" title="Relationship between religion and science">
       religion and science
      </a>
      can co-exist and guide them in their understanding of
      <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">
       nature
      </a>
      and God. They also do not enforce belief in
      <a href="/wiki/Creed" title="Creed">
       creeds
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">
       dogmatic
      </a>
      formulas. Although there is flexibility in the nuances of
      <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">
       belief
      </a>
      or basic
      <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">
       truths
      </a>
      for the individual Unitarian
      <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
       Christian
      </a>
      , general principles of faith have been recognized as a way to bind the group in some commonality. Adherents generally accept
      <a href="/wiki/Religious_pluralism" title="Religious pluralism">
       religious pluralism
      </a>
      and find value in all teachings, but remain committed to their core belief in
      <a href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">
       Christ
      </a>
      's teachings.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Unitarians generally value a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secularity_(non-religiosity)" title="Secularity (non-religiosity)">
       secular
      </a>
      society in which
      <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">
       government
      </a>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">
       kept separate from religious affairs
      </a>
      . Most contemporary Unitarian Christians believe that one's personal moral convictions guide one's political activities, and that a secular society is the most viable, just and fair.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Unitarian Christians reject the doctrine of some Christian denominations that God chooses to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redemption_(religious)" title="Redemption (religious)">
       redeem
      </a>
      or save only those certain individuals that accept the creeds of, or affiliate with, a specific church or religion, from a common ruin or corruption of the mass of humanity.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1938,
      <i>
       The Christian leader
      </i>
      attributed "
      <i>
       the
      </i>
      religion
      <i>
       of
      </i>
      Jesus, not
      <i>
       a
      </i>
      religion
      <i>
       about
      </i>
      Jesus" to Unitarians,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though the phrase was used earlier by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregationalist" title="Congregationalist">
       Congregationalist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Rollin_Lynde_Hartt" title="Rollin Lynde Hartt">
       Rollin Lynde Hartt
      </a>
      in 1924.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and earlier still by US President Thomas Jefferson.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Worship">
       Worship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Worship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Worship within the Unitarian tradition accommodates a wide range of understandings of
      <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
       God
      </a>
      , while the focus of the service may be simply the celebration of life itself. Each Unitarian congregation is at liberty to devise its own form of worship, though commonly, Unitarians will light their chalice (symbol of faith), have a story for all ages; and include sermons, prayers, hymns and songs. Some will allow attendees to publicly share their recent joys or concerns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_and_Transylvanian_Unitarian_Churches">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </a>

       John II Sigismund Zpolya
      </a>

     </h3>
     <p>
      The Unitarian Christian Conference USA is a network of congregations and ministers in the United States identifying with the historic Unitarian Christian tradition. The Unitarian Christian Conference USA promotes the concept of the unity of God and the message and example of Jesus of Nazareth as a rational and enriching spiritual path for personal development and a guide for creating a world of justice, peace and human dignity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="UUCF">
       UUCF
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: UUCF">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Christian_Fellowship" title="Unitarian Universalist Christian Fellowship">
       Unitarian Universalist Christian Fellowship
      </a>
      (UUCF, founded 1945) predates the consolidation of the American Unitarian Association (AUA) and
      <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">
       Universalist Church of America
      </a>
      (UCA) into the
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">
       Unitarian Universalist Association
      </a>
      (UUA) in 1961. UUCF continues as a subgroup of UUA serving the Christian members.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="ICUU">
       ICUU
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: ICUU">
        edit

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Other Unitarian Christian groups are affiliated with the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Unitarians_and_Universalists" title="International Council of Unitarians and Universalists">
       International Council of Unitarians and Universalists
      </a>
      (ICUU), founded in 1995. The ICUU tends to contain a majority membership who express specifically Unitarian Christian beliefs, rather than the religious pluralism of the UUA, but nevertheless remain liberal, open-minded and inclusive communities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The ICUU has "full member" groups in the United States, Australia, New Zealand, United Kingdom, Canada, Brazil, Czech Republic, Finland, Germany, Hungary, Indonesia, India, Nigeria, Pakistan, Philippines, Romania, South Africa, and Sri Lanka.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ICUU includes small "Associate groups", including Congregazione Italiana Cristiano Unitariana,
      <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">
       Turin
      </a>
      (founded in 2004)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">
       Oslo
      </a>
      (founded 2005).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">

      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lancelot_Garrard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lancelot Garrard (page does not exist)">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">
       South Africa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      The Unitarian movement in South Africa was founded in 1867 by the Reverend
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dawid_Faure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dawid Faure (page does not exist)">
       Dawid Faure
      </a>
      , member of a well-known Cape family. He encountered advanced liberal religious thought while completing his studies at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Leiden" title="University of Leiden">
       University of Leiden
      </a>
      in Holland for the ministry of the
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Reformed_Church_in_South_Africa" title="Dutch Reformed Church in South Africa">
       Dutch Reformed Church
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">
       Cape Town
      </a>
      . On his return to South Africa he preached a probationary sermon in the
      <a href="/wiki/Groote_Kerk,_Cape_Town" title="Groote Kerk, Cape Town">
       Groote Kerk, Cape Town
      </a>
      . This led to a public appeal to him to found a community based upon what was called the 'new theology'. The 'new theology' as preached by Dawid Faure was grounded in what he described as "the very essence of religion" - love of God and love of neighbor.

     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Unitarianism" title="Biblical Unitarianism">
       Biblical Unitarianism
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the mainstream of the Protestant Reformation there is the Biblical Unitarian Movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cf._SocinianismServetus_77-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cf._SocinianismServetus-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:TOPIC" title="Wikipedia:TOPIC">
         <span title="The material near this tag may contain the information irrelevant to the article's main topic. (December 2013)">
          relevant?
         </span>
        </a>
        <span class="metadata">
         
         <a href="/wiki/Talk:Unitarianism" title="Talk:Unitarianism">
          discuss
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Today, biblical Unitarianism (or "Biblical Unitarianism" or "biblical unitarianism")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      identifies the
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity#Beliefs" title="Christianity">
       Christian belief
      </a>
      that the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      teaches God is a singular personthe Fatherand that
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      is a distinct being, his son.
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Unitarianism#Denominations" title="Biblical Unitarianism">
       A few denominations
      </a>
      use this term to describe themselves, clarifying the distinction between them and those churches
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        79
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which, from the late 19th century, evolved into
      <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_Unitarian_and_Free_Christian_Churches" title="General Assembly of Unitarian and Free Christian Churches">
       modern British Unitarianism
      </a>
      and, primarily in the United States,
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalism" title="Unitarian Universalism">
       Unitarian Universalism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Italy the Biblical Unitarian Movement powered by the ideas of Sozzini and others
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cf._SocinianismServetus_77-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cf._SocinianismServetus-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is represented today by the churches associated with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Church_in_Italy" title="Christian Church in Italy">
       Christian Church in Italy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
       <a href="#cite_note-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        80
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This Movement in Italy claims a strong Christian and biblical soul. From the analysis of documents that you can find on the official site of the CCI,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sito-CCI_81-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sito-CCI-81">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        81
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiesa_Cristiana_in_Italia_82-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chiesa_Cristiana_in_Italia-82">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        82
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it is clear that the doctrinal position of this Christian confession of faith is therefore akin to the so-called Biblical Unitarian movement
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christadelphians_83-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Christadelphians-83">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        83
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Socinianism_84-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Socinianism-84">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        84
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polish_Brethren_85-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Polish_Brethren-85">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        85
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and on the other hand, far from that of
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">
       Unitarian Universalist Association
      </a>
      who, although sharing a 16th-century origin, have been influenced by many non-biblical ideas (e.g.,
      <a href="/wiki/Universalism" title="Universalism">
       Universalism
      </a>
      ). The Christian Church in Italy has significant similarities with the Biblical Unitarian movement
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2014)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , although it maintains a cautious position on some doctrinal points. Wilbur wrote about the Unitarian Movement:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       "The religious movement whose history we are endeavoring to trace...became fully developed in thought and polity in only four countries, one after another, namely Poland, Transylvania, England and America. But in each of these it showed, along with certain individual characteristics, a general spirit, a common point of view, and a doctrinal pattern that tempt one to regard them as all outgrowths of a single movement which passed from one to another; for nothing could be more natural than to presume that these common features implied a common ancestry. Yet such is not the fact, for in each of these four lands the movement, instead of having originated elsewhere, and been translated only after attaining mature growth, appears to have sprung independently and directly from its own native roots, and to have been influenced by other and similar movements only after it had already developed an independent life and character of its own."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">
        <a href="#cite_note-86">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         86
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The Christian Church in Italy believes that God is only One Person
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87">
       <a href="#cite_note-87">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        87
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in direct contrast with the doctrine of the Trinity which defines God as Three coexisting Persons in one Substance (Essence), merged into one being.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Knight-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      So CCI adheres to strict monotheism by believing that Jesus was a perfect and holy man,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88">
       <a href="#cite_note-88">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        88
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      virginally begotten in Mary, the promised Christ (i.e., Messiah), the Son of God, and is now at the right hand of God praying for the whole Church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89">
       <a href="#cite_note-89">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        89
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90">
       <a href="#cite_note-90">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        90
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Christian Church in Italy rejects certain traditional Christian doctrines
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91">
       <a href="#cite_note-91">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        91
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      including the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soteriological" title="Soteriological">
       soteriological
      </a>
      doctrines of
      <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">
       original sin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Predestination" title="Predestination">
       predestination
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92">
       <a href="#cite_note-92">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        92
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93">
       <a href="#cite_note-93">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        93
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The CCI is distinct from other religious movements which exalt Jesus as the only true God, as for example the
      <a href="/wiki/Oneness_Pentecostalism" title="Oneness Pentecostalism">
       Oneness Pentecostalism
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Pentecostal_Church_International" title="United Pentecostal Church International">
       United Pentecostal Church International
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/True_Jesus_Church" title="True Jesus Church">
       True Jesus Church
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_Unitarians">
       Notable Unitarians
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Unitarians,_Universalists,_and_Unitarian_Universalists" title="List of Unitarians, Universalists, and Unitarian Universalists">
       List of Unitarians, Universalists, and Unitarian Universalists
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Notable Unitarians include
      <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Bla Bartk">
       Bla Bartk
      </a>
      , the 20th-century composer,
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">
       Ralph Waldo Emerson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Parker" title="Theodore Parker">
       Theodore Parker
      </a>
      in theology and ministry,
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">
       Charles Darwin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">
       Joseph Priestley
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Archibald_Wheeler" title="John Archibald Wheeler">
       John Archibald Wheeler
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">
       Linus Pauling
      </a>
      in science,
      <a href="/wiki/George_Boole" title="George Boole">
       George Boole
      </a>
      in mathematics,
      <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">
       Susan B. Anthony
      </a>
      in civil government,
      <a href="/wiki/Florence_Nightingale" title="Florence Nightingale">
       Florence Nightingale
      </a>
      in humanitarianism and social justice,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Bowring" title="John Bowring">
       John Bowring
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">
       Samuel Taylor Coleridge
      </a>
      in literature,
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">
       Frank Lloyd Wright
      </a>
      in the arts,
      <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Wedgwood" title="Josiah Wedgwood">
       Josiah Wedgwood
      </a>
      in industry,
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Starr_King" title="Thomas Starr King">
       Thomas Starr King
      </a>
      in ministry and politics, and
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Eliot" title="Charles William Eliot">
       Charles William Eliot
      </a>
      in education. Although raised a Quaker, Ezra Cornell, founder of Cornell University in Ithaca, New York, attended the Unitarian church and was one of the founders of Ithaca's First Unitarian Church. Eramus Darwin Shattuck, a signatory to the Oregon State Constitution, founded the first Unitarian Church in Oregon in 1865.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97">
       <a href="#cite_note-97">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        97
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Eleven Nobel prizes have been awarded to Unitarians:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Millikan" title="Robert Millikan">
       Robert Millikan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Bardeen" title="John Bardeen">
       John Bardeen
      </a>
      (twice) in Physics;
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emily_Green_Balch" title="Emily Green Balch">
       Emily Green Balch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Schweitzer" title="Albert Schweitzer">
       Albert Schweitzer
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">
       Linus Pauling
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Geoff_Levermore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Geoff Levermore (page does not exist)">
       Geoff Levermore
      </a>
      for Peace;
      <a href="/wiki/George_Wald" title="George Wald">
       George Wald
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/David_H._Hubel" title="David H. Hubel">
       David H. Hubel
      </a>
      in Medicine;
      <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">
       Linus Pauling
      </a>
      in Chemistry; and
      <a href="/wiki/Herbert_A._Simon" title="Herbert A. Simon">
       Herbert A. Simon
      </a>
      in Economics.
     </p>
     <p>
      Four presidents of the United States were Unitarians:
      <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">
       John Adams
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">
       John Quincy Adams
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Millard_Fillmore" title="Millard Fillmore">
       Millard Fillmore
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">
       William Howard Taft
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">
       Adlai Stevenson II
      </a>
      , the Democratic presidential nominee in 1952 and 1956, was a Unitarian, and he was the last Unitarian (so far) to be nominated by a major party for president.
     </p>
     <p>
      British Prime Minister,
      <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">
       Neville Chamberlain
      </a>
      was raised by his Unitarian statesman father,
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">
       Joseph Chamberlain
      </a>
      . Certainly, in the United Kingdom, Unitarianism  the religion of only a small minority of the country's population  had an enormous impact on Victorian politics, not only in the larger cities 
      <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">
       Birmingham
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">
       Leeds
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">
       Manchester
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">
       Liverpool
      </a>
       but in smaller communities like
      <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">
       Leicester
      </a>
      where there were so many Unitarian mayors that the Unitarian Chapel was known as the "Mayors' Nest".
     </p>
     <p>
      In Birmingham, a most impressive Unitarian Church was opened in 1862. The Church of the Messiah, as it was called, was more than the centre of a small sect: it was a cultural and intellectual centre of a whole society, a place where ideas about society were openly and critically discussed. Henry W. Crosskeys Birmingham Unitarian congregation included:
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">
       Joseph Chamberlain
      </a>
      , as well as Arthur, his younger brother, who was married to Louisa Kenrick; William Kenrick, his brother-in-law, who was married to Mary Chamberlain; and
      <a href="/wiki/Martineau_family" title="Martineau family">
       Sir Thomas Martineau
      </a>
      , who was the nephew of
      <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Martineau" title="Harriet Martineau">
       Harriet Martineau
      </a>
      , another outspoken public figure and author of the time. Sir Thomas Martineau (died 1893), was related to the
      <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">
       Chamberlain family
      </a>
      by marriage; Sir Thomas had married Emily Kenrick, the sister of Florence Chamberlain, ne Kenrick.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98">
       <a href="#cite_note-98">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        98
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      These elite British Unitarian families: the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Nettlefold" title="Joseph Nettlefold">
       Nettlefolds
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Martineau_family" title="Martineau family">
       Martineaus
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Lupton_family" title="Lupton family">
       Luptons
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/James_Kitson,_1st_Baron_Airedale" title="James Kitson, 1st Baron Airedale">
       Kitsons
      </a>
      and the Kenricks, found a most significant place in the social and political history of Victorian through to mid-20th-century Britain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99">
       <a href="#cite_note-99">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        99
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100">
       <a href="#cite_note-100">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        100
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other Unitarians include Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">
       Tim Berners-Lee
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101">
       <a href="#cite_note-101">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        101
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Lancelot_Ware" title="Lancelot Ware">
       Lancelot Ware
      </a>
      , founder of
      <a href="/wiki/Mensa_International" title="Mensa International">
       Mensa
      </a>
      , Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Boult" title="Adrian Boult">
       Adrian Boult
      </a>
      , the conductor,
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Kurzweil" title="Ray Kurzweil">
       Ray Kurzweil
      </a>
      , notable inventor and futurist, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._Killick_Millard" title="C. Killick Millard">

      </a>
      , founder of the
      <a href="/wiki/Dignity_in_Dying" title="Dignity in Dying">

         , 712 World Congress of Faiths  1994  "In an otherwise excellent article by Jasbir Singh Ahluwalia, 'Sikh Spirit in an Age of Plurality' (No. 6, November 1993), the writer makes a number of pejorative remarks about 'unitarianism', associating the term with a striving for a monolithic polity and reductionism to a common denominator. This is a very unfortunate misuse of the word. A correct definition of 'unitarianism' (small 'u') is the mono-
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(philosophy)#Trinitarian_definitions" title="Hypostasis (philosophy)">
          hypo-static
         </a>
         belief system of someone not directly associated with the Unitarian movement, almost always applied to a person from the Christian tradition, as the word was coined in distinction to the orthodox 'Trinitarian' doctrine of Christianity. 'Unitarians' (capital 'U') are, of course, those who follow the Unitarian approach to religion and are formally associated with the movement. In neither case can it be claimed that there is an underlying agenda towards reductionism and uniformity. Quite the reverse, in fact. Modern Unitarianism is remarkable among religions in not only welcoming the variety of faiths that there are to be found but also, as a creedless church, welcoming and encouraging acceptance of the same. We readily accept that not all our members are 'realist' theists, for example. Our long-standing commitment to interfaith understanding, evident in our practical support of the International Association for Religious Freedom, the World Congress of Faiths and the newly established International Interfaith centre in Oxford cannot be taken to mean that Unitarians are seeking the creation of a single world religion out of the old. I do not know a single Unitarian who believes or seeks that. On the contrary, we reject uniformity and cherish instead the highest degree of spiritual integrity, both of the existing religious traditions of the world and of religious persons as unique, thinking individuals. Matthew F Smith, Information Officer" (

         "The name originated at the time of the great dispute at Gyulafehrvr in 1568, in the course of which Mlius quite often concluded his argument by saying,

          Ergo Deus est trinitarius
         </i>
         .... Hence his party naturally came to be called Trinitarians and their opponents would naturally be called Unitarians. The name seems thus to have come into general use only gradually and it was long before it was employed in the formal proclamations of their Superintendents.... It is not found in print as the denomination of the church until 1600, when the
         <i>
          unitaria religio
         </i>
         is named as one of the four received religions in a decree of the Diet of Lczfalva (cf. Magyar Emlkek, iv, 551) in the extreme southeastern part of Transylvania. The name was never used by the Socinians in Poland; but late in the seventeenth century Transylvanian Unitarian students made it well-known in Holland, where the Socinians in exile, who had never adopted Socinian as the name of their movement and were more and more objecting to it, welcomed it as distinguishing them from Trinitarians. It thus gradually superseded the term Socinian, and spread to England and America." Earl Morse Wilbur,

Translated from Hungarian by Gyorgy Andrasi, published in The Unitarian Universalist Christian, FALL/WINTER, 1994, Volume 49, Nos.34; VII:107).
        </span>
       </li>
       <li id="cite_note-93">
        <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
         <b>
          <a href="#cite_ref-93">
           ^
          </a>
         </b>
        </span>
        <span class="reference-text">
         In his history of the Unitarians, David Robinson writes: "At their inception, both Unitarians and Universalists shared a common theological enemy: Calvinism." He explains that they "consistently attacked Calvinism on the related issues of original sin and election to salvation, doctrines that in their view undermined human moral exertion." (D. Robinson,
         <i>
          The Unitarians and the Universalists
         </i>
         , Greenwood Press, 1985, pp. 3, 17.)
