         </p>

          Choose a wildlife attraction in Asheville:
         </p>

          1)
          <a href="#section1">
           Visiting the WNC Nature Center
          </a>
          <br/>
          2)
          <a href="#section2">
           Birdwatching in and around Asheville
          </a>
          <br/>
          3)
          <a href="#section3">
           Visiting the elk in the Smoky Mountains
          </a>
          <br/>
          4)
          <a href="#section4">
           Looking for black bears in the Smoky Mountains
          </a>

         <hr/>
         <h3>
          1) Visiting the WNC Nature Center
         </h3>
         <p>
          The
          <a href="http://www.wildwnc.org/" target="_blank" title="WNC Nature Center">
           Western North Carolina Nature Center
          </a>
          is not your usual zoo. You wont find
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/photo-of-the-week-giraffe-in-the-kalahari/" title="Photo of the Week: Giraffe in the Kalahari">
           giraffes
          </a>
          or cute lion cubs but you will find plenty of species native to North Carolina. The center only accepts animals that have been injured or confiscated and cannot be released back into the wild. The mission of the WNC Nature Center is to educate visitors about conservation.
         </p>
         <p>
          A member of the
          <a href="http://www.aza.org/" target="_blank" title="Association of Zoos and Aquariums">
           Association of Zoos and Aquariums
          </a>
          , the WNC Nature Center takes part in the red wolf
          <a href="http://www.aza.org/species-survival-plan-program/" target="_blank" title="Red Wolf Recovery Program">
           Species Survival Plan
          </a>
          and is one of the few places in the US where you can
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/photo-week-red-wolf-wnc-nature-center/" title="Photo of the Week: Red Wolf, WNC Nature Center">
           see this endangered canid
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_5039" style="width: 826px">
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/re_wolf_wnc.jpg" rel="themeblvd_lightbox" title="Red wolf at the WNC Nature Center">
           <img alt="Red wolf at the WNC Nature Center" class=" wp-image-5039 " height="544" src="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/re_wolf_wnc.jpg" title="Red wolf at the WNC Nature Center" width="816"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Red wolf at the WNC Nature Center
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Other highlights of a visit to the WNC Nature Center include sneaking a peek at a napping
          <strong>
           least weasel
          </strong>
          and hunting under rocks for the astonishing
          <strong>
           hellbender
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The least weasel is a nocturnal predator and is the worlds smallest member of the order
          <em>
           Carnivora
          </em>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Also known as the mud puppy or the snot otter, the hellbender is one of the largest salamanders in the world and can grow to be over two feet long. They are very susceptible to water pollution and have been classified as a Species of Concern in various states, including North Carolina.
         </p>
         <p>
          Never seen one?
          <br/>
          <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/piY-tlDkJj8" width="560">
          </iframe>
         </p>

         </p>

         <h3>
          2) Birdwatching in and Around Asheville
         </h3>
         <p>
          From warblers to flycatchers, woodpeckers to cuckoos, Western North Carolina is a great destination for bird enthusiasts.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_5037" style="width: 874px">
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/birdwatching.jpg" rel="themeblvd_lightbox" title="Birdwatching around Asheville">
           <img alt="Birdwatching in North Carolina" class="wp-image-5037 " height="648" src="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/birdwatching.jpg" title="Birdwatching around Asheville" width="864"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Birdwatching in Asheville
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Our friends at
          <a href="http://www.birdventures.com/DayTrips.html" target="_blank" title="Bird Ventures">
           Bird Ventures
          </a>
          offer many day trips around the Asheville area and the Blue Ridge Parkway.
         </p>
         <p>
          They also offer longer birdwatching trips to other parts of the US such as Alaska, and some great international trips including South Africa and Ecuador.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_5038" style="width: 586px">
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/264266_504759202905791_1473246157_n.jpg" rel="themeblvd_lightbox" title="See migratory birds like this Indigo Bunting">
           <img alt="See migratory birds like this Indigo Bunting (Passerina cyanea) " class="wp-image-5038 " height="383" src="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/264266_504759202905791_1473246157_n.jpg" title="See migratory birds like this Indigo Bunting" width="576"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           See migratory birds like this Indigo Bunting (Passerina cyanea)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="#top">
           Back to top
          </a>
          <br/>
          <a name="section3">
          </a>
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <h3>
          3) Visiting Elk in the Smoky Mountains
         </h3>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_4934" style="width: 727px">
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/big-bull.jpg" rel="themeblvd_lightbox" title="Bull elk during rut">
           <img alt="Elk bull" class=" wp-image-4934 " height="477" src="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/big-bull.jpg" title="Bull elk during rut" width="717"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Elk bull with huge antlers in the Smoky Mountains
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Around 140 elk (2012 survey) live in the Smoky Mountains. They concentrate around the eastern end of the park, and Cataloochee Valley is the best place to see them. Luckily, this is the closest part of the Smokies to Asheville and can be reached in 45 minutes.
         </p>
         <p>
          October and November are the high season for watching elk as their antlers are fully grown and the rut is in full swing. The bizarre screeching sounds of bugling bull elk fill every corner of the valley.
         </p>
         <p>
          <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/jxx3NK49DSQ" width="560">
          </iframe>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          For more information about elk seasons, how to get to Calaoochee, and where to stay; visit our post on
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/where-to-see-elk-in-the-smoky-mountains/" title="Where to see Elk in the Smoky Mountains">
           Elk in the Smoky Mountains.
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="#top">
           Back to top
          </a>
          <br/>
          <a name="section4">
          </a>
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <h3>
          4) Spotting Black Bears in the Smoky Mountains
         </h3>
         <p>
          Though black bears do occasionally wander right into Asheville, your best chance of spotting one is in the Great Smoky Mountains National Park a couple hours west of town. The Smoky Mountains have a high density of black bears and at 2 bears per square mile, you are almost guaranteed to see some sign of their presence such as prints or a scat. When hiking in bear country, always be aware of your surroundings and make noise so that bears know where you are. You dont want to startle them.
         </p>
         <p>
          If your goal is to see black bears in the Smokies, your best bet is to head to the western side of the park, into Cades Cove. Bears can be sighted anywhere in the park at any time, but Cades Cove offers open fields and a good deal of edge habitat which makes bear viewing far more predictable. The loop road is especially famous for wildlife watching. If you dont happen to see any black bears, youre still virtually guaranteed to spot white-tailed deer and turkeys. You might even get lucky and catch a glimpse of a coyote or a bobcat.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_5042" style="width: 727px">
          <a href="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/turkey.jpg" rel="themeblvd_lightbox" title="If you don't see bears you are practically guaranteed to spot a handsome wild turkey">
           <img alt="If you don't see bears you are practically guaranteed to spot a handsome wild turkey" class=" wp-image-5042" height="477" src="http://travel4wildlife.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/turkey.jpg" title="If you don't see bears you are practically guaranteed to spot a handsome wild turkey" width="717"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Dont worry, if you dont see black bears you are practically guaranteed to spot a handsome wild turkey
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Note: It is a 3-hour drive to Cades Cove from Asheville, so plan accordingly.
         </p>
