            <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
             Late Victorian pleasure piers Vs inter-war lidos
            </h3>
            <div class="post-header">
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-314165722253325296" itemprop="description articleBody">
             As I showed in my post on
             <a href="http://melbourneblogger.blogspot.com/2009/11/19th-century-amusement-centres-healthy.html">
              late 19th century amusement centres
             </a>
             , sporting activity, fun and healthy living were de rigeur amongst those families with some leisure time on their hands.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
              <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_knHmw4EPfhE/SwvJql1JPZI/AAAAAAAABO4/zFa2Iy6-mQE/s1600/BrightonPier1899.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
               <img border="0" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_knHmw4EPfhE/SwvJql1JPZI/AAAAAAAABO4/zFa2Iy6-mQE/s400/BrightonPier1899.jpg" yr="true"/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
              <span style="font-size: x-small;">
               Brighton Pier 1899
              </span>
             </div>
             <br/>
             <strong>
              iknow Sussex
             </strong>
             made my point about
             <a href="http://www.iknow-sussex.co.uk/tourist_information/all_sussex/all_sussex/seaside_architecture_sussex.htm">
              Victorian and Edwardian seaside architecture
             </a>
             . Hastings Pier was the creation of seaside architect Eugenius Birch who modelled the Hastings design on Brighton's West Pier (since lost). Hastings Pier first opened in 1872 and
             <a href="http://www.thehistoryblog.com/archives/7834#comment-54075">
              until 2010
             </a>
             wasa hub for craft shops and amusements. At Eastbourne, Birch's glorious pier (1872) still stands in good condition. Eastbourne Pier stretches 1000 out to sea and with its wide promenade proved perfect strolling ground for the upper classes arriving at Eastbourne and the Devonshire Hotels through the Victorian and Edwardian era.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Once the railway from London reached Southend, there was a greatinflux of summer time visitors. The large number of people must have made a bit of a mess of the existing wooden pier and in 1873, it was sold to the local council. Soon the
             <a href="http://goldenagepaintings.blogspot.com/2010/09/southend-pier-late-1890s.html">
              Southend council
             </a>
             decided to replace the pier with a new ironstructure which was completed in 1889 at great expense.
             <br/>
             <div>
              <span style="font-family: inherit;">
              </span>
             </div>
             <div>
              <span style="font-family: inherit;">
              </span>
             </div>
             <br/>
             <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
              <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_knHmw4EPfhE/S3u7QiouvQI/AAAAAAAABjo/wZBr2JsH6bc/s1600-h/saltdean-lido.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
               <img border="0" ct="true" height="187" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_knHmw4EPfhE/S3u7QiouvQI/AAAAAAAABjo/wZBr2JsH6bc/s400/saltdean-lido.jpg" width="400"/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none; text-align: center;">
              <span style="font-size: x-small;">
               Saltdean Lido, 1937, with curvedcafe in the centre
              </span>
             </div>
             <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none;">
              Brighton's Palace Pier, when it was opened in 1899, represented the finest architectural example of late Victorian seaside architecture. The Palace Pier was and is a centre for entertainment and for strolling by holiday visitors. [This was unlike its predecessor, the Chain Pier, with its function as docking site]. The late 19th centurywas a golden age of seaside resort architecture and entertainment centres, so the Palace Pier was appropriately ornate and eccentric in its architectural style, with creative use of cast-iron snakes on lamp posts. By 1911 the pier had its own theatre, and ever since then, additions and modernisations have occurred.
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none;">
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none;">
              Something happened in the inter-war period! The popularity of water sports encouraged many seaside resort councils to invest heavily in entertainment areas, to cater for the dramatic increase in the numbers of holiday makers. But now families wanted something different. As
              <strong>
               <a href="http://www.saltdean.info/history.htm">
                Douglas d'Enno
               </a>
              </strong>
              wrote, it became as essential to have a lido as it had been to have a pier,forty years before. A lido was a public outdoor swimming pool andALL the surrounding facilities needed for swimming, sunbaking and water sports. Perhaps the timing of the lidoswas due to the introduction of mixed bathing in the more relaxed inter-war period.
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none;">
              <br/>
              By thelate 1930s, lidos had opened in Prestatyn (1922), Blackpool (1923), Plymouth (1928), Exmouth (1929), Skegness (1932), Hastings and St Leonards (1933), New Brighton and Wallasey (1934), Brighton (1935), Penzance (1935), Morecambe (1936) and Weston-super-Mare (1938). Bournemouth had indoorAND outdoor pools.
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none;">
              <br/>
              <strong>
               <a href="http://newanzac.blogspot.com/2009/03/lido-land.html">
                New Anzac musings
               </a>
              </strong>
              blog wrote that a gem of a Lido Land lieson theSouth Coast Road at Saltdean near Brighton. It was designed by RWH Jones in 1937, in the Art Deco style, and opened the following year. Time-wise, location-wise and style-wise, the Lido owed a debt to the De La Warre Pavilion at nearby Bexhill-On-Sea, which itself had only opened in Dec 1935. The clever use of curved steel and glass created a feeling of space and movement. As in most 1930s lido designs, the buildings were meant to remind the viewer of an ocean cruisers stylish lines. The Lido had a caf in the central block, changing rooms on the ground floor and sun terraces above these, which the
              <a href="http://artdecobuildings.blogspot.com/2009/11/saltdean-lido-saltdean.html">
               Art Deco Blog
              </a>
              showed beautifully.
              <br/>
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
              <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_knHmw4EPfhE/SwvGfUiXJhI/AAAAAAAABOw/8atkmMPUNoA/s1600/BrockwellLido1938.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
               <img border="0" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_knHmw4EPfhE/SwvGfUiXJhI/AAAAAAAABOw/8atkmMPUNoA/s320/BrockwellLido1938.jpg" yr="true"/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <div style="border-bottom: medium none; border-left: medium none; border-right: medium none; border-top: medium none;">
              <div style="text-align: center;">
               <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                Brockwell Lido London, 1937
               </span>
              </div>
              <br/>
              You will remember from my post on
              <a href="http://melbourneblogger.blogspot.com/2009/08/bauhaus-in-britain-chermayeff-and.html">
               The Bexhill Pavilion
              </a>
              thatBexhill provided sun baking on the deck, fabulous afternoon teas in the coffee shop and art work in the gallery. The Lido in Saltdean, renovated in 1998, provided sun baking on the deck, fabulous afternoon teas in the coffee shop and swimming in the Lido pool.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <strong>
               <a href="http://www.tiredoflondontiredoflife.com/2010/06/swim-in-parliament-hill-lido.html">
                Tired of London, Tired of Life
               </a>
              </strong>
              discussed Parliament Hill Lido, opened in 1938in Hampstead Heath. If the entrance of Parliament Hill Lido reminds you of Brockwell Lido, apparently theinitial design intentionally replicated that of Victoria Park Lido 1936(later demolished) and Brockwell Park Lido 1937 (later removed and expanded).
              <a href="http://artdecobuildings.blogspot.com/2011/03/brockwell-lido-london.html?utm_source=feedburner&amp;utm_medium=feed&amp;utm_campaign=Feed%3A+ArtDecoBuildings+%28Art+Deco+Buildings%29">
               <strong>
                Art Deco Buildings
               </strong>
              </a>
              has a tragic list of those lidos that were eventually doomed.
             </div>
