                <p>
                 You've been able to use the Internet to swap
                 <a href="http://www.paperbackswap.com/index.php" rel="nofollow">
                  books
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="http://www.swapacd.com/index.php" rel="nofollow">
                  CDs
                 </a>
                 , and
                 <a href="http://www.squidoo.com/swap-dvds-online" rel="nofollow">
                  DVDs
                 </a>
                 . Now -- kids' clothes?
                </p>
                <p>
                 <a href="http://www.thredup.com/home" rel="nofollow">
                  ThredUP
                 </a>
                 offers a standard form factor for exchanging kids' clothes -- a UPS flat-rate box, packed full of clothes for a girl or a boy, of a certain size, for a certain season, and the clothes can either be tops, bottoms, or mixed. People select a box, pay $13 to have it shipped to them, then pack one of their own and list it for selection by someone else. (The company makes $1.50 on each box.)
                </p>
                <p>
                 But this isn't just an easy way to avoid going to garage sales. ThredUP was named one of five Boston-based startups to watch by
                 <a href="http://techland.com/2010/08/02/five-boston-startups-to-watch/" rel="nofollow">
                  Techland
                 </a>
                 earlier this week, and received $1.4 million from four early-stage investors, including Founder Collective of Cambridge and New York, Trinity Ventures of Menlo Park, Calif., High Line Venture Partners and Nextview Ventures,
                 <a href="http://www.masshightech.com/stories/2010/08/02/daily45-ThredUP-heading-for-California.html" rel="nofollow">
                  according
                 </a>
                 to Mass High Tech, which also reported this week that the company was moving from Cambridge, Mass., to California.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The site launched in April and now has 15,000 users, with 1,000 new ones signing up each week,
                 <a href="http://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5jocH0tVi5WJKuETIzXwOwp3eOehwD9HE6B700" rel="nofollow">
                  reported
                 </a>
                 the Associated Press. Thus far, it has shipped 3,000 boxes of clothes and has 1,500 listed,
                 <a href="http://www.boston.com/business/technology/innoeco/2010/08/thredup_a_trading_site_for_kid.html" rel="nofollow">
                  according
                 </a>
                 to the
                 <em>
                  Boston Globe
                 </em>
                 .
                 <a href="http://www.masshightech.com/stories/2010/04/05/daily64-Two-Boston-startups-bet-on-online-swap-meets.html" rel="nofollow">
                  Initially
                 </a>
                 , it was set up for adults, but shifted its focus to kids. It was also
                 <a href="http://www.redbookmag.com/kids-family/blogs/the-mom-moment/cheap-kids-wardrobe-thredup?click=main_sr" rel="nofollow">
                  featured
                 </a>
                 this month in
                 <em>
                  Redbook
                 </em>
                 , and is also starting to make the
                 <a href="http://www.parentdish.com/2010/08/03/tighter-belts-find-looser-clothing-with-online-kids-clothing-sw/" rel="nofollow">
                  rounds
                 </a>
                 of the Mommy blogs.
                </p>
