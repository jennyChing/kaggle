       <h1>
        Australian icebreaker tender process dealt blow as P&amp;O pulls out
       </h1>

       <p>
        The Federal Government's plans to build a new Antarctic icebreaker have been dealt a blow, with one of two competing shipping companies pulling out of the long-running tender process.
       </p>
       <p>
        P&amp;O Maritime, which operates the Aurora Australia, has quit the tender process after three years of work, blaming what is says was an unusual procurement process.
       </p>
       <p>
        The move has sparked calls for a tender process better suited to Australian companies.
       </p>
       <p>
        P&amp;O Director Radostin Popov said the process put all the risk on those making bids.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The unusual procurement model leads to very large complications in risk management that ultimately means that we found ourselves incapable of delivering a value-for-money  solution," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        With the Aurora Australia approaching retirement, the company was keen to be involved its replacement and was one of two preferred bidders.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Federal Government chose a "design, build, operate and maintain" delivery model for the multi-million dollar investment, which P&amp;O said transferred all the risk to the bidder, and drove up the price of the bid.
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content quote right">
        <blockquote class="pullquote">
         <div class="quote">
          <p>
           We are disappointed to have to pull out of this and we believe we were uniquely position to offer the best solution for the government on the back of decades long experience
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="cite">
          P&amp;O Director Radostin Popov
         </div>
        </blockquote>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        "We are disappointed to have to pull out of this and we believe we were uniquely position to offer the best solution for the Government on the back of decades long experience," Mr Popov said
       </p>
       <p>
        National Secretary of the Australian Manufacturing Workers Union (AMWU) Paul Bastian said the union was not happy with the model either.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It excluded just about all Australian ship builders from the process and we have some 1,800 shipbuilders who, if they don't get work into the yard in the next six months, face job losses," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        With only one preferred bidder left in the tender process, the lucrative contract looks to set to go to DMS Maritime, owned by multi-national company Serco.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Federal Government has said it was committed to a comprehensive evaluation process to determine whether the bid represents value for money.
       </p>
       <p>
        But Mr Popov said the process would create waste.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Any bid under the current framework will not be value for money there's no doubt there will be a lot of waste," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Bastian said the union wanted the Federal Government to go back to the drawing board.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There should only be one requirement to the replace this vessel and that should be that the ship is built in Australia," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mr Bastian warned the Australian industry would collapse if it lost skills and capability.
       </p>
       <p>
        A final decision on Aurora's replacement is expected to be made late this year.
       </p>
