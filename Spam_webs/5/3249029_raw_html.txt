     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Sinterklaas
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <small>
       Dutch pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">
        [sntr'klas]
       </a>
      </span>
      ) or
      <i>
       Sint-Nicolaas
      </i>
      (
      <small>
       Dutch pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Dutch" title="Help:IPA for Dutch">
        [snt 'nikolas]
       </a>
      </span>
      <small class="nowrap metadata">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/File:Nl-Sint_Nikolaas2.ogg" title="File:Nl-Sint Nikolaas2.ogg">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/>
       </a>
       <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/64/Nl-Sint_Nikolaas2.ogg" title="Nl-Sint Nikolaas2.ogg">
        listen
       </a>
       )
      </small>
      ) is a traditional holiday figure based on
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas" title="Saint Nicholas">
       Saint Nicholas
      </a>
      . Other names for the figure include
      <i>
       De Sint
      </i>
      ("The Saint"),
      <i>
       De Goedheiligman
      </i>
      ("The Good Holy Man"), and
      <i>
       De Goede Sint
      </i>
      ("The Good Saint") in
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
       Dutch
      </a>
      ;
      <b>
       Saint-Nicolas
      </b>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      ;
      <b>
       Sinteklaas
      </b>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language" title="West Frisian language">
       Frisian
      </a>
      ; and
      <b>
       Kleeschen
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       Zinniklos
      </b>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" title="Luxembourgish language">
       Luxembourgish
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas is celebrated annually with the giving of gifts on the night before
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas_Day" title="Saint Nicholas Day">
       Saint Nicholas Day
      </a>
      (5 December) in the Netherlands and on the morning of 6 December, Saint Nicholas Day itself, in Belgium, Luxembourg, and Northern France (French Flanders, Lorraine and Artois). He is also well known in territories of the former
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Empire" title="Dutch Empire">
       Dutch Empire
      </a>
      , including Aruba, Bonaire and Curaao.
     </p>
     <p>
      He is one of the sources of the popular Christmas icon of
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">
       Santa Claus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark1998_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Clark1998-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other holiday figures based on Saint Nicholas are celebrated in some parts of Germany and Austria (
      <i>
       Sankt Nikolaus
      </i>
      ); Hungary (
      <i>
       Mikuls
      </i>
      ); Switzerland (
      <i>
       Samichlaus
      </i>
      ); Italy (
      <i>
       San Nicola
      </i>
      in Bari, South Tyrol, Alpine municipalities, and many others); parts of Bosnia and Herzegovina, Croatia and Serbia (
      <i>
       Sveti Nikola
      </i>
      ); Slovenia (
      <i>
       Sveti Nikolaj
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Sveti Miklav
      </i>
      ); and Greece (
      <i>
       Agios Nikolaos
      </i>
      ), among others. See further:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas_Day" title="Saint Nicholas Day">
       Saint Nicholas Day
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sinterklaas">
       Sinterklaas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sinterklaas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinterklaas_2007.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="735" data-file-width="500" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Sinterklaas_2007.jpg/170px-Sinterklaas_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Sinterklaas_2007.jpg/255px-Sinterklaas_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Sinterklaas_2007.jpg/340px-Sinterklaas_2007.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinterklaas_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sinterklaas by Bram van der Vlugt
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      is an elderly, stately and serious man with white hair and a long, full beard. He wears a long red cape or
      <a href="/wiki/Chasuble" title="Chasuble">
       chasuble
      </a>
      over a traditional white bishop's
      <a href="/wiki/Alb" title="Alb">
       alb
      </a>
      and sometimes red
      <a href="/wiki/Stola" title="Stola">
       stola
      </a>
      , dons a red
      <a href="/wiki/Mitre" title="Mitre">
       mitre
      </a>
      and ruby ring, and holds a gold-coloured
      <a href="/wiki/Crosier" title="Crosier">
       crosier
      </a>
      , a long ceremonial shepherd's staff with a fancy curled top.
     </p>
     <p>
      He traditionally rides a white
      <a href="/wiki/Gray_(horse)" title="Gray (horse)">
       horse
      </a>
      . In the Netherlands, the horse is called
      <i>
       Amerigo
      </i>
      , and in Belgium, it is named
      <i>
       Slecht Weer Vandaag
      </i>
      , meaning "Bad Weather Today".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas carries a big book that tells whether each child has been good or naughty in the past year.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Zwarte_Piet">

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zwartepiet.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="532" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Zwartepiet.png/170px-Zwartepiet.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Zwartepiet.png/255px-Zwartepiet.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Zwartepiet.png/340px-Zwartepiet.png 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zwartepiet.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Zwarte_Piet" title="Zwarte Piet">
         Zwarte Piet
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Zwarte_Piet" title="Zwarte Piet">
       Zwarte Piet
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Zwarte Piet (plural Zwarte Pieten) is a companion of Sinterklaas, usually portrayed by a man in
      <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">
       blackface
      </a>
      with black curly hair, dressed up like a 17th-century page in colourful attire, often sporting a
      <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">
       lace
      </a>
      collar and a feathered cap. He first appears in print as the nameless servant of Saint Nicholas in
      <i>
       Sint-Nikolaas en zijn knecht
      </i>
      ("St. Nicholas and His Servant"), published in 1850 by Amsterdam schoolteacher Jan Schenkman; however, the tradition appears to date back at least as far as the early 19th Century.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas and Zwarte Piet are typically depicted carrying a bag which contains candy for nice children and a birch rod, a
      <a href="/wiki/Chimney_sweep" title="Chimney sweep">
       chimney sweep
      </a>
      's broom made of willow branches, used to spank naughty children. Some of the older Sinterklaas songs make mention of naughty children being put in the bag and being taken back to Spain. This part of the legend refers to the times that the
      <a href="/wiki/Barbary_slave_trade" title="Barbary slave trade">
       Moors raided the European coasts
      </a>
      , and as far as Iceland, to abduct the local people into slavery. This quality can be found in other
      <a href="/wiki/Companions_of_Saint_Nicholas" title="Companions of Saint Nicholas">
       companions of Saint Nicholas
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Krampus" title="Krampus">
       Krampus
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_Fouettard" title="Pre Fouettard">
       Pre Fouettard
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Zwarte Pieten toss candy around, a tradition supposedly originating in the story of Saint Nicholas saving three young girls from prostitution by tossing golden coins through their window at night to pay their
      <a href="/wiki/Dowry" title="Dowry">
       dowries
      </a>
      . In recent years the
      <i>
       roe
      </i>
      has been absent, to adapt to modern times.
     </p>
     <p>
      In modern adaptations for television, Sinterklaas has developed a Zwarte Piet for every function: there is a head Piet (
      <i>
       Hoofdpiet
      </i>
      ), a navigation Piet (
      <i>
       Wegwijspiet
      </i>
      ) to navigate the steamboat from Spain to the Netherlands, a packing Piet (
      <i>
       Pakjespiet
      </i>
      ) to pack all the gifts, an acrobatic Piet to climb roofs and stuff presents down the chimney, or to climb down the chimneys themselves. Over the years many stories have been added, and Zwarte Piet has developed from a rather unintelligent helper into a valuable assistant to the absent-minded saint.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Traditionally Zwarte Piet's face is said to be black because he is a
      <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
       Moor
      </a>
      from Spain. Today, some prefer to say that his face is blackened with
      <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">
       soot
      </a>
      because he has to climb through chimneys to deliver gifts for Sinterklaas. The figure of Zwarte Piet is considered by some to be racist. As such, the traditions surrounding the holiday of Sinterklaas have been the subject of numerous editorials, debates, documentaries, protests and even violent clashes at festivals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emma_Thomas_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Emma_Thomas-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Felicity_Morse_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Felicity_Morse-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nevertheless, both Zwarte Piet and the holiday remain popular in the Netherlands. In a 2013 survey, 92% of the Dutch public did not perceive Zwarte Piet as racist or associate him with slavery, and 91% were opposed to altering the character's appearance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, beginning in 2013, several Dutch retail chains including
      <a href="/wiki/HEMA_(store)" title="HEMA (store)">
       Hema
      </a>
      and V&amp;D, in addition to several events around the country, began changing their seasonal Sinterklaas celebrations and displays in order to include a revised version of Zwarte Piet without the makeup or other racially sensitive characteristics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Feast">
       Feast
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feast">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_from_Spain">
       Arrival from Spain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arrival from Spain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sint-intocht-boot.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Sint-intocht-boot.jpg/170px-Sint-intocht-boot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Sint-intocht-boot.jpg/255px-Sint-intocht-boot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Sint-intocht-boot.jpg/340px-Sint-intocht-boot.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sint-intocht-boot.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sinterklaas and his Zwarte Piet helpers arriving by
        <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">
         steamboat
        </a>
        from Spain
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas traditionally arrives in the Netherlands each year in mid-November (the first Saturday after 11 November) by steamboat from Spain. He then parades through the streets on his horse, welcomed by children cheering and singing traditional Sinterklaas songs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His
      <i>
       Zwarte Piet
      </i>
      assistants throw candy and small, round, gingerbread-like cookies, either
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kruidnoten" title="Kruidnoten">
        kruidnoten
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pepernoot" title="Pepernoot">
        pepernoten
       </a>
      </i>
      , into the crowd. The event is broadcast live on national television in the Netherlands and Belgium.
     </p>
     <p>
      Following this national arrival, all towns with a dock usually celebrate their own
      <i>
       intocht van Sinterklaas
      </i>
      (arrival of Sinterklaas). Local arrivals usually take place later on the same Saturday of the national arrival, the next Sunday (the day after he arrives in the Netherlands or Belgium), or one weekend after the national arrival. In places a boat cannot reach, Sinterklaas arrives by train, horse, horse-drawn carriage or even a fire truck.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some suggest that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandarin_oranges" title="Mandarin oranges">
       mandarin oranges
      </a>
      , traditionally gifts associated with the holy man, led to the misconception that he must have been from Spain. This theory is backed by a Dutch poem documented in 1810 in New York and provided with an English translation:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stnicholascenter.org_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-stnicholascenter.org-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div>
      <table role="presentation" style="width: 100%; background-color:transparent;table-layout:fixed; border:none; border-spacing:0;">
       <tr style="vertical-align: top;">
        <td>
         <div style="margin-right:20px;">
          <dl>
           <dt>
            Dutch
           </dt>
          </dl>
          <div class="poem">
           <p>
            Sinterklaas, goedheiligman!
            <br/>
            Trek uwe beste tabberd an,
            <br/>
            Reis daar mee naar Amsterdam,
            <br/>
            Van Amsterdam naar Spanje,
            <br/>
            Daar Appelen van Oranje,
            <br/>
            Daar Appelen van granaten,
            <br/>
            Die rollen door de straten.
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
        </td>
        <td>
         <div style="margin-right: 20px;">
          <dl>
           <dt>
            English
           </dt>
          </dl>
          <div class="poem">
           <p>
            Saint Nicholas, good holy man!
            <br/>
            Put on the Tabard, best you can,
            <br/>
            Go, therewith, to Amsterdam,
            <br/>
            From Amsterdam to Spain,
            <br/>
            Where apples bright of Orange,
            <br/>
            And likewise those granate surnam'd,
            <br/>
            Roll through the streets, all free unclaim'd [...]
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p>
      The text presented here comes from a pamphlet that
      <a href="/wiki/John_Pintard" title="John Pintard">
       John Pintard
      </a>
      released in New York in 1810. It is the earliest source mentioning Spain in connection to
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      . Pintard wanted St. Nicholas to become patron saint of New York and hoped to establish a
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      tradition. Apparently he got help from the Dutch community in New York, that provided him with the original Dutch
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      poem. Strictly speaking, the poem does not state that Sinterklaas
      <i>
       comes
      </i>
      from Spain, but that he needs to
      <i>
       go
      </i>
      to Spain to pick up the oranges and pomegranates. So the link between Sinterklaas and Spain goes through the oranges, a much appreciated treat in the 19th century. Later the connection with the oranges got lost, and Spain became his home.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Period_leading_up_to_Saint_Nicholas.27_Eve">
       Period leading up to Saint Nicholas' Eve
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Period leading up to Saint Nicholas' Eve">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG/170px-Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG/255px-Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG/340px-Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bag_of_pepernoten.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Kruidnoten" title="Kruidnoten">
         Kruidnoten
        </a>
        , small, round
        <a href="/wiki/Gingerbread" title="Gingerbread">
         gingerbread
        </a>
        -like
        <a href="/wiki/Cookie" title="Cookie">
         cookies
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chocoladeletter_A.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Chocoladeletter_A.jpg/170px-Chocoladeletter_A.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Chocoladeletter_A.jpg/255px-Chocoladeletter_A.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Chocoladeletter_A.jpg/340px-Chocoladeletter_A.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chocoladeletter_A.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Chocolate_letter" title="Chocolate letter">
         chocolate letter
        </a>
        , typical Sinterklaas candy in the Netherlands
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the weeks between his arrival and 5 December, Sinterklaas also visits schools, hospitals and shopping centers. He is said to ride his white-grey horse over the rooftops at night, delivering gifts through the chimney to the well-behaved children. Traditionally, naughty children risked being caught by Black Pete, who carried a jute bag and willow cane for that purpose.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Before going to bed, children put their shoes next to the fireplace chimney of the coal-fired stove or fireplace (or in modern times close to the
      <a href="/wiki/Radiator_(heating)" title="Radiator (heating)">
       central heating radiator
      </a>
      ). They leave the shoe with a carrot or some hay in it and a bowl of water nearby "for Sinterklaas' horse", and the children sing a Sinterklaas song. The next day they find some candy or a small present in their shoes. Typical Sinterklaas treats traditionally include
      <a href="/wiki/Hot_chocolate" title="Hot chocolate">
       hot chocolate
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">
       mandarin oranges
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pepernoot" title="Pepernoot">
       <i>
        pepernoten
       </i>
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speculaas" title="Speculaas">
        speculaas
       </a>
      </i>
      (sometimes filled with
      <a href="/wiki/Almond_paste" title="Almond paste">
       almond paste
      </a>
      ), letter-shaped
      <a href="/wiki/Banket_(food)" title="Banket (food)">
       pastry
      </a>
      filled with almond paste or a
      <a href="/wiki/Chocolate_letter" title="Chocolate letter">
       chocolate letter
      </a>
      (the first letter of the child's name made out of chocolate),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chocolate_money" title="Chocolate money">
       chocolate coins
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Marzipan" title="Marzipan">
        marzipan
       </a>
      </i>
      figures. Newer treats include gingerbread biscuits or and a figurine of Sinterklaas made of chocolate and wrapped in colored aluminum foil.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Nicholas.27_Eve_and_Saint_Nicholas.27_Day">
       Saint Nicholas' Eve and Saint Nicholas' Day
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saint Nicholas' Eve and Saint Nicholas' Day">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the Netherlands, Saint Nicholas' Eve, 5 December, became the chief occasion for gift-giving during the winter holiday season. The evening is called
      <i>
       Sinterklaasavond
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Pakjesavond
      </i>
      ("gifts evening", or literally "packages evening").
     </p>
     <p>
      On the evening of 5 December, the main presents will somehow arrive, or a note will be "found" that explains where in house the presents were hidden by Zwarte Piet who left a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burlap" title="Burlap">
       burlap
      </a>
      sack with them. Sometimes a neighbor will knock on the door (pretending to be a Zwarte Piet) and leave the sack outside for the children to retrieve; this varies per family. When the presents arrive, the living room is decked out with them, much as on Christmas Day in English-speaking countries. On 6 December, Sinterklaas departs without any ado, and all festivities are over.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Belgium, most children have to wait until the morning of 6 December to receive their gifts, and Sinterklaas is seen as a festivity almost exclusively for children. The shoes are filled with carrots and sugar cubes on the evening of the fifth and often, a bottle of beer for Zwarte Piet and a cup of coffee for Sinterklaas are placed next to them.
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      largely replaces Christmas as a gift-giving occasion, usually for as long as the children are living at home. Also, when it is time for children to give up their
      <a href="/wiki/Pacifier" title="Pacifier">
       pacifier
      </a>
      , they place it into his or her shoe ("safekeeping by Sinterklaas") and it is replaced with chocolate the next morning.
     </p>
     <p>
      The present is often creatively disguised by being packaged in a humorous, unusual or personalised way. This is called a
      <i>
       surprise
      </i>
      (from the French ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Poems from Sinterklaas usually accompany gifts, bearing a personal message for the receiver. It is usually a humorous poem which often teases the recipient for well-known bad habits or other character deficiencies.
     </p>
     <p>
      When the children reach the age when they know "the big secret of Sinterklaas", some people will shift to Christmas Eve or Christmas Day for the present giving. Older children in Dutch families where the children are too old to believe in Sinterklaas anymore, also often celebrate Christmas with presents instead of
      <i>
       pakjesavond
      </i>
      . Instead of such gifts being brought by Sinterklaas, family members may draw names for an event comparable to
      <a href="/wiki/Secret_Santa" title="Secret Santa">
       Secret Santa
      </a>
      .
     </p>

        Illustration from the 1850 book
        <i>
         St. Nikolaas en zijn knecht
        </i>
        ("Saint Nicholas and his servant"), by Jan Schenkman, 1850
       </div>
      </div>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Christian_Europe">
       Pre-Christian Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pre-Christian Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Parallels have been drawn between the legend of
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      and the figure of
      <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">
       Odin
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a major god among the Germanic peoples, who was worshipped in Northern and Western Europe prior to Christianization. Since some elements of the
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      celebration are unrelated to Christianity, there are theories regarding the
      <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">
       pagan
      </a>
      origins of various customs of the holiday stemming from areas where the Germanic peoples were Christianized and retained elements of their indigenous traditions, surviving in various forms into modern depictions of
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      . Non-Christian elements in
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      that arguably could have been of pagan origin:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Sinterklaas rides the rooftops on his white horse which has various names; Odin rides the sky with his grey horse
       <a href="/wiki/Sleipnir" title="Sleipnir">
        Sleipnir
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Sinterklaas gives chocolate letters to children, like Odin gave the rune letters to man.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sinterklaas carries a staff and has mischievous helpers with black faces, who listen at chimneys to find out whether children are bad or good and report to Sinterklaas; Odin has a spear and his black ravens,
       <a href="/wiki/Huginn_and_Muninn" title="Huginn and Muninn">
        Huginn and Muninn
       </a>
       , who report what happens in the world to Odin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         17
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Sinterklaasfeest
      </i>
      arose during the Middle Ages. The folk feast celebrates the name day, 6 December, of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas" title="Saint Nicholas">
       Saint Nicholas
      </a>
      (270343), patron saint of children. Saint Nicholas was a Greek
      <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">
       bishop
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Myra" title="Myra">
       Myra
      </a>
      in present-day Turkey. In 1087, half of his relics were furtively transported to
      <a href="/wiki/Bari" title="Bari">
       Bari
      </a>
      , in southeastern Italy; for this reason, he is also known as Nikolaos of Bari. Bari later formed part of the Spanish
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">
       Kingdom of Naples
      </a>
      , because it was previously conquered in 1442 by AlfonsoV of Aragon. The city thus became part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">
       Kingdom of Aragon
      </a>
      and later of
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
       Spain
      </a>
      , until the 18th century.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because half the remains of St. Nicholas were in Bari (then a Spanish city), in this tradition St. Nicholas comes from Spain and has a black helper depicted as a
      <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">
       Morisco
      </a>
      page boy known as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zwarte_Piet" title="Zwarte Piet">
        Zwarte Piet
       </a>
      </i>
      . Because of the help he gave to sailors in his home town,
      <i>
       Saint Nicolas
      </i>
      was venerated as the patron saint of sailors, which is why he comes to the Netherlands on a
      <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">
       steamboat
      </a>
      . His anonymous gifts and kindness towards children had already secured him the patronage of children, by the elders of
      <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">
       Lycia
      </a>
      , his home province. Sinterklaas' fame spread throughout Europe. The Roman Catholic Church made his name day a Church holiday. In the north of France, he became the patron saint of school children, then mostly in church schools.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">
       Reformation
      </a>
      in 16th-17th-century Europe, many Protestants and others changed the gift bringer to the Christ Child or
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christkindl" title="Christkindl">
        Christkindl
       </a>
      </i>
      , and the date for giving gifts changed from 6 December to Christmas Eve.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas is assisted by many mischievous helpers with black faces and colourful Moorish dresses. These helpers are called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zwarte_Piet" title="Zwarte Piet">
        Zwarte Pieten
       </a>
      </i>
      (Black Pete(r)s). Although the character of Black Pete later came to acquire racial connotations, his origins were in the evil figure. Good and bad play an important role in the feast: good is rewarded, bad and evil are punished. Hence the duplication of the one Saint in a saint and a (frolicking) devil.
     </p>
     <p>
      The feast was both an occasion to help the poor, by putting money in their shoes (which evolved into putting presents in children's shoes) and a wild feast, similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">
       Carnival
      </a>
      , that often led to costumes, a "topsy-turvy" overturning of daily roles, and mass public drunkenness.
     </p>
     <p>
      In early traditions, students elected one of their classmates as "bishop" on St. Nicholas Day, who would rule until 28 December (Innocents Day), and they sometimes acted out events from the bishop's life. As the festival moved to city streets, it became more lively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Temple_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Temple-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="16th_and_17th_centuries">
       16th and 17th centuries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 16th and 17th centuries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After the rebellion of the Dutch provinces against the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
       Spanish Empire
      </a>
      , Calvinist regents and ministers prohibited celebration of the Saint. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_United_Provinces" title="Republic of the United Provinces">
       Republic of the United Provinces
      </a>
      became an officially
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">
       Protestant
      </a>
      country following the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">
       Reformation
      </a>
      , and its governments abolished public celebrations. The South, however, remained Catholic. People there and students in Amsterdam, which also contained a significant Catholic population, protested. The government eventually allowed private family celebration of Saint Nicholas' Day.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">
       19th century
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 19th century">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the 19th century, the saint emerged from hiding and became more secularized at the same time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Temple_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Temple-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The modern tradition of
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      as a children's feast was likely confirmed with the illustrated children's book
      <i>
       Sint-Nicolaas en zijn knecht
      </i>
      ('Saint Nicholas and his servant'), written in 1850 by the teacher Jan Schenkman (18061863). Some say he introduced the images of Sinterklaas' delivering presents by the chimney, riding over the roofs of houses on a
      <a href="/wiki/Gray_(horse)" title="Gray (horse)">
       grey
      </a>
      horse, and arriving from Spain by
      <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">
       steamboat
      </a>
      , which at that time was an exciting modern invention. Perhaps building on the fact that Sint Nicholas historically is the patron saint of the sailors (many churches dedicated to him have been built near harbors), Schenkman could have been inspired by the Spanish customs and ideas about the saint when he portrayed him arriving via the water in his book. Schenkman introduced the song
      <i>
       Zie ginds komt de stoomboot
      </i>
      ("Look over yonder, the steamboat is arriving"), which is still popular in the Netherlands.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Schenkman's version, the medieval figures of the mock devil, which later changed to Oriental or Moorish helpers, was portrayed for the first time as black African and called
      <i>
       Zwarte Piet
      </i>
      (Black Peter).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Temple_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Temple-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">
       World War II
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: World War II">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_the_Netherlands" title="German occupation of the Netherlands">
       German occupation of the Netherlands
      </a>
      (19401945) many of the traditional
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      rhymes were rewritten to reflect current events.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">
       Royal Air Force
      </a>
      (RAF) was often celebrated. In 1941, for instance, the RAF dropped boxes of candy over the occupied Netherlands. One classical poem turned contemporary was the following:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       original
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Sinterklaas, kapoentje,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Gooi wat in mijn schoentje,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Gooi wat in mijn laarsje,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Dank U Sinterklaasje
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       which became the contemporary version
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        R.A.F. Kapoentje,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Gooi wat in mijn schoentje,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Bij de Moffen gooien,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Maar in Holland strooien!
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       translation
      </dd>
      <dd>
       R.A.F. Capon,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       throw something in my shoe
      </dd>
      <dd>
       throw [bombs] at the Krauts
      </dd>
      <dd>
       but scatter [candy] in Holland!
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This is a variation of one of the best-known traditional Sinterklaas rhymes, with "R.A.F." replacing "Sinterklaas" in the first line (the two expressions have the same metrical characteristics in the first and second, and in the third and fourth lines). The Dutch word
      <i>
       kapoentje
      </i>
      (little rascal) is traditional to the rhyme, but in this case it also alludes to a
      <a href="/wiki/Capon" title="Capon">
       capon
      </a>
      . The second line is straight from the original rhyme, but in the third and fourth line the RAF is encouraged to drop bombs on the
      <i>
       Moffen
      </i>
      (slur for Germans, like "krauts" in English) and candy over the Netherlands. Many of the
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      poems of this time noted the lack of food and basic necessities, and the German occupiers having taken everything of value; others expressed admiration for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Resistance" title="Dutch Resistance">
       Dutch Resistance
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Originally
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      was only accompanied with one (or sometimes two)
      <i>
       Zwarte Pieten
      </i>
      , but just after the liberation of the Netherlands, Canadian soldiers organized a Sinterklaas party with many Zwarte Pieten, and ever since this has been the custom, each Piet normally having his own dedicated task.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sinterklaas_as_a_source_for_Santa_Claus">
       Sinterklaas as a source for Santa Claus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sinterklaas as a source for Santa Claus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      is the basis for the North American figure of
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">
       Santa Claus
      </a>
      . It is often claimed that during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" title="American War of Independence">
       American War of Independence
      </a>
      , the inhabitants of New York City, a former Dutch colonial town (
      <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">
       New Amsterdam
      </a>
      ), reinvented their
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      tradition, as Saint Nicholas was a symbol of the city's non-English past.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 1770s the
      <i>
       New York Gazetteer
      </i>
      noted that the feast day of "St. a Claus" was celebrated "by the descendants of the ancient Dutch families, with their usual festivities."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The name Santa Claus supposedly derived from older Dutch
      <i>
       Sinter Klaas
      </i>
      . However, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas_Society" title="Saint Nicholas Society">
       Saint Nicholas Society
      </a>
      was not founded until 1835, almost half a century after the end of the war.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a study of the "children's books, periodicals and journals" of New Amsterdam, the scholar Charles Jones did not find references to Saint Nicholas or Sinterklaas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Not all scholars agree with Jones' findings, which he reiterated in a book in 1978.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Howard G. Hageman, of New Brunswick Theological Seminary, maintains that the tradition of celebrating Sinterklaas in New York existed in the early settlement of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Valley" title="Hudson Valley">
       Hudson Valley
      </a>
      . He agrees that "there can be no question that by the time the revival of St. Nicholas came with
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">
       Washington Irving
      </a>
      , the traditional New Netherlands observance had completely disappeared."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Irving's stories prominently featured legends of the early Dutch settlers, so while the traditional practice may have died out, Irving's St. Nicholas may have been a revival of that dormant Dutch strand of folklore. In his 1812 revisions to
      <i>
       A History of New York
      </i>
      , Irving inserted a dream sequence featuring St. Nicholas soaring over treetops in a flying wagon  a creation others would later dress up as Santa Claus.
     </p>
     <p>
      But was Irving the first to revive the Dutch folklore of Sinterklaas? In New York, two years earlier
      <a href="/wiki/John_Pintard" title="John Pintard">
       John Pintard
      </a>
      published a pamphlet with illustrations of
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Anderson_(illustrator)" title="Alexander Anderson (illustrator)">
       Alexander Anderson
      </a>
      in which he calls for making Saint Nicholas the patron Saint of New York and starting a Sinterklaas tradition. He was apparently assisted by the Dutch because in his pamphlet he included an old Dutch Sinterklaas poem with an English translation. In the Dutch poem, Saint Nicholas is referred to as 'Sancta Claus'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stnicholascenter.org_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-stnicholascenter.org-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ultimately, his initiative helped
      <i>
       Sinterklaas
      </i>
      to pop up as Santa Claus in the Christmas celebration, which returned  freed of episcopal dignity and ties  via England and later Germany to Europe again.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Saint Nicholas Society of New York celebrates a feast on 6 December to this day. The town of Rhinebeck in
      <a href="/wiki/Dutchess_County,_New_York" title="Dutchess County, New York">
       Dutchess County, New York
      </a>
      , which was founded by Dutch and German immigrants, has an annual Sinterklaas celebration. It includes Sinterklaas' crossing the Hudson River and then a parade to the center of town.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">
       Reformation
      </a>
      in 16th-17th-century Europe, many Protestants changed the gift bringer from Sinterklaas to the Christ Child or
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christkindl" title="Christkindl">
        Christkindl
       </a>
      </i>
      (corrupted in English to Kris Kringle). Similarly, the date of giving gifts changed from 5 or 6 December to Christmas Eve.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sinterklaas_in_fiction">
       Sinterklaas in fiction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinterklaas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sinterklaas in fiction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In a scene in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Miracle_on_34th_Street" title="Miracle on 34th Street">
        Miracle on 34th Street
       </a>
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">
       Dutch
      </a>
      girl recognises
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">
       Kris Kringle
      </a>
      as Sinterklaas. She sits on his lap while they sing a Sinterklaas song, to the amazement of
      <a href="/wiki/Miracle_on_34th_Street#Character_names_in_different_versions" title="Miracle on 34th Street">
       Susan Walker
      </a>
      , who is then convinced that he's the real
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">
       Santa Claus
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas has been the subject of a number of Dutch novels, films and television series, primarily aimed at children. Sinterklaas-themed children's films include
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Winky%27s_Horse" title="Winky's Horse">
        Winky's Horse
       </a>
      </i>
      (2005) and the sequel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Where_Is_Winky%27s_Horse%3F" title="Where Is Winky's Horse?">
        Where Is Winky's Horse?
       </a>
      </i>
      (2007).
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinterklaas-themed films aimed at adults include the
      <a href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">
       drama
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Makkers_Staakt_uw_Wild_Geraas" title="Makkers Staakt uw Wild Geraas">
        Makkers Staakt uw Wild Geraas
       </a>
      </i>
      (1960), which won a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_Bear" title="Silver Bear">
       Silver Bear
