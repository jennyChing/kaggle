         <h2 class="date-header">
          December 04, 2012
         </h2>
         <div class="post hentry uncustomized-post-template">
          <a name="596756298744265022">
          </a>
          <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
           <a href="http://www.thisdayinquotes.com/2009/12/facts-are-stubborn-things-and-different.html">
            Facts are stubborn things  and sometimes stupid
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="post-header-line-1">
          </div>
          <div class="post-body entry-content">
           <div class="wlWriterSmartContent" id="scid:8747F07C-CDE8-481f-B0DF-C6CFD074BF67:6e82c69f-d8db-4c17-af8e-9a8f35c1853b" style="padding-bottom: 0px; margin: 0px; padding-left: 0px; padding-right: 0px; display: inline; float: left; padding-top: 0px">
            <a boston="boston" href="http://lh6.ggpht.com/_5XvBYfxU_dM/SxiiZuLri4I/AAAAAAAAFvw/Kfctuu48vd4/Paul%20Revere%27s%20engraving%20of%20the%20Boston%20Massacre.-8x6.jpg?imgmax=800" massacre.="" rel="thumbnail" title="Paul Revere's famous engraving of the ">
             <font size="2">
              <img border="0" height="379" src="http://lh4.ggpht.com/_5XvBYfxU_dM/SxkV0Q4xXQI/AAAAAAAAFwg/U9E8rNkcquc/Paul%20Revere%27s%20engraving%20of%20the%20Boston%20Massacre.%5B9%5D.png?imgmax=800" width="300"/>
             </font>
            </a>
           </div>
           <br/>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             In the years leading up to the start of the Revolutionary War in 1775, the rebel-rousing
            </font>
            <a href="http://www.ushistory.org/declaration/related/sons.htm" target="_blank">
             <font size="2">
              Sons of Liberty
             </font>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             used an engraving of what they called
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.law.umkc.edu/faculty/projects/ftrials/bostonmassacre/keyfigures.html" target="_blank">
               The Boston Massacre
              </a>
             </strong>
             to arouse anti-British sentiments.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             The engraving, done by
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.earlyamerica.com/review/winter96/massacre.html" target="_blank">
               Paul Revere
              </a>
             </strong>
             , shows a line of British soldiers coldly firing their bayoneted muskets into a crowd of Americans, several of which lay bleeding on the ground.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             A poem underneath that scene describes how the Kings men
             <em>
              With murderous Rancour stretch their bloody hands, Like fierce Barbarians grinning o'er their Prey.
             </em>
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             It was good propaganda. But it did distort what happened at the Boston Massacre on the night of March 5, 1770.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             That night, a local resident got into an argument over a debt with a British soldier. Eight other British soldiers came out on the street to help their comrade. A group of Americans surrounded the soldiers. The Brits were soon being yelled at and pelted with snowballs, ice chunks and debris by the much larger, hostile crowd.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             The bloodletting appears to have started when a mulatto seaman named
            </font>
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crispus_Attucks" target="_blank">
             <strong>
              <font size="2">
               Crispus Attucks
              </font>
             </strong>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             hit one of the soldiers with a piece of wood. The soldiers panicked. Somebody yelled Fire! and they shot into the crowd, killing Attucks and four other Americans.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             When the British soldiers were arrested and put on trial for murder, a Boston merchant asked local lawyer (and future president)
            </font>
            <a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/about/presidents/johnadams" target="_blank">
             <strong>
              <font size="2">
               John Adams
              </font>
             </strong>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             to defend them. He agreed, knowing it would make him unpopular and could ruin his career.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
            </font>
           </p>
           <div class="wlWriterSmartContent" id="scid:8747F07C-CDE8-481f-B0DF-C6CFD074BF67:5404a45f-54e3-4034-9c7c-dadb08c0e927" style="padding-bottom: 0px; margin: 0px; padding-left: 0px; padding-right: 0px; display: inline; float: right; padding-top: 0px">
            <a href="http://lh4.ggpht.com/_5XvBYfxU_dM/Sxiibz_0WOI/AAAAAAAAFwA/0cIQjHvQmKQ/John%20Adams-8x6.jpg?imgmax=800" rel="thumbnail" title="John Adams">
             <font size="2">
              <img border="0" height="277" src="http://lh5.ggpht.com/_5XvBYfxU_dM/SxiicVSbUvI/AAAAAAAAFwI/wje16YutG1k/John%20Adams%5B4%5D.png?imgmax=800" width="221"/>
             </font>
            </a>
           </div>
           <font size="2">
            Adams believed the soldiers deserved legal representation as a matter of principle. After looking into the incident, he also believed they were provoked and should not be executed for murder, as many Bostonians wanted.
           </font>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             On
             <strong>
              <font color="#800000">
               December 4, 1770
              </font>
             </strong>
             , the second day of the brief trial, Adams gave his summation to the jury.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             He argued that anyone might have reacted the same way the soldiers did in such a confusing and potentially life-threatening situation. He suggested Crispus Attucks was more to blame for
             <em>
              the dreadful carnage of that night
             </em>
             than the soldiers, because of his
             <em>
              mad behavior.
             </em>
            </font>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             <font size="2">
              <em>
               
               <strong>
                Facts are stubborn things
               </strong>
               ,
              </em>
              Adams said, coining a famous quotation.
              <em>
               And whatever may be our wishes, our inclinations, or the dictates of our passions, they cannot alter the state of facts and evidence: nor is the law less stable than the fact; if an assault was made to endanger their lives, the law is clear, they had a right to kill in their own defence; if it was not so severe as to endanger their lives, yet if they were assaulted at all, struck and abused by blows of any sort, by snow-balls, oyster-shells, cinders, clubs, or sticks of any kind; this was a provocation, for which the law reduces the offence of killing, down to manslaughter.
              </em>
             </font>
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             The jury was persuaded. Six of the soldiers were acquitted. Two were found guilty of manslaughter and punished by having their thumbs branded.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Several years later, John Adams
            </font>
            <a href="http://www.law.umkc.edu/faculty/projects/ftrials/bostonmassacre/diaryentries.html" target="_blank">
             <font size="2">
              wrote in his diary
             </font>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             that his defense of those British soldiers was
             <em>
              one of the best Pieces of Service I ever rendered my Country.
             </em>
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             It was also the origin of the quote
             <em>
              Facts are stubborn things.
             </em>
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
            </font>
           </p>
           <div class="wlWriterEditableSmartContent" id="scid:8747F07C-CDE8-481f-B0DF-C6CFD074BF67:1cbe82f6-cabc-4597-b333-113b05a72841" style="padding-bottom: 0px; margin: 0px; padding-left: 0px; padding-right: 0px; display: inline; float: left; padding-top: 0px">
            <a are="" at="" august="" convention="" facts="" href="http://lh6.ggpht.com/-RmMKhNeJzHg/UL5-H-u8q_I/AAAAAAAAZPI/9XhTuAp2wgg/Ronald%252520Reagan%25252C%252520August%25252015%25252C%2525201988%252520speech%252520at%252520Republican%252520Convention-8x6.jpg?imgmax=800" national="" on="" rel="thumbnail" republican="" speech="" stubborn="" stupid="" the="" title="Ronald Reagan giving his ">
             <img border="0" height="321" src="http://lh3.ggpht.com/-8MPBVfVLtHI/UL5-ItqidmI/AAAAAAAAZPQ/leG9e5vv0fw/Ronald%252520Reagan%25252C%252520August%25252015%25252C%2525201988%252520speech%252520at%252520Republican%252520Convention%25255B11%25255D.png?imgmax=800" width="310"/>
            </a>
           </div>
           <font size="2">
            Two centuries later, President
           </font>
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             <font size="2">
              Ronald Reagan
             </font>
            </strong>
           </a>
           <font size="2">
            uttered the most famous modern use and perceived misuse of that quote.
           </font>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             It came in
            </font>
            <a href="http://millercenter.org/president/speeches/detail/5469" target="_blank">
             <font size="2">
              his speech at the Republican National Convention
             </font>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             in New Orleans, Louisiana on
             <strong>
              <font color="#800000">
               August 15, 1988
              </font>
             </strong>
             .
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Reagan was there to speak in support of the current Republican presidential candidate, his Vice President
             <strong>
              George H.W. Bush
             </strong>
             , who was running against Democrat
             <strong>
              Michael Dukakis
             </strong>
             .
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             In the speech, Reagan recounted what he viewed as the successes of his administration and the reasons why he felt voters should elect another Republican as president.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Reagan repeated John Adams facts quote several times in the address. It was a rhetorical device he used in the part that focused on the economic problems he blamed on his Democratic predecessor, President
             <strong>
              Jimmy Carter
             </strong>
             .
            </font>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             <font size="2">
              <em>
               Before we came to Washington,
              </em>
              Reagan said,
              <em>
               Americans had just suffered the two worst back-to-back years of inflation in 60 years. Those are the facts, and as John Adams said,
               <strong>
                Facts are stubborn things.
               </strong>
               Interest rates had jumped to over 21 percent
               <strong>
                Facts are stubborn things
               </strong>
               The median family income fell 51/2 percent.
               <strong>
                Facts are stubborn things.
               </strong>
               
              </em>
             </font>
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Then he made what became one of his most-cited gaffes, saying:
            </font>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             <em>
              <font size="2">
               Fuel costs jumped through the atmosphere, more than doubling. Then people waited in gas lines as well as unemployment lines.
               <strong>
                Facts are stupid things.
               </strong>
              </font>
             </em>
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Reagan immediately corrected himself, adding:
             <em>
              Stubborn things, I should say.
             </em>
             But once the word
             <em>
              stupid
             </em>
             came out of his mouth, thats the version that was picked up and cited by his critics.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Today,
            </font>
            <a href="https://www.google.com/search?num=30&amp;hl=en&amp;safe=off&amp;tbo=d&amp;rlz=1G1GGLQ_ENUS242&amp;q=%22Facts+are+stupid+things%22+Reagan+-%22Facts+are+stubborn+things%22&amp;oq=%22Facts+are+stupid+things%22+Reagan+-%22Facts+are+stubborn+things%22&amp;gs_l=serp.3...4200.6360.0.7607.7.7.0.0.0.0.100.598.6j1.7.0.les%3Bepsugrpq1..0.0...1.1.IGFgLcM-f5I" target="_blank">
             <font size="2">
              thousands of websites
             </font>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             quote Reagan as saying
             <em>
              Facts are stupid things
             </em>
             as if it were somehow a significant quote  without noting that it came from a speech in which he said
             <em>
              stubborn things
             </em>
             several other times and quickly corrected his brief slip of the tongue.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             Of course,
            </font>
            <a href="https://www.google.com/search?source=ig&amp;rlz=1G1GGLQ_ENUS242&amp;q=%22Facts+are+stubborn+things.%22+Reagan+-Adams&amp;oq=%22Facts+are+stubborn+things.%22+Reagan+-Adams&amp;gs_l=igoogle.3...877.9644.0.10256.21.15.0.6.0.0.209.1515.10j3j1.14.0...0.0...1ac.1.E-4XnL09sO0" target="_blank">
             <font size="2">
              thousands of others
             </font>
            </a>
            <font size="2">
             note that Reagan said
             <em>
              Facts are stubborn things
             </em>
              without mentioning that he was quoting John Adams, thus creating the impression that Reagan coined the line.
            </font>
           </p>
           <p>
            <font size="2">
             When it comes to quotations on the Internet, facts are often slippery things.
            </font>
           </p>
