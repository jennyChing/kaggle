           <div class="content-header content-header-blue">
            <h2>
             <span class="blue">
              More Efficient Markets Nonsense
             </span>
            </h2>
           </div>
           <div class="article">
            <div class="article-meta">
             <span class="article-date red">
              Tuesday, September 2nd, 2014
             </span>
             by
             <span class="article-author red">
              <a class="url fn" href="https://mises.ca/posts/author/robert-murphy/" rel="author" title="Posts by Robert P. Murphy">
               Robert P. Murphy
              </a>
             </span>
             posted in
             <a href="https://mises.ca/posts/category/economics/" rel="category tag">
              Economics
             </a>
             .
            </div>
            <div class="entry" style="position: relative;">
             <p class="no-break">
              <span class="st_plusone_hcount" st_title="More Efficient Markets Nonsense" st_url="https://mises.ca/posts/blog/more-efficient-markets-nonsense/">
              </span>
              <span class="st_fblike_hcount" st_title="More Efficient Markets Nonsense" st_url="https://mises.ca/posts/blog/more-efficient-markets-nonsense/">
              </span>
              <span class="st_email_hcount" st_title="More Efficient Markets Nonsense" st_url="https://mises.ca/posts/blog/more-efficient-markets-nonsense/">
              </span>
              <span class="st_linkedin_hcount" st_title="More Efficient Markets Nonsense" st_url="https://mises.ca/posts/blog/more-efficient-markets-nonsense/">
              </span>
              <span class="st_twitter_hcount" st_title="More Efficient Markets Nonsense" st_url="https://mises.ca/posts/blog/more-efficient-markets-nonsense/">
              </span>
              <span class="st_facebook_hcount" st_title="More Efficient Markets Nonsense" st_url="https://mises.ca/posts/blog/more-efficient-markets-nonsense/">
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Ive got yet another example of proponents of the Efficient Markets Hypothesis (EMH) writing things that at first sound
              <a href="http://mises.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/stock-market-bubble-2.jpg">
               <img alt="stock-market-bubble-2" class="alignright wp-image-9397 size-medium" height="172" src="http://mises.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/stock-market-bubble-2-300x172.jpg" width="300"/>
              </a>
              profound, but upon further scrutiny descend into nonsense. However, whenever I write a post like this, people pounce on me and explain that the academic literature is quite respectable: There are weak and strong versions of the EMH, the pioneering EMH journal articles had falsifiable tests that it passed, etc.Now to be clear, Im fine with all of that. I admit that the EMH is a useful benchmark to get our thinking straight when we consider financial markets; everybody who wants to think about finance needs to learn the basics of the EMH and the empirical tests behind it. Nonetheless, my claim has always beensee
              <a href="http://mises.org/daily/3835">
               this post
              </a>
              for examplethat when famous EMH economists
              <em>
               casually comment on the markets
              </em>
              , they often spout nonsense. Today I will highlight an example from Nobel laureate and
              <a href="http://www.chicagobooth.edu/capideas/magazine/winter-2013/eugene-fama-efficient-markets-and-the-nobel-prize">
               EMH godfather Eugene Fama
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              Specifically, Scott Sumnerhimself a huge fan of the EMH
              <a href="http://econlog.econlib.org/archives/2014/09/predicting_bubb.html">
               over at EconLog
              </a>
              pooh-poohs Robert Shiller by favorably quoting the following remarks from Famas Nobel acceptance speech. The context here is that Fama (and Sumner following him) are saying Shiller should NOT be congratulated for calling the tech bubble in 1996, or for calling the housing bubble in 2003. With that in mind, lets hear Famas case against Shiller (with some apparent transcription errorscorrected):
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               <em>
                On the website for his book
               </em>
               Irrational Exuberance
               <em>
                , Shiller says that at a December3, 1996, lunch, he warned Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan that the level of stock prices was irrationally high. Greenspans famous Irrational Exuberance speech followed two days later. How good was Shillers forecast? On December3, 1996, the CRSP index of US stock market wealth stood at 1518. It more than doubled to 3191 on September 1, 2000, and then fell. This is the basis for the inference that the original bubble prediction was correct. At its low on March 11, 2003, however, the index, at 1739, was about 15% above 1518, its value on the initial bubble forecast date. These index numbers include reinvested dividends, which seem relevant for investor evaluations of bubble forecasts. If one ignores dividends and focuses on prices alone, the CRSP price index on March 11, 2003, was also above its December 3, 1996, value (648 versus 618). In short, there is not much evidence that prices were irrationally high at the time of the 1996 forecast, unless they have been irrationally high ever since.
               </em>
              </p>
              <p>
               <em>
                The second success story is the forecast in the mid-2000s that real estate prices were irrationally high. Many academics and practitioners made the same forecast, but an easy one to date is Case and Shiller (2003), which was probably written in late 2002 or early 2003. To give their prediction a good shot, I choose July 2003 as the date of the first forecast of a real estate bubble. The S&amp;P/Case Shiller 20-City Home Price Index is 142.99 in July 2003, its peak is 206.52 in July 2006, and its subsequent low is 134.07 in March 2012. Thus, the price decline from what I take to be the first forecast date is only 6.7 percent. The value to homeowners from housing services during those nine years from July 2003 to March 2012 surely exceeds 6.7% of July 2003 home values. Moreover, on the last sample date, October 2013, the real estate index, at 165.91, is 16% above its value on the initial bubble forecast date. Again there is not much evidence that prices were irrationally high at the time of the initial forecast.
               </em>
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              After quoting the above from Fama, Sumner goes on to write, My suggestion is to pay no attention to bubble forecasts. They are useless. It is clear that Fama thinks he has leveled a crushing blow to claims that Shiller called either the tech bubble or the housing bubble, and that Sumner agrees. (Indeed, Fama follows Sumner in thinking that the very concept of a bubble is operationally meaningless, a position
              <a href="http://mises.org/daily/4056/Bursting-Eugene-Famas-Bubble">
               I criticize here
              </a>
              .)
             </p>
             <p>
              For our purposes in the present blog post, I want to circle back and reconsider Famas numerical demonstrations above. Did he actually deliver a kill-shot to Shillers reputation? No, he didnt. At the very least, I will make two points that should cause the reader to realize that the situation is a lot more nuanced than Fama or Sumner thought. Inasmuch as this was Famas Nobel acceptance speech, surely he should be expected not to have made a bonehead move. The fact that he did (as I will show in a moment), and that Sumner high-fived him too in his zeal to discredit Shiller, shows just how sloppy the EMH perspective is
              <em>
               in practice
              </em>
              , even in the hands of presumed world-class experts.
             </p>
             <p>
              First, lets consider Famas apparent take-down of Shillers irrational exuberance call. Taking Famas numbers at face value, he is saying that with reinvestment of dividends, once the U.S. stock market hit its bottom in March 2003, 
              <em>
               the [CRSP] index, at 1739, was about 15% above 1518, its value on the initial bubble forecast date.
              </em>
              Fama takes it as self-evident that his job is done, because investors naturally care about the performance of their assets including reinvested dividends (though he does the analysis even without dividends, just looking at share prices, to further twist the knife).
             </p>
             <p>
              Now hold on a second. Suppose you approached an investor in December 1996 and said, Counting dividends, the CRSP index will be 1739 in March 2003. How much do you think it should be valued right now? Surely the first thing our hypothetical investor would do is look at the
              <a href="http://research.stlouisfed.org/fred2/data/DGS7.txt">
               7-year Treasury
              </a>
              , which was yielding about 6 percent annually at the time. Using round numbers, thats about a cumulative 45 percent return, if the investor plunked his money into a 7-year Treasury and held it six-and-a-quarter years till March 2003. Surely the return on volatile stocks should be
              <em>
               at least
              </em>
              as high as the relatively safe return on Treasuries, and so our hypotheticalinvestor would certainly not buy into the stock market if the relevant index figure were higher than 1200. Since the
              <em>
               actual
              </em>
              index value in late 1996 was 1518, that means the stock market was overvalued by at least 25%, within the confines of this hypothetical scenario.
             </p>
             <p>
              Obviously, Sumner or Fama could come up with various retorts. The more information we gave our hypothetical investor, the better hed doobviously. Clearly, the absolute best thing to do would have been to ride the tech bubble so long as the stock market appreciated faster than bonds, then get out right before the crash. Im not claiming that someone moving to all-Treasuries in late 1996 would have outperformed other possible strategies.
             </p>
             <p>
              However, what I
              <em>
               am
              </em>
              claiming is that Fama was wrong for thinking he had disprovedShillers irrational exuberance call, merely by pointing out the value of the market bottom in 2003. That was a bonehead argument. If you dont see it, try exaggerating it: Suppose Fama waited 100 years, then told us:
              <em>
               Robert Shiller said the stock market was overvalued back in 1996, but now its the year 2096 and guess what? The nominal value of your entire stock portfolio, including dividends, is $1 higher than it was in 1996. So clearly Shiller is a fool. The market was valued just fine back in 1996, thank you very much.
              </em>
              Would *that* statement be a good indictment of Shiller? Of course not. And for the same reason, pointing out that the stock market yielded a cumulative 15 percent return over six years and change, is hardly proof that the market had correctly valued those assets in late 1996.
             </p>
             <p>
              Now lets consider Famas discussion of the real estate bubbleor lack thereof. Just to remind you, here again is Famas dismissal of Shiller alleged prescience on this score:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               <em>
                The S&amp;P/Case Shiller 20-City Home Price Index is 142.99 in July 2003, its peak is 206.52 in July 2006, and its subsequent low is 134.07 in March 2012. Thus, the price decline from what I take to be the first forecast date is only 6.7 percent. The value to homeowners from housing services during those nine years from July 2003 to March 2012 surely exceeds 6.7% of July 2003 home values.
               </em>
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Now this rhetorical move has drastic consequences, the likes of which I dont think Fama or Sumner want to pursue. Fama is arguing that U.S. houses were *not* overvalued in July 2003, even though they would fall 6.7 percent in price by March 2012, because in the nine-year interim they yielded a flow of shelter services, which the owners presumably valued more than the decline in their asset price.
             </p>
             <p>
              So lets just think that through for a moment. If Shiller thinks a
              <em>
               fall
              </em>
              in home prices over a nine-year period is consistent with buyers rationally paying the original purchase prices, then what about the periods in U.S. history when homes would
              <em>
               appreciate
              </em>
              in nominal price over a nine-year stretchwhich is
              <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-ynrQyoAUzgM/UKvzBPEFGMI/AAAAAAAABg4/yLQ6jXe0q3w/s1600/U.S.+Housing+Price+Index+Since+1900.jpg">
               just about all of the 20th century
              </a>
              , except probably for homes purchases in the mid- and late 1920s and in the mid- to late 1980s? Surely Famas rhetorical move would lead us to conclude that those houses were very
              <em>
               undervalued
              </em>
              by the market, no? Think about it: If a home goes up in price, even just $1, within the first nine years from when its purchased, then not only does the purchaser get the capital gain (in nominal terms), but he or she also enjoys a flow of nine free years of housing services. So if we accept that Fama has blown up Shillers claim to fame on calling the housing bubble in 2003, he has given us another puzzle: Why the massive failure in the real estate market, forprobably 90 out of 100 years of the 20th century, to accurately price homes?
             </p>
             <p>
              In conclusion, I hope the reader understands the spirit of my arguments. Im not seriously suggesting that the correct, fundamental value of the CRSP index in December 1996 should have been 1200, and Im certainly not serious when I ask why the U.S. real estate market undervalues houses 90% of the time. Rather, I am showing that Eugene Famas glib dismissal of Robert Shillers bubble warnings dont work. The fact that these sloppy arguments were delivered in Famas Nobel acceptance speech are a great illustration of my claim that the EMH proponents
              <em>
               in practice
              </em>
              can spout nonsense when they discuss real-world financial markets.
             </p>

             </p>
