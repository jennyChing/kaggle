     <p>
      A
      <b>
       prefectural level city
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ),
      <b>
       prefectural city
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       prefectural level municipal city
      </b>
      is an administrative division of the
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       People's Republic of China
      </a>
      (PRC), ranking below a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_China" title="Province of China">
       province
      </a>
      and above a
      <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Counties of the People's Republic of China">
       county
      </a>
      in China's administrative structure. Prefectural level cities form the second level of the administrative structure (alongside
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefecture_of_China" title="Prefecture of China">
       prefectures
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Leagues_of_China" title="Leagues of China">
       leagues
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_prefecture" title="Autonomous prefecture">
       autonomous prefectures
      </a>
      ). Administrative chiefs (
      <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">
       mayors
      </a>
      ) of prefectural level cities generally have the same rank as a division chief (
      <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
       
      </span>
      ) of a national ministry. Since the 1980s, most former
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefecture_of_China" title="Prefecture of China">
       prefectures
      </a>
      have been renamed into prefectural level
      <b>
       cities
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A prefectural level city is a "city" (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        sh
       </span>
      </i>
      ) and "prefecture" (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        dq
       </span>
      </i>
      ) that have been merged into one unified jurisdiction. As such it is simultaneously a city, which is a municipal entry with subordinate districts, and a prefecture with subordinate county-level cities and counties which is an administrative division of a province.
     </p>
     <p>
      A prefectural level city is often not a "
      <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">
       city
      </a>
      " in the usual sense of the term (i.e., a large continuous urban settlement), but instead an administrative unit comprising, typically, a main central urban area (a city in the usual sense, usually with the same name as the prefectural level city), and its much larger surrounding
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural" title="Rural">
       rural
      </a>
      area containing many smaller cities, towns and villages. The larger prefectural level cities span over 100 kilometres (62mi).
     </p>
     <p>
      Prefectural level cities nearly always contain multiple
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_(People%27s_Republic_of_China)" title="County (People's Republic of China)">
       counties
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County-level_cities" title="County-level cities">
       county level cities
      </a>
      , and other such sub-divisions. This results from the fact that the formerly predominant
      <a href="/wiki/Prefecture" title="Prefecture">
       prefectures
      </a>
      , which prefectural level cities have mostly replaced, were themselves large administrative units containing cities, smaller towns, and rural areas. To distinguish a prefectural level city from its actual urban area (city in the strict sense), the term  shq ("urban area"), is used.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first prefectural level cities were created on 5 November 1983. Over the following two decades, prefectural level cities have come to replace the vast majority of Chinese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefecture_of_China" title="Prefecture of China">
       prefectures
      </a>
      ; the process is still ongoing.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most provinces are composed entirely or nearly entirely of prefectural level cities. Of the 22 provinces and 5 autonomous regions of the PRC, only 3 provinces (
      <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">
       Yunnan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">
       Guizhou
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">
       Qinghai
      </a>
      ) and 2 autonomous regions (
      <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">
       Xinjiang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="Tibet Autonomous Region">
       Tibet
      </a>
      ) have more than three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China#Prefectural_level" title="Administrative divisions of the People's Republic of China">
       second level or prefectural level divisions
      </a>
      that are not prefectural level cities.
     </p>
     <p>
      Criteria that a
      <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Prefectures of the People's Republic of China">
       prefecture
      </a>
      must meet to become a prefectural level city:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       An urban centre with a non-rural population over 250,000
      </li>
      <li>
       gross output of value of industry of 200,000,000
       <a href="/wiki/Renminbi" title="Renminbi">
        RMB
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">
        US$
       </a>
       32 million)
      </li>
      <li>
       the output of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tertiary_industry" title="Tertiary industry">
        tertiary industry
       </a>
       supersedes that of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_industry" title="Primary industry">
        primary industry
       </a>
       , contributing over 35% of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">
        GDP
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      15 large prefectural level cities have been granted the status of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-provincial_city" title="Sub-provincial city">
       sub-provincial city
      </a>
      , which gives them much greater autonomy.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Shijiazhuang" title="Shijiazhuang">
       Shijiazhuang
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Zhengzhou" title="Zhengzhou">
       Zhengzhou
      </a>
      are the largest prefectural level cities with populations approaching or exceeding some sub-provincial cities.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Sub-prefecture-level_city" title="Sub-prefecture-level city">
       sub-prefecture-level city
      </a>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/County-level_city" title="County-level city">
       county-level city
      </a>
      with powers approaching those of prefectural level cities.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
       Classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Prefecture-level_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are total of thee classification of prefecture-level city:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Regular prefectural level city
       </b>
       which consist of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPRC" title="CPRC">

       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CLPRC" title="CLPRC">
        county level cities
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DPRC" title="DPRC">
        districts
       </a>
       subdivisions.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Consolidated district-governed prefectural level city
       </b>
       which only consist of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DPRC" title="DPRC">
        districts
       </a>
       as it subdivisions.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Currently there are only 13 cities are under this classification:
         <a href="/wiki/Ezhou" title="Ezhou">
          Ezhou
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Foshan" title="Foshan">
          Foshan
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">
          Guangzhou
         </a>

         <a href="/wiki/Haikou" title="Haikou">
          Haikou
         </a>

         <a href="/wiki/Karamay" title="Karamay">
          Karamay
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Laiwu" title="Laiwu">
          Laiwu
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">
          Nanjing
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Sanya" title="Sanya">
          Sanya
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Shenzhen" title="Shenzhen">
          Shenzhen
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Wuhai" title="Wuhai">
          Wuhai
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Wuhan" title="Wuhan">
          Wuhan
         </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cartographic_expression_and_statistics">
       Cartographic expression and statistics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Prefecture-level_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cartographic expression and statistics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In most European or North American countries, there a clear distinction between "cities" and counties, prefectures, etc. The former are typically thought of as a "
      <i>
       point
      </i>
      " objects, and shown on maps with a circle or other such scale-less sign; the latter, if shown at all, are shown as
      <i>
       areas
      </i>
      with clearly defined borders. The distinction is obvious, since e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Indiana" title="Bloomington, Indiana">
       Bloomington, Indiana
      </a>
      , or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penticton,_British_Columbia" title="Penticton, British Columbia">
       Penticton, British Columbia
      </a>
      , are always thought of as distinct objects from, respectively,
      <a href="/wiki/Monroe_County,_Indiana" title="Monroe County, Indiana">
       Monroe County, Indiana
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Regional_District_of_Okanagan-Similkameen" title="Regional District of Okanagan-Similkameen">
       Regional District of Okanagan-Similkameen
      </a>
      , for which they are their respective seats of governments. In China, however, the same name (e.g.,  (
      <a href="/wiki/Xianning" title="Xianning">
       Xianning
      </a>
      Shi) in Chinese, or
      <a href="/wiki/Xianning" title="Xianning">
       Xianning
      </a>
      [City] in English) are used to refer both to an entire "prefectural level city" and to its urban core (what one would call a "city" in Europe or North America). This results in somewhat different mapping conventions used in China vs. most other countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      Typically, a less detailed map - one that does not show administrative divisions below the provincial level - would merely mark the location of the urban core (or, more precisely, that of the city government) with a circle, just like cities are shown on a map of a European or American country. The circle would be labeled with the name of the city (e.g.  (
      <a href="/wiki/Xianning" title="Xianning">
       Xianning
      </a>
      Shi) in Chinese, or
      <a href="/wiki/Xianning" title="Xianning">
       Xianning
      </a>
      in English) rather than, for example, that of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_(PRC)" title="District (PRC)">
       district
      </a>
      within which the urban core may be located. The same map may also show other sufficiently large populated places within the same prefectural level city, labeling them based on what third level administrative units they correspond to. For example, along with Xianning, a map may show
      <a href="/wiki/Tongshan_County,_Hubei" title="Tongshan County, Hubei">
       Tongshan
      </a>
      without indicating in any way that administratively, the latter is part of the former.
     </p>
     <p>
      A more detailed map (e.g., a typical provincial map) would also show the border of each second level administrative unit (such as a prefectural level city), much like an American county may be shown on a US state map. In this case, the same unit name (such as , Xianning [City]) would be used to label the entire area. Within it, the division of the second level unit into third level units may be shown as well.
     </p>
     <p>
      This convention may sometimes make it difficult to identify places mentioned in older sources with places shown on modern maps. For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Guo_Moruo" title="Guo Moruo">
       Guo Moruo
      </a>
      tells in his autobiography that he was born in the small town of Shawan,
      <a href="/wiki/Leshan" title="Leshan">
       Leshan
      </a>
      Prefecture, and attended primary school in Jiading, the main town of the prefecture. A typical modern map is unlikely to show either town: Shawan, because it's too small, and Jiading, because it is the seat of government for the today's prefectural level city of Leshan, and thus is marked on a modern map by a circle labeled "Leshan". A more detailed map (or Google Maps) would show Shawan District within Leshan, but, most likely, no Jiading per se - just Jiading Street () within Leshan's urban area.
     </p>
     <p>
      Chinese statistical data (area, population, industrial production value, etc.) are typically reported for the entire prefectural level city, as well as for its constituent third level units. Thus, a person not familiar with these conventions may be surprised to learn that a comparatively little known city of
      <a href="/wiki/Huangshi" title="Huangshi">
       Huangshi
      </a>
      , Hubei, has 2.5 million residents - more than most European capital cities - until he or she realizes that this number corresponds to an entire prefectural level area of almost 100 kilometres (62mi) across, covering several places (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Daye,_Hubei" title="Daye, Hubei">
       Daye
      </a>
      ) that are described as (
      <a href="/wiki/County-level_city" title="County-level city">
       county level
      </a>
      ) "cities" in their own right. Even if the reader has a map and the data for individual third level units, arriving to the population count for the actual main urban area of a prefectural level city may not be easy, because it may be split between several districts, some of which may stretch far out into the rural area as well.
     </p>
