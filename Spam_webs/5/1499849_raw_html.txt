              <h2 class="entry-title">
               <a href="http://expressthereal.me/dont-tell-your-grandmother/" title="Dont Tell Your Grandmother!">
                Dont Tell Your Grandmother!
               </a>
              </h2>
              <span class="date-title">
               By
               <a href="http://expressthereal.me/author/expressthereal/" rel="author" title="Posts by ExpressTheReal">
                ExpressTheReal
               </a>
               on January 1, 2015 in
               <a href="http://expressthereal.me/category/stories/" rel="category tag">

              </span>

             <div class="post-content">
              <!-- Post content -->
              <p>
               Dont tell your grandmother you bought a German car.
              </p>
              <p>
               This goes with the long line of dont tell your grandmother scenarios.
              </p>
              <p>
               Dont tell your grandmother you gained weight.
              </p>
              <p>
               Dont tell your grandmother you are living with your boyfriend.
              </p>
              <p>
               Dont tell your grandmother you are pregnant yet.
               <span id="more-216">
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               Why cant I tell my grandmother about the good things that are happening in my life? Ive always been too thin I met someone I want to spend the rest of my life with (and were being smart about it with a trial run!) Im having a baby for goodness sakes.
              </p>
              <p>
               But the response was always the same. We dont want her to have an anxiety attack.
              </p>
              <p>
               I never understood that response because my grandmother was always one of the strongest people Ive known. She survived World War II, started her life over in a country an ocean apart from everything she knew, found the love of her life and lost him too young, raised a family, and outlived nearly everyone she knew.
              </p>
              <p>
               And, she always found out. As a matter of fact, she usually knew before I told my own parents because she always listened without judgment, believed in my abilities to overcome my own obstacles, and, in that confident German accent, told me I would do the right thing, even if it didnt feel that way at the moment.
              </p>
              <p>
               As years went on, she became frail. Cancer took over and the doctors gave her less than six months to live; but, the woman that her daughters wanted so much to protect from an anxiety attack showed everyone and lived almost another two years. She maintained her dignity and her sense of humor and strength despite what was happening.
              </p>
              <p>
               As I look back, I dont know if it was as much about protecting her as it was protecting ourselves from any tinge of disappointment in her not being as proud of us as we were of her for not living up to her level of conviction or strength.
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="font-style: italic;">
                The author of this piece, Leslie Polvado, is a freelance writer and enjoys reflections upon her familys past and the cultural significance that holds for the generations living today.
               </span>
              </p>
