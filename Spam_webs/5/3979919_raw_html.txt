        <block>
         <p>
          TOKYO   Japan's prime minister said Friday that the country's tsunami-damaged nuclear plant has achieved a stable state of cold shutdown, a crucial step toward the eventual lifting of evacuation orders and closing of the plant.
         </p>
         <p>
          Prime Minister Yoshihiko Noda's announcement was meant to reassure the nation of significant progress since the March 11 tsunami sent three reactors at the Fukushima Daiichi plant into meltdowns in the worst nuclear crisis since Chernobyl in 1986.
         </p>
         <p>
          Experts say the plant 140 miles northeast of Tokyo remains vulnerable to problems, its surroundings so contaminated that closing it safely will take 30 years or more.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now that we have achieved stability in the reactors, a major concern for the nation has been resolved, Noda said.
         </p>
         <p>
          Radiation released from the plant has been significantly reduced, and additional measures ensure stability even in the event of another disaster, he said.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are many issues that remain. Our battle is not over, Noda said, adding that he hopes displaced residents can return home even a day sooner.
         </p>
         <p>
          The government's endorsement of the claim by Tokyo Electric Power Co. that the reactors have reached cold shutdown status is a necessary step toward revising evacuation zones and shifting the focus from stabilizing the facility to actually starting the arduous process of shutting it down.
         </p>
         <p>
          The government said the plant has reached cold shutdown conditions  a cautious phrasing reflecting the fact that TEPCO cannot measure the temperatures of melted fuel in the damaged reactors in the same way as with normal ones.
         </p>
        </block>
