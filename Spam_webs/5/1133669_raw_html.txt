     <p>
      During 1217 August 1969,
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
       Northern Ireland
      </a>
      was rocked by intense political and
      <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">
       sectarian
      </a>
      rioting. There had been sporadic violence throughout the year arising from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_civil-rights_movement_(1960s)" title="Northern Ireland civil-rights movement (1960s)">
       civil rights
      </a>
      campaign, which was demanding an end to discrimination against
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">
       Irish Catholics
      </a>
      . Civil rights marches were repeatedly attacked by both
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestant" title="Ulster Protestant">
       Ulster Protestant
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">
       loyalists
      </a>
      and by the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">
       Royal Ulster Constabulary
      </a>
      (RUC), a Unionist and largely Protestant police force.
     </p>
     <p>
      The disorder led to the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bogside" title="Battle of the Bogside">
       Battle of the Bogside
      </a>
      in Derry, a three-day riot in the
      <a href="/wiki/Bogside" title="Bogside">
       Bogside
      </a>
      district between the RUC and the nationalist/Catholic residents. In support of the Bogsiders, nationalists and Catholics launched protests elsewhere in Northern Ireland. Some of these led to attacks by loyalists working alongside the police. The most bloody rioting was in Belfast, where seven people were killed and hundreds more wounded. Scores of houses, most of them owned by Catholics, as well as businesses and factories were burned-out. In addition, thousands of mostly Catholic families were driven from their homes. In certain areas, the RUC helped the loyalists and failed to protect Catholic areas. Events in Belfast have been viewed by some as a
      <a href="/wiki/Pogrom" title="Pogrom">
       pogrom
      </a>
      against the minority Catholic and nationalist community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      was
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Banner" title="Operation Banner">
       deployed
      </a>
      to restore order and state control and
      <a href="/wiki/Peace_lines" title="Peace lines">
       peace lines
      </a>
      began to be built to separate the two sides. The events of August 1969 are widely seen as the beginning of the thirty-year conflict known as
      <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">
       the Troubles
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
       Northern Ireland
      </a>
      was destabilised throughout 1968 by sporadic rioting arising out of the
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">
       civil disobedience
      </a>
      campaign of the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Civil_Rights_Association" title="Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association">
       Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association
      </a>
      (NICRA), which was demanding an end to discrimination against
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">
       Catholics
      </a>
      in voting rights, housing and employment. NICRA was opposed by
      <a href="/wiki/Ian_Paisley" title="Ian Paisley">
       Ian Paisley
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Constitution_Defence_Committee" title="Ulster Constitution Defence Committee">
       Ulster Constitution Defence Committee
      </a>
      (UCDC) and other
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">
       loyalist
      </a>
      groups.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the summer of 1969, before the riots broke out, the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_of_Jurists" title="International Commission of Jurists">
       International Commission of Jurists
      </a>
      (ICJ) published a highly critical report on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_government" title="British government">
       British government
      </a>
      's policy in Northern Ireland.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
        The Times
       </a>
      </i>
      wrote that this report "criticised the Northern Ireland Government for
      <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">
       police brutality
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Religious_discrimination" title="Religious discrimination">
       religious discrimination
      </a>
      [against Catholics] and
      <a href="/wiki/Gerrymandering" title="Gerrymandering">
       gerrymandering
      </a>
      in politics".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose160_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rose160-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The ICJ secretary general said that laws and conditions in Northern Ireland had been cited by the South African government "to justify their own policies of discrimination" (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Africa_under_apartheid" title="South Africa under apartheid">
       South Africa under apartheid
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose160_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rose160-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       The Times
      </i>
      also reported that the
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Special_Constabulary" title="Ulster Special Constabulary">
       Ulster Special Constabulary
      </a>
      (USC), Northern Ireland's
      <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_police" title="Auxiliary police">
       reserve police force
      </a>
      , was "regarded as the militant arm of the Protestant
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_Institution" title="Orange Institution">
       Orange Order
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose160_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-rose160-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Telegraph" title="Belfast Telegraph">
        Belfast Telegraph
       </a>
      </i>
      reported that the ICJ had added Northern Ireland to the list of states/jurisdictions "where the protection of human rights is inadequately assured".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Events_leading_up_to_the_August_riots">
       Events leading up to the August riots
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Events leading up to the August riots">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first major confrontation between Civil Rights activists and the police occurred in
      <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">
       Derry
      </a>
      on 5 October 1968, when a NICRA march was
      <a href="/wiki/Club_(weapon)" title="Club (weapon)">
       baton
      </a>
      -charged by the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">
       Royal Ulster Constabulary
      </a>
      (RUC) police.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Disturbed by the prospect of major violence, the Prime Minister of Northern Ireland,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terence_O%27Neill" title="Terence O'Neill">
       Terence O'Neill
      </a>
      , promised reforms in return for a "truce", whereby no further demonstrations would be held.
     </p>
     <p>
      However the truce was broken in January 1969 when
      <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Democracy_(Ireland)" title="People's Democracy (Ireland)">
       People's Democracy
      </a>
      , a radical left-wing group, staged an anti-government march from Belfast to Derry. Loyalists attacked the marchers a number of times, most determinedly at
      <a href="/wiki/Burntollet_Bridge_incident" title="Burntollet Bridge incident">
       Burntollet Bridge
      </a>
      (about five miles (8km) outside Derry), and the RUC were accused of not protecting the marchers. This action, and the RUC's subsequent entry into the
      <a href="/wiki/Bogside" title="Bogside">
       Bogside
      </a>
      , led to serious rioting in Derry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March and April 1969, there were six bomb attacks on electricity and water infrastructure targets, causing blackouts and water shortages. At first the attacks were blamed on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1922%E2%80%931969)" title="Irish Republican Army (19221969)">
       Irish Republican Army
      </a>
      (IRA). In fact, it later emerged that members of the loyalist
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestant_Volunteers" title="Ulster Protestant Volunteers">
       Ulster Protestant Volunteers
      </a>
      (UPV) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force_(1966)" title="Ulster Volunteer Force (1966)">
       Ulster Volunteer Force
      </a>
      (UVF) had carried out the bombings in an attempt to implicate the IRA, destabilise the
      <a href="/wiki/Executive_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Executive Committee of the Privy Council of Northern Ireland">
       Government
      </a>
      and halt the reforms demanded by the Civil Rights movement and promised by Terence O'Neill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There was some movement on reform in Northern Ireland in the first half of 1969. On 23 April the Unionist Parliamentary Party voted by 28 to 22 to introduce universal adult suffrage in local government elections in Northern Ireland. The call for "one man, one vote" had been one of the key demands of the civil rights movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Five days later, Terence O'Neill resigned as UUP leader and Northern Ireland Prime Minister and was replaced in both roles by
      <a href="/wiki/James_Chichester-Clark" title="James Chichester-Clark">
       James Chichester-Clark
      </a>
      . Chichester-Clark, despite having resigned in protest over the introduction of universal suffrage in local government, announced that he would continue the reforms begun by O'Neill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Street violence, however, continued to escalate. On 19 April there was serious rioting in the Bogside area of Derry following clashes between NICRA marchers, loyalists and the RUC. A Catholic, Samuel Devenny was severely beaten by the RUC and later died of his injuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 12 July, during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_Institution" title="Orange Institution">
       Orange Order
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/The_Twelfth" title="The Twelfth">
       Twelfth of July
      </a>
      marches, there was serious rioting in Derry, Belfast and Dungiven, causing many families in Belfast to flee from their homes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A Catholic civilian Francis McCloskey (67) died one day after being hit on the head with batons by RUC officers during rioting in Dungiven.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron69_7-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-chron69-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_the_Bogside">
       Battle of the Bogside
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Battle of the Bogside">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bogside" title="Battle of the Bogside">
       Battle of the Bogside
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sporadic violence took place throughout the rest of the year between Catholic nationalists, Protestant loyalists and the RUC, and intensified over the summer, during the
      <a href="/wiki/Orange_Order" title="Orange Order">
       Orange Order
      </a>
      's marching season. On 2 August, there was serious rioting in Belfast, when Protestant crowds from the
      <a href="/wiki/Crumlin_Road" title="Crumlin Road">
       Crumlin Road
      </a>
      area tried to storm the Catholic Unity Flats. They were held back with difficulty by the police.
     </p>
     <p>
      This unrest culminated in a pitched battle in Derry from 1215 August. The
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Bogside" title="Battle of the Bogside">
       Battle of the Bogside
      </a>
      began when violence broke out around a loyalist
      <a href="/wiki/Apprentice_Boys_of_Derry" title="Apprentice Boys of Derry">
       Apprentice Boys of Derry
      </a>
      parade on 12 August. The RUC, in trying to disperse the nationalist crowd, drove them back into the nationalist
      <a href="/wiki/Bogside" title="Bogside">
       Bogside
      </a>
      area and then tried to enter the area themselves. The Bogside's inhabitants mobilised en masse to prevent them entering the area and a huge riot ensued between hundreds of RUC personnel and thousands of Bogsiders. On the second day of this confrontation, 13 August, the Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association appealed for demonstrations across Northern Ireland in support of the Bogside, in an effort to draw off police resources from the conflict there. When nationalists elsewhere in Northern Ireland carried out such demonstrations, severe inter-communal violence erupted between Catholics, Protestants and the police.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rioting_in_Belfast">
       Rioting in Belfast
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rioting in Belfast">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Belfast_mural_3.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Belfast_mural_3.jpg/300px-Belfast_mural_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Belfast_mural_3.jpg/450px-Belfast_mural_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Belfast_mural_3.jpg/600px-Belfast_mural_3.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Belfast_mural_3.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A mural in Belfast remembering the 1969 riots
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Belfast saw by far the most intense violence of the August 1969 riots. Unlike Derry, where Catholic nationalists were a majority, in Belfast they were a minority and were also geographically divided and surrounded by Protestants and loyalists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For this reason, whereas in Derry the fighting was largely between nationalists and the RUC, in Belfast it also involved fighting between Catholics and Protestants, including exchanges of gunfire and widespread burning of homes and businesses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On the night of 12 August, bands of Apprentice Boys arrived back in Belfast after taking part in the Derry march. They were met by Protestant pipe bands and a large crowd of supporters. They then marched to Shankill Road waving Union Flags and singing "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Sash" title="The Sash">
       The Sash My Father Wore
      </a>
      " (a popular loyalist ballad).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to journalists Patrick Bishop and Eamonn Mallie, "Both communities were in the grip of a mounting paranoia about the other's intentions. Catholics were convinced that they were about to become victims of a Protestant pogrom; Protestants that they were on the eve of an IRA insurrection".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wednesday_13_August">
       Wednesday 13 August
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wednesday 13 August">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first disturbances in Northern Ireland's capital took place on the night of 13 August. Derry activists
      <a href="/wiki/Eamonn_McCann" title="Eamonn McCann">
       Eamonn McCann
      </a>
      and Sean Keenan contacted Frank Gogarty of the Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association to organise demonstrations in Belfast to draw off police from Derry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Independently, Belfast IRA leader
      <a href="/wiki/Billy_McMillen" title="Billy McMillen">
       Billy McMillen
      </a>
      ordered republicans to organise demonstrations, "in support of Derry".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In protest at the RUC's actions in Derry, a group of 500 nationalists and republicans assembled at Divis flats and staged a rally outside
      <a href="/wiki/Springfield_Road,_Belfast" title="Springfield Road, Belfast">
       Springfield Road
      </a>
      RUC station, where they handed in a petition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swan281_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-swan281-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After handing in the petition, the crowd of 12000 people, including IRA members such as
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_McCann" title="Joe McCann">
       Joe McCann
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanley.2C_Millar.2C_p126_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hanley.2C_Millar.2C_p126-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      began a protest march along Falls Road and Divis Street to the Hastings Street RUC base.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swan281_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-swan281-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When they arrived, about 50 youths broke away from the march and attacked the RUC base with stones and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol_bomb" title="Petrol bomb">
       petrol bombs
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swan281_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-swan281-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geraghty21_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-geraghty21-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The RUC responded by sending out riot police
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swan281_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-swan281-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and by driving Shorland armoured cars at the crowd.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geraghty21_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-geraghty21-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Protesters pushed burning cars onto the road to stop the RUC from entering the nationalist area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At Leeson Street, roughly halfway between the clashes at Springfield and Hastings Street RUC bases, an RUC
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humber_armoured_car" title="Humber armoured car">
       Humber armoured car
      </a>
      was attacked with a hand grenade and rifle fire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geraghty21_16-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-geraghty21-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the time, it was not known who had launched the attack, but it has since emerged that it was IRA members, acting under the orders of Billy McMillen. McMillen also authorised members of the Fianna (IRA youth wing) to petrol bomb the Springfield Road RUC base.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanley.2C_Millar.2C_p126_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hanley.2C_Millar.2C_p126-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shots were exchanged there between the IRA and RUC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geraghty21_16-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-geraghty21-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the attacks on the RUC, the car dealership of Protestant Isaac Agnew, on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falls_Road_(Belfast)" title="Falls Road (Belfast)">
       Falls Road
      </a>
      , was destroyed. The nationalist crowd also burnt a Catholic-owned pub and betting shop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At this stage, loyalist crowds gathered on the
      <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Road" title="Shankill Road">
       Shankill Road
      </a>
      but did not join in the fighting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      That night barricades went up at the
      <a href="/wiki/Interface_area" title="Interface area">
       interface areas
      </a>
      between Catholic and Protestant neighbourhoods.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg/300px-Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg/450px-Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg/600px-Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shorland_armoured_car_mk1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Shorland_armoured_car" title="Shorland armoured car">
         Shorland armoured car
        </a>
        . The RUC used Shorlands mounted with Browning heavy machine-guns during the riots
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thursday_14_August_and_early_hours_of_Friday_15_August">
       Thursday 14 August and early hours of Friday 15 August
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thursday 14 August and early hours of Friday 15 August">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On 14 August, many Catholics and Protestants living on the edge of their ghettos fled their homes for safety.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The loyalists viewed the nationalist attacks of Wednesday night as an organised attempt by the IRA "to undermine the constitutional position of Northern Ireland within the United Kingdom".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The IRA, contrary to loyalist belief, was responding to events rather than orchestrating them. Billy McMillen called up all available IRA members for "defensive duties" and sent parties out to Cupar Street, Divis Street and St Comgall's School on Dover Street. They amounted to 30 IRA Volunteers, 12 women, 40 youths from the Fianna and 1520 girls. Their arms consisted of one
      <a href="/wiki/Thompson_submachine_gun" title="Thompson submachine gun">
       Thompson submachine gun
      </a>
      , one
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sten_submachine_gun" title="Sten submachine gun">
       Sten submachine gun
      </a>
      , one
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee%E2%80%93Enfield_rifle" title="LeeEnfield rifle">
       LeeEnfield rifle
      </a>
      and six handguns. A "wee factory" was also set up in Leeson Street to make petrol bombs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their orders at the outset were to, "disperse people trying to burn houses, but under no circumstances to take life".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Falls.E2.80.93Shankill_interface_near_Divis_Tower">
       FallsShankill interface near Divis Tower
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: FallsShankill interface near Divis Tower">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      That evening, a nationalist crowd marched to Hastings Street RUC station, which they began to attack with stones for a second night.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Loyalist crowds (wielding petrol bombs, bricks, stones, sharpened poles and protective dustbin lids) gathered at neighbouring Dover and Percy Streets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings38_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings38-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were confronted by nationalists, who had hastily blocked their streets with barricades. Fighting broke out between the rival factions at about 11:00 pm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The RUC concentrated their efforts on the nationalist rioters, who they scattered with armoured cars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Catholics claimed that USC officers had been seen giving guns to the loyalists,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while journalists reported seeing pike-wielding loyalists standing among the RUC officers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From the nearby rooftop of Divis Tower flats, a group of nationalists would spend the rest of the night raining missiles on the RUC below.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A chain of people were passing stones and petrol bombs from the ground to the roof.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings39_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings39-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Loyalists began pushing into the Falls Road area along Percy Street, Beverly Street and Dover Street. The rioters contained a rowdy gang of loyalist football supporters who had returned from a match.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On Dover Street, the loyalist crowd was led by
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">
       Ulster Unionist Party
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament_(United_Kingdom)" title="Member of Parliament (United Kingdom)">
       MP
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/John_McQuade" title="John McQuade">
       John McQuade
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hastings.2C_p.40_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hastings.2C_p.40-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On Percy Street, a loyalist opened fire with a shotgun,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings38_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings38-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and USC officers helped the loyalists to push back the nationalists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As they entered the nationalist ghetto, loyalists began burning Catholic homes and businesses on Percy Street, Beverly Street and Dover Street.
     </p>

     <p>
      At the intersection of Dover and Divis Street, an IRA unit
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      opened fire on the crowd of RUC officers and loyalists, who were trying to enter the Catholic area. Protestant Herbert Roy (26) was killed
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings39_26-1">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At this point, the RUC, believing they were facing an organised IRA uprising, deployed
      <a href="/wiki/Shorland_armoured_car" title="Shorland armoured car">
       Shorland armoured cars
      </a>
      mounted with heavy
      <a href="/wiki/M1919_Browning_machine_gun" title="M1919 Browning machine gun">
       Browning machine guns
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      whose .30 calibre bullets "tore through walls as if they were cardboard".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan9192_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan9192-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In response to the RUC coming under fire at Divis Street, three Shorland armoured cars were called to the scene. The Shorlands were immediately attacked with gunfire, an explosive device and petrol bombs. The RUC believed that the shots had come from nearby Divis Tower.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hastings.2C_p.40_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hastings.2C_p.40-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gunners inside the Shorlands returned fire with their heavy machine-guns. At least thirteen Divis Tower flats were hit by high-velocity gunfire. A nine-year-old boy, Patrick Rooney, was killed by machine-gun fire as he lay in bed in one of the flats. He was the first child to be killed in the violence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At about 01:00, not long after the shooting of Patrick Rooney, the RUC again opened fire on Divis Tower. The shots killed Hugh McCabe (20), a Catholic soldier who was 'on leave'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He and another had been on the roof of the Whitehall building (which was part of the Divis complex) and were pulling a wounded man to safety. The RUC claimed he was armed at the time and that gunfire was coming from the roof, but this was denied by many witnesses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostlives38_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lostlives38-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Republican_Labour_Party" title="Republican Labour Party">
       Republican Labour Party
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">
       MP
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Central_(Northern_Ireland_Parliament_constituency)" title="Belfast Central (Northern Ireland Parliament constituency)">
       Belfast Central
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Kennedy_(politician)" title="Paddy Kennedy (politician)">
       Paddy Kennedy
      </a>
      , who was on the scene, phoned the RUC headquarters and appealed to Northern Ireland Minister for Home Affairs,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Porter_(politician)" title="Robert Porter (politician)">
       Robert Porter
      </a>
      , for the Shorlands to be withdrawn and the shooting to stop. Porter replied that this was impossible as, "the whole town is in rebellion". Porter told Kennedy that Donegall Street police station was under heavy machine-gun fire. In fact, it was undisturbed throughout the riots.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some time after the killing of Hugh McCabe, some 200 loyalists attacked Catholic Divis Street and began burning houses there.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings45_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings45-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A unit of six IRA volunteers in St Comgall's School shot at them with a rifle, a thompson machine-gun and some pistols; keeping the attackers back and wounding eight of them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An RUC Shorland then arrived and opened fire on the school.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings45_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings45-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The IRA gunmen returned fire and managed to escape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Falls.E2.80.93Shankill_interface_near_Clonard_Monastery">
       FallsShankill interface near Clonard Monastery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: FallsShankill interface near Clonard Monastery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      West of St Comgall's, loyalists broke through the nationalist barricades on Conway Street and burned two-thirds of the houses. Catholics claimed that the RUC held them back so that the loyalists could burn their homes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings45_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings45-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Scarman Report found that RUC officers were on Conway Street when its houses were set alight, but "failed to take effective action".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Journalist Max Hastings wrote that loyalists on Conway Street had been begging the RUC to give them their guns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hastings45_34-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-hastings45-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ardoyne">
       Ardoyne
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ardoyne">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Rioting in Ardoyne, north of the city centre, began in the evening near Holy Cross Catholic church. Loyalists crossed over to the Catholic/nationalist side of Crumlin Road to attack Brookfield Street, Herbert Street, Butler Street and Hooker Street. These had been hastily blocked by nationalist barricades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Loyalists reportedly threw petrol bombs at Catholics "over the heads of RUC officers",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostlives36_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lostlives36-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as RUC armoured cars were used to smash through the barricades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bishop.2C_Mallie.2C_p113_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bishop.2C_Mallie.2C_p113-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The IRA had little presence in Ardoyne and its defence was organised by a group of ex-servicemen armed with shotguns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bishop.2C_Mallie.2C_p113_37-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bishop.2C_Mallie.2C_p113-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The nationalist gunmen fired the first shots at the RUC, who responded by firing machine-guns down the streets, killing two Catholic civilians (Samuel McLarnon, 27, and Michael Lynch, 28) and wounding ten more.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostlives34_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lostlives34-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Friday_15_August">
       Friday 15 August
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Friday 15 August">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The morning of 15 August saw many Catholic families in central Belfast flee to
      <a href="/wiki/Andersonstown" title="Andersonstown">
       Andersonstown
      </a>
      on the western fringes of the city, to escape the rioting. According to Bishop and Mallie, "Each side's perceptions of the other's intentions had become so warped that the Protestants believed the Catholics were clearing the decks for a further attempt at insurrection in the evening".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At 04:30 on Friday 15 August, the Police Commissioner for Belfast asked for military aid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From the early hours of Friday, the RUC had withdrawn to its bases to defend them. The interface areas were thus left unpoliced for half a day until the British Army arrived.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Deputy Police Commissioner had assumed that the British Army would be deployed by 10:00 or 11:00.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At 12:25 that afternoon, the Northern Ireland cabinet finally sent a request for military aid to the Home Office in London.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, it would be another nine hours until the British Army arrived at the Falls/Shankill interface where it was needed. Many Catholics and nationalists felt that they had been left at the mercy of loyalists by forces of the state who were meant to protect them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The IRA, which had limited manpower and weaponry at the start of the riots, was also exhausted and low on ammunition. Its leader Billy McMillen and 19 other republicans were arrested by the RUC early on 15 August under the
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_Authorities_(Special_Powers)_Act_(Northern_Ireland)_1922" title="Civil Authorities (Special Powers) Act (Northern Ireland) 1922">
       Special Powers Act
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </a>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Falls.E2.80.93Shankill_interface_near_Clonard_Monastery_2">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <p>
      On 15 August, violence continued along the Falls/Shankill interface. Father PJ Egan of
      <a href="/wiki/Clonard_Monastery" title="Clonard Monastery">
       Clonard Monastery
      </a>
      recalled that a large loyalist mob moved down Cupar Street at about 15:00 and was held back by nationalist youths.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan101102_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan101102-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shooting began at about 15:45.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan101102_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan101102-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A small IRA party under
      <a href="/wiki/Billy_McKee" title="Billy McKee">
       Billy McKee
      </a>
      was present and had two .22 rifles at their disposal. They exchanged shots with a loyalist sniper who was firing from a house on Cupar Street, but failed to dislodge him, or to halt the burning of Catholic houses in the area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Almost all of the houses on Bombay street were burned by the loyalists, and many others were burned on Kashmir Road and Cupar Street  the most extensive destruction of property during the riots.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A loyalist sniper shot dead Gerald McAuley (15), a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fianna_%C3%89ireann" title="Fianna ireann">
       Fianna
      </a>
      (IRA's youth wing),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as he helped people flee their homes on Bombay Street.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan104_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan104-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At about 18:30 the British Army's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Royal_Regiment_of_Wales" title="The Royal Regiment of Wales">
       The Royal Regiment of Wales
      </a>
      was deployed on the Falls Road.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where they were greeted with subdued applause and cheering.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, despite pleas from locals, they did not move into the streets that were being attacked.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At about 21:35 that night, the soldiers finally took up positions at the blazing interface
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evelegh_40-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-evelegh-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and blocked the streets with barbed-wire barricades. Father PJ Egan recalled that the soldiers called on the loyalists to surrender but they instead began shooting and throwing petrol bombs at the soldiers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan101102_42-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan101102-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The soldiers could only fire back on the orders of an officer when life was directly threatened.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The loyalists continued shooting and burned more Catholic-owned houses on Bombay Street,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but were stopped by soldiers using
      <a href="/wiki/Tear_gas" title="Tear gas">
       tear gas
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nusight_9-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-nusight-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>

      </sup>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ardoyne_2">

        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ardoyne">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      British soldiers were not deployed in Ardoyne, and violence continued there on Friday night. Nationalists hijacked 50 buses from the local bus depot, set them on fire and used them as makeshift barricades to block access to Ardoyne. According to republican activist
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Meehan_(Irish_republican)" title="Martin Meehan (Irish republican)">
       Martin Meehan
      </a>
      , 20 Catholics were wounded by shotgun fire that night.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      A Protestant civilian, David Linton (48), was shot dead by nationalist gunmen at the Palmer Street/Crumlin Road junction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several Catholic-owned houses were set alight on Brookfield Street.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Scarman Report found that an RUC armoured vehicle was nearby when Brookfield Street was set alight, but made no move.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Saturday_16_August">
       Saturday 16 August
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Saturday 16 August">

        ]
       </span>

     <p>

     <p>
      In aid of the Bogsiders, the NICRA executive decided to launch protests in towns across Northern Ireland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Scarman Report concluded that the spread of the disturbances "owed much to a deliberate decision by some minority groups to relieve police pressure on the rioters in Londonderry". It included the NICRA among these groups.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <p>
      On the evening of 11 August a riot erupted in
      <a href="/wiki/Dungannon" title="Dungannon">
       Dungannon
      </a>
      after a meeting of the NICRA. This was quelled after the RUC baton charged nationalist rioters down Irish Street. There were claims of police brutality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <p>
      On 12 August, protesters attacked the RUC bases in
      <a href="/wiki/Coalisland" title="Coalisland">
       Coalisland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Strabane" title="Strabane">
       Strabane
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Newry" title="Newry">
       Newry
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white72_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-white72-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      On 13 August there were further riots in Dungannon, Coalisland,
      <a href="/wiki/Dungiven" title="Dungiven">
       Dungiven
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">
       Armagh
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Newry" title="Newry">
       Newry
      </a>
      .

        17
        <span>

        17

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">
       Effects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The rioting petered out by Sunday, 17 August. By the end of the riots:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       8 people had been killed, including
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutton69_8-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-sutton69-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <ul>
        <li>
         5 Catholics shot dead by the RUC
        </li>
        <li>
         2 Protestants shot dead by nationalist gunmen
        </li>
        <li>
         1
         <a href="/wiki/Fianna_%C3%89ireann" title="Fianna ireann">
          Fianna
         </a>
         member shot dead by loyalist gunmen
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       750+ people had been injured
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan106_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-coogan106-49">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         49
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
        133 (72 Catholics and 61 Protestants) of those injured suffered gunshot wounds
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan106_49-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-coogan106-49">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         49
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       150+ Catholic homes
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         50
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       and 275+ businesses had been destroyed  83% of all buildings destroyed were owned by Catholics
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan106_49-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-coogan106-49">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         49
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      During July, August and September 1969, 1,820+ families had been forced to flee their homes, including
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan91_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan91-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       1,505 Catholic families
      </li>
      <li>
       315 Protestant families
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Catholics generally fled across
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_border" title="Irish border">
       the border
      </a>
      into the
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">
       Republic of Ireland
      </a>
      , while Protestants generally fled to east Belfast.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan91_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan91-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Irish Defence Forces set up
      <a href="/wiki/Refugee_camp" title="Refugee camp">
       refugee camps
      </a>
      in the Republic  at one point the
      <a href="/wiki/Gormanston_Camp" title="Gormanston Camp">
       Gormanston refugee camp
      </a>
      held 6000 refugees from Northern Ireland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan91_51-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan91-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_effects">
       Long-term effects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Long-term effects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="1024" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg/250px-Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg/375px-Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg/500px-Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Belfast_peaceline_Bombay_Street.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The modern "
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_line" title="Peace line">
         peace line
        </a>
        " at Bombay Street in Belfast, seen from the Irish Catholic/nationalist side. This is the view from the back of a house.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The August riots were the most sustained violence that Northern Ireland had seen since the early 1920s. Many Protestants, loyalists and unionists believed the violence showed the true face of the Northern Ireland Catholic civil rights movement  as a front for the IRA and armed insurrection. They had mixed feelings regarding the deployment of British Army troops into Northern Ireland. Eddie Kinner, a resident of Dover Street who would later join the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force_(1966)" title="Ulster Volunteer Force (1966)">
       Ulster Volunteer Force
      </a>
      (UVF), vividly recalled the troops marching down his street with fixed bayonets and steel helmets. He and his neighbours had felt at the time as if they were being invaded by their "own army".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Catholics and nationalists, on the other hand, saw the riots (particularly in Belfast) as an assault on their community by loyalists and the forces of the state. The disturbances, taken together with the Battle of the Bogside, are often cited as the beginning of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">
       the Troubles
      </a>
      . Violence escalated sharply in Northern Ireland after these events, with the formation of new paramilitary groups on either side, most notably the
      <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">
       Provisional Irish Republican Army
      </a>
      in December of that year. On the loyalist side, the UVF (formed in 1966) were galvanised by the August riots and in 1971, another paramilitary group, the
      <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Defence_Association" title="Ulster Defence Association">
       Ulster Defence Association
      </a>
      was founded out of a coalition of loyalist militants who had been active since August 1969. The largest of these were the
      <a href="/wiki/Woodvale_Defence_Association" title="Woodvale Defence Association">
       Woodvale Defence Association
      </a>
      , led by
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Harding_Smith" title="Charles Harding Smith">
       Charles Harding Smith
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Defence_Association" title="Shankill Defence Association">
       Shankill Defence Association
      </a>
      , led by
      <a href="/wiki/John_McKeague" title="John McKeague">
       John McKeague
      </a>
      , which had been responsible for what organisation there was of loyalist violence in the riots of August 1969. While the thousands of British Army troops sent to Northern Ireland were initially seen as a neutral force, they quickly got dragged into the street violence and by 1971 were devoting most of their attention to combatting republican paramilitaries.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Irish_Republican_Army">
       The Irish Republican Army
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: The Irish Republican Army">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The role of the IRA in the riots has long been disputed. At the time, the organisation was blamed by the Northern Ireland authorities for the violence. However, it was very badly prepared to defend nationalist areas of Belfast, having few weapons or fighters on the ground.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Scarman Inquiry, set up by the British government to investigate the causes of the riots, concluded:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Undoubtedly there was an IRA influence at work in the DCDA (
       <a href="/wiki/Derry_Citizens%27_Defence_Association" title="Derry Citizens' Defence Association">
        Derry Citizens' Defence Association
       </a>
       ) in Londonderry, in the Ardoyne and Falls Road areas of Belfast, and in Newry. But they did not start the riots, or plan them: indeed, the evidence is that the IRA was taken by surprise and did less than many of their supporters thought they should have done.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         17
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In nationalist areas, the IRA was reportedly blamed for having failed to protect areas like Bombay Street and Ardoyne from being burned out. A Catholic priest, Fr Gillespie, reported that in Ardoyne the IRA was being derided in graffiti as "I Ran Away".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, IRA veterans of the time, who spoke to authors Brian Hanley and Scott Millar disputed this interpretation. One, Sean O'Hare, said, "I never saw it written on a wall. That wasn't the attitude. People fell in behind the IRA, stood behind them 100%. Another, Sean Curry recalled, "some people were a bit angry but most praised the people who did defend the area. They knew that if the men weren't there, the area wouldn't have been defended."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the time, the IRA released a statement on 18 August, saying, it had been, "in action in Belfast and Derry" and "fully equipped units had been sent to the border". It had been, "reluctantly compelled into action by Orange murder gangs" and warned the British Army that if it, "was used to supress [
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
        sic
       </a>
      </i>
      ] the legitimate demands of the people they will have to take the consequences" and urged the Irish government to send the
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_Army" title="Irish Army">
       Irish Army
      </a>
      over the border.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cathal_Goulding" title="Cathal Goulding">
       Cathal Goulding
      </a>
      , the IRA Chief of Staff, sent small units from Dublin, Cork and Kerry to border counties of Donegal, Leitrim and Monaghan, with orders to attack RUC posts in Northern Ireland and draw off pressure from Belfast and Derry. A total of 96 weapons and 12,000 rounds of ammunition were also sent to the North.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nevertheless, the poor state of IRA arms and military capability in August 1969 led to a bitter split in the IRA in Belfast. According to Hanley and Millar, "dissensions that pre-dated August [1969] had been given a powerful emotional focus".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In September 1969, a group of IRA men led by
      <a href="/wiki/Billy_McKee" title="Billy McKee">
       Billy McKee
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Cahill" title="Joe Cahill">
       Joe Cahill
      </a>
      stated that they would no longer be taking orders from the Dublin leadership of the IRA, or from Billy McMillen (their commander in Belfast) because they had not provided enough weapons or planning to defend nationalist areas. In December 1969, they broke away to form the Provisional IRA and vowed to defend areas from attack by loyalists and the RUC. The other wing of the IRA became known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Official_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Official Irish Republican Army">
       Official IRA
      </a>
      . Shortly after its formation, the Provisional IRA launched an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA_campaign_1969-1997" title="Provisional IRA campaign 1969-1997">
       offensive campaign
      </a>
      against the state of Northern Ireland.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_RUC_and_USC">
       The RUC and USC
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=1969_Northern_Ireland_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The RUC and USC">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The actions of the RUC in the August 1969 riots are perhaps the most contentious issue arising out of the disturbances. Nationalists argue that the RUC acted in a blatantly biased manner, helping loyalists who were assaulting Catholic neighbourhoods. There were also strong suggestions that police knew when loyalist attacks were to happen and seemed to disappear from some Catholic areas shortly before loyalist mobs attacked.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan106_49-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan106-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This perception discredited the police in the eyes of many nationalists and later allowed the IRA to effectively take over policing in nationalist areas. In his study,
      <i>
       From Civil Rights to Armalites
      </i>
      , nationalist author Niall  Dochartaigh argues that the actions of the RUC and USC were the key factor in the worsening of the conflict. He wrote:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       From the outset, the response of the state and its forces of law and order to Catholic mobilisation was an issue capable of arousing far more anger and activism than the issues around which mobilisation had begun. Police behaviour and their interaction with loyalist protesters probably did more to politically mobilise large sections of the Catholic community than did any of the other grievances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         58
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The Scarman Inquiry found that the RUC were "seriously at fault" on at least six occasions during the rioting. Specifically, they criticised the RUC's use of Browning heavy machine-guns in built-up areas, their failure to stop Protestants from burning down Catholic homes, and their withdrawal from the streets long before the Army arrived. However, the Scarman Report concluded that, "Undoubtedly mistakes were made and certain individual officers acted wrongly on occasions. But the general case of a partisan force co-operating with Protestant crowds to attack Catholic people is devoid of substance, and we reject it utterly".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The report argued that the RUC were under-strength, poorly led and that their conduct in the riots was explained by their perception that they were dealing with a co-ordinated IRA uprising. They pointed to the RUC's dispersal of loyalist rioters in Belfast on 24 August in support of the force's impartiality.
     </p>
     <p>
      Of the B-Specials (Ulster Special Constabulary or USC), the Scarman Report said:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       There were grave objections, well understood by those in authority, to the use of the USC in communal disturbances. In 1969 the USC contained no Catholics but was a force drawn from the Protestant section of the community. Totally distrusted by the Catholics, who saw them as the strong arm of the Protestant ascendancy, they could not show themselves in a Catholic area without heightening tension. Moreover they were neither trained nor equipped for riot control duty.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         17
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The report found that the Specials had fired on Catholic demonstrators in Dungiven, Coalisland, Dungannon and Armagh, causing casualties, which, "was a reckless and irresponsible thing to do". It found that USC officers had, on occasion, sided with loyalists mobs. There were reports that USC officers were spotted hiding among loyalist mobs, using coats to hide their uniforms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coogan106_49-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-coogan106-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nevertheless, the Scarman Report concluded, "there are no grounds for singling out mobilised USC as being guilty of misconduct".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scarman_17-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-scarman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
