           <h1>
            Barack Obama's visit: PM Narendra Modi keen to embed US in his India dream
           </h1>
          </span>
         </div>
         <span class="byline" style="padding:10px 0px;display:inline-block;">

          <div class="section1">
           <div class="Normal">
            NEW DELHI: When Barack Obama touches down on Sunday, the air will be thick with talk of "deliverables"  whether there'll be success on nuclear talks, climate change, defence or investment.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Lost in the din will be the visit's powerful message. Our Republic Day celebrations are unique and, every year, one nation is invited to be part of the ceremony, a gesture of friendship. Given the prickly history of Indo-US ties, no amount of talk about being "natural allies" could breach the hurdle, until
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Narendra-Modi">
             Narendra Modi
            </a>
            sprang the invite on Obama.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <strong>
             READ ALSO:
             <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/india/US-President-Barack-Obamas-Indias-itinerary/articleshow/45978914.cms">
              US President Barack Obama's India's itinerary
             </a>
            </strong>
            <br/>
            <div id="gads">
             <iframe align="center" allowtransparency="true" frameborder="0" height="190" hspace="0" id="adshowbtm" marginheight="0" marginwidth="0" name="adshowbtm" scrolling="no" src="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/configspace/ads/PM_article_middle.html" title="Advertisement" vspace="0" width="660">
             </iframe>
            </div>
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/india/Barack-Obamas-visit-Can-India-US-defence-ties-go-ballistic-with-new-deal/articleshow/45997492.cms">
             <strong>
              Can India-US defence ties go ballistic with new deal?
             </strong>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/india/Ahead-of-Barack-Obama-visit-US-and-India-revive-talks-on-controversial-military-pacts/articleshow/45997508.cms">
             <strong>
              US and India revive talks on controversial military pacts
             </strong>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <a class="inlineshare" href="#">
             What does Modi want to achieve from the visit? "He wants to reflect officially the favourable sentiment Indians have of the US," said an aide. He wants to "embed" the US in his grand India project.
            </a>
            That it will be an "event" isn't in doubt. Expect the unexpected  a joint radio conversation going beyond protocol, etc. It could be the "coming out" moment of Indo-US ties, taking natural convergences to the next level.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            The two sides will fix some knotty problems in ties. Using fulsome language, a normally prosaic MEA described the visit as a "culmination of a qualitative reinvigoration of ties following the meeting between PM Modi and President Obama in September."
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img src="/photo/45998053.cms" style=""/>
            <br/>
            <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/nri/us-canada-news/Indian-Americans-in-time-of-Bharat-Ombaba/articleshow/45997579.cms">
             <strong>
              Indian-Americans in time of 'Bharat Ombaba'
             </strong>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            The key "deliverable" will be a nuclear agreement. There are three components to the Indo-US nuclear tangle - administrative arrangements that'll operationalize the deal, overcoming difficulties arising from India's liability law, and facilitating Indian membership to the four global nonproliferation regimes.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            While the liability issue is unlikely to be solved, both sides are looking at "forward-looking language" that could lend the law respectability, if the government ratifies the Convention for Supplementary Compensation (CSC) for nuclear damage. India signed this convention in 2010, but refrained from ratification because of concern that our laws are incompatible with CSC. Washington announced in September that India had completed preparations for NSG and MTCR membership. USA will have to do more heavy lifting to get India into these groups against Chinese opposition.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            On defence, apart from renewing the 2005 framework pact, they might start a conversation on three 'foundational' agreements of CISMOA, LSA and BECA (acronyms for deals that allow greater sharing of technology, bases and information)  that could take security ties beyond the 50 joint exercises they hold annually. With Modi opening up FDI in defence, India will look for more "co-development" initiatives.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img src="/photo/45998054.cms" style=""/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Energy will occupy a chunk of the discussions, which will include renewable and fossil fuels. The idea is to ensure that by the time India limbers up for the Paris conference, it can make an emission cut commitment.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Counter-terror talks will continue to move up the value ladder. Obama has made positive noises against "safe havens" for terrorists, pressured Pakistan to freeze JUD and Haqqani Network assets. The real conversation here (possibly in the restricted session) will be on the future of Pakistan and Afghanistan. Expect resurrection of the trilateral dialogue with Afghanistan. Rajnath Singh will probably travel to the US for his first counter-terror dialogue with counterpart Jeh Johnson.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <img src="/photo/45998056.cms" style=""/>
            <br/>
            <img src="/photo/45998057.cms" style=""/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            The two countries will pull separately on strategic vision. The US, as Ashley Tellis of Carnegie says, is a "hegemonic superpower". India wants to build itself a bigger space at the high table. Part of the discussions should feature ways to deal with the differences  India will probably not vote with US on many issues in the UN, differ with it on Iran and Russia. But India has a strong case of reminding America that it took the right call on Libya, Myanmar and shouldn't be dismissed on Iran.
