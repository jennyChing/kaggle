      <p>
       <img alt="1Untitled1222" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-28449" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/www.tatoott1009.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/1Untitled1222.jpg?resize=670%2C379"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Billings police officer Grant Morrison testifies during a coroners inquest on Wednesday, Jan. 7, 2015 in Billings, Montana. Morrison testified about the events leading up to his shooting of unarmed man, 38-year-old Richard Ramirez. A seven-person jury on Wednesday determined Morrisons actions were justified. (AP Photo/Matthew Brown)
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       BILLINGS, Mont. (AP)  A jury at a coroners inquest determined Wednesday that a Montana police officer was justified in shooting and killing an unarmed man high on methamphetamine during a traffic stop.
       <br/>
       The ruling came after Billings Police Officer Grant Morrison testified he feared for his life when he fired the three shots that killed 38-year-old Richard Ramirez.
      </p>
      <p>
       The five-year police veteran said he became convinced that Ramirez had a gun after the man reached for his waistband during their 30-second encounter last April in a high-crime area of Montanas most populous city.
      </p>
      <p>
       I knew in that moment, which later was determined to be untrue, but I knew in that moment that he was reaching for a gun, Morrison said. I couldnt take that risk.  I wanted to see my son grow up.
      </p>
      <p>
       The seven-person jury deliberated about an hour before delivering its decision.
      </p>
      <p>
       Yellowstone County Attorney Scott Twito said he does not expect to file any charges given the jurys decision.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="2a5ce9ad20bc81016a0f6a706700ec22" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-28450" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/www.tatoott1009.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/2a5ce9ad20bc81016a0f6a706700ec22.jpg?resize=670%2C455"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Julie Ramirez, left, consoles her mother, Betty Ramirez during a court inquest into the police shooting of Richard Ramirez in Billings, Mont. on Tuesday, Jan. 6, 2015. The unarmed man killed by Billings Police Officer Grant Morrison during a traffic stop was told repeatedly to raise his hands before Morrison shot him three times, according to video footage. The jury will decide if the shooting was justified as part of a mandatory inquest into the shooting. (AP Photo/Matthew Brown)
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       Coroners inquests are mandatory under Montana law whenever someone is killed by law enforcement or dies in custody.
      </p>
      <p>
       The inquest was held as police killings of unarmed suspects in Ferguson, Missouri and New York City have heightened scrutiny of law enforcement nationwide.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ramirez family members said they were disappointed by the ruling and intend to file a lawsuit against Morrison and the Billings Police Department alleging excessive use of force, said Julie Ramirez, a sister of Richard Ramirez.
      </p>
      <p>
       Billings Police Chief Rich St. John said it was the fifth officer-involved shooting in his eight years as head of the department. Each shooting was ruled to be justified, he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       That tells us were doing the right thing in the right way, St. John said.
       <br/>
       Police video showed Morrison repeatedly ordered Ramirez and other occupants of the vehicle to raise their hands. Ramirezs actions were largely obscured in the video. But Morrison said Ramirez dropped his left hand to his side  out of the officers view  and started to jiggle it up and down just before he was shot.
      </p>
      <p>
       Morrison shot and killed another man in 2013. He was cleared of any wrongdoing in that case.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ramirezs family wanted criminal charges against the officer and said Ramirez was a victim of racial profiling.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another sister, Renee Ramirez, criticized the inquest as one-sided. She said testimony that her brother was a drug user was irrelevant.
      </p>
      <p>
       All but three of the 15 people called to testify during the two-day inquest were from law enforcement. Several police officers spoke at length about their prior dealings with Ramirez and others in his family.
       <br/>
       I dont care what things my brother did in the past, Renee Ramirez said. What does that have to do with shooting my brother?
      </p>
      <p>
       Billings Police Detective Brad Tucker, who investigated the case, testified Tuesday that Ramirez might have been trying to stash something when he was shot. A small amount of methamphetamine and a syringe were later found near Ramirezs seat.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="387374ad255797016a0f6a706700ef9d" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-28451" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/www.tatoott1009.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/387374ad255797016a0f6a706700ef9d.jpg?resize=670%2C413"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Billings Police Chief Rich St. John explains his departments use-of-force procedures on Wednesday, Jan. 7, 2015 during a coroners inquest into the shooting death of an unarmed Montana man by a Montana police officer during a traffic stop. A jury on Wednesday determined the shooting by Officer Grant Morrison was justified. (AP Photo/Matthew Brown)
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       An autopsy determined Ramirez had enough methamphetamine in his bloodstream at the time of the shooting to kill a person not accustomed to the drug, forensic pathologist Tom Bennett testified.
      </p>
      <p>
       Twito defended the proceedings as a fair presentation of the facts.
      </p>
      <p>
       The videos speak for themselves, he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Morrison was placed on paid administrative leave immediately after the shooting and has since been assigned to a task force investigating prescription drug crimes, St. John said.
      </p>
