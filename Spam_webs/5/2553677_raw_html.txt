                 </h2>
                 <div class="date-posts">
                  <div class="post-outer">
                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://searchenginewatch.com/IMG/337/314337/emarketer-adspend-formatdevice.jpg?1427488635" itemprop="image_url"/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.my15minutes.in/2015/03/5-trends-to-watch-in-mobile-first.html">
                      5 Trends to Watch in the Mobile-First Search Era
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-605395622792949570" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on">
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        New figures from
                        <em style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased;">
                         eMarketer
                        </em>
                        reflect the growing mobile momentum with estimates that mobile advertising spend will be double that of desktop by 2017, and by 2019, mobile will account for 72 percent of total digital ad spend.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <img alt="emarketer-adspend-formatdevice" border="0" class="center" height="640" src="http://searchenginewatch.com/IMG/337/314337/emarketer-adspend-formatdevice.jpg?1427488635" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; border: 0px; clear: right; display: block; margin: 1em auto; text-align: justify;" title="emarketer-adspend-formatdevice" width="521"/>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        This should come as no surprise as mobile is topping list of where consumers are spending their time. Late last year, comScore reported that 60 percent of time online is spent on mobile, the majority of activity happening within apps.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Shifting audience behavior is dictating a refocusing of search strategy. Early this year, Iwrotethat 2015 should be considered "the year of the consumer," and as it stands, the modern consumer demands a mobile-first strategy.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        In this forward-thinking mobile world, lets look ahead to the five key mobile trends to watch.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        1. Googles Emphasis on Mobile Friendliness
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Mobile search rankings are about to get a bit friendlier. On April 21, Google will be expanding its ranking signals to account for
                        <a href="http://searchenginewatch.com/sew/news/2397418/google-adds-mobile-friendliness-and-indexed-apps-as-ranking-factors" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;">
                         mobile friendliness
                        </a>
                        . While theres been no indication of responsive design being valued over mobile-specific sites, its clear that for marketers who have neither, the time is nigh to make a move. Weigh the
                        <a href="http://searchenginewatch.com/sew/how-to/2398591/-mobilegeddon-is-coming-on-april-21-are-you-ready" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;">
                         pros and cons
                        </a>
                        of your mobile-friendliness approach  and play nice, as Google says this change will have a "significant impact" on search results.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        2. Bings Mobile Moves
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Starting March 23, Bing ads rolled out an upgrade to
                        <a href="http://advertise.bingads.microsoft.com/en-us/blogpost/129509/bing-ads-blog/changes-to-targeting-mobile-devices-coming-to-bing-ads-in-march-2015" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;" target="_blank">
                         Unified Device Targeting
                        </a>
                        . Just like Googles shift to Enhanced Campaigns, this Bing update will mean campaigns will target all devices by default. If theres any lesson we learned the first time around its to stay active in monitoring and optimizing your bid adjustments accordingly.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Beyond device targeting, Bing has also indicated the forthcoming introduction of
                        <a href="http://advertise.bingads.microsoft.com/en-us/blogpost/129509/bing-ads-blog/changes-to-targeting-mobile-devices-coming-to-bing-ads-in-march-2015" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;" target="_blank">
                         mobile app extensions
                        </a>
                        to help marketers drive further discovery and installs in the vast app marketplace.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <img alt="bing-app-extensions" border="0" class="center" src="http://searchenginewatch.com/IMG/336/314336/bing-app-extensions.png?1427488447" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; border: 0px; clear: right; display: block; margin: 1em auto; text-align: justify;" title="bing-app-extensions" width="100%"/>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        3. The App Explosion
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Speaking of apps, discovery is only one part of the game. To really benefit, you must have the tools in place to measure and track beyond the install. This is how youll be able to grow customer lifetime value in the mobile era.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        The sheer number of available apps is creating fragmentation in the market, even when it comes to search activity. Todays mobile consumer seeks speed and convenience and specialty apps that cater to specific needs (think Yelp) are the thing to watch. Yahoo is also trying to capitalize off in-app activity by offering its
                        <a href="https://developer.yahoo.com/search-sdk/" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;" target="_blank">
                         own SDK
                        </a>
                        to integrate Yahoo Search within apps.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        4. The Native Play
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        The mobile format, with its infinite scroll, is highly conducive for a seamless integration of in-stream advertising. Not only does native represent a contextually friendly and engaging experience but it also seems to sparkle with inventory possibilities in an otherwise limited mobile search-scape.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        As mobile and native merge, look no further than Yahoo Gemini, a one-stop shop for mobile search and native advertising.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        5. The Intrinsic Connection of Mobile and Local
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        We cant talk about mobile-first without mentioning the implication for local search as well. Recent research from theLocal Search Associationfound that the majority of U.S. adults turn to mobile when searching for locally focused information.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        When thinking local-mobile, of course there are the relevant extensions  location and click to call  but its more than just that. There are designated call-only campaigns, local inventory on Google Shopping, and opportunities withapps. These capabilities and opportunities are growing and strengthening.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Having a mobile presence can no longer be seen as a competitive advantage for an advertiser but rather serve as a marketing staple. Now more than ever, it pays to be an agile marketer who can embrace the mobile mindset.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        (
                        <a href="http://searchenginewatch.com/sew/how-to/2401910/5-trends-to-watch-in-the-mobile-first-search-era" target="_blank">
                         via

                        )
                       </span>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.my15minutes.in/2015/03/5-ways-of-rethinking-your-referral.html">
                      5 Ways of Rethinking Your Referral Traffic
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8777633848942854961" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on">
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        So you have a site, now what? Well, one of the first things you need to do is drive traffic to it, but how? What if you have a site with traffic, what can you do to better drive users to your pages?
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <img alt="104044-strip" border="0" class="center" src="http://searchenginewatch.com/IMG/233/313233/104044-strip.png?1426264213" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; border: 0px; clear: right; display: block; margin: 1em auto;" title="104044-strip"/>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        In the past we could rely on Google organic and paid to generate most of our site traffic, but not anymore. While organic is still your highest-quality traffic, what if it goes away overnight? And while paid is helpful in generating specific conversions around keyword searches, in the end you are often just renting those visitors. So if you want to stay relevant today, we need to rethink our concepts of referral traffic as it looks much different today than it did even one or two years ago.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Here are five tips on creating a more perfect referral traffic profile and avoiding mistakes that could cause you pain.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        1. Reliance on Social Media
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Often we see sites that get a lot of their traffic from social media. If your site is one of those sites, congratulations - that is not the easiest code to crack. You have done good things.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Social traffic is a positive signal. It means your site is being shared and talked about. It means your site has relevancy to your user base. However, it is imperfect traffic and a strategy that is unsustainable on its own. Why is this?
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        First, if you are relying too heavily on your social media presence for traffic and the site you are getting that traffic from decides to change a factor that takes away that traffic, you have little recourse. There is really nothing you can do. Well, you can pay, but that requires more money and time, which following a loss of large amounts of traffic, you might now have to invest.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Second, unlike organic visitors, social media users are typically "one and done." They see something they like, they head in, they read, they head out.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        While this can be an excellent method for long-term brand building, it is not your best traffic source for in-depth or lengthy visitor engagement. Some will like what they see and come back, but not as many when they find you by other efforts.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        So while there is nothing wrong with this as a part of your comprehensive plan, you want to be careful of relying on social media at the expense of more relevant and engaged traffic such as organic search. In addition, if these sources suffer declines, you can more readily address the issues on other channels than you can with social.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Mostly, just make sure you are not ignoring other avenues for bringing users in to the site.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        2. Reliance on Google Organic
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Just as you can be overly reliant on social media, you can also be too reliant on Google organic. While organic search is your best traffic referrer in terms of engagement, site stickiness, and returning visits, if you accidentally trigger an algorithm negatively and it takes a slap at your site, well you might wake up with a cliff dive on your analytics and a sunken feeling in your stomach.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        While it is very important to spend a lot of your time cultivating your organic visits, it is not a safe bet to rely on Google for that traffic alone. The most important thing you can do is diversify your traffic and find multiple generative and reliable sources.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        3. Reliance on Google and Facebook
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Many sites rely on these two sources are their primary traffic-drivers. Be careful if these are the only two. Dont rely so much on Facebook or organic SEO that you forget to continue to diversify your traffic profile. Your traffic referrals are a delicate balance of visits from paid, organic, social, syndication, etc.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        The most important thing you can do in this day of Google is to make sure you have built up as many positive traffic sources as make sense for your business. This way if you lose in Google or Facebook today, you can start cranking up the traffic in other areas tomorrow.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        4. Organic Is Still King
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Organic traffic is not just Google, though we typically think of that first. It can also be Bing, Duck Duck Go, and Yahoo, or even a contest or event. Whatever your organic source, the users are likely to be more engaged and user intent is typically going to be more aligned with your site content. While Google organic should and will be the largest organic referrer, building up your alternative referral sources will help protect you from getting hit in one area or another.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        5. Watch Out for the Bots
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Semalt, Buttons-For-Websites, and other bots visit your site and show up in your analytics. These referrals are not real humans, but bots meant to send traffic back to its owner by you clicking on the referrals in your analytics. There is no reason to support this traffic and there is no reason to not get rid of it. There are several methods for doing this, however Jon Henshaw of Raven Tools has the mostcomprehensive postexplaining the issue and how to get rid of it.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; color: #444444; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px;">

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.my15minutes.in/2015/03/3-signs-your-seo-campaign-is-dying-and.html">
                      3 Signs Your SEO Campaign Is Dying  and How to Fix It
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4583129337126872640" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on">
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        SEO is a long-term strategy to gain more visibility and more traffic for your online brand, but accurately measuring the effectiveness of that strategy can be challenging. Many of the benefits of SEO, such as increased brand visibility, are qualitative and therefore hard to measure, and even objective metrics like inbound traffic can be subject to random variation and become difficult to interpret.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        After youve maintained an SEO strategy for more than a few months, however, you should have enough information to form meaningful conclusions from your data. Most importantly, youll be able to check in regularly to see if there are any red flags that signal something is wrong with your campaign. These red flags can be hard to spot initially, but if you know what to look for, you should have no problem detecting them and taking corrective action immediately.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        1. A Sharp Drop in Organic Traffic
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Organic traffic is a metric referring to the number of people who found your site through search engines, and its one of the best metrics we have to measure the effectiveness of an SEO campaign. To measure your organic traffic, log into Google Analytics and click on "Acquisitions." Here, youll see a breakdown of how many visits your site received from organic sources, referrals, social media, and direct visits.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        You can click into the organic visits to gain some extra details on the sources of your organic traffic, but the main number is the one were most concerned with. Keep an eye on it on a monthly  if not weekly  basis. While you should be seeing some long-term growth patterns, what you really want to look for is any sharp drop. For example, if youre used to seeing 1,000 hits a week and that inexplicably drops to 200, you might have a major problem on your hands.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        2. Stagnant Growth or Decline for More Than Two Months
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Like I mentioned, the goal here is to see long-term growth. If you check back on a consistent basis, month after month, you should see an overall pattern toward increased traffic. That being said, organic search behavior is anything but predictable, and random factors beyond your or Googles control could artificially leave you with a tough month or a brief stagnation. If you notice one month in particular doesnt result in growth, dont panic.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        However, if your campaign
                        <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jaysondemers/2015/02/17/5-tactics-to-refresh-a-stale-seo-campaign/" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;" target="_blank">
                         remains stagnant for two months or more
                        </a>
                        , you might have a serious problem. What you want to see is steady, measurable growth in organic visits  if youre hovering around the same figures or if you start to see a decline, consider it a red flag. There are instances where multiple months of consistent traffic dont necessarily mean your SEO campaign is failing, but you dont want to take the gamble by keeping things the same for another month.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        3. A Sharp Drop in Keyword Rankings
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Keyword rankings arent nearly as important as they used to be. There was a time when keywords meant everything to SEO, and getting one to rank highly meant you had found success. Today,
                        <a href="http://searchenginewatch.com/sew/how-to/2390744/are-keywords-relevant-to-seo-in-2015" style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; -webkit-transition-duration: 0.25s; text-decoration: none; transition-duration: 0.25s;">
                         keywords are less pivotal
                        </a>
                        ; since Google deciphers user queries based on intent, rather than keyword phrases, your ranks are much more fluid. Its far more important to have relevant, quality content than it is to have content based around certain keywords.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        However, your rankings are still a valuable metric to measure because they can indicate the health of your campaign. Keep a handful of keywords as your targets to measure, and check your ranking on them every once in a while (once a month for most campaigns, or once a week for more aggressive campaigns). If you notice your rankings falling on a majority of those keywords, this is a red flag for your campaign.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h2 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; font-stretch: normal; line-height: 1.25em; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.4em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        Tracking Down the Culprit
                       </span>
                      </h2>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Lets imagine that youve found one of these red flags. What does that mean for your campaign? It means something is off in your strategy, and its interfering with your ability to increase your search visibility. It can be difficult to track down the exact cause of this downturn, especially since it could be multiple factors working together, but its important to identify the source.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h3 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.8em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        Over-Optimized Content
                       </span>
                      </h3>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Are you writing your content with one or a handful of keywords at the forefront? Are you recycling content or using old topics just so you can push more content on your site? If so, you could be over-optimizing, which could be leading to lower ranks. Write fresh, original content on new topics on a regular basis instead.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h3 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.8em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        Poor Link-Building Strategy
                       </span>
                      </h3>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Bad link-building tactics are the most common culprit of poor SEO campaign performance. Googles Penguin algorithm update was launched to penalize such schemes as buying links, building too many links, and building links on irrelevant or low-authority websites. Instead of participating in schemes to manipulate your way up the rankings, try to earn your links through publishing high-quality content and relationship building.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h3 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.8em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        Bad or Missing Reviews
                       </span>
                      </h3>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Todays local search incorporates information from your business all over the Web. If your information is missing from important local directory sites (like Yelp or TripAdvisor), or if you seem to be getting lots of bad reviews, your ranks and search visibility could tank.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <h3 style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; margin: 1.2em 0px 0.8em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: small;">
                        Missing Peripheral Elements
                       </span>
                      </h3>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Neglecting another important tactic or platform in your SEO campaign can also lead to poor performance. For example, if you dont use social media or if you arent incorporating guest blogging, your results could suffer.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
                        Once youve discovered a red flag that your SEO campaign is in trouble, its important to take corrective action as soon as possible. Identifying the source is only the first step of the process; youll have to work for days or weeks to repair the damage left in the wake of your flawed strategy or one-time mistake. The best course of action is to maintain a solid, high-quality SEO strategy to begin with; as long as youre adhering to Googles best practices and working with user experience as your main priority, you shouldnt ever expect to see one of these red flags.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div style="-webkit-font-smoothing: antialiased; background-color: white; line-height: 1.5; margin-bottom: 0.9em; padding: 0px; text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;">
