             3.1 Introduction:
            </span>
           </div>
           <div class="ff3" style="font-size:97px">
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:1381px;word-spacing:2px">
             Born with the will of the victors of the First World War to avoid a repeat of a devastating war, the League
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:1528px;word-spacing:6px">
             of Nations represents an important milestone in the direction of achieving the age old global community
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:1674px;word-spacing:18px">
             aspiration of a global body. It was the first significant institution with a clear objective to maintain
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:1820px;word-spacing:-2px">
             universal peace within the framework of the fundamental principles of the Pact accepted by its
             <span class="w7">
             </span>
             Members: to
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:1966px;word-spacing:-2px">
             develop cooperation among nations and to guarantee them peace
             <span class="w6">
             </span>
             and security. The
             <span class="w6">
             </span>
             Leagues goals included
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2113px;word-spacing:20px">
             disarmament, preventing war through collective security, settling disputes between countries through
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2259px;word-spacing:30px;letter-spacing:-1px">
             negotiation diplomacy and improving global welfare. The diplomatic philosophy behind the League
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2405px;word-spacing:7px">
             represented a fundamental shift in thought from the preceding hundred years. After a number of notable
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2552px;word-spacing:14px">
             successes and some early failures in the 1920s, the League ultimately proved incapable of preventing
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2699px;word-spacing:-1px">
             aggression by the Axis Powers in the 1930s. The onset of
             <span class="w7">
             </span>
             the Second World War suggested that the League
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2844px;word-spacing:-2px">
             had failed in its primary purpose 
             <span class="w6">
             </span>
             to avoid any future world war.
             <span class="w6">
             </span>
             In spite of its
             <span class="w6">
             </span>
             political failure, the legacy
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:2992px;word-spacing:4px">
             of the League of Nations at the same time appears clearly in a number of principles stated by the Charter
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:3137px;word-spacing:-1px">
             and in the competencies and experiences developed in the area

             </span>
             of technical cooperation: the majority of the
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:3284px;word-spacing:12px">

             initiated by the League of
             <span class="w7">
             </span>
             Nations.

            </span>
           </div>
           <div class="ff3" style="font-size:97px">
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:3803px;word-spacing:1px">

            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:3949px;word-spacing:14px">
             and prevent future wars. Initially the effort was confined only to the private initiative which later got
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:4096px;word-spacing:10px">
             received the official support. For example, the League to Enforce Peace founded in 1915 in the United
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:4242px">
             States (US), former President William Howard Taft as the important leader of this movement. A year later,
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:4389px">
             President Woodrow Wilson addressed a meeting of the League to Enforce Peace, and gradually he came to
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:4535px;word-spacing:11px">
             endorse many of its principles. By January 1917, Wilson, in an address to the US Senate, advocated a
            </span>
            <span class="a" style="left:424px;top:4682px;word-spacing:-2px">
             League for Peace backed by superior collective force.

            </span>
