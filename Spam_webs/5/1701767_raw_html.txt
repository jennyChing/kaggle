       <div class="entry">
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6811" height="332" src="http://brooklynbrewshop.com/mashfiles/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/Spent-Grain-Flour1.jpg" title="Spent Grain Flour" width="590"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         You may have realized by now that we really like spent grain. Brewing everyday means we're always thinking of new things to do with our favorite leftover (see:
         <a href="http://brooklynbrewshop.com/themash/category/spentgrainchef/" target="_blank" title="Spent Grain Chef Recipes">
          Spent Grain Chef
         </a>
         ). Whether you're making
         <a href="http://brooklynbrewshop.com/themash/spent-grain-waffles/" target="_blank" title="Recipe: Spent Grain Waffles">
          waffles
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://brooklynbrewshop.com/themash/recipe-spent-grain-cheddar-crackers/" target="_blank" title="Recipe: Spent Grain Cheddar Crackers">
          cheddar crackers
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://brooklynbrewshop.com/themash/recipe-spent-grain-pretzel-rolls/" target="_blank" title="Recipe: Spent Grain Pretzel Rolls">
          pretzel rolls
         </a>
         , or
         <a href="http://brooklynbrewshop.com/themash/recipe-spent-grain-pasta/" target="_blank" title="Recipe: Spent Grain Pasta">
          handmade pasta
         </a>
         , spent grain flour can add a complex warmth and nuttiness to some of your tried and true dishes. Here's how to make it.
        </p>
        <p>
         <!--more-->
        </p>
        <ol>
         <h3>
          What You Do
         </h3>
         <li>
          Set your oven to the lowest setting possible. For most ovens this is 170-200 degrees Fahrenheit.
         </li>
         <li>
          Spread out your spent grain on a clean, ungreased sheet pan in a thin 1/4 inch layer.
         </li>
         <li>
          Place in oven and let dry for approximately 7 hours. Alternately, a food dehydrator works as well.
         </li>
         <li>
          4 hours into drying, pull out barley and toss to mix with a spoon.
         </li>
         <li>
          Drying time may vary depending on your environment or oven. Your barley is dry when you feel absolutely no moisture remaining.
         </li>
         <li>
          Using a coffee or spice grinder, process dried spent grain until it becomes a fine flour.
         </li>
         <li>
          Store your spent grain flour in an airtight jar or plastic container in your pantry.
         </li>
        </ol>

          <p class="entry">
           James: You want to go pretty fine. Put your dried grain through coarsely (think french press) at first then run it through again at a finer setting (closer to espresso). You want to be careful with a burr grinder because it likely has coffee oils on the burrs that you may not want to coat with flour, so if you're super into your coffee, you may want to tread lightly. We use a cheaper blade grinder and keep our burr grinder reserved for just the beans, but a burr grinder will work really well for spent grain flour.

          <p class="entry">
           Great questions, Summer and Sabrina! The grains you use have different malt profiles, so the flavor of your flour will change depending on what grain you use. If you use the grains from a darker beer, say a porter or stout, you'll get a more roasted flavor in your grain; if you use the grains from a lighter beer, like a wheat, the taste will be more nutty and less roast-y. Theres no right or wrong spent grain to use for the flour, it just depends on what kind of recipe youll be using the flour for.
