           <p>
            <strong>
             On the bottom of exposed lakebeds, you can take a walk through the past as water levels continue to drop at reservoirs across Northern California.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            Even with the jet stream delivering the first drencher storm of winter this weekend, many lakes are the lowest right now since the drought of 1977. As the year evolves and water is sent downstream from the reservoirs, many lakes will likely fall to epic lows. That will unveil treasures of the past long forgotten by many.
           </p>
           <p>
            You never quite know what might turn up. When Butt Lake, located downstream of Lake Almanor in Plumas County, was largely drained in 1996 so crews could reinforce the dam, they found a narrow-gauge, steam-powered locomotive from the early 1900s sitting in the exposed silt.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Old railroad lines, tunnels, bridges, towns, roads, building foundations, Indian villages and grinding mortars are now being unveiled.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            In some areas, artifacts, such as arrowheads, are emerging as the lake levels expose lakebed for the first time in more than 35 years; turn them over to local agencies. If you find a historic settlement from the gold rush period, long buried by water, take a metal detector to look for vintage coins from the 1850s (note that metal detectors are banned at state parks.)
           </p>
           <p>
            Sunday Outdoors details finds last week at Almaden Reservoir in San Jose, Folsom Lake near Sacramento, Shasta Lake north of Redding and Lake McClure near Merced.
           </p>
