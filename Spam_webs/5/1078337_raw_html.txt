         <h1 class="entry-title">
          The business of social
         </h1>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         As any parent or teacher will know, exam season is fast-approaching; that eerie time of quiet while cohorts of young people undergo the rites of passage that lead them to the next stage of life.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         And when the exams have done their worst (or best), the young people move forward into new settings, facing new challenges and establishing new social networks.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         For A-Level students heading into further education, this means one thing: Freshers Week. Its the first item on the agenda for any self-respecting university, offering bright-eyed first years a mind-boggling array of clubs and societies to join, reflecting their interests and hobbies; in sociological terms, it presents a range of groups to which they could belong.
         <span id="more-1183">
         </span>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Leaving the world of university, its a different kettle of fish. New graduates leave the protective bubble of education for a job that may (or may not) come with a desk of their own, possibly a swipe card on a lanyard and, if theyre lucky, someone in the department charged with showing them round on their first day.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Of course, they know they belong to the organisation as a whole, but its rare that they would have any immediate membership of any subgroups. This can be a demotivating position to be in and can often inhibit the quality of their work. After all, collaboration is the backbone of strong thought and, if youre not sure who you can reach out to, youre left to tough it out alone.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         The next generation, too, are even more ingrained in this digital culture, growing up with multiplayer games like Minecraft. There are no levels and no rules; the Minecraft world can be populated by anyone. Players work together to build whole worlds, and compete to establish a rich online life. Collaboration is the key to success  in a world without rules, the only way to work out how to achieve is to reach out and ask others.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         The new generation of workers joining organisations will have grown up surrounded by groups that have been fostered and created by social media; their instinct is to seek out a sense of belonging and, if this isnt catered for within an organisation, they are forced to look elsewhere.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         The fact is, we spend the majority of our week at work and, we crave a feeling of belonging that goes beyond our job role. Mvine for Private Business Networks can cater for these different needs, giving you as an employer a tool that not only fosters collaboration and teamwork, but also encourages camaraderie and friendship.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         It means people can set up groups to share and discuss their common interest, hobby or expertise securely within your organisation. It offers your people a safe place to congregate, collaborate and communicate, making sure that private conversations stay that way.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Business and pleasure, work tasks and personal interests, innovation and collaboration all coincide to create a secure community of belonging that can only benefit your people and your organisation.
         <br/>

          Take a look at how we helped BT transform and protect their People Networks.
         </a>
