          Haiti's slow rise from the ashes
         </h1>

         <p>
          On Sunday, Haitian leaders crafted an 11th-hour deal that, if approved, would sidestep another round of one-man rule. It would revive long-stalled elections and allow Haitians to focus again on rising from the ashes of the earthquake that devastated the island nation five years ago Monday.
         </p>
         <aside class="trb_article_yieldmo" data-load-method="trb.vendor.yieldmo.init" data-load-type="method" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-screen-size="mobile" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000">

         <div class="trb_gptAd trb_articleAd" data-adloader-adtype="sidebarbanner" data-adloader-position="articleBanner_3" data-adloader-size="320x50" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" id="">
         </div>
         <p>
          For an assessment of Haiti's recovery efforts, Sentinel Editorial writer Darryl E. Owens conducted an email interview with Andrew MacCalla, who manages recovery efforts in Haiti for Direct Relief, a health-focused humanitarian response organization. Full text is available at OrlandoSentinel.com/opinion.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Q:
          </b>
          What is Haiti's condition five years after the earthquake rocked the country?
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           A:
          </b>
          Still recovering, but not recovered may be a fair way to describe matters. It seems equally important to recognize both that significant progress has been made in many areas and also that severe challenges and difficulties remain across the board.
         </p>

         <p>
          While the rubble is mostly cleared and the tent cities are nearly vacated, millions of people remain as vulnerable to the next disaster as they were for the earthquake that devastated their lives five years ago.
         </p>
         <p>
          A number of health emergencies, such as outbreaks of cholera and chickungunya (a viral disease carried by infected mosquitoes) have had far more awful consequence because of the lack of infrastructure and resources to combat them. The broader circumstances of extremely high unemployment, too few opportunities for education, and political crises like the recent ouster of the country's prime minister are among the significant challenges that exist.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Q:
          </b>
          Have the past five years brought any good news?
         </p>
         <div class="trb_gptAd trb_articleAd_bottomBanner" data-adloader-adtype="cube" data-adloader-position="pageBottomCube" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" id="">
         </div>
         <p>
          <b>
           A:
          </b>
          Haiti has much to inspire optimism. Hundreds of schools have opened and, thanks to Partners in Health and others, new hospitals are operating at levels that surpass anything that existed prior to the earthquake.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Q:
          </b>
          What's the housing situation today?
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           A:
          </b>
          More than 90 percent of the 1.5 million people left homeless in Port-au-Prince by the earthquake have transitioned to more permanent housing. While stricter building codes have improved the structural safety of commercial structures since 2010, few single-family homes are built to such standards. This is particularly evident in rural areas, where roughly 70 percent of households live on less than $2 a day and struggle to meet their most basic needs.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Q:
          </b>
          How have disaster-relief donations been used?
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           A:
          </b>
          Amid promises of "never again" and "build back better," the global outpouring of commitments for Haiti exceeded $10 billion. Roughly 30 percent has been allocated so far, the U.S. Congress reported last year. An even smaller slice has gone to local Haitian groups involved in the response.
         </p>
         <p>
          Few would argue the response is beyond reproach; however, working with local Haitian groups these past years to strengthen the country's health system, I've been inspired far more than frustrated.
         </p>
         <p>
          It's important to remember just how soaring an aim it is to "build back better." The earthquake destroyed roughly 70 of the buildings in Port-au-Prince, a city with 1.5 times the inhabitants of Manhattan. For context, only last year did One World Trade Center reopen after the terror attacks 13 years ago. This is not to compare one tragedy with another, but to emphasize that five years is far too soon to concede failure or anything else. There's more work to do.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Q:
          </b>
          Are you optimistic for Haiti's future?
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           A:
          </b>
          Things are improving in Haiti. Within three years, hundreds of thousands of people who lost their homes have relocated to semi-permanent or permanent housing. Business parks have opened and jobs are being created. New, state-of-the-art hospitals are in operation. People with disabilities are getting better care than ever before by groups such as Healing Hands for Haiti. Cholera's mortality rate dropped from 10 percent to below 1 percent. My optimism is constantly renewed by the people of Haiti, who are working hard to improve conditions in their country.
         </p>
