                <div class="leading">
                 <div class="post-57731 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-editorial-in-the-circle-fresh-focus tag-corn tag-food tag-food-security tag-global-grain-stocks tag-rice tag-thanksgiving tag-water tag-wheat" id="post-57731">
                  <!-- Begin Title -->
                  <div class="article-rel-wrapper">
                   <div class="contentheading" style="font-size:20px;">
                    Thanksgiving: World Food Entree and Water News On The Side
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <!-- End Title -->
                  <!-- Begin Meta -->
                  <div class="article-info-surround">
                   <!-- Begin Date &amp; Time -->
                   <span class="createdate">
                    Thursday, 27 November 2014 11:22
                   </span>
                   <br/>
                   <!-- End Date &amp; Time -->
                  </div>
                  <!-- End Meta -->
                  <div class="main-content-post">
                   <div class="post-content">
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Sharing the table with a discussion of global grain supplies.
                     </em>
                     <span id="more-57731">
                     </span>
                    </p>
                    <div class="photoCenter">
                     <a data-lightboxplus="lightbox[57731]" href="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/facebook1.jpg" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/facebook1.jpg', '']);" title="Thanksgiving feast.">
                      <img alt="Thanksgiving turkey feast global food grain supply Aubrey Ann parker circle of Blue" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-57754" src="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/facebook1.jpg" title="Thanksgiving feast." width="590"/>
                     </a>
                     <div class="photoCredit">
                      Photos  Aubrey Ann Parker/ Circle of Blue
                     </div>
                     <div class="photoCaption">
                      How do global stocks of grains affect the price of your Thanksgiving feast?
                      <em>
                       Click image to enlarge.
                      </em>
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <p>
                     As Americans tuck into their Thanksgiving meals today, most probably wont be thinking of the record U.S. corn harvest expected this year. The harvest  and other sizeable harvests of staple grains like corn, rice, and wheat in major food-growing countries  will help boost global grain stocks to their highest levels in 15 years.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Thats something the whole world can be thankful for after a decade of tight food supplies.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Global stocks of major grains have been significantly lower this decade than those in the 1990s, and the difference between the amount of food that the world has stored and the amount that it annually uses has also declined. On top of that, food prices have more than doubled since 2000.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     As Ive been reporting since 2012,
                     <a href="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2012/world/global-perspectives-how-dry-weather-in-argentina-could-affect-chicken-prices-in-chicago/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2012/world/global-perspectives-how-dry-weather-in-argentina-could-affect-chicken-prices-in-chicago/', 'the connection between water, food stocks, and food prices']);" target="_blank">
                      the connection between water, food stocks, and food prices
                     </a>
                     is complicated, but generally water scarcity is expected to constrain the amount of food the world will be able to produce in the future. Extreme weather events like droughts and floods can also act as shocks to global food markets, increasing food-price volatility. This was the case during severe droughts in places like the
                     <a href="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2013/world/global-grain-reserves-are-low-legacy-of-u-s-drought/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2013/world/global-grain-reserves-are-low-legacy-of-u-s-drought/', 'United States']);" target="_blank">
                      United States
                     </a>
                     and
                     <a href="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2009/world/australia-drought-water-warning/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2009/world/australia-drought-water-warning/', 'Australia ']);" target="_blank">
                      Australia
                     </a>
                     . (You can read more about it here in m
                     <a href="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2014/world/world-food-supplies-recover-droughts-reach-15-year-high/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2014/world/world-food-supplies-recover-droughts-reach-15-year-high/', 'y latest update on global food stocks']);">
                      y latest update on global food stocks
                     </a>
                     , which has a great graphic by my colleague, Kaye LaFond.)
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Perhaps the most interesting point to digest is how concentrated the worlds food markets have become. Most of the corn, rice, and wheat sold in the world is produced by just a few countries, including the United States. If those countries get hit by droughts, floods, or heat waves, it can have an outsize effect on the worlds food supply and food prices  prices countries that import the majority of their food have to pay. So while the drought that drastically reduced the 2012 U.S. corn crop didnt lead to widespread food shortages in the United States (
                     <a href="http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2012/world/infographic-u-s-drought-impacts-2012-corn-crops/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://www.circleofblue.org/waternews/2012/world/infographic-u-s-drought-impacts-2012-corn-crops/', 'though it did have other effects domestically']);" target="_blank">
                      though it did have other effects domestically
                     </a>
                     ), it pushed up corn prices to record highs.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The good news is that higher food stocks can help guard against shocks like drought, but even that may not be enough to bring food prices down to levels seen before the spike in the past decade.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Have you noticed your Thanksgiving feast getting pricier in the past few years? Do you know the water footprint of the foods on your Thanksgiving table? Do these facts influence your choices? What are you thankful for this year? Email me at codi@circleofblue.org, send me a tweet to @codikozacek, or comment below.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Codi Kozacek, reporter
                    </p>
