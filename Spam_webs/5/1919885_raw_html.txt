         <div class="content-scroll" id="featured-content">
          <span class="author-byline">
           by
           <strong>
            <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/author/mike-graham/" rel="author" title="Posts by Mike Graham">
             Mike Graham
            </a>
           </strong>
           <span class="published-date">
            <i class="glyphicon glyphicon-calendar" title="Published date">
            </i>
            <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/2014/11/giving-financial-planners-bum-rap/" title="3:45 pm">
             <time class="entry-date" datetime="2014-11-25T15:45:05+00:00">
              November 25, 2014
             </time>
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
          <p>
           Take  a look at this table.
          </p>
          <p>
           What  it shows is how trusted you are, depending on what job you do.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its  from a study done in America a few years ago. But Id say the results at least  roughly correspond to Australia today. Have a
           <a href="http://www.portphillippublishing.com.au/ESC-Nov25_clip_image001.png" target="_blank">
            look here
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="1354554833373" height="538" src="http://www.portphillippublishing.com.au/ESC-Nov25_clip_image001.png" width="462"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           If youre in the medical  profession, you are very trustworthy. Ditto if youre an engineer or a teacher.
          </p>
          <p>
           Im rather disheartened,  as a copywriter by trade, to see that advertising practitioners rank below  bloody stockbrokers on the trustworthy scale!
          </p>
          <p>
           Putting that aside  though, look whos languishing sheepishly at the bottom of that table
          </p>
          <p>
           Its the humble car  salesman.
          </p>
          <p>
           But let me ask you?
          </p>
          <h2>
           Are  car salesmen REALLY the
           <br/>
           villains  theyre made out to be?
          </h2>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           I ask this question  after listening to a fascinating episode of the radio show
           <em>
            This American Life
           </em>
           called Cars.
          </p>
          <p>
           It follows a group of  salesmen in one single car lot in the month of October.
          </p>
          <p>
           Theyre  a Chrysler lot. So if they hit 129 sales by the end of the month, Chrysler will  pay them a bonus of up to $85,000, depending on which models they sell. Fall  one car short and they get nada.
          </p>
          <p>
           From  the show
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            <em>
             Marc, who  owns this dealership, and Freddie, who runs it, both know that most people do  not trust car dealers. Most people think that theyre ripping us off, throwing  on extra charges, milking us for every dime. And they say, if you and I only  knew the reality, we would see that, in fact, the dealer is not making a  killing off us.
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             In fact, the  dealer is often squeaking by with a very thin profit margin. They say it is all  very different from what people think.
            </em>
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Youll  see what he means if you listen to the show. You can do so
           <a href="http://www.thisamericanlife.org/radio-archives/episode/513/129-cars" rel="nofollow">
            here
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Youll find that a few  of the characters and practices during that desperate October stick to  stereotype. One salesman, for instance, bases his entire selling technique  around
           <em>
            The Art of War
           </em>
           by Sun Tzu.
          </p>
          <p>
           Mostly, though, youll have  your preconceptions challenged.
          </p>
          <p>
           These guys are human  under pressure and working incredibly hard for very little money.
          </p>
          <p>
           They have empathy. Most  of them, while doggedly chasing their targets, still keep the customers needs  front and center.
          </p>
          <p>
           And they help each other  out. One guy, for instance, notices his colleague is just one sale from his  quota, with an hour to go before closing on the final day in October. So he  lets his colleague chalk up a sale HE just made.
          </p>
          <p>
           The lack of trust of car  salesman is actually the vestige of a bygone era. A time when you really did  walk onto a sales lot with two hands tied behind your back. Specifically if you  were after a used car.
          </p>
          <p>
           You basically had to go  on what you saw with your own eyes and the salesmans word.
          </p>
          <p>
           There was a pre-internet  bag of tricks: fiddling with the odometer, fibbing about previous owners, covering  up accidents etc. Forty years ago you could shift a dunger with these tactics.  Not today. Stronger  consumer protection laws, the internet and an array of resources like CARFAX generally keep dealerships  and their  sales staff  on the straight and narrow.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sure, these guys are in  the business of shifting wheels and making money.
          </p>
          <p>
           But these days  dealerships are more worried about getting you to come back and buy your NEXT  car a few years down the track and getting positive ratings on websites than  fleecing you the second you walk on the lot.
          </p>
          <p>
           Which got me thinking
          </p>
          <h2>
           WHO  ELSE in the personal finance
           <br/>
           space  might we be giving a raw deal?
          </h2>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           What  about real estate agents? Im surprised they dont feature on the table above.  Youve probably heard people utter unspeakably dark things about real estate  agents before. Maybe youve uttered them yourself.
          </p>
          <p>
           Im  probably unable to give an unbiased assessment here, because my old man is a  real estate agent.
          </p>
          <p>
           But  he came to it only relatively recently. And since then Ive had many of my own  preconceptions challenged. In fact Ive had my eyes opened as to just how  misunderstood most real estate agents are by the general public.
          </p>
          <p>
           How  misunderstood? Well, where do you start?
          </p>
          <p>
           What  about with their basic function?
          </p>
          <p>
           Youve  probably heard many times someone say
           <em>
            you dont need a real estate agent to  sell your house.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           And,  yes, the number of independent sales of houses have gone up in recent years, at  least in New Zealand.
          </p>
          <p>
           But,  still, the majority of people DO require the services of a real estate agent.  On average, youre going to sell a house every 6-8 years. That gives you not a  heck of a lot of time to perfect a fairly specialised negotiation process. That  aside:
           <em>
            most
           </em>
           big ticket sales require a middleman. When youre talking  about the sums of money involved in a house sale, you need someone who will  filter out the tension, emotions and biases both the seller and the buyer bring  to the table.
          </p>
          <p>
           How  about
           <em>
            real estate agents rake in the  cash at your expense
           </em>
           ? Well, thats fairly easy to debunk. Here in New  Zealand the average income for an agent is around $45k. Adjusted for tax  offsets, that brings it perilously close to the minimum "living wage"  of $38,000.
          </p>
          <p>
           Tell  my dad hes doing nothing and living the life of riley and hell laugh in your  face, before jamming a cheese toastie down his gob and racing to his fifth open  home on a Sunday.
          </p>
          <p>
           What  about with the myth that an agent will try and get you to pay as much as  possible for a house so they get more money?
           <em>
            Every extra cent you pay, your  agents bank account gets bigger. So dont trust a bloody thing that comes out  of their mouth!
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           This  is stupid too. The spread between $500,000 and $520,000 is about $300 to an  agent. Do you really think your agent is going to delay a sale to eke out  another three hundred bucks in commission? It doesnt work that way. Hed much  rather get a sale logged so he can move onto the next listing.
          </p>
          <p>
           Im  conflicted, though.
          </p>
          <p>
           The  real estate agent we worked with to sell our last house did a terrible job. Had  we taken her stern advice and taken an early offer (the very first offer that  came in, in fact.
           <em>
            Weeks
           </em>
           before the  actual auction) we would have lost around $30,000.
          </p>
          <p>
           I  guess, like any industry, there will be a few bad eggs.
          </p>
          <p>
           So,  we get to
           <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/category/retirement-living/retirement-planning/" title="More on retirement planning">
            <strong>
             financial planners
            </strong>
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <h2>
           Are we giving financial planners a bum rap?
          </h2>
          <p>
           <br/>
          </p>
          <p>
           Were  big into holding finance planners and advisors feet to the fire here at
           <em>
            The Escapologist
           </em>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Is  there a chance the humble financial planner, like the car salesman, is being  unfairly maligned?
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Nope.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Sorry.
          </p>
          <p>
           No change of heart here.
          </p>
          <p>
           Alright, alright, before you bury me in emails:  Im sure there are a few golden-hearted individuals in the financial services  industry who are the epitome of integrity. People who get up every morning and  want to do their level best for their clients. Problem is, this is an  exceedingly rare breed. And even these fine folks are often hamstrung by their  employers forced to give "pre-engineered" advice.
          </p>
          <p>
           I would say  and this  is just subjective opinion  that the industry remains awash with ethically  questionable individuals. The truth is the vast, VAST majority of financial  advisors out there neither have the ability nor inclination to give you "conflict-free"  advice.
          </p>
          <p>
           Put  it this way
          </p>
          <p>
           As  far as Im aware
           <em>
            The Sydney Morning  Herald
           </em>
           website doesnt have an entire hub titled
           <em>
            Car Salesman Go Rogue,
           </em>
           with dozens of articles outlining  vehicular law-breaking and malfeasance.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.smh.com.au/business/planners-go-rogue" rel="nofollow">
            The Planners, however, DO have this dubious honour.
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Certainly,  to my knowledge at least, a Royal Commission hasnt been called into the fraud,  forgery and cover-ups taking place at Woollongong City Motors.
          </p>
          <p>
           Thankfully,  though, the rolling back of the Coalitions regulations that protect financial  planners could soon level the playing field again.
          </p>
          <p>
           Financial  planners are spitting the dummy because, starting next year, they may have to  start phoning thousands of customers to ask them if they want to stay  customers.
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            <em>
             The advice industry reacted  furiously, claiming it would be thrown into disarray and face millionsof  dollars in extra costs,
            </em>
            says
            <em>
             The Sydney Morning Herald
            </em>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             If the governments regulations are  disallowed, previous legislation passed by the Gillard government would come  into force.
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             These laws include a requirement for  clients who signed up after July 2013 to "opt-in" every two years; a  broader duty for advisers to act in clients best interests; and tougher  restrictions on payments from the makers of wealth products to advisers.
            </em>
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Fancy  having to check in with your paying client every two years to see if theyre  happy with your service! What an outrage!
          </p>
          <p>
           Imagine  laws that enforce you to act in your clients best interests! How appallingly  unreasonable!
          </p>
          <p>
           And  tougher restrictions on kickbacks for selling certain financial products? Its  like were practically forcing these poor people to find REAL jobs. (Maybe they  should look at selling cars)
          </p>
          <p>
           No,  sorry, I have to stamp financial advisors and planners with:
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            STILL CROOKS!
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Until  next time,
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Mike Graham
            <br/>

           Editor,

            <div class="ts-fab-content">
             Head of Electrical Department (weekends) for New Zealands The Warehouse. Saturday Shift Supervisor at Video Ezy. Breakfast host for no-rating rock radio station Bayrock. To say that Mike Graham comes from humble beginnings is an understatement. Then he escaped to London. And the rest, as they say, is history
             <br/>
             <br/>
             <div id="expand-div">
              <div id="cathiddencontent">
               Mikes proper career began in 2000, as a junior copywriter for the UKs leading independent financial publisher Fleet Street Publications (now called MoneyWeek.) Since then his advertising campaigns for publishers in the United Kingdom, Ireland, the United States and Australia have generated tens of millions in gross sales. He also helmed one of the most popular UK business opportunity e-letters of the 2000s, The Shortcut Bulletin.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Mike came to Australia in 2005 to help a man called Dan Denning build a financial newsletter business. He helped create and launch now-iconic titles like The Daily Reckoning Australia, Australian Small Cap Investigator and Diggers and Drillers.
               <br/>
               Mikes next big thing is helping his pal Simon Munton with something called The Escapologist. Today he thinks, writes, consults, parents and broods in a fortified office-bunker in Sandringham, Auckland.
