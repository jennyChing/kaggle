             <p>
              <img alt="Identify Talent" border="0" class="alignLeft" height="123" id="img-1420671855681" src="/Portals/63683/images/job-search-276893_640.jpg" style="float: left;" width="185"/>
              Elevator pitch (according to
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevator_pitch" target="_blank" title="Wikipedia">
               Wikipedia
              </a>
              ): a short summary used to quickly and simply define a person, profession, product, service, organization or event and its value proposition. The name "elevator pitch" reflects the idea that it should be possible to deliver the summary in the time span of an elevator ride, or approximately thirty seconds to two minutes and is widely credited to Ilene Rosenzweig and Michael Caruso (while he was Editor for Vanity Fair) for its origin. The term itself comes from a scenario of an accidental meeting with someone important in the elevator. If the conversation inside the elevator in those few seconds is interesting and value adding, the conversation will continue after the elevator ride or end in exchange of business card or a scheduled meeting. Thats exactly what your rsum should be. Short, simple, to the point, and enough to keep the other party interested, especially when they are trying to identify talent.
             </p>
             <p>
              Writing a rsum is hard, and it seems that nobody really knows how to do it. The
              <a href="https://hbr.org/2009/06/how-to-write-a-resume-that-doe" target="_blank" title="Harvard Business Review">
               Harvard Business Review
              </a>
              said this: A Google search for rsum results in over 178,000,000 hits, whereas possum nets only 5,340,000. Thus the documentation of work experience is 33 and 1/3 [times] more popular than arboreal marsupials. When it comes to a managers hiring strategy, there are some things theyre looking for. Here are a few tips to keep your rsum out of the trash can.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               1. Proper formatting.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Keep an eye out for spelling and grammatical mistakes. Make sure all dates are flush against the right margin. If you cant manage to keep your rsum neat and tidy, how are you supposed to manage a team or oversee a million-dollar project?
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               2. Provide dates.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              If theres a gap in your employment, explain it in the cover letter; its not an excuse to omit dates. You might be worried youll look too old, or couldnt maintain steady work, but it will make you look either sloppy or sneaky if you dont provide dates of employment.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               3. Use buzzwords.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              I know, Im cringing too, but buzzwords are what get your foot in the door as far as HRs concerned with recruiting talent. HR is screening for matches with the words in the job description. That means you should read what they want, and tailor your rsum to the specific job your applying for. Now, there are definitely some buzzwords that you want to avoid, and some youll want to use. According to a
              <a href="http://www.tlnt.com/2014/03/13/the-best-and-worst-buzz-words-candidates-use-on-resumes/" target="_blank" title="TLNT">
               TLNT
              </a>
              article, those words and phrases (and how often they were mentioned in rsum respondents) are:
             </p>
             <p>
              Worst rsum terms:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Best of breed: 38 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Go-getter: 27 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Think outside the box: 26 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Synergy: 22 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Go-to person: 22 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Thought leadership: 16 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Value add: 16 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Results-driven: 16 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Team player: 15 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Bottom-line: 14 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Hard worker: 13 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Strategic thinker: 12 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Dynamic: 12 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Self-motivated: 12 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Detail-oriented: 11 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Proactively: 11 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Track record: 10 percent.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Here are terms employers say they would like to see on a rsum:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Achieved: 52 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Improved: 48 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Trained/Mentored: 47 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Managed: 44 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Created: 43 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Resolved: 40 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Volunteered: 35 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Influenced: 29 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Increased/Decreased: 28 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Ideas: 27 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Negotiated: 25 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Launched: 24 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Revenue/Profits: 23 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Under budget: 16 percent;
              </li>
              <li>
               Won: 13 percent.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              <b>
               4. Actually say something.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Now that you know which words to use and which to avoid, be sure to use verbs that actually mean something. Instead of saying assisted, worked on, contributed to, etc., say what you really did. Say you wrote, designed, or managed something. Its best to lean on the side of specificity.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               5. Modify your rsum to specific jobs.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Dont be lazy and send the same rsum to eighteen different job listings. Highlight the top three to seven jobs or roles youve had that match up to the job you want. At the very least, be original in your cover letter.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               6. Be careful when stating career objectives.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              If you know exactly what the hiring manager is looking for, and its you to a T, then put it on your rsum. You can also add volunteer work or hobbies, but its probably best to save the non-work-related stuff for the cover letter or interview.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               7. The older your job is, the less detail you should provide.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Thats it. You dont need eleven bullet points for a job you had 20 years ago.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               8. Keep it brief.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Dont feel the need to write about everything youve ever done. HBR put it nicely by saying Consider the rsum of a CEO. He doesnt need to say that he attended meetings, assigned work and whatever other tasks. He ran a company. One line.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               9. DO NOT MAKE TYPOS!
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              Seriously, just dont. I already mentioned proper formatting, grammar, and spelling, but its worth mentioning again. Look over your rsum over and over again. Have someone (or three) give it a look over. Be obsessive. But for the love of all that is holy, do not make typos.
             </p>
             <p>
              Finally,
              <a href="https://hbr.org/2014/12/how-to-write-a-resume-that-stands-out" target="_blank" title="a few things to remember">
               a few things to remember
              </a>
              :
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               Do:
              </b>
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Start with a short summary of who you are and why youre the right person for the job
              </li>
              <li>
               Emphasize accomplishments over responsibilities
              </li>
              <li>
               Create a new version of your rsum for every opportunity
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              <b>
               Dont:
              </b>
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Use clichs  explain what makes you a good candidate in concrete, specific words
              </li>
              <li>
               Cram text in or use a small font size  it has to be readable
              </li>
              <li>
               Cut and paste your rsum into your LinkedIn profile
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              When it comes to hiring strategy, managers who want to identify talent know what theyre looking for in a rsum. Theres no way of knowing exactly what, but sticking to the rules outlined above should give you a pretty good chance.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               What do you think? Are there specific things you look for in a rsum? Let us know in the comments section below.
              </b>
             </p>
