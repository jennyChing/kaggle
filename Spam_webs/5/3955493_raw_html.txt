          <div class="cq-article-content-media section">
           <figure class="media media--photo">
            <img alt="Wannabe iPhone 5 owners in the United Kingdom, France and Germany could have to wait as long as three weeks to receive orders when the iPhone 5 starts shipping." class="lazy620x350" data-src="/content/dam/images/j/b/h/q/l/image.related.afrArticleLead.620x350.j1qjr.png/1423577530481.jpg" height="350" width="620"/>
            <noscript>
             <img alt="Wannabe iPhone 5 owners in the United Kingdom, France and Germany could have to wait as long as three weeks to receive orders when the iPhone 5 starts shipping." class="lazy620x350" height="350" src="/content/dam/images/j/b/h/q/l/image.related.afrArticleLead.620x350.j1qjr.png/1423577530481.jpg" width="620"/>
            </noscript>
            <figcaption>
             Wannabe iPhone 5 owners in the United Kingdom, France and Germany could have to wait as long as three weeks to receive orders when the iPhone 5 starts shipping.
             <cite>
              Photo: AP
             </cite>
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-paras section">
           by
           <span style="font-weight:bold">
            Poornima Gupta | Sayantani Ghosh
           </span>
           <p>
            Apple shares jumped on Friday, touching a record high on what one analyst called iPhone 5 fever" as the company said some customers must wait two to three weeks for the new, slimmer, faster smartphone that accounts for half its revenue.
           </p>
           <p>
            Apples United States store, at
            <a href="http://www.apple.com">
             www.apple.com,
            </a>
            was projecting shipments for the iPhone 5 would take two weeks to fulfil, with analysts saying the date slipped within an hour of the start of pre-sales.
           </p>
           <p>
            The companys website showed buyers in United Kingdom, France and Germany would have to wait as long as three weeks to receive orders when the iPhone 5 starts shipping next Friday, the first day of deliveries.
           </p>
           <p>
            AT&amp;T, Verizon Communications and Sprint Nextel  the three US carriers who will sell the iPhone 5  showed delays ranging from a week to a month by Friday afternoon.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-media section">
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-paras section">
           <p>
            We have seen significant volumes since we started to accept pre-orders at 3am (New York time on Friday)," a Verizon spokeswoman said in an email but declined to give further details. AT&amp;T and Sprint did not return messages for comment.
           </p>
           <p>
            The new model will also be available in Apples stores next Friday for walk-in purchase. Retailers like Wal-Mart Stores and Best Buy are also taking pre-orders.
           </p>
           <p>
            Clearly, iPhone 5 fever is in full swing," said Topeka Capital Markets analyst Brian White.
           </p>
           <p>
            When you do a pre-order, the last thing you want to do is upset customers, so obviously they are overwhelmed with the demand. No one wants to sell out in an hour."
           </p>

          <div class="cq-article-content-paras section">
           <p>
            Apple shares rose 1.2 per cent to $US691.28 on the Nasdaq. The shares earlier touched a record high of $US696.98.
           </p>
           <p>
            Apple began taking orders for the iPhone 5 at midnight Pacific Time on Friday. The smartphone is being rolled out in phases and will be sold in 100 countries by the end of the year.
           </p>
           <p>
            The iPhone 5 sports a 4-inch (10-centimetre) retina" display, supports the high-speed 4G LTE wireless network and is 20 per cent lighter than the previous iPhone 4S.
           </p>
           <p>
            It is not unusual for Apple products to sell out the first day. Orders for the iPhone 4S, the last product the company introduced before the death of co-founder
            <person commonname="Steve Jobs" fullname="Steven Jobs" occupation="CEO Apple Inc." title="(null)" whoswhoid="(null)">
             Steve Jobs
            </person>
            , surpassed a million in the first 24 hours, beating Apples previous one-day record of 600,000 sales for the iPhone 4.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-media section">
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-paras section">
           <p>
            <b>
            </b>
            Analysts have expressed surprise at how quickly Apple planned to roll out the new model around the world, saying this was the fastest rollout of the phone since it launched in 2007. On average, Wall Street analysts forecast Apple may sell well over 42 million units this year.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some analysts said the early move may also point to a potential supply crunch.
           </p>
           <p>
            We believe the fast sellout indicates both high levels of demand and constrained supply," said Shannon Cross, an analyst with Cross Research, an independent research house.
           </p>
           <p>
            However, given that the company announced an aggressive rollout schedule, we assume managements plan includes a rapid increase in production."
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-media section">
          </div>
          <div class="cq-article-content-paras section">
           <p>
            One of Apples key suppliers for screens, Sharp, is struggling with high costs and scrambling to raise funds to pay debt.
           </p>
           <p>
            Sharp, a Japanese company that is negotiating a deal to sell a big stake in itself to Taiwans Hon Hai Precision Industry, Apples largest contract manufacturer, has fallen behind schedule on production of screens for Apples latest iPhone, a said in late August.
           </p>
           <p>
            The source, who is familiar with Sharps production operations, did not say how far behind the output had fallen.
           </p>
           <p>
            Apple did not return calls seeking comment.
           </p>
