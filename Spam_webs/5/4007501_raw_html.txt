     <p>
      <b>
       Henry Norman Bethune
      </b>
      (last name pronounced Baythun)
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
           b
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'e' in 'bed'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'th' in 'thigh'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// secondary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">
           j
          </span>
          <span title="/u/ long 'oo' in 'food'">
           u
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      (March 4, 1890  November 12, 1939;
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       <a class="extiw" href="//zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%99%BD%E6%B1%82%E6%81%A9" title="zh:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Bi Qin
       </span>
      </i>
      ) was a
      <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">
       Canadian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">
       physician
      </a>
      , medical innovator, and noted
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">
       anti-fascist
      </a>
      . Bethune came to international prominence first for his service as a frontline surgeon supporting the democratically-elected Republican government during the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      . But it was his service with the Communist
      <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Route_Army" title="Eighth Route Army">
       Eighth Route Army
      </a>
      (Ba Lu Jun) during the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">
       Second Sino-Japanese War
      </a>
      that would earn him enduring acclaim. Dr. Bethune effectively brought modern medicine to rural China and often treated sick villagers as much as wounded soldiers. His selfless commitment to the Chinese people made such an impression on
      <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">
       Mao Zedong
      </a>
      that generations of Chinese students were required to memorise the Chairman's eulogy to him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ironically, while Bethune was the man responsible for developing a mobile
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">
       blood-transfusion
      </a>
      service for frontline operations in the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      , he himself died of blood poisoning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A prominent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">
       Communist
      </a>
      and veteran of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">
       First World War
      </a>
      , he wrote that wars were motivated by profits, not principles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Statues in his honour can be found in cities throughout
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Family_history">
       Family history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Bethune&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dr. Norman Bethune came from a prominent
      <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Canadian" title="Scottish Canadian">
       Scottish Canadian
      </a>
      family. His great-great-grandfather, the Reverend Doctor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Bethune_(reverend)" title="John Bethune (reverend)">
       John Bethune
      </a>
      (17511815), the family patriarch, established the first
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">
       Presbyterian
      </a>
      congregation in
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      , the first five Presbyterian churches in Ontario and was one of the founders of the Presbyterian Church of Canada. Norman Bethunes great-grandfather,
      <a href="/wiki/Angus_Bethune_(fur_trader)" title="Angus Bethune (fur trader)">
       Angus Bethune
      </a>
      (17831858), joined the
      <a href="/wiki/North_West_Company" title="North West Company">
       North West Company
      </a>
      at an early age and travelled extensively throughout the North Western territories, exploring and trading for furs. He eventually reached the Pacific at
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Astoria" title="Fort Astoria">
       Fort Astoria, Oregon
      </a>
      . He became chief factor of the Lake Huron district for the
      <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">
       Hudson's Bay Company
      </a>
      after the merger of the rival companies. Upon retirement from the HBC in 1839, he successfully ran for a post as an alderman on Toronto City Council.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Normans grandfather, also named
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Bethune,_Sr." title="Norman Bethune, Sr.">
       Norman
      </a>
      (182292), was educated as a doctor at King's College,
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">
       University of Toronto
      </a>
      , and in
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      , England at
      <a href="/wiki/Guy%27s_Hospital" title="Guy's Hospital">
       Guy's Hospital
      </a>
      , graduating in 1848 as a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Physicians" title="Royal College of Physicians">
       Royal College of Physicians
      </a>
      . Upon his return to Canada, he became one of the founders of the Upper Canada School of Medicine,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which was incorporated into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Trinity_College" title="University of Trinity College">
       Trinity College, Toronto
      </a>
      and eventually the University of Toronto.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bethunes father, the Rev. Malcolm Nicolson Bethune, led an uneventful life as a small-town pastor, initially at Gravenhurst, Ontario, from 1889 to 1892. His mother was Elizabeth Ann Goodwin, an English immigrant to Canada. Both his parents were very religious. But although he was raised in a religious family, Bethune himself was an atheist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Norman grew up with a "fear of being mediocre", instilled into him by his emotionally strict father and domineering mother.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archives.cbc.ca_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-archives.cbc.ca-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Norman was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Gravenhurst,_Ontario" title="Gravenhurst, Ontario">
       Gravenhurst
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      on March 4, 1890. His birth certificate erroneously stated March 3.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phoenix_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Phoenix-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His siblings were his sister Janet and brother Malcolm.
     </p>
     <p>
      As a youth Bethune attended Owen Sound Collegiate Institute in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Owen_Sound,_Ontario" title="Owen Sound, Ontario">
       Owen Sound, Ontario
      </a>
      , now known as
      <a href="/wiki/Owen_Sound_Collegiate_and_Vocational_Institute" title="Owen Sound Collegiate and Vocational Institute">
       Owen Sound Collegiate and Vocational Institute
      </a>
      (OSCVI). He graduated from OSCVI in 1907. In September 1909 he enrolled at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">
       University of Toronto
      </a>
      . He interrupted his studies for one year in 1911 to be a volunteer labourer-teacher with
      <a href="/wiki/Frontier_College" title="Frontier College">
       Frontier College
      </a>
      at remote
      <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">
       lumber
      </a>
      and mining camps throughout northern Ontario, teaching immigrant mine labourers how to read and write English.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1914 when
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      was declared in Europe, he once again suspended his medical studies. In a flourish of patriotism he joined the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">
       Canadian Army
      </a>
      's No. 2 Field Ambulance to serve as a stretcher-bearer in France. He was wounded by
      <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">
       shrapnel
      </a>
      and spent three months recovering in an English hospital. When he had recuperated from his injuries, he returned to Toronto to complete his medical degree. He received his M.D. in 1916.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archives.cbc.ca_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-archives.cbc.ca-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Bethune&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1917, with the war still in progress, Bethune joined the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
       Royal Navy
      </a>
      as a Surgeon-Lieutenant at the Chatham Hospital in England. In 1919, he began an internship specialising in children's diseases at
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Ormond_Street_Hospital" title="Great Ormond Street Hospital">
       The Hospital for Sick Children at Great Ormond Street, London
      </a>
      . Later he went to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edinburgh,_Scotland" title="Edinburgh, Scotland">
       Edinburgh
      </a>
      , where he earned the
      <a href="/wiki/Fellowship_of_the_Royal_College_of_Surgeons" title="Fellowship of the Royal College of Surgeons">
       FRCS
      </a>
      qualification at the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Surgeons_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal College of Surgeons of Edinburgh">
       Royal College of Surgeons
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-maclean-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1920 he met Frances Penny whom he married in 1923. After an one-year 
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Tour" title="Grand Tour">
       Grand Tour
      </a>
       of Europe, during which they spent much of her inheritance, they moved to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" title="Detroit, Michigan">
       Detroit, Michigan
      </a>
      , where Bethune took up private practice and also took a part-time job as an instructor at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit_College_of_Medicine_and_Surgery" title="Detroit College of Medicine and Surgery">
       Detroit College of Medicine and Surgery
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1926 Bethune contracted
      <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">
       tuberculosis
      </a>
      . He sought treatment at the
      <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_Cottage_Sanitarium" title="Adirondack Cottage Sanitarium">
       Trudeau Sanatorium
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Saranac_Lake,_New_York" title="Saranac Lake, New York">
       Saranac Lake, New York
      </a>
      . At this time, Frances divorced Bethune and returned to her home in
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1920s the established treatment for TB was total bed rest in a sanatorium. While convalescing Bethune read about a radical new treatment for tuberculosis called
      <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">
       pneumothorax
      </a>
      . This involved artificially collapsing the tubercular (diseased) lung, thus allowing it to rest and heal itself. The physicians at the Trudeau thought this procedure was too new and risky. But Bethune insisted. He had the operation performed and made a full and complete recovery.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1929 Bethune remarried Frances, the Best Man at the wedding was his friend and colleague Dr. Graham Ross. They divorced again, for the final time, in 1933.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1928 Bethune joined the thoracic surgical pioneer, Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_William_Archibald" title="Edward William Archibald">
       Edward William Archibald
      </a>
      , the Surgeon-in-Chief of the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Victoria_Hospital,_Montreal" title="Royal Victoria Hospital, Montreal">
       Royal Victoria Hospital
      </a>
      in Montreal, the teaching hospital affiliated with
      <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">
       McGill University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-maclean-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From 1928 to 1936 Bethune perfected his skills in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thoracic_surgery" title="Thoracic surgery">
       thoracic surgery
      </a>
      and developed or modified more than a dozen new surgical tools. His most famous instrument was the Bethune Rib Shears, which still remains in use today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He published 14 articles describing his innovations in thoracic technique. He started his career in surgery at the Toronto General Hospital in 1921.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_activities">
       Political activities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Bethune&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political activities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Bethune became increasingly concerned with the socioeconomic aspects of disease. As a concerned doctor in Montreal during the economic depression years of the 1930s, Bethune frequently sought out the poor and gave them free medical care. He challenged his professional colleagues and agitated, without success, for the government to make radical reforms of medical care and health services in Canada.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bethune was an early proponent of
      <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">
       socialized medicine
      </a>
      and formed the Montreal Group for the Security of People's Health. In 1935 Bethune travelled to the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      to observe first hand their system of health care. During this year he became a committed Communist and joined the
      <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Canada" title="Communist Party of Canada">
       Communist Party of Canada
      </a>
      . When returning from the Spanish Civil War to raise support for the Loyalist cause, he openly identified with the Communist cause.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_Civil_War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Bethune&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spanish Civil War">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="640" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg/250px-Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg/375px-Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg/500px-Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Norman_Bethune_transfusion_unit_1936.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Canadian Blood Transfusion Unit which operated during the Spanish Civil War. Dr Norman Bethune is to the right (c. 1936/37, Spain).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Shortly after the outbreak of the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      in 1936, with the financial backing of the Committee to Aid Spanish Democracy, Bethune went to Spain to offer his services to the government (Loyalist) forces. He arrived in
      <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
       Madrid
      </a>
      on November 3.
     </p>
     <p>
      Unable to find a place where he could be used as a surgeon, he seized on an idea which may have been inspired by his limited experience of administering blood transfusions as Head of Thoracic Surgery at the
      <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4pital_du_Sacr%C3%A9-C%C5%93ur_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Hpital du Sacr-Cur de Montral">
       Sacred Heart Hospital
      </a>
      in Montreal between 1932 and 1936. The idea was to set up a mobile blood transfusion service by which he could take blood donated by civilians in bottles to wounded soldiers near the front lines. Though Bethune's unit, the
      <i>
       Servicio canadiense de transfusion de sangre
      </i>
      , was not the first of its kinda similar service had been set up in Barcelona by a Spanish haematologist, Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Dur%C3%A1n-Jord%C3%A0" title="Frederic Durn-Jord">
       Frederic Durn-Jord
      </a>
      , and had been functioning since SeptemberBethune's Madrid-based unit covered a far wider area of operation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bethune returned to Canada on June 6, 1937, where he went on a speaking tour to raise money and volunteers for the Spanish Civil War.
     </p>
     <p>
      Shortly before leaving for Spain, Bethune wrote the following poem, published in the July 1937 edition of
      <i>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Forum" title="Canadian Forum">
        Canadian Forum
       </a>
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       And this same pallid moon tonight,
       <br/>
       Which rides so quietly, clear and high,
       <br/>
       The mirror of our pale and troubled gaze,
       <br/>
       Raised to a cool Canadian sky.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       Above the shattered mountain tops,
       <br/>
       Last night, rose low and wild and red,
       <br/>
       Reflecting back from her illumined shield,
       <br/>
       The blood bespattered faces of the dead.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       To that pale disc, we raise our clenched fists,
       <br/>
       And to those nameless dead our vows renew,
       <br/>
       Comrades, who fought for freedom and the future world,
       <br/>
       Who died for us, we will remember you.
      </p>
     </blockquote>

     <p>
      In January 1938 Bethune travelled to
      <a href="/wiki/Yan%27an" title="Yan'an">
       Yan'an
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Shanbei" title="Shanbei">
       Shanbei
      </a>
      region of
      <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">
       Shaanxi
      </a>
      province in
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      . There he joined the Chinese Communists led by Mao Zedong. The Lebanese-American doctor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ma_Haide_(George_Hatem)" title="Ma Haide (George Hatem)">
       George Hatem
      </a>
      who had come to Yan'an earlier was instrumental in helping Bethune get started at his task of organizing medical services for the front and the region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In China, Bethune performed emergency
      <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_medicine" title="Battlefield medicine">
       battlefield surgical operations
      </a>
      on war casualties and established training for doctors, nurses and orderlies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He did not distinguish between casualties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bethune had thoughts of medicinal disciplines and states: " Medicine, as we are practising it, is a luxury trade. We are selling bread at the price of jewels. ... Let us take the profit, the private economic profit, out of medicine, and purify our profession of rapacious individualism ... Let us say to the people not ' How much have you got?' but ' How best can we serve you?' "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">

     <p>
      In the summer of 1939 Bethune was appointed the Medical Advisor to the Jin-Cha-Ji (Shanxi-
      <a href="/wiki/Chahar_Province" title="Chahar Province">
       Chahar
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">
       Hebei
      </a>
      )
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaan-Gan-Ning" title="Shaan-Gan-Ning">
       Border Region
      </a>
      Military District, under the direction of General
      <a href="/wiki/Nie_Rongzhen" title="Nie Rongzhen">
       Nie Rongzhen
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Stationed with the
      <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">
       Communist Party of China
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Route_Army" title="Eighth Route Army">
       Eighth Route Army
      </a>
      in the midst of the Second Sino-Japanese War, Bethune cut his finger while operating on a soldier. Probably due to his weakened state, he contracted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septicaemia" title="Septicaemia">
       septicaemia
      </a>
      (blood poisoning) and died of his wounds on November 12, 1939.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">

       Tim Buck
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Kodak_Retina" title="Kodak Retina">
       Kodak Retina
      </a>
      II camera to comrade Sha Fei. Norman Bethune, 04:20pm, November 11th, 1939".

     <p>
      Virtually unknown in his homeland during his lifetime, Bethune received international recognition when Chairman
      <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">

      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">
       altruistic
      </a>
      help to China, the nation's normal elementary school text book still has the essay today:
     </p>
     <p>
      "Comrade Bethunes spirit, his utter devotion to others without any thought of self, was shown in his great sense of responsibility in his work and his great warm-heartedness towards all comrades and the people ... We must all learn the spirit of absolute selflessness from him. With this spirit everyone can be very useful to the people. A mans ability may be great or small, but if he has this spirit, he is already noble-minded and pure, a man of moral integrity and above vulgar interests, a man who is of value to the people (
      <i>
       In Memory of Bethune
      </i>
      , Mao 1939, pp.337338) "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">

     <p>
      Bethune is one of the few Westerners to whom China has dedicated statues, of which many have been erected in his honour throughout the country. He is buried in the Revolutionary Martyrs' Cemetery,
      <a href="/wiki/Shijiazhuang" title="Shijiazhuang">
       Shijiazhuang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">
       Hebei
      </a>
      Province,
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      , where his tomb and memorial hall lie opposite the tomb of
      <a href="/wiki/Dwarkanath_Kotnis" title="Dwarkanath Kotnis">
       Dwarkanath Kotnis
      </a>
      , an Indian doctor also honoured for his humanitarian contribution to the Chinese. One of the three honoured in this memorial is the hero of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">
       Academy Award
      </a>
      winning film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">
        Chariots of Fire
       </a>
      </i>
      , Reverend
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Liddell" title="Eric Liddell">
       Eric Liddell
      </a>
      of Scotland. He died while incarcerated in a Japanese prisoner of war camp in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shandong_Province" title="Shandong Province">
       Shandong Province
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Elsewhere in China,
      <i>
       Norman Bethune University of Medical Sciences
      </i>
      , founded in
      <a href="/wiki/Changchun" title="Changchun">
       Changchun
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">
       Jilin
      </a>
      and later merged into
      <a href="/wiki/Jilin_University" title="Jilin University">
       Jilin University
      </a>
      as
      <a class="external text" href="http://bqeyxy.jlu.edu.cn/bqen/" rel="nofollow">
       Norman Bethune College of Medicine
      </a>
      , is named after him. He is also commemorated at three institutions in Shijiazhuang - Bethune Military Medical College, Bethune Specialized Medical College and Bethune International Peace Hospital. In Canada, Norman Bethune College at
      <a href="/wiki/York_University" title="York University">
       York University
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._Norman_Bethune_Collegiate_Institute" title="Dr. Norman Bethune Collegiate Institute">
       Dr. Norman Bethune Collegiate Institute
      </a>
      (a secondary school) in
      <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_Ontario" title="Scarborough, Ontario">
       Scarborough, Ontario
      </a>
      , are named after him.
     </p>

     <p>
      The Government of Canada purchased in 1973 the manse in which he was born in Gravenhurst following the visit of Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Trudeau" title="Pierre Trudeau">
       Pierre Trudeau
      </a>
      to China. The previous year, Dr. Bethune had been declared a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persons_of_National_Historic_Significance_(Canada)" title="Persons of National Historic Significance (Canada)">
       Person of National Historic Significance
      </a>
      . In 1976, the restored building was opened to the public as
      <a href="/wiki/Bethune_Memorial_House" title="Bethune Memorial House">
       Bethune Memorial House
      </a>
      . In 2012, the Government of Canada opened a new visitor centre, to enhance the experience of visitors to the site.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The house is operated as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Historic_Site_of_Canada" title="National Historic Site of Canada">
       National Historic Site of Canada
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Parks_Canada" title="Parks Canada">
       Parks Canada
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 1990, to commemorate the centenary of his birth, Canada and China each issued two postage stamps of the same design in his honour.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Norman Bethune Medal
      </b>
      is the highest medical honour in China, bestowed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Health of the People's Republic of China">
       Ministry of Health
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Human_Resources_and_Social_Security_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security of the People's Republic of China">
       Ministry of Personnel
      </a>
      of China, to recognize an individual's outstanding contribution, heroic spirit and great humanitarianism in the medical field. The Norman Bethune Medal was established in 1991. Biannually, one to seven medical personnel in China received this award.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1998, Bethune was inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Medical_Hall_of_Fame" title="Canadian Medical Hall of Fame">
       Canadian Medical Hall of Fame
      </a>
      located in
      <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">
       London, Ontario
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      In August 2000, then-
      <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">
       Governor General
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Adrienne_Clarkson" title="Adrienne Clarkson">
       Adrienne Clarkson
      </a>
      , who is of Chinese birth, visited Gravenhurst and unveiled a bronze statue of him erected by the town. It stands in front of the Opera House on the town's main street, Muskoka Road.
     </p>
     <p>
      On February 7, 2006, the city of
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Mlaga">
       Mlaga
      </a>
      , Spain, opened the Walk of Canadians in his memory. This avenue, which runs parallel to the beach "Crow Rock" direction to Almeria, paid tribute to the solidarity action of Dr. Norman Bethune and his colleagues who helped the population of Mlaga during the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      . During the ceremony, a commemorative plaque was unveiled with the inscription: "Walk of Canadians - In memory of aid from the people of Canada at the hands of Norman Bethune, provided to the refugees of Mlaga in February 1937." The ceremony also included a planting of an olive tree and a maple tree representative of Spain and Canada, symbols of friendship between the two peoples.
     </p>

      The city of Montreal, Quebec, has created a
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Bethune_Square" title="Norman Bethune Square">
       public square
      </a>
      and erected a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Bethune_Memorial_(Montreal)" title="Norman Bethune Memorial (Montreal)">
       statue of him
      </a>
      in his honour, located near the
      <a href="/wiki/Guy-Concordia_(Montreal_Metro)" title="Guy-Concordia (Montreal Metro)">
       Guy-Concordia Metro station
      </a>

     </p>

      The 2007
      <a href="/wiki/Harbin_International_Ice_and_Snow_Sculpture_Festival" title="Harbin International Ice and Snow Sculpture Festival">
       Harbin International Ice and Snow Sculpture Festival
      </a>
      featured as its central theme a memorial to Bethune.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bethune_in_film_and_literature">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      <i>
       Dr Bethune
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Bi Qin Difu
       </span>
      </i>
      ), was made in 1964;
      <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Tannebaum" title="Gerald Tannebaum">
       Gerald Tannebaum
      </a>
      , an American humanitarian, played Bethune.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bethune was the subject of a 1964
      <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Board_of_Canada" title="National Film Board of Canada">
       National Film Board of Canada
      </a>
      documentary
      <i>
       Bethune,
      </i>
      directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Brittain" title="Donald Brittain">
       Donald Brittain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brittain_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brittain-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film includes interviews with many people close to Bethune, including his biographer
      <a href="/wiki/Ted_Allan" title="Ted Allan">
       Ted Allan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sutherland" title="Donald Sutherland">
       Donald Sutherland
      </a>
      played Bethune in the 197475 television show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Witness_to_Yesterday" title="Witness to Yesterday">
        Witness to Yesterday
       </a>
      </i>
      hosted by
      <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Watson_(producer)" title="Patrick Watson (producer)">
       Patrick Watson
      </a>
      , produced by Arthur Veronka, and sponsored by Shell Canada Limited.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sutherland's portrayal of Bethune was eerily compelling and (likely) led to him playing Bethune in two
      <a href="/wiki/Biography" title="Biography">
       biographical
      </a>
      films:
      <i>
       Bethune
      </i>
      (1977), made for television on a low budget, and
      <i>
       Bethune: The Making of a Hero
      </i>
      (1990). The latter, based on a 1952 book
      <i>
       The Scalpel, The Sword; The Story Of Doctor Norman Bethune
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Ted_Allan" title="Ted Allan">
       Ted Allan
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sydney_Gordon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sydney Gordon (page does not exist)">
       Sydney Gordon
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was a co-production of
      <a href="/wiki/Telefilm_Canada" title="Telefilm Canada">
       Telefilm Canada
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">
       Canadian Broadcasting Corporation
      </a>
      , FR3 TV France and China Film Co-production.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the CBC's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Canadian" title="The Greatest Canadian">
        The Greatest Canadian
       </a>
      </i>
      program in 2004, he was voted the 26th Greatest Canadian by viewers.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006
      <a href="/wiki/China_Central_Television" title="China Central Television">
       China Central Television
      </a>
      produced a 20-part drama series,
      <i>
       Dr. Norman Bethune
      </i>
      (), documenting his life, which with a budget of
      <a href="/wiki/Renminbi" title="Renminbi">
       yuan
      </a>
      30million (US$3.75million) was the most expensive Chinese TV series to date. The series is directed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yang_Yang_(director)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yang Yang (director) (page does not exist)">
       Yang Yang
      </a>
      and stars Canadian actor
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trevor_Hayes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trevor Hayes (page does not exist)">
       Trevor Hayes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">

        </span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      </i>

      <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7npkIYEGyNw" rel="nofollow">

      on

      </a>
      .

     <p>
      The Bethune biographer, Roderick Stewart, has authored four books on Norman Bethune, including "Bethune," (1973), "The Canadians: Norman Bethune," (1974), "The Mind of Norman Bethune," (1990). And then in 2011, he co-authored with Sharon Stewart, "Phoenix: The Life of Norman Bethune." a book which Canadian author Michael Bliss, in his review in the "Globe and Mail," said, "should become the definitive basis for all serious discussion of Bethune."

      </a>

      <i>
       Bethune
      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">
       Detroit
      </a>
      , Michigan, to his death. It was this play on which the 1977 Donald Sutherland film was based.

     <p>
      The character Jerome Martell in
      <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacLennan" title="Hugh MacLennan">
       Hugh MacLennan

       <a href="/wiki/The_Watch_That_Ends_the_Night" title="The Watch That Ends the Night">

      <a href="/wiki/The_Tragically_Hip" title="The Tragically Hip">
       The Tragically Hip
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Courage_(for_Hugh_MacLennan)" title="Courage (for Hugh MacLennan)">

      <i>
       The Watch
      </i>
      in particular. The song's refrain 'Courage, it couldn't have come at a worse time' is a reference to the novel's climax, in which the 'Bethunian' qualities of Jerome Martell are at their peak.
     </p>
