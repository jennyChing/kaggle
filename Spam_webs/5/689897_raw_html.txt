             <p>
              <b>
               Dear Backers: Kickstarter projects live and die by the buzz they generate. We need your help to get the word out! If you read this and decide to back us, please use the "Share this project" button above to Share us on Facebook and Tweet about us as well, so we can tell as many people as possible about Phasor!Thank you so much!
              </b>

             <h1>
              Introducing Phasor. Arduino + Wifi + Webserver. For just $20.
             </h1>
             <p>
              Introducing
              <b>
               Phasor
              </b>
              . This revolutionary development board can connect any project to the Internet, quickly and economically.
              <b>
               Phasor
              </b>
              has the following hardware features, which we think blow away every comparably priced IoT development solution:
             </p>
             <a href="http://www.phasor.io" target="_blank">
              <div class="template asset" contenteditable="false" data-id="3452701">
               <figure>
                <img alt="Phasor's major components." class="fit" src="https://ksr-ugc.imgix.net/assets/003/452/701/220ca709f39994c50ddfdf44dd7b7581_original.jpg?v=1426561533&amp;w=680&amp;fit=max&amp;auto=format&amp;q=92&amp;s=26651639d34a66e061d7581fcb52dada"/>
                <figcaption>
                 Phasor's major components.
                </figcaption>
               </figure>
              </div>
             </a>
             <ul>
              <li>
               ESP8266 WiFi SoC, running at 80 MHz
              </li>
              <li>
               802.11 b/g/n WiFi connectivity
              </li>
              <li>
               32 Mbit flash to hold code and webpages
              </li>
              <li>
               USB connectivity for power and debugging
              </li>
              <li>
               Onboard USB-&gt;JTAG and USB-&gt;UART bridge with virtual COM port
              </li>
              <li>
               Onboard 1A regulator to power add-on boards and breadboard circuitry from USB or external 3.3-18V power supplies
              </li>
              <li>
               Small, breadboardable form factor... 1.2" x 0.8" footprint, 1.5" x 0.8" overall.
              </li>
              <li>
               Lots of IO: 10 GPIO, SPI, UART, 1 ADC, 3 PWM
              </li>
             </ul>
             <h1>
              A Selection of Awesome Shields
             </h1>
             <p>
              We have also developed stackable add-on shields forPhasor. The shield boards provide Phasor with a ton of cool abilities that other ESP8266 boards just don't offer. And because they are stackable, you don't even need any wires to connect them... just plug them in, one on top of the other. The final version will be keyed, so there's no risk of plugging them in backwards.
             </p>
             <div class="template asset" contenteditable="false" data-id="3391329">
              <figure>
               <img alt="Phasor's awesome shield lineup." class="fit" src="https://ksr-ugc.imgix.net/assets/003/391/329/ce2e44f47f2deeb6f9fd3dd7a0220a3f_original.png?v=1425600667&amp;w=680&amp;fit=max&amp;auto=format&amp;lossless=true&amp;s=fb7f55ce7abe0e6fb525a4154ea062db"/>
               <figcaption>
                Phasor's awesome shield lineup.
               </figcaption>
              </figure>
             </div>
             <p>
              Phasor's shield boards provide:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               A 128x32 graphical OLED display to plot data, display images and show messages
              </li>
              <li>
               A relay with screw terminals, capable of switching 120V AC loads
              </li>
              <li>
               An SD card slot, for storing files and logs
               <b>
                ($60K Stretch goal)
               </b>
              </li>
              <li>
               Four additional ADC channels
               <b>
                ($70K Stretch goal)
               </b>
              </li>
              <li>
               An adapter board (not pictured) to connect the Phasor to your Arduino Uno
               <b>
                ($80K Stretch goal)
               </b>
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Not all of these shields are going to be available right away... only the OLED shield and relay shield are being offered initially. The other three shields will be offered as stretch goals. Read our section on funding goals to learn more about when the other shields will become available.
             </p>
             <p>
              Want to see the shields in action? Check out the YouTube video below, which shows the OLED and ADC shields in action. You'll see why Phasor Systems has the best ESP8266 development hardware around.
             </p>
             <div class="template oembed" contenteditable="false" data-href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m8arrn-6Fto&amp;feature=youtu.be">
              <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="344" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/m8arrn-6Fto?feature=oembed&amp;wmode=transparent" width="612">
              </iframe>

             <h1>
              A Top-Notch Toolchain for the ESP8266
             </h1>
             <div>
              <p>
               Phasor's integrated bootloader provides a lot of great features. In addition to dynamically booting user code images, It has an integrated webserver that serves pages off of the flash. The bootloader also serves a configuration page that lets you configure Phasor to join a network, set up an access point, toggle its pins, or flash new firmware.
              </p>
              <div class="template asset" contenteditable="false" data-id="3378172">
               <figure>
                <img alt="" class="fit" src="https://ksr-ugc.imgix.net/assets/003/378/172/f5ba53dc47fca0ad32d293ed80ae305c_original.png?v=1425426556&amp;w=680&amp;fit=max&amp;auto=format&amp;lossless=true&amp;s=0db53d4999c5a36a37fac4a75973317d"/>
               </figure>
              </div>
              <p>
               Phasor's bootloader provides the following features
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                Embedded web server with integrated flash filesystem
               </li>
               <li>
                Onboard configuration page for provisioning and controlling pins
               </li>
               <li>
                Ability to register CGI and REST API callbacks call any function via HTTP request!
               </li>
               <li>
                Toggle pins from the configuration page without writing any code
               </li>
               <li>
                Read filesystems and the contents of flash from the config page
               </li>
               <li>
                Unexpected reset handling - watchdog timeout reboots into safe mode
               </li>
              </ul>
              <p>
               Additional software features that are still under construction include:
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                Drag-and-drop over-the-air firmware upgrade via the onboard configuration webpage
               </li>
               <li>
                MQTT/NAT traversal system, so you can interact with your board from anywhere in the world!
               </li>
               <li>
                Our GCC-based toolchain works in Windows and Linux, with Mac support currently under development.
               </li>
               <li>
                Native build support in the Arduino IDE via a custom core, to make the
                <b>
                 Phasor
                </b>
                Arduino compatible!
               </li>
               <li>
                JTAG debug via Eclipse/GDB/OpenOCD

             <h3 class="normal mb4">
              Risks and challenges
             </h3>
             <p>
              Risk #1: Our primary risk at this point lies in government compliance testing.  In the US, no electronic product can legally be sold without completing FCC compliance testing to verify it does not interfere with other electronic devices.  This compliance testing can be tricky and requires outside help to complete, and if you fail, your product may need to be redesigned to ensure compliance.
             </p>
             <p>
              Risk mitigation plan: We have already taken steps to mitigate this risk by having our design informally reviewed by experts in FCC compliance testing, which revealed no red flags.  Espressif recently got a similar design through FCC testing, which means the chip itself is certifiable, i.e.it doesn't produce a bunch of spurs and out-of-band radiation.  You can view Espressif's certification report here:
              <a href="http://fccid.net/number.php?fcc=2AC7Z-ESP8266EX&amp;id=794971#axzz3TNIclMFt" rel="nofollow">
               http://fccid.net/number.php
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              In addition, we have built $20,000 into our budget for FCC compliance testing costs, which will pay for several days at our chosen testing house.  We feel this should be adequate to ensure we can get certified, even if we fail the first time and have to make minor changes to the design.  We are prepared to add a shielding can and additional bandpass filtering to the board if necessary, which should mitigate any problems with out-of-band radiated emissions.
             </p>
             <p>
              Additionally, Adam has experience shepherding boards through FCC compliance testing, and Alex has experience in RF design, which he can use to help mitigate any issues that arise during testing.
             </p>
             <p>
              Risk #2: Our second most significant risk is trouble with assemblers.  We will be using contract manufacturers to produce our PCBs and assemble them.  In the event of a significant number of factory defects, some less scrupulous assemblers may attempt to wash their hands of the issue and refuse to cover the costs of replacements.  This is especially a problem with Chinese assemblers, who are beyond the reach of U.S. law and thus are not subject to litigation.
             </p>
             <p>
              Risk mitigation plan: We are using an assembler that performs their back-end assembly work in China but is headquartered in the US, and as such is under the jurisdiction of U.S. law.  We will work out a contract with them prior to production that spells out who is responsible for defects, and will be able to hold them accountable if any quality issues arise.
             </p>
