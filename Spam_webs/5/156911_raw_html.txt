     <p>
      A
      <b>
       joystick
      </b>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Input_device" title="Input device">
       input device
      </a>
      consisting of a stick that pivots on a base and reports its angle or direction to the device it is controlling. A joystick, also known as the
      <b>
       control column
      </b>
      , is the principal control device in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">
       cockpit
      </a>
      of many civilian and military aircraft, either as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_stick" title="Center stick">
       center stick
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Side-stick" title="Side-stick">
       side-stick
      </a>
      . It often has supplementary switches to control various aspects of the aircraft's flight.
     </p>
     <p>
      Joysticks are often used to control video games, and usually have one or more push-buttons whose state can also be read by the computer. A popular variation of the joystick used on modern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_consoles" title="Video game consoles">
       video game consoles
      </a>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Analog_stick" title="Analog stick">
       analog stick
      </a>
      . Joysticks are also used for controlling machines such as cranes, trucks, underwater unmanned vehicles, wheelchairs, surveillance cameras, and zero turning radius lawn mowers. Miniature finger-operated joysticks have been adopted as input devices for smaller electronic equipment such as
      <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">
       mobile phones
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      Joysticks originated as controls for aircraft
      <a href="/wiki/Aileron" title="Aileron">
       ailerons
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elevator_(aircraft)" title="Elevator (aircraft)">
       elevators
      </a>
      , and is first known to have been used as such on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Bleriot" title="Louis Bleriot">
       Louis Bleriot
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleriot_VIII" title="Bleriot VIII">
       Bleriot VIII
      </a>
      aircraft of 1908, in combination with a foot-operated rudder bar for the yaw control surface on the tail.
     </p>
     <p>
      The name "joystick" is thought to originate with early 20th century French pilot
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Esnault-Pelterie" title="Robert Esnault-Pelterie">
       Robert Esnault-Pelterie
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are also competing claims on behalf of fellow pilots
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Loraine" title="Robert Loraine">
       Robert Loraine
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Henry_Joyce&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Henry Joyce (page does not exist)">
       James Henry Joyce
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Edward_George" title="Arthur Edward George">
       A. E. George
      </a>
      . Loraine is cited by the
      <i>
       Oxford English Dictionary
      </i>
      for using the term "joystick" in his diary in 1909 when he went to
      <a href="/wiki/Pau,_Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es-Atlantiques" title="Pau, Pyrnes-Atlantiques">
       Pau
      </a>
      to learn to fly at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Bleriot" title="Louis Bleriot">
       Bleriot's
      </a>
      school. George was a pioneer aviator who with his colleague Jobling built and flew a biplane at Newcastle in England in 1910. He is alleged to have invented the "George Stick" which became more popularly known as the joystick. The George and Jobling aircraft control column is in the collection of the Discovery Museum in Newcastle upon Tyne, England. Joysticks were present in early planes, though their mechanical origins are uncertain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The coining of the term "joystick" may actually be credited to Loraine, as his is the earliest known usage of the term, although he most certainly did not invent the device.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_joysticks">
       Electronic joysticks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joystick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electronic joysticks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joystick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The electrical two-axis joystick was invented by C. B. Mirick at the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="United States Naval Research Laboratory">
       United States Naval Research Laboratory
      </a>
      (NRL) and patented in 1926 (U.S. Patent no. 1,597,416)".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      NRL was actively developing remote controlled aircraft at the time and the joystick was possibly used to support this effort. In the awarded patent, Mirick writes: "My control system is particularly applicable in maneuvering aircraft without a pilot."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Germans developed an electrical two-axis joystick around 1944 . The device was used as part of the Germans'
      <i>
       Funkgert
      </i>
      FuG 203
      <i>
       Kehl
      </i>
      radio control transmitter system used in certain German bomber aircraft, used to guide both the rocket-boosted anti-ship missile
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Henschel_Hs_293" title="Henschel Hs 293">
        Henschel Hs 293
       </a>
      </i>
      , and the unpowered pioneering precision-guided munition
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fritz-X" title="Fritz-X">
        Fritz-X
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      against maritime and other targets. Here, the joystick of the
      <i>
       Kehl
      </i>
      transmitter was used by an operator to steer the missile towards its target. This joystick had on-off switches rather than analogue sensors. Both the Hs 293 and Fritz-X used FuG 230
      <i>
       Straburg
      </i>
      radio receivers in them to send the
      <i>
       Kehl's
      </i>
      control signals to the ordnance's control surfaces. A comparable joystick unit was used for the contemporary American
      <a href="/wiki/Azon" title="Azon">
       Azon
      </a>
      steerable munition, strictly to laterally steer the munition in the yaw axis only.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This German invention was picked up by someone in the team of scientists assembled at the
      <i>
       Heeresversuchsanstalt
      </i>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peenemuende" title="Peenemuende">
       Peenemnde
      </a>
      . Here a part of the team on the German
      <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
       rocket
      </a>
      program was developing the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasserfall_missile" title="Wasserfall missile">
       Wasserfall missile
      </a>
      , a variant of the
      <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">
       V-2 rocket
      </a>
      , the first ground-to-air
      <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">
       missile
      </a>
      . The Wasserfall steering equipment converted the electrical signal to radio signals and transmitted these to the missile.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1960s the use of joysticks became widespread in radio-controlled model aircraft systems such as the Kwik Fly produced by Phill Kraft (1964). The now-defunct Kraft Systems firm eventually became an important OEM supplier of joysticks to the computer industry and other users. The first use of joysticks outside the radio-controlled aircraft industry may have been in the control of powered wheelchairs, such as the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Permobil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Permobil (page does not exist)">
       Permobil
      </a>
      (1963). During this time period NASA used joysticks as control devices as part of the Apollo missions. For example, the lunar lander test models were controlled with a joystick.
     </p>
     <p>
      In many modern airliners aircraft, for example all Airbus aircraft developed from the 1980s, the joystick has received a new lease on life for flight control in the form of a "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidestick" title="Sidestick">
       sidestick
      </a>
      " a controller similar to a gaming joystick, but which is used to control the flight, replacing the traditional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoke_(aircraft)" title="Yoke (aircraft)">
       yoke
      </a>
      . The sidestick saves weight; improves movement and visibility in the cockpit; and may be safer in an accident than the traditional "control yoke".
     </p>

     <p>

      </a>

        Mortal Kombat
       </a>

      cannot be comfortably emulated on a console joypad, so licensed home arcade sticks for these games have been manufactured for home consoles and PCs.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_details">
       Technical details
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>

     <p>
      Most joysticks are two-dimensional, having two axes of movement (similar to a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_mouse" title="Computer mouse">
       mouse
      </a>
      ), though one and three-dimensional joysticks do exist. A joystick is generally configured so that moving the stick left or right signals movement along the X axis, and moving it forward (up) or back (down) signals movement along the Y axis. In joysticks that are configured for three-dimensional movement, twisting the stick left (counter-clockwise) or right (clockwise) signals movement along the Z axis. These three axesX, Y, and Zare respectively an aircraft's roll, pitch, and yaw.
     </p>
     <p>
      An
      <b>
       analog joystick
      </b>
      is a joystick which has a continuous range of
      <i>
       positional states
      </i>
      , which can be measured as X,Y axis values (usually as the calibrated
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance_and_conductance" title="Electrical resistance and conductance">
       resistance
      </a>
      of a pair of
      <a href="/wiki/Potentiometer" title="Potentiometer">
       potentiometers
      </a>
      ). These positions can be sampled at frequent intervals in order to track motion. Often the design includes a mechanism for returning the stick to a center position; in some cases this can be disabled when not desired. A
      <b>
       digital joystick
      </b>
      gives only the on-off states of a group of switches, each corresponding to a
      <i>
       direction of applied force
      </i>
      . The simplest form uses one switch for each of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_directions" title="Cardinal directions">
       cardinal directions
      </a>
      , and will usually allow for the activation of adjacent pairs, providing the popular "8-directional" capability. Digital joysticks were very common as
      <a href="/wiki/Game_controller" title="Game controller">
       game controllers
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">
       video game consoles
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcade_machine" title="Arcade machine">
       arcade machines
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Home_computer" title="Home computer">
       home computers
      </a>
      of the 1980s, and survive as "directional pads" on more recent devices.
     </p>
     <p>
      Additionally, joysticks often have one or more
      <i>
       fire buttons
      </i>
      , used to trigger some kind of action. These are simply switches.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some joysticks have
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haptic_feedback" title="Haptic feedback">
       haptic feedback
      </a>
      capability, making them active devices, not just input devices. The computer can return a signal to the joystick that causes it to resist the movement with a returning force or make the joystick vibrate.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many I/O interface cards for
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">
       PCs
      </a>
      had a
      <a href="/wiki/Game_port" title="Game port">
       joystick (game control) port
      </a>
      . The most recent versions of
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">
       Microsoft Windows
      </a>
      however do not support game port, thus most modern joysticks use a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus">
       USB
      </a>
      interface for connection to the PC.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hat_switch">
       Hat switch
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joystick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hat switch">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JoystickDirections.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="560" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/JoystickDirections.svg/170px-JoystickDirections.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/JoystickDirections.svg/255px-JoystickDirections.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/JoystickDirections.svg/340px-JoystickDirections.svg.png 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JoystickDirections.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hat switch - at top, in green
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A hat switch is a control on some joysticks. It is also known as a POV (point of view) switch. It allows one to look around in one's virtual world, browse menus, etc. For example, many flight simulators use it to switch the player's views,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while other games sometimes use it as a substitute for the
      <a href="/wiki/D-pad" title="D-pad">
       D-pad
      </a>
      ; while computer gamepads modelled after PlayStation DualShock controllers assign POV switch scancodes to the D-pad
     </p>
     <p>
      The term hat switch is a sanitization of the term "
      <a href="/wiki/Coolie" title="Coolie">
       Coolie
      </a>
      Hat", named for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conical_straw_hat" title="Conical straw hat">
       similar-looking headgear
      </a>
      , which may be considered offensive.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a real aircraft, the hat switch may control things like
      <a href="/wiki/Aileron" title="Aileron">
       aileron
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">
       rudder
      </a>
      trim.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_applications">
       Industrial applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joystick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Industrial applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In recent times, the employment of joysticks has become commonplace in many industrial and manufacturing applications, such as; cranes, assembly lines, forestry equipment, mining trucks, and excavators. In fact, the use of such joysticks is in such high demand, that it has virtually replaced the traditional mechanical control lever in nearly all modern hydraulic control systems. Additionally, most
      <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">
       unmanned aerial vehicles
      </a>
      (UAVs) and submersible
      <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_vehicle" title="Remotely operated vehicle">
       remotely operated vehicles
      </a>
      (ROVs) require at least one joystick to control either the vehicle, the on-board cameras, sensors, and manipulators.
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the highly hands-on, rough nature of such applications, the industrial joystick tends to be more robust than the typical video-game controller, and able to function over a high cycle life. This led to the development and employment of
      <a href="/wiki/Hall_effect" title="Hall effect">
       Hall effect
      </a>
      sensing to such applications in the 1980s as a means of contactless sensing. Several companies produce joysticks for industrial applications using Hall effect technology. Another technology used in joystick design is the use of strain gauges to build force transducers from which the output is proportional to the force applied rather than physical deflection. Miniature force transducers are used as additional controls on joysticks for menu selection functions.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some larger manufacturers of joysticks are able to customize joystick handles and grips specific to the OEM needs while small regional manufacturers often concentrate on selling standard products at higher prices to smaller OEMs.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Assistive_technology">
       Assistive technology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joystick&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Assistive technology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Specialist joysticks, classed as an
      <a href="/wiki/Assistive_technology" title="Assistive technology">
       assistive technology
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Pointing_device" title="Pointing device">
       pointing device
      </a>
      , are used to replace the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_mouse" title="Computer mouse">
       computer mouse
      </a>
      for people with fairly severe physical disabilities. Rather than controlling games, these joysticks control the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pointer_(computing_WIMP)" title="Pointer (computing WIMP)">
       pointer
      </a>
      . They are often useful to people with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athetoid" title="Athetoid">
       athetoid
      </a>
      conditions, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">
       cerebral palsy
      </a>
      , who find them easier to grasp than a standard mouse. Miniature joysticks are also available for people with conditions involving muscular weakness such as
      <a href="/wiki/Muscular_dystrophy" title="Muscular dystrophy">
       muscular dystrophy
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_neurone_disease" title="Motor neurone disease">
       motor neurone disease
      </a>
      . They are also used on
      <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair#Electric_Powered_Wheelchairs" title="Wheelchair">
       electric powered wheelchairs
      </a>
      for control since they are simple and effective to use as a control method.
     </p>

Light guns differ from positional guns, such as in Gunblade NY (bottom), that are essentially analog joysticks. ... Positional guns are essentially analog sticks mounted in a fixed location with respect to the screen. Light guns, in contrast, have no fixed a priori relationship with a display.

         </b>
        </span>
        <span class="reference-text">
         <span class="citation" id="CITEREFYo-Sung_Ho_.26_Hyoung_Joong_Kim2005">
          Yo-Sung Ho &amp; Hyoung Joong Kim (November 1316, 2005),
          <a class="external text" href="http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=z-KQDQ0BtG4C&amp;pg=PA688" rel="nofollow">
           <i>
            Advances in Multimedia Information Processing-PCM 2005: 6th Pacific-Rim Conference on Multimedia, Jeju Island, Korea
           </i>
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Springer_Science%2BBusiness_Media" title="Springer Science+Business Media">
           Springer Science &amp; Business
          </a>
          , p.688,
          <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">
           ISBN
          </a>
          <a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/3-540-30040-6" title="Special:BookSources/3-540-30040-6">

          <span class="reference-accessdate">
           , retrieved
           <span class="nowrap">
            2011-04-03

          </span>

          <q>
           The two routes to conventional gun control are light guns and positional guns. Light guns are the most common for video game systems of any type. They work optically with screen and do not keep track of location on the screen until the gun is fired. When the gun is fired, the screen blanks for a moment, and the optics in the gun register where on the screen the gun is aimed. That information is sent to the computer, which registers the shot. ... Positional guns are mounted stationary on the arcade cabinet with the ability to aim left/right and up/down. They function much like joysticks, which maintain a known location on screen at all times and register the current location when fired.
          </q>
