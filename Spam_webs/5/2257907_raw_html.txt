   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Ha'amonga 'a Maui
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1165" data-file-width="1739" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG/250px-TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG/375px-TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG/500px-TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TO-Haamonga_A_Maui.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Ha'amonga 'a Maui
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Ha
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       amonga
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       a Maui
      </b>
      (Burden of Maui) - is a stone
      <a href="/wiki/Trilithon" title="Trilithon">
       trilithon
      </a>
      located in
      <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">
       Tonga
      </a>
      , in the north of the island of
      <a href="/wiki/Tongatapu" title="Tongatapu">
       Tongatapu
      </a>
      , near the village of
      <a href="/wiki/Niutoua" title="Niutoua">
       Niutua
      </a>
      , in Heket.
     </p>
     <p>
      Trilithon is constructed from three coral limestone slabs, and is up to 5.2 m high, 1.4 m wide, 5.8 m long.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wondermondo_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wondermondo-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The weight of the visible part of each upright stone is approximately 30 - 40 tons. There are hewn deep mortises in the top of each upright stone to fit in the lintel.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ha'amonga 'a Maui was built at the beginning of the 13th century under the 11th
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu%27i_Tonga" title="Tu'i Tonga">
       Tu
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       i Tonga
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Tu%27itatui" title="Tu'itatui">
       Tu
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       ittui
      </a>
      (king strike the knee), most likely as a gateway to his royal compound Heket. One can pass through the portal and walk the short distance towards the
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      esi maka faakinanga (stone to lean against), which served as the king's throne. Sitting with his back to that stone, he was safe from assassins from behind, and with his long stick he could hit every potential foe from the front on his knees.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2000" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg/240px-Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg/360px-Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg/480px-Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ha%CA%BBamonga.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Haamonga
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to the oral chronicles of Tonga nobleman, trilithon was built to symbolise the brotherhood of the sons of Tu
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      ittui - Lafa (eastern stone) and Talaihaapepe (western stone). The construction works involved large number of people and was done by transporting the stones on wooden slits and placing the stones with the help of earthen inclines and wooden constructions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wondermondo_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wondermondo-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In popular myths the Ha
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      amonga is believed to have been made by the demigod
      <a href="/wiki/Maui" title="Maui">
       Maui
      </a>
      , as the stones would be too huge for mortals to handle. The word ha
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      amonga means: a stick with loads on both ends, carried over the shoulder. Maui was supposed to have the stones obtained from
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      Uvea (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wallis_Island" title="Wallis Island">
       Wallis Island
      </a>
      ) and carried on to Tonga in a giant canoe. In reality the stones are of coral limestone, which structure matches that of old quarries along the neighbouring coasts.
     </p>
     <p>
      The resemblance of the trilithon with
      <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">
       Stonehenge
      </a>
      is often quoted, but Stonehenge has many more pillars and the lintels are on top of the sarsens, and not sunken down in a slit. Stonehenge may have been an archaeoastronomical observatory, as claimed by
      <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Hawkins" title="Gerald Hawkins">
       Gerald Hawkins
      </a>
      in 1963, now largely believed to have been overhyped. Nevertheless this was enough for the king of Tonga
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taufa%27ahau_Tupou_IV" title="Taufa'ahau Tupou IV">
       Tufa
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       hau Tupou IV
      </a>
      to come in 1967 with the theory that the Ha
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      amonga had an astronomical significance too, telling the position of sunrise at
      <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
       solstices
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
       equinoxes
      </a>
      . Because he was the king, this account is still quoted nowadays, although any evidence for it is imaginary.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maka_faakinanga.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Maka_faakinanga.jpg/230px-Maka_faakinanga.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Maka_faakinanga.jpg/345px-Maka_faakinanga.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Maka_faakinanga.jpg/460px-Maka_faakinanga.jpg 2x" width="230"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maka_faakinanga.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Maka faakinanga
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In fact there is only one 'proof': According to the roadsign, there is a V shaped mark on the top of the lintel, whose 2 legs point towards the solstice directions, and it is copied on the ground below. Refute: the V on top is an arrow directed along the main axis of the lintel (about ESE, 1175 E of N), only 10 cm long, too short to be a reliable indicator of any direction. The copy on the ground disappeared before 1990. It was rotated to point in the right directions, though, but with 6 cm size it was even smaller. Some people claim that another V mark was meant. Striations on the top of the stone would be the direction pointers. Refute: The northwest pier had clearly sagged over the centuries, rotating the lintel by about 5. Any mark supposedly pointing in the right direction now, could never have done so at construction. The striations only match the structure of the stone.
     </p>
     <p>
      A reference to a 19th-century manuscript from Elia Malup, corrected by the last
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu%27i_Tonga" title="Tu'i Tonga">
       Tu
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       i Tonga
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Laufilitonga" title="Laufilitonga">
       Laufilitonga
      </a>
      himself, is sometimes quoted:
      <i>
       tu
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       u
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       a e Ha
       <span class="unicode">
        
       </span>
       amonga ko e mtanga
      </i>
      (the Ha
      <span class="unicode">
       
      </span>
      amonga stands as an observatory). But the daily meaning of the word
      <i>
       mtanga
      </i>
      is, and always has been, a
      <i>
       (scenic) spot (to look at)
      </i>
      , not to look
      <i>
       from
      </i>
      .
     </p>
