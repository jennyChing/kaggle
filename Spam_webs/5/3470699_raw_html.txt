         <img alt="The 26 Best Places to Camp in the U.S." class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/The%2026%20Best%20Places%20to%20Camp%20in%20the%20U.S..png?itok=WXGEBCs7"/>

        <p>
         After spending the day wandering wooded paths, watching a family of deer leisurely grazing in a field of wild flowers, and dipping a toe or two in the frigid crystal clear creek, you curl up in a warm down sleeping bag next to a campfire to peer up at the glowing stars and enjoy a few (hundred) smores. Ahh, peace and quiet! You zip up into your tent for a few (mosquito-free) hours, and wake to the birds chirping and the faint hint of early morning sunlight.
         <em>
          This
         </em>
         is what camping is all about.
        </p>
        <p>
         While
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/know-you-go-camping">
          camping
         </a>
         itself is wonderful, getting there can sometimes be a challenge. For National Camping Week (and because summer is a perfect time to camp), weve done the digging for you. We've figured out where to camp, how much it costs, and what to look out for during your stay (we've also included
         <em>
          lots
         </em>
         of pretty pictures). Here are 26 stunningly gorgeous spots to head with your tent (and maybe a few good friends) for a few nights away from the hustle and bustle of everyday life. (Oh, we've also organized the list by location, so if you're looking for a specific state or region, scroll down until you see what you need!)
        </p>
        <div class="ym" data-yieldmo="ym_966591849689717803" data-yieldmo-tablet="ym_976628814724144286" id="ym_966591849689717803">
        </div>
        <h3>
         Northeast
        </h3>
        <h5>
         Acadia National Park, Maine
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Acadia National Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Acadia_604.jpg?itok=bUzZnkO5"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/common/uploads/photogallery/ner/park/acad/F8547448-155D-451F-67B54B78AD718DCF/F8547448-155D-451F-67B54B78AD718DCF-large.jpg">
          National Park Service
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         Maine is called 
         <a href="http://www.maine.gov/sos/kids/about/treefacts.htm">
          The Pine Tree State
         </a>
          for a reason: Its covered in
         <em>
          17 million
         </em>
         acres of forest. Plus, it has 6,000 lakes and ponds and 32,000 miles of rivers and streams. Basically, its a campers paradise. Located on Mount Desert Island, Acadia National Park makes the perfect camping destination for nature lovers of all skill levels. Looking for a unique experience?
         <a href="http://www.fodors.com/trip-ideas/national-parks/experience:123.html">
          Hike
         </a>
         to the top of Cadillac Mountain (the
         <a href="http://www.acadia.ws/cadillac-mountain.htm">
          highest point
         </a>
         along the east coast) just before sunrise and be the first person in the U.S. to say hello to Mr. Sun that morning.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         The park has
         <a href="file:///C:\%89%80%A2%09http\::www.nps.gov:acad:index.htm">
          two campgrounds
         </a>
         : Blackwoods (closer to the islands town center, Bar Harbor), and Seawall (which offers a more rustic, less touristy environment). While visitors can enjoy hiking throughout the entire park, camping is only allowed in these designated areas (backcountry enthusiasts, take note).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         Bears and coyotes are sighted on a semi-regular basis, while moose and bobcats are rare  but occasional  camp-mates.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         Park open year round. Most facilities and campgrounds close in the off-season, but Blackwoods campground is open year-round (permit required December  March).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         $20 per vehicle entrance fee, valid for seven-days, from May through October. Annual passes available for $40. Campsite prices range from $10 to $20 depending on site and time of year. For more information, visit
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/acad">
          www.nps.gov/acad
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h5>
         White Mountain National Forest, New Hampshire
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="White Mountains National Forest" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/White%20Mountains_604.jpg?itok=Qw7mY3x0"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/susancolekelly/8075419987/">
          Susan Cole Kelly
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         If looking for a more rustic experience in the Northeast, the White Mountains are your best bet. The hikings pretty rugged in this section of the Appalachian range, but its worth it if youre up for the challenge. The sights here are particularly stunning in the fall, when the foliage turns to all shades of red, orange, and yellow.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         While the forest does have 24 drive-in campgrounds (with a combined 800 campsites  wowza!), the eight walk-in state park campgrounds in the northern part of the state are really what campings all about. Some campsites require reservations; some dont. Backcountry tent camping is also allowed (except in noted
         <a href="http://www.fs.usda.gov/Internet/FSE_DOCUMENTS/stelprdb5363715.pdf">
          no-camping areas
         </a>
         ); there are also log lean-tos scattered throughout the forest (a small fee may apply).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         The occasional moose and possibly a bear or two.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         Forest accessible year round. Visitor center hours vary.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         No entrance fee. Campsites vary from $16 to $22 per night. Backcountry tent camping is free, no permit required. Parking at a trailhead may require a permit; check signage at your chosen lot. For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.fs.usda.gov/whitemountain">
          www.fs.usda.gov/whitemountain
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h5>
         Green Mountain National Forest, Vermont
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Green Mountains National Forest" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Green%20Mountains_604.jpg?itok=-sy9ushb"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/79666107@N00/6306277194/">
          Chris M Morris
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         The famous Long Trail is one of the biggest draws to the Green Mountain State, so try finding a camping spot close by to enjoy hiking a portion of the trail during your stay. Aside from being absolutely gorgeous, the trail is the
         <a href="http://www.greenmountainclub.org/page.php?id=2">
          oldest long-distance trail
         </a>
         in the U.S.! It follows the ridge of the Green Mountains through Vermont from the Massachusetts border to Canada.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         <a href="http://www.fs.fed.us/r9/forests/greenmountain/htm/greenmountain/g_camping.htm">
          The forest
         </a>
         offers five developed campgrounds. There are no electrical hookups or dump stations, so arrive prepared. Campground accessibility varies
         <a href="http://www.fs.usda.gov/activity/greenmountain/recreation/camping-cabins/?recid=64897&amp;actid=29">
          by season
         </a>
         .
         <a href="http://www.fs.usda.gov/activity/greenmountain/recreation/camping-cabins/?recid=64897&amp;actid=34">
          Dispersed
         </a>
         or backcountry camping is allowed anywhere in the park unless specifically posted.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         The forest is made up of a mix of private, state, and federal land, so the rules can change from one trail to the next. Figure out where youll be heading before you leave, and make sure you know the deal for that specific area.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         Park accessible year round. Visitor center and campground accessibility vary by season, but one campground is always open all year.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         Heres the best part: No entrance fees, and most of the campsites are free, too.
         <a href="http://www.greenmountainclub.org/page.php?id=54">
          The Green Mountain Club
         </a>
         maintains about 70 campsites along The Long Trail, all with a water source and privy, for which GMC caretakers will come by to charge a small fee during the summer and fall. For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.fs.usda.gov/greenmountain">
          www.fs.usda.gov/greenmountain
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h3>
         Mid-Atlantic
        </h3>
        <h5>
         Pine Grove Furnace State Park, Pennsylvania
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Pine Grove Furnace State Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Pine%20Grove%20Furnace_604.jpg?itok=5o_Xi8Ib"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/kmoliver/8132940094/">
          Kevin Oliver
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         Located in south-central PA, this scenic park sits at the northern tip of the Blue Ridge Mountains in an area known as South Mountain (confusing, we know).
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/we-did-it-appalachian-trail" style="line-height: 1.5em;">
          The Appalachian Trail
         </a>
         , perhaps the most famous foot trail in the world, runs through the forest, which is home to the trails halfway point. While only 2,000 people attempt to hike the whole 2,186-mile trail each year (about a quarter actually finish), between two and three million people hike or walk a portion of it.  Whether you do two miles or 20 miles, its still cool to say youve done it! Have some time after the hike? Check out the
         <a href="http://www.atmuseum.org/intro.htm" style="line-height: 1.5em;">
          Appalachian Trail Museum
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         The forest has a mix of
         <a href="http://www.dcnr.state.pa.us/stateparks/findapark/pinegrovefurnace/index.htm#camping">
          70 tent and trailer sites
         </a>
         (mostly rustic) available from late March to mid-December. Reservations can be made up to 11 months in advance. Backpacking and overnight hikes are not permitted.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         Pretty safe!
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         Park open year round.
         <a href="http://www.dcnr.state.pa.us/cs/groups/public/documents/document/dcnr_003375.pdf">
          Campgrounds open
         </a>
         from April through December.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         No entrance fee. Campsites range from $4$5 per person, or $15$17 per night. For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.dcnr.state.pa.us/stateparks/findapark/pinegrovefurnace/">
          www.dcnr.state.pa.us/stateparks
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h5>
         Assateague Island National Seashore, Maryland
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Assateague National Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Assateague_604.jpg?itok=t_hNACPf"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/assateaguenps/9008649425/in/set-72157632182106431">
          AssateagueNPS
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         If you love beaches, and you love camping, then this is the spot for you. Assateague is a barrier island off the coast of Maryland and Virginia thats covered in sandy beaches, salt marches, forests, and costal bays. Theres even a community of
         <em>
          wild horses
         </em>
         (how exotic!). Enjoy relaxing on the 37 miles of beach or
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/asis/planyourvisit/hiking.htm">
          hiking
         </a>
         by day, and buckle down your tent right by (err... a safe distance from) the crashing waves for a night under the stars.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/asis/planyourvisit/marylandcamping.htm">
          Camping
         </a>
         is only allowed on the Maryland side of the island. There are two oceanside and four bayside camping areas available. October 16 through April 14, the sites are first-come first-served. Two campsites are also open for horse camping during this time. April 15 through October 15,
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/asis/planyourvisit/marylandcamping.htm">
          reservations
         </a>
         can be made up to 6 months in advance. Backcountry camping is allowed ($5 permit required), but it's only accessible by backpacking or water.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         Nothing too dangerous here  just stay away during hurricane season. Oh, and its not a great idea to approach the wild horses.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/asis/planyourvisit/hours.htm">
          Open year round
         </a>
         ; visitor center and ranger station hours fluctuate from season to season.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         $15 per vehicle
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/asis/planyourvisit/feesandreservations.htm">
          entrance fee
         </a>
         , valid for seven-days. Annual passes also available for $30. Campsites range from $20 to $30 per night depending on season and location. For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/asis">
          www.nps.gov/asis
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h3>
         Pacific Coast
        </h3>
        <h5>
         Yosemite National Park, California
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Yosemite National Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Yosemite_604.jpg?itok=9YBfzm26"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/19942094@N00/6358356807/" style="line-height: 1.5em;">
          Randy Lemoine
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         Nearly 95 percent of the park is designated wilderness  that means no cars, no structures, no roads, and no electricity. After a night spent under the stars,
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/trailheads.htm">
          take a hike
         </a>
         up to
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/glacierpoint.htm">
          Glacier Point
         </a>
         , which overlooks the parks famous Yosemite Valley, Half Dome (a rock structure revered among climbers), and the High Sierra peaks. The hike on Four Mile Trail from Yosemite Valley to the top of Glacier Point takes about 3-4 hours each way. If youre looking for something a bit tougher, the Panorama Trail is about twice as long.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         There are
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/campground.htm">
          13 popular campgrounds
         </a>
         scattered throughout the park, and those requiring reservations are usually full from about April  September. If you
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/nrcamping.htm">
          dont have a reservation
         </a>
         for summertime camping, there are seven campgrounds that operate on a first-come first-served basis. Backcountry camping is also allowed, but requires a free wilderness permit (which can be reserved ahead of time). Reservations are recommended.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         Black bears are common  follow appropriate
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/bearcans.htm">
          food storage rules
         </a>
         !
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         Park open year round. Campgrounds vary by season.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         $20 per vehicle entrance fee, valid for seven days. Campsites range from $5 to $20 per night.
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/wildpermits.htm">
          Wilderness permits
         </a>
         are free and required for backcountry camping  there is a $5 per reservation plus $5 per person fee to reserve permits ahead of time. For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/yose">
          www.nps.gov/yose
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h5>
         Joshua Tree National Park, California
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Joshua Tree National Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Joshua%20Tree_604.jpg?itok=Iu5xWRma"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/cityprojectca/5989992928/">
          The City Project
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         We know  camping in the desert doesnt sound like so much fun (hello, sunburn). But the famous Joshua Tree National Park is
         <em>
          oh
         </em>
         so much more than just desert. The park actually sits at the intersection of two very different deserts: To the east is the low-lying Colorado Desert; to the west lies the slightly higher, cooler, wetter
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/jotr/naturescience/mojave.htm">
          Mojave Desert
         </a>
         (home to the parks namesake, the
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/jotr/naturescience/jtrees.htm">
          Joshua tree
         </a>
         ). In addition to the deserts, the park also has ten mountain peaks higher than 5,000 feet in elevation. Need to get vertical? Joshua Tree is a popular
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/jotr/planyourvisit/climbing.htm">
          rock-climbing
         </a>
         destination. (Just be sure you
         <a href="http://greatist.com/fitness/know-you-go-rock-climbing">
          know what youre doing

         Denali National Park, Alaska
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Denali National Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Denali_604.jpg?itok=wSYoDluw"/>
         </span>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/circlek/236328423/">
          circlek
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         <a href="file:///C:\%89%80%A2%09http\::travel.nationalgeographic.com:travel:national-parks:denali-national-park:">
          Six million acres
         </a>
         of open land? Check. Unbelievable wildlife? Check. Hiking to please even the most experienced of outdoorspeople? Check. Basically, it doesnt get cooler than Denali. The central draw to the park (especially
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/planyourvisit/mountaineering.htm">
          for mountaineers
         </a>
         ) is Denali itself  otherwise known as
         <a href="http://traveltips.usatoday.com/denali-national-park-5211.html">
          Mt. McKinley
         </a>
         , or the great one. The park offers
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/planyourvisit/dayhiking.htm">
          hikes
         </a>
         for pros and beginners alike. Most trails start near the visitor center and are considered easy to moderate in difficulty. A few trails start deeper in the park, beyond the first three miles of the access road.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         The park has six
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/planyourvisit/campgrounds.htm">
          established campgrounds
         </a>
         with a combined 291 sites, and also allows backcountry camping with a (free) permit.
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/planyourvisit/campground-riley.htm">
          Riley Creek
         </a>
         is the only campground reachable by car, and requires a minimum 3-night stay to reduce car traffic. The other two sites are only reachable by bus. One campground is also open year-round, and no fees are charged in winter. Reservations can be made starting December 1st for the following summer. Do your
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/planyourvisit/backcountry.htm">
          research
         </a>
         before embarking on a backcountry camping trip to Denali  this trip is not for the inexperienced.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/frequently-asked-questions-regarding-bears.htm">
          Black and grizzly bears.
         </a>
         Lots of them. Also, no fires allowed, so plan ahead.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         It really all depends on the weather in a given year. Generally, the park is open to private vehicles starting in mid-April. Summer bus service begins May 20 and operates through the second Thursday after Labor Day each year (September 12 this year). The park sees the most visitors between mid-June and mid-August.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         $10
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena/planyourvisit/feesandreservations.htm">
          entrance fee
         </a>
         per person, valid for seven days. Annual and national passes are also available and accepted. For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/dena">
          www.nps.gov/dena
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <h5>
         Glacier Bay National Park, Alaska
        </h5>
        <div class="caption-full">
         <span class="media media-element-container media-g_full_width_inline_image">
          <img alt="Glacier Bay National Park" class="media-element file-g-full-width-inline-image" src="http://greatist.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_main/public/Glacier%20Bay_604.jpg?itok=JOWBU6Yl"/>
         </span>
         Photo: National Park Service
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why Its Cool:
         </strong>
         Glacier Bay National Park is actually
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/cruising.htm">
          mostly water
         </a>
         , the bay itself serving as the passageway to the inner section of the park  which is (awesomely enough) a glacier. After spending the night under the stars, try cruising the bay on a tour, charter, or private boat. There arent any marked trails in the park, so backpacking is more strenuous here than elsewhere.
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/rafting.htm">
          Rafting
         </a>
         one of the parks two rivers is a great alternative that allows campers to easily tow supplies  but make sure youre with someone who knows what theyre doing.
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/join-a-park-ranger.htm">
          Park rangers
         </a>
         also lead a variety of tours and talks every day during the summer.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where to Camp:
         </strong>
         The park has only one
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/camp.htm">
          campground
         </a>
         , in Bartlet Cove, which has outhouses, a warming shelter, and safe food storage. Camping permits are required for campgrounds and backcountry from May 1 through September 30. To receive the permit (and be issued a bear-resistant food container!), all campers must attend a short orientation at the visitor center before heading out. Permits are free.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch Out For:
         </strong>
         Black and brown/grizzly bears. A note on water
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/safety.htm">
          safety
         </a>
         : It may look safe, but it often isnt. Filter your water first so you dont regret it later. Also, be on the lookout for
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/moose-safety.htm">
          moose
         </a>
         !
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          When Its Open:
         </strong>
         Park is open year round, but accessibility and services are very limited in winter. Visitor center is open from late May through early September.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Cost:
         </strong>
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/feesandreservations.htm">
          No entrance fees for private visitors
         </a>
         ! Reservations are required for boating, camping, rafting, and other visitor services. Campground and wilderness reservations are not accepted or necessary, but a
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/permits.htm">
          free permit
         </a>
         is required. For rafting, fees, permits, and reservations. Boaters need permits during peak season.
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba/planyourvisit/camp.htm">
          Camping permits are free.
         </a>
         For more information visit
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/glba">
          www.nps.gov/glba
         </a>
         .
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         So which park or forest strikes your fancy? If its one of the national parks listed (or if you want to visit more than one national park this year) there are a few things to consider.
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/findapark/passes.htm">
          Annual, national passes
         </a>
         are available and accepted at all national parks. There are also discounted passes for volunteers, seniors, and military service men and women. The national parks also have a few select
         <a href="http://www.nps.gov/findapark/feefreeparks.htm">
          free-admission days
         </a>
         throughout the year. In 2013, those days are: Martin Luther King Jr. Day (January 21), National Park Week (April 22-26), National Park Service Birthday (August 25), National Public Lands Day (September 28), and Veterans Day Weekend (November 9-11). Happy camping!
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Note:
         </em>
         <em>
          All cost and camping information is for individual, private, small groups  none for larger private or commercial groups. Those rules vary by park and more information is available on each parks website.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Have you had the chance to visit any of these parks? Ever camped there? Share with us in the comments below or tweet the author
         </em>
         <a href="http://www.twitter.com/ksmorin">
          <em>
           @ksmorin
          </em>
         </a>
         <em>
          to tell us about your experience!
         </em>
        </p>
