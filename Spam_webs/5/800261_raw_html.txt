     <p>
      <b>
       Jean-Luc Godard
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       French:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">
        [lyk da]
       </a>
      </span>
      ; born 3 December 1930) is a French-Swiss film director, screenwriter and
      <a href="/wiki/Film_criticism" title="Film criticism">
       film critic
      </a>
      . He is often identified with the 1960s French film movement
      <i>
       La Nouvelle Vague
      </i>
      , or "
      <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">
       New Wave
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._235_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._235-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Like his New Wave contemporaries, Godard criticized mainstream French cinema's "Tradition of Quality",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._235_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._235-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which "emphasized craft over innovation, privileged established directors over new directors, and preferred the great works of the past to experimentation."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._2.2C_p._259_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._2.2C_p._259-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To challenge this tradition, he and like-minded critics started to make their own films.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._235_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._235-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many of Godard's films challenge the conventions of traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">
       Hollywood
      </a>
      in addition to French cinema.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is often considered the most
      <a href="/wiki/Radicalization" title="Radicalization">
       radical
      </a>
      French filmmaker of the 1960s and 1970s;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._126_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._126-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      his approach in film conventions, politics and philosophies made him arguably the most influential director of the French New Wave. Along with showing knowledge of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Film" title="History of Film">
       film history
      </a>
      through homages and references, several of his films expressed his political views; he was an avid reader of
      <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">
       existential
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">
       Marxist philosophy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._126_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._126-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the New Wave, his politics have been much less radical and his recent films are about
      <a href="/wiki/Representation_(arts)" title="Representation (arts)">
       representation
      </a>
      and human conflict from a
      <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">
       humanist
      </a>
      , and a Marxist perspective.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._126_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._126-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a 2002
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">
        Sight &amp; Sound
       </a>
      </i>
      poll, Godard ranked third in the critics' top-ten directors of all time (which was put together by assembling the directors of the individual films for which the critics voted).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is said to have "created one of the largest bodies of critical analysis of any filmmaker since the mid-twentieth century."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._238_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._238-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He and his work have been central to
      <a href="/wiki/Narratology" title="Narratology">
       narrative theory
      </a>
      and have "challenged both commercial narrative cinema norms and film criticism's vocabulary."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._202_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._4.2C_p._202-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2010, Godard was awarded an
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Honorary_Award" title="Academy Honorary Award">
       Academy Honorary Award
      </a>
      , but did not attend the award ceremony.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Godard's films have inspired many directors including
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">
       Martin Scorsese
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">
       Quentin Tarantino
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Soderbergh" title="Steven Soderbergh">
       Steven Soderbergh
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/D._A._Pennebaker" title="D. A. Pennebaker">
       D. A. Pennebaker
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Altman" title="Robert Altman">
       Robert Altman
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jarmusch" title="Jim Jarmusch">
       Jim Jarmusch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wong_Kar-wai" title="Wong Kar-wai">
       Wong Kar-wai
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wim_Wenders" title="Wim Wenders">
       Wim Wenders
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfibio_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bfibio-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Bertolucci" title="Bernardo Bertolucci">
       Bernardo Bertolucci
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._3.2C_p._49_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._3.2C_p._49-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pier_Paolo_Pasolini" title="Pier Paolo Pasolini">
       Pier Paolo Pasolini
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._3.2C_p._49_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2007Vol._3.2C_p._49-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Jean-Luc Godard was born on 3 December 1930
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moullet_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-moullet-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/7th_arrondissement_of_Paris" title="7th arrondissement of Paris">
       7th arrondissement of Paris
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the son of Odile (ne Monod) and Paul Godard, a Swiss physician.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorrey2005.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DKby7RaRmTt4C.26pg.3DPA1_p._1.5D_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorrey2005.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DKby7RaRmTt4C.26pg.3DPA1_p._1.5D-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His wealthy parents came from Protestant families of
      <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">
       Franco
      </a>
      
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_(people)" title="Swiss (people)">
       Swiss
      </a>
      descent, and his mother was the daughter of Julien Monod, a founder of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banque_Paribas" title="Banque Paribas">
       Banque Paribas
      </a>
      . She was the great-granddaughter of theologian
      <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Monod" title="Adolphe Monod">
       Adolphe Monod
      </a>
      . Relatives on his mother's side include also composer
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_Monod" title="Jacques-Louis Monod">
       Jacques-Louis Monod
      </a>
      , naturalist
      <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_Monod" title="Thodore Monod">
       Thodore Monod
      </a>
      and pastor
      <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Monod" title="Frdric Monod">
       Frdric Monod
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Four years after Jean-Luc's birth, his father moved the family to Switzerland. At the outbreak of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
       Second World War
      </a>
      , Godard was in France and returned to Switzerland with difficulty. He spent most of the war in Switzerland, although his family made clandestine trips to his grandfather's estate on the French side of
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">
       Lake Geneva
      </a>
      . Godard attended school in
      <a href="/wiki/Nyon" title="Nyon">
       Nyon
      </a>
      , Switzerland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Not a frequent cinema-goer, he attributed his introduction to cinema to a reading of Malraux's essay
      <i>
       Outline of a Psychology of Cinema
      </i>
      , and his reading of
      <i>
       La Revue du cinma
      </i>
      , which was relaunched in 1946.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1946, he went to study at the Lyce Buffon in Paris and, through family connections, mixed with members of its cultural elite. He lodged with the writer
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Schlumberger" title="Jean Schlumberger">
       Jean Schlumberger
      </a>
      . Having failed his baccalaureate exam in 1948 he returned to Switzerland. He studied in
      <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">
       Lausanne
      </a>
      and lived with his parents, whose marriage was breaking up. He spent time in Geneva also with a group that included another film fanatic, Roland Tolmatchoff, and the extreme rightist philosopher
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Parvulesco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Parvulesco (page does not exist)">
       Jean Parvulesco
      </a>
      . His older sister Rachel encouraged him to paint, which he did, in an abstract style. After time spent at a boarding school in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thonon" title="Thonon">
       Thonon
      </a>
      to prepare for the retest, which he passed, he returned to Paris in 1949.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He registered for a certificate in
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropology
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">
       University of Paris
      </a>
      (Sorbonne), but did not attend class.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacCabe2005.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DFwLqbp0cEp8C.26pg.3DPA37_p._36.5D_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacCabe2005.5Bhttp:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DFwLqbp0cEp8C.26pg.3DPA37_p._36.5D-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He got involved with the young group of film critics at the
      <a href="/wiki/Film_society#France" title="Film society">
       cin-clubs
      </a>
      that started the New Wave. Godard originally held only French citizenship, then in 1953, he became a citizen of
      <a href="/wiki/Gland,_Switzerland" title="Gland, Switzerland">
       Gland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">
       canton
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">
       Vaud
      </a>
      , Switzerland, possibly through simplified naturalisation through his Swiss father.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career_.281950.E2.80.9359.29">
       Early career (195059)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career (195059)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Film_criticism">
       Film criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Paris, in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quartier_Latin" title="Quartier Latin">
       Latin Quarter
      </a>
      just prior to 1950,
      <i>
       cin-clubs
      </i>
      (film societies) were gaining prominence. Godard began attending these clubsthe Cinmathque, the CCQL, Work and Culture cin Club, and others, became his regular haunts. The Cinmathque had been founded by
      <a href="/wiki/Henri_Langlois" title="Henri Langlois">
       Henri Langlois
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_Franju" title="Georges Franju">
       Georges Franju
      </a>
      in 1936; Work and Culture was a workers' education group for which
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Bazin" title="Andr Bazin">
       Andr Bazin
      </a>
      had organized wartime film screenings and discussions and which had become a model for the film clubs that had risen throughout France after the Liberation; Cin-Club du Quartier Latin (CCQL), founded 1947-48, was animated and intellectually led by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurice_Sch%C3%A9rer" title="Maurice Schrer">
       Maurice Schrer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At these clubs he met fellow film enthusiasts including
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Rivette" title="Jacques Rivette">
       Jacques Rivette
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chabrol" title="Claude Chabrol">
       Claude Chabrol
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="Franois Truffaut">
       Franois Truffaut
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Godard was part of a generation for whom cinema took on a special importance. He has said: "In the 1950s cinema was as important as breadbut it isn't the case any more. We thought cinema would assert itself as an instrument of knowledge, a microscope... a telescope.... At the
      <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9math%C3%A8que_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Cinmathque Franaise">
       Cinmathque
      </a>
      I discovered a world which nobody had spoken to me about. They'd told us about Goethe, but not
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Theodor_Dreyer" title="Carl Theodor Dreyer">
       Dreyer
      </a>
      . ... We watched silent films in the era of talkies. We dreamed about film. We were like Christians in the catacombs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His foray into films began in the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Film_criticism" title="Film criticism">
       criticism
      </a>
      . Along with Maurice Schrer (writing under the to-be-famous pseudonym ric Rohmer) and Rivette, he founded the short-lived film journal
      <i>
       Gazette du cinma
      </i>
      , which saw publication of five issues in 1950.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When Bazin co-founded the influential critical magazine
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cahiers_du_cin%C3%A9ma" title="Cahiers du cinma">
        Cahiers du cinma
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1951, Godard was the first of the younger critics from the CCQL/Cinmathque group to be publishedthe January 1952 issue featured his review of an American melodrama directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Mat%C3%A9" title="Rudolph Mat">
       Rudolph Mat
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/No_Sad_Songs_for_Me" title="No Sad Songs for Me">
        No Sad Songs for Me
       </a>
      </i>
      . His "Defence and Illustration of Classical Dcoupage" published in September 1952, in which he attacks an earlier article by Bazin and defends the use of the shot-reverse shot technique, is one of his earliest important contributions to cinema.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Praising
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Preminger" title="Otto Preminger">
       Otto Preminger
      </a>
      and, "the greatest American artist
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hawks" title="Howard Hawks">
       Howard Hawks
      </a>
      ", Godard raises their harsh melodramas above the more "formalistic and overtly artful films of
      <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">
       Welles
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_De_Sica" title="Vittorio De Sica">
       De Sica
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/William_Wyler" title="William Wyler">
       Wyler
      </a>
      which Bazin endorsed""
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At this point Godard's activities did not include making filmsrather he watched films, and wrote about them, and helped others make films, notably Rohmer, with whom he worked on
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pr%C3%A9sentation_ou_Charlotte_et_son_steak" title="fr:Prsentation ou Charlotte et son steak">
        w:fr:Prsentation ou Charlotte et son steak
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Filmmaking">
       Filmmaking
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filmmaking">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Having left Paris in the autumn of 1952, Godard returned to Switzerland and went to live with his mother in Lausanne. He became friendly with his mother's lover, Jean-Pierre Laubscher, who was a labourer on the
      <a href="/wiki/Grande_Dixence_Dam" title="Grande Dixence Dam">
       Grande Dixence Dam
      </a>
      . Through Laubscher he secured work himself as a construction worker at the Plaz Fleuri work site at the dam. He saw the possibility of making a documentary film about the dam and when his initial contract ended, in order to prolong his time at the dam, moved to the post of telephone switchboard operator. It was whilst on duty, in April 1954, that he put through a call to Laubscher that relayed the fact that Odile Monod, his mother, had died in a scooter accident. Thanks to Swiss friends who lent him a 35mm movie camera, he was able to shoot on 35mm film. He rewrote the commentary that Laubscher had written, and gave his film a rhyming title
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ration_b%C3%A9ton" title="Opration bton">
        Opration bton
       </a>
      </i>
      (Operation concrete). The company that administered the dam bought the film and used it for publicity purposes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As he continued to work for
      <i>
       Cahiers
      </i>
      , he made
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Une_femme_coquette" title="Une femme coquette">
        Une femme coquette
       </a>
      </i>
      (1955), in
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">
       Geneva
      </a>
      , a ten-minute short; and in January 1956 he returned to Paris. A plan for a feature film of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">
       Goethe
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Elective_Affinities" title="Elective Affinities">
        Elective Affinities
       </a>
      </i>
      proved too ambitious and came to nothing. Truffaut enlisted his help to work on an idea he had for a film based on the true-crime story of a petty criminal, Michel Portail, who had shot a motorcycle policeman and whose girlfriend had turned him in to the police. But Truffaut failed to interest any producers. Another project with Truffaut, a comedy about a country girl arriving in Paris, was also abandoned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He worked with Rohmer on a planned series of short films centering around the lives of two young women, Charlotte and Vronique; and in the autumn of 1957,
      <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Braunberger" title="Pierre Braunberger">
       Pierre Braunberger
      </a>
      produced the first film in the series,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tous_les_gar%C3%A7ons_s%27appellent_Patrick" title="Tous les garons s'appellent Patrick">
        All the Boys Are Named Patrick
       </a>
      </i>
      , directed by Godard from Rohmer's script.
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Une_histoire_d%27eau" title="Une histoire d'eau">
        Une histoire d'eau
       </a>
      </i>
      (1958) was created largely out of unused footage shot by Truffaut. In 1958, Godard, with a cast that included
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Belmondo" title="Jean-Paul Belmondo">
       Jean-Paul Belmondo
      </a>
      and Anne Colette, made his last short before gaining international prominence as a filmmaker,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlotte_et_son_Jules" title="Charlotte et son Jules">
        Charlotte et son Jules
       </a>
      </i>
      , an homage to
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">
       Jean Cocteau
      </a>
      . The film was shot in Godard's hotel room on the rue de Rennes and apparently reflected something of the 'romantic austerity' of Godard's own life at this time. His Swiss friend Roland Tolmatchoff noted; "In Paris he had a big
      <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Bogart" title="Humphrey Bogart">
       Bogart
      </a>
      poster on the wall and nothing else."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In December 1958, Godard reported from the Festival of Short Films in
      <a href="/wiki/Tours" title="Tours">
       Tours
      </a>
      and praised the work of, and became friends with,
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Demy" title="Jacques Demy">
       Jacques Demy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Rozier" title="Jacques Rozier">
       Jacques Rozier
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Agn%C3%A8s_Varda" title="Agns Varda">
       Agns Varda
      </a>
      he already knew
      <a href="/wiki/Alain_Resnais" title="Alain Resnais">
       Alain Resnais
      </a>
      whose entry he also praisedbut Godard now wanted to make a feature film. He travelled to the
      <a href="/wiki/1959_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1959 Cannes Film Festival">
       1959 Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      and asked Truffaut to let him use the story on which they had collaborated in 1956, about the car thief Michel Portail. He sought money from the producer
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_de_Beauregard" title="Georges de Beauregard">
       Georges de Beauregard
      </a>
      whom he had met previously whilst working briefly in the publicity department of Twentieth Century Fox's Paris office, and who was also at the Festival. Beauregard could offer his expertise, but was in debt from two productions based on
      <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Loti" title="Pierre Loti">
       Pierre Loti
      </a>
      stories and so finance came rather from a film distributor, Ren Pignires.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cinematic_period_.281960.E2.80.9368.29">
       Cinematic period (196068)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cinematic period (196068)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Godard's most celebrated period as a director spans roughly from his first feature,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Breathless_(1960_film)" title="Breathless (1960 film)">
        Breathless
       </a>
      </i>
      (1960), through to
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Week_End_(1967_film)" title="Week End (1967 film)">
        Week End
       </a>
      </i>
      (1967). His work during this period focused on relatively conventional films that often refer to different aspects of film history. Although Godard's work during this time is considered groundbreaking in its own right, the period stands in contrast to that which immediately followed it, during which Godard ideologically denounced much of cinema's history as "bourgeois" and therefore without merit.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Films">
       Films
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Films">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Godard's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Breathless_(1960_film)" title="Breathless (1960 film)">
        Breathless
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
        bout de souffle
      </i>
      , 1960), starring
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Belmondo" title="Jean-Paul Belmondo">
       Jean-Paul Belmondo
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Seberg" title="Jean Seberg">
       Jean Seberg
      </a>
      distinctly expressed the
      <a href="/wiki/French_New_Wave" title="French New Wave">
       French New Wave
      </a>
      's style, and incorporated quotations from several elements of popular culturespecifically
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_cinema" title="American cinema">
       American
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">
       film noir
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film employed various innovative techniques such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jump_cuts" title="Jump cuts">
       jump cuts
      </a>
      traditionally considered amateurish
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      
      <a href="/wiki/Aside" title="Aside">
       character asides
      </a>
      and breaking the
      <a href="/wiki/Eyeline_match" title="Eyeline match">
       eyeline match
      </a>
      rule in
      <a href="/wiki/Continuity_editing" title="Continuity editing">
       continuity editing
      </a>
      . Truffaut co-wrote
      <i>
       Breathless
      </i>
      with Godard.
     </p>
     <p>
      Godard viewed film-making as an extension of criticism and was more interested in redefining film structure and style than actually being understood by the public. Often his movies were more about the presentation of a story than anything else. The stories in his films were very simple yet unfocused and constantly digressing from the main story line (Jean-Luc Godard and Vivre Sa Vie by Tom Milne, 1962).
     </p>
     <p>
      From the beginning of his career, Godard included more film references into his movies than did any of his New Wave colleagues. In
      <i>
       Breathless
      </i>
      , his citations include a movie poster showing
      <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Bogart" title="Humphrey Bogart">
       Humphrey Bogart
      </a>
      , -from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Harder_They_Fall" title="The Harder They Fall">
        The Harder They Fall
       </a>
      </i>
      , his last film-,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (whose expression the lead actor Jean-Paul Belmondo tries reverently to imitate); visual quotations from films of
      <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">
       Ingmar Bergman
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Fuller" title="Samuel Fuller">
       Samuel Fuller
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">
       Fritz Lang
      </a>
      , and others; and an onscreen dedication to
      <a href="/wiki/Monogram_Pictures" title="Monogram Pictures">
       Monogram Pictures
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      an American
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-movie" title="B-movie">
       B-movie
      </a>
      studio. Quotations from, and references to literature include
      <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">
       William Faulkner
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">
       Dylan Thomas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Aragon" title="Louis Aragon">
       Louis Aragon
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rilke" title="Rilke">
       Rilke
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Sagan" title="Franoise Sagan">
       Franoise Sagan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Sachs" title="Maurice Sachs">
       Maurice Sachs
      </a>
      . The film also contains citations in images or on the soundtrack
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">
       Mozart
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">
       Picasso
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">
       J.S. Bach
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">
       Paul Klee
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auguste_Renoir" title="Auguste Renoir">
       Auguste Renoir
      </a>
      . "This first-person cinema invoked not the director's experience but his presence".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If, in Rohmer's words, "life was the cinema", then a film filled with movie references was supremely autobiographical.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bresson.jpg">

        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bresson" title="Robert Bresson">
         Robert Bresson
        </a>
        (above). Godard told a journalist, "I really made it [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Petit_Soldat" title="Le Petit Soldat">
          Le Petit Soldat
         </a>
        </i>
        ] under the influence of Bresson and of Malraux." Bresson's 1959 film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Pickpocket_(film)" title="Pickpocket (film)">
          Pickpocket
         </a>
        </i>
        was a model for Godard and had "an effect on Godard's work that was profound and enduring".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
         <a href="#cite_note-39">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          39
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Godard wanted to hire the American actress Jean Seberg, who was living in Paris with her husband Franois Moreuil, an attorney, to play the American woman. Seberg had become famous in 1956 when
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Preminger" title="Otto Preminger">
       Otto Preminger
      </a>
      had chosen her to play
      <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_Arc" title="Joan of Arc">
       Joan of Arc
      </a>
      in his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joan_(film)" title="Saint Joan (film)">
        Saint Joan
       </a>
      </i>
      , and had then cast her in his acidulous 1958 adaptation of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bonjour_Tristesse_(film)" title="Bonjour Tristesse (film)">
        Bonjour Tristesse
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Her performance in this film had not been generally regarded as a successthe
      <i>
       New York Times
      </i>
      critic called her a "misplaced amateur"but Truffaut and Godard disagreed. In the role of Michel Poiccard, Godard cast Belmondo, an actor he had already called, writing in
      <i>
       Arts
      </i>
      in 1958, "the
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Simon" title="Michel Simon">
       Michel Simon
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Jules_Berry" title="Jules Berry">
       Jules Berry
      </a>
      of tomorrow."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cameraman was
      <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Coutard" title="Raoul Coutard">
       Raoul Coutard
      </a>
      , the producer Beauregard's choice. Godard wanted
      <i>
       Breathless
      </i>
      to be shot like a documentary, with a lightweight handheld camera and a minimum of added lighting and Coutard had had experience as a documentary cameraman while working for the French army's information service in Indochina during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French-Indochina_War" title="French-Indochina War">
       French-Indochina War
      </a>
      . Tracking shots were filmed by Coutard from a wheelchair pushed by Godard. Though he had prepared a traditional screenplay, he dispensed with it and Godard wrote the dialogue day by day as the production went ahead.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film's importance was recognized immediately and in January 1960, Godard won the
      <a href="/wiki/Prix_Jean_Vigo" title="Prix Jean Vigo">
       Jean Vigo Prize
      </a>
      , awarded " to encourage an
      <a href="/wiki/Auteur_theory" title="Auteur theory">
       auteur
      </a>
      of the future". One reviewer mentioned
      <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Astruc" title="Alexandre Astruc">
       Alexandre Astruc
      </a>
      's prophecy of the age of the
      <i>
       camra-stylo
      </i>
      , the camera that a new generation would use with the efficacy with which a writer uses his pen"here is in fact the first work authentically written with a
      <i>
       camra-stylo
      </i>
      <span style="padding-left:.2em;">
       "
      </span>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anna_karina2.jpg">

       </a>

        <div class="magnify">

        </a>
        having rejected a role in
        <i>
         Breathless
        </i>
        she appeared in Godard's next film
        <i>
         Le Petit Soldat
        </i>
        "it will be something about torture" Godard told
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/France-Observateur" title="France-Observateur">
          France-Observateur
         </a>
        </i>
        and concerned France's war in Algeria
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The following year Godard made
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Petit_Soldat" title="Le Petit Soldat">
        Le Petit Soldat
       </a>
      </i>
      (The Little Soldier), filmed on location in
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">
       Geneva
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and dealing with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algerian_War_of_Independence" title="Algerian War of Independence">
       Algerian War of Independence
      </a>
      . The film begins on 13 May 1958, the date of the
      <a href="/wiki/May_1958_crisis" title="May 1958 crisis">
       attempted putsch in Algeria
      </a>
      , and ends later the same month. In the film, Bruno Forestier a photojournalist who has links with a right wing paramilitary group working for the French government, is ordered to murder a professor accused of aiding the Algerian resistance. He is in love with Veronica Dreyer, a young woman who has worked with the Algerian fighters. He is captured by Algerian militants and tortured. His organisation captures and tortures her. The 'little soldier' was played by
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Subor" title="Michel Subor">
       Michel Subor
      </a>
      and Veronica Dreyer by
      <a href="/wiki/Anna_Karina" title="Anna Karina">
       Anna Karina
      </a>
      the first collaboration between Godard and the Danish-bornof Russian extractionactress. Unlike Seberg, Karina had virtually no experience as an actress and Godard used her awkwardness as an element of her performance. He wrote the dialogue every day and, since it was filmed without direct sound and was dubbed, called dialogue to the actors. Forestier was a character close to Godard himself, an image-maker and intellectual, 'more or less my spokesman, but not totally' Godard told an interviewer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film, due to its political nature, implied that France was involved in a dirty war, engaging in torture, and was banned by the French government until January 1963. Godard and Karina were a couple by the end of the shoot. She appeared again, along with Belmondo, in Godard's first color film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Woman_Is_a_Woman" title="A Woman Is a Woman">
        A Woman Is a Woman
       </a>
      </i>
      (1961), which was intended as a homage to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_Film" title="Musical Film">
       American musical
      </a>
      . Adjustments that Godard made to the original version of the story gave it autobiographical resonances, 'specifically in regard to his relationship with Anna Karina'. The film revealed 'the confinement within the four walls of domestic life', and 'the emotional and artistic fault lines that threatened their relationship'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Godard's next film,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivre_sa_vie" title="Vivre sa vie">
        Vivre sa vie
       </a>
      </i>
      (My Life to Live) (1962), was one of his most popular among critics. Karina starred as Nana, an errant mother and aspiring actress whose financially strained circumstances lead her to the life of a streetwalker. It is an episodic account of her rationalizations to prove she is free, even though she is tethered at the end of her pimp's short leash. In one touching scene in a cafe, she spreads her arms out and announces she is free to raise or lower them as she wishes.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Les_Carabiniers" title="Les Carabiniers">
        Les Carabiniers
       </a>
      </i>
      (1963) was about the horror of war and its inherent injustice. It was the influence and suggestion of
      <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Rossellini" title="Roberto Rossellini">
       Roberto Rossellini
      </a>
      that led Godard to make this film which follows two peasants who join the army of a king, only to find futility in the whole thing as the king reveals the deception of war-administrating leaders. His most commercially successful film was
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Contempt_(film)" title="Contempt (film)">
        Le Mpris
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Contempt
      </i>
      ) (1963), starring
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Piccoli" title="Michel Piccoli">
       Michel Piccoli
      </a>
      and one of France's biggest female stars,
      <a href="/wiki/Brigitte_Bardot" title="Brigitte Bardot">
       Brigitte Bardot
      </a>
      . A coproduction between
      <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Italy" title="Cinema of Italy">
       Italy
      </a>
      and France,
      <i>
       Contempt
      </i>
      became known as a pinnacle in cinematic
      <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">
       modernism
      </a>
      with its profound reflexivity. The film follows Paul (Piccoli), a screenwriter who is commissioned by the arrogant American movie producer Prokosch (
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Palance" title="Jack Palance">
       Jack Palance
      </a>
      ) to rewrite the script for an adaptation of
      <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">
       Homer
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">
        Odyssey
       </a>
      </i>
      , which the Austrian director
      <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">
       Fritz Lang
      </a>
      has been filming. Lang's '
      <a href="/wiki/High_culture" title="High culture">
       high culture
      </a>
      ' interpretation of the story is lost on Prokosch, whose character is a firm indictment of the commercial motion picture hierarchy. Another prominent theme is the inability to reconcile love and labor, which is illustrated by Paul's crumbling marriage to Camille (Bardot) during the course of shooting.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1964, Godard and Karina formed a production company, Anouchka Films. He directed
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bande_%C3%A0_part_(film)" title="Bande  part (film)">
        Bande  part
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Band of Outsiders
      </i>
      ), another collaboration between the two and described by Godard as "
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland" title="Alice in Wonderland">
        Alice in Wonderland
       </a>
      </i>
      meets
      <a href="/wiki/Franz_Kafka" title="Franz Kafka">
       Franz Kafka
      </a>
      ." It follows two young men, looking to score on a heist, who both fall in love with Karina, and quotes from several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangster_film" title="Gangster film">
       gangster film
      </a>
      conventions.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Une_femme_mari%C3%A9e" title="Une femme marie">
        Une femme marie
       </a>
      </i>
      (A Married Woman) (1964) followed
      <i>
       Band of Outsiders
      </i>
      . It was a slow, deliberate, toned-down black-and-white picture without a real story. The film was shot in four weeks
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was "an explicitly and stringently modernist film". It showed Godard's "engagement with the most advanced thinking of the day, as expressed in the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lvi-Strauss">
       Claude Lvi-Strauss
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Roland_Barthes" title="Roland Barthes">
       Roland Barthes
      </a>
      " and its fragmentation and abstraction reflected also "his loss of faith in the familiar
      <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">
       Hollywood
      </a>
      styles."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Godard made the film while he acquired funding for
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierrot_le_fou" title="Pierrot le fou">
        Pierrot le fou
       </a>
      </i>
      (1965).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1965, Godard directed
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alphaville_(film)" title="Alphaville (film)">
        Alphaville
       </a>
      </i>
      , a futuristic blend of science fiction,
      <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">
       film noir
      </a>
      , and satire.
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Constantine" title="Eddie Constantine">
       Eddie Constantine
      </a>
      starred as
      <a href="/wiki/Lemmy_Caution" title="Lemmy Caution">
       Lemmy Caution
      </a>
      , a detective who is sent into a city controlled by a giant computer named Alpha 60. His mission is to make contact with Professor von Braun (
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Vernon" title="Howard Vernon">
       Howard Vernon
      </a>
      ), a famous scientist who has fallen mysteriously silent, and is believed to be suppressed by the computer.
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierrot_le_fou" title="Pierrot le fou">
        Pierrot le fou
       </a>
      </i>
      (1965) featured a complex storyline, distinctive personalities, and a violent ending.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gilles_Jacob&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gilles Jacob (page does not exist)">
       Gilles Jacob
      </a>
      , an author, critic, and president of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">
       Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      , called it both a "retrospective" and recapitulation in the way it played on so many of Godard's earlier characters and themes. With an extensive cast and variety of locations, the film was expensive enough to warrant significant problems with funding. Shot in color, it departed from Godard's minimalist works (typified by
      <i>
       Breathless
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Vivre sa vie
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       Une femme marie
      </i>
      ). He solicited the participation of Jean-Paul Belmondo, by then a famous actor, in order to guarantee the necessary amount of capital.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masculin,_f%C3%A9minin" title="Masculin, fminin">
        Masculin, fminin
       </a>
      </i>
      (1966), based on two
      <a href="/wiki/Guy_de_Maupassant" title="Guy de Maupassant">
       Guy de Maupassant
      </a>
      stories,
      <i>
       La Femme de Paul
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Le Signe
      </i>
      , was a study of contemporary French youth and their involvement with cultural politics. An intertitle refers to the characters as "The children of
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
       Marx
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">
       Coca-Cola
      </a>
      ." Although Godard's cinema is sometimes thought to depict a wholly masculine point of view, Phillip John Usher has demonstrated how the film, by the way it connects images and disparate events, seems to blur gender lines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-test-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Godard followed with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Made_in_U.S.A._(1966_film)" title="Made in U.S.A. (1966 film)">
        Made in U.S.A
       </a>
      </i>
      (1966), whose source material was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Stark" title="Richard Stark">
       Richard Stark
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       The Jugger
      </i>
      ; and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Two_or_Three_Things_I_Know_About_Her" title="Two or Three Things I Know About Her">
        Two or Three Things I Know About Her
       </a>
      </i>
      (1967), in which
      <a href="/wiki/Marina_Vlady" title="Marina Vlady">
       Marina Vlady
      </a>
      portrays a woman leading a double life as housewife and prostitute. A Classic New Wave crime thiller, "Made in the U.S.A" is inspired by American Noir films.
      <a href="/wiki/Anna_Karina" title="Anna Karina">
       Anna Karina
      </a>
      stars as the anti-hero searching for her murdered lover; the film includes a cameo by
      <a href="/wiki/Marianne_Faithfull" title="Marianne Faithfull">
       Marianne Faithfull
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/La_Chinoise" title="La Chinoise">
        La Chinoise
       </a>
      </i>
      (1967) saw Godard at his most politically forthright so far. The film focused on a group of students and engaged with the ideas coming out of the student activist groups in contemporary France. Released just before the May 1968 events, the film is thought by some to foreshadow the student rebellions that took place.
     </p>
     <p>
      That same year, Godard made a more colorful and political film,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Week_End_(1967_film)" title="Week End (1967 film)">
        Week End
       </a>
      </i>
      . It follows a Parisian couple as they leave on a weekend trip across the French countryside to collect an inheritance. What ensues is a confrontation with the tragic flaws of the over-consuming bourgeoisie. The film contains some of the most written-about scenes in cinema's history. One of them, an eight-minute
      <a href="/wiki/Tracking_shot" title="Tracking shot">
       tracking shot
      </a>
      of the couple stuck in an unremitting traffic jam as they leave the city, is cited as a new technique Godard used to deconstruct bourgeois trends.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Startlingly, a few shots contain extra footage from, as it were, before the beginning of the take (while the actors are preparing) and after the end of the take (while the actors are coming out of character).
      <i>
       Week End'
      </i>
      s enigmatic and audacious end title sequence, which reads "End of Cinema", appropriately marked an end to the narrative and cinematic period in Godard's filmmaking career.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">
       Politics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Politics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Politics are never far from the surface in Godard's films. One of his earliest features,
      <i>
       Le Petit Soldat
      </i>
      , dealt with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algerian_War_of_Independence" title="Algerian War of Independence">
       Algerian War of Independence
      </a>
      , and was notable for its attempt to present the complexity of the dispute rather than pursue any specific ideological agenda. Along these lines,
      <i>
       Les Carabiniers
      </i>
      presents a fictional war that is initially romanticized in the way its characters approach their service, but becomes a stiff anti-war
      <a href="/wiki/Metonymy" title="Metonymy">
       metonym
      </a>
      . In addition to the international conflicts Godard sought an artistic response to, he was also very concerned with the social problems in France. The earliest and best example of this is Karina's potent portrayal of a prostitute in
      <i>
       Vivre sa vie
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1960s Paris, the political milieu was not overwhelmed by one specific movement. There was, however, a distinct post-war climate shaped by various international conflicts such as the colonialism in North Africa and Southeast Asia. The side that opposed such colonization included the majority of French workers, who belonged to the French communist party, and the Parisian artists and writers who positioned themselves on the side of social reform and class equality. A large portion of this group had a particular affinity for the teachings of
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
       Karl Marx
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Godard#Marxism" title="Jean-Luc Godard">
       Godard's Marxist disposition
      </a>
      did not become abundantly explicit until
      <i>
       La Chinoise
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Week End
      </i>
      , but is evident in several filmsnamely
      <i>
       Pierrot
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Une femme marie
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Godard has been accused by some of harboring
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Semitic" title="Anti-Semitic">
       anti-Semitic
      </a>
      views: in 2010, in the lead-up to the presentation of Godard's honorary Oscar, a prominent article in the New York Times by Michael Cieply drew attention to the idea, which had been circulating through press in previous weeks, that Godard might be an anti-Semite, and thus undeserving of the accolade. Cieply makes reference to
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Brody" title="Richard Brody">
       Richard Brody
      </a>
      's book, "Everything is Cinema: The Working Life of Jean-Luc Godard", and alluded to a previous, longer article published by the Jewish Journal as lying near the origin of the debate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The article also draws upon Brody's book, for example in the following quotation, which Godard made on television in 1981: "Moses is my principal enemy...Moses, when he received the commandments, he saw images and translated them. Then he brought the texts, he didn't show what he had seen. That's why the Jewish people are accursed."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Immediately after Cieply's article was published, Brody made a clear point of criticizing the "extremely selective and narrow use" of passages in his book, and noted that Godard's work has approached the Holocaust with "the greatest moral seriousness".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Indeed, his documentaries feature images from the Holocaust in a context suggesting he considers Nazism and the Holocaust as the nadir of human history. Godard's views become more complex regarding the State of Israel. In 1970, Godard traveled to the Middle East to make a pro-Palestinian film he didn't complete and whose footage eventually became part of the 1976 film
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ici_et_ailleurs" title="Ici et ailleurs">
        Ici et ailleurs
       </a>
      </i>
      . In this film, Godard seems to view the Palestinian cause as one of many worldwide Leftist revolutionary movements. Elsewhere, Godard has explicitly identified himself as an anti-Zionist but has denied the accusations of anti-Semitism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam_War">
       Vietnam War
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vietnam War">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Godard produced several pieces that directly address the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      . Furthermore, there are two scenes in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierrot_le_fou" title="Pierrot le fou">
        Pierrot le fou
       </a>
      </i>
      that tackle the issue. The first is a scene that takes place in the initial car ride between Ferdinand (Belmondo) and Marianne (Karina). Over the car radio, the two hear the message "garrison massacred by the
      <a href="/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">
       Viet Cong
      </a>
      who lost 115 men". Marianne responds with an extended musing on the way the radio dehumanizes the Northern Vietnamese combatants.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the same film, the lovers accost a group of American sailors along the course of their liberating crime spree. Their immediate reaction, expressed by Marianne, is "Damn Americans!", an obvious outlet of the frustration so many
      <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">
       French communists
      </a>
      felt towards
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Empire_(term)" title="American Empire (term)">
       American hegemony
      </a>
      . Ferdinand then reconsiders, "That's OK, well change our politics. We can put on a play. Maybe theyll give us some dollars." Marianne is puzzled, but Ferdinand suggests that something the Americans would like would be the Vietnam War. The ensuing sequence is a makeshift play where Marianne dresses up as a stereotypical Vietnamese woman and Ferdinand as an
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       American sailor
      </a>
      . The scene ends on a brief shot revealing a chalk message left on the floor by the pair, "Long live
      <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">
       Mao
      </a>
      !" (
      <i>
       Vive Mao!
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Notably, he also participated in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loin_du_Vietnam" title="Loin du Vietnam">
        Loin du Vietnam
       </a>
      </i>
      (1967). An anti-war project, it consists of seven sketches directed by Godard (who used
      <a href="/wiki/Stock_footage" title="Stock footage">
       stock footage
      </a>
      from
      <i>
       La Chinoise
      </i>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lelouch" title="Claude Lelouch">
       Claude Lelouch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Joris_Ivens" title="Joris Ivens">
       Joris Ivens
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/William_Klein_(photographer)" title="William Klein (photographer)">
       William Klein
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Marker" title="Chris Marker">
       Chris Marker
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Alain_Resnais" title="Alain Resnais">
       Alain Resnais
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Agn%C3%A8s_Varda" title="Agns Varda">
       Agns Varda
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bertolt_Brecht">
       Bertolt Brecht
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bertolt Brecht">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Godard's engagement with German poet and playwright
      <a href="/wiki/Bertolt_Brecht" title="Bertolt Brecht">
       Bertolt Brecht
      </a>
      stems primarily from his attempt to transpose Brecht's theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Epic_theatre" title="Epic theatre">
       epic theatre
      </a>
      and its prospect of alienating the viewer (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verfremdungseffekt" title="Verfremdungseffekt">
        Verfremdungseffekt
       </a>
      </i>
      ) through a radical separation of the elements of the medium (in Brecht's case theater, but in Godard's, film). Brecht's influence is keenly felt through much of Godard's work, particularly before 1980, when Godard used filmic expression for specific political ends.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Breathless_(1960_film)" title="Breathless (1960 film)">
        Breathless
       </a>
       '
      </i>
      elliptical editing, which denies the viewer a fluid narrative typical of mainstream cinema, forces the viewers to take on more critical roles, connecting the pieces themselves and coming away with more investment in the work's content.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Godard also employs other devices, including asynchronous sound and alarming title frames, with perhaps his favorite being the character aside. In many of his most political pieces, specifically
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Week_End_(1967_film)" title="Week End (1967 film)">
        Week End
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierrot_le_fou" title="Pierrot le fou">
        Pierrot le fou
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/La_Chinoise" title="La Chinoise">
        La Chinoise
       </a>
      </i>
      , characters address the audience with thoughts, feelings, and instructions.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism">
       Marxism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Marxism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx_in_film" title="Karl Marx in film">
       Karl Marx in film
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist_theory" title="Marxist theory">
       Marxist
      </a>
      reading is possible with most if not all of Godard's early work. Godard's direct interaction with
      <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">
       Marxism
      </a>
      does not become explicitly apparent, however, until
      <i>
       Week End
      </i>
      , where the name
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
        Karl Marx
       </a>
      </i>
      is cited in conjunction with figures such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">
       Jesus Christ
      </a>
      . A constant refrain throughout Godard's cinematic period is that of the bourgeoisie's
      <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">
       consumerism
      </a>
      , the commodification of daily life and activity, and man's
      <a href="/wiki/Social_alienation" title="Social alienation">
       alienation
      </a>
      all central features of Marx's critique of capitalism.
     </p>
     <p>
      In an essay on Godard, philosopher and aesthetics scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Ranci%C3%A8re" title="Jacques Rancire">
       Jacques Rancire
      </a>
      states, "When in
      <i>
       Pierrot le fou
      </i>
      , 1965, a film without a clear political message, Belmondo played on the word 'scandal' and the 'freedom' that the Scandal girdle supposedly offered women, the context of a Marxist critique of
      <a href="/wiki/Commodification" title="Commodification">
       commodification
      </a>
      , of
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">
       pop art
      </a>
      derision at consumerism, and of a feminist denunciation of women's false 'liberation', was enough to foster a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialectics#Marxist_dialectics" title="Dialectics">
       dialectical
      </a>
      reading of the joke and the whole story." The way Godard treated politics in his cinematic period was in the context of a joke, a piece of art, or a relationship, presented to be used as tools of reference, romanticizing the Marxist rhetoric, rather than being solely tools of education.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Une_femme_mari%C3%A9e" title="Une femme marie">
        Une femme marie
       </a>
      </i>
      is also structured around Marx's concept of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Commodity_fetishism" title="Commodity fetishism">
        commodity fetishism
       </a>
      </i>
      . Godard once said that it is "a film in which individuals are considered as things, in which chases in a taxi alternate with ethological interviews, in which the spectacle of life is intermingled with its analysis". He was very conscious of the way he wished to portray the human being. His efforts are overtly characteristic of Marx, who in his
      <i>
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1844/manuscripts/preface.htm" rel="nofollow">
        Economic and Philosophical Manuscripts of 1844
       </a>
      </i>
      gives one of his most nuanced elaborations, analyzing how the worker is alienated from his product, the object of his productive activity.
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_Sadoul" title="Georges Sadoul">
       Georges Sadoul
      </a>
      , in his short rumination on the film, describes it as a "sociological study of the alienation of the modern woman".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Revolutionary_period_.281968.E2.80.9379.29">
       Revolutionary period (196879)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Revolutionary period (196879)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The period that spans from May 1968 indistinctly into the 1970s has been subject to an even larger volume of varying labeling. They include everything from his "militant" period, to his "radical" period, along with terms as specific as "
      <a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">
       Maoist
      </a>
      " and vague as "political". The period saw Godard align himself with a specific revolution and employ a consistent revolutionary rhetoric.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Films_2">
       Films
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Films">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Amid the upheavals of the late 1960s, Godard became interested in Maoist ideology. He formed the socialist-idealist
      <a href="/wiki/Dziga_Vertov_Group" title="Dziga Vertov Group">
       Dziga-Vertov
      </a>
      cinema group with
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Gorin" title="Jean-Pierre Gorin">
       Jean-Pierre Gorin
      </a>
      and produced a number of shorts outlining his politics. In that period he travelled extensively and shot a number of films, most of which remained unfinished or were refused showings. His films became intensely politicized and experimental, a phase that lasted until 1980.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1978 Godard was commissioned by the
      <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">
       Mozambican
      </a>
      government to make a short film. During this time his experience with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kodak" title="Kodak">
       Kodak
      </a>
      film led him to criticize the stock film as "inherently racist" since it did not reflect the variety, nuance or complexity in dark brown or dark
      <a href="/wiki/Human_skin_color" title="Human skin color">
       skin
      </a>
      . This was because Kodak
      <a href="/wiki/Color_chart#Shirley_cards" title="Color chart">
       Shirley cards
      </a>
      were only made for Caucasian subjects, a problem that was not rectified until 1995.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Gould" title="Elliott Gould">
       Elliott Gould
      </a>
      , he and Godard met to discuss the possibility of Godard's directing
      <a href="/wiki/Jules_Feiffer" title="Jules Feiffer">
       Jules Feiffer
      </a>
      's 1971 surrealist play
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Little_Murders" title="Little Murders">
        Little Murders
       </a>
      </i>
      . During this meeting, Godard said his two favorite American writers were Feiffer and
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_M._Schulz" title="Charles M. Schulz">
       Charles M. Schulz
      </a>
      . Godard soon declined the opportunity to direct; the job later went to
      <a href="/wiki/Alan_Arkin" title="Alan Arkin">
       Alan Arkin
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jean-Pierre_Gorin">
       Jean-Pierre Gorin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Jean-Pierre Gorin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After the events of
      <a href="/wiki/May_1968_events_in_France" title="May 1968 events in France">
       May 1968
      </a>
      , when the city of Paris saw total upheaval in response to the "authoritarian
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">
       de Gaulle
      </a>
      ", and Godard's professional objective was reconsidered, he began to collaborate with like-minded individuals in the filmmaking arena. The most notable of these collaborations was with a young Maoist student,
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Gorin" title="Jean-Pierre Gorin">
       Jean-Pierre Gorin
      </a>
      , who displayed a passion for cinema that grabbed Godard's attention.
     </p>
     <p>
      Between 1968 and 1973, Godard and Gorin collaborated to make a total of five films with strong Maoist messages. The most prominent film from the collaboration was
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tout_va_bien" title="Tout va bien">
        Tout va bien
       </a>
      </i>
      , which starred
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Fonda" title="Jane Fonda">
       Jane Fonda
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yves_Montand" title="Yves Montand">
       Yves Montand
      </a>
      , at the time very big stars. Jean-Pierre Gorin now teaches the
      <a href="/wiki/Film_theory" title="Film theory">
       study of film
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Diego" title="University of California, San Diego">
       University of California, San Diego
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dziga_Vertov_group">
       The Dziga Vertov group
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Dziga Vertov group">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The small group of Maoists that Godard had brought together, which included Gorin, adopted the name
      <a href="/wiki/Dziga_Vertov_Group" title="Dziga Vertov Group">
       Dziga Vertov Group
      </a>
      . Godard had a specific interest in
      <a href="/wiki/Dziga_Vertov" title="Dziga Vertov">
       Vertov
      </a>
      , a Soviet filmmakerwhose adopted name is derived from the verb to spin or rotate
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is best remembered for
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Man_with_the_Movie_Camera" title="Man with the Movie Camera">
        Man with the Movie Camera
       </a>
      </i>
      (1929) and a contemporary of both the great Soviet
      <a href="/wiki/Film_editing" title="Film editing">
       montage
      </a>
      theorists, most notably
      <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Eisenstein" title="Sergei Eisenstein">
       Sergei Eisenstein
      </a>
      , and Russian
      <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(art)" title="Constructivism (art)">
       constructivist
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_avant_garde" title="Russian avant garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      artists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Rodchenko" title="Alexander Rodchenko">
       Alexander Rodchenko
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Tatlin" title="Vladimir Tatlin">
       Vladimir Tatlin
      </a>
      . Part of Godard's political shift after May 1968 was toward a proactive participation in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle">
       class struggle
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sonimage">
       Sonimage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sonimage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1972, Godard and Swiss filmmaker
      <a href="/wiki/Anne-Marie_Mi%C3%A9ville" title="Anne-Marie Miville">
       Anne-Marie Miville
      </a>
      started the alternative video production and distribution company Sonimage, based in Grenoble.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Under Sonimage, Godard produced both
      <i>
       Numro Deux
      </i>
      (1975) and "Sauve qui peut (la vie)" (1980). In 1976, Godard and Miville, his wife,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      collaborated on a series of innovative video works for European broadcast television called "
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Six_fois_deux/Sur_et_sous_la_communication&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Six fois deux/Sur et sous la communication (page does not exist)">
       Six fois deux/Sur et sous la communication
      </a>
      " (1976)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and "France/tour/dtour/deux/enfants" (1978).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1980.E2.80.93present">
       1980present
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 1980present">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Godard's return to somewhat more traditional fiction was marked with
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sauve_qui_peut_(la_vie)" title="Sauve qui peut (la vie)">
        Sauve qui peut (la vie)
       </a>
      </i>
      (1980), the first of a series of more mainstream films marked by autobiographical currents: for example
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Passion_(1982_film)" title="Passion (1982 film)">
        Passion
       </a>
      </i>
      (1982),
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lettre_%C3%A0_Freddy_Buache" title="Lettre  Freddy Buache">
        Lettre  Freddy Buache
       </a>
      </i>
      (1982),
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A9nom_Carmen" title="Prnom Carmen">
        Prnom Carmen
       </a>
      </i>
      (1984), and
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grandeur_et_d%C3%A9cadence_d%27un_petit_commerce_de_cin%C3%A9ma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grandeur et dcadence d'un petit commerce de cinma (page does not exist)">
        Grandeur et dcadence d'un petit commerce de cinma
       </a>
      </i>
      (1986). There was, though, another flurry of controversy with
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Je_vous_salue,_Marie" title="Je vous salue, Marie">
        Je vous salue, Marie
       </a>
      </i>
      (1985), which was condemned by the Catholic Church for alleged
      <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">
       heresy
      </a>
      , and also with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/King_Lear_(1987_film)" title="King Lear (1987 film)">
        King Lear
       </a>
      </i>
      (1987), an extraordinary but much-excoriated essay on
      <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
       William Shakespeare
      </a>
      and language. Also completed in 1987 was a segment in the film
      <a href="/wiki/Aria_(film)" title="Aria (film)">
       ARIA
      </a>
      which was based loosely from the plot of
      <a href="/wiki/Armida" title="Armida">
       Armide
      </a>
      ; it is set in a gym and uses several
      <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">
       arias
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lully" title="Jean-Baptiste Lully">
       Jean-Baptiste Lully
      </a>
      from his famous
      <a href="/wiki/Armide_(Lully)" title="Armide (Lully)">
       Armide
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      His later films have been marked by great formal beauty and frequently a sense of requiem
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_Vague_(film)" title="Nouvelle Vague (film)">
        Nouvelle Vague
       </a>
      </i>
      (New Wave, 1990), the autobiographical
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/JLG/JLG_%E2%80%93_Self-Portrait_in_December" title="JLG/JLG  Self-Portrait in December">
        JLG/JLG, autoportrait de dcembre
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       JLG/JLG: Self-Portrait in December
      </i>
      , 1995), and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/For_Ever_Mozart" title="For Ever Mozart">
        For Ever Mozart
       </a>
      </i>
      (1996).
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allemagne_ann%C3%A9e_90_neuf_z%C3%A9ro" title="Allemagne anne 90 neuf zro">
        Allemagne anne 90 neuf zro
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Germany Year 90 Nine Zero
      </i>
      , 1991) was a quasi-sequel to
      <i>
       Alphaville
      </i>
      but done with an elegiac tone and focus on the inevitable decay of age. Between 1988 and 1998 he produced perhaps the most important work of his career in the multi-part series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Histoire(s)_du_cin%C3%A9ma" title="Histoire(s) du cinma">
        Histoire(s) du cinma
       </a>
      </i>
      , a monumental project which combined all the innovations of his video work with a passionate engagement in the issues of twentieth-century history and the history of film itself.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2001,
      <a href="/wiki/In_Praise_of_Love_(film)" title="In Praise of Love (film)">
       <i>
        In Praise of Love
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        loge de l'amour
       </i>
       )
      </a>
      was released. The film is notable for its use of both film and videothe first half captured in 35-mm black and white, the latter half shot in color on DVand subsequently transferred to film for editing. The blending of film and video recalls the statement from
      <i>
       Sauve Qui Peut
      </i>
      , in which the tension between film and video evokes the struggle between Cain and Abel. The film is also noted for containing themes of aging, love, separation, and rediscovery as it follows the young artist Edgar in his contemplation of a new work on the four stages of love.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Notre_musique" title="Notre musique">
        Notre musique
       </a>
      </i>
      (2004), Godard turned his focus to war, specifically, the
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Sarajevo" title="Siege of Sarajevo">
       war in Sarajevo
      </a>
      , but with attention to all war, including the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      , the war between the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Wars" title="American Indian Wars">
       US and Native Americans
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_conflict" title="IsraeliPalestinian conflict">
       IsraeliPalestinian conflict
      </a>
      . The film is structured into three
      <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
       Dantean
      </a>
      kingdoms:
      <a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">
       Hell, Purgatory and Paradise
      </a>
      . Godard's fascination with paradox is a constant in the film. It opens with a long, ponderous montage of war images that occasionally lapses into the comic; Paradise is shown as a lush wooded beach patrolled by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marines" title="US Marines">
       US Marines
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Godard's film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Film_Socialisme" title="Film Socialisme">
        Film Socialisme
       </a>
      </i>
      , premiered in the
      <a href="/wiki/Un_Certain_Regard" title="Un Certain Regard">
       Un Certain Regard
      </a>
      section at the
      <a href="/wiki/2010_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2010 Cannes Film Festival">
       2010 Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hollywoodreporter_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hollywoodreporter-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was released theatrically in France in May 2010.
     </p>
     <p>
      Godard was rumored to be considering directing a film adaptation of
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Mendelsohn" title="Daniel Mendelsohn">
       Daniel Mendelsohn
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lost:_A_Search_for_Six_of_Six_Million" title="The Lost: A Search for Six of Six Million">
        The Lost: A Search for Six of Six Million
       </a>
      </i>
      , an award-winning book about the Holocaust.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hollywood_Reporter_62-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Hollywood_Reporter-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2013, Godard released the short
      <i>
       Les trois dsastres
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       The Three Disasters
      </i>
      ) as part of the omnibus film
      <i>
       3X3D
      </i>
      with filmmakers
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Greenaway" title="Peter Greenaway">
       Peter Greenaway
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edgar_Pera" title="Edgar Pera">
       Edgar Pera
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       3X3D
      </i>
      premiered at the
      <a href="/wiki/2013_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2013 Cannes Film Festival">
       2013 Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His 2014 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Goodbye_to_Language" title="Goodbye to Language">
        Goodbye to Language
       </a>
      </i>
      , shot in

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Collaboration_with_ECM_Records">
       Collaboration with ECM Records
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Luc_Godard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Collaboration with ECM Records">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Godard shares a friendship with
      <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Eicher" title="Manfred Eicher">
       Manfred Eicher
      </a>
      , founder and head of the innovative German music label
      <a href="/wiki/ECM_Records" title="ECM Records">
       ECM Records
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The label has released the soundtracks of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_Vague_(film)" title="Nouvelle Vague (film)">
        Nouvelle Vague
       </a>
      </i>
      (ECM NewSeries 1600-01) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Histoire(s)_du_cin%C3%A9ma" title="Histoire(s) du cinma">
        Histoire(s) du cinma
       </a>
      </i>
      (ECM NewSeries 1706) by Godard. This collaboration expanded over the years and led on the one hand into the contribution of several stills from Godards movies for album covers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the other hand Eicher took over the musical direction of many of Godards films like
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allemagne_90_neuf_z%C3%A9ro" title="Allemagne 90 neuf zro">
        Allemagne 90 neuf zro
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9las_Pour_Moi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hlas Pour Moi (page does not exist)">
        Hlas Pour Moi
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/JLG/JLG_%E2%80%93_Self-Portrait_in_December" title="JLG/JLG  Self-Portrait in December">
        JLG
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/For_Ever_Mozart" title="For Ever Mozart">
        For Ever Mozart
       </a>
      </i>
      . Additionally Godard has released a collection of short films on the label with
      <a href="/wiki/Anne-Marie_Mi%C3%A9ville" title="Anne-Marie Miville">
       Anne-Marie Miville
      </a>
      called
      <i>
       Four Short Films
      </i>
      (ECM 5001).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Album covers with Godard's contribution include:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
