           <div class="action-points">
            <div class="ap-heading">
             <h4>
              Action Points
             </h4>
            </div>
            <div class="ap-content">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Note that this study was published as an abstract and presented at a conference. These data and conclusions should be considered to be preliminary until published in a peer-reviewed journal.
              </li>
              <li>
               <em>
                FTO
               </em>
               , the stongest known genetic susceptibility gene for adiposity and one that is associated with type 2 diabetes, appears to confer a predisposition to higher body mass index associated with higher total energy intake and high dietary protein intake.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           SAN FRANCISCO  --  A certain genetic variant that predisposes patients to obesity was associated with higher total energy intake in children and adolescents, researchers reported here.
          </p>
          <p>
           In a meta-analysis of several studies, an
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           gene variant was significantly associated with both a higher body mass index (BMI) as well as with total energy intake in children,
           <a href="http://www.einstein.yu.edu/faculty/13887/qibin-qi/" target="_blank">
            Qibin Qi, PhD
           </a>
           , of Albert Einstein College of Medicine in New York City, reported during a late-breaking session at the American Diabetes Association meeting here.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           , which is associated with type 2 diabetes, is the strongest known genetic susceptibility gene for adiposity, and it's been suggested that
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           may be involved in regulating food intake. Both animal studies and human studies  --  including one in the
           <a href="http://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa0803839" target="_blank">
            <em>
             New England Journal of Medicine
            </em>
           </a>
           --  have linked the variant with higher total energy intake.
          </p>
          <p>
           Qi and colleagues decided to assess the association between one
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           gene variant (
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           -rs9939609) and total energy intake as well as macronutrient intake in children. They also looked at the interaction between this gene variant and dietary intakes on BMI.
          </p>
          <p>
           They performed a meta-analysis using data from 15 studies totaling 16,094 children, most of whom were white.
          </p>
          <p>
           They found that the minor allele of the
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           -rs9939609 variant was associated with a higher BMI, with an effect size of 0.07 (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =6.0 x 10
           <sup>
            -10
           </sup>
           ). It was also associated with total energy intake, at an effect size of 14.6, they reported (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.008).
          </p>
          <p>
           The
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           variant was not, however, directly associated with dietary protein, carbohydrate, or total fat intake, Qi said. Yet the association between the
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           variant and BMI was much stronger in patients with a higher protein intake than it was in those with a low protein intake, they reported.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           When breaking down the overall 0.07 effect size of the link with BMI, it was lowered to 0.04 for patients in the low-protein-intake group (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.02), but was raised to 0.10 for those who had a high intake of protein (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =8.2 x 10
           <sup>
            -10
           </sup>
           ).
          </p>
          <p>
           Qi and colleagues also found that the association between
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           and total energy intake was stronger in children ages 10 to 18 compared with those ages 3 to 9.
          </p>
          <p>
           They said the strengths of the study included its large sample size and a wide range of data from early childhood to late adolescence, but it was limited by its reliance on cross-sectional data, possible measurement errors in assessing dietary intake, and a lack of generalizability outside of white patients.
          </p>
          <p>
           Still, they concluded that the results suggest the
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           variant that confers a greater risk of obesity is associated with higher total energy intake, and that greater intake of dietary protein may accentuate the association between this gene variant and adiposity in children.
          </p>
          <p>
           Further studies are needed to examine the role of
           <em>
            FTO
           </em>
           in the relationships between dietary intake, obesity, and type 2 diabetes, Qi said.
          </p>
