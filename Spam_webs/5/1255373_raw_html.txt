     <p>
      <b>
       Charlies Bunion
      </b>
      is a mountain in the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Smoky_Mountains" title="Great Smoky Mountains">
       Great Smoky Mountains
      </a>
      of the Southeastern United States, characterized by a large boulder-like protrusion just below the summit on its otherwise-sheer northern face. The mountain is a rare instance of a bare-rock summit in the Smokies.
     </p>
     <p>
      Charlies Bunion rises to an elevation of 5,565 feet (1,696 m) above sea level, and is situated along the state line between
      <a href="/wiki/Sevier_County,_Tennessee" title="Sevier County, Tennessee">
       Sevier County, Tennessee
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Swain_County,_North_Carolina" title="Swain County, North Carolina">
       Swain County, North Carolina
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Trail" title="Appalachian Trail">
       Appalachian Trail
      </a>
      crosses the mountain's northern slope some four miles (6km) east of
      <a href="/wiki/Newfound_Gap" title="Newfound Gap">
       Newfound Gap
      </a>
      , allowing easy access to dayhikers and rock climbers.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">
       Geology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlies_Bunion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="895" data-file-width="1199" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg/210px-Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg/315px-Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg/420px-Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg 2x" width="210"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charlies-bunion-protrusion.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The characteristic boulder-like protrusion on northern face of Charlies Bunion, with the summit rising on the right
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Charlies Bunion comprises the western wing of
      <b>
       The Sawteeth
      </b>
      , a series of narrow, jagged cliffs connecting the high peaks of the Central Smokies with the high peaks of the Eastern Smokies. These cliffs are underlain by a
      <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">
       Precambrian
      </a>
      metamorphic rock of the Ocoee Supergroup known as "Anakeesta Formation," which consists mainly of slate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Anakeesta Formation, which comprises much of the crest of the Central Smokies, was created nearly a billion years ago from ancient ocean sediments. The rock was thrust upward during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appalachian_orogeny" title="Appalachian orogeny">
       Appalachian orogeny
      </a>
      , when the North American and African plates collided around 400 million years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The steep northern face of Charlies Bunion is drained by the headwaters of the Middle Fork of the Little Pigeon River, and loses 2,000 feet (609 m) in elevation in just over a mile. The southern face, which is more gradual, is drained by the headwaters of the Oconaluftee River, specifically Kephart Prong and Bradley Fork.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charlies_Bunion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="894" data-file-width="1198" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg/210px-Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg/315px-Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg/420px-Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg 2x" width="210"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charlies-bunion-mount-leconte.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The view looking northwest from Charlies Bunion, with Mount Le Conte rising on the left
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Other than the occasional surveyor or naturalist, few people ventured to the Sawteeth before the 1900s. This area of the Smokies was part of a 40-mile (64km) stretch of wilderness described by
      <a href="/wiki/Horace_Kephart" title="Horace Kephart">
       Horace Kephart
      </a>
      as "so rough that you could not make seven miles a day in it to save your life..."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While the main route across the crest of the Smokies was the old Indian Gap Trail (now
      <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_441" title="U.S. Route 441">
       U.S. Route 441
      </a>
      ), which crested between Newfound Gap and
      <a href="/wiki/Clingmans_Dome" title="Clingmans Dome">
       Clingmans Dome
      </a>
      , an alternative route was to ascend Kephart Prong to Dry Sluice Gap, on the eastern flank of Charlies Bunion, and descend into the Porters Creek Valley. The Whaley brothers, William and Middleton, used this route around 1800 while traveling from
      <a href="/wiki/Oconaluftee_(Great_Smoky_Mountains)" title="Oconaluftee (Great Smoky Mountains)">
       Oconaluftee
      </a>
      to what is now
      <a href="/wiki/Greenbrier_(Great_Smoky_Mountains)" title="Greenbrier (Great Smoky Mountains)">
       Greenbrier
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Charlies Bunion, like most of the central crest of the Smokies, was fairly heavily forested until the 20th century. Large-scale logging operations in the Oconaluftee valley, however, left large piles of dry, dead brushwood scattered about the streambeds above Smokemont. In 1925, a massive forest fire, feeding off this dry foliage, raced up Kephart Prong, and quickly spread across the Sawteeth and over into Tennessee. The heat from the fire was so intense, it not only scorched every plant in its path, but it rendered the soil sterile. Then in 1929, a
      <a href="/wiki/Cloudburst" title="Cloudburst">
       cloudburst
      </a>
      dumped torrential rains on the Smokies, washing away the scalded soil from the western flank of the Sawteeth and giving it its current rocky appearance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="640" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg/210px-Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg/315px-Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg/420px-Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg 2x" width="210"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charlies-bunion-porters-creek.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The view looking northeast from Charlies Bunion, with the Porters Creek Valley below
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A few days after the 1929 cloudburst, a group of mountaineers led by
      <a href="/wiki/Horace_Kephart" title="Horace Kephart">
       Horace Kephart
      </a>
      climbed to the crest of the Central Smokies to inspect the damage. When they reached the now-barren western flank of the Sawteeth,
      <b>
       Charlie Conner
      </b>
      , a mountain guide from Oconaluftee, removed his shoe, revealing a badly-swollen foot. Upon seeing this, Kephart was said to have remarked, "I'm going to get this put on a Government map for you."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is another, slightly different, story told by Paul M. Fink in his book "The Names and Lore of The Great Smoky Mountains" in which Fink says that one of the group upon looking at the bare rock pinnacle left by the storm, and knowing of Conner's foot affliction, remarked: "Well that sticks out like Charlie's bunion" prompting Kephart to decide on that name. The
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
       United States Geological Survey
      </a>
      gave it the name "Charlie's Bunion" shortly thereafter. The
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kephart" title="Mount Kephart">
       mountain rising above Charlies Bunion
      </a>
      to the west was named after Kephart himself.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1935, the
      <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">
       Civilian Conservation Corps
      </a>
      constructed a leg of the Appalachian Trail across the northern face of Charlies Bunion for the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Smoky_Mountains_National_Park" title="Great Smoky Mountains National Park">
       Great Smoky Mountains National Park
      </a>
      , which had officially opened the previous year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Today, the mountain's summit and characteristic boulder-like protrusion offer great views of
      <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">
       Tennessee
      </a>
      and the other peaks of the main ridge in the Smokies, especially Mount Le Conte and Mount Kephart.
     </p>
