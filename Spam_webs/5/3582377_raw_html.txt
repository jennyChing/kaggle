           <h1>
            The real cost of shooting video
           </h1>
           <h4>

           <div class="social-bar">

          <article>
           <p>
            Its long been assumed that using stock video is less expensive than doing your own shoot, but is it really worth it? Is it a matter of saving a few bucks here and there, or are the savings great enough to make a real impact?
           </p>
           <p>
            We figured the best way to answer this was to do a real-world test, so we shot a video clip that you might use in a professional project and tracked all of the costs involved. This is just a baseline, and obviously theres a huge range of production costs depending on the type of shot  from computer animation with no crew required to extreme location shoots with a crew on a helicopter. We went somewhere in the middle, using a commonly requested concept shot of a doctor and his patient (whos a puppet because why not?).
           </p>
           <div class="video-container">
            <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="377" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/5gE3hmnbzRc?list=UU3A-zX2ockfb5Fe73KCpQyg" width="670">
            </iframe>
           </div>
           <!-- END OF VIDEO CONTAINER -->
           <p>
            There are two common misconceptions regarding the cost of producing a shot. One is that it will only take an hour to shoot, and the other is that your brother/cousin/friend can do it for free. Sometimes thats true, but you will probably be unhappy with the results.
           </p>
           <p>
            The actual stock shot we produced from this shoot only took 20 minutes to get right, but it involved about 5 hours of total filming. The prices shown are very real, based on actual receipts rounded down. That said, it does get complicated and some explanation is needed to sort out the numbers.
           </p>
           <a href="http://www.istockphoto.com/stock-photo-24346015-camera-operatror.php" style="float:right;margin-left:10px" target="_blank">
            <img alt="Film Crew - On Location" src="http://i.istockimg.com/generic_image_view/232212/232212"/>
           </a>
           <ul>
            <li>
             While the shoot took 5 hours, including the scripted mishaps, the numbers reflect a full day of shooting. Actors and crew dont hire out in 20-minute or 5-hour intervals. A day rate is the norm.
            </li>
            <li>
             We actually spent 3 days on this shoot, including half a day to pick up all the equipment and supplies; one day to build, dress and light the set; one day to shoot; and another half day to return the gear. The lighting and grip was rented for 3 days (the cost in the video reflects one-third of the actual price), and the camera was a one-day-rental equivalent with lens and slider.
            </li>
            <li>
             Other costs not included in the total: location rental (we shot in our office); construction of the set (we had volunteers); truck rental to move the gear; insurance; or any of the pre-production planning.
            </li>
            <li>
             Yup, those are iStock images decorating the set. Here is a
             <a href="http://www.istockphoto.com/search/lightbox/14173544" target="_blank">
              lightbox
             </a>
             of images and sound effects used.
            </li>
            <li>
             The puppet was custom made, and his name is Murray.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            To be fair, most stock video shooters wouldnt spend this much on just one shot (even with Murray as the star). Typically, they would shoot many camera angles, situations, actor interactions, concepts and artistic/directional styles. So even if it does cost these same dollars, they would be able to squeeze more than one shot out of this budget and amortize the cost of some of the equipment.
           </p>
           <p>
            But to compare apples with apples, we have to assume that our customers still only need one or two shots, not the entire series. So even if you did decide to make the shot more cost effective by shooting more angles, chances are you still only need one or two.
           </p>
           <p>
            It might seem obvious now that if you want a well-directed shot that adds value to your production, without the true costs, stock is the way to go. But what about the shots that appear to be created by someone simply pointing their camera and capturing the world as it goes by? Like a shot of rain falling on a window?
           </p>
           <a href="http://www.istockphoto.com/stock-photo-16984413-rain-on-the-window.php?st=d7b165b" style="float:right;margin-left:10px" target="_blank">
            <img alt="Rain on the Window" src="http://i.istockimg.com/generic_image_view/255536/255536"/>
           </a>
           <h4>
            Shooting quality video takes time
           </h4>
           <p>
            A shot like that still takes close to an hour: to set up your camera, shoot, download and process. While some files in the iStock collection do appear to be simple, easy-to-capture content, the camera operator may have had to wait for the perfect combination of rain, mist and just enough light piercing through the atmosphere to get that hopeful look of the fishing boats bobbing at the wharf, or that moment just before the snowplow comes down the street and throws a perfect curl of snow at the camera. The financial cost of producing these kinds of stock files may be low, but the investment in patience can be very high.
           </p>
           <p>
            Then there are those breathtaking time-lapse and hyper-lapse files that are allowing us to re-discover our planet in beautiful new ways, the multi-copter mounted cameras and the tiny extreme POV cameras that we simply cannot expect to master and produce quality results on a moments notice. But we could do a whole other story on that.
           </p>
           <p>
            At the end of the very long day, stock video is far less draining on your budget, time and patience than your own shoot would be. So is it worth it? Yes. By a wide margin.
           </p>
