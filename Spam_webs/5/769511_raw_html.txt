      </span>
      ; a variant and derivatives:
      <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">
       traditional Chinese
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
       Japanese
      </a>
      : ;
      <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">
       simplified Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       w wi
      </i>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
       Korean
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
       ()
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">
       Vietnamese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="vi" xml:lang="vi">
       <i>
        V vi
       </i>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      , lit.
      <i>
       non-doing
      </i>
      ) is an important concept in
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoism
      </a>
      that literally means
      <i>
       non-action
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       non-doing
      </i>
      . In the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tao_te_Ching" title="Tao te Ching">
        Tao te Ching
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lao_Tzu" title="Lao Tzu">
       Lao Tzu
      </a>
      explains that beings (or phenomena) that are wholly in harmony with the
      <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">
       Tao
      </a>
      behave in a completely natural, uncontrived way. The goal of spiritual practice for the human being is, according to Lao Tzu, the attainment of this purely natural way of behaving, as when the planets revolve around the sun. The planets effortlessly do this revolving without any sort of control, force, or attempt to revolve themselves, instead engaging in effortless and spontaneous movement.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meanings">
       Meanings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu_wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meanings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Wu
      </i>
      may be translated as
      <i>
       not have
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       without
      </i>
      ;
      <i>
       Wei
      </i>
      may be translated as
      <i>
       do, act, serve as, govern
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       effort
      </i>
      . The literal meaning of
      <i>
       wu wei
      </i>
      is "without action", "without effort", or "without control", and is often included in the paradox
      <i>
       <b>
        wei wu wei
       </b>
      </i>
      : "action without action" or "effortless doing". The practice of
      <i>
       wu wei
      </i>
      and the efficacy of
      <i>
       wei wu wei
      </i>
      are fundamental tenets in
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_philosophy" title="Chinese philosophy">
       Chinese thought
      </a>
      and have been mostly emphasized by the
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoist school
      </a>
      . One cannot actively pursue
      <i>
       wu wei
      </i>
      . It manifests as a result of cultivation. The
      <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">
       Tao
      </a>
      is a guide.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is another less commonly referenced sense of
      <i>
       wu wei
      </i>
      ; "action that does not involve struggle or excessive effort". In this instance,
      <i>
       wu
      </i>
      means "without" and Wei means "effort" (instinct?). The concept of "effortless action" is a part of
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Neijia" title="Neijia">
       Internal martial arts
      </a>
      such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%27ai_chi_ch%27uan" title="T'ai chi ch'uan">
       T'ai chi ch'uan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Baguazhang" title="Baguazhang">
       Baguazhang
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xingyiquan" title="Xingyiquan">
       Xing Yi
      </a>
      . It follows that wu wei complies with the main feature and distinguishing characteristic of Taoism, that of being natural.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitsuzendo" title="Hitsuzendo">
       Zen Calligraphy
      </a>
      , wu wei has been represented as an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ens%C5%8D" title="Ens">
        ens
       </a>
      </i>
      (circle); in China, the calligraphic inscriptions of the words
      <i>
       wu wei
      </i>
      themselves resonate with old Taoist stories.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2013)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dao_De_Jing">
       <i>
        Dao De Jing
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu_wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dao De Jing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the original Taoist texts,
      <i>
       wu wei
      </i>
      is often associated with water and its yielding nature. In illustration, it can assume any form or shape it inhabits.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">
       Philosophy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wu_wei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosophy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Several chapters of the most important Taoist text, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tao_Te_Ching" title="Tao Te Ching">
        Tao Te Ching
       </a>
      </i>
      , attributed to
      <a href="/wiki/Laozi" title="Laozi">
       Laozi
      </a>
      , allude to "diminishing doing" or "diminishing will" as the key aspect of the sage's success.
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoist
      </a>
      philosophy recognizes that the
      <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">
       Universe
      </a>
      already works harmoniously according to its own ways; as a person exerts their will against or upon the world they disrupt the harmony that already exists. This is not to say that a person should not exert agency and will. Rather, it is
      <i>
       how
      </i>
      one acts in relation to the natural processes already extant. The
      <i>
       how
      </i>
      , the Tao of intention and motivation,
      <i>
       that
      </i>
      is key.
     </p>
     <p>
      Related translation from the
      <i>
       Tao T Ching
      </i>
      by Priya Hemenway, Chapter II:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       2
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        The Sage is occupied with the unspoken
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        and acts without effort.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Teaching without verbosity,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        producing without possessing,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        creating without regard to result,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        claiming nothing,
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>
        the Sage has nothing to lose.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
