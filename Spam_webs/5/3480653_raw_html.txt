     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Asphalt Jungle
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1950
      <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">
       film noir
      </a>
      directed by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Huston" title="John Huston">
       John Huston
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Heist_film" title="Heist film">
       heist film
      </a>
      is based on the 1949 novel of the same name by
      <a href="/wiki/W._R._Burnett" title="W. R. Burnett">
       W. R. Burnett
      </a>
      and stars an ensemble cast including
      <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Hayden" title="Sterling Hayden">
       Sterling Hayden
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Hagen" title="Jean Hagen">
       Jean Hagen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Jaffe" title="Sam Jaffe">
       Sam Jaffe
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Calhern" title="Louis Calhern">
       Louis Calhern
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/James_Whitmore" title="James Whitmore">
       James Whitmore
      </a>
      , and, in a minor but key role,
      <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">
       Marilyn Monroe
      </a>
      , an unknown at the time who was pictured but not mentioned on the posters.
     </p>
     <p>
      The film tells the story of a group of men planning and executing a jewel robbery. It was nominated for four
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">
       Academy Awards
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008,
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      was selected for preservation in the United States
      <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">
       National Film Registry
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      as being "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant".
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">
       Plot summary
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      When criminal mastermind Erwin "Doc" Riedenschneider (
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Jaffe" title="Sam Jaffe">
       Sam Jaffe
      </a>
      ) is released from prison after seven years, he immediately goes to see a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bookie" title="Bookie">
       bookie
      </a>
      named Cobby (
      <a href="/wiki/Marc_Lawrence" title="Marc Lawrence">
       Marc Lawrence
      </a>
      ) in an unnamed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">
       Midwest
      </a>
      river city, who arranges a meeting with Alonzo Emmerich (
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Calhern" title="Louis Calhern">
       Louis Calhern
      </a>
      ), a high-profile lawyer. Emmerich listens with interest to Doc's plan to steal jewelry worth a million dollars or more. Doc needs $50,000 to hire three mena "box man" (safe cracker), a driver and a "hooligan"to help him pull off the caper. Emmerich agrees to provide the money, then suggests that he (not one or more
      <a href="/wiki/Fence_(criminal)" title="Fence (criminal)">
       fences
      </a>
      ) assume the responsibility for disposing of the loot.
     </p>
     <p>
      Doc first hires Louie Ciavelli (
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Caruso_(actor)" title="Anthony Caruso (actor)">
       Anthony Caruso
      </a>
      ), a professional
      <a href="/wiki/Safe-cracking" title="Safe-cracking">
       safecracker
      </a>
      . Ciavelli only trusts Gus Minissi (
      <a href="/wiki/James_Whitmore" title="James Whitmore">
       James Whitmore
      </a>
      ), a hunchbacked diner owner, as the
      <a href="/wiki/Crime_scene_getaway" title="Crime scene getaway">
       getaway driver
      </a>
      . The final member of the gang is Dix Handley (
      <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Hayden" title="Sterling Hayden">
       Sterling Hayden
      </a>
      ), a friend of Gus's. Dix explains his ultimate goal to Doll Conovan (
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Hagen" title="Jean Hagen">
       Jean Hagen
      </a>
      ), who is in love with him. His dream is to buy back the horse farm that his father lost during the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
      . Dix, however, just keeps losing his ill-gotten gains betting on the horses via Cobby. This job would pay him the amount he needs.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the meticulously planned crime (an 11-minute sequence in the film), the criminals carry out their work in a calm, professional manner. Ciavelli hammers through a brick wall to get into the jewelry store, deactivates a door alarm and lets in Doc and Dix, then opens the main safe in minutes using an explosive liquid ("the soup"). Unfortunately, the explosion somehow sets off the alarms of nearby businesses and brings the police to the scene more quickly than expected. On their way out, Dix has to slug an arriving security guard, who drops his gun, which discharges and wounds Ciavelli in the belly. The men get away unseen, but a police manhunt quickly begins.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ciavelli insists on being taken home by Gus. Dix and Doc take the loot to Emmerich, who confesses he needs some more time to raise the cash they had expected. In reality, he is broke. He had sent a private detective named Bob Brannom (
      <a href="/wiki/Brad_Dexter" title="Brad Dexter">
       Brad Dexter
      </a>
      ) to collect sums owed to him, but Brannom returned only with excuses. Emmerich then plotted to double cross the others with Brannom's help (for an equal share). Emmerich suggests to Doc that he leave the jewelry with him, but Doc and Dix become suspicious. Brannom then pulls out his gun. Dix is able to kill Brannom, but not without being wounded himself. Dix wants to shoot Emmerich as well, but Doc persuades him not to. Doc tells the lawyer to contact the insurance companies and offer to return the valuables for 25% of their value.
     </p>
     <p>
      Emmerich disposes of Brannom's body in the river, but the police find the corpse, along with the list of people who owe Emmerich money, and question him. He lies about his whereabouts, and after they leave, hurriedly calls Angela Phinlay (
      <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">
       Marilyn Monroe
      </a>
      in her first important role), his beautiful young mistress, to set up an alibi.
     </p>
     <p>
      Under increasing pressure from Police Commissioner Hardy (
      <a href="/wiki/John_McIntire" title="John McIntire">
       John McIntire
      </a>
      ), a police lieutenant named Ditrich (
      <a href="/wiki/Barry_Kelley" title="Barry Kelley">
       Barry Kelley
      </a>
      ) (who had previously protected Cobby for money) beats the bookie into confessing everything in a vain attempt to save himself (he is later arrested for corruption).
     </p>
     <p>
      With the confession, Hardy personally arrests Emmerich, who manages to commit suicide. Gus is soon picked up, but when the police break down Ciavelli's door, they find they have interrupted his funeral.
     </p>
     <p>
      That leaves only Doc and Dix, who separate. Doc asks a taxi driver to drive him to
      <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">
       Cleveland
      </a>
      , but is spotted at a roadside cafe (where he has spent some time watching an attractive young woman dance) by two policemen. He offers no resistance. Doll gets Dix a car, then insists on going along. When he passes out from loss of blood, Doll takes him to a doctor, who phones the police to report the gunshot wound. Dix regains consciousness after a plasma transfusion and escapes before they arrive. With Doll, he makes it all the way back to his beloved
      <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">
       Kentucky
      </a>
      horse farm across the Ohio river from Cincinnati. He stumbles into the pasture and collapses, dead on arrival.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_production">
       Background and production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background and production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The film was an adaptation by director
      <a href="/wiki/John_Huston" title="John Huston">
       John Huston
      </a>
      and screenwriter
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Maddow" title="Ben Maddow">
       Ben Maddow
      </a>
      of the 1949 novel by
      <a href="/wiki/W._R._Burnett" title="W. R. Burnett">
       W. R. Burnett
      </a>
      . It was backed by the major film studio
      <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">
       Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer
      </a>
      , which allowed the production a relatively free hand.
     </p>
     <p>
      Both Huston and war hero star
      <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Hayden" title="Sterling Hayden">
       Sterling Hayden
      </a>
      were members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_the_First_Amendment" title="Committee for the First Amendment">
       Committee for the First Amendment
      </a>
      , which opposed the blacklisting of alleged communists active in the film industry during the
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">
       Red Scare
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naremore_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-naremore-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Production_Code_Administration" title="Production Code Administration">
       PCA
      </a>
      's main concerns with the script were the detailed depiction of the heist and the fact that the character of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fence_(criminal)" title="Fence (criminal)">
       fence
      </a>
      Emmerich seemed to cheat justice by killing himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naremore_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-naremore-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Neither the studio nor the censors interfered significantly with the script, however, and both the heist and the suicide featured in the final cut.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naremore_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-naremore-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
       Reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">
       Box office
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Box office">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to MGM records the film made $1,077,000 in the US and Canada and $1,060,000 overseas resulting in a profit of only $40,000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannix_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mannix-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-scott-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">
       Critical response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      When the film first opened, the staff at
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">
        Variety
       </a>
      </i>
      liked it, and wrote, "
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      is a study in crime, hard-hitting in its expose of the underworld. Ironic realism is striven for and achieved in the writing, production and direction. An audience will quite easily pull for the crooks in their execution of the million-dollar jewelry theft around which the plot is built."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">
       Bosley Crowther
      </a>
      , the film critic for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      , also liked the film, yet with a few caveats, writing, "This film, derived by Ben Maddow and John Huston from Mr. Burnett's book and directed by Mr. Huston in brilliantly naturalistic style, gives such an electrifying picture of the whole vicious circle of a crime...that one finds it hard to tag the item of repulsive exhibition in itself. Yet that is our inevitable judgment of this film, now on the Capitol's screen...But, in that meager interest, we've got to hand it to the boys, particularly to Mr. Huston: they've done a terrific job! From the very first shot, in which the camera picks up a prowling thug, sliding along between buildings to avoid a police car in the gray and liquid dawn, there is ruthless authority in this picture, the hardness and clarity of steel, and remarkably subtle suggestion that conveys a whole involvement of distorted personality and inveterate crime."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Film writer David M. Meyer noted, "The robbery is among the best-staged heists in
      <i>
       noir
      </i>
      . The simple visual treatment, the precise movements of the actors, and the absence of music on the sound track raise the tension to a boiling point."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-meyer-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The review aggregator
      <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">
       Rotten Tomatoes
      </a>
      reported that 96% of critics gave the film a positive review, based on twenty-five reviews.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotten_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rotten-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      was one of the most influential crime films of the 1950s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nnat_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nnat-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The film spawned a television series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Asphalt_Jungle_(TV_series)" title="The Asphalt Jungle (TV series)">
        The Asphalt Jungle
       </a>
      </i>
      , starring
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Warden" title="Jack Warden">
       Jack Warden
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arch_Johnson" title="Arch Johnson">
       Arch Johnson
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_(actor)" title="William Smith (actor)">
       William Smith
      </a>
      (billed as "Bill Smith"), which ran for thirteen episodes in the spring and summer of 1961 on
      <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
       ABC
      </a>
      . The series, though, resembled the film in name only, except for one episode, "The Professor," which was constructed as a sequel to the feature. Aside from this one-shot, however, none of the characters in the film appeared in the television scripts, and the plots were devoted to the exploits of the major case squad of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Police_Department" title="New York Police Department">
       New York Police Department
      </a>
      . One of the most notable features of the series is the theme song written by
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">
       Duke Ellington
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctva_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ctva-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Burnett's novel
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      was the basis of the western film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Badlanders" title="The Badlanders">
        The Badlanders
       </a>
      </i>
      (1958) directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Delmer_Daves" title="Delmer Daves">
       Delmer Daves
      </a>
      , as well as the
      <a href="/wiki/Blaxploitation" title="Blaxploitation">
       blaxploitation
      </a>
      film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cool_Breeze_(film)" title="Cool Breeze (film)">
        Cool Breeze
       </a>
      </i>
      (1972), directed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Pollack&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Barry Pollack (page does not exist)">
       Barry Pollack
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      instigated the crime thriller subgenre of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caper_film" title="Caper film">
       caper films
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naremore_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-naremore-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 1955 French film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rififi" title="Rififi">
        Rififi
       </a>
      </i>
      , which critics such as
      <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Maltin" title="Leonard Maltin">
       Leonard Maltin
      </a>
      have labeled as the best heist film ever, drew much inspiration from
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nnat_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nnat-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008,
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      was selected for preservation in the United States
      <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">
       National Film Registry
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      as being "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Colorization">
       Colorization
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Asphalt_Jungle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colorization">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The movie was the subject of a
      <a href="/wiki/Film_colorization" title="Film colorization">
       film colorization
      </a>
      lawsuit and controversy in France. Turner Entertainment Company entered into an agreement with the French television channel,
      <a href="/wiki/La_Cinq" title="La Cinq">
       La Cinq
      </a>
      , to broadcast the colorized movie.
      <a href="/wiki/John_Huston" title="John Huston">
       John Huston
      </a>
      's heirs objected, filing a lawsuit against broadcasting this version. In November 23, 1988,
      <i>
       The Asphalt Jungle
      </i>
      was prohibited from being broadcast in France. On July 6, 1989,
      <a href="/wiki/La_Cinq" title="La Cinq">
       La Cinq
      </a>
      won on appeal, broadcasting the film on August 6, 1989, the day after the anniversary of the death of
      <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">
       Marilyn Monroe
      </a>
      . Finally in
      <i>
       Turner Entertainment Co. v. Huston,
      </i>
      on May 28, 1991, the
      <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Cassation_(France)" title="Court of Cassation (France)">
       Court of Cassation
      </a>
      cancelled the judgment delivered on July 6, 1989, stating that colorizing the movie transformed the original artwork enough to potentially transgress the author's moral rights. Huston's heirs had sought to block the broadcast of the colorized version.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
