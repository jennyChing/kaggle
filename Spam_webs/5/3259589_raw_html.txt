        <h1 class="entry-title">
         The tax implications of getting married
        </h1>
        <div class="comments-link">
         <a href="http://wefile.it/blog/2013/03/the-tax-implications-of-getting-married/#respond">
          <span class="leave-reply">
           Leave a reply
          </span>
         </a>
        </div>
        <!-- .comments-link -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         If you get married, your financial situation is likely to change drastically. It should come as no surprise that your taxes are going to change as well.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="marriage" class="alignnone wp-image-199" height="240" src="http://wefile.it/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/03/marriage.png" width="512"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Married one day? Married all year according to the IRS
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Your marital status on the last day of the year determines what filing options you have for the year. Therefore, even if you get married on December 31st (like my parents did actually) then you were essentially married all year in the eyes of the IRS. As a married taxpayer you can either file a joint return with your spouse, you could both file separately, or you could file as head of household.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The advantages of filing a joint return
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         First and foremost, it can be easier when you file one joint return as opposed to two separate returns. Its also usually cheaper if you go to a tax preparer since youll only be paying to have one return prepared.
        </p>
        <p>
         Filing a joint return youll also be eligible for significant tax breaks such as the child care credit, and the higher education credits, that are not available to taxpayers filingseparately.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The marriage penalty
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         As a single taxpayer income up to $87,850 is taxed at 25%. Therefore 2 single taxpayers could make up to $175,700 and be taxed 25%. However, the limit on the 25% tax bracket for couples filing a joint return is $146,400. Anything above that (up to $223,050) is taxes at 28%. That 3% tax on $47,350 is income is over $1,400 in additional federal taxes per year.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Financial responsibility of filing a joint return
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Its worth noting that when you file a joint return you and your spouse are jointly and severally liability for the combine federal income tax. Therefore, the IRS can come after both spouses for any underpayments and penalties.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          IRS contributions for a spouse
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         If you have a traditional IRA you can make tax-deductible contributions, up to $5,000 for most taxpayers. Normally, you can only deduct contributions to your own IRA, but if you are married you can put money into a spouses IRA and deduct up to $10,000 in combined contributions.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Image viahttp://www.indusbusinessjournal.com/
         </em>
        </p>
