      <div class="page-title">
       <h1>
        03/05/14
        <br/>
        Keep Your Legs Under You:  Avoiding Injury on the Job
       </h1>
      </div>
      <div class="post-view">
       <div id="sharethis-icons">
        <span class="st_facebook_hcount" displaytext="share" st_title="Keep Your Legs Under You:  Avoiding Injury on the Job" st_url="http://www.blauer.com/dispatch/keep-your-legs-under-you-avoiding-injury-on-the-job/">
        </span>
        <span class="st_twitter_hcount" displaytext="share" st_title="Keep Your Legs Under You:  Avoiding Injury on the Job" st_url="http://www.blauer.com/dispatch/keep-your-legs-under-you-avoiding-injury-on-the-job/">
        </span>
        <span class="st_email_hcount" displaytext="share" st_title="Keep Your Legs Under You:  Avoiding Injury on the Job" st_url="http://www.blauer.com/dispatch/keep-your-legs-under-you-avoiding-injury-on-the-job/">
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry std">
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/wp/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/DSC_7720-copy.jpg">
          <img alt="DSC_7720 copy" class="size-medium wp-image-1825 alignleft" height="198" src="http://i113.photobucket.com/albums/n205/brandonblauer/DSC_7720-copy-300x198_zps9cdebb9c.jpg" width="300"/>
         </a>
         When was the last time you chased a suspect in exactly a straight line? Seems like a bit of a silly question  every time we run after someone, day or night, no matter the weather, chances are theyre going to be ducking, dodging, and generally changing direction at every possible opportunity to try to get away from us.
        </p>
        <p>
         Not surprisingly, then, in a recent
         <a href="http://www.theiacp.org/roi" target="_blank">
          IACP study
         </a>
         , the majority of injuries sustained by officers on a routine basis happened from the knees on down. There are a lot of factors contributing to why thats true, not least of which is the fact that we sometimes go from long periods of sitting in one position directly into running after someone, without the luxury of stretching beforehand. (Assuming youre not
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/dispatch/maintaining-your-microclimate-the-polar-vortex/" target="_blank">
          in the polar vortex
         </a>
         , its great to get out every so often to move around, by the way.)
        </p>
        <p>
         But one key factor, above all else, plays into the prevalence of leg, knee, and ankle injuries: the shoes or boots you choose to
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/law-enforcement/footwear.html" target="_blank">
          put on your feet
         </a>
         may not, in fact, be the right choice for the types of activity which occur on the job, and may be putting you at higher risk of getting hurt. Lets talk about that a bit.
        </p>
        <p>
         Im sure you remember being a kid, going into the shoe store and having the salesman check your foot size with one of
         <a href="http://www.brannock.com/" target="_blank">
          these
         </a>
         , because hey, you were a kid, and probably changing sizes on a weekly basis (or so it seemed to your parents). When most of us become adults, however, we simply assume that we know our size, and tend to stick with one particular brand because we believe that their shoes just fit our feet better. Even in one brand, however, the last (or mold) that the shoe is created on can vary more significantly than you might think.
        </p>
        <p>
         The truth of the matter is that getting measured for the proper
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/law-enforcement/footwear.html" target="_blank">
          footwear
         </a>
         fit is
         <i>
          critical
         </i>
         to not only not getting hurt, but also how well you can perform and how comfortable you are throughout your shift. Most of us with narrower feet, for example, tend to wear a smaller size because we think it fits us better, but in reality whats happening is that the reduction in material over the top of the foot is poorly compensating for the incorrect width, compressing your toes unnecessarily. The opposite is true for those of us with wide feet, where getting enough room for our forefoot often means trying to crank down the laces to keep our ankles supported.
        </p>
        <p>
         From there, something else to consider: many of the
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/law-enforcement/footwear.html" target="_blank">
          boots
         </a>
         now on the public safety market were designed around an athletic shoe type  which is okay for comfort, but do you really want to be wearing a running shoe when youre unable to focus on hazards on the ground because youre watching a suspects movements for your own safety? Most running shoes, if you look at them, are narrow at the heel, making it easy to roll your ankle.
        </p>
        <p>
         A lot of whether or not you get injured
         <a href="http://fitnesspainfree.com/6-common-causes-of-knee-pain-and-how-to-fix-them-part-1/" target="_blank">
          comes down to stability
         </a>
          the pivots, twists, turns, lateral movements, and more that come not only when youre chasing down a suspect on foot, but also during the course of everything you do, place strain on your feet, legs, and ankles. If youre doing all of that, and you dont have the right platform to base your movement off of (for example, a boot with a
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/law-enforcement/footwear/blitz-8-waterproof-boot-fw028wp.html" target="_blank">
          midsole board
         </a>
         can not only provide more support, but also prevent puncture injuries as well), your chances of getting injured are much higher. Other considerations include having the proper ankle support (including a good
         <a href="http://www.boatechnology.com/" target="_blank">
          lacing system
         </a>
         ), a cushioned heel cage, and a good cleat pattern (like one found in a tennis shoe  which allows for movement in all directions.)
        </p>
        <p>
         If you do get an injury, most orthopedic specialists will recommend that you follow the RICE treatment method  Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation. For those in law enforcement, however, the acronym seems to be ICER  I Cant Escape Running  a lot of the time meaning well keep plowing through even when our bodies are telling us that somethings not right, ignoring the pain so that we can keep going and doing our jobs. Its important to realize that you need to listen to that in order to prevent even further injury, however  even a twinge could mean putting yourself or others at risk, so get it checked out.
        </p>
        <p>
         Overall, having the
         <a href="http://www.blauer.com/law-enforcement/footwear.html" target="_blank">
          right footwear
         </a>
         for the job can prevent you from getting hurt in the first place, which not only affects your safety at work, but your life off the job as well. That means finding the right boots, with the right features, for you  so go get measured, and really try on the different options to find not only the one that will feel right while youre in the store, but every day out on the street as well, which means going through as many of your everyday actions as you can. It could make the difference between staying safe, and being injured and out of the fight.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>

He was additionally a member of a Federal medical disaster relief team for ten years, with experience responding to the aftermath of Hurricane Katrina, and the pre-deployment of resources for Hurricane Ike. Greg currently has a passion for educating public safety professionals about matters which impact their lives every day, and welcomes feedback and suggestions in the spirit of ensuring that best practices make it out there for all to benefit from.
         </i>
