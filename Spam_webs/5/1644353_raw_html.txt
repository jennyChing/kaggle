     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Gila monster
      </b>
      (
      <i>
       Heloderma suspectum
      </i>
      ,
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
           h
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ long 'e' in 'bead'">
           i
          </span>
          <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
           l
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      <span class="Unicode" title="English pronunciation respelling">
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i>
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             HEE
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -l
        </i>
       </a>
      </span>
      ) is a species of
      <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">
       venomous
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">
       lizard
      </a>
      native to the
      <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">
       southwestern United States
      </a>
      and northwestern Mexican state of
      <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">
       Sonora
      </a>
      . A heavy, slow-moving lizard, up to 60cm (2.0ft) long, the Gila monster is the only venomous lizard native to the United States and one of only two known species of venomous lizards in North America, the other being its close relative, the Mexican
      <a href="/wiki/Beaded_lizard" title="Beaded lizard">
       beaded lizard
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       H. horridum
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fry_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fry-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Though the Gila monster is venomous, its sluggish nature means it represents little threat to humans. However, it has earned a fearsome reputation and is sometimes killed despite being protected by state law in
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
       Arizona
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITIS_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ITIS-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description.2C_taxonomy_and_etymology">
       Description, taxonomy and etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description, taxonomy and etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In this species, the largest extant lizard native to
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      north of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_border" title="Mexican border">
       Mexican border
      </a>
      (non-natives like
      <a href="/wiki/Green_iguana" title="Green iguana">
       green iguanas
      </a>
      are larger), snout-to-vent length is from 26 to 36cm (10 to 14in). The tail is about 20% of the body size and the largest specimens may reach 51 to 56cm (20 to 22in) in total length. Body mass is typically in the range of 350 to 700g (0.77 to 1.54lb), with 11 males having been found to average 468g (1.032lb). Reportedly, the very heaviest, largest specimens can weigh as much as 2,300g (5.1lb).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Gila monster has one close living relative, the beaded lizard (
      <i>
       H. horridum
      </i>
      ), as well as many extinct relatives in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helodermatidae" title="Helodermatidae">
       Helodermatidae
      </a>
      , the evolutionary history of which may be traced back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">
       Cretaceous
      </a>
      period. The genus
      <i>
       Heloderma
      </i>
      has existed since the
      <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">
       Miocene
      </a>
      , when
      <i>
       H. texana
      </i>
      lived, and fragments of
      <a href="/wiki/Osteoderm" title="Osteoderm">
       osteoderms
      </a>
      from the Gila monster have been found in late
      <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">
       Pleistocene
      </a>
      (10,0008,000 years ago) deposits near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">
       Las Vegas, Nevada
      </a>
      . Because the helodermatids have remained relatively unchanged morphologically, they are occasionally regarded as
      <a href="/wiki/Living_fossil" title="Living fossil">
       living fossils
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varanoid_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-varanoid-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the Gila monster appears closely related to the
      <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">
       monitor lizards
      </a>
      (varanids) of Africa, Asia and Australia, their wide geographical separation and the unique features not found in the varanids indicate the Gila monster is better placed in a separate family.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mattison_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mattison-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="357" data-file-width="1387" height="57" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg/220px-Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg/330px-Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg/440px-Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gila_monster_ncd_2012.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Plate from the
        <i>
         Century Cyclopedia
        </i>
        depicts the Gila monster
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The name "Gila" refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/Gila_River" title="Gila River">
       Gila River
      </a>
      Basin in the U.S. states of
      <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
       New Mexico
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
       Arizona
      </a>
      , where the Gila monster was once plentiful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Heloderma
      </i>
      means "studded skin", from the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Ancient Greek
      </a>
      words
      <i>
       helos
      </i>
      (), "the head of a nail or stud", and
      <i>
       derma
      </i>
      (), "skin".
      <i>
       Suspectum
      </i>
      comes from the describer, paleontologist
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">
       Edward Drinker Cope
      </a>
      , who suspected the lizard might be venomous due to the grooves in the teeth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varanoid_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-varanoid-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div style="clear:both;">
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitats">
       Distribution and habitats
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitats">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gila_Monster_head.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1321" data-file-width="1458" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Gila_Monster_head.jpg/220px-Gila_Monster_head.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Gila_Monster_head.jpg/330px-Gila_Monster_head.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Gila_Monster_head.jpg/440px-Gila_Monster_head.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gila_Monster_head.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Head with bead-like scales and strong forelegs and claws suitable for digging
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Gila monster is found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">
       Southwestern United States
      </a>
      and Mexico, a range including
      <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">
       Sonora
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
       Arizona
      </a>
      , parts of
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">
       Nevada
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">
       Utah
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
       New Mexico
      </a>
      (but not Baja California). They inhabit
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland">
       scrubland
      </a>
      , succulent desert, and oak woodland, seeking shelter in burrows, thickets, and under rocks in locations with ready access to moisture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stebbins_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stebbins-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In fact, Gila monsters seem to like water and can be observed immersing themselves in puddles of water after a summer rain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cavendish_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cavendish-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They avoid living in open areas such as flats and farmland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nzp_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nzp-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">
       Ecology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Gila monsters spend 90% of their time underground in burrows or rocky shelters.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      They are active in the morning during the dry season (spring and early summer); later in the summer, they may be active on warm nights or after a
      <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">
       thunderstorm
      </a>
      . They maintain a surface body temperature of about 30C (86F).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greene_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-greene-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gila monsters are slow in sprinting ability, but they have relatively high endurance and maximal aerobic capacity (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VO2max" title="VO2max">
       VO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       max
      </a>
      ) for a lizard.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becketal1985_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Becketal1985-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They are preyed upon by coyotes and raptors.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">
       Diet
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Gila monster eats small birds, mammals, frogs, lizards, insects, and
      <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">
       carrion
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animal_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-animal-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Gila monster feeds primarily on bird and reptile
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">
       eggs
      </a>
      , and eats infrequently (only five to ten times a year in the wild),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but when it does feed, it may eat up to one-third of its body mass.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It uses its extremely acute sense of smell to locate prey, especially eggs. Its sense of smell is so keen, it can locate and dig up chicken eggs buried 15cm (6in) deep and accurately follow a trail made by rolling an egg.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mattison_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mattison-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Prey may be crushed to death if large or eaten alive if small, swallowed head-first, and helped down by muscular contractions and neck flexing. Unusually, after food has been swallowed, the Gila monster immediately resumes tongue flicking and search behavior, probably as a result of a history of finding clumped prey such as eggs and young in nests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greene_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-greene-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gila monsters are able to climb trees and
      <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">
       cacti
      </a>
      in search of eggs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netherton_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-netherton-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Venom">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_beliefs">
       Pioneer beliefs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pioneer beliefs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/American_frontier" title="American frontier">
       Old West
      </a>
      , the pioneers believed a number of myths about the Gila monster, including that the lizard had foul or toxic breath and that its bite is fatal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapidus_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lapidus-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tombstone_Epitaph" title="The Tombstone Epitaph">
        The Tombstone Epitaph
       </a>
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Tombstone,_Arizona" title="Tombstone, Arizona">
       Tombstone, Arizona
      </a>
      , wrote about a Gila monster that a local person caught on May 14, 1881:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       This is a monster, and no baby at that, it being probably the largest specimen ever captured in Arizona. It is 27 inches long and weighs 35 lb. It was caught by H. C. Hiatt on the road between Tombstone and Grand Central Mill and was purchased by Messrs. Ed Baker and Charles Eastman, who now have it on exhibition at Kelley's Wine House, next door above Grand Hotel, Allen Street. Eastern people who have never seen one of these monsters should not fail to inspect his Aztecship, for they might accidentally stumble upon one some fine day and get badly frightened, except they know what it is.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       
       <cite>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapidus_19-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-lapidus-19">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          19
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      On May 8, 1890, southeast of
      <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">
       Tucson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Territory" title="Arizona Territory">
       Arizona Territory
      </a>
      , Empire Ranch owner Walter Vail captured and thought he had killed a Gila monster. He tied it to his saddle and it bit the middle finger of his right hand and wouldn't let go. A ranch hand pried open the lizard's mouth with a pocketknife, cut open his finger to stimulate bleeding, and then tied saddle strings around his finger and wrist. They summoned Dr. John C. Handy of Tucson, who took Vail back to Tucson for treatment, but Vail experienced swollen and bleeding
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymph_nodes" title="Lymph nodes">
       glands
      </a>
      in his throat for sometime afterward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapidus_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-lapidus-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dr. Handy's friend, Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/George_E._Goodfellow" title="George E. Goodfellow">
       George Goodfellow
      </a>
      of Tombstone, was among the first to research the actual effects of Gila monster venom.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">
        Scientific American
       </a>
      </i>
      reported in 1890 that "The breath is very fetid, and its odor can be detected at some little distance from the lizard. It is supposed that this is one way in which the monster catches the insects and small animals which form a part of its food supplythe foul gas overcoming them." Goodfellow offered to pay local residents $5.00 for Gila monster specimens. He bought several and collected more on his own. In 1891 he purposefully provoked one of his captive lizards into biting him on his finger. The bite made him ill and he spent the next five days in bed, but he completely recovered. When
      <i>
       Scientific American
      </i>
      ran another ill-founded report on the lizard's ability to kill people, he wrote in reply and described his own studies and personal experience. He wrote that he knew several people who had been bitten by Gila monsters but had not died from the bite.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapidus_19-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-lapidus-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Goodfellow published articles about rattlesnake and Gila monster bites in
      <i>
       Scientific American
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Southern California Practitioner
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allen_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-allen-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <br/>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Delivery">
       Delivery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Delivery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Gila monster produces venom in modified
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salivary_glands" title="Salivary glands">
       salivary glands
      </a>
      in its lower jaw, unlike snakes, whose venom is produced in the upper jaw.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animal_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-animal-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Gila monster lacks the musculature to forcibly inject the venom; instead, the venom is propelled from the gland to the tooth by chewing.
      <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">
       Capillary action
      </a>
      brings the venom out of the tooth and into the victim.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varanoid_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-varanoid-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The teeth are loosely anchored, which allows them to be broken off and replaced throughout life. Gila monsters have been observed to flip over while
      <a href="/wiki/Lizard_bite" title="Lizard bite">
       biting
      </a>
      the victim, presumably to aid the flow of the venom into the wound. Because the Gila monster's prey consists mainly of eggs, small animals, and otherwise "helpless" prey, the Gila monster's venom is thought to have evolved for defensive rather than for hunting use. A defensive use would explain the Gila monster's bright warning coloration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mattison_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-mattison-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">
       Toxicity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Toxicity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2189" data-file-width="2916" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg/220px-Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg/330px-Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg/440px-Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reticulate_Gila_Monster.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A reticulated Gila monster (
        <i>
         H. s. suspectum
        </i>
        )
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although the venom is a
      <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxin" title="Neurotoxin">
       neurotoxin
      </a>
      as toxic as that of a
      <a href="/wiki/Coral_snake" title="Coral snake">
       coral snake
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       H. suspectum
      </i>
      produces only small amounts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nzp_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nzp-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Gila monster's bite is not fatal to healthy adult humans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      No reports of fatalities have been confirmed after 1939, and those recorded prior to that year are possibly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iatrogenic" title="Iatrogenic">
       iatrogenic
      </a>
      , or resulting from attempts to treat the bite itself.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The Gila monster can bite quickly (especially by swinging its head sideways) and hold on tenaciously and painfully.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greene_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-greene-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If bitten, the victim may need to fully submerge the attacking lizard in water to break free from its bite. Symptoms of the bite include excruciating
      <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">
       pain
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">
       edema
      </a>
      , and weakness associated with a rapid drop in
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">
       blood pressure
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      More than a dozen
      <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">
       peptides
      </a>
      and other substances have been isolated from the Gila monster's venom, including
      <a href="/wiki/Hyaluronidase" title="Hyaluronidase">
       hyaluronidase
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">
       serotonin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Phospholipase" title="Phospholipase">
       phospholipase
      </a>
      A
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      , and several
      <a href="/wiki/Kallikrein" title="Kallikrein">
       kallikrein
      </a>
      -like
      <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">
       glycoproteins
      </a>
      responsible for the pain and edema caused by a bite. Four potentially lethal toxins have been isolated from the Gila monster's venom, including horridum venom, which causes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage">
       hemorrhage
      </a>
      in internal organs and exophthalmos (bulging of the eyes)
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.herpetology.com/helobite.txt" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
      , and helothermine, which causes
      <a href="/wiki/Lethargy" title="Lethargy">
       lethargy
      </a>
      , partial
      <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">
       paralysis
      </a>
      of the limbs, and
      <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">
       hypothermia
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">
       rats
      </a>
      . Most are similar in form to
      <a href="/wiki/Vasoactive_intestinal_peptide" title="Vasoactive intestinal peptide">
       vasoactive intestinal peptide
      </a>
      (VIP), which relaxes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">
       smooth muscle
      </a>
      and regulates water and
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
       electrolyte
      </a>
      secretion between the
      <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">
       small
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">
       large intestines
      </a>
      . These bioactive peptides are able to bind to VIP receptors in many different human tissues. One of these, helodermin, has been shown to inhibit the growth of
      <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">
       lung cancer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varanoid_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-varanoid-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The constituents of the lizard's venom that have received the most attention from researchers are the bioactive peptides, including helodermin, helospectin, exendin-3, and exendin-4.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Exendin-4 has formed the basis of a class of medications for the treatment of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_2_diabetes" title="Type 2 diabetes">
       type 2 diabetes
      </a>
      , known as
      <a href="/wiki/Glucagon-like_peptide-1" title="Glucagon-like peptide-1">
       Glucagon-like peptide-1
      </a>
      agonists.
      <a href="/wiki/Exenatide" title="Exenatide">
       Exenatide
      </a>
      was the first product in the class to reach the market and was launched in 2005.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Drug_research">
       Drug research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Drug research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="border:1px solid #aaa;background-color:#f9f9f9">
      <tr>
       <td class="mbox-image">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="759" height="22" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Wikinews-logo.svg/40px-Wikinews-logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Wikinews-logo.svg/60px-Wikinews-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Wikinews-logo.svg/80px-Wikinews-logo.svg.png 2x" width="40"/>
       </td>
       <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
        Wikinews has related news:
        <i>
         <b>
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikinews.org/wiki/New_drug_from_lizard%27s_saliva" title="wikinews:New drug from lizard's saliva">
           New drug from lizard's saliva
          </a>
         </b>
        </i>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      In 2005, the US
      <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">
       Food and Drug Administration
      </a>
      approved the drug
      <a href="/wiki/Exenatide" title="Exenatide">
       exenatide
      </a>
      (marketed as Byetta) for the management of
      <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_2" title="Diabetes mellitus type 2">
       type 2 diabetes
      </a>
      . It is a synthetic version of a protein, exendin-4, derived from the Gila monster's saliva.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baylor_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Baylor-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a three-year study with people with type 2 diabetes, exenatide led to healthy sustained
      <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">
       glucose
      </a>
      levels and progressive weight loss. The effectiveness is because the lizard protein is about 50% identical to
      <a href="/wiki/Glucagon-like_peptide-1" title="Glucagon-like peptide-1">
       glucagon-like peptide-1
      </a>
      analog (GLP-1), a hormone released from the human digestive tract that helps to regulate
      <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">
       insulin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Glucagon" title="Glucagon">
       glucagon
      </a>
      . The lizard protein remains effective much longer than the human hormone, helping diabetics keep their blood sugar levels under control. Exenatide slows the emptying of the stomach and causes a decrease in appetite, contributing to weight loss.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drug_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-drug-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The saliva of the Gila monster contains many chemicals which can be deadly. One of these has been shown to affect memory. Several companies have been researching the abilities of this chemical to help memory loss due to various diseases such as Alzheimers, schizophrenia, and ADHD. Gilatide, derived from exendin-4, has been shown to dramatically heighten memory in a study with mice. Gilatide is likely to be researched further to provide help to Alzheimers patients.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_history">
       Life history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Life history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gila.monster.arp.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1345" data-file-width="1813" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gila.monster.arp.jpg/220px-Gila.monster.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gila.monster.arp.jpg/330px-Gila.monster.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gila.monster.arp.jpg/440px-Gila.monster.arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gila.monster.arp.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Gila monster at the
        <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Zoo" title="Bristol Zoo">
         Bristol Zoo
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Gila monster emerges from
      <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">
       hibernation
      </a>
      in January or February and mates in May and June.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animal_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-animal-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The male initiates courtship by flicking his tongue to search for the female's scent. If the female rejects his advances, she will bite him and crawl away. When successful,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copulation" title="Copulation">
       copulation
      </a>
      has been observed to last from 15 minutes to as long as two and a half hours. The female lays eggs in July or August, burying them in sand 5in (13cm) below the surface. The clutch consists of two to 12 eggs: five is the average.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stebbins_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stebbins-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The incubation lasts nine months, as the hatchlings emerge during April through June the following year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seward_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Seward-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The hatchlings are about 16cm (6.3in) long and can bite and inject venom upon hatching. The juveniles typically have larger bands of pink scales than adults, although the banded Gila monster (
      <i>
       H. s. cinctum
      </i>
      ) has a tendency to retain the band pattern.
      <i>
       H. suspectum
      </i>
      sexually matures at three to five years old. After egg-laying, adult Gila monsters gradually spend less time on the surface to avoid the hottest part of the summer (although they may be active in the evening), eventually starting their
      <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">
       hibernation
      </a>
      around November.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mattison_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-mattison-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Little is known about the social behavior of
      <i>
       H. suspectum
      </i>
      , but they have been observed engaging in male-male combat, in which the dominant male lies on top of the subordinate one and pins it with its front and hind limbs. Both lizards arch their bodies, pushing against each other and twisting around in an effort to gain the dominant position. A wrestling match ends when the pressure exerted forces them to separate, although bouts may be repeated one after the other. These bouts are typically observed just before the mating season. Those with greater strength and endurance are thought to win more often and enjoy greater reproductive success.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the Gila monster has a low metabolism and one of the lowest lizard sprint speeds, it has one of the highest aerobic scope values (the increase in
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      consumption from rest to maximum metabolic exertion) among lizards, allowing them to engage in intense aerobic activity for a sustained period of time. Males have been observed to have higher aerobic scopes than females, presumably because of
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">
       sexual selection
      </a>
      for a trait advantageous in prolonged combat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becketal1985_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Becketal1985-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Gila monster may live up to 20 years in the wild, or 30 in captivity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandiego_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sandiego-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       <a href="#cite_note-cavendish-11">

        <span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_humans">
       Relationship with humans
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gila_monster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Relationship with humans">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Though the Gila monster is venomous, its laggard movement means it poses little threat to humans. However, it has earned a fearsome reputation and is often killed by humans because of fear. Among Native American tribes, the Gila monster had a mixed standing. The
      <a href="/wiki/Apache" title="Apache">
       Apache
      </a>
      believed its breath could kill a man, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tohono_O%27Odham" title="Tohono O'Odham">
       Tohono O'Odham
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Pima_people" title="Pima people">
       Pima
      </a>
      believed it possessed a spiritual power that could cause sickness. In contrast, the
      <a href="/wiki/Seri_people" title="Seri people">
       Seri
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaqui_people" title="Yaqui people">
       Yaqui
      </a>
      believed the Gila monster's hide had healing properties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nzp_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nzp-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Gila monster has even starred as a monster in a
      <a href="/wiki/B_movie" title="B movie">
       B movie
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Giant_Gila_Monster" title="The Giant Gila Monster">
        The Giant Gila Monster
       </a>
      </i>
      . It played a minor role in the motion picture
      <a href="/wiki/The_Treasure_of_the_Sierra_Madre_(film)" title="The Treasure of the Sierra Madre (film)">
       <i>
        The Treasure of the Sierra Madre
       </i>
      </a>
      . Myths about the animal include that the animal's breath is toxic enough to kill humans, that it can spit venom and that it can leap several feet in the air to attack.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandiego_29-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sandiego-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another myth held that the Gila monster did not have an anus and therefore expelled waste from its mouth, the source of its venom and "fetid breath".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greene_13-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-greene-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The official mascot of
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Arizona_College" title="Eastern Arizona College">
       Eastern Arizona College
      </a>
      located in
      <a href="/wiki/Thatcher,_Arizona" title="Thatcher, Arizona">
       Thatcher, Arizona
      </a>
      is Gila Hank, a gun-toting, cowboy hat-wearing Gila monster. A similar character as an old western outlaw was seen in 2011 animated film
      <i>
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rango" title="Rango">
        Rango
       </a>
