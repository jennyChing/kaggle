   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Nonpoint source pollution
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muddy_USGS.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="732" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Muddy_USGS.jpg/300px-Muddy_USGS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Muddy_USGS.jpg/450px-Muddy_USGS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Muddy_USGS.jpg/600px-Muddy_USGS.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muddy_USGS.jpg" title="Enlarge">

        </div>
        Muddy river
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Non-point source (NPS) pollution
      </b>
      refers to both water and
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollution
      </a>
      from diffuse sources. Non-point source
      <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">
       water pollution
      </a>
      affects a water body from sources such as polluted
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">
       runoff
      </a>
      from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural" title="Agricultural">
       agricultural
      </a>
      areas draining into a river, or wind-borne debris blowing out to sea. Non-point source air pollution affects air quality from sources such as smokestacks or car tailpipes. Although these pollutants have originated from a point source, the long-range transport ability and multiple sources of the pollutant make it a non-point source of pollution. Non-point source pollution can be contrasted with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_source_(pollution)" title="Point source (pollution)">
       point source pollution
      </a>
      , where discharges occur to a body of water or into the atmosphere at a single location.
     </p>
     <p>
      NPS may derive from many different sources with no specific solution may change to rectify the problem, making it difficult to regulate. Non point source water pollution is difficult to control because it comes from the everyday activities of many different people, such as fertilizing a lawn, using a pesticide, or constructing a road or building.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is the leading cause of water pollution in the United States today, with polluted runoff from agriculture the primary cause.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other significant sources of runoff include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydromodification" title="Hydromodification">
       hydrological
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">
       habitat modification
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">
       silviculture
      </a>
      (forestry).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Contaminated storm-water washed off parking lots, roads and highways, and lawns (often containing
      <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">
       fertilizers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">
       pesticides
      </a>
      ) is called
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">
       urban runoff
      </a>
      . This runoff is often classified as a type of NPS pollution. Some people may also consider it a point source because many times it is channeled into municipal storm drain systems and discharged through pipes to nearby
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">
       surface waters
      </a>
      . However, not all urban runoff flows through
      <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">
       storm drain
      </a>
      systems before entering water bodies. Some may flow directly into water bodies, especially in developing and suburban areas. Also, unlike other types of point sources, such as industrial discharges,
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">
       sewage treatment
      </a>
      plants and other operations, pollution in urban runoff cannot be attributed to one activity or even group of activities. Therefore, because it is not caused by an easily identified and regulated activity, urban runoff pollution sources are also often treated as true non-point sources as municipalities work to abate them.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sediment">
       Sediment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sediment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">
       Sediment
      </a>
      (loose
      <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
       soil
      </a>
      ) includes
      <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">
       silt
      </a>
      (fine particles) and
      <a href="/wiki/Suspended_solids" title="Suspended solids">
       suspended solids
      </a>
      (larger particles). Sediment may enter surface waters from eroding stream banks, and from
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">
       surface runoff
      </a>
      due to improper plant cover on urban and rural land
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAfact_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IAfact-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sediment creates
      <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">
       turbidity
      </a>
      (cloudiness) in water bodies, reducing the amount of light reaching lower depths, which can inhibit growth of submerged
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_plants" title="Aquatic plants">
       aquatic plants
      </a>
      and consequently affect species which are dependent on them, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">
       fish
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">
       shellfish
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      High turbidity levels also inhibit drinking
      <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">
       water purification
      </a>
      systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sediment can also be discharged from multiple different sources. Sources include
      <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">
       construction
      </a>
      sites (although these are point sources, which can be managed with
      <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">
       erosion controls
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sediment_control" title="Sediment control">
       sediment controls
      </a>
      ), agricultural fields, stream banks, and highly disturbed areas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSUNonpoint_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PSUNonpoint-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrients">
       Nutrients
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nutrients">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Nutrients mainly refers to inorganic matter from runoff,
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">
       landfills
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">
       livestock
      </a>
      operations and crop lands. The two primary nutrients of concern are phosphorus and nitrogen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohio_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ohio-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">
       Phosphorus
      </a>
      is a nutrient that occurs in many forms that are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioavailable" title="Bioavailable">
       bioavailable
      </a>
      . It is notoriously over-abundant in human sewage
      <a href="/wiki/Sludge" title="Sludge">
       sludge
      </a>
      . It is a main ingredient in many fertilizers used for agriculture as well as on residential and commercial properties, and may become a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limiting_nutrient" title="Limiting nutrient">
       limiting nutrient
      </a>
      in freshwater systems and some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">
       estuaries
      </a>
      . Phosphorus is most often transported to water bodies via
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">
       soil erosion
      </a>
      because many forms of phosphorus tend to be
      <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">
       adsorbed
      </a>
      on to soil particles. Excess amounts of phosphorus in aquatic systems (particularly freshwater lakes, reservoirs, and ponds) leads to proliferation of microscopic algae called
      <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">
       phytoplankton
      </a>
      . The increase of organic matter supply due to the excessive growth of the phytoplankton is called
      <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">
       eutrophication
      </a>
      . A common symptom of eutrophication is
      <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">
       algae
      </a>
      blooms that can produce unsightly surface scums, shade out beneficial types of plants, and poison the water due to toxins produced by the algae. These toxins are a particular problem in systems used for drinking water because some toxins can cause human illness and removal of the toxins is difficult and expensive. Bacterial decomposition of algal blooms consumes dissolved oxygen in the water, generating
      <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">
       hypoxia
      </a>
      with detrimental consequences for fish and aquatic invertebrates.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       Nitrogen
      </a>
      is the other key ingredient in fertilizers, and it generally becomes a pollutant in saltwater or brackish estuarine systems where nitrogen is a limiting nutrient. Similar to phosphorus in fresh-waters, excess amounts of bioavailable nitrogen in marine systems lead to eutrophication and algae blooms. Hypoxia is an increasingly common result of eutrophication in marine systems and can impact large areas of estuaries, bays, and near shore coastal waters. Each summer, hypoxic conditions form in
      <a href="/wiki/Bottom_water" title="Bottom water">
       bottom waters
      </a>
      where the
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">
       Mississippi River
      </a>
      enters the
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">
       Gulf of Mexico
      </a>
      . During recent summers, the aereal extent of this "dead zone" is comparable to the area of
      <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">
       New Jersey
      </a>
      and has major detrimental consequences for fisheries in the region.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nitrogen is most often transported by water as
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">
       nitrate
      </a>
      (NO
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      ). The nitrogen is usually added to a watershed as organic-N or
      <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
       ammonia
      </a>
      (NH
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      ), so nitrogen stays attached to the soil until
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">
       oxidation
      </a>
      converts it into nitrate. Since the nitrate is generally already incorporated into the soil, the water traveling through the soil (i.e.,
      <a href="/wiki/Interflow" title="Interflow">
       interflow
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tile_drainage" title="Tile drainage">
       tile drainage
      </a>
      ) is the most likely to transport it, rather than surface runoff.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Toxic_contaminants_and_chemicals">
       Toxic contaminants and chemicals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Toxic contaminants and chemicals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Compounds including heavy metals like
      <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">
       lead
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
       mercury
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
       zinc
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">
       cadmium
      </a>
      , organics like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyls" title="Polychlorinated biphenyls">
       polychlorinated biphenyls
      </a>
      (PCBs) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">
       polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons
      </a>
      (PAHs), fire retardants, and other substances are resistant to breakdown.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSUNonpoint_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-PSUNonpoint-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These contaminants can come from a variety of sources including human sewage sludge,
      <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">
       mining
      </a>
      operations, vehicle emissions,
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuel
      </a>
      combustion, urban runoff, industrial operations and landfills.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohio_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ohio-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Toxic chemicals mainly include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">
       organic compounds
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic_compounds" title="Inorganic compounds">
       inorganic compounds
      </a>
      . These compounds include pesticides like
      <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">
       DDT
      </a>
      , acids, and salts that have severe effects to the ecosystem and water-bodies. These compounds can threaten the health of both humans and aquatic species while being resistant to environmental breakdown, thus allowing them to persist in the environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSUNonpoint_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-PSUNonpoint-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These toxic chemicals could come from croplands, nurseries, orchards, building sites, gardens, lawns and landfills.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohio_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ohio-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Acids and salts mainly are inorganic pollutants from irrigated lands, mining operations, urban runoff, industrial sites and landfills.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohio_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ohio-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogens">
       Pathogens
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pathogens">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">
       Pathogens
      </a>
      are bacteria and viruses that can be found in water and cause diseases in humans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSUNonpoint_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-PSUNonpoint-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Typically, pathogens cause disease when they are present in public drinking water supplies. Pathogens found in contaminated runoff may include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidium_parvum" title="Cryptosporidium parvum">
         Cryptosporidium parvum
        </a>
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Giardia_lamblia" title="Giardia lamblia">
         Giardia lamblia
        </a>
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">
         Salmonella
        </a>
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novovirus" title="Novovirus">
         Novovirus
        </a>
       </i>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">
        viruses
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasitic_worm" title="Parasitic worm">
        Parasitic worms
       </a>
       (helminths).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Coliform_bacteria" title="Coliform bacteria">
       Coliform bacteria
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fecal_matter" title="Fecal matter">
       fecal matter
      </a>
      may also be detected in runoff.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSUNonpoint_8-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-PSUNonpoint-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These
      <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">
       bacteria
      </a>
      are a commonly used
      <a href="/wiki/Indicator_bacteria" title="Indicator bacteria">
       indicator
      </a>
      of water pollution, but not an actual cause of disease.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Pathogens may contaminate runoff due to poorly managed livestock operations, faulty
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septic_system" title="Septic system">
       septic systems
      </a>
      , improper handling of pet waste, the over application of human sewage
      <a href="/wiki/Sludge" title="Sludge">
       sludge
      </a>
      , contaminated storm sewers, and
      <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">
       sanitary sewer overflows
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAfact_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IAfact-6">

        </span>
        6
        <span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_sources">
       Principal sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Principal sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_and_suburban_areas">
       Urban and suburban areas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Urban and suburban areas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_Area" title="Urban Area">
       Urban
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban">
       suburban areas
      </a>
      are a main sources of nonpoint source pollution due to the amount of runoff that is produced due to the large amount of paved surfaces. Paved surfaces, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">
       asphalt
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">
       concrete
      </a>
      are impervious to water penetrating them. Any water that is on contact with these surfaces will run off and be absorbed by the surrounding environment. These surfaces make it easier for stormwater to carry pollutants into the surrounding soil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Construction sites tend to have disturbed soil that is easily eroded by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">
       precipitation
      </a>
      like
      <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">
       rain
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">
       snow
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">
       hail
      </a>
      . Additionally, discarded debris on the site can be carried away by runoff waters and enter the aquatic environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Typically, in suburban areas, chemicals are used for lawn care. These chemicals can end up in runoff and enter the surrounding environment via storm drains in the city. Since the water in storm drains is not treated before flowing into surrounding water bodies, the chemicals enter the water directly.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_operations">
       Agricultural operations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Agricultural operations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Agricultural operations account for a large percentage of all nonpoint source pollution in the United States. When large tracts of land are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plow" title="Plow">
       plowed
      </a>
      to grow
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crops" title="Crops">
       crops
      </a>
      , it exposes and loosens soil that was once buried. This makes the exposed soil more vulnerable to
      <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
       erosion
      </a>
      during
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainstorm" title="Rainstorm">
       rainstorms
      </a>
      . It also can increase the amount of fertilizer and pesticides carried into nearby bodies of water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_inputs">
       Atmospheric inputs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Atmospheric inputs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Atmospheric inputs of pollution into the air can come from multiple sources. Typically, industrial facilities, like
      <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">
       factories
      </a>
      , emit air pollution via a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smokestack" title="Smokestack">
       smokestack
      </a>
      . Although this is a point source, due to the distributional nature, long-range transport, and multiple sources of the pollution, it is considered a nonpoint source. Additionally, atmospheric pollution can become water pollution, by being washed out of the atmosphere in the form of rain or snow.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Highway_Runoff">
       Highway Runoff
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Highway Runoff">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Highway runoff accounts for a small but widespread percentage of all nonpoint source pollution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Harned (1988) estimated that runoff loads were composed of atmospheric fallout (9%), vehicle deposition (25%) and highway maintenance materials (67%) he also estimated that about 9 percent of these loads were reentrained in the atmosphere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>

        </span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_and_mining_operations">
       Forestry and mining operations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Forestry and mining operations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">
       Forestry
      </a>
      and mining operations can have significant inputs to non-point source pollution.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry">
       Forestry
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Forestry">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Forestry operations reduce the number of trees in a given area, thus reducing the soil stability in that area as well. This action, coupled with the heavy machinery rolling over the soil increases the risk of erosion.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">
       Mining
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mining">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Active mining operations are considered point sources, however runoff from abandoned mining operations contribute to nonpoint source pollution. In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strip_mining" title="Strip mining">
       strip mining
      </a>
      operations, the top of the mountain is removed to expose the desired
      <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">
       ore
      </a>
      . If this area is not properly reclaimed once the mining has finished,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">
       soil erosion
      </a>
      can occur. Additionally, there can be chemical reactions with the air and newly exposed rock to create acidic runoff. Water that seeps out of abandoned
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underground_mining" title="Underground mining">
       subsurface mines
      </a>
      can also be highly acidic. This can seep into the nearest body of water and change the pH in the aquatic environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marinas_and_boating_activities">
       Marinas and boating activities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Marinas and boating activities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Chemicals used for boat maintenance, like
      <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">
       paint
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">
       solvents
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">
       oils
      </a>
      find their way into water through runoff. Additionally, spilling fuels or leaking fuels directly into the water from boats contribute to nonpoint source pollution. Nutrient and bacteria levels are increased by poorly maintained sanitary waste receptacles on the boat and pump-out stations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_and_suburban_areas_2">
       Urban and suburban areas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Urban and suburban areas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      To control non-point source pollution, many different approaches can be undertaken in both urban and suburban areas.
      <a href="/wiki/Buffer_strip" title="Buffer strip">
       Buffer strips
      </a>
      provide a barrier of grass in between impervious paving material like parking lots and roads, and the closest body of water. This allows the soil to absorb any pollution before it enters the local aquatic system.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retention_pond" title="Retention pond">
       Retention ponds
      </a>
      can be built in drainage areas to create an aquatic buffer between runoff pollution and the aquatic environment. Runoff and storm water drain into the retention pond allowing for the contaminates to settle out and become trapped in the pond. The use of
      <a href="/wiki/Permeable_paving" title="Permeable paving">
       porous pavement
      </a>
      allows for rain and storm water to drain into the ground beneath the pavement, reducing the amount of runoff that drains directly into the water body. Restoration methods such as
      <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">
       constructing wetlands
      </a>
      are also used to slow runoff as well as absorb contamination.
     </p>
     <p>
      Construction sites typically implement simple measures to reduce pollution and runoff. Firstly, sediment or
      <a href="/wiki/Silt_fence" title="Silt fence">
       silt fences
      </a>
      are erected around construction sites to reduce the amount of sediment and large material draining into the nearby water body. Secondly, laying grass or straw along the border of construction sites also work to reduce nonpoint source pollution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In areas served by single-home septic systems, local government regulations can force septic system maintenance to ensure compliance with water quality standards. In
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">
       Washington (state)
      </a>
      , a novel approach to was developed through creation of a "shellfish protection district" when either a commercial or recreational shellfish bed is downgraded because of ongoing nonpoint source pollution. The shellfish protection district is a geographic area designated by a county to protect water quality and tideland resources, and provides a mechanism to generate local funds for water quality services to control nonpoint sources of pollution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At least two shellfish protection districts in south
      <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">
       Puget Sound
      </a>
      have instituted septic system operation and maintenance requirements with program fees tied directly to property taxes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_operations_2">
       Agricultural operations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Agricultural operations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      To control sediment and runoff, farmers may utilize erosion controls to reduce runoff flows and retain soil on their fields. Common techniques include
      <a href="/wiki/Contour_plowing" title="Contour plowing">
       contour plowing
      </a>
      , crop
      <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">
       mulching
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">
       crop rotation
      </a>
      , planting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perennial" title="Perennial">
       perennial
      </a>
      crops and installing
      <a href="/wiki/Riparian_buffer" title="Riparian buffer">
       riparian buffers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :pp. 4-954-96
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_tillage" title="Conservation tillage">
        Conservation tillage
       </a>
      </i>
      is a concept used to reduce runoff while planting a new crop. The farmer leaves some crop reside from the previous planting in the ground to help prevent runoff during the planting process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nutrients are typically applied to farmland as commercial fertilizer; animal
      <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">
       manure
      </a>
      ; or spraying of municipal or industrial wastewater (effluent) or sludge. Nutrients may also enter runoff from
      <a href="/wiki/Crop_residue" title="Crop residue">
       crop residues
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      water,
      <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">
       wildlife
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(Aerosol_physics)" title="Deposition (Aerosol physics)">
       atmospheric deposition
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :p. 29
      </sup>
      Farmers can develop and implement
      <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_management" title="Nutrient management">
       nutrient management
      </a>
      plans to reduce excess application of nutrients.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :pp. 4-374-38
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To minimize pesticide impacts, farmers may use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Pest_Management" title="Integrated Pest Management">
       Integrated Pest Management
      </a>
      (IPM) techniques (which can include
      <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">
       biological pest control
      </a>
      ) to maintain control over pests, reduce reliance on chemical pesticides, and protect
      <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">
       water quality
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_operations">
       Forestry operations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Forestry operations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      With well planned placement of both logging trails, also called skid trails, can reduce the amount of sediment generated. By planning the trails location as far away from the logging activity as possible as well as contouring the trails with the land, it can reduce the amount of loose sediment in the runoff. Additionally, by replanting trees on the land after logging, it provides a structure for the soil to regain stability as well as replaces the logged environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marinas">
       Marinas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonpoint_source_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Marinas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Installing shut off valves on fuel pumps at a marina dock can help reduce the amount of spillover into the water. Additionally, pump-out stations that are easily accessible to boaters in a marina can provide a clean place in which to dispose of sanitary waste without dumping it directly into the water. Finally, something as simple as having trash containers around a marina can prevent larger objects entering the water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_11-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
