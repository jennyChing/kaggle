complete various tasks with it, according to \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/nature.com\/articles\/doi:10.1038\/srep10767\u0022\u003Ea study\u003C\/a\u003E published today in \u003Cem\u003EScientific Reports\u003C\/em\u003E. If such a connection were possible in humans, we might be able to solve problems far better than any individual alone.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EResearchers have known for a long time that brains have a tendency to sync up. When two people are tasked with the same activity, neurons in their brains start to fire at the same speed and in the same place, even if the people weren\u2019t instructed to cooperate. No one really knows why this happens, but it\u2019s a well-observed phenomenon\u2014our brains are naturally inclined to cooperate with one another.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe researchers behind this study knew that the same held true for monkeys, and they wanted to see how this convergence could be applied to the brain-machine interface. In the study, they sat groups of two or three monkeys in separate rooms in front of a computer screen. Each of these monkeys had electrodes implanted in the parts of their brains associated with motor skills and somatosensation, the sense of where the body is positioned in space. The monkeys all shared control of the digital arm, able to move it along various axes so that, together, the monkeys could complete a common task of moving the arm towards a circular target on the screen. Once they achieved that, the researchers gave them juice as a reward. They first learned to move the arm with a joystick, then the researchers hooked up their brain implants to control the arm with just their minds.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EOver the course of several weeks, the monkeys got much better\u2014and faster\u2014at working together.          <header class="content-header basic-sidebar">
          <div class="panel-pane pane-panels-mini pane-node-header">
           <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
           <div class="content-onecol one-col" id="mini-panel-node_header">
            <div>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-primary-channel">

              <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
             </div>
             <h1>
              Extreme Rainfall Is The New Normal, And We're Not Ready For It
             </h1>
             <div class="field-subtitle">
              <p>
               Heavy rains are flooding cities and towns from Nebraska to Michigan to Maine. Is the problem failing infrastructure, global warming...or both?
              </p>
             </div>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">
              <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
              <span class="author">
               <span class="label">
                By
               </span>
               <a datatype="" href="/popsci-authors/emily-gertz" property="prefLabel" typeof="Concept">
                Emily Gertz
               </a>
              </span>
              <span class="date" data-timestamp="1408116603">
               <span class="label">
                posted
               </span>
               Aug 15th, 2014 at 11:30am
              </span>
              <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
          </div>
          <div class="panel-pane pane-block pane-post-social-post-social-block">
           <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
           <div class="postButtons" style="float:left;">
           </div>
           <a class="customIcon pw-widget-customIcon" href="#" name="customIcon">
            <img alt="" data-src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/all/modules/bonnier/post_social/theme/post_icon_24.png" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
            <noscript>
             <img alt="" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/all/modules/bonnier/post_social/theme/post_icon_24.png" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
            </noscript>
           </a>
           <span class="pw-counter-custom1">
            <span class="pw-button-counter_custom1">
             <b class="customCounter" style="font-weight: normal!important;">
              0
             </b>
            </span>
           </span>
           <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
          </div>
         </header>
         <article class="content-basic basic-sidebar">
          <div class="content-main basic-sidebar">
           <div class="panel-pane pane-entity-field pane-node-body">
            <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-detroit-flood_0.jpg?itok=yJqK4XID" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-detroit-flood_0.jpg?itok=B-4qzRtv" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-detroit-flood_0.jpg?itok=JHOBsbHq" height="900" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="540" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-detroit-flood_0.jpg?itok=B-4qzRtv" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Detroit Flood.
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                <a href="http://www.detroitnews.com/">
                 The Detroit News
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-body">
               <p>
                On August 11, more than 4 inches of rain fell on Detroit in less than a day. Drivers abandoned their cars as many metro area highways turned into lakes.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Six weeks worth of rain fell on greater Detroit on Monday, much of it during afternoon rush hour. Local drainage systems quickly topped out, and the deluge transformed highways into lakes studded with hundreds of stranded drivers and submerged cars.
              <a href="http://online.wsj.com/articles/detroit-highways-begin-to-reopen-1407959091">
               Flooded roads and highways in greater Detroit began to reopen on Wednesday
              </a>
              , two days after the storm, according to
              <em>
               The Wall Street Journal,
              </em>
              but the cleanup and repair is likely to take months.
             </p>
             <p>
              The flooding came about, most directly, due to very high humidity on Monday combined with a low pressure system from the southwest. The resulting storms moved so slowly (sometimes even reforming after initially raining themselves out) that the rain was concentrated across a small geographic area.
             </p>
             <p>
              This is weather common to tropical regions of the world, not temperate Michigan. But it's in line with the
              <a href="http://nca2014.globalchange.gov/">
               National Climate Assessment
              </a>
              , which found that over the past six decades incidents of extreme precipitation have increased across the continental U.S. due to human-propelled climate change. Rising temperatures increase the evaporation of water into vapor, and warmer air can take on greater amounts of water vapor than cooler. When all that vapor finally condenses into rain (or snow), there's more of it to dump onto the communities below.
             </p>
             <p>
              This new normal of extreme precipitation is hitting Northeastern states the hardest, followed by the upper Midwest, as this map from
              <em>
               Climate Central
              </em>
              shows:
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2014/assets-climatecentral-org-2014-HeavyDownpours-660x372.jpg?itok=y-yP3_mN" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/assets-climatecentral-org-2014-HeavyDownpours-660x372.jpg?itok=8AjMZgNU" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2014/assets-climatecentral-org-2014-HeavyDownpours-660x372.jpg?itok=ERrPxpI8" height="562" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="337" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/assets-climatecentral-org-2014-HeavyDownpours-660x372.jpg?itok=8AjMZgNU" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Extreme Precipitation in US Increasing
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                <a href="http://www.climatecentral.org/gallery/maps/extreme-precipitation-events-are-on-the-rise">
                 Climate Central
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-body">
               <p>
                Data from the latest National Climate Assessment shows that brief, heavy downpours are increasing across the United States, with the Northeastern and Upper Midwestern states hardest hit.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              There's another thing that's changed since the 1950s: the built environment. There is a lot more of it.
             </p>
             <p>
              Officially Monday's rainfall in Detroit totaled 4.57 inches (some areas saw up to 6 inches), just missing the one-day rainfall record of 4.75 inches set in 1925. But as bad as that Prohibition-era deluge must have been,
              <a href="http://www.wxyz.com/news/what-caused-this-whats-ahead-questions-answered-regarding-historic-metro-detroit-flood">
               wrote
               <em>
                WXYZ
               </em>
               's Chris Edwards,
              </a>
              it fell on a city with a lot less paved area than we have now.
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               The large amount of pavement in Detroit, or any large urban area, allows less rainfall to soak into the ground and creates more runoff during intense storms. So when considering how the metro area has changed since 1925, this may have been the most serious flooding event ever recorded in Detroit.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Much of U.S. highway system was planned and built in the mid-20th century with historical rain and snow conditions in mind. Now these same systems are decades-old, under-maintained, and failing under the new normal we've created of extreme precipitation.
             </p>
             <p>
              Much of Detroit's infrastructure is
              <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/archive/technology/engineering/rebuilding-america/0/10">
               due for major renovation
              </a>
              , replacement, or re-imagining. While many civic leaders and engineering visionaries imagine a bright future for the Motor City, in the present the city's near-bankruptcy has meant
              <a href="http://michiganradio.org/post/detroits-infrastructure-crumbling-while-city-has-trouble-collecting-cash">
               tens of millions of dollars in neglected maintenance on basic urban necessities like water mains
              </a>
              , as Michigan Radio reported in February.
             </p>
             <p>
              But it's unfair to single out Detroit. Failing infrastructure and flash flooding are big problems are big problems across the U.S.
             </p>
             <p>
              A day after the Michigan floods, these factors came together again in Tuesday's torrential downpours along the East Coast. A low pressure storm system crawling up the mid-Atlantic seaboard dropped several inches of rainfall within 24 hours on communities from Maryland to New York.
              <a href="http://www.climatecentral.org/news/10-images-explain-northeast-flooding-17895">
               A near-record 6.3 inches of rain fell at Baltimore-Washington International Airport
              </a>
                trumped only by the amount of rain that fell during a 1933 hurricane,
              <em>
               Climate Central
              </em>
              reports. Some parts of greater Baltimore saw over 10 inches of rain before the storms moved off.
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-BWI-flooding-1schoiceweather.jpg?itok=CJ-P_Ge0" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-BWI-flooding-1schoiceweather.jpg?itok=wpOF8blJ" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-BWI-flooding-1schoiceweather.jpg?itok=ZQPp9O7b" height="575" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="345" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-BWI-flooding-1schoiceweather.jpg?itok=wpOF8blJ" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Baltimore Flooding
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                <a href="https://twitter.com/1stChoiceWeathr/status/499270980125335553">
                 @1stChoiceWeathr on Twitter
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-body">
               <p>
                Sudden heavy rains swamped parts of the Baltimore area on August 12, including this parking lot at BWI Airport.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              By 9:30 am Wednesday, the system was moving over the New York City metro area.
              <a href="https://nwschat.weather.gov/p.php?pid=201408131357-KOKX-SXUS71-RERISP">
               Long Island recorded 13.26 inches of rainfall in less than a day
              </a>
              , according to the National Weather Service, breaking the New York state record set during Hurricane Irene in 2011. Severe roadway flooding trapped many drivers, and cut off parts of eastern Long Island from the rest of the state.
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-MTA-LIRR-Aug13-IslipFlooding.jpg?itok=O_tHatl4" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-MTA-LIRR-Aug13-IslipFlooding.jpg?itok=icfaI5HL" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-MTA-LIRR-Aug13-IslipFlooding.jpg?itok=oYclDEWd" height="820" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="492" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/2014-MTA-LIRR-Aug13-IslipFlooding.jpg?itok=icfaI5HL" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Flooding on Long Island
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                MTA
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-body">
               <p>
                On August 13, 2014, torrential early morning rainfall flooded roads and highways in Long Island, New York, turning this parking lot at the Long Island Rail Road station in Islip into a lake. Water quickly rose in many areas, closing roads and highways and breaking the state's record for rainfall in a 24-hour period.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              By Wednesday night the storm system was hoving over southeastern Maine. Over 4 inches of rain fell on Portland between 9 and 11pm; by midnight the deluge officially hit a record-setting 6.44 inches,
              <a href="http://www.pressherald.com/2014/08/14/drizzle-expected-to-turn-into-heavy-rain/">
               according to the
               <em>
                Portland Press Herald
               </em>
              </a>
              . (By some unofficial weather radar estimates, it topped 8 inches.) Thousands lost power as streets and basements flooded all over southeastern Maine.
             </p>
             <p>
              And just so the U.S. heartland doesn't feel left out, this video of flash-flooding in Kearney, Nebraska shows a 9-foot-high wall of storm water breaking through tall glass doors to engulf a hospital dining room:
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <div class="media-youtube-video full-width media-youtube-1">
               <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="media-youtube-player" frameborder="0" height="390" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/92X5VyBZvUE?wmode=opaque&amp;modestbranding=1&amp;rel=0" title="92X5VyBZvUE" width="640">
                Video of 92X5VyBZvUE
               </iframe>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Kearny historically has averaged barely two inches of precipitation per month. But from Friday into Saturday, as nearly 4 inches of rain poured down on Kearney over just a few hours, spurring floods that "overwhelmed the city's storm sewer system and broke through the two-story ground-to-ceiling windows of the dinining room at Good Samaritan's Central Cafe,"
              <a href="http://www.omaha.com/news/nebraska/severe-storm-causes-flooding-at-kearney-s-good-samaritan-hospital/article_3e2ecb48-201f-11e4-9b0a-001a4bcf6878.html">
               reported the
               <em>
                Omaha World-Herald
               </em>
              </a>
              . "The hospital basement flooded, and there was water damage in other portions of the main building."
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="https://www.facebook.com/GSH.Kearney">
               On Facebook the hospital expressed relief
              </a>
              that no one was injured in the flash flood:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               Its hard to put into words exactly what Saturdays conditions were like and just how seriously our facility was impacted. And to say that were emotional about the whole situation is a bit of an understatement. This security camera footage is just a glimpse into the series of events that unfolded Saturday.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
            </div>
