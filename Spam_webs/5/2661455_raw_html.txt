         <h2>
          Small, cute and quirky it still has valuable information.
         </h2>
         <p>
          We dont often talk about the end of the world as we know it (TEOTWAWKI) here at PreparednessMama. We dont approach preparedness from a survivalist view, but rather as a way to be self-reliant during times of trial. You may think me naive, and thats okay. Our promise is preparedness without the hype.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="100 Skills You'll need for the End of the World as We Know It: book review | PreparednessMama" class="alignleft wp-image-10492" height="701" src="http://preparednessmama.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/100SkillsCoverWeb-730x1024.jpg" width="500"/>
          Given that view, I was a little hesitant to read the new book by Ana Maria Spagna called 100 Skills Youll Need for the End of the World (as We Know It). Im pleasantly surprised.
         </p>
         <p>
          I found that its a quirky introduction to preparedness and contains more humor than hype.The book is small, measuring 5 x 7 inches. Half of its 223 pages are illustrations. That of course, doesnt leave much room foranin depth instruction for any one skill.
         </p>
         <p>
          In 100 skills you will not find specific details about how to do Basic First Aid, Beekeeping, or Bicycle Repair (skill number 4,5,6) nor will it teach you to master Latrine digging, laughing and listening (skill 59,60,61).
         </p>
         <p>
          What you will find is a collection of 100 ideas that will help you become more self-reliant. You can use these skills today  or at the end of the world  your choice, but I think you might want to get starting now.

         <h1>
          Put this versatile appliance to work in your kitchen.
         </h1>
         <p>
          I purchased a rice cooker several years ago. I can count on it to make perfect rice  every time. The rest of the time it sits on a shelf in the garage looking  wellforlorn. There must be something else to do with it, because why waste the money for the convenience of cooking rice once a week.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="13 (other) things you can make in a rice cooker | PreparednessMama" class="alignleft wp-image-10467" height="560" src="http://preparednessmama.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/Rice-Cooker-Recipes.jpg" width="300"/>
          There are several benefits to using a rice cooker daily.
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           // Frees up stove-top space on busy cooking nights.
          </li>
          <li>
           // Keeps the kitchen cooler on hot days.
          </li>
          <li>
           // No fuss, pour and wait for your food to finish.
          </li>
          <li>
           // Keeps food warm until youre ready to serve.
          </li>
          <li>
           // It turns out that a rice cooker is a versatile appliance for every kitchen.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          The rice cooker intuitively cooks until all the water has been completely absorbed by your grain, and then it switches itself over to its warming setting to keep your dish warm until you serve. Because of the machines tight seal, rice cookers lose less liquid to evaporation, so using a slightly smaller amount of water, rather than more, is always a better idea. (
          <a href="http://wholegrainscouncil.org/recipes/cooking-grains-with-your-rice-cooker" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://wholegrainscouncil.org/recipes/cooking-grains-with-your-rice-cooker', 'Whole Grain Council');" target="_blank">
           Whole Grain Council
          </a>
          )
         </p>
         <p>
          1.
          <strong>
           Grains
          </strong>
           Once I got adventurous, the next thing I cooked in my rice cooker was quinoa. It has the same cooking requirement as white rice, so its a natural progression. In fact all kinds of grains (and seeds like quinoa) can be cooked in a rice cooker. They are usually cooked at a ratio of 1 cup grains to 2 cups water. Try substituting vegetable or chicken stock for the water as a flavorful addition.

         <p>

          Not so with dry sugar.
         </p>

          Removing the oxygen for long term sugar storage is not required and not recommended.
