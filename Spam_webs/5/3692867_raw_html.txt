       <div class="art-dek">
        <p>
         A new website promises to find affordable flightsfor a price.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="article-body">
       <div class="article-figure landscape">
        <div class="article-image-container">
         <img alt="" class="article-image" src="http://photos.cntraveler.com/2014/12/17/5491f12e860c74c1162cb006_galleria-vittorio-emanuele-milan-italy.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="article-figure-info">
         <div class="article-figure-contributor">
          TommL/Getty Images
         </div>
         <div class="article-figure-caption">
          <p>
           There's a new way to book a more affordable trip to Milan.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        Its one thing to know that one of the best ways to save on airfare to Europe is to buy the cheapest ticket you can find to any European city and then book a separate flight on one of the continents budget carriers to your final destination.
       </p>
       <p>
        Its another entirely to have the time and patience to search all the potential routes to find those possible savings.
        <a href="http://cleverlayover.com/">
         Cleverlayover
        </a>
        , however, automates this trick.
       </p>
       <p>
        Launched last month by students at Harvard Business School, the new site searches routes to and from the worlds 500 largest airportsin Europe and beyondto combine two roundtrip tickets from non-partner airlines to find the cheapest fares," as Cleverlayover puts it.
       </p>
       <p>
        Phil Hu will be the company's CEO when Harvard transfers the Cleverlayover intellectual property to the students at the end of the semester. He and his five classmates behind the project came up with the idea when searching for flights themselves. Hu's analysis showed that combining tickets from non-partner airlines leads to cheaper airfare for 70 percent of all routes worldwide. And when better deals are found, those savings average 20 percent, or about $400. (During its soft launch, the site has returned cheaper options on 33 percent of all searches, with an average savings of $300.)
       </p>
       <p>
        If you look at flight search in general, there hasnt been a huge innovation in the industry since meta search, said Hu. We built this search engine thats able to search the entire graph of all possible flights and find you the savings. And we do that based on several graph-search and machine-learning algorithms that are required. Because you cant just search a couple thousand flights at once."
       </p>
       <p>
        One drawback to combining tickets from multiple carriers, which
        <a href="http://www.cntraveler.com/stories/2015-04-07/morning-news-uber-s-growth-surges-in-2014">
         <em>
          Cond Nast Traveler
         </em>
         recently noted
        </a>
        , is that if a problem arises with flights on the first ticket that cause you to miss flights purchased on the second ticket, that second carrier wont be obliged to help you. To address this possibilityalbeit it an additional costCleverlayover offers a protect against delays option that leads users to the travel insurer Allianz Global Assistance.
       </p>
       <p>
        So how much money can it save you? I tested the site with a hypothetical trip from Washington, D.C. to Milan departing Aug. 19 and returning Aug. 26. Cleverlayover found tickets for $785, which it said was $114 cheaper than lowest fare on Kayak, Expedia, Hipmunk, Momondo, Orbitz, Google Flights, etc. To buy the tickets, I was redirected to Cheapoair, with a reminder to verify that you are able to select the right itinerary at the correct price, to look through search results and find the flights Cleverlayover returned.
       </p>
       <p>
        The itinerary was complicated, and Cheapoair quoted a price ($836) that was a bit higher than Cleverlayover had displayed. The site suggested flying from BWI to Reykjavik on WOW Air and, after a one-hour, 50-minute layover, continuing on WOW Air to Copenhagen. After another layover of five hours, I'd take EasyJet to Milan. Total travel time would be 17 hours, 45 minutes there and, with slightly different connections, about 16 hours back.
       </p>
       <p>
        For comparison, I also searched for that route on those same dates via Kayak. The cheapest result was a $967 flight on Aeroflot, going Dulles to New York City to Moscow to Milan, that would take
        <em>
         more than 42 hours.
        </em>
        Another option, a $1,048 flight on American, had just one stop (in New York) and landed in Milan less than 12 hours after leaving Washington's Reagan National. Compared to the Cleverlayover trip, it cost about $200 moreand was about six hours faster. Round trip, American would save me nearly 10 hours of travel time.
       </p>
       <p>
        Still, Cleverlayover is worth checking before you buy airfare. Even if the site can't find you a dealor the length of a layover isn't worth the savingsyou've still only ventured a few minutes. Just this weekend, in fact, relatives of mine said they were looking for ways to save on airfare to Europe this summer. I sent them the link to Cleverlayover before I finished this article.
       </p>
      </div>
