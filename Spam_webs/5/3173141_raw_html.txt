       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Understanding the Birthday Paradox
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         by Kalid Azad  183 comments
         <style type="text/css">
          #twitter-widget-0{display:inline-block;position:relative;top:4px;left:5px}
         </style>
         <a class="twitter-share-button" data-via="betterexplained" href="https://twitter.com/share">
         </a>
        </div>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          23 people
         </strong>
         . In a room of just 23 people theres a 50-50 chance of two people having the same birthday. In a room of 75 theres a 99.9% chance of two people matching.
        </p>
        <p>
         Put down the calculator and pitchfork, I dont speak heresy. The birthday paradox is strange, counter-intuitive, and
         <strong>
          completely true
         </strong>
         . Its only a paradox because our brains cant handle the compounding power of exponents. We expect probabilities to be linear and only consider the scenarios were involved in (both faulty assumptions, by the way).
        </p>
        <p>
         Lets see why the paradox happens and how it works.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Problem 1: Exponents arent intuitive
        </h2>
        <p>
         Weve taught ourselves mathematics and statistics, but lets not kid ourselves: its not natural.
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres an example: Whats the chance of getting 10 heads in a row when flipping coins? The untrained brain might think like this:
        </p>
        <p>
         Well, getting one head is a 50% chance. Getting two heads is twice as hard, so a 25% chance. Getting
         <strong>
          ten
         </strong>
         heads is probably 10 times harder so about 50%/10 or a 5% chance.
        </p>
        <p>
         And there we sit, smug as a bug on a rug. No dice bub.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          After pounding your head with statistics
         </strong>
         , you know not to divide, but use
         <strong>
          exponents
         </strong>
         . The chance of 10 heads is not .5/10 but
         <span alt=".5^10" class="tex-inline-html">
          .5
          <sup>
           10
          </sup>
         </span>
         , or about .001.
        </p>
        <p>
         But even after training, we get caught again. At 5% interest well double our money in 14 years, rather than the expected 20. Did you naturally infer the
         <a href="http://betterexplained.com/articles/the-rule-of-72/">
          Rule of 72
         </a>
         when learning about interest rates? Probably not. Understanding compound exponential growth with our linear brains is hard.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Problem 2: Humans are a tad bit selfish
        </h2>
        <p>
         Take a look at the
         <a href="http://news.google.com">
          news
         </a>
         . Notice how much of the negative news is the result of acting without considering others. Im an optimist and
         <em>
          do
         </em>
         have hope for mankind, but thats a separate discussion :).
        </p>
        <p>
         In a room of 23, do you think of the 22 comparisons where
         <strong>
          your
         </strong>
         birthday is being compared against someone elses? Probably.
        </p>
        <p>
         Do you think of the
         <strong>
          231
         </strong>
         comparisons where someone who is not you is being checked against someone else who is not you? Do you realize there are so many? Probably not.
        </p>
        <p>
         The fact that we neglect the
         <strong>
          10 times as many
         </strong>
         comparisons that dont include us helps us see why the paradox can happen.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Ok, fine, humans are awful: Show me the math!
        </h2>
        <p>
         The question: What are the chances that two people share a birthday in a group of 23?
        </p>
        <p>
         Sure, we could list the pairs and count all the ways they could match. But thats hard: there could be 1, 2, 3 or even 23 matches!
        </p>
        <p>
         Its like asking Whats the chance of getting one or more heads in 23 coin flips? There are so many possibilities: heads on the first throw, or the 3rd, or the last, or the 1st and 3rd, the 2nd and 21st, and so on.
        </p>
        <p>
         How do we solve the coin problem? Flip it around (Get it? Get it?). Rather than counting every way to get heads,
         <strong>
          find the chance of getting all tails, our problem scenario
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         If theres a 1% chance of getting all tails (more like .5^23 but work with me here), theres a 99% chance of having
         <strong>
          at least one head
         </strong>
         . I dont know if its 1 head, or 2, or 15 or 23: we got heads, and thats what matters. If we subtract the chance of a problem scenario from 1 we are left with the probability of a good scenario.
        </p>
        <p>
         The same principle applies for birthdays. Instead of finding all the ways we match,
         <strong>
          find the chance that everyone is different, the problem scenario
         </strong>
         . We then take the opposite probability and get the chance of a match. It may be 1 match, or 2, or 20, but somebody matched, which is what we need to find.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Explanation: Counting Pairs
        </h2>
        <p>
         With 23 people we have 253 pairs:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{\frac{23 \cdot 22}{2} = 253}" class="tex" height="37" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/342da5d50fb2e1f6b6dec572354d482a.png" title="\displaystyle{\frac{23 \cdot 22}{2} = 253}" width="102"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         (Brush up on
         <a href="http://betterexplained.com/articles/easy-permutations-and-combinations/">
          combinations and permutations
         </a>
         if you like).
        </p>
        <p>
         The chance of 2 people having different birthdays is:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{1 - \frac{1}{365} = \frac{364}{365} = .997260}" class="tex" height="37" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/a6704954961ee4b46f25aa989f10e099.png" title="\displaystyle{1 - \frac{1}{365} = \frac{364}{365} = .997260}" width="199.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Makes sense, right? Theres 364 out of 365 birthdays that are OK.
        </p>
        <p>
         Having all
         <strong>
          253 pairs
         </strong>
         be different is like getting heads 253 times in a row (well, sort-of: lets assume birthdays are independent). We use exponents to find the probability:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{\left(\frac{364}{365}\right)^{253} = .4995}" class="tex" height="47.5" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/7c815fd10739f8e604442ea7d0342f28.png" title="\displaystyle{\left(\frac{364}{365}\right)^{253} = .4995}" width="141"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         99.7260% is really close to one, but when you multiply it by itself a few hundred times, it shrinks. Really fast.
        </p>
        <p>
         The chance that we have a match is: 1  49.95% = 50.05%, or just over half! If you want to find the probability of a match for any number of people n the formula is:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(n) = 1 - \left(\frac{364}{365}\right)^{C(n,2)} = 1 - \left(\frac{364}{365}\right)^{n(n-1)/2} }" class="tex" height="49" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/b19f67d82cee00406b5b1f17901dd1d1.png" title="\displaystyle{p(n) = 1 - \left(\frac{364}{365}\right)^{C(n,2)} = 1 - \left(\frac{364}{365}\right)^{n(n-1)/2} }" width="364"/>
        </p>
        <h2>
         Interactive Example
        </h2>
        <p>
         I didnt believe we needed only 23 people. The math works out, but is it real?
        </p>
        <p>
         You bet. Try the example below: Pick a number of items (365), a number of people (23) and run a few trials. Youll see the theoretical match
         <span>
          and your actual match
         </span>
         as you run your trials. Go ahead, click the button (or
         <a href="http://betterexplained.com/examples/birthday/birthday.html">
          see the full page
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <iframe border="1px solid #ccc" height="300px" src="http://betterexplained.com/examples/birthday/birthday.html" width="100%">
        </iframe>
        <p>
         As you run more and more trials (keep clicking!) the actual probability should approach the theoretical one.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Examples and Takeaways
        </h2>
        <p>
         Here are a few lessons from the birthday paradox:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong>
           sqrt(n)
          </strong>
          is roughly the number you need to have a 50% chance of a match with n items. sqrt(365) is about 20. This comes into play in cryptography for the birthday attack.
         </li>
         <li>
          Even though there are 2
          <sup>
           128
          </sup>
          (1e38)
          <a href="http://betterexplained.com/articles/the-quick-guide-to-guids/">
           <span class="caps">
            GUID
           </span>
           s
          </a>
          , we only have 2
          <sup>
           64
          </sup>
          (1e19) to use up before a 50% chance of collision. And 50% is really, really high.
         </li>
         <li>
          You only need 13 people picking letters of the alphabet to have 95% chance of a match. Try it above (people = 13, items = 26).
         </li>
         <li>
          Exponential growth rapidly decreases the chance of picking unique items (aka it increases the cranes of a match). Remember: exponents are non-intuitive and humans are selfish!
          <img alt=":)" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/icon_smile.gif"/>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         After thinking about it a lot, the birthday paradox finally clicks with me. But I still check out the interactive example just to make sure.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Appendix A: Repeated Multiplication Explanation (Geeky Math Alert!)
        </h2>
        <p>
         Remember how we assumed birthdays are independent? Well, they arent.
        </p>
        <p>
         If Person 1 and Person 3 match, and Person 3 and 5 match, we know that 1 and 5 match also. The outcome of 1 and 5 depends on their results with 3, which means the results arent an independent 1/365 chance (in our case, its a 100% chance of a match).
        </p>
        <p>
         When counting pairs we did math as if birthdays were like independent coin flips, and multiplied probabilities. This assumption isnt strictly true but its good enough for a small number of people (23) compared to the sample size (365). Its unlikely to have multiple people match and screw up the independence, so its a good approximation.
        </p>
        <p>
         Its unlikely, but it can happen. Lets figure out the real chances of each person picking a different number:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          The first person has a 100% chance of a unique number (of course)
         </li>
         <li>
          The second has a (1  1/365) chance (all but 1 number from the 365)
         </li>
         <li>
          The third has a (1  2/365) chance (all but 2 numbers)
         </li>
         <li>
          The 23rd has a (1  22/365) (all but 22 numbers)
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         The multiplication looks pretty ugly:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) = 1 \cdot \left(1-\frac{1}{365}\right) \cdot \left(1-\frac{2}{365}\right)  \cdots \left(1-\frac{22}{365}\right)}" class="tex" height="44" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/867ad9a64d2c234aa4ac44ce58e3e63d.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) = 1 \cdot \left(1-\frac{1}{365}\right) \cdot \left(1-\frac{2}{365}\right)  \cdots \left(1-\frac{22}{365}\right)}" width="456"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         But theres a shortcut we can take. When x is close to 0, a coarse first-order Taylor approximation for
         <span alt="e^x" class="tex-inline-html">
          e
          <sup>
           x
          </sup>
         </span>
         is:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{e^x  \approx 1 + x}" class="tex" height="14.5" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/db7e38c1814620c167d4e1204600aaef.png" title="\displaystyle{e^x  \approx 1 + x}" width="82"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         so
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{ 1 - \frac{1}{365} \approx e^{-1/365}}" class="tex" height="37" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/768103c5390603209b7cf5b78fdcf871.png" title="\displaystyle{ 1 - \frac{1}{365} \approx e^{-1/365}}" width="139.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Using our handy shortcut we can rewrite the big equation to:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx 1 \cdot e^{-1/365} \cdot e^{-2/365} \cdots e^{-22/365}}" class="tex" height="22" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/ea9e96883683e70eac5c56e555ac5779.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx 1 \cdot e^{-1/365} \cdot e^{-2/365} \cdots e^{-22/365}}" width="363.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{(-1 -2 -3 ... -22)/365}}" class="tex" height="22" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/8507032654ac39235fc5c15e9cf60bd3.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{(-1 -2 -3 ... -22)/365}}" width="266.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(1 + 2 + ... 22)/365}}" class="tex" height="22" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/ac8c1b13881dc4522fb0ce83507cf74b.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(1 + 2 + ... 22)/365}}" width="248.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         But we remember that
         <a href="http://betterexplained.com/articles/techniques-for-adding-the-numbers-1-to-100/">
          adding the numbers 1 to n
         </a>
         = n(n + 1)/2. Dont confuse this with n(n-1)/2, which is C(n,2) or the number of pairs of n items. They look almost the same!
        </p>
        <p>
         Adding 1 to 22 is (22 * 23)/2 so we get:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-((23 \cdot 22) /(2 \cdot 365))} = .499998}" class="tex" height="22" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/34df9d475fd68b667553b7b1b7d068da.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-((23 \cdot 22) /(2 \cdot 365))} = .499998}" width="336"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Phew. This approximation is
         <a href="http://instacalc.com/?d=Q2FsY3VsYXRpbmcgdGhlIGFwcHJveGltYXRlIGFuZCBhY3R1YWwgcHJvYmFiaWxpdGllcyBmb3IgdGhlIGJpcnRoZGF5IHBhcmFkb3gu&amp;%23038;c=cG93ZXIgPSAtKDIzKjIyKS8oMiogMzY1KXx8ZXN0aW1hdGVkID0gRV5wb3dlcnxhY3R1YWwgPSAoMzY0LzM2NSleMjUxfGVycm9yX21hcmdpbiA9IChlc3RpbWF0ZWQgLSBhY3R1YWwpIC8gYWN0dWFsfHw&amp;%23038;s=sssssss&amp;%23038;v=0.9">
          very close
         </a>
         and good enough for government work, as they say. If you simplify the formula a bit and swap in
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         for 23 you get:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(n^2 / (2 \cdot 365))}}" class="tex" height="23" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/24296fe80fa1e21e12845610bc6ae014.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(n^2 / (2 \cdot 365))}}" width="223.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         and
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(match) = 1 - p(different) \approx 1 - e^{-(n^2 / (2 \cdot 365))}}" class="tex" height="23" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/e31a26fa3bf93adbd2f254b10b74dcfd.png" title="\displaystyle{p(match) = 1 - p(different) \approx 1 - e^{-(n^2 / (2 \cdot 365))}}" width="384"/>
        </p>
        <h2>
         Appendix B: The General Birthday Formula
        </h2>
        <p>
         Lets generalize the formula to picking
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         people from
         <em>
          T
         </em>
         total items (instead of 365):
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" class="tex" height="23" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/f96517aec2f4183513f957925811fb27.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" width="202"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         If we choose a probability (like 50% chance of a match) and solve for
         <em>
          n
         </em>
         :
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" class="tex" height="23" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/f96517aec2f4183513f957925811fb27.png" title="\displaystyle{p(different) \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" width="202"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{1 - p(match) \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" class="tex" height="23" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/7cacc718d713a80b55605d11f0ab203b.png" title="\displaystyle{1 - p(match) \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" width="201"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{1 - .5 \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" class="tex" height="18.5" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/b01d949a5fc5f6a0266a9c2ec4815b6d.png" title="\displaystyle{1 - .5 \approx e^{-(n^2 / 2 \cdot T)}}" width="141.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{-2ln(.5)\cdot T \approx n^2}" class="tex" height="21" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/5c072f9fb83cf58116a6927be8398c51.png" title="\displaystyle{-2ln(.5)\cdot T \approx n^2}" width="134.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{n \approx 1.177 \sqrt{T}}" class="tex" height="18" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/abd90df80b35f59813757a0757c7d5f8.png" title="\displaystyle{n \approx 1.177 \sqrt{T}}" width="105"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Voila! If you take sqrt(T) items (17% more if you want to be picky) then you have about a 50-50 chance of getting a match. If you
         <a href="http://tinyurl.com/2rhwwu">
          plug in other numbers
         </a>
         you can solve for other probabilities:
        </p>
        <p>
         <img align="absmiddle" alt="\displaystyle{n \approx \sqrt{-2ln(1-m)} \cdot \sqrt{T}}" class="tex" height="22" src="http://betterexplained.com/wp-content/plugins/wp-latexrender/pictures/b080c3a0bbc55b4ce014037ea6c78958.png" title="\displaystyle{n \approx \sqrt{-2ln(1-m)} \cdot \sqrt{T}}" width="196.5"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Remember that m is the
         <em>
          desired chance of a match
         </em>
         (its easy to get confused, I did it myself). If you want a 90% chance of matching birthdays, plug m=90% and T=365 into the equation and see that you need
         <a href="http://tinyurl.com/yw6fmx">
          41 people
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Wikipedia has
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birthday_paradox">
          even more details
         </a>
         to satisfy your inner nerd. Go forth and enjoy.
        </p>

           <p>
            Hi, youre correct 1/365 is the chance of 2 people having the same birthday. However, (1/365)^253 would be the chance of 253 people having the *same* birthday! (Which, as you see, is pretty close to zero).
           </p>
           <p>
            For this problem, its important not to mix up 1/365 (the chance of 1 collision) and 364/365 (the chance of no collision). We first find the chance that somehow, everyone manages to be different:
           </p>
           <p>
            p(23 people have different birthdays) = (364/365)^253
           </p>
           <p>
            If there is a 40% chance that everyone is different, there is 1-40% = 60% chance that there was an overlap somewhere. Hope this helps. (Technically, we are assuming independent events but that subtlety is not important for the main point).
           </p>

           <p>
            The explanations given are all approximations, in order to get an exact result you follow the start to Appendix A, but dont attempt to simplify with e^x.  The solution is actually fairly simple, for n possibilities (days in the year) and k events (people at the party) we get a probability of:
           </p>
           <p>
            1  (P(n-1,k-1)/(n^(k-1))).
            <br/>
            Where P(n,k) is the number of ways to pick k elements from a set of n, or n!/(n-k)!.
           </p>
           <p>
            This will give an exact solution, the probability of finding two people with the same birthday from a crowd of 23 is more accurately: 50.7297234%
           </p>
           <p>
            I hope that this makes sense, if it doesnt, look at the page on combinatorics and/or think about the fact that (1-(j/n)) = ((n-j)/n) with reference to Appendix A.
           </p>

           <p>
            I think you discount the formula at the beginning of Appendix A (1-1*(1-1/365)*(1-2/365)*) too much by jumping immediately into an approximate shortcut. Lets see what happens if we simplify that equation first.
           </p>
           <p>
            The denominator of the equation is simple to work out  its 365 multiplied by itself as many times as there are people. For x people, the denominator will be 365^x.
           </p>
           <p>
            The numerator also has a familiar pattern. For x people, It will be 365*364*363**365-x.
           </p>
           <p>
            So, we have a pattern something like a factorial, but that stops after x numbers. How do we handle that? Yep, its our old friend the permutation formula!
           </p>
           <p>
            So, the short form of the formula would be P(365,x)/365^x. Writing the long form of the formula, we end up with: x!/(((365-x)!)365^x)
           </p>
           <p>
            Prefer to think of it with combinations instead of permutations? Permutations are just combinations with redundancies taken into account to focus on particular orders of events, or mathematically: P(365,x)=(C(365,x))x!
           </p>
           <p>
            This makes the full formula: ((C(365,x))x!)/365^x
           </p>
           <p>
            Yes the formula you write out at the start of Appendix A looks bad, but it simplifies quickly to a clear and understandable form. Examining it several ways in terms of combinations and permutations helps make it clearer.
           </p>

            SAMAKSHI:

           <p>
            The more people there are, the more opportunities for two of those people to share a birthday. When there are 23 people in a room, the number of opportunities is SO big that the chances of a match between two people is better than half.
           </p>
           <p>
            So about half of all 23-person rooms should be able to say Yes, two of the people in this room share a birthday.
           </p>
           <p>
            Were not interested in a specific birthday, such as January 15. Were asking about the situation where any two people have a birthday in common.
           </p>
           <p>
            It can help to remember that if there were 367 people in the room, then the chances we have a hit are not just very high, but are actually 100%. EVERY room with 367 or more people has two or more people sharing a birthday. Thats because the only other possibility is that there are 367 unique birthdays in that room, and there arent that many birthdays to go around.
           </p>
           <p>
            What if there were 366 people? Then its possible they each have their own birthday (including a Leap Day birthday!), but thats EXTREMELY unlikely. So the chances of a match are very close to 100%.
           </p>
           <p>
            What if there were 365 people? Just a bit less. 364 people? Slightly less than that.
           </p>
           <p>
             and so on, down to 23. At 23 people, the chances are just over 50%. At 22, they are below 50%. At 2, they are about 1/365, or well below 1%.
           </p>
           <p>
            The whole thing is just a graph that curves differently than you might expect.
           </p>

          <div class="comment-content">
           <p>
            dan: There are two problems with your approach.
           </p>
           <p>
            One is that you are treating the probabilities as independent when they arent. Consider just three people: A, B, and C. There are three pairs out of these people (AB, AC, BC). By itself, the probability that a given pair has the same birthday is 1/365. But if AB shares a birthday, and BC shares a birthday, then the probability that AC shares a birthday is not 1/365 but simply 1. However, for the sake of a simple estimate this fact doesnt make much difference, because with only 23 people, triple-birthdays are very rare.
           </p>
           <p>
            The second problem is that independent probabilities dont work that way  you cant just add them up. The probability of rolling a one on a 6-sided die is 1/6. But the probability of rolling at least 1 one out of two dice is
            <em>
             not
            </em>
            2/6. If it worked that way, then rolling 6 dice would give us a probability of 6/6, or 1  not only guaranteeing at least 1 one, but at least 1 of every number, since all of the numbers are equivalent here. Of course it doesnt work that way  if I roll six dice, getting a straight of all the numbers is extremely
            <em>
             unlikely
            </em>
            , not guaranteed.
           </p>
           <p>
            Instead, you work out the dice probabilities by asking the opposite question: What is the probability of rolling anything but a one? With two dice, there are five-times-five equally-likely ways to do this (as if the dice only had 5 sides): a two and a two, a two and a three, etc, all the way to a six and a six. And here are 36 total equally-likely possible combinations: one-one, one-two, and so on. So our answer to
            <em>
             this
            </em>
            question is 25/36. That means the answer to our original question is just 1-25/36, or 11/36.
           </p>
           <p>
            Getting back to the birthdays, the way to determine the probability with 23 people is not to add the independent probabilities, because then you get a false guarantee of a pair at 28 people (there are 378 pairs, so you would find a probability of 378/365 for a pair, and that number cant even be right in itself  probabilities should never exceed 1). Instead, we do multiplication, and what we multiply is the inverse scenario: 364/365, or the probability that two people dont share a birthday.
           </p>
           <p>
            We multiply this number by itself 253 times (in other words, raise it to the 253rd power). The result is a number slightly
            <em>
             less than
            </em>
            1/2: 0.4995. This means that the probability for the reverse question is slightly more than 1/2, about 0.5005.
           </p>
           <p>
            However, this is slightly off because of the non-independence factor. The more correct way to calculate it is to build the sets of possible rooms that lack matches, and this isnt as hard as you might think. (Its simpler to understand than the methods shown in the blog post.)
           </p>
           <p>
            The first person who enters the room is permitted to have any of 365 birthdays. The second person we put into it is only allowed one of 364 birthdays, because they cant be a match. The third person is allowed one of 363 birthdays, because she cant match either of the first two. All these possibilites multiply out, for the twenty-three people: 365 x 364 x 363x 362 x 361 x 345 x 344 x 343.
           </p>
           <p>
            Thats our numerator: The total set of equally-likely combinations that fulfill a principle of lacking any matched pairs. For our denominator, we need the total set of possible combinations in general, and that would be 365 x 365 x 365 x 365 twenty-three times.
           </p>
           <p>
            The resultant number is about 0.4927  which, as before, is just under one-half. Hence, although we have a more precise probability, we still find that the same number of people (23) is the threshold here.
           </p>
