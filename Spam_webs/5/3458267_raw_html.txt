       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         <a href="http://foorious.com/blog/2014/08/vagrant/" rel="bookmark">
          Vagrant
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="image-1" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1285" height="800" src="http://www.iknownothing.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/image-1.jpg" width="980"/>
         <a href="http://www.vagrantup.com" title="Vagrant">
          Vagrant
         </a>
         is absolutely awesome. I see it as a wrapper of VirtualBox (and other VMs) and Puppet/Chef.
        </p>
        <h2>
         &lt;3
        </h2>
        <p>
         Regardless, what it does is pretty cool: it abstracts your development environmentincluding the OS, modules, settings etc. etc. into what is called a Vagrantfilea plain text file that contains info about your system. When you feed this file to Vagrant on any other system, it will create a virtual machine that is an exact working copy of the system described in the Vagrantfile, installing/setting things up by itself, so that onecan just get startedwith his work. This is a huge time-saver. You dont have to go crazy to replicate systems between stage/production, or between developers/testers working on the same application: just give them the Vagrantfile and theyll be up and running with an identical environmentas the one youre work on,in literally 10 seconds.
        </p>
        <h2>
         &lt;/3
        </h2>
        <p>
         Theres one thing so far that had me raise my eyebrows: according to
         <a href="http://docs.vagrantup.com/v2/installation/backwards-compatibility.html">
          http://docs.vagrantup.com/v2/installation/backwards-compatibility.html
         </a>
         , Vagrant promises to keep compatibilityamongmajor versions, while minor versions might break your Vagrantfiles.I dont know if I misinterpreted the docs, but if this is true, the safest way to use Vagrant would be to stick to the latest major version (1.0 at the time of writing), and wait until 2.0 gets released. Weird.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Video(s)
        </h2>
        <p>
         I like watching videos to learn new stuff.
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres a great 16-mins introductory video:
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FSxS6iPJMFw">
          Introduction to Vagrant
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/FSxS6iPJMFw" width="556">
         </iframe>
        </p>
        <p>
         and heres a complete overview of Vagrant (52-mins):
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Im4wNqlolqQ">
          Automated Development Environments w/ Vagrant
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/Im4wNqlolqQ" width="560">
         </iframe>
        </p>
        <h2>
         Will experiment with it
        </h2>
        <p>
         In the next few days, Im planning on playing with
         <a href="http://coreos.com" title="CoreOS">
          CoreOS
         </a>
         , and Ill use Vagrant to create VMs and even whole clusters, mainly to learn more about specific features that I just looked at briefly while reading the documentation. Im pretty excited about this tool, though. Wonder if Ill end up using it a lot in my workflow
        </p>
