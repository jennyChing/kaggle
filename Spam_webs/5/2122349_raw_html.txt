         <h1>
          5 Reasons Why Responsive Web Design is the Way to Go
         </h1>
         <div class="metadata">
          <div class="published">
           Wednesday, April 22, 2015 2:40:00 PM
          </div>
         </div>
        </header>
        <p>
         You see Road Warriors all over the place (you're probably one, yourself). Last year, mobile Internet browsing (phones and tablets) surpassed that of desktop/laptop. That trend continues and the majority of your web presence will be communicated to the mobile device of a customer or potential customer.
        </p>
        <p>
         eMail is probably the most frequently used component of your mobile office. People that don't transact eMails from their phone are few and far between. Social Media campaigns are growing in popularity and a lot of their payoff is in driving customers to your site. So, you'd better be ready and have a website that handlesyour visitors as they experiencewhat you have to say on the train going home, at the airport waiting for a flight or at night on the couch.
        </p>
        <p>
         Your message, delivered by your website, has to provide an enjoyable experience to the viewer.
        </p>
        <p>
         Old School desktop-oriented sites are not acceptable in this manner. They were created to deliver their message on a big desktop monitor or laptop screen, not the smaller real estate found on tablets and (smaller, still) phones.
        </p>
        <p>
         An effective (and efficient) way to bring your web message to the widest array of visitors is to use Responsive Web Design (RWD) in your site.
        </p>
        <p>
         RWD is not the only way to implement amobile-friendly presence- you can implement a separate "Mobile Site" for one or more alternate devices. Sometimes that's not a bad option - if your mobile site and full site will differ dramatically or if your site is "good but legacy" and you can't realistically change the structure of the existing site to RWD. However in the VAST MAJORITY of cases, RWD is the best option to implement.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Here are Five (5) Reasons (or so) why:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1)
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           SEO
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         As of 4/21/15, Google officiallybrought this to the forefront, boosting the rankings of Mobile Friendly sites. In general, having a"www." website and a "m." or "mobile." website would cause them to "split the points" found by search engine hits unless you took some additional measures. Now, you are outright getting a reward for making your site Mobile Friendly.
        </p>
        <p>
         RWD, implemented in your site, makes it mobile friendly - enjoy that improved ranking.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          2)
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Ease of Content Maintenance
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         No site should be put up and forgotten. SEO likes fresh content, and I'm sure that you want to want to deliver a fresh statement to your fans (even if it's just reinforcing your prior message). New products or services, new locations and fresh images all provide a good excuse for 'saying something new'.
        </p>
        <p>
         Using RWD allows you to keep one version of content and a properly designed site should deliver to any size device. When you publish the content, your consumers are free to enjoy it on whatever devices they use.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          3)
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Lower Bounce Rate
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         If you have an Old-School site that renders nicely enough on a workstation but is a tiny shadow of itself viewed on a mobile (requiring "pinch-n-pull" techniques show a part of it at a time), then you certainly have people bouncing out (arriving and leaving in a matter of seconds).
        </p>
        <p>
         Not what you want...you want potential customers spending time on your site and viewing what you have to offer. If your visitors have to start pinching and pulling to see what you are talking about - they're not staying.
        </p>
        <p>
         RWD, since it adjusts to the viewing device, will keep visitors on your site longer and provide you with a platform from which to "tell your story".
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          4)
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Increased Audience
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         If you build it, they will come. Make browsing from phones and tablets more enjoyable and you will increase your visitors and your potential market.
         <em>
          Recall
         </em>
         : most browsing is done off mobile devices; you probably don't want to shut out the majority of visitors.
        </p>
        <p>
         RWD allows you to appeal to the largest group of visitors possible without the need to set up additional sites (with additional maintenance).
        </p>
        <p>
         While you're giving thought of Responsive Design of your site, our first impression is to think "mobile first" and we design for the small screen - understandable. Keep in mind - many people now have larger HDTVs and other monitors available that allow the designer to creatively use the larger canvas for building interesting user experiences which run on a larger, HD screen.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          5)
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Site Cost Savings
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Additional sites translate into additional cost - cost of development, ofmaintenance or of hosting feeds, a separate mobile site is a separate site (period).
        </p>
        <p>
         RWD lets you consolidate your costs into a single site, which translates into real savings)
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          6)
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Combined Analytics
          </span>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Bonus Point: If you have split sites and are looking at your site analytics, you have to combine them to get a true picture of how your Internet "site" is performing (actually, multiple sites).
        </p>
        <p>
         With RWD, it's one site - no need to combine analytics in giving you a total picture.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         At 2M, we've embraced RWD and have made it a part of all of our web development. Read a little more about it
         <a href="http://2mtech.com/WebDesign" target="_blank" title="2M Tech Services - Web Design">
          here
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         If you want to talk about making your site Responsive, please

          contact us
         </a>
         .
        </p>
