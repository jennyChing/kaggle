      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Happiness Is The New Success: Why Millennials Are Reprioritizing
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">

      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         <em>
          <a href="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/85broads/files/2012/01/0910_ladder_400x500.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-1142" height="300" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/85broads/files/2012/01/0910_ladder_400x500-240x300.jpg" width="240"/>
          </a>
          <strong>
           Now that the ladder of success has been reduced to splinters, the question remains: what does success mean in the 21st century and how do we achieve it?
          </strong>
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          By Lisa Curtis
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         There used to be a ladder to success. It was the collegegood jobmarriagehousefamilycushy retirement. Sure, not everyone made it, there were a few broken rungs near the bottom but that was the guiding light to the good life and enough people made it that it seemed within reach. A few people questioned this ladder as really being the good life, but those were just hippies or crazies, no one worth paying attention to. Now all this has changed; my generation is growing up without a ladder.
        </p>
        <p>
         Before you scoff, lets think about that for a second. The first rung on the ladder, college, used to be seen as a straight shot to success. Now, for too many of us, its a straight shot to
         <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2011/09/are-todays-youth-really-a-lost-generation/245524/" target="_blank">
          our parents couch
         </a>
         and thousands of dollars in student loans, totaling
         <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/money/perfi/college/story/2011-10-19/student-loan-debt/50818676/1" target="_blank">
          over $1 trillion annually
         </a>
         . As for a good job, well, many of us are busing tables in restaurants and shuffling papers in unpaid internships, but were the lucky ones. For those who didnt make it to college, the unemployment rate is more than doubled at
         <a href="http://www.bls.gov/news.release/pdf/empsit.pdf" target="_blank">
          8.7%
         </a>
         , leading to a total of 14% of young workers (20-24) who are unemployed. While the economy will certainly improve, those years spent doing menial labor will never come back to us, with
         <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2010/03/how-a-new-jobless-era-will-transform-america/7919/?single_page=true" target="_blank">
          estimates
         </a>
         that we could end up earning 10 percent less on average than somebody who left school a few years before or after the recession due to the loss of critical entry-level work experience.
        </p>
        <p>
         As Derek Thompson of the
         <em>
          Altantic
         </em>
         <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2011/09/are-todays-youth-really-a-lost-generation/245524/" target="_blank">
          put it
         </a>
         , For Millennials, this is the great irony of the Great Recession. A crisis that started in the housing market could wind up having the most lasting negative impact on the one generation that didnt own any homes before the bust.
        </p>
        <p>
         Marriage is
         <a href="http://www.pewsocialtrends.org/2010/11/18/the-decline-of-marriage-and-rise-of-new-families/" target="_blank">
          in decline
         </a>
         with many young people choosing to wait or simply throwing marriage out as an outdated concept and opting for cohabitation or other new family forms instead. The idea that all of us should strive to own a home is what brought our economy to its knees so were lowering our expectations on that one a bit. As for retirement, dont think we dont know that social security is just a big ponzi schemeone thats expected to
         <a href="http://money.usnews.com/money/personal-finance/articles/2009/06/16/how-to-prepare-for-the-end-of-social-security" target="_blank">
          run out in 2037
         </a>
         , well before most of us retire.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now that our ladder has been reduced to splinters, the question remains: what does success mean in the 21st century and how do we achieve it?
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-1139">
         </span>
         We know how we
         <em>
          dont
         </em>
         achieve it. We know that decades of runaway capitalism with ever more desperate attempts to improve the bottom line and lobby for more deregulation have failed. We know that measuring our country merely by GDP has put us
         <a href="http://hbr.org/2012/01/the-economics-of-well-being/ar/1" target="_blank">
          25th on the inequality-adjusted Human Development Index
         </a>
         meaning that theres a good reason why the 99% took to Americas streets.
        </p>
        <p>
         So if were not measuring Americas success by GDP, what should we measure? Recently, economists and national leadershave begun pushing for a something radically simple: measure success by happiness. Of course, measuring something as complex as happiness isnt easy but as the recent
         <a href="http://hbr.org/2012/01/executive-summaries/ar/1" target="_blank">
          Harvard Business Review
         </a>
         issue devoted to the topic will tell you, not only is measuring happiness possible, valuing it can greatly increase company profits.
        </p>
        <p>
         Success for my generation will be a shift from business as usual to somethingUmair Haque calls 
         <a href="http://blogs.hbr.org/haque/2010/05/the_betterness_manifesto.html" target="_blank">
          Betterness
         </a>
         . A transitionfrom climbing the ladder of unfulfilling societal expectations and consumerism to blazing a trail with a life guided by a holistic focus on well-being, community and sustainability. Following a better path wont be easy but as we lie dreaming under the glow-in-the-dark stars of our childhood room we know that its at least one dream worth fighting for.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          <a href="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/85broads/files/2012/01/Lisa-Picture-235x300.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-1141" height="270" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/85broads/files/2012/01/Lisa-Picture-235x300.jpg" width="212"/>
          </a>
          A writer, activist and traveler, Lisa Curtiss experience ranges from working in the White House to serving as Peace Corps Volunteer in a small Nigerien village and working with women entrepreneurs in India. For more about Lisa, check out
          <a href="http://www.lisamariecurtis.com/">
           www.lisamariecurtis.com
          </a>
          or follow her on Twitter
          <a href="https://twitter.com/#!/lisacurtis" target="_blank">
           @LisaCurtis
          </a>
          .
         </em>
        </p>

           Hi Frieda,
          </p>
          <p>
           Glad you enjoyed the piece! I certainly agree that some rungs on the traditional ladder are worth keeping in a different form, i.e. Im a strong believer in making college more affordable and I agree that marriage/partnerships and the ability to purchase a house are all worthwhile values. The point of my article wasnt that these rungs are undesirable but rather that they are simply becoming far more difficult to attain. Instead of grasping futilely at the traditional job/house/marriage definition of success, I propose that we re-think why we are striving towards them and what, if anything, could provide an equivalent or greater amount of happiness in their stead. As the old adage goes, what gets measured, gets managed and thus if we measure our life choices by the happiness they provide, we are more likely to manage our life in a way that optimizes our happiness.
          </p>

         <div class="comment_content">
          <p>
           Great article. College used to lead to a job and marriage used to lead to happiness. It seems people are beginning to realize that there is no blueprint or cookie cutter approach to getting either and at the very least everyone needs to take a different path. We shouldnt always look at what others are doing and assume this is the correct route but instead listen to what our gut tells us. Not everyone should go to college and not everyone should get married. Although finding a job and achieving happiness may not be easy for some people, neither one needs to be that difficult as long as we are willing to drop our preconceived notions regarding how to achieve them and what they should look like. Life shouldnt be that complicated and when it becomes complicated we are doing something wrong and need to simplify things. Millennials are moving toward a simplified life which equals a happier life. posted by matthew pisoni, fort lauderdale
