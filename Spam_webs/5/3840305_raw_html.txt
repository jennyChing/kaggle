         An incredible eclipse: Nasa shows the view from its solar spacecraft as the Earth blocks its view

        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          It is a unique view of an eclipse - but one that causes more problems for astronomers that any other.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Twice a year, for three weeks near the equinox, NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) spacecraft moves into its eclipse seasona brief spell when Earth blocks its view of the sun for a period of time each day.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          For engineers, it is a huge problem, as the lack of light causes one of the key instruments aboard, which usually captures stunning images of the sun, to blur.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p style="font-weight: bold;">
         <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
          Scroll down for video
         </font>
        </p>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
        <div>
         <img alt="The Earth begins to obstruct the SDO's view of the Sun" class="blkBorder" height="721" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2012/11/09/article-0-15EF64CA000005DC-368_964x721.jpg" width="964"/>
         <p class="imageCaption">
          The Earth begins to obstruct the SDO's view of the Sun
         </p>

         <div class="art-insert sciencetech">
          <h3 class="wocc">
           <span style="font-weight: bold;">
            A BLURRY PROBLEM
           </span>
           <br/>
          </h3>
          <div class="ins cleared xolcc bdrcc">
           <p>
            <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
             The Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) on SDO observes the sun through a glass window.
            </font>
           </p>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <img alt="image003.jpg" class="blkBorder" height="150" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2012/11/09/article-0-15EF64D4000005DC-751_223x150.jpg" width="223"/>
           <p>
            <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
             The window can change shape in response to temperature changes, and does
 so dramatically and quickly when it doesnt directly feel the suns 
heat.
            </font>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           Engineers designed the orbit of the craft to avoid problems, but the images of an exlipse in September reveal the astonishing images collected, and the blur the eclipse causes.
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           The most recent 
eclipse season took place September 6-29.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
           As Earth entered and 
exited SDOs field of vision, its advanced imaging instruments captured 
partial views of the sun at multiple wavelengths.
          </font>
         </p>
         <div class="related-carousel with-fb sciencetech half" data-track-module="am-related_carousel^related_carousel" data-track-pos="static" data-track-selector=".rotator-panels a:not([class*=external])" id="p-18">

         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           'Solar scientists do 
not get much use from these images, though atmospheric researchers may 
be able to gain some insights by observing the sun's light as it moves 
through Earth's atmosphere,' says Nasa.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           The Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) on SDO observes the sun through a glass window.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           The window can change shape in response to temperature changes, and does so dramatically and quickly when it doesnt directly feel the suns heat.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           The Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) aboard the Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) maps the magnetic field on the sun's surface.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           'Youve got a piece of glass looking at the sun, and then suddenly it isnt,' said Dean Pesnell, the project scientist for SDO at NASAs Goddard Space Flight Center in Greenbelt, Md.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           'The glass gets colder and flexes. It becomes like a lens.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           'Its as if we put a set of eye glasses in front of the instrument, causing the observations to blur.'
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <div class="clear">
         </div>
         <div>
          <img alt="An eclipse causes a blurry image for about 45 minutes after Earth finishes its transit across the sun, as shown on the left. The right half shows normal data." class="blkBorder" height="541" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2012/11/09/article-0-15EF64DC000005DC-158_964x541.jpg" width="964"/>
          <p class="imageCaption">
           An eclipse causes a blurry image for about 45 minutes after Earth finishes its transit across the sun, as shown on the left. The right half shows normal data.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           To counteract this effect, HMI was built with heaters to warm the window during an eclipse.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           By adjusting the timing and temperature of the heater, the HMI team has learned the best procedures for improving resolution quickly.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           Without adjusting the HMI front window heaters, it takes about two hours to return to optimal observing.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           Over the two years since SDO launched in 2010, the team has brought the time it takes to get a clear image down from 60 minutes to around 45 to 50 minutes after an eclipse.
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size:1.2em;">
           'We allocated an hour for these more blurry images,' says Pesnell. 'And weve learned to do a lot better than that.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font style="font-size: 1.2em;">
           'With 45 eclipses a year, the team gets a lot of practice.'
          </font>
         </p>
