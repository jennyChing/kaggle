         <h2 class="date-header">
          01/09/2012
         </h2>
         <div class="entry-author-carl_chatfield entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a0128764b6c2a970c0168e537b762970c">

           <h3 class="entry-header">
            The Predicament of Problem-Solvers
           </h3>
           <div class="entry-content">
            <div class="entry-body">
             <p>
              One smart writer I follow makes a compelling distinction between problems and predicaments (
              <a href="http://thearchdruidreport.blogspot.com/2006/08/problems-and-predicaments.html" target="_self">
               here
              </a>
              ). Some people might think these two terms are interchangeable, but they are not. The difference is best shown through illustration.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              First, let's look at a problem:
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              <a href="http://www.projhugger.com/.a/6a0128764b6c2a970c01676036a8d7970b-pi" style="display: inline;">
               <img alt="Problem-Predicament1" border="0" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a0128764b6c2a970c01676036a8d7970b" src="http://www.projhugger.com/.a/6a0128764b6c2a970c01676036a8d7970b-800wi" title="Problem-Predicament1"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              The climber has a problem to solve (or more likely a set of smaller problems). The resolution will see him arrive safely up with his buddy. Then he and his buddy face another set of problems to descend. The key point is that these are solvable problems. The climber can reach his goal. He might not be successful, but success is possible.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              Contrast this with this illustration of a predicament:
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              <a href="http://www.projhugger.com/.a/6a0128764b6c2a970c0168e53799ba970c-pi" style="display: inline;">
               <img alt="Problem-Predicament2" border="0" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a0128764b6c2a970c0168e53799ba970c" src="http://www.projhugger.com/.a/6a0128764b6c2a970c0168e53799ba970c-800wi" title="Problem-Predicament2"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              This adventurous lad finds himself in an predicament we can call freefall. No matter what else our diver might want to happen, he soon will deal with the issue of landing safely in water (we'll assume there's water below). No set of risk management skills, no amount of prepping can change the expected outcome of his situation at this moment: "I'm in the air, headed for the water below at high velocity."
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              While a problem is solvable, a predicament is not. While a problem might have many possible outcomes, a predicament has essentially one, and try as you might you can't change it.You can think of a predicament as the surrounding environment in which we may find ourselves and basically we have to work within the constraints of the environment. The midair diver cannot change the laws of physics or alter gravity to avoid falling into the water. The climber on the sheer wall however can (we'll assume) apply specific skills and techniques to move himself up (and later back down) the mountain. Do you see the difference?
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              Now in both examples I've chosen, both the climber is working within some predicaments and the diver is applying some problem-solving. Notice our diver is pinching his nostrils closed, for example. This will prevent water from shooting up his sinuses (problem avoided) when he inevitably reaches the water (his inevitable outcome).
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              Our climber likewise is working within the predicament as he applies his problem-solving skills. In this case his predicament is established by the conditions of the mountain he's on, the weather, and even his own prior training and skills development. If he discovers that he forgot to bring an essential piece of equipment with him that he'll need in two seconds, that too is part of his predicament and not a solvable problem.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              Now, let's consider this predicament/problem distinction as it applies to project management and knowledge worker teams. I believe this is worth exploring because I sometimes see smart people try to apply their considerable problem-solving skills to what are at the end of the day predicaments.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              Predicaments are, essentially, constraints that the project manager or team must work within. I don't mean "constraint" here in the narrow technical sense that Project users may be familiar with, such as a "Start No Earlier Than" (SNET) constraint. Instead, I mean the broader aspects of the project's environment that the project manager cannot fundamentally change.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              In my own projects, I usually find that I am resource- and time-constrained. My budgets are defined by others, and generally do not increase (but can decrease) in the short term. My most critical deadlines are likewise usually externally imposed, and not flexible. If I try to apply my resource and time management skills in problem-solving mode, I will at best optimize the situation but cannot fundamentally change these constraining aspects of the project's environment.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              If I think that I ought to be able to "solve" a predicament in the same sense that I might solve a problem, the likely result is what we call frustration. I frequently see very smart people mistake a predicament for a problem, then burn themselves out applying problem-solving strategies to these non-problems.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              Conversely, I might instead mistake a very solvable problem as a predicament and fail to properly address it. This can be just as bad. In fact, it's worse because it leaves potentially solvable problems unaddressed.
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              The next time you're working through the defining issues of a complex project, give some thought to this question: What here is a solvable problem, and what is a predicament?
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              (My thanks to financial blogger
              <a href="http://www.chrismartenson.com/" target="_self">
               Chris Martenson
              </a>
              for illustrating the problem/predicament distinction so well.)
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
             </p>
             <p style="margin: 0in; font-family: Calibri; font-size: 11pt;">
              ###
             </p>
