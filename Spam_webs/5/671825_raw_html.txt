        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #800000;">
           Rising from the edge of the Karakum Desert is Turkmenistans capital city,
           <strong>
            Ashgabat
           </strong>
           , a modern metropolis that sparkles and shimmers with a kind of glamour that seems unfitting in time-warped Central Asia. Over-the-top white marble buildings flank wide and empty boulevards, while psychedelic government edifices loom over perfectly manicured gardens. The city is a jumble of lavish golden-domed palaces, Bellagio fountains, neon-lit monuments and Stalinist ministry buildings. From the surface, the city rivals Abu Dhabi
           <a href="http://yourdailyexperience.com/2013/10/decorate-your-kids-room/" target="_bank" title="Decorate your kids room">
            <strong>
             or
            </strong>
           </a>

            <strong>
             Dubai
            </strong>
           </a>
           in terms of opulence and wealth.
          </span>
         </p>

          <em>
           The Alem Entertainment Center in Ashgabat. In 2012 the wheel atop this complex was entered into the Guinness Book of World Records as the worlds largest enclosed Ferris wheel. The structure was built at a cost of $90m.
           <span style="color: #999999;">
            <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/infocus/2013/06/the-city-of-white-marble-ashgabat-turkmenistan/100528/" rel="nofollow">

        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #800000;">
           In 2013 Ashgabat entered the Guinness World Records for having the highest concentration of white marble buildings than any other city in the world. In an area measuring just 22 square km in the capital Ashgabat, there are 543 new buildings clad with 4.5 million cubic meters of white marble. If the marble was laid out flat, according to Guinness, there would be one square meter of marble for every 4.87 m square meter of land. The main avenue, Bitarap Trkmenistan Sayolu, is 12.6 km long and lined with 170 buildings clad with a total of 1.1 million cubic meters of white marble.
          </span>
         </p>
