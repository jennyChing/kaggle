      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Is the road safety message getting through?" src="/content/dam/images/1/o/b/n/v/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.1obd6.png/1322841600000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Is the road safety message getting through?
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       <em>
        <strong>
         <a href="#die">
          Click here
         </a>
         for the original article.
        </strong>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       KAY Fenton-Branson was a young teacher at Rushworth High, near Shepparton, in the early '70s when police officers addressed a school assembly she will never forget. There had been a succession of horrendous car accidents: ''Year after year young people were killing themselves very efficiently,'' Fenton-Branson says.
      </p>
      <p>
       The police pulled no punches. Every student was given a copy of journalist Roger Aldridge's gruesome account of how people die in car crashes. It contained no flowery metaphors, no cushioning of the truth: ''Some people explode like a thin plastic envelope full of offal '' The police also showed graphic images and told confronting stories about accident scenes filled with dead, dying and injured people. The impact the assembly had on every student and teacher was extraordinary. ''I think they actually started to think, 'This could happen to me,' '' Fenton-Branson says.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imagePortrait">
       <img alt="Reprinted from The Age, Thursday October 26, 1972." itemprop="image" src="/content/dam/images/1/o/b/n/w/image.related.articleLeadNarrow.300x0.1obd6.png/1322841600000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Reprinted from The Age, Thursday October 26, 1972.
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       ''One of my lovely year 12 students was telling me she always kept Roger Aldridge's article in the bottom of her drawer. She said sometimes when she was getting ready to go out she would see the article and it would remind her not to get into a car with anyone drink-driving.''
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       Fenton-Branson, now an academic at La Trobe University, worries this jarring message has been lost. She fears the horror of car crashes, the blood and gore, have been masked by romanticised images of roadside shrines and hugging teenagers, supporting one another through crisis. ''Is it time our students were given a copy of this explicit article to remind them that a road accident is an awful, hideous and macabre event that changes lives forever?''
      </p>
      <p>
       The efficacy of so-called ''fear appeals'' - scaring people into change - is a hotly debated topic in road-safety education. Some say research shows shock tactics fail when it comes to young people. Others argue that exposing them to the grisly reality could save lives.
      </p>
      <p>
       ''There is a fair bit of controversy about what's OK and what's not OK in schools,'' says Road Trauma Support Services education services manager Chris Harrison. ''I think we are still finding our way around it.'' Road Trauma Support Services run sessions in schools, with accident victims or culpable drivers as guest speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       ''Having real people tell their personal story and maybe showing the crashed car and pictures of them through recovery can be very powerful as it is real,'' Harrison says. However, she stresses it's important the sessions do not have a negative impact on students. ''The aim is not to traumatise them or to scare them as we want them to drive and drive safely.''
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the first Transport Accident Commission commercial went to air on December 10, 1989, TV viewers have been bombarded with distressing images of bloodied bodies, heads slamming through windscreens and grieving parents. However, these ads are not aimed at school students, according to TAC road safety project manager Liz Knight.
      </p>
      <p>
       ''The research is quite strong that often when presented with something they feel is confronting and threatening, there can be a switch-off in their brain,'' Knight says. There are all sorts of reasons why students may dismiss shocking road safety messages. Some are overconfident, Knight says, believing they have the skills to prevent accidents.
      </p>
      <p>
       Others think they are immune because they don't speed or drink-drive. Some are so traumatised they simply shut down. ''There is often a community belief or intuition that fear appeals will be effective,'' Knight says. ''If we knew it worked, we would be supporting it but  the research is really clear it doesn't work.''
      </p>
      <p>
       The Victorian Education Department says it is inappropriate to ''traumatise'' young people. ''The duty of care of the teacher precludes them from using any technique that uses graphic material to try and traumatise the students,'' a spokesman says. Instead, primary students are taught bus, passenger and bike safety.
      </p>
      <p>
       Year 10 students, targeted just before they get their licences, are taught about the impact of fatigue, drugs, alcohol and speed. ''We ask teachers to ensure they provide a positive approach,'' the spokesman says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Three years ago Pam Knight received the phone call every parent dreads. Her son had been a passenger in a car that had slammed into a tree. ''Tim was on life support for a long time, he's still recovering,'' Knight says. ''There was no alcohol involved; however, we think the driver was driving foolishly. We heard afterwards he had [previously] been doing burnouts at Chirnside Park Shopping Centre.''
      </p>
      <p>
       These days Knight talks to young people about the accident. She shows photos of the car, so mangled that students are amazed Tim got out alive. Recently she spoke at Croydon TAFE with a guy who killed his own best mate in a crash because he was drunk.
      </p>
      <p>
       ''That was so powerful,'' Knight says. ''I could see the looks on [students'] faces - they were shocked. I think the more shock the better because it might stick in their mind. What do they want: [students] to die rather than be traumatised?''
      </p>
      <p>
       Is the road safety message getting through? In 1972, when Rushworth High students were handed Roger Aldridge's article
       <em>
        And this is how you die
       </em>
        491 people aged 25 or under were killed on Victoria's roads. By last year, the number had shrunk to 88.
      </p>
      <p>
       Professor Max Cameron, of the Monash University Accident Research Centre, says research has constantly shown fear appeals don't work. A better investment in road safety is improving road systems, he says, such as lowering speed limits and increasing speed enforcement.
      </p>
      <p>
       He says technologies such as alcohol interlocks, which prevent a car starting if the driver has been drinking, are also effective. ''There is no doubt about it - it's a physical constraint that prevents drink-driving '' he says. ''You can waste a lot of time and resources thinking pouring money into driver education is a panacea when it's not the case.''
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>

        <em>
         The Age
        </em>
        39 years ago, contains explicit descriptions  but it may save your life
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <br/>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a name="die">

      </p>
      <p>
       <a name="die">
        <span style="font-size: large;">
         <strong>
          And this is how you die
         </strong>
        </span>
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a name="die">
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        By Roger Aldridge
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       How do people die in motor "accidents"?
      </p>
      <p>
       I'll tell you.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some people explode  like a thin plastic envelope full of offal which has been hurled against a brick wall. No pain.
      </p>
      <p>
       They put them on a sheet of canvas and pick it up at the corners like, as one tow truck driver described it: "A tub of guts"
      </p>
      <p>
       I haven't seen one of these.
      </p>
      <p>
       Others die intact. Ruptured inside, you understand, but un-harmed to look at. There may be a thin, trickle
       <br/>
       of blood from an ear or nostril.
      </p>
      <p>
       It annoys you, subconsciously ... you wish they'd raise a dead hand and wipe it away.
      </p>
      <p>
       Death is not instantaneous.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rather, it comes in a matter of minutes. There is no pain as we know it ... nothing sharp, exquisite, searing. It is an inner numbness, a bubbling frothing thing and a terrible inability to breathe.
      </p>
      <p>
       They are winded, punched in the stomach by a ton of metal moving at 60 mph or more, shattering
       <br/>
       every bone in the body as a fist would shatter a wine glass wrapped in a rug.
      </p>
      <p>
       They never breathe again.
      </p>
      <p>
       I've seen a number of these.
      </p>
      <p>
       Men die with their trousers on, which somehow lends them dignity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Women die with their legs apart in a lewd display.
      </p>
      <p>
       Children die most horribly because they are seldom properly seated or braced. And they
       <br/>
       are very small. They are thrown through jagged windscreens to roll and skid along road surfaces
       <br/>
       as abrasive as cheese-graters.
      </p>
      <p>
       Or, cradled in their mothers laps, they are sandwiched between her and the unyielding dashboard. Mummy might just as well have jumped on the child from a third-storey window.
      </p>
      <p>
       Without meaning to, of course.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some people are burned to death.
      </p>
      <p>
       They are not incinerated, as you'd imagine, but tend to bake or char.
      </p>
      <p>
       Their clothes burn off them if it is wool it forms a ghastly black"crackling" and the skin bakes into quite a hard rind which makes a hollow sound if you tap it.
      </p>
      <p>
       When the corpse is lifted from the wreckage it is as rigid as a papier mache dummy.
      </p>
      <p>
       Often it is set in a sculpted, lifelike posture, but unnaturally stiff, like the little plastic drivers that toy manufacturers put in the front seats of model cars.
      </p>
      <p>
       I've seen a couple of these, too.
      </p>
      <p>
       I've seen mens faces buried in the stringy bark of a tree trunk; fixed there, seemingly, by
       <br/>
       a gob of sticky red gum.
      </p>
      <p>
       And men hanging from halfopen car doors; fl ung rag dolls of men embracing steel power pylons; men skewered on steering columns; men whose faces are gone, as if nibbled by rats.
      </p>
      <p>
       I've seen men survive.
      </p>
      <p>
       Dragged from the back seat, soaked in a shandy of blood and beer, the shards of smashed bottles glinting in the frantic blue of the revolving police light.
      </p>
      <p>
       Carried into casualty on a stretcher, hurt, frightened, shocked.
      </p>
      <p>
       Men without dignity, crying while other men cut away their blood-soaked rags and yet other men explore abdomen and groin with fingers that feel like fence-posts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Men blinking through blood and tears into bright lights while probes and tweezers remove chunks and slivers of glass from facial wounds  eyes, cheeks, gums  that big bit was a tooth. Two teeth, actually.
       <br/>
       Having trouble talking.
      </p>
      <p>
       Panic-stricken men with crushed rib-cages trying to breathe through broken bellows. Grey-faced, incoherent, being asked questions:
      </p>
      <p>
       What's your name? Are you married? Where do you live? Where does it hurt ... here ... here ... does THAT hurt? Any children?
      </p>
      <p>
       Thighs as flexible as a rolled-up towel, pushed back into shape and splinted. Men wheeled into the X-ray room and laid this way, then that while the ragged edges of a broken pelvic girdle scrape together. Got to get a good picture.
      </p>
      <p>
       <br/>
       Men denied pain-killers while an eternity of assessment passes and other men pierce their arms and insert tubes and hold up little canisters of blood ... blood donors love life, but butter eaters make better lovers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Then sliding blissfully into euphoria as the pain-killing injection hits and they are wheeled into the operating theatre.
      </p>
      <p>
       And I've seen men survive this, too.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Russians were criticised in the 1930s for severing a dog's head and keeping it clinically alive for a number of hours.
      </p>
      <p>
       Alive enough to salivate at the smell of food.
      </p>
      <p>
       I've seen men in the quadriplegic wards at the Austin Hospital and at Mont Park who might just as well not have bodies, although their heads are alive.
      </p>
      <p>
       The unlucky ones are mentally unimpaired and strive for months and years to learn to write with a pencil held in their teeth, or to type by flicking one of the few remaining responsive muscles in their bodies.
      </p>
      <p>
       Their intelligence is sharp, their appetite for books and learning is gargantuan, their role in life that of the eternal spectator ... eternity being, in some cases, a "lif" expectancy of 50 or more years.
      </p>
      <p>
       They make the best of it, but many wish for death.
      </p>
      <p>
       And I've seen the lucky ones, those with brain damage, whose minds were shaken loose in the
       <br/>
       cataclysm of car with car.
      </p>
      <p>
       Men with glazed, half-lidded eyes, with neither bowel nor bladder control who sog in bed with no sensation below the shoulders so that bowel obstructions, appendicitis, bladder problems go undetected
       <br/>
       by the normal warning systems which we know as pain.
      </p>
      <p>
       Men whose total sexual impotence is parodied by an apparent state of constant sexual excitation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Men who were mothers' sons, wives' husbands, girls' lovers, children's fathers. Men who recognise no one.
      </p>
      <p>
       Or men whose eyes ignite for a brief moment with recognition, whose mouths open to speak a flubbery sound like deflating bubble gum, then sink exhausted into the pillow.
      </p>
      <p>
       I've seen things that make me sick to the heart. I thought you should know.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Reprinted from The Age, Thursday, October 26, 1972
       </strong>
      </p>
