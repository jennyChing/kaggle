            <h1 class="entry-title single-title" itemprop="headline">
             Fitness Fix: Help for Weak Wrists
            </h1>
           </header>
           <!-- end article header -->
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="addthis_custom_sharing desktop">
           </div>
           <article class="clearfix post-52866 article type-article status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-fitness category-injury-recovery departments-fitness-fixes lifetime-tags-move-it issues-julyaugust-2014-built-to-last department-fitness-fixes issue-julyaugust-2014-built-to-last fitness injury-recovery" id="post-52866" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting" role="article">
            <div class="entry-content clearfix" itemprop="articleBody">
             <img alt="Screen-Shot-2014-08-27-at-7.42.03-AM" class="featured-image wp-post-image" height="278" src="https://experiencelife.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Screen-Shot-2014-08-27-at-7.42.03-AM1.png" width="478"/>
             <h5 class="byline">
              <span class="author vcard" itemprop="author">
               <a class="url fn n" href="https://experiencelife.com/author/noauthor/" rel="author" title="More from Experience Life Staff">
                Experience Life Staff
               </a>
              </span>
              <span class="sep">
               
              </span>
              <time class="entry-date updated dtstamp pubdate" datetime="July/August 2014: Built To Last">
               <a href="/issues/julyaugust-2014-built-to-last">
                July/August 2014
               </a>
              </time>
              <time style="float:right;margin-top:3px;clear:right;">
              </time>
             </h5>
             <div class="excerpt">
              <p>
               Loosen up tight wrists with these simple drills.
              </p>
             </div>
             <p>
              When people get wrist pain during weight-bearing exercises, they tend to assume that the problem is weakness, says chiropractor and athletic-movement expert Eric Cobb, DC. But the real issue is often mobility or stability.
             </p>
             <p>
              <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="250" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/BHfKutz21do?feature=player_embedded" width="400">
              </iframe>
             </p>
             <h2>
              Wrist Mobilizer
             </h2>
             <a href="http://experiencelife.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Wrist-Mobilizer.jpg">
              <img alt="Wrist-Mobilizer" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-52466" height="250" src="https://experiencelife.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Wrist-Mobilizer.jpg" width="400"/>
             </a>
             <ul style="color: #000000;">
              <li style="color: #414042;">
               To mobilize your wrists  and improve their suppleness and pain-free range of motion  Cobb recommends a simple wrist-circling drill, done at low intensity (two or three on a 10-point scale) for five to six reps in either direction:
              </li>
              <li style="color: #414042;">
               Stand in a neutral
               <a href="http://experiencelife.com/article/back-on-track-a-posture-recovery-workout/" style="color: #f15b25;" target="_blank" title="Back on Track: A Posture Recovery Workout">
                posture
               </a>
               . Keeping your upper arms close to your body and your palms down, bend your elbows until your forearms are parallel to the floor and hold your hands in loose fists.
              </li>
              <li style="color: #414042;">
               Focusing on moving your wrist  not your hand  slowly circle your wrists counterclockwise, forming a complete circle with your wrist (it may help to imagine that you are loosely gripping a stationary object, like a steering wheel).
              </li>
              <li style="color: #414042;">
               Do five circles, then do five in the other direction.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <h2>
              Wrist Stabilizer
             </h2>
             <a href="http://experiencelife.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Wrist-Stabilizer.jpg">
              <img alt="Wrist-Stabilizer" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-52467" height="200" src="https://experiencelife.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Wrist-Stabilizer.jpg" width="500"/>
             </a>
             <p>
              Next, Cobb recommends a low-intensity drill that will help stabilize the wrists by freeing up the nerves that connect the spinal column to the hands:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Stand upright with a tall spine. Keep your arms at your sides with palms facing backward.
              </li>
              <li>
               Spread the fingers of your left hand and extend your wrist so your palm is facing the floor (your fingers will now be pointing forward).
              </li>
              <li>
               Keeping your elbow straight, use your shoulder to rotate your left arm outward (your fingers pointing backward) and lift the arm slightly out to the side.
              </li>
              <li>
               Pull your left
               <a href="http://experiencelife.com/article/shifty-shoulders/" target="_blank" title="Shifty Shoulders">
                shoulder
               </a>
               downward and tilt your head directly to the right.
              </li>
              <li>
               From that basic position, gently lift and lower your left shoulder three times.
              </li>
              <li>
               Returning to the starting position, slightly bend then straighten your left elbow three times. Then bend your wrist up and down fully, three times.
              </li>
              <li>
               Repeat the entire drill on your right side.
              </li>
             </ul>
