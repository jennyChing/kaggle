        There is a thing inherent and
        <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">
         natural
        </a>

         Mother
        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">
         Universe
        </a>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">
         name
        </a>
        . If I am forced to give it a name, I call it
        <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">
         Tao
        </a>
        , and I name it as supreme.

     <p>
      <b>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laozi" title="w:Laozi">
         Loz
       </a>
      </b>
      (c. 6th-5th century BCE) was a Chinese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monist" title="Monist">
       monist
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">
       philosopher
      </a>
      ; also called
      <b>
       Lao Zi
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Lao Tzu
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Lao Tse
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       Lao Tze
      </b>
      . The
      <i>
       <b>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tao_Te_Ching" title="w:Tao Te Ching">
         Tao Te Ching
        </a>
       </b>
      </i>
      (,
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin" title="w:Pinyin">
       Pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Do D Jng
      </i>
      , or
      <i>
       Dao De Jing
      </i>
      ) represents the sole document generally attributed to Laozi.
     </p>

     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        We believe that the Daoist tradition started as a response to the excesses of
        <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">
         civilization
        </a>
        . That was Lao Tzu's deal anyway.
       </b>
       Lots of similar traditions dealt with issues of work and status and anxiety and nature the same way. But they were all, pretty much, taken over by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fascists" title="Fascists">
        fascists
       </a>
       and real reactionaries. Even
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism" title="w:Taoism">
        Taoism
       </a>
       was taken over by charlatans and phonies.
       <b>
        But the pure undogmatic centre of lots of traditions (
        <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
         Christianity
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedism" title="w:Vedism">
         Vedism
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
         Buddhism
        </a>
        etc) is all the same. And that's Daoism.
       </b>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Benjamin" title="w:Oliver Benjamin">
          Oliver Benjamin
         </a>
         , as quoted in
         <a class="external text" href="http://www.dontpaniconline.com/magazine/radar/big-lebowski-spawns-religion" rel="nofollow">
          "Big Lebowski Spawns Religion" by Yusuf Laher in
          <i>
           Don't Panic Online
          </i>
          (11 April 2011)
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       According to religious scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Huston_Smith" title="Huston Smith">
        Huston Smith
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism" title="w:Taoism">
        Taoism
       </a>
       has only one basic text, the
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tao_Te_Ching" title="w:Tao Te Ching">
         Tao Te Ching
        </a>
       </i>
       (or, in English,
       <i>
        The Way and Its Power
       </i>
       ), a slim volume that, as Smith says, can be read in half an hour or a lifetime.
       <b>
        Legend has it that a Chinaman by the name of
        <strong class="selflink">
         Lao Tzu
        </strong>
        one day said "Enough!" (loosely translated from the Chinese), hopped on a water buffalo (possibly with rust coloration), and started heading a-way out west to Tibet.
       </b>
       <br/>
       On his way out, someone stopped Lao Tzu and asked if he would write down the tenets of his
       <i>
        <b>
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ethos" title="w:ethos">
          ethos
         </a>
        </b>
       </i>
       before leaving town. Being a lazy man, Lao Tzu lodged his water buffalo against an abutment long enough to write the Tao Te Chings 81 short verses. When finished, he kicked his water buffalo into gear and, tossing his ringer to the man, rode off into the misty horizon of legend and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">
        myth
       </a>
       .
       <br/>
       <b>
        Regardless of whether the legend is true, or whether Lao Tzu even really existed, the Chinaman is not the issue here, Dudes.
       </b>
       The issue is that the
       <i>
        Tao Te Ching
       </i>
       is the perfect expression of Taoisms
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/wu_wei" title="w:wu wei">
         wu wei
        </a>
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">
        life
       </a>
       , or in the parlance of Huston Smith, a life of creative quietude in which "the conscious mind must relax, stop standing in its own
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       , let go" so that it can flow with the
       <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">
        Tao
       </a>
       (or Way) of the
       <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">
        universe
       </a>
       .
       <ul>
        <li>
         Rev. Dwayne Eutsey, in the Introduction of the Daoist holy book
         <a class="external text" href="http://dudeism.com/tao/" rel="nofollow">
          <i>
           The Tao De Ching
          </i>
          (2010)
         </a>
         .
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>

      <li>
       <b>
        The oldest known Chinese sage is Lao-Tze, the founder of Taoism. "Lao Tze" is not really a proper name, but means merely "the old philosopher."
       </b>
       He was (according to tradition) an older contemporary of
       <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">
        Confucius
       </a>
       , and his philosophy is to my mind far more interesting. He held that every person, every animal, and every thing has a certain way or manner of behaving which is natural to him, or her, or it, and that we ought to conform to this way ourselves and encourage others to conform to it. "
       <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">
        Tao
       </a>
       " means "way," but used in a more or less
       <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">
        mystical
       </a>
       sense, as in the text: "I am the Way and the
       <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">
        Truth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">
        Life
       </a>
       ." I think he fancied that
       <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">
        death
       </a>
       was due to departing from the "way," and that if we all lived strictly according to
       <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">
        nature
       </a>
       we should be immortal, like the heavenly bodies.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">
          Bertrand Russell
         </a>
         in
         <i>
          The Problem of China
         </i>
         (1922), Ch. XI - Chinese and Western Civilization Contrasted
        </li>
       </ul>
