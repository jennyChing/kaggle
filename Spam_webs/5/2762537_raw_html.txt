     <p>
      <b>
       Self-help
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       self-improvement
      </b>
      , is a self-guided improvement
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APA_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-APA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      economically, intellectually, or emotionallyoften with a substantial
      <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
       psychological
      </a>
      basis. Many different self-help group programs exist, each with its own focus, techniques, associated beliefs, proponents and in some cases,
      <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">
       leaders
      </a>
      . "Self-help culture, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Twelve-step_program" title="Twelve-step program">
       Twelve-Step
      </a>
      culture, has provided some of our most robust new language:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recovery_model" title="Recovery model">
       recovery
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysfunctional_families" title="Dysfunctional families">
       dysfunctional families
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Codependency" title="Codependency">
       codependency
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Self-help often utilizes publicly available information or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Support_groups" title="Support groups">
       support groups
      </a>
      , on the Internet as well as in person, where people in similar situations join together.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APA_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-APA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From early examples in self-driven legal practice
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salerno_2005_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Salerno_2005-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and home-spun advice, the connotations of the word have spread and often apply particularly to
      <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">
       education
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">
       business
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
       psychology
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Psychotherapy" title="Psychotherapy">
       psychotherapy
      </a>
      , commonly distributed through the popular genre of
      <a href="/wiki/Self-help_book" title="Self-help book">
       self-help books
      </a>
      . According to the
      <i>
       APA Dictionary of Psychology
      </i>
      , potential benefits of self-help groups that professionals may not be able to provide include friendship, emotional support, experiential knowledge,
      <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">
       identity
      </a>
      , meaningful roles, and a sense of belonging.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APA_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-APA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Groups associated with health conditions may consist of patients and
      <a href="/wiki/Caregiver" title="Caregiver">
       caregivers
      </a>
      . As well as featuring long-time members sharing
      <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">
       experiences
      </a>
      , these health groups can become
      <a href="/wiki/Support_group" title="Support group">
       support groups
      </a>
      and clearing-houses for educational material. Those who help themselves by learning and identifying about health problems can be said to exemplify self-help, while self-help groups can be seen more as peer-to-peer support.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Within
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">
       classical antiquity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">
       Hesiod
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Works_and_Days" title="Works and Days">
        Works and Days
       </a>
      </i>
      "opens with moral remonstrances, hammered home in every way that Hesiod can think of."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoics" title="Stoics">
       Stoics
      </a>
      offered ethical advice "on the notion of
      <i>
       eudaimonia
      </i>
      of well-being, welfare, flourishing."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The genre of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirror-of-princes_writing" title="Mirror-of-princes writing">
       mirror-of-princes writings
      </a>
      , which has a long history in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" title="Greco-Roman">
       Greco-Roman
      </a>
      and Western
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
       Renaissance
      </a>
      literature, represents a secular cognate of Biblical wisdom-literature.
      <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">
       Proverbs
      </a>
      from many periods, collected and uncollected, embody traditional moral and practical advice of diverse cultures.
     </p>
     <p>
      The hyphenated
      <a href="/wiki/English_compound" title="English compound">
       compound
      </a>
      word "self-help" often appeared in the 1800s in a legal context, referring to the doctrine that a party in a dispute has the right to use lawful means on their own initiative to remedy a wrong.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For some,
      <a href="/wiki/George_Combe" title="George Combe">
       George Combe
      </a>
      's "
      <i>
       Constitution
      </i>
      [1828], in the way that it advocated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_responsibility" title="Personal responsibility">
       personal responsibility
      </a>
      and the possibility of naturally sanctioned self-improvement through education or proper self-control, largely inaugurated the self-help movement;"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1841, an essay by
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">
       Ralph Waldo Emerson
      </a>
      , entitled
      <a href="/wiki/Compensation_(essay)" title="Compensation (essay)">
       Compensation
      </a>
      , was published suggesting "every man in his lifetime needs to thank his faults" and "acquire habits of
      <i>
       self-help
      </i>
      " as "our strength grows out of our weakness."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Smiles" title="Samuel Smiles">
       Samuel Smiles
      </a>
      (18121904) published the first self-consciously personal-development "self-help" bookentitled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Self-Help_(book)" title="Self-Help (book)">
        Self-Help
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1859. Its opening sentence: "Heaven helps those who help themselves", provides a variation of "God helps them that help themselves", the oft-quoted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxim_(saying)" title="Maxim (saying)">
       maxim
      </a>
      that had also appeared previously in
      <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">
       Benjamin Franklin
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poor_Richard%27s_Almanac" title="Poor Richard's Almanac">
        Poor Richard's Almanac
       </a>
      </i>
      (17331758). In the 20th century, "
      <a href="/wiki/Dale_Carnegie" title="Dale Carnegie">
       Carnegie
      </a>
      's remarkable success as a self-help author"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      further developed the genre with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/How_to_Win_Friends_and_Influence_People" title="How to Win Friends and Influence People">
        How to Win Friends and Influence People
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1936. Having failed in several careers, Carnegie became fascinated with success and its link to
      <a href="/wiki/Self-confidence" title="Self-confidence">
       self-confidence
      </a>
      , and his books have since sold over 50 million copies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Earlier in 1902
      <a href="/wiki/James_Allen_(author)" title="James Allen (author)">
       James Allen
      </a>
      published
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/As_a_Man_Thinketh" title="As a Man Thinketh">
        As a Man Thinketh
       </a>
      </i>
      , which proceeds from the conviction that "a man is literally what he thinks, his character being the complete sum of all his thoughts." Noble thoughts, the book maintains, make for a noble person, whilst lowly thoughts make for a miserable person; and
      <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Hill" title="Napoleon Hill">
       Napoleon Hill's
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Think_and_Grow_Rich" title="Think and Grow Rich">
        Think and Grow Rich
       </a>
      </i>
      (1937) described the use of repeated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_thought" title="Positive thought">
       positive thoughts
      </a>
      to attract happiness and wealth by tapping into an "
      <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">
       Infinite
      </a>
      Intelligence".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th_century">
       Late 20th century
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Late 20th century">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the final third of the 20th century "the tremendous growth in self-help publishing...in self-improvement culture"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      really took offsomething which must be linked to
      <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">
       postmodernism
      </a>
      itselfto the way "postmodern subjectivity constructs self-reflexive subjects-in-process."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Arguably at least, "in the literatures of self-improvement...that crisis of subjecthood is not articulated but enacteddemonstrated in ever-expanding self-help book sales."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The conservative turn of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoliberal" title="Neoliberal">
       neoliberal
      </a>
      decades also meant a decline in traditional political activism, and increasing "social isolation; Twelve-Step recovery groups were one context in which individuals sought a sense of community...yet another symptom of the psychologizing of the personal"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to more radical critics. Indeed, "some social theorist [
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
        sic
       </a>
      </i>
      ] have argued that the late-20th century preoccupation with the self serves as a tool of social control: soothing political unrest...[for] one's own pursuit of self-invention."'
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_market">
       The market
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The market">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Within the context of the market, group and corporate attempts to aid the "seeker" have moved into the "self-help" marketplace, with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_Group_Awareness_Training" title="Large Group Awareness Training">
       LGATs
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Psychotherapy" title="Psychotherapy">
       psychotherapy
      </a>
      systems represented. These offer more-or-less prepackaged solutions to instruct people seeking their own individual betterment
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , just as "the literature of self-improvement directs the reader to familiar frameworks...what the French
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fin_de_si%C3%A8cle" title="Fin de sicle">
        fin de sicle
       </a>
      </i>
      social theorist
      <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Tarde" title="Gabriel Tarde">
       Gabriel Tarde
      </a>
      called 'the grooves of borrowed thought'."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A subgenre of self-help book series also exists: such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/...for_Dummies" title="...for Dummies">
       <i>
        for Dummies
       </i>
       guides
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Complete_Idiot%27s_Guide_to..." title="The Complete Idiot's Guide to...">
        The Complete Idiot's Guide to...
       </a>
      </i>
      compare
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/How-to_book" title="How-to book">
       how-to books
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">
       Statistics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Statistics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      At the start of the 21st century, "the self-improvement industry, inclusive of books, seminars, audio and video products, and personal coaching, [was] said to constitute a 2.48-billion dollars-a-year industry"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the United States alone. By 2006, research firm Marketdata estimated the "self-improvement" market in the U.S. as worth more than $9 billionincluding
      <a href="/wiki/Infomercial" title="Infomercial">
       infomercials
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail-order_catalog" title="Mail-order catalog">
       mail-order catalogs
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic" title="Holistic">
       holistic
      </a>
      institutes, books,
      <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">
       audio cassettes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Motivational_speaker" title="Motivational speaker">
       motivation-speaker
      </a>
      seminars, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_coaching" title="Personal coaching">
       personal coaching
      </a>
      market,
      <a href="/wiki/Weight_loss" title="Weight loss">
       weight-loss
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Stress_management" title="Stress management">
       stress-management
      </a>
      programs. Marketdata projected that the total market size would grow to over $11 billion by 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2012 Laura Vanderkam wrote of a turnover of 12 billion dollars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2013
      <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Schulz" title="Kathryn Schulz">
       Kathryn Schulz
      </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Self-help_and_professional_service_delivery">
       Self-help and professional service delivery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Self-help and professional service delivery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Self-help and mutual-help are very different fromthough they may complementservice delivery by professionals: note for example the interface between local self-help and International Aid's service delivery model.
     </p>
     <p>
      Conflicts can and do arise on that interface, however, with some professionals considering that "the twelve-step approach encourages a kind of contemporary version of 19th-century amateurism or enthusiasm in which self-examination and very general social observations are enough to draw rather large conclusions."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
       Research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The rise of self-help culture has inevitably led to boundary disputes with other approaches and disciplines. Some would object to their classification as "self-help" literature, as with "
      <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Tannen" title="Deborah Tannen">
       Deborah Tannen
      </a>
      's denial of the self-help role of her books" so as to maintain her academic credibility, aware of the danger that "writing a book that becomes a popular success...all but ensures that one's work will lose its long-term legitimacy."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Placebo" title="Placebo">
       Placebo
      </a>
      effects can never be wholly discounted. Thus careful studies of "the power of subliminal self-help tapes...showed that their content had no real effect...But that's not what the participants thought."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "If they thought they'd listened to a self-esteem tape (even though half the labels were wrong), they felt that their self-esteem had gone up. No wonder people keep buying subliminal tape: even though the tapes don't work, people think they do."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One might then see much of the self-help industry as part of the "skin trades. People need haircuts, massage, dentistry, wigs and glasses, sociology and surgery, as well as love and advice."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a skin trade, "not a profession and a science"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its practitioners would thus be functioning as "part of the personal service industry rather than as mental health professionals."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While "there is no proof that twelve-step programs 'are superior to any other intervention in reducing alcohol dependence or alcohol-related problems',"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      at the same time it is clear that "there is something about 'groupishness' itself which is curative."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus for example "smoking increases mortality risk by a factor of just 1.6, while social isolation does so by a factor of 2.0...suggest[ing] an added value to self-help groups such as
      <a href="/wiki/Alcoholics_Anonymous" title="Alcoholics Anonymous">
       Alcoholics Anonymous
      </a>
      as surrogate communities."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_positive_psychologists" title="List of positive psychologists">
       Some psychologists
      </a>
      advocate a
      <a href="/wiki/Positive_psychology" title="Positive psychology">
       positive psychology
      </a>
      , and explicitly embrace an empirical self-help philosophy; "the role of positive psychology is to become a bridge between the ivory tower and the main streetbetween the rigor of academe and the fun of the self-help movement."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They aim to refine the self-improvement field by way of an intentional increase in scientifically sound research and well-engineered models. The division of focus and methodologies has produced several subfields, in particular: general positive psychology, focusing primarily on the study of psychological phenomenon and effects; and
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_effectiveness" title="Personal effectiveness">
       personal effectiveness
      </a>
      , focusing primarily on analysis, design and implementation of qualitative personal growth. This includes the intentional training of new patterns of thought and feeling. As business strategy communicator
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Tapscott" title="Don Tapscott">
       Don Tapscott
      </a>
      puts it, "The design industry is something done to us. I'm proposing we each become designers. But I suppose 'I love the way she thinks' could take on new meaning."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Scholars have targeted self-help claims as misleading and incorrect. In 2005 Steve Salerno portrayed the American self-help movementhe uses the acronym
      <i>
       SHAM: the Self-Help and Actualization Movement
      </i>
      not only as ineffective in achieving its goals, but also as socially harmful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salerno_2005_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Salerno_2005-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Salerno says that 80 percent of self-help and motivational customers are repeat customers and they keep coming back 'whether the program worked for them or not'."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Others similarly point out that with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-help_books" title="Self-help books">
       self-help books
      </a>
      "supply increases the demand... The more people read them, the more they think they need them... more like an addiction than an alliance."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Self-help writers have been described as working "in the area of the ideological, the imagined, the narrativized.... although a veneer of scientism permeates the[ir] work, there is also an underlying armature of moralizing."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Buckley_(novelist)" title="Christopher Buckley (novelist)">
       Christopher Buckley
      </a>
      in his book
      <i>
       God is My Broker
      </i>
      asserts: "The only way to get rich from a self-help book is to write one".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-balance_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-balance-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_media">
       In the media
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In the media">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </span>

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Schulz" title="Kathryn Schulz">
       Kathryn Schulz
      </a>
      suggests that "the underlying theory of the self-help industry is contradicted by the self-help industrys existence".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies_and_fictional_analogies">
       Parodies and fictional analogies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Self-help&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Parodies and fictional analogies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The self-help world has become the target of
      <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">
       parodies
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Walker_Percy" title="Walker Percy">
       Walker Percy
      </a>
      's odd genre-busting
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Lost_in_the_Cosmos" title="Lost in the Cosmos">
        Lost in the Cosmos
       </a>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      has been described as "a parody of self-help books, a philosophy textbook, and a collection of short stories, quizzes, diagrams, thought experiments, mathematical formulas, made-up dialogue".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In their 2006 book
      <i>
       Secrets of The Superoptimist
      </i>
      , authors W.R. Morton and Nathanel Whitten revealed the concept of "superoptimism" as a humorous antidote to the overblown self-help book category. In his comedy special
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Complaints_and_Grievances" title="Complaints and Grievances">
        Complaints and Grievances
       </a>
      </i>
      (2001),
      <a href="/wiki/George_Carlin" title="George Carlin">
       George Carlin
      </a>
      observes that there is "no such thing" as self-help: anyone looking for help from someone else does not technically get "self" help; and one who accomplishes something without help, did not need help to begin with.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Atwood" title="Margaret Atwood">
       Margaret Atwood
      </a>
      's semi-satiric dystopia
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oryx_and_Crake" title="Oryx and Crake">
        Oryx and Crake
       </a>
      </i>
      , university literary studies have declined to the point that the protagonist, Snowman, is instructed to write his thesis on self-help books as literature; more revealing of the authors and of the society that produced them than genuinely helpful.
     </p>
