     <p>
      <b>
       Glacier Peak
      </b>
      (known in the Sauk Indian dialect of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lushootseed" title="Lushootseed">
       Lushootseed
      </a>
      as "Tda-ko-buh-ba" or "Takobia"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bedal_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bedal-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) is the most isolated of the five major
      <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">
       stratovolcanoes
      </a>
      (composite volcanoes) of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Volcanoes" title="Cascade Volcanoes">
       Cascade Volcanic Arc
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       U.S
      </a>
      state of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">
       Washington
      </a>
      . Located in the
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier_Peak_Wilderness" title="Glacier Peak Wilderness">
       Glacier Peak Wilderness
      </a>
      , the volcano is visible from the west in
      <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">
       Seattle
      </a>
      , and from the north in the higher areas of eastern suburbs of Vancouver such as
      <a href="/wiki/Coquitlam" title="Coquitlam">
       Coquitlam
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Port_Coquitlam" title="Port Coquitlam">
       Port Coquitlam
      </a>
      . The volcano is the fourth tallest peak in Washington state, and not as much is known about it compared to other volcanoes in the area. Local
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       Native Americans
      </a>
      have recognized Glacier Peak and other Washington volcanoes in their histories and stories. When foreign explorers reached the region, they learned basic information about surrounding landforms, but did not initially understand that Glacier Peak was a volcano. Positioned in
      <a href="/wiki/Snohomish_County,_Washington" title="Snohomish County, Washington">
       Snohomish County
      </a>
      , the volcano is only 70 miles (110km) northeast of Seattle. The only volcano closer to Seattle is
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier" title="Mount Rainier">
       Mount Rainier
      </a>
      , but as Glacier Peak is set farther into the Cascades and almost 4,000 feet (1,200m) shorter, it is less noticeable than
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier" title="Mount Rainier">
       Mount Rainier
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Glacier Peak is one of the most active of Washington's volcanoes. The volcano formed during the
      <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">
       Pleistocene
      </a>
      epoch, about one million years ago, and since the most recent
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
       ice age
      </a>
      , it has produced some of largest and most explosive eruptions in the state. When continental ice sheets retreated from the region, Glacier Peak began to erupt regularly, erupting explosively five times in the past 3,000 years. It has erupted repeatedly during at least six periods; two of these eruptions have been among the largest in Washington.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">
       Geology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Remnants of past, prehistoric
      <a href="/wiki/Lava_dome" title="Lava dome">
       lava domes
      </a>
      are main components of the summit of the volcano, in addition to its false summit, Disappointment Peak. Past
      <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">
       pyroclastic flow
      </a>
      deposits are easily visible in river valleys near the volcano, likely caused by lava dome collapse, along with ridges found east of the summit consisting of ash cloud remains.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On its western flank, the volcano also has a
      <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">
       lahar
      </a>
      , or mudflow deposit, which runs for about 35 kilometres (22mi) into the White Chuck River Valley around 14,000 years ago. 10 other pyroclastic flow deposits are visible, all identified as relatively 10,000 years old. There is also a considerably newer mudflow, about 5,500 years old, which covers an area of 15 kilometres (9.3mi) between the same river valley, along with two small incidents both under 3,000 years old. Another lahar, of unidentified age, was rich in
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhornblende&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oxyhornblende (page does not exist)">
       oxyhornblende
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Dacite" title="Dacite">
       dacite
      </a>
      ; and continued for 30 kilometres (19mi) into the
      <a href="/wiki/Sauk_River_(Washington)" title="Sauk River (Washington)">
       Sauk River
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beget_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beget-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are also ash cloud deposits on the opposite eastern flank of the volcano. Studies of the mountain have to date been unable to find any correspondence with pyroclastic flows, but several past mudflows have been identified. In the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dusty_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dusty Creek (page does not exist)">
       Dusty Creek
      </a>
      , located by the mountain, there is a lahar at least 6 kilometres (3.7mi) thick, containing pyroclastic flow deposits and other mudflows. However, this large mudflow is part of a 300 metres (980ft) thick concentration of past incidents at the volcano that spans the Dusty and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chocolate_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chocolate Creek (page does not exist)">
       Chocolate Creek
      </a>
      . In the area at least ten cubic kilometers of lithic debris are contained.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beget_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beget-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">
       Tephra
      </a>
      deposits are for the most part constrained to the left flank of the volcano, and at least nine past incidents have been identified. These form several layers of tephra constructing the mountain. Smaller eruptions involving tephra occurred between 6,900-5,500 years ago, 3,450200 years ago, and as recent as 31690 years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beget_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beget-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On the mountain, three additional
      <a href="/wiki/Cinder_cone" title="Cinder cone">
       cinder cones
      </a>
      , Dishpan Gap, Indian Pass, and White Chuck, are located about 1,800m (5,900ft) up. The volcano has also caused such thermal events such as
      <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">
       hot springs
      </a>
      . There were three hot springs on the mountain: Gamma, Kennedy, and Sulphur,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but Kennedy Hot Springs was destroyed and buried in a slide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestservice_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-forestservice-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonic_setting">
       Tectonic setting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tectonic setting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="642" data-file-width="471" height="341" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg/250px-Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg/375px-Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg/500px-Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cascade_Range_related_plate_tectonics-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Diagram of plate tectonics for
        <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">
         Cascade Range
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The volcano is located in
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">
       Washington
      </a>
      , and is one of the five major stratovolcanoes there. Situated in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Volcanoes" title="Cascade Volcanoes">
       Cascade Volcanic Arc
      </a>
      , the volcano was created by
      <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
       subduction
      </a>
      of the oceanic
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Fuca_Plate" title="Juan de Fuca Plate">
       Juan de Fuca Plate
      </a>
      under the
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">
       North American Plate
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Convergence between the two continues at a rate of 4 centimetres (1.6in) per year. This range has been volcanically active for about 36 million years, and the rocks that make up its volcanoes are between 55 and 42million years old. Eruptions within the range are irregular and do not occur all at once. In an attempt to organize the volcanoes by age, scientists typically divide them into the High Cascades, younger volcanoes, and the Western Cascades, consisting of the older volcanoes. However, the vents in Washington are all of different ages so none of its volcanoes are included in either of the sections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Around the area, there were many Native Americans, and along with other Washington volcanoes, the mountain was recognized by them as a spirit. When European-American explorers reached the area, they learned about the mountain, though only partially, through local legends. Although the local people described Glacier Peak as a vital part of their storytelling and beliefs; when other volcanoes in the area were mapped, Glacier Peak was left out. In 1850 natives mentioned the volcano to
      <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)" title="Naturalism (philosophy)">
       naturalist
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gibbs_(geologist)" title="George Gibbs (geologist)">
       George Gibbs
      </a>
      saying that the volcano had once "smoked".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1898 the volcano was finally documented on a map.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Native Americans also used the area around the Cascades for their agriculture, leading them to often congregate in the region. As a result, gold miners eventually reached the area in the 1870s-1890s, searching for resources and rich land. The first white man recorded to observe the mountainDaniel Lindsleywas an employee of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Pacific_Railroad_Company" title="Northern Pacific Railroad Company">
       Northern Pacific Railroad Company
      </a>
      searching for possible railroad routes when he saw it in 1870.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eruptive_history">
       Eruptive history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eruptive history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Despite its elevation of 10,541 feet (3,213m), Glacier Peak is a small stratovolcano. Its relatively high summit is a consequence of its location atop a high ridge, but its volcanic portion extends only 1,6003,200 feet (5001,000m) above the underlying ridge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another Cascade Arc volcano with similar
      <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">
       geomorphology
      </a>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Meager" title="Mount Meager">
       Mount Meager
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">
       massif
      </a>
      in southwestern
      <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">
       British Columbia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      , which is situated on a 1,300ft (400m) ridge of nonvolcanic, crystalline and
      <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">
       metamorphic rock
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg/220px-Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg/330px-Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg/440px-Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Peak_7137b.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        View from Liberty Cap across the Suiattle River Valley
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Of the five major volcanoes in Washington, only Glacier Peak and
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">
       Mount St. Helens
      </a>
      have had large eruptions in the past 15,000 years. Since both volcanoes generate magma of
      <a href="/wiki/Dacite" title="Dacite">
       dacitic
      </a>
      origin, the viscous magma builds up since it cannot flow through the eruptive vent. Gradually, the pressure grows, culminating in an explosion that ejects materials such as
      <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">
       tephra
      </a>
      , which in its simplest form, is
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">
       ash
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Tephrochronology" title="Tephrochronology">
       Tephrochronology
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">
       radiocarbon dating
      </a>
      indicate that Glacier Peak eruptions occurred in 1700
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">
       AD
      </a>
       100 years, 1300 AD  300 years, 900 AD  50 years, 200 AD  50 years, 850 BC, 3150 BC, and in 3550 BC. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">
       Volcanic Explosivity Index
      </a>
      (VEI) for three of these was 2 to 4, small compared to the 5 of the
      <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">
       1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens
      </a>
      . They were characterized mainly by a central vent eruption, followed by an explosive eruption. These eruptions varied in outcome; some produced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lahars" title="Lahars">
       lahars
      </a>
      , some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flows" title="Pyroclastic flows">
       pyroclastic flows
      </a>
      , and others
      <a href="/wiki/Lava_dome" title="Lava dome">
       lava domes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A little more than 13,000 years ago, a sequence of nine tephra eruptions occurred within a period of less than a few hundred years. Associated with these eruptions were pyroclastic flows. Mixed with snow, ice and water, these formed lahars that raced into three nearby rivers, filling their valleys with deep deposits. Subsequently the mudflows drained into both the North Fork of the
      <a href="/wiki/Stillaguamish_River" title="Stillaguamish River">
       Stillaguamish River
      </a>
      (at that time an outlet of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sauk_River_(Washington)" title="Sauk River (Washington)">
       Sauk River
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Skagit_River" title="Skagit River">
       Skagit Rivers
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Washington" title="Arlington, Washington">
       Arlington
      </a>
      , 60 miles (97km) downstream, lahars deposited seven feet of sediment. Subsequent erosion of lahar deposits near
      <a href="/wiki/Darrington,_Washington" title="Darrington, Washington">
       Darrington
      </a>
      led to the current river system with the Stillaguamish River separated from the Sauk/Skagit Rivers. Lahar debris was deposited along both the Skagit and Stillaguamish Rivers all the way to
      <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">
       Puget Sound
      </a>
      . A small portion of the erupted tephra was deposited locally. However, most of the tephra reached higher levels of the atmosphere, and was transported by the wind hundreds of miles. Deposits from this congregation were as thick as 1 foot (0.30m) near
      <a href="/wiki/Chelan,_Washington" title="Chelan, Washington">
       Chelan
      </a>
      and 0.3 inches (7.6mm) near
      <a href="/wiki/Missoula,_Montana" title="Missoula, Montana">
       Missoula, Montana
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Peak.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="243" data-file-width="388" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Glacier_Peak.jpg/220px-Glacier_Peak.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Glacier_Peak.jpg/330px-Glacier_Peak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Glacier_Peak.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Peak.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Glacier Peak from the southeast
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Since these events, Glacier Peak has produced several lahars. The largest events were 5,900 and 1,800 years ago and were associated with dome-building eruptions. In both cases, the
      <i>
       lahars
      </i>
      traveled down the Skagit River to Puget Sound.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">
       Hazards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hazards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      When lahars reach populated areas, they can bury structures and people. An example was the
      <a href="/wiki/Armero_tragedy" title="Armero tragedy">
       Armero tragedy
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Nevado_del_Ruiz" title="Nevado del Ruiz">
       Nevado del Ruiz
      </a>
      where 23,000 died from an enormous mudflow. Lahars from Glacier Peak pose a similar threat to the small communities of Darrington and
      <a href="/wiki/Concrete,_Washington" title="Concrete, Washington">
       Concrete
      </a>
      and a lesser threat to the larger and rapidly growing towns of
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vernon,_Washington" title="Mount Vernon, Washington">
       Mount Vernon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Washington" title="Burlington, Washington">
       Burlington
      </a>
      , as well as other communities along the lower Skagit and Stillaguamish Rivers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastinWaitt_5-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-MastinWaitt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A 2005 study conducted by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
       United States Geological Survey
      </a>
      identified nine Cascade volcanoes, including Glacier Peak, as "very-high-threat volcanoes with inadequate monitoring". At the time of the study, only one seismometer was installed on Glacier Peak that had not "worked in two years".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciers">
       Glaciers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Glaciers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg/220px-N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg/330px-N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg/440px-N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N2_Glacier_Peak.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        View of the northeast face from
        <a href="/wiki/Image_Lake" title="Image Lake">
         Image Lake
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Eleven significant glaciers cover Glacier Peak. When C.E. Rusk first saw these glaciers in 1906 they were beginning to retreat, but were still very advanced. The average retreat of Glacier Peak glaciers from the
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">
       Little Ice Age
      </a>
      to the 1958 positions was 5,381 feet (1,640m). Richard Hubley noted that North Cascade glaciers began to advance in the early 1950s, after 30years of rapid retreat. The advance was in response to a sharp rise in winter precipitation and a decline in summer temperature beginning in 1944. Ten of the fifteen glaciers around Glacier Peak advanced, including all of the glaciers directly on the mountain's slopes. Advances of Glacier Peak glaciers ranged from 50 to 1,575 feet (15 to 480m) and culminated in 1978. All elevenGlacier Peak glaciers that advanced during the 195079 period emplaced identifiable maximum advance terminal moraines. From 1984 to 2005, the average retreat of eight Glacier Peak glaciers from their recent maximum positions was 1,017 feet (310m). Milk Lake Glacier, on the north flank of the mountain, melted away altogether in the 1990s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">
       Recreation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_Peak&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recreation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Crest_Trail" title="Pacific Crest Trail">
       Pacific Crest Trail
      </a>
      passes near Glacier Peak. The
      <a href="/wiki/Suiattle_River" title="Suiattle River">
       Suiattle River
      </a>
      crossing is a well known feature on the Pacific Crest Trail (PCT) as it passes through the area. The Suiattle PCT crossing used to have a bridge crossing until it was flooded out by storms in late 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first person to climb the mountain was Thomas Gerdine, along with a group of
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
       United States Geological Survey
      </a>
      scientists, in 1897. The volcano is also available to skiers. To reach the mountain itself, it takes 5 miles of walking along the White Chuck River Trail (Forest Service Trail No. 643) and up the Sitkum Glacier. The trail is reached via Forest Service Road No. 23. Mainly, the slopes consist of black diamonds for both the ascent and descent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
