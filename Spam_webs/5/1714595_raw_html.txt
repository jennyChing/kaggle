         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           <strong>
            <a href="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/ubuntu-classic-desktop.jpg">
             <img alt="ubuntu-classic-desktop" class="aligncenter wp-image-90373 size-full" height="378" src="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/ubuntu-classic-desktop.jpg" width="752"/>
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Migrating fromUbuntu 12.04 LTS to Ubuntu 14.04 LTS offers a number of benefits, such as improved hardware support and performance plusaccess to a whole rangeof updated applications.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           But there are a few drawbacks to be found as well. Those on low-end hardware used to running the less resource-intensive 2D version of Unity will find it no longer available (instead Unity relies on software rendering where sufficient graphics capabilities arent available).
          </p>
          <p>
           Furthermore, the old
           <a href="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2012/03/gnome-classic-in-ubuntu-12-04-its-like-nothing-ever-changed" target="_blank" title="GNOME Classic in Ubuntu 12.04: Its Like Nothing Ever Changed">
            GNOME 2 option
           </a>
           that those not wanting to use Unity could opt for isnt listed in the Software Centre.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ifyouve upgraded to Trusty but miss the classic desktop you were using previously you dont need to jump distros. Getting a simple, fuss-free desktop set up on Ubuntu 14.04 is straightforward. In this post Im going to show you how.
          </p>
          <h3>
           What is a Classic Desktop?
          </h3>
          <p>
           First things first. Beforewe look at how to set up a so-called Classic desktop lets define what we mean by one.
          </p>
          <p>
           I asked our Twitter followers to picture a classicenvironmentin their head and let us know what they saw it as being.Theresponsewas pretty consistent:
          </p>
          <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="550">
           <p>

           </p>

             April 28, 2014
            </a>
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>

          </p>
          <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="550">
           <p>

           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
           </script>
          </p>
          <p>
           Based on the responses we received we can say that the broadestdefinition  the sort of layout you likely picture in your head  is based off of the old GNOME 2 desktop. That means two panels, one at the bottom of the screen and one at the top; a system tray area; a simple applications menu, and an easy way to switch between running applications.
          </p>
          <p>
           Aclassic desktop should haveno excessively high resource demands but should allow for some customisation. It shouldnt look like it was designed fora mobile phone.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Oh, and it shouldnt show Amazon shopping results, either!
           </em>
          </p>
          <h4>
           Alternatives to Unity &amp; GNOME Shell Are Available
          </h4>
          <p>
           Xfce (used in Xubuntu), LXDE (default on Lubuntu) and, to a degree, KDE (basis of Kubuntu) all offer traditional desktop experiences moulded in this classic shape and may be worth investigating if youre yet to upgrade.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_90372" style="width: 350px">
           <a href="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/classic.jpg">
            <img alt="classic" class="wp-image-90372" height="207" src="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/classic-300x177.jpg" width="350"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Everything Remains Familiar
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Theyre far from being the only choices. The almost synchronised arrival of the next genGNOME Shell and Canonicals Unity desktoppulled the rug of familiarity out from under a lot of people.
          </p>
          <p>
           If theres one thing that peopledislike more than anything its change for changes sake. The early iterations of both GNOME Shell and Unity felt very much like this.
          </p>
          <p>
           Both projects have sinceblossomed into distinctive and differing desktops that offerintuitive and user friendly desktops  just like the respective design teams behind each insisted they would be.
          </p>
          <p>
           Not everyone has adapted or is willing to. For some, switchingdesktop paradigms is anything but intuitive or efficient; its a hassle. Old habits have to be retooled, new ways of doing things discovered.This desire to 
           <em>
            not fix what aint broke
           </em>
            saw new projects aimed at preserving the old ways. The GNOME 2 codebase was forked into MATE, while Linux Mint rolled their own way with Cinnamon.
           <strong>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Keen not to alienate everyone, the
           <a href="https://wiki.gnome.org/action/show/Projects/GnomeFlashback" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'https://wiki.gnome.org/action/show/Projects/GnomeFlashback', 'GNOME project also maintains a fallback session']);" target="_blank" title="Flashback information on GNOME Wiki">
            GNOME project also maintains a fallback session
           </a>
           that provides a similar user experience to the old classic desktopused in pre-Unity Ubuntubut based on GNOME 3. Its this halfway house thatIm going to cover installing.
          </p>
          <h3>
           Install GNOME Flashback in Ubuntu 14.04 LTS
          </h3>
          <p>
           To get the best of the modern desktop but with the simplicity offeredby thatold school two panel vibe the
           <strong>
            GNOME Flashback
           </strong>
           session available in Ubuntu 14.04 LTS is well worthgiving a go.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_90374" style="width: 350px">
           <a href="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/session-chooser-ubuntu-lightdm.jpg">
            <img alt="session-chooser-ubuntu-lightdm" class="wp-image-90374 size-thumbnail" height="200" src="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/session-chooser-ubuntu-lightdm-350x200.jpg" width="350"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Select the session at the Unity Greeter
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           <em>
            Flashback
           </em>
           isavailable to install from the Ubuntu Software Centre (unlike Cinnamon) and is styled like the Ubuntu desktop of old. It offers everything you need to get work done without putting strain on system resources or your muscle memory!
          </p>
          <p>
           It might not be as forward-looking or academically efficient as Unity, but if youre more interested in getting things done than having things get in the way, its by far an easier option than opting for an entirelydifferent OS.
          </p>
          <h4>
           Install
          </h4>
          <p>
           To install it click the button below or open the Ubuntu Software Centre and search for gnome-session-flashback.
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <a class="omg-button usc-link" href="apt://gnome-session-flashback" target="_blank" title="Install GNOME Classic Session in 14.04">
            Install GNOME Classic Session in 14.04
           </a>
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: left;">
           The installation process is fairly brief and pulls in everything it needs to.
          </p>
          <h4 style="text-align: left;">
           Login
          </h4>
          <p>
           Next, save any work and log out of the Unity desktop. At the login screen click the Ubuntu emblem next to your username and, from the list that appears, click GNOME Flashback (Metacity) from the session selector. Proceed to login as normal.
          </p>
          <p>
           The GNOME Flashback desktop will load and you should 
           <em>
            should
           </em>
            find everything where youexpect it to be.
          </p>
          <h4 style="text-align: left;">
           Extras
          </h4>
          <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_90662" style="width: 350px">
           <a href="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/Screen-Shot-2014-04-21-at-17.53.31.png">
            <img alt="Screen Shot 2014-04-21 at 17.53.31" class="wp-image-90662" height="271" src="http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/Screen-Shot-2014-04-21-at-17.53.31.png" width="350"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Remember this?
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Metacity compositing can be enabled to add drop shadows and smoother animations to windows; you can swap GTK themes and icon packs out using Unity Tweak Tool; and you can replace the bottom panelwith an application like Docky or AWN if you want.
          </p>
          <p>
           If you want to use the global app menu you can  just install the
           <em>
            indicator-applet-appmenu
           </em>
           package from the Software Centre. Under the Metacity session I found that adding this to the panel caused the main GNOME menu to disappear.
          </p>
          <p>
           You can also add, move and remove applets/panels by holding the Alt+Super keys and right-clicking on the area/item you want to adjust.
          </p>
          <p>
           Aside from accessing applications a different way, theres little else that changes drastically. You still have access to all of Ubuntus applications, the Software Centre and items like desktop notifications and indictor applets.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Whether youre looking to indulge your nostalgia for Ubuntu of yore or simply want a less demanding desktopfor your hardware, Im sure that the GNOME Flashback session will fit the bill  but let us know what you think in the comments below.
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
