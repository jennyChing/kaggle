      and the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Colorado" title="List of cities and towns in Colorado">
       most populous municipality
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Mesa_County,_Colorado" title="Mesa County, Colorado">
       Mesa County
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
       Colorado
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      . The city has a councilmanager form of government, and is most populous municipality in all of
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Western_Slope" title="Colorado Western Slope">
       western Colorado
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GR6-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Grand Junction is situated 247 miles (398km) west-southwest of the
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_Capitol" title="Colorado State Capitol">
       Colorado State Capitol
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">
       Denver
      </a>
      . As of the
      <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">
       2010 census
      </a>
      , the population of the city was 58,566.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Grand Junction is the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Colorado#Table" title="List of cities and towns in Colorado">
       15th most populous city
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
       State of Colorado
      </a>
      and the most populous city on the
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Western_Slope" title="Colorado Western Slope">
       Colorado Western Slope
      </a>
      . Grand Junction serves as a major commercial and transportation hub within the large area between the
      <a href="/wiki/Green_River_(Colorado_River)" title="Green River (Colorado River)">
       Green River
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Continental_Divide_of_the_Americas" title="Continental Divide of the Americas">
       Continental Divide
      </a>
      . It is the principal city of the
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Junction_Metropolitan_Statistical_Area" title="Grand Junction Metropolitan Statistical Area">
       Grand Junction Metropolitan Statistical Area
      </a>
      which had a population of 146,723 in
      <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">
       2010 census
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The city is located along the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorado_River_(U.S.)" title="Colorado River (U.S.)">
       Colorado River
      </a>
      , where it receives the
      <a href="/wiki/Gunnison_River" title="Gunnison River">
       Gunnison River
      </a>
      from the south. The name "Grand" refers to the historical
      <i>
       Grand River
      </i>
      , which was renamed the upper Colorado River in 1921, and the word "Junction" is from the joining of the Colorado and Gunnison rivers. Hence, Grand Junction has been given the nickname "River City". The city sits near the midpoint of a 30-mile (48km) arcing valley, known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Valley_(Colorado)" title="Grand Valley (Colorado)">
       Grand Valley
      </a>
      , a major
      <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">
       fruit
      </a>
      -growing region, historically home to the
      <a href="/wiki/Ute_people" title="Ute people">
       Ute
      </a>
      people and settled by white farmers in the 1880s. In recent years, several
      <a href="/wiki/Winery" title="Winery">
       wineries
      </a>
      have been established in the area as well. The
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado_National_Monument" title="Colorado National Monument">
       Colorado National Monument
      </a>
      , a unique series of canyons and mesas, overlooks the city on the west, while most of the area is surrounded by public lands managed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">
       Bureau of Land Management
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Book_Cliffs" title="Book Cliffs">
       Book Cliffs
      </a>
      are a prominent series of cliffs that define the northern side of the Grand Valley.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_70_(Colorado)" title="Interstate 70 (Colorado)">
       Interstate 70
      </a>
      connects the city eastward to
      <a href="/wiki/Glenwood_Springs,_Colorado" title="Glenwood Springs, Colorado">
       Glenwood Springs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">
       Denver
      </a>
      and westward to
      <a href="/wiki/Green_River,_Utah" title="Green River, Utah">
       Green River, Utah
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">
       Salt Lake City
      </a>
      (via
      <a href="/wiki/Interstate_70_in_Utah" title="Interstate 70 in Utah">
       Interstate 70
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_6_in_Utah" title="U.S. Route 6 in Utah">
       U.S Route 6
      </a>
      ); and
      <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">
       Las Vegas
      </a>
      (via
      <a href="/wiki/Interstate_70_in_Utah" title="Interstate 70 in Utah">
       Interstate 70
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Interstate_15_in_Utah" title="Interstate 15 in Utah">
       Interstate 15
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Country_Jam_Ranch" title="Country Jam Ranch">
       Country Jam Ranch
      </a>
      is located near Grand Junction just north of I-70 at the Mack exit. This is a permanent festival site built for music festivals, including Country Jam, an event that has been held since 1992 and one that draws thousands of country music fans to the area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Grand Junction area has turned into a major mountain biking destination, with many bikers coming from the Front Range of Colorado, the Salt Lake City area, and even as far away as California to enjoy the area's abundant single-track trails. Two prominent trails among others are the Tabeguache and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kokopelli_trail" title="Kokopelli trail">
       Kokopelli trails
      </a>
      , the latter running from near
      <a href="/wiki/Loma,_Colorado" title="Loma, Colorado">
       Loma
      </a>
      all the way to
      <a href="/wiki/Moab,_Utah" title="Moab, Utah">
       Moab, Utah
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Fruita,_Colorado" title="Fruita, Colorado">
       Fruita, Colorado
      </a>
      with its 18 Road trail system is within 10 miles of the city and has become a major mountain biking destination.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      To the northeast, the weathered Little Bookcliffs cut across the skyline. To the southeast soars the Grand Mesa, one of the world's largest flat-topped mountains. The photogenic canyons and monoliths of the Colorado National Monument form a western wall. In between the three natural barriers sprawls Western Colorado's Grand Valley. Cut out of the rugged terrain by the Colorado and Gunnison rivers, the valley was also one of the last locales in the lower 48 states to be settled by pioneer Americans.
     </p>
     <p>
      The region's colorful history stretches much further back in time. A little-known aboriginal civilization known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Fremont_culture" title="Fremont culture">
       Fremont
      </a>
      first moved into the area about 200 A.D. Living in pit-houses, eating insects, small animals and sparse produce from tiny gardens, the mysterious Fremont left Western Colorado about 1300 A.D. Roughly 100 years later, the first bands of wandering Utes moved into the region. The various Ute tribes eventually called much of Colorado and Utah home until forced onto reservations in 1881. Both Indian groups left behind numerous examples of colorful rock paintings and canyon carvings. Some of the unexplained rock art can still be spotted today.
     </p>
     <p>
      Until 1821, the Grand Valley was part of the kingdom of Spain. And during the early and mid 1700's, hardy Spanish and Mexican soldiers, explorers and priests poked and prodded through the region. Some were looking for gold, others seeking new trails to Spanish California. Most were not too successful.
     </p>
     <p>
      At first, trail-blazing American mountain men weren't very successful either. Hoping to trap valuable beaver or trade with Ute Indians, most of the Americans were kept out of the territory by Spanish officials. However, when Western Colorado became part of Mexico in 1821, the mountains were suddenly wide open to trappers, traders and wandering buck-skinners of the U.S.
     </p>
     <p>
      A few of the same mountain men to first see Colorado's Western Slope later helped guide U.S. Army expeditions and Government Surveying parties through the region. Some of the Old West's best known explorers - Kit Carson, John Charles Fremont and Capt. John Gunnison - all passed through the Grand Valley in the 1840s and 1850s.
     </p>
     <p>
      In spite of anti-Indian politicians, a large part of Western Colorado remained Ute Indian Territory until September 1881. The region was opened to homesteaders, ranchers and town builders the very day the Utes were being forced out by Army troopers. By the time Kansas politician and real estate developer George Crawford decided the unclaimed Grand Valley would make a good town site, Denver, Colorado already had a population of 50,000. And Grand Junction, Colorado was just being born!
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
       Geography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">
       United States Census Bureau
      </a>
      , the city has a total area of 38.6 square miles (100.0km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ), with 38.2 square miles (99.0km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) of it land, and 0.39 square miles (1.0km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ), or 0.87% of it
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">
       water
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
       Climate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG/220px-Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG/330px-Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG/440px-Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grand_Junction_Winter.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Grand Junction looking north, Winter January 2011
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The downtown area displays a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-arid" title="Semi-arid">
       semi-arid
      </a>
      climate (
      <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Kppen climate classification">
       Kppen
      </a>
      <i>
       BSk
      </i>
      ), almost grading into an
      <a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">
       arid type
      </a>
      . Grand Junction sits in a large area of "high desert" lands in Western Colorado. Winters are cold and dry, with a January mean temperature of 27.4F (2.6C). Due to its location
      <i>
       west
      </i>
      of the Rockies, Grand Junction does not receive as much influence from the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinook_wind" title="Chinook wind">
       Chinook winds
      </a>
      as locations in Colorado east of the
      <a href="/wiki/Front_Range" title="Front Range">
       Front Range
      </a>
      , yet it does receive protection from the Arctic masses that can settle to the east of the Rockies. This is illustrated by the fact that from December to February, highs reach 50F (10C) only 18 days.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lows drop to 0F (17.8C) or below on 2.9 nights per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Snowfall is low compared to much of the rest of the state, averaging 19.1 inches (49cm) per season; only once in the entire period of record dating to 1893, has observed 10 inches (25cm) in a calendar day, though the median is 6.3 inches (16.0cm), and moreover, snow cover is intermittent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOAA-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Snow is greatest in December and January. Spring warming is gradual but quickens when nearing June; the average last freeze date is April 24. Summer is hot but dry, with a July mean temperature of 78.2F (25.7C). Grand Junction averages 64.5 days a year with temperatures at 90F (32C) or above, and an average 6.5 days attaining 100F (38C) or more.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Autumn cooling is rapid, with the average first freeze date being October 15. The area receives little precipitation year-round, averaging 9.42 inches (239.3mm), with no real seasonal spike. Sunshine hours are abundant, even in winter, and total just over 3200 hours per year, or 73% of the possible total.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKO_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HKO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_history">
       Economic history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economic history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From the time settlers arrived in the 1880s until the 1960s, three of the main economic activities in the region were farming, fruit growing, and cattle raising. Fruit orchards, particularly between Grand Junction and Palisade to the east, remain important to the region's reputation and economy to the present day. Fruits most often grown are peaches, pears, apricots, plums, cherries, and, particularly since the 1980s, grapes for wine. In this semi-arid environment, these orchards thrive from a combination of abundant sunshine and irrigation from a system of canals that divert water from the Colorado River. Retail sales have been important to the economy for decades (e.g., gasoline, and hunting and fishing related sales), and uranium mining-related activities have also been significant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Grand Junction was home to the
      <a href="/wiki/Climax_Uranium_Mill" title="Climax Uranium Mill">
       Climax Uranium Mill
      </a>
      , a now decommissioned mill that provided uranium ore to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="US Atomic Energy Commission">
       US Atomic Energy Commission
      </a>
      . Education and healthcare have been important to the economy of the area, especially since the 1950s, with Colorado Mesa University and St. Mary's Hospital as leading employers in these fields. Vast
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">
       oil shale
      </a>
      reserves were known to exist near
      <a href="/wiki/Parachute,_Colorado" title="Parachute, Colorado">
       Parachute, Colorado
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Piceance_Basin" title="Piceance Basin">
       Piceance Basin
      </a>
      . The oil embargoes of the 1970s and high gas prices resulted in major financial interest in the region.
      <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">
       Exxon
      </a>
      purchased rights and used Grand Junction as its seat of operations.
     </p>
     <p>
      Grand Junction and the surrounding
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Valley_(Colorado)" title="Grand Valley (Colorado)">
       Grand Valley
      </a>
      were prosperous in the 1970s and early 1980s largely because of the impact of
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">
       oil shale
      </a>
      development. The United States, western Colorado in particular, has the largest known concentration of oil shale in the world (according to the Bureau of Land Management) and holds an estimated 800 gigabarrels of recoverable oil, enough to meet U.S. demand for oil at current levels for 110 years. Known as the "Rock That Burns" the shale can be mined and processed to produce oil, although in the past it was significantly more expensive than conventional oil. Sustained prices above $95 per barrel, however, may make extraction economically attractive in the coming years (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_Shale_Economics" title="Oil Shale Economics">
       Oil Shale Economics
      </a>
      ). ExxonMobil was forced to pull out of the region because of lower oil prices, which led to economic hardship in the region.
     </p>
     <p>
      The economic bust, known as "Black Sunday" (May 2, 1982) to the locals, started with a phone call from the President of Exxon to the then
      <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Colorado" title="Governor of Colorado">
       Governor of Colorado
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Douglas_Lamm" title="Richard Douglas Lamm">
       Richard Douglas Lamm
      </a>
      , stating that Exxon would cut its losses while retaining mining rights to the (then and currently) uneconomic oil. The economic bust was felt statewide, as Exxon had invested more than 5 billion USD in the state. Colorado historian
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Noel_(historian)" title="Thomas Noel (historian)">
       Tom Noel
      </a>
      observed "I think that was a definite turning point, and it was a reminder that we were a boom-and-bust state...There were parallels to the silver crash of 1893."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 2008, the economy of Grand Junction appeared to be more diverse and stable than it had been in previous decades. Major contributors to the economy were health care, tourism, agriculture, livestock, and energy mining (gas and oil). Major energy companies had once again invested large amounts of money due to increases in oil and natural gas prices (such as in the years 20052008). However, a major drop (in the summer of 2008) of market natural gas prices led to reduced gas well drilling and related capital expenditures in the area, significantly slowing the Grand Junction economy in 2009. Reports given in 2009 suggested that Grand Junction had once again been hard-hit economically, with one report by April 2010 listing the area as having had the largest percentage drop in employment of any "small city" in the entire United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 2008, Grand Junction was being discovered by the "nation's elite business and leisure travelers" for private jet travel, with nearby
      <a href="/wiki/Powderhorn_Resort" title="Powderhorn Resort">
       Powderhorn Resort
      </a>
      and other ski resorts a major attraction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="K-12">
       K-12
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: K-12">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Mesa Valley School District No. 51 (
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.mesa.k12.co.us/" rel="nofollow">
       website
      </a>
      ) provides comprehensive K-12 public education to the Grand Junction area. School District 51 operates five high schools:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Fruita_Monument_High_School" title="Fruita Monument High School">
        Fruita Monument High School
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Junction_High_School" title="Grand Junction High School">
        Grand Junction High School
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Central_High_School_(Grand_Junction,_Colorado)" title="Central High School (Grand Junction, Colorado)">
        Central High School
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Palisade_High_School" title="Palisade High School">
        Palisade High School
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       R-5 High School.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In addition, the district operates numerous middle, elementary, and other types of schools. District 51 partners with the Western Colorado Community College (WCCC) to operate a vocational school, owned and operated by Colorado Mesa University. The WCCC was formerly named, and is still commonly called, UTEC.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Colleges_and_universities">
       Colleges and universities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Colleges and universities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Mesa_University" title="Colorado Mesa University">
       Colorado Mesa University
      </a>
      , a public, four-year, liberal arts institution, serves as the primary provider of higher education on the Western Slope from its campus in central Grand Junction. This growing campus has an average enrollment of just under 10,000 students and offers a variety of degrees, including a Masters in Business Administration, Educational Leadership, and ESOL. The university has particularly strong science, art, music, nursing, and kinesiology programs.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">
       Rankings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Rankings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Grand Junction, Colorado has placed number six in
      <a href="/wiki/Outdoor_Life" title="Outdoor Life">
       Outdoor Life
      </a>
      's 2012 list of The 35 Best Hunting and Fishing Towns in the US, number twelve in
      <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">
       Forbes
      </a>
      2012 list of The Best Small Places For Business and Careers, number five in
      <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
       The New York Times
      </a>
      2011 list of Where to Live to Avoid a Natural Disaster, and number seven in
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.tourism-review.com" rel="nofollow">
       Tourism-Review.com
      </a>
      's 2009 list of the 8 Sunniest Cities in the USA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">
       Radio
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Radio">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Grand Junction
      <a href="/wiki/Media_market" title="Media market">
       radio market
      </a>
      includes all of
      <a href="/wiki/Mesa_County,_Colorado" title="Mesa County, Colorado">
       Mesa County, Colorado
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Six AM radio stations and more than 25 FM stations are
      <a href="/wiki/City_of_license" title="City of license">
       licensed
      </a>
      to and/or broadcast from the city.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Locator_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Locator-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">
       Newspapers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Newspapers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Grand Junction is serviced by two newspapers, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Junction_Daily_Sentinel" title="Grand Junction Daily Sentinel">
        Daily Sentinel
       </a>
      </i>
      (daily) and the
      <i>
       Grand Junction Free Press
      </i>
      . The Grand Junction area also receives newspaper influence from the
      <a href="/wiki/Front_Range" title="Front Range">
       Front Range
      </a>
      such as the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver_Post" title="Denver Post">
        Denver Post
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Television">
       Television
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Television">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Grand Junction has
      <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
       ABC
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">
       NBC
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">
       CBS
      </a>
      television station affiliates under the call signs of
      <a href="/wiki/KJCT-LP" title="KJCT-LP">
       KJCT-LP
      </a>
      (Channel 8),
      <a href="/wiki/KKCO" title="KKCO">
       KKCO
      </a>
      (Channel 11), and
      <a href="/wiki/KREX-TV" title="KREX-TV">
       KREX-TV
      </a>
      (Channel 5) respectively. Also, Grand Junction has a
      <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">
       Fox
      </a>
      (Channel 4) affiliate station under the call sign of
      <a href="/wiki/KFQX" title="KFQX">
       KFQX
      </a>
      that receives news from the Denver FOX affiliate,
      <a href="/wiki/KDVR" title="KDVR">
       KDVR
      </a>
      (Channel 31) at 9 pm.
      <a href="/wiki/KGBY_(TV)" title="KGBY (TV)">
       KGBY
      </a>
      (Channel 20) broadcasts
      <a href="/wiki/Cozi_TV" title="Cozi TV">
       Cozi TV
      </a>
      programming. KRMJ (Channel 18) is the local PBS affiliate, part of the statewide Rocky Mountain PBS network.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Venues">
       Venues
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Junction,_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Venues">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Grand Junction's downtown is home to the historic Avalon Theatre which hosts both movies and live entertainment from documentaries like
      <a href="/wiki/March_of_the_Penguins" title="March of the Penguins">
       March of the Penguins
      </a>
      to live performers such as
      <a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic" title='"Weird Al" Yankovic'>
       "Weird Al" Yankovic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wilco" title="Wilco">
       Wilco
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Colbie_Caillat" title="Colbie Caillat">
       Colbie Caillat
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Isaak" title="Chris Isaak">
       Chris Isaak
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Regan_(comedian)" title="Brian Regan (comedian)">
       Brian Regan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
