         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           A quarter of Britain's teachers, a third of police officers and one in 10 nurses have been dragged into the 40p rate in the past decade as it has become the "norm" for millions of people, official figures have revealed for first time.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           Over the past decade more than 1.6 million people have been dragged into the higher rate of tax, which effects people earning more than 41,865 a year, including hundreds of thousands of workers who would not traditionally be regarded as high earners.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           Figures from the Office for National Statistics reveal that more than 100,000 primary and secondary school teachers, 70,000 police officers and up to 72,000 nurses have been dragged into the higher rate of tax.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           The figures, which have more than doubled over the past decade, will increase pressure on David Cameron to pledge to raise the threshold in the Conservative manifesto next year.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           Last month the Prime Minister said he would love to raise the 40p threshold and admitted that it is increasingly paid by people who dont consider themselves to be wealthy.
          </p>

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           Nick de Bois, a Conservative MP, said: I have always maintained that the Conservative Party needs to recognise that the higher rate of tax kicks in at a level where people are far from well off.
          </p>
          <p>
           Teachers, police officers and nurses are now paying tax at a rate that was once reserved for the very wealthy and I hope we can address this in the next budget and make it clear a Conservative government will ease this burden."
          </p>
          <p>
           According to the Office for Budget Responsibility, one in three workers will be higher rate taxpayers within two decades. More than 10 million people will have been dragged over the higher rate threshold by 2033, twice as many as at present.
          </p>
          <p>
           In April, George Osborne used his Budget to increase the tax-free personal allowance from 10,000 to 10,500, a change that the Treasury says benefits all workers earning up to 100,000.
          </p>
          <p>
           But he ignored calls from many Conservative MPs to raise the threshold for the higher rates so that it keeps up with rising incomes.
          </p>
          <p>
           This so-called fiscal drag has been lucrative for the Treasury but Conservative MPs believe it has hurt families already suffering from rising food and energy prices. The OBR forecast is likely to be an underestimate as the Chancellor has restricted rises in the 40p threshold to below the level of inflation.
          </p>
          <p>
           From this year it will go up by a flat 1 per cent until 2016, well below the current Consumer Prices Index inflation measure.
          </p>
          <p>
           According to the Office for National Statistics, the number of teaching professionals paying the higher rate has trebled, rising from 100,000 to more than 300,000 in the past decade. They now include 87,000 secondary school teachers and 34,000 primary school teachers.
          </p>
          <p>
           In 2003, the figures suggest that there were no nurses who were paying the higher rate. By last year, the figure had risen to 72,000, while there are also more than 5,000 paramedics on the higher rate.
          </p>
          <p>
           Electricians have also been dragged into the higher rate for the first time, with more than 10,000 paying to the top rate of tax.
          </p>
          <p>
           Mr de Bois said: The point you start to pay higher rate tax your take-home pay is only 4,000 more than the household benefit cap, which illustrates how outdated the threshold is.
          </p>
          <p>
           Last month Mr Cameron told a question and answer event: I know that a lot of people believe that the 40p rate now kicks in quite early and quite of people who dont see themselves as fundamentally very wealthy are paying that 40p tax rate.
          </p>
          <p>
           Now I would love to be able to stand here and say we are going to sort all this out, we will raise the thresholds of all these tax rates, I cant make that promise today.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, he added that the government's policy of increasing the personal allowance means hundreds of thousands of people no longer have to pay income tax.
          </p>
          <p>
           He said: I think that should continue to be the priority  it is to try to make sure that if you work hard, do the right thing, we are supporting and helping you.
          </p>
          <p>
           And I think that is the start  tax cuts for the lowest paid. That is what we have focused on.
          </p>
          <p>
           I would love to make more promises, I understand the problem with the 40p rate kicking in when people are not earning a lot of money, and I have to look very carefully at the books before I can make any promises about it.
          </p>

                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
