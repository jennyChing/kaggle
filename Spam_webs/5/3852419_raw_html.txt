   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Baltic Sea anomaly
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Baltic Sea anomaly
      </b>
      is a 60-metre (200ft) diameter circular rock-like formation on the floor of the
      <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">
       Baltic Sea
      </a>
      , discovered by Peter Lindberg, Dennis sberg and their Swedish "Ocean X" diving team in June 2011. The team reported that the formation rests on a pillar and includes a structure similar in appearance to a staircase, leading to a dark hole.
     </p>
     <p>
      Commentators have suggested that the structure could be a World War II anti-submarine device, a battleship gun turret, sediment dropped by a fishing trawler, or a
      <a href="/wiki/Flying_saucer" title="Flying saucer">
       flying saucer
      </a>
      . Geologists have stated that it is most likely a natural
      <a href="/wiki/Geological_formation" title="Geological formation">
       geological formation
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
       Discovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Sea_anomaly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The discovery was made on June 19, 2011 by the Swedish based "Ocean X Team" during a dive in the
      <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">
       Baltic Sea
      </a>
      between Sweden and Finland while searching for an old shipwreck. The group describes themselves as treasure hunters and salvage operators who specialize in underwater searches for sunken "antique high-end alcoholic beverages and historic artefacts".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanXteam_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OceanXteam-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In March 2012, Ocean X formalized a partnership with entertainment producers Titan TV to produce a TV documentary series and documentary film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanXpress_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OceanXpress-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Sea_anomaly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to Ocean X, the formation has an appearance of "rough granite", is round, 3 to 4 metres (9.8 to 13.1ft) thick and approximately 60 metres (200ft) in diameter, stands on an 8-meter (26ft) tall pillar-like feature, and is located at a depth of 85 to 90 metres (279 to 295ft). There is also another smaller object not far away.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vid120716_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vid120716-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-express120615_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-express120615-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oex20120615_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oex20120615-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The object is at the end of what resembles a 300-metre (980ft) "runway".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Ocean X team has published one additional close-up sonar scan on their Web site and nine additional close-up sonar scans on their YouTube page that appear to show a 90-degree angle and other features of the object.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On their second expedition, they reported that they found something that looks like a staircase and a round black hole that goes directly into the structure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-express120812_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-express120812-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_composition">
       Chemical composition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Sea_anomaly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical composition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A sample recovered by divers was reportedly found to contain
      <a href="/wiki/Limonite" title="Limonite">
       limonite
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Goethite" title="Goethite">
       goethite
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
       Israeli
      </a>
      geologist Steve Weiner claimed that these materials are "metals which nature could not reproduce itself",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      however,
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Swedish
      </a>
      geologists Fredrik Klingberg and Martin Jakobsson say that the chemical composition of the sample resembles that of
      <a href="/wiki/Nodule_(geology)" title="Nodule (geology)">
       nodules
      </a>
      that are not uncommon in sea beds, and that the materials found, including limonite and goethite, can indeed be formed by nature itself.
     </p>
     <p>
      Stone samples have also been analyzed by Volker Brchert, an associate professor of geology at Stockholm University. According to Brchert, most of the samples that were brought up from the sea bottom are granites, gneisses and sandstones. Among the samples was also a single loose piece of
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       basaltic
      </a>
      (volcanic) rock, which is out of place on the seafloor, but not unusual. "Because the whole northern Baltic region is so heavily influenced by glacial thawing processes, both the feature and the rock samples are likely to have formed in connection with glacial and postglacial processes. [...] Possibly these rocks were transported there by glaciers", explained Brchert.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YahooNews_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-YahooNews-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">
       Reaction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Sea_anomaly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While a former
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Swedish
      </a>
      naval officer speculated that the sonar image could show the remains of a
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      German anti-submarine device,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DailyMail_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DailyMail-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery.com" title="Discovery.com">
       Discovery.com
      </a>
      contributor
      <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Radford" title="Benjamin Radford">
       Benjamin Radford
      </a>
      considered that it could be a damaged gun turret from an old battleship,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Discovery-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      scientist Charles Paull of the
      <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_Research_Institute" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute">
       Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute
      </a>
      told
      <a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">
       Popular Mechanics
      </a>
      it was more likely simply a rock outcrop, sediment dropped from a fishing trawler, or even a school of fish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMech_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PopMech-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gran Ekberg, marine archaeologist at Sjhistoriska museet (Maritime museum) in Stockholm was quoted as saying, "A natural, geological formation can't be ruled out. I agree the finding looks weird since it's completely circular. But nature has produced stranger things than that."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-express120812_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-express120812-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Martin Jakobsson, professor of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Geology" title="Marine Geology">
       Marine Geology
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">
       Geophysics
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_University" title="Stockholm University">
       Stockholm University
      </a>
      also examined images from the dive and said, "I'm guessing it's some sort of sandstone. But to make things clear, I've only seen the media images, and I need more material before making an official statement."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Expressene.se_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Expressene.se-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Danish archaeologist Jrgen Dencker said the formation was quite possibly a grouping of rocks deposited by
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
       Ice age
      </a>
      glaciers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vid120716_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-vid120716-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other experts say that the formation may be a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pillow_basalt" title="Pillow basalt">
       pillow basalt
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">
       moraine
      </a>
      , or the product of a
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">
       hydrothermal vent
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although Ocean X spokesman Lindbergh maintains that the formation contains unnaturally straight lines and other constructions, according to Zmescience.com writer Tibi Puiu, "If anything...this discovery perfectly illustrates mans power of fitting patterns together, something that has allowed for one of the worlds greatest scientific discoveries to be made, but which also plunged man in making demented claims."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zmescience.com_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zmescience.com-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Sea_anomaly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The single sonar image provided by Ocean X has drawn criticism from various sources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hanumant Singh of the
      <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole_Oceanographic_Institution" title="Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution">
       Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution
      </a>
      has said that it cannot be trusted. Stating that a cheap inaccurate instrument was used for the image,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc2_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-msnbc2-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT.COM_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RT.COM-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      he added that it had been improperly wired and calibrated.
      <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">
       MSNBC
      </a>
      said media comparisons to a
      <a href="/wiki/Flying_saucer" title="Flying saucer">
       flying saucer
      </a>
      were likely suggested by graphic outlines drawn onto the sonar image, said to resemble the fictional spaceship
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Falcon" title="Millennium Falcon">
        Millennium Falcon
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Paull of the Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute characterized the discovery as curious and fun, but much ado about nothing."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMech_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-PopMech-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Jonathan Hill of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mars_Space_Flight_Facility" title="Mars Space Flight Facility">
       Mars Space Flight Facility
      </a>
      questioned the motives involved in Ocean X announcements, which included plans to take wealthy tourists in a submarine to visit the site. He was quoted as saying "Whenever people make extraordinary claims, it's always a good idea to consider for a moment whether they are personally benefiting from the claim or if it's a truly objective observation." He also suggested that it would have been simple to break off a piece and have it geologically tested, and said that test results showing it was simply rock would not have benefited Peter Lindberg.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC_17-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ocean X founder Peter Lindberg responded to charges of poor science and attempting to benefit from the publicity, saying "First we thought this was only stone, but this is something else. And since no volcanic activity has ever been reported in the Baltic Sea the find becomes even stranger."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT.COM_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-RT.COM-20">
        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>
