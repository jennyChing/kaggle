        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignright" height="288" src="http://i38.photobucket.com/albums/e120/sdonato1/Drain1.jpg" title="Drain cleaning supplies" width="384"/>
         When a drain stops draining like its supposed to, the un-natural among us haul out the Drano. But there IS a better, cheaper and more natural way to get the lead (or lead-like sludge) out of a drain.
        </p>
        <p>
         Products like Drano are bad for the environment on several counts. For one,
         <a href="http://householdproducts.nlm.nih.gov/cgi-bin/household/brands?tbl=brands&amp;id=19001016" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/householdproducts.nlm.nih.gov/cgi-bin/household/brands?tbl=brands&amp;id=19001016');" target="_blank" title="Drano ingredients">
          Dranos ingredients pose serious health hazards
         </a>
         if humans or other living things come in contact with them. Its ingredients
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dr%C4%81no" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dr%C4%81no');" target="_blank" title="How Drano works">
          work by heating up lye in a chemical reaction and swirling shards of aluminum in the drain to cut through hair and other clog agents
         </a>
         . If the compound doesnt wash all the way out of pipes, it can be harmful to plumbers working on pipes later, or to people who are splashed by water coming up out of a Drano-containing drain.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-1137">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          There is a better way
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Fortunately, a quick and easy way exists to clean out drains  and its completely gentle and nontoxic to humans.
        </p>
        <p>
         It does require a little elbow grease, a little grossness (but honestly  worse than Drano?), and it works best BEFORE the drain is completely clogged. Do this procedure once every week or two, or whenever you notice a drain getting slow, and youll be good to go.
        </p>
        <p>
         1. Remove the drain cover, if you can. For most sinks, you can just pull it out.
        </p>
        <p>
         2. Remove all the sludge, slime, hair, etc. that you can. This is the nasty part! Use a rag or even a paper towel (its OK, you can compost it).
        </p>
        <p>
         3. Look into the drain. Do you see clogs of hair in there? You can purchase a flexible plastic drain-cleaning tool (like an extra-long plastic stick). Or use something homemade, like an actual stick (be careful! dont let it break off in the drain!) or a coat hanger. If you use a hanger, put a tight crimp in the end to catch the nasty, nasty goo, and also so that the wire doesnt punch through PVC or flexible pipe  or even worn-out metal pipes.
        </p>
        <p>
         4. Sprinkle a good 1/2 cup of baking soda into the drain.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignnone" height="288" src="http://i38.photobucket.com/albums/e120/sdonato1/Drain2.jpg" title="baking soda" width="384"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         5. Remember your fourth-grade science-project volcano? Time to relive your childhood! Follow up with about 1 cup of white vinegar. Pour it right in there. Let it sizzle, sizzle, sizzle away.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignnone" height="288" src="http://i38.photobucket.com/albums/e120/sdonato1/Drain3.jpg" title="vinegar in drain" width="384"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         6. Meanwhile, start heating up 1/2 to 1 gallon of water to a boil. (I use my electric kettle to save some energy.)
        </p>
        <p>
         7. Check on the volcano. If the drain is really gummed up, the liquid might be just sitting there. Pull out your trusty stick again and gently swizzle it around, up and down in the drain to get the liquid circulating.
        </p>
        <p>
         8. Pour the boiling water down the drain. Use the stick, if necessary, to keep things moving. Odds are good that youll be left with some nasty bits scattered around the sink  old toothpaste and more, being devoured by gummy bacteria or other scientific creatures.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignnone" height="288" src="http://i38.photobucket.com/albums/e120/sdonato1/Drain4.jpg" title="ooh, nasty!" width="384"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         9. If the drain was really foul, or is still a bit slow, repeat the above steps.
        </p>
        <p>
         10. Pour an extra half-gallon or so of boiling water down. By now the drain should be running smoothly!
        </p>
        <p>
         Clean the sink (but of course!) and you are on your way  ready for your sink to swirl away your water for another few weeks or months, all naturally.
        </p>
