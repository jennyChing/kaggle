       <div class="chem-con">
        <h3 class="rank-title">
         Chemicals most responsible for the toxicity outside this school
        </h3>
        <ul class="rank-list">
         <li>
          <a rel="htmltooltip">
           Manganese and manganese compounds
          </a>
          <span class="toxicity">
           <strong>
            80%
           </strong>
           of overall toxicity
          </span>
          <div class="htmltooltip">
           <p>
            <b>
             Definition:
            </b>
            Manganese is used to produce pesticides, batteries and other industrial products.  Although trace elements are found in food and are considered essential for good health, overexposure for long periods can cause mental and emotional disturbances and slow and clumsy body movements -- a disease of the brain called manganism. Children are more vulnerable to such exposures than adults. EPA says that it cannot be determined if there is a link between manganese and cancer.
           </p>
          </div>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a rel="htmltooltip">
           Cobalt and cobalt compounds
          </a>
          <span class="toxicity">
           <strong>
            6%
           </strong>
           of overall toxicity
          </span>
          <div class="htmltooltip">
           <p>
            <b>
             Definition:
            </b>
            Cobalt, a naturally occurring element, is used in the manufacture of aircraft engines, magnets, grinding and cutting tools, artificial hip and knee joints. Radioactive cobalt is used to treat cancer patients and to irradiate food. Normal exposures to nonradioactive cobalt are not harmful, but large amounts of radiation from radioactive cobalt can damage cells and cause nausea, vomiting, coma and even death. The International Agency for Research on Cancer has determined that cobalt and cobalt compounds are possibly carcinogenic to humans.
           </p>
          </div>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a rel="htmltooltip">
           Nickel and nickel compounds
          </a>
          <span class="toxicity">
           <strong>
            5%
           </strong>
           of overall toxicity
          </span>
          <div class="htmltooltip">
           <p>
            <b>
             Definition:
            </b>
            Nickel, a metal, can be combined with other metals to make coins, jewelry, and stainless steel. People working in nickel refineries or nickel-processing plants have experienced chronic bronchitis, reduced lung function, and lung and sinus cancers. EPA classifies nickel refinery dust and nickel subsulfide as human carcinogens.
Source: EPA, HHS
           </p>
          </div>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a rel="htmltooltip">
           Copper and copper compounds
          </a>
          <span class="toxicity">
           <strong>
            2%
           </strong>
           of overall toxicity
          </span>
          <div class="htmltooltip">
           <p>
            <b>
             Definition:
            </b>
            Copper, a metal, is essential to plants and animals, including humans. It is used to make wire,plumbing pipes, sheet metal and other products. Exposure to high levels of copper can be harmful, irritating the nose and throat and, in some cases, damaging the liver and kidneys. EPA says copper is not classifiable as to human carcinogenicity.
           </p>
          </div>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a rel="htmltooltip">
           Lead and lead compounds
          </a>
          <span class="toxicity">
           <strong>
            1%
           </strong>
           of overall toxicity
          </span>
          <div class="htmltooltip">
           <p>
            <b>
             Definition:
            </b>
            Lead in the air comes from mining, manufacturing and burning fossil fuels.  Lead has been banned from gasoline for more than a decade and has been removed from most paints. Children are more vulnerable to lead poisoning than adults; swallowing even small amounts of lead can cause anemia, muscle weakness and brain damage. Adults exposed to high lead levels can suffer high blood pressure and damage to the nervous system. EPA and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services consider lead and its compounds a probable or possible carcinogen. Source: EPA, HHS
           </p>
          </div>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </div>
