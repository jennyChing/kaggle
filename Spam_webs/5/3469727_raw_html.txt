             <section class="article-content">
              <p>
               Herpetologist, naturalist and author Paul Rosolie is just 30, but his experiences while living and working in the forests of Peru's remote Madre de Dios region could fill many volumes. Rosolie suffered a jaguar panting down his neck and survived a potentially deadly infection while alone for days in the wilderness. He raised a baby anteater, foiled gun-toting poachers trafficking endangered species and wrestled enormous reptiles as he gathered data on them.
              </p>
              <p>
               It's hard, however, to top his latest project: being eaten alive by a giant green anaconda, which is the largest and most powerful snake in the world. The snake was completely unharmed, and although Rosolie wore a protective suit, he wound up battered and bruised. Now healed, Rosolie shared thoughts about the encounter and some of his other adventures in the western Amazon.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                This wasn't your first close encounter with an anaconda. What happened during those earlier encounters?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Rosolie:
               </strong>
               In order to study snakes that are anywhere from 15 to over 25 feet long, you have to catch them from behind the head to measure and weigh them. During one expedition, with only myself and another individual present, we encountered an enormous anaconda that weighed about 350 pounds.
              </p>
              <p>
               I have a six-foot arm span, so I jumped on the snake when I couldn't wrap my arms around it. It kept slithering and moving rapidly, so I wound up riding on the snake while it wanted to dive under water, with me nearly going down with the anaconda. It was an overwhelming experience.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                SEE ALSO:
                <a href="http://www.discovery.com/tv-shows/eaten-alive" target="_blank">
                 Watch 'Eaten Alive' Sunday at 9 PM ET/8C
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               This happened in a newly discovered ecosystem called a floating forest. The discovery helped me to realize the importance of such huge apex predators. When their population decreases, everything else can suffer too.
              </p>
              <p>
               Conservation of this region is essential. Consider that one-fifth of the planet's oxygen is produced by the Amazon rainforest. Nearly half of the world's species will be destroyed or severely threatened over the next quarter century due to rainforest deforestation. We need to act now to save the Amazon rainforest. My colleagues and I have focused on the Madre de Dios region, where the Amazon River begins.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                What gave you the idea to create and work on the "Eaten Alive" project? What do you say to critics who question why you, as a naturalist, would do something so outrageous?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               The huge loss of biodiversity in the region cannot continue. We need to call attention to an area that a lot of people don't even know or think about.
              </p>
              <p>
               Snakes regurgitate all of the time. It's a common behavioral response. There are also accounts of anacondas eating humans. The father of the cook who tours with us in the Amazon was eaten by an anaconda, so it's not a myth. Anacondas eat prey that is much bigger and heavier than a human.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                SEE ALSO:
                <a href="http://news.discovery.com/animals/2013-year-of-the-snake-130208.htm" target="_blank">
                 Top 12 Most Amazing Snakes
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               When you're in a small village and your mother gets eaten by an anaconda, no cameras are running. You don't call the press. In our project, we explored the possibility. We kept it within the parameters of a normal day in the wild. A snake did lose its lunch: me.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Were either you or the anaconda injured in any way? Was the snake examined by a vet?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               I was pretty beaten up and suffered from battering and bruising. The snake is alive and well and remains healthy. She was examined by a vet throughout the project, including directly after my very close encounter with her. I would not have done this if there were any real chance of hurting or stressing out the snake.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Describe the special carbon fiber protective suit that you wore and how it was developed.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               The suit is incredible. It was 3-D printed for a custom fit to my body. I was completely sealed off, but swallowed a pill beforehand with a machine that broadcast my vitals to scientists who kept me alive.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                SEE ALSO:
                <a href="http://news.discovery.com/animals/endangered-species/snakes-swim-in-midair-to-fly-140129.htm" target="_blank">
                 Snakes Take on UFO Shape to Fly
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               The carbon fiber was smooth, so it was safer for the snake to swallow. The chest portion of the suit was designed to prevent my ribs from being crushed. Prior studies determined that the anacondas possible crush force is 90 pounds per square inch. Thats comparable to an elephant sitting on your chest.
              </p>
              <p>
               We used tug-boat ropes to test the suit beforehand between trucks. It withstood squeezing at 90 psi. That is equivalent to the previously determined anaconda constriction force.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                You seem fearless, but how did you feel during your extremely close encounter with the anaconda?
                <br/>
               </strong>
               <br/>
               I love this species, so it was exhilarating to have this intimate experience. I was amazed by her sheer power, but I was also scared at the same time. At first, she did not want to eat me. But when I showed myself as a predator, she defended herself.
              </p>
              <p>
               She got me right in the face. The last thing I saw was her mouth wide open before everything went black. As this happened, she wrapped around me and took me off of my feet. I felt the suit cracking. It felt as if my arms were ripping out of their sockets.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                SEE ALSO:
                <a href="http://news.discovery.com/animals/sea-snake-dries-to-a-crisp-before-rehydrating-140318.htm" target="_blank">
                 Sea Snake Dries to a Crisp Before Rehydrating
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               It was terrifying, particularly because I am somewhat claustrophobic. I was wrapped up in there for an hour and needed to keep my fears in check. Once an anaconda has you, it does not let go.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                You have a distinguished career as a naturalist. For example, your memoir  Mother of God: An Extraordinary Journey into the Uncharted Tributaries of the Western Amazon earned praise from renowned primatologist Jane Goodall and other notables.
                <br/>
                <br/>
                For several years, you have helped to run the ecotourism company Tamandua Expeditions, which promotes conservation. So Im still wondering why you would pull such a stunt, and how the project promotes conservation.
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               We are leading the first-ever extensive study of anacondas. We have a successful fundraising campaign and are educating the public about these apex predators.
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                SEE ALSO:
                <a href="http://news.discovery.com/animals/10-surprising-facts-about-animal-intelligence-140402.htm" target="_blank">
                 10 Surprising Facts About Animal Intelligence
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               To critics I say: What have you done to protect the rainforest and its endangered species? Respect that desperate times call for desperate measures.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Can you share any information about your next projects?
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               We are working on tiger migrations in India. If our generation does not take appropriate steps to protect tigers in the wild, we could lose them.
              </p>
              <p>
               We are also on a search for the biggest anaconda. During an earlier expedition in the Amazon, we encountered a 26-foot-long anaconda that weighed hundreds of pounds. The snake for the Eaten Alive project was big, about 18 feet long, but we know that there are even larger anacondas out there.
              </p>
