       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Switching inhibition on  SLC12A5/KCC2 variants in human epilepsy
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         Posted on
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2014/06/16/switching-inhibition-on-slc12a5kcc2-variants-in-human-epilepsy/" rel="bookmark" title="2:40 pm">
          <time class="entry-date" datetime="2014-06-16T14:40:56+00:00" pubdate="">
           June 16, 2014
          </time>
         </a>
         <span class="byline">
          by
          <span class="author vcard">
           <a class="url fn n" href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/author/ingo-helbig/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Ingo Helbig">
            Ingo Helbig
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <strong>
          Inhibition.
         </strong>
         We usually like to think of
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2013/08/28/the-endozepine-mystery/" title="The endozepine mystery">
          GABA
         </a>
         as an inhibitory neurotransmitter, which counteracts the excitatory and potentially epileptogenic effects of
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2013/11/19/shank3-epilepsy-and-the-excitatoryinhibitory-imbalance/" title="SHANK3, epilepsy, and the excitatory/inhibitory imbalance">
          glutamate
         </a>
         . However, this is not always true during brain development. Initially, GABA is a powerful excitatory neurotransmitter. The excitatory effect of GABA has been shown to be important for brain development and the formation of dendritic spines  and the switch from excitation to inhibition is due to a single ion channel: KCC2, encoded by
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         . Two recent publications in
         <a href="http://embor.embopress.org/content/early/2014/06/10/embr.201439039">
          EMBO Reports
         </a>
         now implicate genetic variation in
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         in human epilepsy.
         <span id="more-3135">
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_3136" style="width: 664px">
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/KCC2.jpg">
          <img alt="KCC2 encoded by SLC12A5. (A) Rare variant found in a family with febrile seizures in the study by Puskarjov and collaborators. (B) Amino acid sequency showing a high degree of conservation. (C) Membrane topology of KCC2 and the location of the R952H variant. " class="wp-image-3136 size-large" height="487" src="http://epilepsygenetics.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/KCC2-1024x764.jpg" width="654"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          <strong>
           KCC2 encoded by SLC12A5.
          </strong>
          (A) Rare variant found in a family with febrile seizures in the study by Puskarjov and collaborators. (B) Amino acid sequency showing a high degree of conservation. (C) Membrane topology of KCC2 and the location of the R952H variant. (Reproduced a Creative Commons Attribution from http://embor.embopress.org/content/15/6/723)
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The GABA switch.
         </strong>
         Let me try to give you a brief outline of how KCC2 works. The question of whether GABA is excitatory or inhibitory depends on the intraneuronal concentration of chloride. GABA receptors are chloride channels and when the intracellular concentration of chloride is high, chloride leaves the cell after channel opening, triggering
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2013/09/05/thalamus-timing-and-tsc1-deletions/" title="Thalamus, timing and TSC1 deletions">
          action potentials
         </a>
         . When the concentration of GABA inside of the neuron is low, chloride enters the cell, hyperpolarizing the neuronal membrane. Early during development, intracellular chloride is high, and GABA acts as an excitatory neurotransmitter. Then KCC2 enters the scene and drastically reduces the intraneuronal chloride concentration. This leaves GABA as the inhibitory neurotransmitter that we know. It is, however, fascinating that this is due to a single protein. Its easy to imagine that disruption of KCC2 might lead to downstream problems with GABA action, leading to
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2014/01/14/cacna2d2-the-ducky-mouse-and-what-it-takes-to-be-an-epilepsy-gene/" title="CACNA2D2, the ducky mouse, and what it takes to be an epilepsy gene">
          hyperexcitability
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The genetic evidence.
         </strong>
         Both
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24668262">
          Puskarjov and collaborators
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.15252/embr.201438840/full">
          Kahle and collaborators
         </a>
         hypothesized that genetic variation in
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         may lead to seizures and sequenced this gene.
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24668262">
          Puskarjov and collaborators
         </a>
         identified a family with febrile seizures with a R952H variant, a variant previously annotated in dbSNP (
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/projects/SNP/snp_ref.cgi?rs=142740233">
          rs142740233
         </a>
         ). This variant is very rare and was only found in 7/~6500 individuals in the Exome Variant Server (
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2013/04/15/how-a-pathogenic-de-novo-mutation-in-scn1a-ended-up-in-the-exome-variant-server/" title="How a pathogenic de novo mutation in SCN1A ended up in the Exome Variant Server">
          EVS
         </a>
         ).
         <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.15252/embr.201438840/full">
          Kahle and collaborators
         </a>
         sequenced
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         in 380 probands with Idiopathic/Genetic Generalized Epilepsy and found the same variant in 5/380 probands and 7/~1600 controls. While not statistically significant on a genome-wide basis, this suggests an intriguing enrichment in patients. In addition,
         <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.15252/embr.201438840/full">
          Kahle and collaborators
         </a>
         found a second variant in the C-terminus of KCC2 (R1049K) in three patients and a single control individual.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The function.
         </strong>
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24668262">
          Puskarjov and collaborators
         </a>
         investigated the effect of the R952H variant. In mouse neuronal stem cells, this variant led to a reduced surface expression, and when introduced into young rodent pyramidal neurons, the R952H variant led to significantly less chloride extrusion than the wildtype. In addition, the effect of wild type KCC2 in inducing
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2014/04/20/the-return-of-the-h-current-hcn1-mutations-in-atypical-dravet-syndrome/" title="The return of the h-current: HCN1 mutations in atypical Dravet Syndrome">
          dendric spines
         </a>
         was impaired, demonstrating how the GABA effect is tightly coupled with maturation of the CNS.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Stats versus function.
         </strong>
         There are two competing philosophies when it comes to interpreting the significance of these findings.
        </p>
        <p>
         (1) First, there is the
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2014/04/15/popper-kuhn-and-the-paradigm-shifts-of-the-genomic-revolution/" title="Popper, Kuhn, and the paradigm shifts of the genomic revolution">
          conservative genomic
         </a>
         concept, influenced by our difficulties in reproducing findings and awareness of the amount of genomic variation in the human genome. According to modern genomic criteria, even the joint finding of both studies is
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2014/03/05/the-age-of-mega-genomics-type-2-diabetes-and-protective-variants-in-slc30a8/" title="The age of mega-genomics, type 2 diabetes, and protective variants in SLC30A8">
          not significant
         </a>
         . The p-values reported by
         <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.15252/embr.201438840/full">
          Kahle and collaborators
         </a>
         do not reach 0.05 significance level when corrected for multiple testing of two variants. Also, segregation in the small febrile seizure family reported by
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24668262">
          Puskarjov and collaborators
         </a>
         is compatible with random segregation of a rare variant.
        </p>
        <p>
         (2) Secondly, there is a mixed genomic-functional concept. This concept accepts the fact that it is difficult to generate sufficient genetic evidence for an association for rare variants and derives additional information from functional studies as in the study by
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24668262">
          Puskarjov and collaborators
         </a>
         , which provides convincing functional data that shows a functional consequence of the R952H variant.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Conclusion.
         </strong>
         KCC2 encoded by
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         is an interesting candidate gene for epilepsy, given its central role in controlling the inhibitory effect of GABA. Both studies by
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24668262">
          Puskarjov and collaborators
         </a>
         and by
         <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.15252/embr.201438840/full">
          Kahle and collaborators
         </a>
         stumble upon a rare variant in the
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         gene that might predispose to various types of epilepsies including IGE/GGE and febrile seizures as a rare genetic risk factor.
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         is one of the top 5%
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2013/10/06/mutation-intolerance-why-some-genes-withstand-mutations-and-others-dont/" title="Mutation intolerance  why some genes withstand mutations and others dont">
          mutation intolerant
         </a>
         genes in the human genome and has previously never been found as a
         <em>
          de novo
         </em>
         mutation in epilepsy, autism, intellectual disability, or schizophrenia. It will be interesting to see how the genetic evidence for this variant and other
         <em>
          SLC12A5
         </em>
         variants accumulates in current
         <a href="http://epilepsygenetics.net/2014/05/01/the-final-euroepinomics-general-assembly-impressions-from-helsinki/" title="The final EuroEPINOMICS General Assembly  Impressions from Helsinki">
          large-scale
         </a>
         exome studies.
        </p>
