      <p>
       An apartment project planned by one of Ithacas major landlords was turned down for a package of tax incentives Thursday, pleasing residents who came out to protest while evoking warnings that Tompkins County will be seen as anti-development.
      </p>
      <p>
       The developer said he wont build it without the incentives on property and sales taxes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate--ccix-command-243">
        By a 3-2 vote, the Tompkins County Industrial Development Agency board rejected a resolution backing a package of property- and sales-tax abatements that would have reduced the liability on the increase in the property value at 130 E. Clinton St., just uphill from the city police headquarters.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate--ccix-command-243">
        <span class="-newsgate--ccix-command-243">
         The company known as 130 E. Clinton LLC, controlled by Ithaca landlord and developer Jason Fane, sought the package under the citys Community Investment Incentive Program to stimulate development downtown. The $4.6 million project is planned for a steep hillside of about six-tenths of an acre just east of the Ithaca police headquarters.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       The incentives dont absolve the owner of property taxes but reduce the increased liability caused by new development. The abatement package that was sought begins at 90 percent of the increased assessment in the first year and decreases equally over seven years.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the abatement, the property would have been subject to total city, county and Ithaca City School District taxes of $304,557 over the seven years. The value of property taxes saved with the abatement would have been $297,395.
      </p>
      <p>
       Fane, speaking to the IDA board after some 20 people spoke against it and one in favor, said its a marginal site in the first place, chosen only after others eligible for the citys density program proved unworkable, he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       If its not approved, the project will die, Fane said. Its not a question of probably; it will die, maybe it will die, I kind of think it might die. Absolutely, it will die today.
      </p>
      <p>
       Board members and county legislators Will Burbank and Nate Shinagawa sought to table Thursdays motion to have time to weigh more information, but their motion failed to get enough support.
      </p>
      <p>
       They were joined by board member Grace Chiang, of Chiang OBrien Architects in Ithaca, in voting against the abatements resolution. A lack of special environmental features, and the sensitive hillside and creekside site were reasons they cited. Shinagawa also said a mixed-use building, such as with ground-floor retail, would add more vitality, and Burbank held out hope there would be a better project someday proposed for the location.
      </p>
      <p>
       In support were county legislator Jim Dennis and businessman Larry Baum. Both cited the need for housing in the county and city.
      </p>
      <p>
       I think this was a really good infill project, just the kind of thing downtown Ithaca needs, Baum said.
      </p>
      <p>
       A developer with a different name would have gotten the OK, Dennis said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Not at the meeting were board members Martha Robertson, also a county legislator, and Ithaca Mayor Svante Myrick.
      </p>
      <p>
       The debate prompted Shinagawa to call for the city to consider revising its downtown incentives package. Five other projects have won approval of the program, including two hotels, and the board unanimously approved an enhanced incentives package for an expansion of the Carey Building on State/Martin Luther King Jr. Street, a block east of The Commons, owned by Travis-Hyde Properties, also a major city developer and landlord.
      </p>
      <p>
       The citys Planning and Development Board approved the Clinton Street project last year after getting modifications and a conservation easement on about an acre of the involved parcel that includes the steepest slope and the side closest to Six Mile Creek.
      </p>
      <p>
       But the citys Conservation Advisory Council recommended against it because it runs counter to the goals of maintaining water quality and reducing sedimentation, according to a statement from the council, which noted the city, unlike the Town of Ithaca, is working on but lacks specific measures to regulate development of steep slopes.
      </p>
