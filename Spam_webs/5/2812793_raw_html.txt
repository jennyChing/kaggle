             <p>
              A loud cheer must have erupted across the planet from countless Facebook users when a study, which debunked the utility of Facebook to employers as a tool forits candidate screening and recruitment process, was published.
             </p>
             <p>
              For over three years or more, people all over the world have been bombarded by news, blogs and editorials about how Facebook is the new medium for recruiters to screen out prospective employees based on their social networking etiquettes.
             </p>
             <p>
              Countless authors, experts and bloggers have been constantly telling everyone and anyone aspiring for a bright future to maintain social decorum and manners in whatever they chose to post or share on their Facebook page.
             </p>
             <p>
              The golden rule of posting on Facebook was suddenly
              <em>
               Dont post at all!
              </em>
             </p>
             <p>
              However, a recent study has debunked each of those theories and has put some balance back into a candidate's life. Until now candidates have sadly been cribbing about a lack of privacy and freedom in theirsocial life.
             </p>
             <p>
              The was study published in a Management Journal and is titled:
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://jom.sagepub.com/content/early/2013/12/13/0149206313515524.abstract" target="_blank">
               
               <strong>
                <em>
                 Social Media for Selection? Validity and Adverse Impact Potential of a Facebook-Based Assessment
                </em>
               </strong>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              The study set out to establish whether or notan accurate relationship exists between the content of a candidates Facebook profile and their individual potential for excellence inthe job that they are being screened for.
             </p>
             <p>
              In the absence of any substantial factor to predict a candidates capability, selectionbased on Facebook would mean that several companies are inaccurately screening out candidates. As a result they risk losing out a possibly high performing talent pool as well.
             </p>
             <p>
              The study also warns employers from using social media as a screening tool since it could put them in a potential landmine of lawsuits. Rejected candidates could consider the practice against local federal hiring norms.
             </p>
             <p>
              The worry being that the candidates can consider the rejection being based on possible identification of race, sexual orientation. The candidate could potentiallydecide to take the employer to court for discrimination based on this perceived reasoning.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               The fear of Facebook as a hiring medium
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p>
              A U.S based report published in 2013 put forward some interesting facts which had left many candidates worried about the impact their own Facebook profile either will have or has already had on jobs they have applied for.
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               73% of recruiters will expand their investment in social networks in 2013-14, while 62% will increase incentives from referrals.
              </li>
              <li>
               52% of job seekers used Facebook to help find work in 2012
              </li>
              <li>
               14% job seekers searched Facebook for job listings
              </li>
              <li>
               1 in 5 job seekers had a friend or acquaintance share a job listing on Facebook
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Such an escalated use of Facebook by companies was also considered inappropriate by employees since they would like to keeptheir personal life and work life separate. The two different identities that they have should remain hidden from the prying eyes of others.
             </p>
             <p>
              With the report debunking the utilityof Facebook to recruiters completely, candidates will surely be wishing that more and more companies will take a cue from the study and leave their respective social lives where it belongs, PRIVATE!
             </p>
