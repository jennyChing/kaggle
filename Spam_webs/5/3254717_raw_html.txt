        <p>
         <a href="http://weatherwars.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/NASA-1-July-2015-Waveforms.jpg">
          <img alt="NASA 1 July 2015 - Waveforms" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-1215" height="298" src="http://weatherwars.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/NASA-1-July-2015-Waveforms-1024x523.jpg" width="584"/>

         I often talk about the management of waves in the atmosphere, these waves are, in many ways, responsible for upwards of 80% of the creation or formation of micro scale weather events Events that matter to us on the ground. Mostly, it has to do with whether it rains or not, whether it is sunny or not, whether it is windy or not You get the idea.
         <br/>
         In this single image one can begin to get a grasp of the swarm of differing waveform patterns that must ALL be measured, controlled and eventually canceled out. This is why, ahead of developing storms, and underneath zones of clear skies under high pressure regions, there are so many chemtrails present. Each trail is laced with a specific chemical signature and nano-particles so that it, specifically, can be traced (for a set amount of time) as it flows through the atmosphere.
         <br/>
         You can see the water vapor haze laced with condensation nuclei from the trails seen most clearly in the lower portion of the image  This metallic soup allows the EM technology onboard these planes the ability to pulse the atmosphere creating these waves. Much like turning on-and-off a magnet and watching the iron filings dance in response to this energy. Dope up the atmosphere with these metals, and itll dance/resonate/respond/undulate to your tune.
         <br/>
         Your evening weatherman is more interested in talking to 6th graders about chasing tornadoes, flood safety and the problems caused by global warming, aka Climate Change rather than doing any kind of authentic atmospheric research. God forbid a single one of them being bold enough to step outside of the box that ensures a paycheck every other Friday and figuring this thing out for themselves.
        </p>

          New cloud comes a step closer   From the UK Met Office
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="sep">
          Posted on
         </span>
         <a href="http://weatherwars.info/2015/06/22/new-cloud-comes-a-step-closer-from-the-uk-met-office/" rel="bookmark" title="12:31">

         <span class="by-author">
          <span class="sep">

          </span>

       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="twitter-share">
         <a class="twitter-share-button" data-count="none" data-size="large" href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fweatherwars.info%2F2015%2F06%2F22%2Fnew-cloud-comes-a-step-closer-from-the-uk-met-office%2F&amp;via=weatherwarsinfo">
          Tweet

        <h2>
         From the UK Met Office:
        </h2>
        <h2>
         Asperitas
        </h2>
        <p>
         <em>
          Newly discovered cloud Asperitas (Latin for roughness) has taken another step towards being officially recognized and named in the
          <a href="http://library.wmo.int/pmb_ged/wmo_407_en-v2.pdf">
           International Cloud Atlas
          </a>
          .
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://weatherwars.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/asperatus_undulatus.png">
          <img alt="asperatus_undulatus" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1202" height="450" src="http://weatherwars.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/asperatus_undulatus.png" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          The cloud has been named Asperitas because it looks like rough or turbulent seas and has been put forward for inclusion in the Atlas by the UK
          <a href="https://cloudappreciationsociety.org/">
           Cloud Appreciation Society
          </a>
          .
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Gavin Pretor-Pinney, founder of the Cloud Appreciation Society said Its really exciting to see Asperitas that bit closer to becoming official. Its great that the general public and amateur observations have influenced the atlas, it feels very democratic. The internet has resulted in increased connectivity, these days everyone has a camera at their fingertips, and this has resulted overwhelming evidence for this new type of cloud.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          The
          <a href="https://www.wmo.int/pages/index_en.html">
           World Meteorological Organization
          </a>
          (WMO) is currently updating the International Cloud Atlas, first published in 1896, and has now presented details of the new cloud to the World Meteorological Congress.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          The WMO are making the Atlas more user-friendly and accessible and expect to publish the new edition next year. It is also expected to include the new cloud species Volutus (Latin for rolled), as well as some new special clouds like Homogenitus (from the Latin homo meaning man, and genitus meaning generated or made).
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Met Office Meteorologist and
          <a href="http://www.rmets.org/">
           Royal Meteorological Society
          </a>
          George Anderson is heavily involved in updating the Atlas and said Science, technology and photography have moved on in the past 40 years, so there is a need to update the Cloud Atlas.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Met Office Scientist, Graeme Anderson, completed a dissertation on Asperitas, for his Masters degree at the University of Reading. He said The challenge with this particular cloud formation is its rarity. It is very difficult to get good measurement data from this type of cloud if you dont know when or where it will appear, or how long it will last. It became clear to me that these cloud formations did not fit into the existing classifications. Its good to see this update taking place to make the International Cloud Atlas fully comprehensive.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          A scanned version of both volumes of the Atlas is available on the
          <a href="http://library.wmo.int/pmb_ged/wmo_407_en-v2.pdf">
           WMO
          </a>
          website. You can use the Met Office
          <a href="http://www.metoffice.gov.uk/learning/clouds/cloud-spotting-guide">
           cloud spotting guide
          </a>
          to help you identify different types of clouds, this can be a fun activity to try with children.
         </em>
        </p>
        <hr/>
        <p>
         I found this article only because of a
         <a href="https://www.facebook.com/saved/?collection_token=1324165842%3A586254444758776%3A92&amp;cref=35" target="_blank">
          Facebook posting
         </a>
         a few days back, and I thought to myself: Ive seen this kind of cloud before and Yes, it is a new cloud! But why are there suddenly several new clouds forms gaining new cloud status by being scientifically named and officially classified there by included in the Official Cloud Atlas? Well, Geo-engineering is now wholly in the game and the meteorological establishment is almost entirely clueless that this aggressive global operation is underway. For most of these scientists, TV presenters, university professors, commodities forecasters, NWS employees and the vast majority of climate researchers, this global game with our planets weather systems has been underway their ENTIRE career. They simply dont know anything different. 1976 being the Zero Year, 39 years ago. I happen to be 10 years old at the time and was still contemplating why it didnt start getting warmer right after the day that the Sun was the lowest in the sky (Winter Solstice) giving us our shortest day?Boy, did I have a lot to understand!
        </p>
        <p>
         So I dug back through my time lapse video archive and found one, really it was just the first one I found, Im sure there are others, but the searching is a bit tedious. Then I shared the
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1Mq76vu4V2Q" target="_blank">
          YouTube file
         </a>
         with the blog at the UK Met Office. I was sure that they would recognize the cloud type as that of the newly coined Asperitas and give me a pat on the back. (Well kinda..) But, I suppose I did kind of harshly call them out for NOT recognizing that it wasnt manipulated cloud form.  The reply was short and politely curt directing me to their her are the cloud types so you dork, figure it out from there kind of vibe.
        </p>
        <p>
         Here is a screen capture of the discussion.. It appears I am in good company!
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://weatherwars.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/New-cloud-comes-a-step-closer-Met-Office-News-Blog-2015-06-21-22-22-08.jpg">
          <img alt="New cloud comes a step closer | Met Office News Blog 2015-06-21 22-22-08" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-1204" height="825" src="http://weatherwars.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/New-cloud-comes-a-step-closer-Met-Office-News-Blog-2015-06-21-22-22-08-725x1024.jpg" width="584"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         I knew the time lapse this cloud over the mountains just to the east of me were odd, thats why I was time lapsingthem! After thirteen years of looking at the sky with this particular point of view, my discernment of what is and is not weird has matured.
        </p>
        <p>
         So, today, I put on my detective hat to dig up archive data to PROVE that my time lapse is NOT of simple lenticular clouds. Here is the classic definition of lenticular from Wikipedia (not the best source for off the beaten path information):
         <em>
          Where stable moist air flows over a
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
           mountain
          </a>
          or a range of mountains, a series of large-scale
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_waves" title="Lee waves">
           standing waves
          </a>
          may form on the downwind side. If the temperature at the crest of the wave drops to the
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">
           dew point
          </a>
          , moisture in the air may condense to form lenticular clouds. As the moist air moves back down into the trough of the wave, the cloud may evaporate back into vapor. Under certain conditions, long strings of lenticular clouds can form near the crest of each successive wave, creating a formation known as a 
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_cloud" title="Wave cloud">
           wave cloud
          </a>
          .
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>

        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="sep">

         <span class="by-author">

          </span>

       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="twitter-share">
         <a class="twitter-share-button" data-count="none" data-size="large" href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fweatherwars.info%2F2013%2F12%2F09%2F2013-1209-a-new-global-all-time-record-cold-temperature%2F&amp;via=weatherwarsinfo">
          Tweet
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
         December 9, 2013
        </p>
        <p>
         http://www.reuters.com/article/2013/12/10/climate-cold-idUSL1N0JP03720131210
        </p>
        <p>
         Dec 9 (Reuters)  The
         <a data-ls-seen="1" href="http://www.reuters.com/places/arctic?lc=int_mb_1001">
          Arctic
         </a>
         air blasting the eastern United States is positively balmy compared to the record minus 136 degrees Fahrenheit (minus 93 degrees Celsius) temperature measured in
         <a data-ls-seen="1" href="http://www.reuters.com/places/antarctica?lc=int_mb_1001">
          Antarctica
         </a>
         in August 2010, according to research released on Monday.
        </p>
        <p>
         Scientists made the discovery while analyzing 32 years of global surface temperatures recorded by satellites.
        </p>
        <p>
         They found that a high ridge in the East Antarctic Plateau contains pockets of trapped air that dipped as low as minus 136 Fahrenheit on Aug. 10, 2010, researchers said at the American Geophysical Union meeting in San Francisco.
        </p>
        <p>
         The previous record low was minus 128.6 F (minus 89.2 C), set in 1983 at the Russian Vostok Research Station in East
         <a data-ls-seen="1" href="http://www.reuters.com/places/antarctica?lc=int_mb_1001">
          Antarctica
         </a>
         , said Ted Scambos, lead scientist at the National Snow and Ice Data Center in Boulder, Colorado.
        </p>
        <p>
         We had a suspicion this Antarctic ridge was likely to be extremely cold, and colder than Vostok because its higher up the hill, Scambos said in a statement.
        </p>
        <p>
         The temperatures are about 50 degrees F colder than anything recorded in Alaska or Siberia.
        </p>

       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="twitter-share">
         <a class="twitter-share-button" data-count="none" data-size="large" href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fweatherwars.info%2F2013%2F11%2F07%2F2013-1107%2F&amp;via=weatherwarsinfo">

        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         November 7, 2013
        </p>
        <h1>
         Did Super Typhoon Haiyan Just Shatter The Global Hurricane Intensity Record?
        </h1>
        <p>
         <em>
          Just another weather record falling, this one, however, is major. -Scott
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         http://www.motherjones.com/blue-marble/2013/11/did-super-typhoon-haiyan-just-shatter-global-hurricane-intensity-record
        </p>
        <p>
         By at least one measurement, it appears that
         <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-asia-24846813" target="_blank">
          Super Typhoon Haiyan
         </a>
         , which just slammed into the Philippine island of Samar, may be the strongest storm reliably recorded on Earth. Additional measurements and analysis will surely be necessary to confirm this, but for now, heres what we know:
        </p>
        <p>
         The U.S. Navys
         <a href="http://www.usno.navy.mil/JTWC/" target="_blank">
          Joint Typhoon Warning Center
         </a>
         , which tracks typhoons and Super Typhoonsthe most powerful storms on the planet
         <a href="http://www.usno.navy.mil/NOOC/nmfc-ph/RSS/jtwc/warnings/wp3113web.txt" target="_blank">
          estimated
         </a>
         Haiyans maximum 1-minute sustained winds at 170 knots, which
         <a href="https://www.google.com/search?q=convert+knots+to+mph&amp;rlz=1C1CHFX_enUS513US513&amp;oq=convert+knots+to+mph&amp;aqs=chrome..69i57j0l5.3413j0j4&amp;sourceid=chrome&amp;espv=210&amp;es_sm=93&amp;ie=UTF-8" target="_blank">
          translates into
         </a>
         about 195 miles per hour.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.wunderground.com/blog/JeffMasters/comment.html?entrynum=2573" target="_blank">
          According to
         </a>
         meteorologist Jeff Masters, a number of Pacific storms prior to 1969 were measured with wind speeds equal to or above 170 knots, but these estimates are now considered unreliable. Since 1969, the three strongest storms on record by wind speed all had winds of 165 knots, or 190 miles per hour: 1979s Super Typhoon Tip, 1969s Atlantic Hurricane Camille, and 1980s Atlantic Hurricane Allen. Haiyan just passed all three by this metric, though Masters notes that there is less confidence in Haiyans true intensity, since Tip, Camille, and Allen were all investigated by hurricane hunter aircraft. Haiyans intensity has only been estimated based on satellite images (you can read more about how these satellite measurements are done, and why Haiyan presented such a stunning satellite image, in this
         <a href="http://www.newrepublic.com/article/115518/super-typhoon-haiyan-among-strongest-ever" target="_blank">
          great
          <em>
           New Republic
          </em>
          article
         </a>
         by Nate Cohn).
        </p>
       </div>
