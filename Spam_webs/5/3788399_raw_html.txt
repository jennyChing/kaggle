       </span>
      </span>
      <span class="Unicode" title="English pronunciation respelling">
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i>
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             GAL
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -i-
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             MY
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -ms
        </i>
       </a>
      </span>
      ; meaning "chicken or rooster mimic") is a
      <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">
       genus
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Ornithomimidae" title="Ornithomimidae">
       ornithomimid
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropod" title="Theropod">
       theropod
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">
       dinosaur
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">
       Cretaceous
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">
       period
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">
       Maastrichtian
      </a>
      stage)
      <a href="/wiki/Nemegt_Formation" title="Nemegt Formation">
       Nemegt Formation
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">
       Mongolia
      </a>
      . With individuals as long as 8 metres (26ft),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mako2009_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mako2009-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it was one of the largest ornithomimosaurs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSP88_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GSP88-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      is known from multiple individuals, ranging from juvenile (about 0.5 metres tall at the hip) to adult (about two metres tall at the hip).

        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="370" data-file-width="900" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png/220px-Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png/330px-Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png/440px-Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human-gallimimus_size_comparison.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Size comparison

     <p>
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      was rather
      <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">
       ostrich
      </a>
      -like, with a small head, toothless beak, large eyes, a long neck, short arms, long legs, and a long tail. A diagnostic character of
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      is a distinctly short 'hand' relative to the
      <a href="/wiki/Humerus" title="Humerus">
       humerus
      </a>
      length, when compared to other ornithomimids. The tail was used as a counterbalance. The eyes were located on the sides of its head, meaning that it did not possess
      <a href="/wiki/Binocular_vision" title="Binocular vision">
       binocular vision
      </a>
      . Like most modern birds and other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theropods" title="Theropods">
       theropods
      </a>
      , it had hollow bones.
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      had a number of adaptations which suggest good running ability, such as a strong
      <a href="/wiki/Ilium_(bone)" title="Ilium (bone)">
       ilium
      </a>
      , heavy tail base, long limbs, a long
      <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">
       tibia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Metatarsus" title="Metatarsus">
       metatarsus
      </a>
      and short toes, but it is unknown how fast it could run. All ornithomimids had long skulls but that of
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      was exceptionally elongated, due to an elongation of the snout. The snouts of the juvenile specimens are much shorter.
     </p>
     <p>
      Norwegian researcher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B8rn_H._Hurum" title="Jrn H. Hurum">
       Jrn H. Hurum
      </a>
      in 2001 published a detailed description of a complete lower jaw bone from
      <i>
       Gallimimus bullatus
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurum-jaw-abs-34_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hurum-jaw-abs-34-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He observed that the bones composing the jaw were "paper thin". and corrects minor mistakes made in previous reconstructions of the lower jaw of
      <i>
       G. bullatus
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurum-jaw-matmeth-35_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hurum-jaw-matmeth-35-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also observed that the tight
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intramandibular_joint" title="Intramandibular joint">
       intramandibular joint
      </a>
      would prevent any movement between the front and rear portions of the lower jaw.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurum-jaw-abs-34_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hurum-jaw-abs-34-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
       Discovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallimimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallimimus_fossils.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Gallimimus_fossils.jpg/220px-Gallimimus_fossils.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Gallimimus_fossils.jpg/330px-Gallimimus_fossils.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Gallimimus_fossils.jpg/440px-Gallimimus_fossils.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallimimus_fossils.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A group of
        <i>
         Gallimimus
        </i>
        skeletons
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
       fossil
      </a>
      remains of this dinosaur were discovered in early August 1963 by a team of Professor
      <a href="/wiki/Zofia_Kielan-Jaworowska" title="Zofia Kielan-Jaworowska">
       Zofia Kielan-Jaworowska
      </a>
      at Tsagan Khushu during a Polish-Mongolian expedition to the
      <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">
       Gobi Desert
      </a>
      of Mongolia. The find was reported by her in 1965.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1972, it was named and described by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologist" title="Paleontologist">
       paleontologists
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Rinchen_Barsbold" title="Rinchen Barsbold">
       Rinchen Barsbold
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Halszka_Osm%C3%B3lska" title="Halszka Osmlska">
       Halszka Osmlska
      </a>
      , and Ewa Roniewicz. The only named species is the
      <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">
       type species
      </a>
      <i>
       Gallimimus bullatus
      </i>
      . The generic name is derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       gallus
      </i>
      , "chicken", and
      <i>
       mimus
      </i>
      , "mimic", in reference to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_arch" title="Neural arch">
       neural arches
      </a>
      of the front neck vertebrae which resemble those of the
      <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">
       Galliformes
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">
       specific name
      </a>
      is derived from Latin
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bulla_(amulet)" title="Bulla (amulet)">
        bulla
       </a>
      </i>
      , a magic capsule worn by Roman youth around the neck, in reference to a bulbous swelling in the braincase on the underside of the
      <a href="/wiki/Parasphenoid" title="Parasphenoid">
       parasphenoid
      </a>
      , in the form of a capsule.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Holotype" title="Holotype">
       holotype
      </a>
      specimen,
      <b>
       IGM 100/11
      </b>
      , consists of a partial skeleton including the skull and lower jaws. It is a larger skeleton; several other partial skeletons have been described, most of them of juveniles, and numerous single bones.
     </p>
     <p>
      A second species announced by Barsbold in 1996, "Gallimimus mongoliensis" based on specimen IGM 100/14 from the older
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayanshiree_Formation" title="Bayanshiree Formation">
       Bayanshiree Formation
      </a>
      , has never been formally referred to this genus. In a reanalysis of the nearly complete skeleton of "Gallimimus mongoliensis" Barsbold concluded in 2006 that it is not a species of
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      but may represent a new, currently unnamed ornithomimid genus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kobayashi.26barsbold2006_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kobayashi.26barsbold2006-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Beak_and_paleoecology">
       Beak and paleoecology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallimimus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Beak and paleoecology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1143" data-file-width="1846" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG/220px-Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG/330px-Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG/440px-Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallimimus_bullatus_skull.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Skull
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The feeding habits of ornithomimids have been controversial. The original describers thought
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      preyed upon small animals, using its long arms as rakes to remove covering plant material on the soil. Later suggestions included
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnivory" title="Omnivory">
       omnivory
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivory" title="Herbivory">
       herbivory
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2001 Norell et al. reported a specimen of
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      (IGM 100/1133): a skull with soft tissue preservation. This specimen, as well as another new fossil skull of
      <i>
       Ornithomimus
      </i>
      , had a keratinous beak with vertical grooves projecting from the bony upper mandible. These structures are reminiscent of the lamellae seen in ducks, in which they function to strain small edible items like plants, forams, mollusks, and ostracods from the water. The authors further noted that ornithomimids were abundant in mesic environments, and rarer in more arid environments, suggesting that they may have depended on waterborne sources of food, possibly filter feeding. They noted that primitive ornithomimids had well developed teeth, while derived forms were edentulous and probably could not feed on large animals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norelletal2001_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-norelletal2001-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="1024" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg/220px-Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg/330px-Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg/440px-Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallimimus_Steveoc86.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Reconstruction
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      One later paper questioned the conclusions of Norell et al. Barrett (2005) noted that vertical ridges are seen on the inner surface of the beaks of strictly herbivorous turtles, and also the hadrosaurid
      <i>
       Edmontosaurus
      </i>
      . Barrett also offered calculations, estimating how much energy could be derived from filter feeding and the probable energy needs of an animal as big as
      <i>
       Gallimimus
      </i>
      . He concluded that herbivory was more likely.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barrett2005_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-barrett2005-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The rock
      <a href="/wiki/Facies" title="Facies">
       facies
      </a>
      of the Nemegt formation suggest the presence of stream and river channels, mudflats, and shallow lakes. Sediments also indicate that there existed a rich habitat, offering diverse food in abundant amounts that could sustain massive Cretaceous dinosaurs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
