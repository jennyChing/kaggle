      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       How Obamacare's Employer Mandate Harms Low-Wage Workers
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="bylines">
       <cite class="entity_block type_inline_block">
        <span class="attach_user_popup" data-user-id="" data-user-json="">
         <a class="avatar" href="http://blogs.forbes.com/people/aroy/">
          <img alt="Avik Roy" itemprop="image" src="http://0.gravatar.com/avatar/63e50440e403d1dcd4c8f8b263b51216?s=40&amp;d=http%3A%2F%2F0.gravatar.com%2Favatar%2Fad516503a11cd5ca435acc9bb6523536%3Fs%3D40&amp;r=G"/>

         <span>
          <a class="user" href="http://blogs.forbes.com/people/aroy/">
           Avik Roy
          </a>
          <span class="desc">

          </span>

        </span>
       </cite>
      </div>
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">

       </a>

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <div class="guest_contributor_block top">
         <img class="square" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/theapothecary/files/2014/09/Bill_Cassidy_official_portrait_112th_Congress1.jpg"/>
         <small>
          Guest post written by
         </small>
         <strong class="guest_moniker">
          Rep. Bill Cassidy and Chase Lindsey
         </strong>
         <p class="guest_bio">
          Dr. Cassidy represents the 6th District of Louisiana in the U.S. House of Representatives. Mr. Lindsey is a junior at Louisiana State.
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <!--donotpaginate-->
        </p>
        <p>
         Years after the Great Recession officially ended, workforce participation, unemployment and the number of workers involuntarily working part-time have remained stubbornly high. In contrast to what is typicalthat the deeper the recession, the more robust the reboundthe United States is in a jobless recovery with tepid growth in Gross Domestic Product after a particularly severe recession. This begs the question of why?
        </p>
        <p>
         The Affordable Care Act (ACA) is a possible cause. The ACA with its employer mandate has decreased the average hours worked per week of low-wage workers and, compared to higher paid workers, low-wage workers have fewer full-time job opportunities. This becomes apparent when low-wage workers are analyzed as a subgroup from the entire, aggregated workforce. Aggregate data shows an economy recovering from the recession with little effects from the ACA. However, if low-income workers are looked at separately, the ACA appears to be distorting the job market. These findings are supported by data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics, reports and surveys of employers, and are predicted by academic studies, which found that an employer mandate encourages the conversion of full-time to part-time jobs.
        </p>
        <p>
         The ACAs employer mandate, requiring businesses with 50 or more employees to cover health insurance for full-time employees (defined as 30 or more hours per week), will be incrementally implemented over the next few years. Employers with 100 or more employees must cover 70% of their full-time employees healthcare coverage by 2015 and 95% by 2016. Employers with 50-99 employees must cover at least 95% of employees coverage by 2016. Employers with over 50 employees who do not offer coverage or who offer coverage with lower standards than required, are fined. The employer must choose a look-back period of at least 3 months and at most 12 months. If an employee averaged 30 or more hours per week in that look-back period, the employee is considered full-time.
        </p>
        <iframe class="youtube-player" frameborder="0" height="365" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/sVC_nrpktMM" type="text/html" width="485">
        </iframe>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Evading the employer mandate
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         There are obvious, logical ways that an employer could evade the employer mandate. One is to cut employee hours to below 30 hours per week. This is not readily seen because the Bureau of Labor Statistics counts full-time employees as anyone working 35 hours or more a week. A person working two part-time jobs is considered a full-time employee if he or she totals 35 or more hours per week, even though the two jobs are with different employers. This is pertinent because, looking at the entire workforce, the number of workers having more than one part-time job has increased since 2011 and the number of
         <a href="http://www.economonitor.com/dolanecon/2014/03/13/multiple-jobholders-another-sign-of-a-job-market-in-crisis/">
          part-time workers in the labor force
         </a>
         has yet to reach pre-recessional levels. This does not reveal if these consequences affect higher-wage and lower-wage workers equally. Intuitively, lower-wage workers would be more likely to have their hours reduced to less than 30 hours per week as the marginal cost of providing low-wage workers health insurance is a greater cost as a percentage of their compensation than for higher-wage workers.
        </p>
        <p>
         Rather than focusing on the number of full-time
         <i>
          employees
         </i>
         in the entire workforce, a better way to study the impact of the ACA is to focus on the number of part-time versus full-time
         <i>
          jobs
         </i>
         and to subgroup lower-wage and higher-wage workers. While there is no single data source for this information, it can be inferred by the average hours worked per week by lower- and higher-wage workers. It must also account for the effects of the recession. The Heritage Foundations Filip Jolevski and James Sherk did this.
         <a href="http://www.heritage.org/research/reports/2014/06/shrinking-workweeks-a-sign-of-unequal-recovery-from-the-great-recession">
          Their data
         </a>
         is displayed in the attached charts entitled, Lowest-Income Workers Hit Hardest by Recession and Low-Income Workers Have Lost Most Work Hours.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Cassidy fig 1" class=" size-full wp-image-21770" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/theapothecary/files/2014/09/Cassidy-fig-1.png"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Jolevski and Sherk examined microdata from the Current Population Study and found that during the recession, most earning quintiles had a net loss of full-time jobs and the bottom three earning quintiles had a loss of average hours worked per week.But, after the recession ended in 2009, the top four quintiles began to recover full-time jobs and average hours worked per week increased substantially, whereas the bottom quintile did not recover. Temporally, the ACA passed in 2010. Although a temporal relationship does not prove that the ACA is the cause of low-income workers not recovering hours, they are associated.
        </p>
        <p>
         These charts pertain to workers, not jobs, and therefore do not account for workers who have two part-time jobs and therefore are considered full-time for the purposes of this analysis. Jolevski and Sherk did note, Average working hours dropped for workers in the bottom quintile during the recession, and government policy will probably accentuate this trend. Obamacare creates strong incentives for employers to create and employees to prefer part-time jobs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Workweek length is declining
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Also using Current Population Study data,
         <a href="http://news.investors.com/politics-obamacare/030514-692127-white-house-changes-study-now-shows-obamacare-work-effect.htm?nav=NewsLatest">
          Jed Graham found
         </a>
         differences in length of workweeks between low-wage earners, defined as anyone making $7.25 to $10 per hour, and all workers, as shown in the chart, Hours Breakdown. Graham defined 25-29 hours per week as a part-time worker and 31-34 hours per week as a full-time worker. The 30-hour threshold is removed because the Bureau of Labor Statistics rounds 29.5 hours up to 30 hours, which confuses the findings. The chart shows the difference between the entire workforce and low-wage earners ($7.25-$10/hour). The sharpest decline in full-time employment for these low-wageworkers seems to be from mid-2013 to 2014. He demonstrated this by calculating the ratio between the number of workers in the 31-34 hour group and the 25-29 hour group. As the number of 31-34 workers has decreased, the ratio has decreased.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Cassidy fig 2" class=" size-full wp-image-21771" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/theapothecary/files/2014/09/Cassidy-fig-2.png"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         As the red line on the charts show, this was especially true for lower-wage workers. In the graph, A Tale of Two Recoveries, Graham shows the difference in average workweeks between low-wage
         <i>
          industries
         </i>
         (retail trade, leisure and hospitality), in which workers average $14.50 per hour or less, and higher-wage industries. Comparing the percent change of average weekly hours since the recession began in December 2007, Graham found that industries with an average pay up to about $14.50 stagnantly gained a minimum number of hours from 2010 to 2012 and then sharply lost hours from 2012 through 2013. There was a net decrease of about 2.5% in hours worked between December 2007 through 2013. In contrast, work hours in higher-wage industries grew since 2009, so that by 2013, they averaged more hours than the December 2007 benchmark.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/theapothecary/2014/02/24/obamacare-may-be-causing-a-shift-to-part-time-workers-in-illinois/">
          Naomi Bauman also found
         </a>
         a temporal relationship between the passage of the ACA and shorter workweeks for low-wage employees in Illinois. Baumann found a loss of hours from 2011 to 2013 in Illinois lowest-paid sectors (retail trade, food and beverage and general merchandise), which made up one-fifth of the states total employment. Bauman also distinguishes recessional effects and ACA effects by revealing that average work hours were stable in all three mentioned sectors and actually increased for two of the three sectors from 2008 to 2010, the year the ACA passed, then decreased afterwards in all three sectors.
        </p>
        <p>
         Aside from labor statistics, further evidence that employers are decreasing employees hours and the number of full-time jobs is that employers say that they are, especially low-wage jobs. Jed Graham compiled
         <a href="http://news.investors.com/politics-obamacare/062414-669013-obamacare-employer-mandate-a-list-of-cuts-to-work-hours-jobs.htm">
          a list of over 400 employers
         </a>
         who have or will cut work hours or change work procedures in response to the ACAs employer mandate.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Federal Reserve surveys
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia conducted the 
         <a href="http://www.philadelphiafed.org/research-and-data/regional-economy/south-jersey-business-survey/2013/sjq213.pdf">
          South Jersey Business Survey
         </a>
         . A range of firms was canvassed to gauge the present and future effects of the employer mandate. In this 2013 second-quarter report, the firms were asked what steps, if any, that they had already taken or would take in response to ACA requirements. Of the surveyed firms, 11.6% had shifted full-time workers to part-time and 16.3% planned to switch full-time workers to part-time in the near future. Likewise, from a report released by the Federal Reserve of New York that
         <a href="http://www.newyorkfed.org/survey/business_leaders/2014/2014_08Supplemental.pdf">
          published the findings
         </a>
         from two surveys, the
         <i>
          Empire State Manufacturing Survey
         </i>
         found that 19.3% of the surveyed firms had increased or would increase the proportion of part-time employees, 21.6% had reduced or would reduce the number of employees, and 36.4% had increased or would increase the prices charged to consumers; in the
         <i>
          Business Leaders Survey
         </i>
         ,
         <sup>
         </sup>
         20.2% of the surveyed firms had increased or would increase the proportion of part-time employees, 16.9% had reduced or would reduce the number of employees, and 25% had increased or would increase the prices charged to consumers.
        </p>
        <p>
         There is also a body of academic literature that predicts or finds that an employer mandate decreases the number of full-time jobs and increases part-time employment. In 
         <a href="http://www2.hawaii.edu/~katyas/pdf/health_insuranceEI0306.pdf">
          Labor Market Eects of Employer-Provided Health Insurance
         </a>
         ,Katerina Sherstyuk, Yoav Wachsman and Gerard Russo used theoretical predictions and fictitious experimental studies, and found that mandating the insurance only for full-time workers leads to a higher coverage then under no mandate, [and] an increased number of part-time workers.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the 2011 research paper, 
         <a href="http://www.frbsf.org/economic-research/files/wp09-08bk.pdf">
          The Effect of an Employer Health Insurance Mandate on Health Insurance Coverage and the Demand for Labor: Evidence from Hawaii
         </a>
         , sponsored by the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, Thomas Buchmueller, John DiNardo, and Robert Valletta found, Its [the employer mandate's] primary detectable effect was an increased reliance on part-time workers who are exempt from the law.The Hawaii employer mandate differs from other states and the ACA, but this supports that an employer mandate generally incentivizes use of part-time labor.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is possible that this is not the employer decreasing hours but rather the employee choosing to work fewer hours. The
         <a href="http://www.cbo.gov/sites/default/files/cbofiles/attachments/45010-Outlook2014_Feb.pdf">
          Congressional Budget Office
         </a>
         , estimates that the ACA will reduce the total number of hours worked, on net, by about 1.5 percent to 2.0 percent during the period from 2017 to 2024, almost entirely because workers will choose to supply less labor because of economic benefits and disincentives to work. Although the CBO predicted this cause for decreased hours would begin in 2017 and therefore not currently an influence, it is of interest that the CBO states that, the largest declines in labor supply will probably occur among lower-wage workers.
        </p>
        <p>
         In conclusion, the recovery from the recent recession has been weak and low-wage workers have not experienced much of a recovery. Low-wage hours are low, full-time workers are shifting to part-time, and many people are holding two part-time jobs. Responsibility for this lays, at least in part, with the Affordable Care Act and in particular, the employee mandate. This is particularly seen when lower-wage workers are examined separately from higher-wage workers.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Why not eliminate the employer mandate?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         This could be an argument to eliminate the employer mandate. Indeed, the article 
         <a href="http://www.rwjf.org/content/dam/farm/reports/issue_briefs/2014/rwjf413248">
          Why Not Just Eliminate the Employer Mandate
         </a>
         , by Linda Blumberg, John Holahan, and Matthew Buettgens, from the Urban Institute, states, Eliminating the employer mandate would eliminate labor market distortions in the law, lessen opposition to the law from employers, and have little effect on coverage.It is important to address this if improving the prospects of low-income workers is a priority. Addressing the negative effects of the employer mandate is particularly important as the CBO predicts that the ACA will decrease the number of hours worked by lower-wage workers.
        </p>
        <p>
         The cumulative effects of the ACA could create a sect of Americans perpetually underemployed and increasingly dependent upon the government. This is not good.
        </p>
