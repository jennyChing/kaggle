               </span>
               <h1>
                Where Does MS Begin?
               </h1>
              </header>
              <aside class="disclaimer">
              </aside>
              <h2>
               New research suggests neurologists have been looking in the wrong place for decades.
              </h2>
              <div class="bottom">
               <div class="byline">
                Video featuring Dr. Sanjay Gupta
               </div>
               <div class="reviewedby">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="clear">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div id="video-player-top">
             </div>
             <aside class="aside">
              <img alt="Where Does MS Begin? (MS and the Brain)" class="left" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/cs_Multiple Sclerosis_Where Does MS Begin (MS and the Brain)_1_article.jpg"/>
             </aside>
             <p>
              Since the 1960s, neurologists have understood
              <a href="/health-report/living-with-multiple-sclerosis.aspx">
               MS
              </a>
              as
an illness of the brains white matter. White matter, in the center of the
brain, can be thought of as the cabling that connects brain regions with each
other. It is like the bundles of wires coming off the back of a computer.
             </p>
             <p>
              But new research is suggesting that MS may in fact
begin in the gray matter, the processing regions, located at the periphery of
the brain under the skull.
             </p>
             <p>
              Steven Schutzer, MD, of Rutgers New Jersey Medical
School is studying specific proteins involved at the earliest stages of the
disease. To his surprise, he is finding those proteins are associated not with
the white matter, but the gray. That was a big alert that the gray matter
seems to be involved at the very beginning of the first manifestations of MS,
he says.
             </p>
             <p>
              Patricia Coyle, MD, who has been treating MS for
years, calls the new research mind boggling. After decades of looking in the
wrong place, she says the evidence is now hitting us in the face, saying gray
matter, gray matter, gray matter is involved at the earliest time point.
             </p>
             <p>
              The lesions of MS show up clearly in the white
matter, and the symptoms suggest this is in fact a disease of the brains
wiring. White matter gets its color from myelin, the protective coating
around those wires. In MS, the bodys immune system attacks the myelin. That
leads to
              <a href="/health-report/living-well-with-multiple-sclerosis/improve-life-with-ms.aspx">
               symptoms
              </a>
              like blurry vision, weakness, and loss of muscle control.
             </p>
             <p>
              If you take off enough of the insulating sheath,
the myelin, you really lose the ability to nerve conduct along that wire, Dr.
Coyle says.
             </p>
             <p>
              But Coyle says this research may lead to a new
understanding of MS and ultimately
              <a href="/health-report/sanjay-gupta/is-ms-treatment-working.aspx">
               better treatments
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              The bottom line is we dont know what causes MS,
she says. If we could get a better handle on that then I think you really
begin to talk about the possibility of potentially designing, dare we say it, a
cure.
             </p>
