        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="name">
         Home Cured Corned Beef
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <meta content="UserComments:43" itemprop="interactionCount"/>
         <span class="entry-commentcount">
          <a href="#comments" title="Comments on Home Cured Corned Beef">

         <span class="entry-byline">
          Posted by
          <span class="author" itemprop="author">
           <a href="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/about/" rel="author">
            Elise
           </a>
          </span>

          <time class="entry-date" content="2011-03-08" datetime="2011-03-08T14:27:59+00:00" itemprop="datePublished" pubdate="">

       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="recipe-description" itemprop="description">
         <p>
          Vibrant pink, salty, and spicy, corned beef is always a welcome meal in our home, whether in a
          <a href="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/recipes/new_england_boiled_dinner/">
           boiled dinner
          </a>
          , with
          <a href="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/recipes/corned_beef_and_cabbage/">
           cabbage
          </a>
          , or in a
          <a href="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/recipes/reuben_sandwich/">
           sandwich
          </a>
          with Swiss cheese and sauerkraut. Who knew it was so easy to make? Corned beef is essentially beef cured in a salt brine, with some pickling spices for added flavor. It gets its name corn from an old English word for grain, or small pieces of hard things the size of grain, such as salt.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span id="more-10031">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          Over the years, many of my
          <a href="http://ruhlman.com/">
           friends
          </a>
          have encouraged me to cure my own, insisting that it wasnt hard to do, and well worth the effort. After finally getting around to it, Im happy to report that my friends were right! It really is easy; it just takes about 5 days to cure.
         </p>
         <p>
          Because you get to choose what pickling spices to use, you can make your own distinctively flavored corned beef. You know how BBQ masters have their own favorite homemade dry rubs? Its sort of like that. Pretty much every packaged corned beef brisket Ive bought tastes about the same. The one I home cured? Wonderful and different.
         </p>
         <p>
          While I researched several online sources for curing your own corned beef, as well as interrogating my colleague
          <a href="http://honest-food.net">
           Hank
          </a>
          , the source I referred to the most was Michael Ruhlmans brilliant
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0393058298?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=elisecom&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0393058298">
           Charcuterie: The Craft of Salting, Smoking, and Curing
          </a>
          (high recommend).
          <img alt="" border="0" height="1" src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=elisecom&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=0393058298" style="border: none !important; margin: 0px !important;" width="1"/>
          You can also see his instructions on
          <a href="http://leitesculinaria.com/5912/recipes-homemade-corned-beef.html">
           Leites Culinaria
          </a>
          . I played around a bit with the spice mix, and kept the garlic out of the brine, but other than that, pretty much followed Michaels method. Ive linked to a couple other methods from food bloggers in the links below if you are looking for some recipes with which to compare this one.
         </p>
        </div>

         <h2>
          Home Cured Corned Beef Recipe
         </h2>
         <div class="recipe-meta">
          <ul>
           <li class="recipe-prep">
            <span class="recipemeta-label">
             Prep time:
            </span>
            <span class="preptime" content="PT5D" itemprop="prepTime">
             5 days
             <span class="value-title" title="PT5D">
             </span>
            </span>
           </li>
           <li class="recipe-cook">
            <span class="recipemeta-label">
             Cook time:
            </span>
            <span class="cooktime" content="PT03H00M" itemprop="cookTime">
             3 hours
             <span class="value-title" title="PT03H00M">
             </span>
            </span>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
         <div class="entry-details recipe-intronote">
          <p>
           The spice mix with the gallon of brine makes easily enough curing brine for a 5 pound brisket, cured in a somewhat large-ish container. If you were to use a 2-gallon freezer bag or marinating bag, you would likely need just half (or less) of the amount of brine and brine spices.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="entry-details recipe-ingredients">
          <div class="yummly-button">
           <a class="YUMMLY-YUM-BUTTON" href="//yummly.com" rel="nofollow">
            Yum
           </a>
          </div>
          <h3>
           Ingredients
          </h3>
          <p>
           <em>
            Pickling spices:
           </em>
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp whole allspice berries
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp whole mustard seeds (brown or yellow)
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp coriander seeds
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp red pepper flakes
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp whole cloves
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp whole black peppercorns
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            9 whole cardamom pods
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            6 large bay leaves, crumbled
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            2 teaspoons ground ginger
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1/2 stick cinnamon
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           <em>
            Brine:
           </em>
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 gallon water
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            2 cups Kosher salt
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            5 teaspoons pink curing salt*
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            3 Tbsp pickling spices
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1/2 cup brown sugar
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           <em>
            *Pink curing salt, or sodium nitrite, goes by many names, such as Prague Powder #1 or DQ Curing Salt #1, and
            <a href="http://www.google.com/search?q=curing+salt&amp;hl=en&amp;tbs=shop%3A1&amp;aq=f">
             is available online
            </a>
            and may be available at your local specialty market or butcher shop. If you don't have it, you can still make corned beef, but it is necessary for that vibrant pink color we associate with corned beef. And it adds flavor too. Without it the corned beef will be a dull grey color.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Brisket:
           </em>
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 4-5 pound beef brisket
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 Tbsp pickling spices
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
         <div class="widget-ad ad-mid-callout" id="widget-ad-mid-callout">
          <!-- ADSENSE || Mid-Callout Ad -->
          <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-5527714554151844" data-ad-format="auto" data-ad-slot="1194169405" style="display:inline-block;width:300px;height:250px;">
          </ins>
          <script>
           (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
          </script>
         </div>
         <div class="entry-details recipe-method instructions">
          <h3>
           Method
          </h3>
          <div itemprop="recipeInstructions">
           <p>
            <b>
             1
            </b>
            You can either used store-bought pickling spices or you can make your own. To make your own, toast the allspice berries, mustard seeds, coriander seeds, red pepper flakes, cloves, peppercorns, and cardamom pods in a small frying pan on high heat until fragrant and you hear the mustard seeds start to pop. Remove from heat and place in a small bowl. Use a mortar and pestle to crush the spices a little (or the back of a spoon or the side of a knife on a flat surface). Add to a small bowl and stir in the crumbled bay leaves and ground ginger.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-1" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17357" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-1.jpg" width="600"/>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-2" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17362" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-2.jpg" width="600"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             2
            </b>
            Add about 3 Tbsp of the spice mix (reserve the rest for cooking the corned beef after it has cured), plus the half stick of cinnamon, to a gallon of water in a large pot, along with the Kosher salt, pink salt (if using), and brown sugar. Bring to a boil, then remove from heat and let cool to room temperature. Then refrigerate until well chilled.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             3
            </b>
            Place the brisket in a large, flat container or pan, and cover with the brine. The brine should cover the meat. The meat may float in which case you may want to weigh it down with a plate.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-2.5" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17364" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-2.5.jpg" width="600"/>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-3" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17361" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-3.jpg" width="600"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            Alternatively you can use a 2-gallon freezer bag (placed in a container so if it leaks it doesn't leak all over your refrigerator), place the brisket in the freezer bag and about 2 quarts of brine, squeezing out the air from the bag before sealing. Place in the refrigerator and chill from 5-7 days. Every day flip the brisket over, so that all sides get brined equally.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-4" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17360" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-4.jpg" width="600"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             4
            </b>
            At the end of the cure, remove the brisket from the brine and rinse off the brine with cold water. Place the brisket in a large pot that just fits around the brisket and cover with at least one inch of water. If you want your brisket less salty, add another inch of water to the pot. Add a tablespoon of the pickling spices to the pot. Bring to a boil, reduce to a very low simmer (barely bubbling), and cook 3-4 hours, until the corned beef is fork tender. (At this point you can store in the fridge for up to a week.)
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-5" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17359" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-5.jpg" width="600"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             5
            </strong>
            Remove the meat to a cutting board. (You can use the spiced cooking liquid to cook vegetables for
            <a href="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/recipes/new_england_boiled_dinner/">
             boiled dinner
            </a>
            or
            <a href="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/recipes/corned_beef_and_cabbage/">
             corned beef and cabbage
            </a>
            .) Notice the visible lines on the meat; this is the "grain" of the meat, or the direction of the muscle fibers. To make the meat easier to cut, cut it first in half, along the grain of the meat. Then make thin crosswise cuts, across the grain to cut the meat to serve.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="homemade-corned-beef-method-600-7" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-17363" height="400" src="http://www.simplyrecipes.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/03/homemade-corned-beef-method-600-7.jpg" width="600"/>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
