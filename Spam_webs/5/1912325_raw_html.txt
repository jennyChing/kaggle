     <p>
      <b>
       Zora Neale Hurston
      </b>
      (January 7, 1891
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
       January 28, 1960) was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Folkloristics" title="Folkloristics">
       folklorist
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">
       anthropologist
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">
       author
      </a>
      . Of Hurston's four novels and more than 50 published short stories, plays, and essays, she is best known for her 1937 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Their_Eyes_Were_Watching_God" title="Their Eyes Were Watching God">
        Their Eyes Were Watching God
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to new editions of her work being published after a revival of interest in her in 1975, her manuscript
      <i>
       Every Tongue Got to Confess
      </i>
      (2001), a collection of folktales gathered in the 1920s, was published posthumously after being discovered in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smithsonian" title="Smithsonian">
       Smithsonian
      </a>
      archives.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Hurston was the fifth of eight children of John Hurston and Lucy Ann Hurston (
      <i>
       ne
      </i>
      Potts). Her father was a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">
       Baptist
      </a>
      preacher, tenant farmer, and carpenter, and her mother was a school teacher. She was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Notasulga,_Alabama" title="Notasulga, Alabama">
       Notasulga, Alabama
      </a>
      , on January 7, 1891, where her father grew up and her grandfather was the preacher of a Baptist church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When she was three, her family moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Eatonville,_Florida" title="Eatonville, Florida">
       Eatonville, Florida
      </a>
      ; in 1887 it was one of the first all-
      <a href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">
       black
      </a>
      towns to be incorporated in the United States. Hurston said she always felt that Eatonville was "home" to her as she grew up there, and sometimes she claimed it as her birthplace.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Her father later was elected as mayor of the town in 1897 and in 1902 became preacher of its largest church, Macedonia Missionary Baptist.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hurston later glorified Eatonville in her stories as a place where African Americans could live as they desired, independent of white society. In 1901, some northern schoolteachers visited Eatonville and gave Hurston a number of books that opened her mind to literature; she described it as a kind of "birth".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston spent the remainder of her childhood in Eatonville, and describes the experience of growing up there in her 1928 essay, "How It Feels to Be Colored Me".
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1904, Hurston's mother died. Her father remarried to Matte Moge; this was considered a minor scandal, as it was rumored that he had relations with Moge before his first wife's death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston's father and stepmother sent her away to a Baptist boarding school in
      <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">
       Jacksonville, Florida
      </a>
      . They eventually stopped paying her tuition and the school expelled her. She later worked as a maid to the lead singer in a traveling
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilbert_%26_Sullivan" title="Gilbert &amp; Sullivan">
       Gilbert &amp; Sullivan
      </a>
      theatrical company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zora_Neale_Hurston_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zora_Neale_Hurston-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1917, Hurston began attending Morgan College, the high school division of
      <a href="/wiki/Morgan_State_University" title="Morgan State University">
       Morgan State University
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historically_black_college" title="Historically black college">
       historically black college
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">
       Baltimore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">
       Maryland
      </a>
      . At this time, apparently to qualify for a free high-school education (as well, perhaps to reflect her literary birth), the 26-year-old Hurston began claiming 1901 as her year of birth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zora_Neale_Hurston_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zora_Neale_Hurston-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She graduated from the high school of Morgan State University in 1918.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="College">
       College
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: College">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1918, Hurston began studies at
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">
       Howard University
      </a>
      , where she became one of the earliest initiates of
      <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Phi_Beta" title="Zeta Phi Beta">
       Zeta Phi Beta
      </a>
      Sorority and co-founded
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Hilltop_(newspaper)" title="The Hilltop (newspaper)">
        The Hilltop
       </a>
      </i>
      , the university's student newspaper.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While there, she took courses in Spanish, English, Greek and public speaking and earned an associate degree in 1920.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1921, she wrote a short story,
      <i>
       John Redding Goes to Sea
      </i>
      , which qualified her to become a member of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alain_Locke" title="Alain Locke">
       Alaine Locke's
      </a>
      literary club, The Stylus. Hurston left Howard in 1924 and in 1925 was offered a scholarship by Barnard trustee
      <a href="/wiki/Annie_Nathan_Meyer" title="Annie Nathan Meyer">
       Annie Nathan Meyer
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Barnard_College" title="Barnard College">
       Barnard College
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia University
      </a>
      , where she was the college's sole black student.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfordreference.com_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oxfordreference.com-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston received her B.A. in
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropology
      </a>
      in 1928, when she was 37. While she was at Barnard, she conducted
      <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">
       ethnographic
      </a>
      research with noted anthropologist
      <a href="/wiki/Franz_Boas" title="Franz Boas">
       Franz Boas
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia University
      </a>
      . She also worked with
      <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Benedict" title="Ruth Benedict">
       Ruth Benedict
      </a>
      as well as fellow anthropology student
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mead" title="Margaret Mead">
       Margaret Mead
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After graduating from Barnard, Hurston spent two years as a graduate student in anthropology at Columbia University.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurston.2C_Zora_Neale_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hurston.2C_Zora_Neale-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adulthood">
       Adulthood
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adulthood">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1927, Hurston married
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Sheen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Herbert Sheen (page does not exist)">
       Herbert Sheen
      </a>
      , a jazz musician and former classmate at Howard who later became a physician. Their marriage ended in 1931. In 1939, while Hurston was working for the
      <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">
       WPA
      </a>
      , she married Albert Price, who was 25 years younger than she; this marriage ended after only seven months.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfordreference.com_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-oxfordreference.com-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She lived in a cottage in
      <a href="/wiki/Eau_Gallie,_Florida" title="Eau Gallie, Florida">
       Eau Gallie, Florida
      </a>
      , twice: once in 1929 and again in 1951.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ft110306_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ft110306-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the 1930s, Hurston was a resident of
      <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_New_Jersey" title="Westfield, New Jersey">
       Westfield, New Jersey
      </a>
      , where
      <a href="/wiki/Langston_Hughes" title="Langston Hughes">
       Langston Hughes
      </a>
      was among her neighbors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1934 she established a school of dramatic arts "based on pure Negro expression" at
      <a href="/wiki/Bethune-Cookman_University" title="Bethune-Cookman University">
       Bethune-Cookman University
      </a>
      (at the time, Bethune-Cookman College), a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historically_black_college" title="Historically black college">
       historically black college
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">
       Daytona Beach, Florida
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porter_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Porter-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In later life, in addition to continuing her literary career, Hurston served on the faculty of North Carolina College for Negroes (now
      <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Central_University" title="North Carolina Central University">
       North Carolina Central University
      </a>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">
       Durham, North Carolina
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurston.2C_Zora_Neale_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hurston.2C_Zora_Neale-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honors">
       Legacy and honors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy and honors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1956 Hurston received the Bethune-Cookman College Award for Education and Human Relations in recognition of her achievements. The English Department at Bethune-Cookman College remains dedicated to preserving her cultural legacy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timeline_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Timeline-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropological_and_folkloric_fieldwork">
       Anthropological and folkloric fieldwork
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anthropological and folkloric fieldwork">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Hurston traveled extensively in the Caribbean and the American South and immersed herself in local cultural practices to conduct her anthropological research. Based on her work in the South, sponsored from 1928 to 1932 by
      <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Osgood_Mason" title="Charlotte Osgood Mason">
       Charlotte Osgood Mason
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a wealthy philanthropist, Hurston wrote
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mules_and_Men&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mules and Men (page does not exist)">
        Mules and Men
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1935. She was doing research in lumber camps and commented on the practice of white men in power taking black women as sexual
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubines" title="Concubines">
       concubines
      </a>
      , including having them bear children. This later was referred to as "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramour_rights" title="Paramour rights">
       paramour rights
      </a>
      ," based in the men's power under
      <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation_in_the_United_States" title="Racial segregation in the United States">
       racial
      </a>
      and related to practices during slavery times. The book also includes much folklore. She used this material as well in fictional treatment developed for her novels such as
      <i>
       Jonah's Gourd Vine
      </i>
      (1934).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1936 and 1937, Hurston traveled to Jamaica and Haiti for research, with support from the
      <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Foundation" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Foundation">
       Guggenheim Foundation
      </a>
      . She drew from this for her anthropological work,
      <i>
       Tell My Horse
      </i>
      (1938).
     </p>
     <p>
      From October 1947 to February 1948, she lived in Honduras, at the north coastal town of
      <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Puerto Corts">
       Puerto Corts
      </a>
      . She had some hopes of locating either Mayan ruins or vestiges of an as yet undiscovered civilization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While in Puerto Corts, she wrote much of
      <i>
       Seraph on the Suwanee
      </i>
      , set in Florida. Hurston expressed interest in the polyethnic nature of the population in the region (many, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Miskito_Sambu" title="Miskito Sambu">
       Miskito Zambu
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" title="Garifuna people">
       Garifuna
      </a>
      , were of partial African ancestry and had developed
      <a href="/wiki/Creole_peoples" title="Creole peoples">
       creole
      </a>
      cultures).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">
       Later years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1948, Hurston was falsely accused of molesting a ten-year-old boy. Although the case was dismissed after Hurston presented evidence that she was in Honduras when the crime supposedly occurred in the U.S., her personal life was seriously disrupted by the scandal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfordreference.com_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-oxfordreference.com-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During her last decade, Hurston worked as a freelance writer for magazines and newspapers. In the fall of 1952 she was contacted by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sam_Nunn_(editor)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sam Nunn (editor) (page does not exist)">
       Sam Nunn
      </a>
      , editor of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Courier" title="Pittsburgh Courier">
        Pittsburgh Courier
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      to go to Florida to cover the murder trial of
      <a href="/wiki/Ruby_McCollum" title="Ruby McCollum">
       Ruby McCollum
      </a>
      . The wealthy black married woman was charged with murdering a prominent white doctor and politician, also married, whom McCollum said had forced her to have sex and bear his child.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-movie-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston recalled what she had seen of white male sexual dominance in the lumber camps in North Florida, and discussed it with Nunn. They both thought the case might be about such "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramour_rights" title="Paramour rights">
       paramour rights
      </a>
      ," and wanted to "expose it to a national audience."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-movie-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon reaching Live Oak, Hurston was surprised not only by the gag order the judge in the trial placed on the defense, but by her inability to get residents in town to talk about the case; both blacks and whites were silent. She believed that might have been related to Dr. Adams' alleged involvement as well in Sam McCollum's gambling operation. Her articles were published by the newspaper during the trial. Ruby McCollum was convicted by an all-white, all-male jury, and sentenced to death. Hurston had a special assignment to write a serialized account,
      <i>
       The Life Story of Ruby McCollum
      </i>
      , over three months in 1953 in the newspaper.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Her part was ended abruptly when she and Nunn disagreed about her pay, and she left.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie_24-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-movie-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Unable to pay independently to return for the appeal and second trial, she contacted journalist
      <a href="/wiki/William_Bradford_Huie" title="William Bradford Huie">
       William Bradford Huie
      </a>
      , with whom she had worked at
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_American_Mercury" title="The American Mercury">
        The American Mercury
       </a>
      </i>
      , to try to interest him in the case. He covered the appeal and second trial, and also developed material from a background investigation. Hurston shared her material with him from the first trial, but he acknowledged her only briefly in his book,
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruby_McCollum:_Woman_in_the_Suwannee_Jail&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruby McCollum: Woman in the Suwannee Jail (page does not exist)">
        Ruby McCollum: Woman in the Suwannee Jail
       </a>
      </i>
      (1956), which became a bestseller.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boyd_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-boyd-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston celebrated that "McCollums testimony in her own defense marked the first time that a woman of African-American descent was allowed to testify as to the paternity of her child by a white man. Hurston firmly believed that Ruby McCollums testimony sounded the death toll of '
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramour_rights" title="Paramour rights">
       paramour rights
      </a>
      ' in the Segregationist South."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-movie_24-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-movie-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Among other positions, Hurston later worked at the
      <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">
       Pan American World Airways
      </a>
      Technical Library at
      <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Air_Force_Base" title="Patrick Air Force Base">
       Patrick Air Force Base
      </a>
      in 1957. She was fired for being "too well-educated" for her job.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ft140204_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ft140204-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pierce,_Florida" title="Fort Pierce, Florida">
       Fort Pierce
      </a>
      . Taking jobs where she could find them, she worked as a substitute teacher and as a maid.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death">
       Death
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During a period of financial and medical difficulties, Hurston was forced to enter St. Lucie County Welfare Home, where she suffered a stroke. She died of
      <a href="/wiki/Hypertensive_heart_disease" title="Hypertensive heart disease">
       hypertensive heart disease
      </a>
      on January 28, 1960, and was buried at the Garden of Heavenly Rest in Fort Pierce, Florida. Her remains were in an unmarked grave until 1973. Novelist
      <a href="/wiki/Alice_Walker" title="Alice Walker">
       Alice Walker
      </a>
      and literary scholar
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charlotte_Hunt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charlotte Hunt (page does not exist)">
       Charlotte Hunt
      </a>
      found an unmarked grave in the general area where Hurston had been buried, and decided to mark it as hers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After Hurston died her papers were ordered to be burned. A law officer and friend, Patrick DuVal, passing by the house where she had lived, stopped and put out the fire, thus saving an invaluable collection of literary documents for posterity. The nucleus of this collection was given to the University of Florida libraries in 1961 by Mrs. Marjorie Silver, friend and neighbor of Hurston. Other materials were donated in 1970 and 1971 by Frances Grover, daughter of E. O. Grover, a Rollins College professor and long-time friend of Hurston's. In 1979 Stetson Kennedy of Jacksonville, who knew Hurston through his work with the Federal Writers Project, added additional papers. [(Zora Neal Hurston Papers, University of Florida Smathers Libraries, August 2008)]
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">
       Literary career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1920s">
       1920s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1920s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      When Hurston arrived in New York City in 1925, the
      <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">
       Harlem Renaissance
      </a>
      was at its peak, and she soon became one of the writers at its center. Shortly before she entered Barnard, Hurston's short story Spunk was selected for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_Negro" title="The New Negro">
        The New Negro
       </a>
      </i>
      , a landmark anthology of fiction, poetry, and essays focusing on African and African-American art and literature.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1926, a group of young black writers including Hurston,
      <a href="/wiki/Langston_Hughes" title="Langston Hughes">
       Langston Hughes
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Thurman" title="Wallace Thurman">
       Wallace Thurman
      </a>
      , calling themselves the
      <a href="/wiki/Niggerati" title="Niggerati">
       Niggerati
      </a>
      , produced a literary magazine called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fire!!" title="Fire!!">
        Fire!!
       </a>
      </i>
      that featured many of the young artists and writers of the
      <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Renaissance" title="Harlem Renaissance">
       Harlem Renaissance
      </a>
      . In 1929, Hurston moved to Eau Gallie in Florida where she wrote
      <i>
       Mules and Men
      </i>
      , which was later published in 1935.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indian_River_Journal_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Indian_River_Journal-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1930s">
       1930s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1930s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      By the mid-1930s, Hurston had published several short stories and the critically acclaimed
      <i>
       Mules and Men
      </i>
      (1935), a groundbreaking work of "literary anthropology" documenting African-American
      <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
       folklore
      </a>
      from timber camps in North Florida. In 1930, she collaborated with
      <a href="/wiki/Langston_Hughes" title="Langston Hughes">
       Langston Hughes
      </a>
      on
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mule_Bone" title="Mule Bone">
        Mule Bone: A Comedy of Negro Life in Three Acts
       </a>
      </i>
      , a play that they never finished. It was published posthumously in 1991.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurston.2C_Zora_Neale_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hurston.2C_Zora_Neale-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1937, Hurston was awarded a prestigious
      <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellowship" title="Guggenheim Fellowship">
       Guggenheim Fellowship
      </a>
      to conduct ethnographic research in
      <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">
       Jamaica
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
       Haiti
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Tell My Horse
      </i>
      (1938) documents her account of her fieldwork studying spiritual and cultural rituals in Jamaica and
      <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">
       vodoun
      </a>
      in Haiti. Hurston also translated her anthropological work into the performing arts, and her folk revue,
      <i>
       The Great Day,
      </i>
      premiered at the John Golden Theatre in New York in 1932.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hurston's first three novels were published in the 1930s:
      <i>
       Jonah's Gourd Vine
      </i>
      (1934);
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Their_Eyes_Were_Watching_God" title="Their Eyes Were Watching God">
        Their Eyes Were Watching God
       </a>
      </i>
      (1937), written during her fieldwork in Haiti and considered her masterwork; and
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moses,_Man_of_the_Mountain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moses, Man of the Mountain (page does not exist)">
        Moses, Man of the Mountain
       </a>
      </i>
      (1939).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1940s_and_1950s">
       1940s and 1950s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1940s and 1950s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the 1940s, Hurston's work was published in such periodicals as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_American_Mercury" title="The American Mercury">
        The American Mercury
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Evening_Post" title="The Saturday Evening Post">
        The Saturday Evening Post
       </a>
      </i>
      . Her last published novel,
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seraph_on_the_Suwanee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seraph on the Suwanee (page does not exist)">
        Seraph on the Suwanee
       </a>
      </i>
      , notable principally for its focus on white characters, was published in 1948. It explores images of "
      <a href="/wiki/White_trash" title="White trash">
       white trash
      </a>
      " women. Jackson (2000) argues that Hurston's meditation on abjection, waste, and the construction of class and gender identities among poor whites reflects the
      <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">
       eugenics
      </a>
      discourses of the 1920s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1952, Hurston was assigned by the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Courier" title="Pittsburgh Courier">
        Pittsburgh Courier
       </a>
      </i>
      to cover the small-town murder trial of
      <a href="/wiki/Ruby_McCollum" title="Ruby McCollum">
       Ruby McCollum
      </a>
      , the prosperous black wife of the local
      <a href="/wiki/Bolita" title="Bolita">
       bolita
      </a>
      racketeer, who had killed a racist white doctor. She also contributed to
      <i>
       Woman in the Suwannee County Jail
      </i>
      , a book by journalist and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Civil_Rights_Movement_(1955%E2%80%931968)" title="American Civil Rights Movement (19551968)">
       civil rights
      </a>
      advocate
      <a href="/wiki/William_Bradford_Huie" title="William Bradford Huie">
       William Bradford Huie
      </a>
      . In 2008,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Library_of_America" title="The Library of America">
       The Library of America
      </a>
      selected excerpts from this work for inclusion in its two-century retrospective of American True Crime writing.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_obscurity">
       Public obscurity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Public obscurity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Hurston's work slid into obscurity for decades, for a number of cultural and political reasons.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many readers objected to the representation of
      <a href="/wiki/African_American_Vernacular_English" title="African American Vernacular English">
       African-American dialect
      </a>
      in Hurston's novels, given the racially charged history of
      <a href="/wiki/Eye_dialect#Use" title="Eye dialect">
       dialect fiction
      </a>
      in American literature. Her stylistic choices in terms of dialogue were influenced by her academic experiences. Thinking like a folklorist, Hurston strove to represent speech patterns of the period which she documented through ethnographic research.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      For example, a character in
      <i>
       Jonah's Gourd Vine
      </i>
      expresses herself in this manner:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        "Dat's a big ole resurrection lie, Ned. Uh slew-foot, drag-leg lie at dat, and Ah dare yuh tuh hit me too. You know Ahm uh fightin' dawg and mah hide is worth money. Hit me if you dare! Ah'll wash yo' tub uh 'gator guts and dat quick."
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Several of Hurston's literary contemporaries criticized Hurston's use of dialect as a caricature of African-American culture rooted in a racist tradition. In particular, a number of writers associated with the Harlem Renaissance were critical of Hurston's later writings, on the basis that they did not agree with or further the position of the overall movement. One particular criticism came from
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wright_(author)" title="Richard Wright (author)">
       Richard Wright
      </a>
      in his review of
      <i>
       Their Eyes Were Watching God
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       ... The sensory sweep of her novel carries no theme, no message, no thought. In the main, her novel is not addressed to the Negro, but to a white audience whose chauvinistic tastes she knows how to satisfy. She exploits that phase of Negro life which is "quaint," the phase which evokes a piteous smile on the lips of the "superior" race.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         32
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      More recently, many critics have praised Hurston's skillful use of idiomatic speech.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the 1930s and 1940s when her work was published, the pre-eminent African-American author was Richard Wright.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike Hurston, Wright wrote in explicitly political terms, as someone who had become disenchanted with communism, using the struggle of African Americans for respect and economic advancement as both the setting and the motivation for his work. Other popular African-American authors of the time, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Ellison" title="Ralph Ellison">
       Ralph Ellison
      </a>
      , dealt with the same concerns as Wright. Hurston's work, which did not engage these political issues, did not fit in with this struggle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1951, for example, Hurston argued that New Deal economic support created a harmful dependency by African Americans on the government, and that this dependency ceded too much power to politicians.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">
       Politics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Politics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/John_McWhorter" title="John McWhorter">
       John McWhorter
      </a>
      has called Hurston "America's favorite
      <a href="/wiki/Black_conservatism" title="Black conservatism">
       black conservative
      </a>
      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McWhoter_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McWhoter-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Root-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while
      <a href="/wiki/David_T._Beito" title="David T. Beito">
       David T. Beito
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Linda_Royster_Beito" title="Linda Royster Beito">
       Linda Royster Beito
      </a>
      have argued that she can better be characterized as a "
      <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">
       libertarian
      </a>
      ." She was a Republican who was generally sympathetic to the foreign policy non-interventionism of the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Right_(United_States)" title="Old Right (United States)">
       Old Right
      </a>
      and a fan of
      <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Washington" title="Booker T. Washington">
       Booker T. Washington
      </a>
      's self-help politics. She disagreed with the philosophies (including Communism and the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">
       New Deal
      </a>
      ) supported by many of her colleagues in the Harlem Renaissance, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Langston_Hughes" title="Langston Hughes">
       Langston Hughes
      </a>
      , who was in the 1930s a supporter of the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      and praised it in several of his poems. Despite much common ground with the Old Right in domestic and foreign policy, Hurston was not a
      <a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism" title="Social conservatism">
       social conservative
      </a>
      . Her writings show an affinity for feminist individualism. In this respect, her views were similar to two
      <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">
       libertarian
      </a>
      novelists who were her contemporaries:
      <a href="/wiki/Rose_Wilder_Lane" title="Rose Wilder Lane">
       Rose Wilder Lane
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Paterson" title="Isabel Paterson">
       Isabel Paterson
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beito_48-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beito-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although her personal quotes show a disbelief of religion, Hurston did not negate spiritual matters as evidenced from her 1942 autobiography
      <i>
       Dust Tracks on a Road
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Prayer seems to me a cry of weakness, and an attempt to avoid, by trickery, the rules of the game as laid down. I do not choose to admit weakness. I accept the challenge of responsibility. Life, as it is, does not frighten me, since I have made my peace with the universe as I find it, and bow to its laws. The ever-sleepless sea in its bed, crying out how long? to Time; million-formed and never motionless flame; the contemplation of these two aspects alone, affords me sufficient food for ten spans of my expected lifetime. It seems to me that organized creeds are collections of words around a wish. I feel no need for such. However, I would not, by word or deed, attempt to deprive another of the consolation it affords. It is simply not for me. Somebody else may have my rapturous glance at the archangels. The springing of the yellow line of morning out of the misty deep of dawn, is glory enough for me. I know that nothing is destructible; things merely change forms. When the consciousness we know as life ceases, I know that I shall still be part and parcel of the world. I was a part before the sun rolled into shape and burst forth in the glory of change. I was, when the earth was hurled out from its fiery rim. I shall return with the earth to Father Sun, and still exist in substance when the sun has lost its fire, and disintegrated into infinity to perhaps become a part of the whirling rubble of space. Why fear? The stuff of my being is matter, ever changing, ever moving, but never lost; so what need of denominations and creeds to deny myself the comfort of all my fellow men? The wide belt of the universe has no need for finger-rings. I am one with the infinite and need no other assurance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FFRF_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FFRF-49">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         49
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In 1952, Hurston supported the presidential campaign of Senator
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Taft" title="Robert A. Taft">
       Robert A. Taft
      </a>
      . Like Taft, Hurston was against
      <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">
       Franklin D. Roosevelt
      </a>
      's New Deal policies. She also shared his opposition to Roosevelt and
      <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">
       Truman
      </a>
      's interventionist foreign policy. In the original draft of her autobiography,
      <i>
       Dust Tracks on a Road
      </i>
      , Hurston compared the United States government to a "fence" in stolen goods and to a
      <a href="/wiki/Mafia" title="Mafia">
       Mafia
      </a>
      -like protection racket. Hurston thought it ironic that the same people who claim that it is a noble thing to die for freedom and democracy ... wax frothy if anyone points out the inconsistency of their morals.... We, too, consider machine gun bullets good laxatives for heathens who get constipated with toxic ideas about a country of their own. She was scathing about those who sought "freedoms" for those abroad but denied it to people in their home countries: Roosevelt "can call names across an ocean" for his
      <a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms" title="Four Freedoms">
       Four Freedoms
      </a>
      , but he did not have the courage to speak even softly at home.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeing_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Seeing-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When Truman
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">
       dropped the atomic bombs on Japan
      </a>
      she called him the Butcher of Asia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beito_48-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beito-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Hurston opposed the
      <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">
       Supreme Court
      </a>
      ruling in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_v._Board_of_Education" title="Brown v. Board of Education">
        Brown v. Board of Education
       </a>
      </i>
      case of 1954. She felt that if separate schools were truly equal (and she believed that they were rapidly becoming so), educating black students in physical proximity to white students would not result in better education. In addition, she worried about the demise of black schools and black teachers as a way to pass on cultural tradition to future generations of African Americans. She voiced this opposition in a letter, "Court Order Can't Make the Races Mix", that was published in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sentinel" title="Orlando Sentinel">
        Orlando Sentinel
       </a>
      </i>
      in August 1955. Hurston had not reversed her long-time opposition to segregation. Rather, she feared that the Court's ruling could become a precedent for an all-powerful federal government to undermine individual liberty on a broad range of issues in the future.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston also opposed preferential treatment for African-Americans, saying:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       If I say a whole system must be upset for me to win, I am saying that I cannot sit in the game, and that safer rules must be made to give me a chance. I repudiate that. If others are in there, deal me a hand and let me see what I can make of it, even though I know some in there are dealing from the bottom and cheating like hell in other ways.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Root_47-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Root-47">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         47
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_plagiarism">
       Criticism and plagiarism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zora_Neale_Hurston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism and plagiarism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Darwin Turner, one of Hurston's biographers, faulted her for racism for opposing integration, and for opposing programs to guarantee blacks the right to work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other authors criticized Hurston for her sensationalist representation of voodoo.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Crisis" title="The Crisis">
        The Crisis
       </a>
      </i>
      , Harold Preece criticized Hurston for her perpetuation of "Negro primitivism" in order to advance her own literary career.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_Negro_History" title="Journal of Negro History">
        Journal of Negro History
       </a>
      </i>
      complained that her work on voodoo was an indictment of African American ignorance and supersition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Jeffrey Anderson states that Hurston's research methods were questionable, and that she fabricated material for her works on voodoo. He observed that she admitted inventing dialogue for her book
      <i>
       Mules and Men
      </i>
      in a letter to Ruth Benedict. She described fabricating the
      <i>
       Mules and Men
      </i>
      story of rival voodoo doctors as a child in her later autobiography. Anderson observes that many of Hurston's other claims in her voodoo writings are dubious as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Several authors have observed that Hurston engaged in significant plagiarism in at least three works. Her article "Cudjo's own story of the last African slaver" was only 25% original, the rest being plagiarized.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hurston also plagiarized much of her work on voodoo.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
