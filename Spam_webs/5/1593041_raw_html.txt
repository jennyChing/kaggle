               In 1951 a poor woman's cells were taken without permission, and they revolutionised medicine
              </div>

               <p>
                <em>
                 Henrietta Lacks' immortal cells are one of the most important and prolific tools in medicine, used in developing the polio vaccine, cloning and gene mapping.
                </em>

               <p>
                Henrietta Lacks was a 31-year-old mother of five when she died of cervical cancer on 4 October 1951. While the poor tobacco farmer was being treated at the John Hopkins Hospital, two samples of her cervix were removed - a healthy part and a cancerous part - without her permission or knowledge.
               </p>
               <p>
                These cells were passed onto Dr George Otto Gey, who discovered they could do something never seen before in humans cells - be kept alive and keep growing indefinitely. Before this, cultured cells had only survived a few days in the lab.
               </p>
               <p>
                Gey was able to isolate one specific cell and start the first ever immortal cell line,
                <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HeLa">
                 which he named HeLa
                </a>
                after Henrietta.
               </p>
               <p>
                <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/science/2010/jun/23/henrietta-lacks-cells-medical-advances">
                 Since then these cells have been exposed to toxins, viruses and raidation, sent into space and replicated countless times
                </a>
                . They've been involved in thousands of medical breakthroughs and helped to develop the polio vaccine, cloning and gene mapping.
               </p>
               <p>
                Scientists have grown around 20 tons of HeLa cells, and there are almost 11,000 patents involving HeLa cells.
                <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/science/2010/jun/23/henrietta-lacks-cells-medical-advances">
                 One researcher has estimated
                </a>
                that if you laid all the HeLa cells in existence end-to-end, they'd wrap around the planet at least three times.
               </p>
               <p>
                In 2010, a tube of HeLa cells was selling
                <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/science/2010/jun/23/henrietta-lacks-cells-medical-advances">
                 for around US$260
                </a>
                .
                <a href="http://www.smithsonianmag.com/science-nature/henrietta-lacks-immortal-cells-6421299/?page=1">
                 But unfortunately Henrietta's family were never compensated
                </a>
                for her donation and most of them could't afford health insurance.
               </p>
               <p>
                Journalist Rebecca Skloot wrote a best-selling book
                <em>
                 <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1400052181?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creativeASIN=1400052181&amp;linkCode=xm2&amp;tag=smithsonianco-20">
                  The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks
                 </a>
                </em>
                on the subject in 2010.
               </p>
               <p>
                After reading it, Dr Roland Pattillo of the Morehouse School of Medicine in the US donated a headstone for Lacks. For decades, her grave had been unmarked.
               </p>
               <p>
                The headstone, which is shaped like a book, reads:
               </p>
               <p>
                <em>
                 Henrietta Lacks, August 01, 1920  October 04, 1951.
                </em>
               </p>
               <p>
                <em>
                 In loving memory of a phenomenal woman, wife and mother who touched the lives of many.
                </em>
               </p>
               <p>
                <em>
                 Here lies Henrietta Lacks (HeLa). Her immortal cells will continue to help mankind forever.
                </em>
               </p>
               <p>
                <em>
                 Eternal Love and Admiration, From Your Family
                </em>
               </p>
               <p>
                Thank you, Henrietta.
               </p>
