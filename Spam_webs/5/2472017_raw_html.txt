		&lt;h1 class="entry-title"&gt;Earth Day: Nature to Celebrate With Meteor Shower&lt;/h1&gt;

		&lt;ul class="post-details clearfix"&gt;
            &lt;li&gt;5 years ago by &lt;a href="http://www.blisstree.com/author/blisstreestaff/" title="Posts by Blisstree Staff" rel="author"&gt;Blisstree Staff&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/li&gt;
            &lt;li&gt;&lt;a href="http://www.blisstree.com/2010/04/21/sex-relationships/earth-day-nature-to-celebrate-with-meteor-shower/#respond" title="Comment on Earth Day: Nature to Celebrate With Meteor Shower"&gt;&lt;span class="dsq-postid" rel="175367 http://www.blisstree.com/?p=175367"&gt;No Comments &lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/li&gt;
			&lt;li&gt;
                &lt;a href="#top" onclick="show_modal_window('share-tip-window');"&gt;Share a Tip&lt;/a&gt;			&lt;/li&gt;
            &lt;li&gt;&lt;/li&gt;
        &lt;/ul&gt;
	&lt;/header&gt;&lt;!-- .entry-header --&gt;

	&lt;div class="entry-content"&gt;
		&lt;p&gt;&lt;a href="http://www.blisstree.com/files/2010/04/meteor1.jpg" &gt;&lt;img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-175382" title="95371708" src="http://www.blisstree.com/files/2010/04/meteor1.jpg" alt="" width="506" height="338" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;While we mortals are busy doing our part this &lt;a href="http://www.blisstree.com/tag/Earth-Day-2010/" &gt;Earth Day&lt;/a&gt;, nature will be putting on a performance for us in the night sky, perhaps as a reminder of what all this Earth Day stuff is for. The annual Lyrid meteor shower will reach its peak on April 22  when we&#8217;re all feeling our earthiest. (Opening night was April 16 and the free show will close on April 25.) Astronomer Anthony Cook told &lt;em&gt;&lt;a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2010/04/100419-lyrids-meteor-shower-earth-day-peak/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://news.nationalgeographic.com']);"&gt;National Geographic&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/em&gt; that an average Lyrid meteor shower &#8220;produces between 10 and 20 meteors per hour, but occasionally these rates increase to 90 per hour.&#8221;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;So when and where can we catch a glimpse of the Lyrids? Cook advises watching &#8220;around the time of moonset (April 22 between 1 a.m. and 2 a.m., local time) and dawn.&#8221; The Lyrids will be more visible in the Northern Hemisphere than in the Southern Hemisphere, and it&#8217;s best to avoid urban areas. Cook also suggests finding an open horizon, &#8220;facing anywhere from north to east and gazing nearly overhead.&#8221; We hope you enjoy the show.&lt;/p&gt;
