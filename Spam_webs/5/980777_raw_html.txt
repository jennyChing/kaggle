    <p align="left" class="MsoNormal" style="text-align:left">
     <b>
      <font color="#000000">
       METHODS
OF LEARNING MORSE CODE
       <o:p>
        -
        <a href="http://ac6v.com/DXSAMPLE.htm">
         FROM AC6V's Book - DX101x
        </a>
       </o:p>
      </font>
     </b>
    </p>
    <font color="#000000">
     <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
      <span style="font-size:
11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
       There is
       <u>
        no one way to learn the code
       </u>
       that will work for everyone. Beware of the ham who says here is a sure fire
way to learn the code. Here are several methods  choose the one that works
best for you.
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
       <br/>
       <br/>
      </span>
      <b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        AUDIBLE
       </span>
      </b>
      <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
       - Probably the most popular and effective method. For many, the instant recognition of a
SOUND and the association with a character (letter, number, punctuation, prosign)
or whole word is usually
recommended. The less interpretation needed between the initial hearing and
recognition, the better.As you progress - try not to repeat the sound --
just strive for instant recognition.
      </span>
      <b>
       <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
       </span>
      </b>
     </p>
     <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
      <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
       Most
      </span>
      <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
       USA tests are given in the Farnsworth method - that is 12 WPM character speed, 5 WPM overall speed.
BEFORE ANYTHING -- check with your local testing group FIRST to determine if you are tested in Farnsworth -- then study that way --
many who have
studied straight 5 WPM
       <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
       </span>
       - couldn't
copy a darn thing in Farnsworth.
       <o:p>
       </o:p>
      </span>
     </p>
    </font>
    <font color="#000000" face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
     Super Morse has provision for
Farnsworth. Also see
     <a href="#farns">
      Farnsworth
     </a>
     below.
    </font>
    <font color="#0000FF" face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
     <font color="#000000">
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
        <font size="3">
         <b>
          MUSICALLY
OR LANGUAGE ASSOCIATED --
         </b>
        </font>
        S
       </span>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        ome learn musically or language associated- some learn it as a song or word --
        <br/>
        dah dah ditit -- The zebra did it - letter Z.
        <br/>
        dah dah
di dah here comes the bride -
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
         she is a
        </span>
        queen
- letter Q.
        <br/>
        Dog did it," "dah-di-di!" Letter D - See Code Quick
        <br/>
        <br/>
        One musician recommends: "As for the music and CW, it may appeal to you. It's the rhythm
that does it. Tap your foot to a 4 x 1 cadence.Then tap your finger on the desk
in sequence with it. Tap it three times and you have a "S" tap it four
times you have a "H" tap it five times you have a
"5". Now do this while keeping the foot going: Send a "V"
di di dit dah. Keep in cadence. Catch the rhythm? great
isn't it?
       </span>
       "
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         VISUAL
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        -- some learn best by seeing it on the screen as they hear it.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         AUDIBLE
- INVERSE
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        -
Some study the opposites A (didah) then N (dahdit), K then R, etc.
        <o:p>
         Some find this confusing -- try it if it works for you.
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         AUDIBLE
PHONETICS
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        -
Some learn by associating it with the NATO Alphabet
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        i.e, dit-dah followed by A or ALPHA after a while you will hear dit dah
and A or alpha pops into the brain.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         PATTERNS
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        -There are patterns in morse code letters. For example: the letter A is similar
to the letter W except that the letter W has an additional dah to it. One might
study A (didah), W (didadah), J (didahdahdah), 1(didahdahdahdah) in order, then mixed. This is effective if one has difficulty
in discerning J from 1 etc.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         TYPING
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        - some find that they have an automatic reflex to hearing a character and
hitting the right key on a keyboard. Check with your VEC if they will allow you
to take the test this way. One of our local examiners does allow this as well as
a Braille keyboard for the blind.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         SENDING
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         Some folks learn better by sending with a key. Others read signs while
driving on the highway and mentally send the characters. This is usually in conjunction with a listening method.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         CHARACTERS
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        - at 5 WPM (non-farnsworth) it is feasible to repeat (or count) the dits and dahs in your head
and pass the test --- this is the hard way and Farnsworth makes this tough 
NOT RECOMMENDED but may be the only way for some.
        <o:p>
         Best to have the characters sent at 12 wpm with 5 wpm spacing. Below this
speed, the mind perceives the sound of the individual elements, and not the
sound of the whole element.
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         PLAIN
TEXT VS MIXED RANDOM CHARACTERS.
         <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
         </span>
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        Studying
Morse in plain text gives the advantage of being able to anticipate what is
coming next where mixed random characters  ya gotta know them all. Maybe
practice both if that works for you.
        <b>
         <o:p>
         </o:p>
        </b>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         <a href="http://www.g4fon.net/">
          KOCH
METHOD
         </a>
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        -
Learn morse code using high speed random characters.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         <a name="farns">
         </a>
         FARNSWORTH
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        -
       </span>
       Farnsworth morse is composed of
       <i>
        higher
       </i>
       speed characters
sent with
       <i>
        longer than standard spacing
       </i>
       between them. This means that the
mind gets used to hearing the "right sounds" while having plenty of
time to think about them.
       <i>
        All
       </i>
       the great code schools of the past
used Farnsworth teaching.
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoList" style="margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;line-height:
normal;mso-pagination:widow-orphan;page-break-after:auto;tab-stops:.5in">
       <b>
        <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
         BRAIN
SOAK
        </span>
       </b>
       <span style="font-size:11.0pt;mso-bidi-font-size:10.0pt">
        
Some folks give up on methods and just plain listen to code practice until one
day it just all comes together. The impediment to increasing speed and
proficiency is called a barrier which you may feel you will never hurdle, but is
amazing when one day after brain soaking  it magically all comes together.
Your mileage may vary.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoList" style="margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;line-height:
normal;mso-pagination:widow-orphan;page-break-after:auto;tab-stops:.5in">
       <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
        <b>
         COMPUTER
PROGRAMS AND COURSES
        </b>
        - There are many available -- see
        <b>
         <a href="http://ac6v.com/morseprograms.htm">
          Morse
Programs
         </a>
        </b>
        and try them out -- choose the one that works the best for you.
Remember that the one that works for your buddy or elmer may or may not be the
best for you.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoList" style="margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;line-height:
normal;mso-pagination:widow-orphan;page-break-after:auto;tab-stops:.5in">
       <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
        <font size="4">
         FOR
MANY --
         <font color="#FF0000">
          <b>
           studying EVERY day for 15 to 30 minutes is
successful
          </b>
         </font>
         . Leaving long periods between study sessions is usually
counter-productive. Studying for long periods at a session - frazzles many a
brain.
        </font>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoList" style="margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;line-height:
normal;mso-pagination:widow-orphan;page-break-after:auto;tab-stops:.5in">
       <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
        One
long time code instructor advises; "Most people's problems with the code
stem from trying to learn it too fast without sufficient drilling before they
move on to each new letter. The average person needs about 30 hours of
study and practice on the code to hit 5 wpm. Now this is an average.
Some will take longer and some will take less." So a half hour in the
morning, a half hour in the evening and in a month -- you should have it.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoList" style="margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;line-height:
normal;mso-pagination:widow-orphan;page-break-after:auto;tab-stops:.5in">
       <span style="font-size: 11.0pt; mso-bidi-font-size: 10.0pt">
        Another
advises: Whatever method you use, try to *hear* the code directly as a letter.
IOW, don't try to translate the sound into dot-dash and translate that into A,
just hear the dot-dash and think A. Easier said than done, but when you
master it your receiving speed will increase dramatically.
       </span>
      </p>

EN-US;mso-bidi-language:AR-SA'>

LEARNER IS ENCOURAGED TO TRY THE METHODS AND PROGRAMS THAT GIVE THE BEST RESULTS.

        PASSING THE CODE
TEST From W6YOO, Escondido, CA VE
       </b>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoBodyText">
       (of ourse, code is no longer required to pass, but we're keeping the following for historical sake)
      </p>
      <font color="#0000ff">
       <font color="#0000ff" face="Verdana, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
        <font color="#000000">
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           NOTE: Following is a typical exam handout 
sheet used in San Diego. Before you study the code or
          </b>
          <b>
           prepare for a test, 
call YOUR VEC and get the latest information. You will want to know in what 
manner to study, note that in the example, code characters are sent at 13 wpm, 
with the spacing adjusted for overall 5 wpm speed  study accordingly is 
recommended. The VECs in your particular area can be found at URL:
           <a href="http://www.arrl.org/arrlvec/veclist.html">
            http://www.arrl.org/arrlvec/veclist.html
           </a>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           <span style="TEXT-TRANSFORM: none; FONT-FAMILY: 'Times New Roman'">
            <o:p>
            </o:p>
           </span>
           This is a typical
           <b style="COLOR: black; BACKGROUND-COLOR: #a0ffff">
            Morse
           </b>
           code test as given by our local Volunteer Examiners in San Diego, 
California. It is presented here as a matter of what to expect when you take the 
test.
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           When you put the earphones on and the tape 
is started, you will hear one minute of practice to help you loosen up. This 
will also give you the opportunity to make sure the volume is set high enough to 
allow you to hear the characters being sent. An example of the one-minute 
practice might be like the following:
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
           V V V The following is one minute of practice at 5 wpm to allow for adjustment 
of the volume.
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
           Following the one minute of practice, there will be a slight pause followed by 
text sent by one operator in a typical QSO. What follows is a sample of what you 
might hear:
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           V V V WA7VXB/8 de N3YZW BT RRR Thanks 
Roger for the nice report. Your report is RST 569 ? 569 here in Gambrills, MD. 
My name is Julles and I work as a Keypunch Operator. My age is 30 and I am 
married and have 2 children. The rig is a Yaesu 101B putting out 75 watts to a 2 
element Quad antenna. I feed the antenna with 50 ohm coaxial cable. The weather 
here is warm and breezy, temperature is 74 degrees. So how copy? AR WA7VXB/8 de 
N3YZW SK
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           You will note that the sample QSO contains 
all of the alphabet letters, numbers 1 thru 0, punctuation such as the period, 
comma, question mark, slant bar, and the prosigns BT, AR, and SK. All of these 
are required by the FCC to demonstrate your knowledge of the
           <b style="COLOR: black; BACKGROUND-COLOR: #a0ffff">
            Morse
           </b>
           Code. The actual 
QSO you hear will be approximately five minutes in duration.
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           Following your listening to the QSO, we 
will check your copy looking for a string of 25 characters in a row without 
error. If you have copied 25 characters in a row without error, you pass. If 
not, you will be provided with an answer sheet with ten questions about the QSO 
you just copied. If you answer seven (7) or more correctly, you pass. Each 
letter in the text counts as one (1) character, numbers, punctuation and 
prosigns count as two. If you have the appropriate number of characters in a row 
correct, you pass the code test. As you see, you have two chances to pass
          </b>
          .
          <o:p>
          </o:p>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What are the questions like? Following are 
some examples, along with the
           <font color="#ff0000">
            correct answer
           </font>
           :
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What is the callsign of the station being 
called?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            WA7VXB/8
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What is the callsign of the calling 
station?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            N3YZW
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What is the name of the operator being 
called?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            Roger
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What is the name of the calling operator?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            Julles
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What is the calling operators job?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            Keypunch Operator
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What make and model radio is he using?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            Yaesu l01B
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What kind of antenna is he using?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            2 Element Quad
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What kind of feedline is he using?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            50 Ohm Coaxial Cable
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           How many children does he have?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            2
           </font>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           What is the temperature at his 
location?
           <font color="#ff0000">
            74 Degrees
           </font>
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           You will note that there is emphasis on
           <font color="#ff0000">
            callsigns
           </font>
           and
           <font color="#ff0000">
            numbers
           </font>
           , 
pay close attention to the make and model of the radio being used and the 
antenna system. Remember which is the calling station and the station being 
called. Some have had the calls correct, but reversed them on the answer sheet. 
READ THE QUESTIONS CAREFULLY!
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           Remember, your first chance at passing is 
your text. Go back over it carefully, you are going to have some blank spots. 
Use your best judgment as to what is missing. Remember, state abbreviations will 
probably be sent as two letters, e.g. CA not CAL or Calif. We will start 
counting with the first correct character and continue until you miss. We will 
start again at the next correct character and continue until you miss again. We 
do this until the end of the text, counting letters as one (1), numbers and 
punctuation, and prosigns as two (2) characters. If we find enough correct in a 
string, i.e. 25 you pass.
          </b>
          <o:p>
          </o:p>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           Suppose your text looked like this before 
you went back over it to see if you could fill in where you missed a character: 
"The rig i_, a Ya_su 101B put _g out 75 w___s to a 2 el_m_nt Q __d ant__na."
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           That line is sixty-six (66) characters 
long. When you go back over your text, don't you think you could fill in the 
blanks? If you know rig names and models, types of antennas, feed lines, etc. 
you should have no problems. Another thing -- penmanship is important, write or 
print your letters and numbers carefully, so we can read your writing. Block 
printing is best, but try not to lose out because a cursive "c" looked like an 
"e". It is not pleasant for us to have you miss out by a single character.
           <o:p>
           </o:p>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
          <b>
           If you have any specific questions, be sure 
to ask one of the Volunteer Examiners before your
           <b style="COLOR: black; BACKGROUND-COLOR: #a0ffff">
            Morse
           </b>
           Code Receiving Test 
begins. We wish you the best of luck and look forward to hearing you on the 
bands. YOUR VOLUNTEER EXAMINER TEAM
          </b>

      <p align="left">
       <b>
        <font color="#0000FF">
         <a name="CWOP">
         </a>
         CW OPERATING PROCEDURES -- From AC6V's Book
         <a href="http://ac6v.com/DXSAMPLE.htm">
          DX101X
         </a>
        </font>
       </b>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoBodyText">
       <b>
        A1-1.
        <span style="font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-size: 7.0pt; font-family: Times New Roman">
        </span>
        CW TUTORIAL
        <!--[if supportFields]&gt;&lt;span style='mso-element:
field-begin'&gt;&lt;/span&gt; XE &quot;CW TUTORIAL&quot; &lt;![endif]-->
        <!--[if supportFields]&gt;&lt;span
style='mso-element:field-end'&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;![endif]-->
       </b>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal">
       <b>
        In addition to the quickie DXpedition and
contest CW contacts, you can work a lot of DX in a one-on-one QSO.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        A lot of common DX is available on CW, with very little competition. For
example, the mob will pileup on a European station on phone, but on CW on many
occasions you can hear that same country calling CQ with no takers. An excellent
CW Tutorial can be found by Jack Wagoner WB8FSV at URL:
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        http://www.netwalk.com/~fsv/CWguide.htm
        <span style='font-family:"Times New Roman";text-transform:none'>
         <o:p>
         </o:p>
         <br/>
        </span>
        Lets take it step by step.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </b>
      </p>
      <ol start="1" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Have a list of CW Abbreviations, Prefixes, and Q-Signals
    handy. Some DX stations cannot converse in English but you both can get the
    essentials across with Q-Signals.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="2" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Know how to zero beat a CW signal. Many stations have
    very narrow filters and you want to be in their bandpass. Refer to the
    operating manual for your radio.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="3" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Know how to use your RIT, XIT, Dual VFOs, and CW filters.
    See Chapter 2.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="4" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Listen for a DX station calling CQ or wait until they have
    finished with a QSO.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="5" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Good operators will send KN as a turn over, which is go
    ahead, over, others keep out. Sending just K opens it for others to break
    in and this is OK if that is desired.
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          SK
    is the signoff that should be used or CL (clear) if closing your
    station.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="6" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Give a call in 1 X 2 call format
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          --
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DX11DX
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DE
          <span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">
          </span>
          WZ9UUU
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          WA9UUU AR
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          (The
    AR is a prosign sent as one character, i.e. didahdidahdit and means that I
    am through with this transmission). The DX station knows their call, so send
    it once. Sending your call twice allows the other station to hear it, then
    confirm it.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="7" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          If you make the connection, the usual follow up is his or
    her signal report, repeated twice if the contact is shaky and weak, then
    your name and QTH. Dont send more than that on the first round. Turn it
    back to the DX with a K or KN. This will allow the two of you to evaluate if
    a QSO is sustainable or desirable.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="8" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          On the next over, ask about QSL information if you want it,
    before the band slips out.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="9" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          If no DX is calling CQ, but the band seems open, find a
    clear frequency and listen for a bit, if clear, then send QRL?
          <span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">
          </span>
          QRL asks is the frequency busy? If someone responds with
    C, or QRL, no need to respond and clutter up the frequency. If no response
    to your QRL, repeat a couple of times and then call CQ. Sending just QRL
    without your callsign is against the rules, but most do it anyway.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="10" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Calling CQ is typically in a 3 X 2 format
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          CQ CQ CQ
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DE
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          WZ7UUU
          <span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">
          </span>
          WZ7UUU
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          K.
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          Long long
    CQs are likely to be ignored. Dont use AR instead of K as it means
    ending the transmission, and not an invitation for an answer. KN is a turn
    over to the station you are already working in a QSO
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="11" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Listen for a few seconds using RIT to check for off
    frequency responses. If you have a narrow CW filter in line, use RIT and
    tune up and down from your transmit frequency to determine if someone is
    responding.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="12" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Repeat your CQ or QSY to a clear frequency, as you may be on
    a Big Gun frequency that cant hear you.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="13" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          After the initial contact, it is typically
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DX11DX
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DE
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
           WX6DDD
          </span>
          GM (GA, GE)
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          OM
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          TNX
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          FER
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          CALL
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          UR
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          RST ###
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          (339, 599, 549,
    etc.) NAME
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          HR
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          IS
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          ROD
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          ROD.
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          QTH
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          IS
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          SAN
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DIEGO, CA.
          <span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">
          </span>
          SAN
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DIEGO,
    CA. HW?
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          AR
          <span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">
          </span>
          DX11DX
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DE
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          WX6DDD
          <span style="mso-spacerun:
     yes">
          </span>
          K (OR) KN. See your list of abbreviations if you are not
    familiar with these.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="14" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          DX11DX returns with essentially the same info, you may get
    R indicating that DX11DX copied all, or QSL on all is sometimes sent.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="15" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          The next round is an invitation to rag chew. If DX11DX is
    too fast for you, send a QRS (send slower please). Longer QSOs usually
    include your station configuration, the weather (WX), jobs, ages, etc. You
    may receive an invitation to operate QSK (break-in) where the QSO is much
    more conversational. Practice with a friend first as this takes some getting
    used to and proper equipment settings.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="16" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          It is not necessary to do a
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          (DX11DX
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          DE
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          WD6YYY) every time except every 10 minutes of course. When you turn
    it over  you can use BK or just KN or K.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="17" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          A signoff looks like this:
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.5in">
       <b>
        DX11DX DE WF6TTT, FB
VLAD TNX NICE QSO HPE CUL VY 73 GM
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        SK
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        DX11DX
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        DE
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        WF6TTT
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Use SK or CL (Closing
Station) on your final transmission not AR or K (N)
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </b>
      </p>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.5in">
       <b>
        Then there are some
cuties signoffs
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        dit dit,
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        and a response of
        <span style="mso-spacerun:
yes">
        </span>
        dit. Old Military types use dit dita dit dit (Shave and a
Haircut) with the response of dit dit (2 Bits)!
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </b>
      </p>
      <ol start="18" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          For contests, a common CQ is test AC6V test.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="19" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Tail-ending. Wait until another QSO is complete, and then
    call the station you want to contact.
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <ol start="20" style="margin-top:0in" type="1">
       <li class="MsoNormal" style="mso-list:l9 level1 lfo40;tab-stops:list .5in">
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Breaking into a QSO is not commonly done on CW and should be
    approached with caution. If it obvious that two old friends are in
    conversation, it is not advisable. If the exchanges include KN  its a
    signal that others are not welcome, best wait until the QSO is over and then
    tail-end. The standard break-in method on CW is to wait between
    transmissions and then send "BK" for break, or
          <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
          </span>
          "BK de WT8III".
         </b>
        </p>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p align="left" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.25in">
       <b>
        It is very common to
send RST reports in abbreviated form, for example 599, is sent as 5NN.
"N" in place of the number "9". Also another time saver is
for the zero using a long "T". "T" is sent in place of the
number zero as in " POWER HR IS 3TT WATTS". There is a number code for
all numbers; however,the N and T codes are the most common ones.
        <br/>
        Also CW stations sometimes report their zones as "A4" or
"A5" instead of sending "14" or "15".
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        1 = A, 2 = U, 3 = V, 4 = 4,
5 = E, 6 = 6, 7 = B, 8 = D, 9 =
N, 0 = T
       </b>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-left:.25in">
       <b>
        <font face="Arial" size="2">
         <a name="SP">
          <font color="#0000FF">
           <img border="0" height="32" src="images/jetbar.gif" width="400"/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
          </font>
         </a>
         <font color="#0000FF">
          <a name="BW">
          </a>
         </font>
        </font>
        <a name="SP">
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          CW Bandwidth = wpm X 4 (e.g., 40 WPM = 160 Hz)
          <br/>
          From the ARRL License Manual 1976:
          <br/>
          "With proper shaping, the necessary keying bandwidth is equal to 4
          <br/>
          times the speed in words per minute for International Morse Code;
          <br/>
          e.g. at 25 words per minute, the bandwidth is approximately 100 cycles.
         </font>
        </a>
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         "
        </font>
       </b>
      </p>
      <p align="left">
       <a name="MS">
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         Character Spacing and Calculating Morse Code Speed
        </font>
        <p align="left">
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          The word PARIS is the standard to determine CW code speed. Each dit is one element, each dah is three elements, intra-character spacing is one element, inter-character spacing is three elements and inter-word spacing is seven elements. The word PARIS is exactly 50 elements.
          <br/>
          Note that after each dit/dah of the letter P -- one element spacing is used except the last one. (Intra-Character).
          <br/>
          After the last dit of P is sent, 3 elements are added (Inter-Character). After the word PARIS - 7 elements are used.
          <br/>
          Thus:
          <br/>
          P
          <br/>
          di da da di
          <br/>
          1 1 3 1 3 1 1 (3) =
         </font>
         <font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">
          14 elements
         </font>
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          <br/>
          A
          <br/>
          di da
          <br/>
          1 1 3 (3) =
         </font>
         <font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">
          8 elements
         </font>
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          <br/>
          R
          <br/>
          di da di
          <br/>
          1 1 3 1 1 (3) =
         </font>
         <font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">
          10 elements
         </font>
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          <br/>
          I
          <br/>
          di di
          <br/>
          1 1 1 (3) =
         </font>
         <font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">
          6 elements
         </font>
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          <br/>
          S
          <br/>
          di di di
          <br/>
          1 1 1 1 1 [7] =
         </font>
         <font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">
          12 elements
         </font>
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          <br/>
          Total =
         </font>
         <font color="#FF0000" face="Arial" size="2">
          50 elements
         </font>
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          <br/>
          () = intercharacter
          <br/>
          [] = interword
         </font>
        </p>
        <p align="left">
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          If you send PARIS 5 times in a minute (5WPM) you have sent 250 elements (using correct spacing). 250 elements into 60 seconds per minute = 240 milliseconds per element.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p align="left">
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          13 words-per-minute is one element every 92.31 milliseconds.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p align="left">
         <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
          The Farnsworth method sends the dits and dahs and intra-character spacing at a higher speed, then increasing the inter-character and inter-word spacing to slow the sending speed down to the overall speed. For example, to send at 5 wpm with 13 wpm characters in Farnsworth method, the dits and intra-character spacing would be 92.3 milliseconds, the dah would be 276.9 milliseconds, the inter-character spacing would be 1.443 seconds and inter-word spacing would be 3.367 seconds.
         </font>

          <p align="left">

       <p align="center">
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         <a name="RST">
         </a>
        </font>
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="3">
         The RST (Readability-Strength-Tone)
System
        </font>
       </p>
       <p align="left">
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         The RST System of Signal Reporting has been used for years (circa 1934) as a shorthand method of reporting
Readability, Signal Strength and for CW, Tone (i.e., quality of the CW tone). For voice contacts only the R and S are used. The S component is usually not the same as your S-Meter reading as most S-Meters aren't calibrated to track the RST System. The RST is also reported on QSL Cards and must be filled in correctly -- e.g., a 569 report for a Voice Contact is invalid. Note that many DX operations and contest stations merely report 59(9) as a convenience to
avoid having to log each of the real reports. A questionable practice but a fact of DXing/Contesting
        </font>
        <font size="0">
         <hr/>
        </font>
       </p>
       <div align="left">
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         READABILITY
         <br/>
         1 -- Unreadable
         <br/>
         2 -- Barely readable, occasional words distinguishable
         <br/>
         3 -- Readable with considerable difficulty
         <br/>
         4 -- Readable with practically no difficulty
         <br/>
         5 -- Perfectly readable
        </font>
       </div>
       <div align="left">
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         <br/>
         SIGNAL STRENGTH
         <br/>
         1 -- Faint signals, barely perceptible
         <br/>
         2 -- Very weak signals
         <br/>
         3 -- Weak signals
         <br/>
         4 -- Fair signals
         <br/>
         5 -- Fairly good signals
         <br/>
         6 -- Good signals
         <br/>
         7 -- Moderately strong signals
         <br/>
         8 -- Strong signals
         <br/>
         9 -- Extremely strong signals
         <br/>
         <br/>
         TONE 1 -- Sixty cycle a.c. or less, very rough and broad
         <br/>
         2 -- Very rough a.c. , very harsh and broad
         <br/>
         3 -- Rough a.c. tone, rectified but not filtered
         <br/>
         4 -- Rough note, some trace of filtering
         <br/>
         5 -- Filtered rectified a.c.but strongly ripple-modulated
         <br/>
         6 -- Filtered tone, definite trace of ripple modulation
         <br/>
         7 -- Near pure tone, trace of ripple modulation
         <br/>
         8 -- Near perfect tone, slight trace of modulation
         <br/>
         9 -- Perfect tone, no trace of ripple or modulation of any kind
        </font>
       </div>
       <p align="left">
        <font color="#000000" face="Arial" size="2">
         Infrequently used is the addition of a letter to the end of the 3 numbers.
         <br/>
         These are: X = the signal is rock steady like a crystal controlled signal;
         <br/>
         C = the signal is chirpy as the frequency varies slightly with keying;
         <br/>
         and K = the signal has key clicks.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         X is from the early days of radio when such steady signals were rare.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Today most all signals could be given an X but it is hardly ever used. It is helpful to report a chirpy or clicky signal by using the C or K, e.g. 579C or 579K.
        </font>
       </p>
       <p align="left">
        <font face="Arial" size="2">
         It is very common to send RST reports in abbreviated
form, for example 599, is sent as 5NN. "N" in place of the number "9". Also
another time saver is for the zero using a long "T". "T" is sent in place of the number
zero as in "POWER HR IS 3TT WATTS". There is a number code for all numbers,
however, the N and T codes are the most common ones.
         <br/>
         Also CW stations sometimes report their zones as "A4" or
"A5" instead of sending "14" or "15".
         <br/>
         <br/>
         1 = A, 2 = U,
3 = V, 4 = 4, 5 = E, 6 = 6, 7 =
B, 8 = D, 9 = N, 0 = T
        </font>
       </p>
