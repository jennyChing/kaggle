&lt;b&gt;Introduction&lt;/b&gt;&lt;br /&gt;

&lt;p class = "a" &gt;
&lt;p class = "a"&gt; Important Note:&lt;br /&gt;
The equations used are mainly derived from the technical information provided in the SDP/SI technical library see links below.&lt;br /&gt;
I
recommend that for serious work the linked information is more suitable.&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
&lt;hr align="center" width="70%" color="#FF0000"&gt;
&lt;p class = "a" &gt;The efficiency of a gear system is simple calculated as the &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p align = "center"&gt;[output shaft power /Input shaft power ].100 %.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p class = "a" &gt;The output power is the (input power - the power losses).&nbsp;&nbsp; Power losses 
in gear systems  are associated primarily with  tooth friction and lubrication churning losses. &nbsp;&nbsp;
Churning losses are relatively independent of the nature of the gears and the gear ratios - they are primarily realated
to the peripheral speed of the gears passing through the fluid.&nbsp;&nbsp;Churning losses are difficult to calculate and
estimates based on experience are often used in initial gear design.&nbsp;&nbsp;  
The frictional losses are  related to  the gear design,the reduction ratio,the pressure 
angle,  gear size, and the coefficient of friction.&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;

The notes below relate primarily to estimating /calculating the the part of the 
efficiency of gear trains which is associated with the tooth friction. &lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
A simple table is provided below showing the efficiencies of various gear types.&nbsp;&nbsp;
These efficiencies related to tooth friction losses only for single tooth meshes.&nbsp;&nbsp;For drive
trains the efficiencies or each mesh in the line is multiplied together ( 2 gears pairs of 90% efficiency result in
a gear train efficiency of 81%.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;b&gt; Epicyclic Gears&lt;/b&gt;

&lt;p class = "a"&gt;Consider the two meshing gears below which are part of an epicyclic gear train.&nbsp;&nbsp;This is provided
as and example of a typical component in an epicyclic gear. &nbsp;&nbsp;The two gears and the arms are rotating
as shown .&lt;/p&gt; 
&lt;p align = "center"&gt;&lt;img src="../../images/gear_eff_3.gif" width="283" height="318" alt="" border="0"&gt; &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p class = "a"&gt;Now it the arm was stationary the contact point P would have a instantaneous velocity
= -&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt; R&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;.  (velocities to the right are positive ).&nbsp;&nbsp;
Now if the whole system was rotating as a rigid assembly with the velocity of the arm 
the instantaneous velocity  -&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;3&lt;/sub&gt; R&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;.&nbsp;&nbsp;Combining these two motions
together, the linear velocity of the tooth engagement (gear 2 -&gt; gear 1)is therefore&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p align = "center"&gt;&lt;big&gt; v&lt;sub&gt;12&lt;/sub&gt; = - ( &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt; R&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt; -

(&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt; - &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;sub&gt;3&lt;/sub&gt;)R &lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;&lt;/big&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p class = "a"&gt; Now the magnitude of the transmitted tangential force F&lt;sub&gt;12&lt;/sub&gt; x the 
tooth engagement velocity v&lt;sub&gt;12&lt;/sub&gt; is called the potential power and the power loss 
due to tooth friction is proportional to this power.&nbsp;&nbsp;Generally for spur gears ( and helical gears) it is sufficient to 
estimate the power loss as 1% of the potential power.&nbsp;For more accurate estimations the
equations above can be used.&lt;/p&gt;

( &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;1&lt;/sub&gt; - &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;3&lt;/sub&gt; ) for gear 2.&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
The actual pitch line velocity of the gear mesh is -(&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;) and 
therefore the ration of the potential power to the actual power is &lt;/p&gt;

greater than the actual power and the losses proportionally greater.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;b&gt;Example Epicyclic Gear efficiency calculation.&lt;/b&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
&lt;p class = "a"&gt;Consider the epicyclic gear chain shown below.&nbsp;&nbsp;
The input speed = 250 RPM (ACW) and the input torque
= 2.5 Nm&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p align = "center"&gt;&lt;img src="../../images/gear_eff_5.gif" width="518"
 height="224" alt="" border="0"&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p class = "a"&gt; &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt; = (250.2.&lt;span&gt;&#960;&lt;/span&gt;)/60 = 26,18rads/s.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;and&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;1&lt;/sub&gt; = 0 &lt;br /&gt;

The power into the gear = T&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt; = 2,5.26,18 = 65 Watts.
Calculating forces &lt;br /&gt;
The torque on the arm 2 =  M2 = 2.5Nm &lt;br /&gt;
The force (F2)  at Radius R2 (= R1 + R3) : = -(M2/R2) : F2  = -2.5 /(0,1m+.025m) = -20 N (forces to right are positive)&lt;br /&gt;
Now all forces and torques on the link between Arm(2) and gears (3) &amp; (4) are in equilibrium therefore &lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p align = "center"&gt;&lt;img src="../../images/gear_eff_6.gif" width="386" height="89" alt="" border="0"&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p align = "center"&gt;&lt;img src="../../images/gear_eff_7.gif" width="384" height="28" alt="" border="0"&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p class = "a"&gt; 
For the gear mesh between gear 3 and the fixed sun 2 the velocity of tooth engagement is calculated
 by the product of the angular velocity of the arm 2 at pitch radius of R2. =   &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;R1 = 26,18.0,075 = 1,96m/s&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
The potential power relates to the gear pair operated in isolation - &lt;br /&gt;
in this case for gear 3 to be in equilibrium (With forces and moments)&lt;br /&gt;
F2.R3 + F3.R3 = 0 and F2.R3 + F3R3 = 0  therefore F3 = -F2 = 20N&lt;br /&gt;

The potential power of the mesh between the sun gear 1 and the planet gear 3 &lt;br /&gt;
= the gear engagement velocity(1,96m/s) x the tangential force (F3= 20N) = 1,96.20 =3,92W&lt;br/ &gt;
Allowing 1% loss on a typical spur gear engagement the power loss at this engagement = 0,39W.&lt;br /&gt; &lt;br /&gt;
For the gear engagement between the gears 4 and 5 the angular velocity of gear five = &lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p align = "center"&gt;&lt;img src="../../images/gear_eff_8.gif" width="337" height="34" alt="" border="0"&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p class ="a"&gt;The velocity of tooth engagement between gear (4) and gear (5) = &lt;br /&gt;
R5 (&lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;5&lt;/sub&gt; - &lt;span&gt;&#969;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;) = 0,068(-5,95- 26,18)=-2.18 m/s&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
The potential power at this tooth engagement = &lt;br /&gt;the gear engagement velocity (2,18 m/s). x the tangential force (F4 = -167N) = 2,18.167 = 364 W &lt;br /&gt;
Assuming 1% power loss then the loss at this tooth engagement = 3,64 W&lt;br /&gt;&lt;br /&gt;
The power input to the epicyclic gear = 65 W therefore the Gear efficiency = 100(1 - (3,64 + 0,39) /65 ) = 94%&lt;/p&gt;
