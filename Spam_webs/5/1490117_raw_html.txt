     <p>
      The
      <b>
       BridgmanStockbarger technique
      </b>
      is named after Harvard physicist
      <a href="/wiki/Percy_Williams_Bridgman" title="Percy Williams Bridgman">
       Percy Williams Bridgman
      </a>
      and MIT physicist
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_C._Stockbarger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Donald C. Stockbarger (page does not exist)">
       Donald C. Stockbarger
      </a>
      (18951952). They are two similar methods primarily used for growing
      <a href="/wiki/Single_crystal" title="Single crystal">
       single crystal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">
       ingots
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Boule_(crystal)" title="Boule (crystal)">
       boules
      </a>
      ), but which can be used for solidifying polycrystalline ingots as well.
     </p>
     <p>
      The methods involve heating polycrystalline material above its melting point and slowly cooling it from one end of its container, where a
      <a href="/wiki/Seed_crystal" title="Seed crystal">
       seed crystal
      </a>
      is located. A single crystal of the same crystallographic orientation as the seed material is grown on the seed and is progressively formed along the length of the container. The process can be carried out in a horizontal or vertical geometry.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Bridgman method is a popular way of producing certain
      <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">
       semiconductor
      </a>
      crystals such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">
       gallium arsenide
      </a>
      , for which the
      <a href="/wiki/Czochralski_process" title="Czochralski process">
       Czochralski process
      </a>
      is more difficult.
     </p>
     <p>
      The difference between the Bridgman
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      technique and Stockbarger
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      technique is subtle: While both methods utilize a temperature gradient and a moving crucible, the Bridgman technique utilizes the relatively uncontrolled gradient produced at the exit of the furnace; the Stockbarger technique introduces a baffle, or shelf, separating two coupled furnaces with temperatures above and below the freezing point. Stockbarger's modification of the Bridgman technique allows for better control over the temperature gradient at the melt/crystal interface.
     </p>
     <p>
      When seed crystals are not employed as described above, polycrystalline ingots can be produced from a feedstock consisting of rods, chunks, or any irregularly shaped pieces once they are melted and allowed to re-solidify. The resultant microstructures of the ingots so obtained are characteristic of directionally solidified metals and alloys with their aligned grains.
     </p>
