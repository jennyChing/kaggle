        Simply perfect pancakes
        <span class="subtitle">

      <div class="entry-content">
       <p>
        The perfect pancake.
       </p>
       <p>
        Fact, or fiction?
       </p>
       <p>
        For such a simple dish  flour, milk, salt, butter, eggs, and leavening  there are certainly a plethora of recipes out there. And not just variations (read: blueberry, chocolate chip); but hundreds and hundreds of recipes for just plain pancakes.
       </p>
       <p>
        And it seems no one is content with the recipe they have. Its a big game of musical chairs out there: Lets go round and round and round on Google, and when the music stops  grab a recipe, any new recipe!
       </p>
       <p>
        Maybe thats why our
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes2008/guaranteed-recipes-overview.html">
         guaranteed
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes/simply-perfect-pancakes-recipe">
         Simply Perfect Pancakes
        </a>
        is one of the top recipe landing pages on our
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipe/">
         online recipe site.
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        To mark todays launch of our
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/RecipeHome">
         newly redesigned site,
        </a>
        I decided to determine the recipe with the most customer hits that hadnt yet been blogged.
       </p>
       <p>
        And this is it: pancakes.
       </p>
       <p>
        Simple, everyday pancakes.
       </p>
       <p>
        Maybe we never blogged it because we figure everyone knows how to make pancakes, and already has a favorite recipe. But given the number of people searching this site for pancake recipe  well, we must have figured wrong.
       </p>
       <p>
        So here it is: yet another pancake recipe. But this one is for perfect pancakes 
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes/simply-perfect-pancakes-recipe">
         Simply Perfect Pancakes.
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        And whats the secret to these particular pancakes?
       </p>
       <p>
        The perfect balance of ingredients:
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/king-arthur-unbleached-all-purpose-flour-5-lb">
         King Arthur Flour
        </a>
        for body, baking powder for rise, eggs for structure, butter for richness, and milk to bring it all together.
       </p>
       <p>
        Plus a certain secret ingredient 
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45312" src="/blog/files/2011/06/IMG_55151.jpg" title="IMG_5515"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/malted-milk-powder-16-oz">
         Malted milk powder
        </a>
        , a richly flavored sweetener that gives these cakes a certain diner-style
        <em>
         je ne sais quoi.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Still hunting for the ultimate pancake recipe? Well, the music has stopped; grab a chair, and give this recipe a try. Your long search may finally be over.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45314" src="/blog/files/2011/06/IMG_5516.jpg" title="IMG_5516"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Put the following in a mixing bowl:
       </p>
       <p>
        2 large eggs
        <br/>
        1 1/4 cups milk*
       </p>
       <p>
        *Start with just 1 cup milk if youre baking under hot, humid conditions, or if youre going to let the batter rest longer than 15 minutes. Use up to 1 1/4 cups milk in cold, dry conditions.
       </p>
       <p>
        Whats the difference? Depending on the amount of milk used the pancake batter will be thicker/thinner, and thus the cakes will be thicker/thinner.
       </p>
       <p>
        In summer, or when its stored in hot, humid conditions, flour acts like a sponge, soaking up moisture from the atmosphere. Thus youll usually use less liquid in your recipes in summer, more in winter.
       </p>
       <p>
        And how about resting the batter? The longer it rests, the more it thickens, as the flour gradually absorbs the liquid.
       </p>
       <p>
        Bottom line: If you prefer thicker pancakes, go easy with the milk. You can always thin the batter with more milk  but its hard to use less milk once youve already added it!
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45322" src="/blog/files/2011/06/IMG_55181.jpg" title="IMG_5518"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Beat the eggs and milk until light and foamy, about 3 minutes at high speed of a stand or hand mixer.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45318" src="/blog/files/2011/06/IMG_5519.jpg" title="IMG_5519"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Whisk together the following:
       </p>
       <p>
        1 1/2 cups
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/king-arthur-unbleached-all-purpose-flour-5-lb">
         King Arthur Unbleached All-Purpose Flour
        </a>
        <br/>
        3/4 teaspoon salt
        <br/>
        2 teaspoons baking powder
        <br/>
        2 tablespoons sugar OR 1/4 cup
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/malted-milk-powder-16-oz">
         malted milk powder
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Stir into the milk/egg. Dont beat; some lumps are OK.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45324" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_5697.jpg" title="IMG_5697"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Add 3 tablespoons melted butter or vegetable oil. Stir it in.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Note: If you read our online recipe for these pancakes, it tells you to add the melted butter/oil to the milk/egg, prior to adding the dry ingredients. I use melted butter (rather than oil), and when I add warm butter to cold milk/egg, it coagulates into little globules that, in my opinion, dont blend into the batter very nicely. Thus I add the melted butter at the end, when the room-temperature flour has warmed the batter up a bit.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45326" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_5701.jpg" title="IMG_5701"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Notice how this batter drips off the spatula in a narrow stream; its pretty thin. Im going to let it rest for at least 15 minutes, while my griddle is heating; itll thicken slightly.
       </p>
       <p>
        Even after a rest, though, the batter will be fairly thin, and the pancakes I make will be on the thin side  which is the way I like them.
       </p>
       <p>
        Heat a heavy frying pan over medium heat, or set an electric griddle to 375F. Lightly grease the frying pan or griddle. The pan or griddle is ready if a drop of water skitters across the surface, evaporating immediately.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45330" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_5707.jpg" title="IMG_5707"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Drop 1/4 cupfuls of batter onto the lightly greased griddle.
       </p>
       <p>
        Cook on one side until bubbles begin to form and break, about 2 minutes.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45332" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_5704.jpg" title="IMG_5704"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Then turn the pancakes and cook the other side until brown, about 1 1/2 to 2 minutes.
       </p>
       <p>
        Turn over only once; lots of flipping will yield tough pancakes.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45334" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_5718.jpg" title="IMG_5718"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Serve immediately. First comes the butter
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-45338" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_5721.jpg" title="IMG_5721"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        then the maple syrup. I prefer
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/grade-b-maple-syrup-1-pint">
         Grade B maple syrup,
        </a>
        for the best, most assertive flavor.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-46826" src="/blog/files/2011/07/IMG_57151.jpg" title="IMG_5715"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Look at that lovely interior! Nice and light.
       </p>
       <p>

           PJ, I make these all of the time! My family loves them, and I use the same recipe without any changes in my waffle iron with rave reviews! I usually warm up some grade B Vermont maple syrup with a cinnamon stick in it and pour it over top. I have also added pumpkin pie spice to the batter and they turn out wonderful! One question though, my kids want chocolate chip waffles and when I make them, the chocolate ends up burning. Is there a way to help prevent burnt chocolate?

           <strong>
            The best way to prevent any burning, be it blueberries, raspberries or chocolate, is to pour some batter in the iron, then sprinkle in fruit or chocolate, then drizzle the rest of the batter over the top so they are buried in the batter and protected from the iron.  I hope this helps.   ~Amy

             Hi, P.J. ah, its so nice to receive a comment back from you! I had these pancakes this morning, as a matter of fact. To save time, I always make up a full batch of whatever and then freeze the remaining amount. Took them out of the freezer while I drank my coffee, microwaved for 60 seconds, spread home-made cherry jam on top with a sprinkling of powdered sugarand NO choking! LOL
            </p>
            <p>
             Regarding the room temperature: even if YOU are a Spur Of The Moment sorta gal (which I most definitely am NOT!), if you first take out the eggs/milk/butter while youre reading the recipe and measuring out everything else, that will help. My work is highly chaotic and unpredictable so at home, I need to plan things out to counter-balance the triage-like work conditions I do during the day. So, I guess its easier for me to say at 6:00 am on a day off, Gee, Id like to make pancakes at 8:00 am, then to say at 8:00 am, Gee, Id like to make pancakes NOW! ~snort~
            </p>
            <p>
             I was also taught by my Gran that  (unless heat is involved, like custard), to immediately put the vanilla into the beaten eggs or butter so it has a chance in deeply infuse into the proteins/fats. It makes the world of a difference with the way an item tastes. You only place vanilla into a product AFTER heat has been done, like cocoa, because vanilla evaporates very quickly and you want the flavour of the vanilla  trapped within the item as it bakes, not lost to evaporation.
            </p>
           </section>
