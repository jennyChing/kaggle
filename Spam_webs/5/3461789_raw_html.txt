            <article id="article-body" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Article" section="articleBody">
             <meta content="http://cnet1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2009/03/13/6c6b1bd2-f8df-11e2-8c7c-d4ae52e62bcc/thumbnail/770x433/635eaa3eaf426805786bb5a722ee7185/DSC_0088.JPG" itemprop="thumbnailUrl"/>
             <meta content="Tech Culture" itemprop="keywords"/>
             <meta content="http://www.cnet.com/news/to-green-the-worlds-buildings-think-retrofits/" itemprop="url"/>
             <div class="article-main-body row" data-component="lazyloadImages" data-use-autolinker="true" itemprop="articleBody">
              <p>
               BOSTON--The cutting edge of building science these days seems to be more about expanding foam than solar power research.
              </p>
              <p>
               Last Wednesday, I stopped by the
               <a data-component="externalLink" href="http://www.buildingenergy.nesea.org/" target="_blank">
                Building Energy Conference
               </a>
               put on by the Northeast Sustainable Energy Association (NESEA). If there was one theme that jumped out, it was
               <a data-component="externalLink" href="http://www.energycircle.com/blog/2009/03/12/building-science-corporation-stresses-the-importance-of-efficiency/" target="_blank">
                energy efficiency
               </a>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               Insulating and air sealing a building, with stuff like expanding foam, has always been a sensible way to lower utility bills. But
               <span class="link" section="shortcodeLink">
                <a href="/news/weatherizing-homes-the-next-big-green-industry/">
                 weatherizing homes
                </a>
               </span>
               is increasingly seen as the first and vital step to perhaps more exciting technologies like solar and wind.
              </p>
              <p>
               <figure class="image image-medium pull-left" section="shortcodeImage">
                <span class="imageContainer">
                 <img alt="" class="" height="auto" itemprop="image" src="http://cnet1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2009/03/13/6c6b1bd2-f8df-11e2-8c7c-d4ae52e62bcc/resize/370xauto/53eda0e36f2d7889666d335607493226/DSC_0088.JPG" width="370"/>
                </span>
                <figcaption>
                 <span class="caption">
                  Needed: more green-tech gear like solar hot water tubes to make existing homes energy-efficient.
                 </span>
                 <span class="credit">
                  Martin LaMonica/CNET
                 </span>
                </figcaption>
               </figure>
              </p>
              <p>
               At the morning keynote discussion, Mark Rosenbaum of building firm Energysmiths, which specializes in energy-efficiency retrofits, argued that "fixing" the millions of existing homes through efficiency will have a far bigger impact on lowering greenhouse gas emissions than any new constructions.
              </p>
              <p>
               "The majority of the potential is in retrofits," he said. "If you're increasing the building stock one percent a year, it's not going to get you anywhere. You have to fix the existing building stock."
              </p>
              <div class="ad-inpage-video-top" data-ad="inpage-video-top" id="inpage-video-top-55a572547827f">
               <script>
                window.console &amp;&amp; console.log &amp;&amp; console.log("ADS: queuing inpage-video-top-55a572547827f for display");
        var cbsiGptDivIds = cbsiGptDivIds || [];
        cbsiGptDivIds.push("inpage-video-top-55a572547827f");
               </script>
              </div>
              <p>
               Installing solar panels on an efficient home will have a bigger impact than on a home that isn't well insulated or doesn't use efficient appliances, he noted.
              </p>
              <p>
               Rosenbaum pointed to several examples of retrofit homes in the New England area that were able to reduce their energy consumption significantly--some over 50 percent. Many steps are relatively easy, such as reducing the parasitic load from appliances, while others require bigger investments and changes to behavior.
              </p>
              <p>
               Realistically, a 70 percent improvement--a goal set in an initiative called the
               <a data-component="externalLink" href="http://www.affordablecomfort.org/initiatives.php?PageID=16" target="_blank">
                Thousand Home Challenge
               </a>
               --is challenging but doable in many cases. Some
               <span class="link" section="shortcodeLink">
                <a href="/news/weatherizing-homes-the-next-big-green-industry/">
                 super-insulated homes
                </a>
               </span>
               --well-sealed homes with insulation on the outside of the structure--with renewable energy systems like solar panels have shown that they can be net producers of energy.
              </p>
              <p>
               At this point, there isn't a standardized way in the U.S. of reporting a building's energy performance. In Germany, the
               <a data-component="externalLink" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passivhaus" target="_blank">
                Passivhaus
               </a>
               standard for air-tight homes that use efficient energy technologies such as
               <span class="link" section="shortcodeLink">
                <a href="/news/tapping-the-earth-for-home-heating-and-cooling/">
                 ground-source heat pumps
                </a>
               </span>
               have set the benchmark around the world.
              </p>
              <p>
               Because of the wide variety of climates, there need to be different techniques for making homes efficient, said Rosenbaum. He sees a very high demand for green technology home products and much more skilled labor in this field.
              </p>
              <p>
               "Where are we going to find enough people who know what they're doing?" Rosenbaum said. "We need a lot of people to get trained. Otherwise, we're going to create a lot of disasters."
              </p>
