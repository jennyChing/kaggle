           <p>
            I have a stress ball at my desk. Its one of the alien-faced
            <a href="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/stress-balls/emoticon-stress-balls.htm" target="_blank">
             Emoticon Stress Balls
            </a>
            . I call him Spot.
           </p>
           <p>
            Because of my time here at QLP, I can tell you a little bit about Spot and his ilk: how theyre made, when polyurethane first came into use, all of that. (Or you can check out this
            <a href="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/history-of-stress-balls-why-theyre-popular/" target="_blank">
             blog post on stress relievers
            </a>
            and get your learn on.) I also can tell you a little bit about the histories of pens, bookmarks, you name it. Who knew? Turns out theres a lot of information on the Internet.
           </p>
           <p>
            One thing I hadnt learned since I started here, however, was where promotional products came fromnot in a How are babies made? kind of way, but a What were the first promotional products ever used? kind of way. Who came up with the idea to slap logos on things, and when? And what things did people first slap their logos on?
           </p>
           <p>
            Lo, a blog post was born.
           </p>
           <h2 style="text-align: center">
            The (Pretty Much) Official History of Promotional Products
           </h2>
           <p>
            Theres an organization called
            <a href="http://www.ppai.org/about/faq">
             PPAI
            </a>
            , or the Promotional Product Association International. Its a trade association within the promotional products industry that provides networking events, classes and certifications, and all of that.
           </p>
           <p>
            According to PPAI,
            <a href="http://www.promotionalproductswork.org/collegiate-corner/history-of-promotional-products" target="_blank">
             the first promotional product ever used
            </a>
            was not a pen, not a stress ball, but a simple button. When George Washington became the first president of the newly formed United States in 1789, commemorative buttons were produced to celebrate the occasion. First president, first promotional productWashington, you trailblazer, you!
           </p>
           <div class="pullquote">
            <div class="tm-tweet-clear">
            </div>
            <div class="tm-click-to-tweet">
             <div class="tm-ctt-text">
              <a href="https://twitter.com/share?text=According+to+PPAI%2C+the+first+promotional+product+ever+used+was+a+simple+button.&amp;via=qualitylogo&amp;related=qualitylogo&amp;url=http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/history-of-promotional-products/" target="_blank">
               According to PPAI, the first promotional product ever used was a simple button.
              </a>
             </div>
             <p>
              <a class="tm-ctt-btn" href="https://twitter.com/share?text=According+to+PPAI%2C+the+first+promotional+product+ever+used+was+a+simple+button.&amp;via=qualitylogo&amp;related=qualitylogo&amp;url=http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/history-of-promotional-products/" target="_blank">
               Click To Tweet
              </a>
             </p>
             <div class="tm-ctt-tip">
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            However, PPAI also notes that nearly a century passed before anyone made a business out of promotional merchandise. It was in 1886 that an Ohio newspaper man named
            <a href="http://www.usbiographies.org/ohio/bios/tuscarawas/jaspermeek.txt" target="_blank">
             Jasper Meek
            </a>
            approached a local shoe store owner with an idea: instead of letting his printing press go idle between editions of the paper, Meek would use it to print a message advertising the store onto bags, which could be given away with every shoe purchase. That way, when kids used the bags to carry their school books, theyd also advertise the store. Sound like a familiar approach?
           </p>
           <p>
            It turned out to be a popular approach. By 1904, representatives from twelve promotional products companies had gathered to form PPAI, which continues to this day to lobby on behalf of the promo products industry. That, in a nutshell, is the history of promo products.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its kind of a tiny nutshell, though, and if you know nothing else about QLP, you should know that this company likes big nuts.
           </p>
           <h2 style="text-align: center">
            Objets de Promotion
           </h2>
           <p>
            Its true; most of the development of the promotional products industry occurred during the 1800s, for reasons Ill discuss later. However, for as long as humans have been able to craft, create, and communicate, theyve been able to advertise using objects. History has some outstanding examples:
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_50027" style="width: 160px">
            <img alt="Babylonian Clay Tablet" class="wp-image-50027 size-thumbnail" height="150" src="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Clay-Tablet-Resized-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             An ancient clay tablet
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Clay tablets and bricks
            </strong>
            . Okay, so theyre not exactly the most practical products, but clay tablets provided an early way for people to pass messages among their groups. Clay bricks were essential to the buildings of
            <a href="http://muse.jhu.edu/login?auth=0&amp;type=summary&amp;url=/journals/asr/v001/1.1presbrey.html" target="_blank">
             the Babylonians around 3000 BC
            </a>
            , and when a ruler had his own personal symbolor brandstamped into one, it pretty much had the effect of saying, This building is brought to you by  (which isnt anyones symbol, just me playing with characters). Fortunately for all of us, promo products have gotten a lot more portable since then. Like
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_50026" style="width: 160px">
            <img alt="The Chi-Rho monogram" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-50026" height="150" src="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Chi-Rho-Resized-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             The Chi-Rho monogram
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Coins and jewelry.
            </strong>
            Late into the Roman Empirewere talking 312 AD, to be exactthe emperor
            <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/history/ancient/romans/romanpropaganda_article_01.shtml" target="_blank">
             Constantine the Great
            </a>
            bid farewell to fragmented paganism and declared that the empire would be united as a Christian one. To spread the word, silversmiths and metalsmiths began incorporating Christian symbolism, such as the cross and
            <a href="http://artmuseum.princeton.edu/collections/objects/24620" target="_blank">
             the Chi-Rho monogram
            </a>
            , into their jewelry and coins. As our sales team will tell you, promo products arent just for promoting sales.
           </p>
           <p>
            Advertising, like scientific thought, really took off thanks to
            <a href="http://www.google.com/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=&amp;esrc=s&amp;source=web&amp;cd=6&amp;ved=0CEcQFjAF&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Facademic.brooklyn.cuny.edu%2Feconomic%2Ffriedman%2FADVDMKTopic1.doc&amp;ei=63LyU4DdBdWcygSpgILQAw&amp;usg=AFQjCNHXkCHY4gZQ3qnSU4uFtVe8r7J1IA&amp;sig2=cHX9FII4pa6VjYcUv0N7nA&amp;bvm=bv.73231344,d.aWw&amp;cad=rja" target="_blank">
             Gutenbergs invention of the printing press
            </a>
            in 1438 and its immeasurable contribution to the spread of ideas. However, as you know, fliers and signs arent the same as promotional products. They dont have any other use in and of themselves.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.designer-daily.com/a-history-of-business-cards-20266" target="_blank">
             Business cards
            </a>
            became popular in Europe during the 1600s, but for this posts purposes, they pose the same problem as fliers: they serve no purpose except to promote the names printed on them. (If only theyd had
            <a href="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/custom-magnets/fourcolorprocess-business-card-magnet.htm" target="_blank">
             business card magnets
            </a>
            back then!)
           </p>
           <p>
            It wasnt until the mid-18
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            and 19
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            centuries, when the Industrial Revolution took hold, that promotional products began to take hold as well.
           </p>
           <h2 style="text-align: center">
            You Say You Want a Revolution
           </h2>
           <p>
            You know that printing press thingamabob I mentioned? It might not have been
            <em>
             directly
            </em>
            responsible for the creation of promotional products, but
            <em>
             indirectly
            </em>
            responsible? Sure seems that way.
           </p>
           <p>
            The mass printing of books made it possible for ideas and innovations in technology to spread to more people more quickly than ever before. Its the reason why the invention of the printing press is considered one of
            <a href="http://www2.fiu.edu/~grenierg/chapter9.htm" target="_blank">
             the main causes of the Industrial Revolution
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            The Industrial Revolution saw human society begin to create even more machines to handle tasks that previously had been done by hand, or refine existing machines (the
            <a href="http://www.princeton.edu/~achaney/tmve/wiki100k/docs/Sewing_machine.html" target="_blank">
             sewing machine
            </a>
            got a kickass upgrade in 1830). Its as if society suddenly was filled with Gutenbergs, and they all had knowledge of how to use power sources like steam and coal to work enormous contraptions that could produce goods and components of goods on an unprecedented scale. Crazy times.
           </p>
           <p>
            There were two especially big
            <a href="http://www.crvp.org/book/series05/v-4/chapter_vi.htm" target="_blank">
             consequences of the Industrial Revolution
            </a>
            :
           </p>
           <ol>
            <li>
             The new, increased capacity for production led to
             <strong>
              the rise of the manufacturer
             </strong>
             , a company responsible strictly for making products.
            </li>
            <li>
             The emergence of multiple manufacturers led to
             <strong>
              the need to establish a brand
             </strong>
             , even more than in times past, to allow one companys product to stand out among a swelling sea of stuff.
            </li>
           </ol>
           <p>
            And those consequences made the 1800s a ripe time not only for advertising in general to flourish but also for promotional products to catch the eye. With the worlds increased manufacturing capabilities, companies didnt have to stick solely to print ads to tell the world about their wares.
           </p>
           <p>
            So what products were among those that were used widely in advertising in 19
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            century society? It certainly wasnt all about bags, though oddly enough, a lot of the examples also begin with a B:
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_50025" style="width: 160px">
            <img alt="Promotional Bookmark" class="wp-image-50025" height="228" src="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Bookmark-Resized.jpg" width="150"/>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             An example of a promotional bookmark
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            *
            <a href="http://www.ephemerasociety.org/examples/ex-bookmarks.html" target="_blank">
             <strong>
              Bookmarks
             </strong>
            </a>
            : Chromolithography, a printing process refined in the middle of the 1800s, allowed printers to reproduce illustrations in glorious color. Advertisers took advantage of that, and of the Victorian worlds interest in reading, by producing full-color bookmarks as free giveaways to promote everything from insurance to perfumes to stoves. Because variety is awesome.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             *
            </strong>
            <a href="http://www.slate.com/articles/life/design/2012/06/button_history_a_visual_tour_of_button_design_through_the_ages_.html" target="_blank">
             <strong>
              Buttons
             </strong>
            </a>
            : The use of buttons to spread messages didnt stop with ol George. In fact, Abraham Lincolns presidency helped establish the use of
            <a href="http://content.time.com/time/photogallery/0,29307,2106314_2334640,00.html" target="_blank">
             political buttons
            </a>
            that we still see today.
           </p>
           <p>
            Beyond politics, though, buttons of the 19
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            century also were used to communicate more personal messages. Manufacturers used their newfound resources to create button molds that depicted
            <a href="http://bnb.jewelrymakingmagazines.com/community/button-corner/2009/05/tussie-mussies" target="_blank">
             tussie-mussies
            </a>
            . What the heck is a tussie-mussie? you might ask. Well, its essentially a small bouquet that includes flowers and
            <a href="http://www.dowlingcommunitygarden.org/pdf/lang06.pdf" target="_blank">
             plants chosen for their meaning
            </a>
            (rosemary for remembrance, that kind of thing). Not only did fashionable, gardening-obsessed Victorian ladies exchange these bouquets; they also wore buttons depicting them (and sharing their meanings).
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_50024" style="width: 160px">
            <img alt="Promotional Bobblehead" class="wp-image-50024" height="190" src="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Bobblehead_Resized.jpg" width="150"/>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             A promotional basketball bobblehead
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            *
            <a href="http://www.bobbleheads.com/history-of-bobbleheads.html" target="_blank">
             <strong>
              Bobbleheads
             </strong>
            </a>
            : Admittedly, Im cheating a little bit on this one. While the earliest reference to a bobblehead toy dates back to 1842, the earliest reference found to one used for promotional purposes dates back to the 1920s, when a
            <a href="http://articles.mcall.com/2007-09-25/features/3777764_1_bobblehead-doll-collectors">
             New York Knicks bobblehead
            </a>
            was produced. Who knew that those toys were part of such a long-standing promotional tradition?
           </p>
           <p>
            *
            <a href="http://anheuser-busch.com/index.php/our-heritage/history/marketing-and-advertising/">
             <strong>
              Corkscrews
             </strong>
            </a>
            : What? I said a
            <em>
             lot
            </em>
            of the products began with a B. Adolphus Busch, the beer brewer who in the second part of the 19
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            century developed what we know today as the Anheuser-Busch company, pioneered a lot more than lager. When the companys traveling salesmen went out into taverns to sell Buschs products, he sent them with branded items to give to customers. A corkscrew was one popular item; another was a branded pocketknife, which established
            <a href="http://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/tradeshow-promotions/jar-of-knife-2-engraved.htm" target="_blank">
             a fine tradition
            </a>
            that we contribute to today at QLP.
           </p>
           <p>
            As industry grew, more products came into being. Stress relievers like my beloved Emoticon Stress Ball, Spot, were developed after
            <a href="http://itech.dickinson.edu/chemistry/?p=920" target="_blank">
             polyurethane was first synthesized
            </a>
            in 1937, in response to the demand for a rubber substitute during World War II. More products surely will be created as technology matures.
           </p>
           <p>
            But there you have it, folks: as Content team manager Mandy put it, promotional products are older than the car. And that brings us to now, with more than 25,000 different promotional products for you to choose from, all thanks to the tradition of innovation thats continued alongside human society.
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             What have been your favorite developments in the history of promotional products? (Ours is probably the corkscrewis there any problem wine cant solve?) Isnt it great that we dont have to carry around clay tablets anymore? Let us know what you think in the comments below!
            </em>
           </p>
