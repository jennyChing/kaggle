     <p>
      An
      <b>
       audiophile
      </b>
      is a person enthusiastic about
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-fidelity" title="High-fidelity">
       high-fidelity
      </a>
      sound reproduction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Audiophile values may be applied at all stages of music reproduction: the initial
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_recording" title="Audio recording">
       audio recording
      </a>
      , the production process, and the playback, which is usually in a home setting.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Audiophile" recordings include those using conventional formats but with special attention to audio quality, as well as recordings in high-resolution formats such as
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Audio_CD" title="Super Audio CD">
       Super Audio CD
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/DVD-Audio" title="DVD-Audio">
       DVD-Audio
      </a>
      . Recently, there has been interest in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless" title="Lossless">
       lossless
      </a>
      file formats such as
      <a href="/wiki/WAV" title="WAV">
       WAV
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/FLAC" title="FLAC">
       FLAC
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMA_Lossless" title="WMA Lossless">
       WMA Lossless
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lossless" title="Apple Lossless">
       Apple Lossless
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A key goal of audiophiles is to capture the experience of a live musical performance in a room with good acoustics, and reproduce it at home. It is widely agreed that this is very difficult and that even the best-regarded recording and playback systems rarely, if ever, achieve it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/High-end_audio" title="High-end audio">
        High-end audio
       </a>
      </i>
      refers to playback equipment used by audiophiles, which may be bought at specialist shops and websites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perlman1_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-perlman1-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      High-end components include
      <a href="/wiki/Phonograph#Turntable_technology" title="Phonograph">
       turntables
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">
       digital-to-analog converters
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Equalization_(audio)" title="Equalization (audio)">
       equalization
      </a>
      devices,
      <a href="/wiki/Preamplifier" title="Preamplifier">
       preamplifiers
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amplifiers" title="Amplifiers">
       amplifiers
      </a>
      (both solid-state and
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">
       vacuum tube
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Horn_loudspeaker" title="Horn loudspeaker">
       horn
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_loudspeaker" title="Electrostatic loudspeaker">
       electrostatic
      </a>
      speakers,
      <a href="/wiki/Power_conditioner" title="Power conditioner">
       power conditioners
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Subwoofer" title="Subwoofer">
       subwoofers
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Headphones" title="Headphones">
       headphones
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Soundproofing" title="Soundproofing">
       acoustic room treatment
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veen1_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Veen1-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OConnell1_5-0">

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Audio_playback_components">
       Audio playback components
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Audio playback components">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An audio system typically consists of one or more source components, one or more amplification components, and (for
      <a href="/wiki/Stereophonic_sound" title="Stereophonic sound">
       stereo
      </a>
      ) two or more
      <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">
       loudspeakers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_and_video_connector" title="Audio and video connector">
       Signal cables
      </a>
      (analog audio, speaker, digital audio etc.) are used to link these components. There are also a variety of accessories, including equipment racks,
      <a href="/wiki/Power_conditioner" title="Power conditioner">
       power conditioners
      </a>
      , devices to reduce or control
      <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">
       vibration
      </a>
      , record cleaners, anti-static devices, phonograph needle cleaners,
      <a href="/wiki/Reverberation" title="Reverberation">
       reverberation
      </a>
      reducing devices such as speaker pads and stands, sound absorbent foam, and
      <a href="/wiki/Soundproofing" title="Soundproofing">
       soundproofing
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The interaction between the loudspeakers and the room (
      <a href="/wiki/Room_acoustics" title="Room acoustics">
       room acoustics
      </a>
      ) plays an important part in sound quality. Sound vibrations are reflected from walls, floor and ceiling, and are affected by the contents of the room. Room dimensions can create
      <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">
       standing waves
      </a>
      at particular (usually low) frequencies. There are devices and materials for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustic_treatment#Absorption" title="Acoustic treatment">
       room treatment
      </a>
      that affect sound quality. Soft materials, such as draperies and carpets, can absorb higher frequencies, whereas hard walls and floors can cause excess reverberation.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sound_sources">
       Sound sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sound sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Audiophiles play music from a wide variety of sources including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonograph_record" title="Phonograph record">
       phonograph records
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">
       compact discs
      </a>
      (CDs), and digital
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_file_format" title="Audio file format">
       audio file formats
      </a>
      that are uncompressed as well as ones that are compressed using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" title="Lossless data compression">
       lossless data compression
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/FLAC" title="FLAC">
       FLAC
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_Media_Audio_9_Lossless" title="Windows Media Audio 9 Lossless">
       Windows Media Audio 9 Lossless
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Lossless" title="Apple Lossless">
       Apple Lossless (ALAC)
      </a>
      . Since the early 1990s, CDs have become the most common source of high-quality music. Nevertheless,
      <a href="/wiki/Phonograph#Turntable_technology" title="Phonograph">
       turntables
      </a>
      , tonearms, and
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_cartridge" title="Magnetic cartridge">
       magnetic cartridges
      </a>
      are still used, despite the difficulties of keeping records free from dust and the delicate set-up associated with turntables.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 44.1kHz
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sampling_rate" title="Sampling rate">
       sampling rate
      </a>
      of the CD format, in theory, restricts CD information losses to above the theoretical upper-frequency limit of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_hearing" title="Human hearing">
       human hearing
      </a>
       20kHz, see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyquist_limit" title="Nyquist limit">
       Nyquist limit
      </a>
      . Despite this, newer formats such as
      <a href="/wiki/DVD-Audio" title="DVD-Audio">
       DVD-Audio
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Audio_CD" title="Super Audio CD">
       Super Audio Compact Disc
      </a>
      (SACD), have sampling rates of 88.2kHz or 96kHz or even higher.
     </p>
     <p>
      CD audio signals are encoded in 16-bit values. Some higher-definition consumer formats such as HDCD-encoded CDs contain 20-bit and even 24-bit audio streams. With more bits more
      <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">
       dynamic range
      </a>
      is possible; 20 bit dynamic range is theoretically 120dBthe limit of most consumer electronic playback equipment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuberRunstein2005_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HuberRunstein2005-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      MP3 encoding, widely used in portable audio devices, is an example of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_compression_(data)#Lossy_audio_compression" title="Audio compression (data)">
       lossy compression
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amplifiers">
       Amplifiers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amplifiers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Preamplifier" title="Preamplifier">
       preamplifier
      </a>
      selects among several audio inputs, amplifies source-level signals (such as those from a turntable), and allows the listener to adjust the sound with volume and tone controls. Many audiophile-oriented preamplifiers lack tone controls. A power
      <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">
       amplifier
      </a>
      takes the "line-level" audio signal from the preamplifier and drives the
      <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">
       loudspeakers
      </a>
      . An integrated amplifier combines the functions of power amplification with input switching and volume and tone control. Both pre/power combinations and integrated amplifiers are widely used by audiophiles.
     </p>
     <p>
      Audiophile amplifiers are available based on solid-state (
      <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">
       semiconductor
      </a>
      ) technology,
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">
       vacuum-tube
      </a>
      (valve) technology, or hybrid technologysemiconductors and vacuum tubes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dedicated amplifiers are also commonly used by audiophiles to drive headphones, especially those with high impedance and/or low sensitivity, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_headphones" title="Electrostatic headphones">
       electrostatic headphones
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Loudspeakers">
       Loudspeakers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Loudspeakers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The cabinet of the loudspeaker is known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker_enclosure" title="Loudspeaker enclosure">
       enclosure
      </a>
      . There are a variety of loudspeaker enclosure designs, including sealed cabinets (
      <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_suspension" title="Acoustic suspension">
       acoustic suspension
      </a>
      ), ported cabinets (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bass-reflex" title="Bass-reflex">
       bass-reflex
      </a>
      ), transmission line, infinite baffle, horn loaded. The enclosure plays a major role in the sound of the loudspeaker.
     </p>
     <p>
      The drivers that produce the sound are referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Tweeter" title="Tweeter">
       tweeters
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-range_speaker" title="Mid-range speaker">
       midranges
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Woofer" title="Woofer">
       woofers
      </a>
      . Driver designs include dynamic, electrostatic, plasma, ribbon, planar, ionic, and servo-actuated. Drivers are made from various materials, including paper pulp, polypropylene, kevlar, aluminum, magnesium, beryllium, and vapor-deposited diamond.
     </p>
     <p>
      The direction and intensity of the output of a loudspeaker, called dispersion or polar response, has a large effect on its sound.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Various methods are employed to control the dispersion. These methods include monopolar, bipolar, dipolar, 360-degree, horn, waveguide, and line source. These terms refer to the configuration and arrangement of the various drivers in the enclosure.
     </p>
     <p>
      The positioning of loudspeakers in the room has a strong influence on the sound experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Loudspeaker output is influenced by interaction with room boundaries, particularly bass response, and high frequency transducers are directional, or "beaming."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Accessories">
       Accessories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accessories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Audiophiles use a wide variety of accessories and fine-tuning techniques, sometimes referred to as "tweaks," to improve the sound of their systems. These include filters to "clean" the electricity,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      equipment racks to isolate components from floor vibrations, specialty power and audio cables, loudspeaker stands (and footers to isolate speakers from stands), and room treatments.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are several types of room treatment. Sound-absorbing materials may be placed strategically within a listening room to reduce the amplitude of early reflections, and to deal with resonance modes. Other treatments are designed to produce diffusion, reflection of sound in a scattered fashion. Room treatments can be expensive and difficult to optimize.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Headphones">
       Headphones
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Headphones">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Headphones are regularly used by audiophiles. These products can be remarkably expensive, some over $10,000,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but in general are much cheaper than comparable speaker systems. They have the advantage of not requiring room treatment and being usable without requiring others to listen at the same time. Newer
      <a href="/wiki/Headphones#In-ear_headphones" title="Headphones">
       canalphones
      </a>
      can be driven by the less powerful outputs found on portable music players.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_Variety">
       Design Variety
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design Variety">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For music storage,
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio" title="Digital audio">
       digital
      </a>
      formats offer an absence of clicks, pops,
      <a href="/wiki/Wow_(recording)" title="Wow (recording)">
       wow
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Flutter_(electronics_and_communication)" title="Flutter (electronics and communication)">
       flutter
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustic_feedback" title="Acoustic feedback">
       acoustic feedback
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Rumble_(noise)" title="Rumble (noise)">
       rumble
      </a>
      , compared to vinyl records. Digital is also claimed to have a higher
      <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">
       signal-to-noise ratio
      </a>
      , a wider
      <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">
       dynamic range
      </a>
      , less
      <a href="/wiki/Total_harmonic_distortion" title="Total harmonic distortion">
       total harmonic distortion
      </a>
      , and a flatter and more extended
      <a href="/wiki/Frequency_response" title="Frequency response">
       frequency response
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masters_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Masters-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite this, vinyl records remain popular, and discussion about the relative merits of analog and digital sound continues (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_sound_vs._digital_sound" title="Analog sound vs. digital sound">
       Analog sound vs. digital sound
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In the amplification stage, both solid state and
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">
       vacuum-tube
      </a>
      electronics remain popular, even though vacuum-tube amplifiers have been alleged to have higher
      <a href="/wiki/Total_harmonic_distortion" title="Total harmonic distortion">
       total harmonic distortion
      </a>
      , require rebiasing, be less reliable, generate more heat, be less powerful, and cost more.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is also continuing debate about the proper use of
      <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">
       negative feedback
      </a>
      in amplifier design.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">
       Controversies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Audiophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There is substantial controversy on the subject of audiophile components; many have asserted that the occasionally high cost produces no measurable improvement in audio reproduction. For example, skeptic
      <a href="/wiki/James_Randi" title="James Randi">
       James Randi
      </a>
      , through his foundation
      <a href="/wiki/One_Million_Dollar_Paranormal_Challenge" title="One Million Dollar Paranormal Challenge">
       One Million Dollar Paranormal Challenge
      </a>
      , has offered a prize of $1 million to anyone who can demonstrate that $7,250 audio cables "are any better than ordinary audio cables".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2008, audio reviewer Michael Fremer attempted to claim the prize, and said that Randi declined the challenge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Randi said that the cable manufacturer Pear Cables was the one who withdrew.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Criticisms usually focus on claims around so-called "tweaks" and accessories beyond the core source, amplification, and speaker products. Examples of these accessories include speaker cables, component interconnects, stones, cones, CD markers, and power cables or conditioners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Russell-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is disagreement on how
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_equipment_testing" title="Audio equipment testing">
       equipment testing
      </a>
      should be conducted and as to its utility. Audiophile publications frequently describe differences in quality which are not detected by standard
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_system_measurements" title="Audio system measurements">
       audio system measurements
      </a>
      and double blind testing, claiming that they perceive differences in audio quality which cannot be measured by current instrumentation,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and cannot be detected by listeners if listening conditions are
      <a href="/wiki/Scientific_control" title="Scientific control">
       controlled
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but without providing an explanation for those claims.
     </p>
