         Albums of the (half) year: 2014
        </span>

         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           By March of this year, Id already heard more praiseworthy new music than I did in all of 2013. Big-name artists  the music industrys heavy hitters  have been pretty absent this year. No matter; plenty of others are picking up the slack. In conjunction with the years halfway mark, here are my five favorite albums of the year so far.
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            Daley: Days &amp; Nights
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Ive listened to this album more than any other this year. Gradually and subtly, the 25-year-old British singers full-length debut reveals itself as R&amp;B/soul perfection. His thin, breathy, buttery voice has drawn comparisons to Miguel  and thats accurate  but Daleys songs feel less subversive. Instead of Miguels lusty come-ons, Daley remains romantic and slightly, endearingly wounded. The production on Days &amp; Nights is masterful  a keen undercurrent of electronic sounds mixed with lush strings. The perfect blend of the processed and organic.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Favorite tracks: Blame The World, Love &amp; Affection, Good News
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/jlBWR3SqdDE" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            Courtney Barnett: The Double EP: A Sea of Split Peas
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           <em>
            Technically,
           </em>
           A Sea of Split Peas was released in 2013, but that release was only digital. It was re-released on CD and vinyl earlier this year, so allow me the technicality here. A Sea of Split Peas combines two of Barnetts previously released EPs, and its a remarkable batch of songs. When I think of my reaction to the Australian songwriters music, I think of Roger Eberts review of the Humphrey Bogart classic The Big Sleep.
          </p>
          <p>
           It's unusual to find yourself laughing in a movie not because something is funny but because it's so wickedly clever, Ebert said. Barnett spins phrases so odd and ingenious, its hard not to laugh sometimes, just out of reflex. Listen to the opening lines of Lance Jr. and try to remain un-phased  I dare you. Music this lyrically engaging doesnt come around every day.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Favorite tracks: History Eraser, Lance Jr., Avant Gardener
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/k6_G5PlEXdk" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            The Belle Brigade: Just Because
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           The brother-sister duo of Ethan and Barbara Gruska became critical darlings in 2011 with their self-titled debut album  a lovable batch of songs that referenced Simon &amp; Garfunkel and Fleetwood Mac pretty heavily. On Just Because, theyve abandoned the retro sheen of its predecessor, replacing those classic production sounds with a more modern, experimental palette. These songs might sound less classic on the surface, but the Gruskas' songwriting is sharper than ever. And those harmonies! Two voices eerily, infectiously bound by shared DNA.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Favorite tracks: Likely to Use Something, Be Like Him, Metropolis
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/t-2t6XiKY3k" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            Bombay Bicycle Club: So Long, See You Tomorrow
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Trying to accurately describe So Long, See You Tomorrow is like trying to hold liquid in your bare hands. Its a slippery album, and the available signifiers  especially the term indie-pop  are reductive and dont do this album justice. Sonically daring and rhythmically frisky, So Long, See You Tomorrow feels both playful and focused. Give credit to singer Jack Steadman. He keeps these musical excursions grounded with a haunting, quivering-yet-determined tenor. In less capable hands, the ambitions of So Long, See You Tomorrow would have spiraled out of control, but the bands fourth LP never loses its sense of purpose.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Favorite tracks: Luna, Eyes Off You, Overdone
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/JwlgC-jSPTk" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            Meshell Ndegeocello: Comet, Come to Me
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Ndegeocello has gone countless directions in her 20-year career, avoiding any kind of easy classification. On Comet, Come to Me, her 11th studio album, she continues that genre-bending streak. She begins it with flamenco guitar, plunging into distorted synths, then Motown-era horns, then folk banjos and Delta blues guitar, then greasy dub reggae  and thats just the first four songs. Its a real trip. But it works. Few artists have the expertise to pull off something like Comet, Come to Me, and Ndegeocello makes it sound easy. From her voice, though  one that, like Nina Simone, sounds deeply wise but not preachy  its clear such eclecticism has been earned.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Favorite tracks: Friends, Tom, Comet, Come to Me
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe frameborder="0" height="315" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/WKDqO8PUd8c" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
