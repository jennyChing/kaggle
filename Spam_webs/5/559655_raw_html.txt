         <p>

         <div class="story-embed">
          <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="story-embed-item" frameborder="0" height="100%" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/G-FegG0U40k" type="text/html" width="100%">

         </div>
         Passengers on a whale-watching boat leaving the charming harbour of St. Andrews, N.B, were shocked and thrilled to encounter a great white shark that got so close to them, that they were able to take vivid photos and videos of it.
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          The thing about it was just the size. The thing was massive. The girth in the middle was so big, says Nick Hawkins, a Naturalist Guide with Quoddy Link Marine tours.
         </p>
         <div class="figure col4width floatright">
          <img alt="Great White Shark" src="http://i.cbc.ca/1.2715663.1406133799!/fileImage/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/original_300/great-white-shark.jpg" width="100%"/>
          <p class="figure-caption">
           Nick Hawkins, naturalist guide with Quoddy Link Marine, says the great white shark spotted by whale watchers in New Brunswick had a large scar near its gills. (Nick Hawkins)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          It just was a very impressive creature to be so close to.
         </p>
         <p>
          
          <span>
           The tour boat pulled alongside the shark for about 10minutes while it calmly swam in large circles.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          Size estimates of the shark based on photos and videos captured by crew and passengers range from 3.5 to 6 metres.
         </p>
         <p>
          Hawkins says the predator was apparently scouting something for dinner Monday when sighted.
         </p>
         <p>
          When we first spotted it, it was definitely going after something, he says.
         </p>
         <p>
          About a mile after leaving the harbour we spotted some thrashing at the surface and we turned the boat around and approached the area and managed to find the shark with the dorsal just out of the water.
         </p>
         <p>
          He was just cruising around slowly. We managed to get some really good looks at it.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Shark sighting rare, expert says
         </h2>
         <p>
          Steve Campana, head of the Canadian Shark Research Lab at the Bedford Institute of Oceanography in Nova Scotia, says a sighting like this is rare, but great whites are known to swim around the Atlantic provinces.
         </p>
         <p>
          A typical shark is really only driven by a couple of things; one is sex and one is food. In this case, theres no indication it was mating. It was probably cruising around in appropriate water temperatures to look for food, says Campana. It was probably just exploring.
         </p>
         <p>
          The sharks location took Hawkins by surprise. He has been working with the whale-watching tour for five seasons.
         </p>
         <div class="figure col4width floatright">
          <img alt="Great White Shark" src="http://i.cbc.ca/1.2715658.1406133562!/fileImage/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/original_300/great-white-shark.jpg" width="100%"/>
          <p class="figure-caption">
           A great white shark was spotted just outside St. Andrews, N.B., harbour by passengers on a boat who were in awe of the close encounter. (Nick Hawkins)
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          To see it where we did was even more unusual because we cross that body of water, the Passamaquoddy Bay, six times a day. We dont usually slow down there because most of the wildlife is out further in the islands. There are some seals and porpoise there, which is maybe why the shark was around.
         </p>
         <p>
          Scientists who study sharks agree their numbers have increased about 10 to 20 per cent over the last decade in the Atlantic Ocean after reaching a low point in the 1980s and 1990s. It'snot clear exactly how many there are off Canadian shores. Campanacan only estimate in the order of thousands.
         </p>
         <p>
          Still, he says swimmers and boatershave nothing to worry about.
         </p>
         <p>
          Nobody has been bitten by a shark of any kind in Canadian waters. Its very safe. These whites are not particularly interested in people. Their preferred diet is something like seal.
         </p>
         <p>
          The sighting is one Hawkins will never forget, and he suspects the passengers wont either.
         </p>
         <p>
          There were some people on the boat who had never even been on a boat before, so to get on a boat and within fiveminutes be right next to a great white shark, thats pretty amazing.
         </p>
