           <div class="field field-name-body">
            <p>
             Imagine the entire American automotive industry represented by 100 cars. What would it look like? How many are manual or transmission? Bought or leased? What are the most popular colors, makes, and models? And, more importantly, how do you compare?
            </p>
            <p>
             View the infographic below for an inside look at popular consumer trends in the American auto industry.
            </p>
            <div id="mustang-video">
             <p>
              <iframe frameborder="0" scrolling="no" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/cnSNP4jC55o">
              </iframe>
             </p>
            </div>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Type of Vehicle
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             While light-duty truck sales saw a dip during the recession, they have since recovered and continued to outsell cars in 2014.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Manufacturer
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Five years after the recession and U.S. automotive industry crisis, Detroit's Big Three -- GM, Ford, and Chrysler -- are still in the top spots at 18%, 16%, and 13% respectively.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              New vs. Used
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Used vehicles continue to make up over 75% of American vehicles. While the cost of used vehicles rose slightly in 2014 -- due to a rise in demand for used vehicles -- prices are expected to come back down due to an increased inventory of used vehicles.
             <a href="http://www.detroitnews.com/story/business/autos/2015/02/16/used-car-prices-expected-fall/23476001/">
              25% of all vehicles purchased in 2014
             </a>
             came from leases, and it's expected that inventory will receive a further boost from more leased vehicles being turned in this year.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Best-Selling Brands
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             The Ford F-Series pickup was the most popular vehicle of 2014 by a stretch -- making it the
             <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2015-auto-sales/">
              top-selling vehicle for the 33rd year in a row
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Domestic vs. Import
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Although the top manufacturers are domestic, imports continue to represent the majority of American vehicles.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Standard vs. Luxury
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             While luxury cars represent just 7% of car purchases, the luxury market has continue to grow faster than the overall new-car market in recent years. This growth is expected to continue through 2015.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Vehicle Color
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             White has been the most popular vehicle color in the U.S. for the last eight years. The benefits of white vehicles include: increased safety at dawn and dusk and less sunlight absorption than darker colors. White vehicles also retain their value better than other colors due to the consistent demand for white vehicles.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Purchased vs. Leased
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             In 2014, the rate of new-car sales that were leases was the highest it has ever been for the last decade. While just over 25% of new-car sales were leases, many are observing a shift in consumers. While consumers are increasingly valuing lower monthly payments over vehicle ownership.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Fuel Types
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Despite the buzz surrounding electric and other alternative-fuel vehicles, sales are equal with those of diesel vehicles (4%). When asked why they don't purchase an alternative-fuel vehicle,
             <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/brian-moody/why-arent-more-people-buy_b_5134499.html">
              70% of respondents cited the cost
             </a>
             of these vehicles as their reason in a recent survey.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Transmission
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             It makes sense that manual vehicles make up just 6.5% of cars. Only about
             <a href="http://www.foxnews.com/leisure/2014/09/11/manual-transmissions-getting-rarer-in-us/">
              10% of vehicles made in North America
             </a>
             now have manual transmission.
            </p>
