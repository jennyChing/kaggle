   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    In-situ resource utilization
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
       space exploration
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       in-situ resource utilization
      </b>
      (ISRU) describes the proposed use of resources found or manufactured on other
      <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
       astronomical objects
      </a>
      (the Moon, Mars, asteroids, etc.) to further the goals of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_mission" title="Space mission">
       space mission
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
       NASA
      </a>
      , "in-situ resource utilization will enable the affordable establishment of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_exploration" title="Extraterrestrial exploration">
       extraterrestrial exploration
      </a>
      and operations by minimizing the materials carried from Earth."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      ISRU can provide materials for
      <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">
       life support
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">
       propellants
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Construction_material" title="Construction material">
       construction materials
      </a>
      , and energy to a science
      <a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">
       payload
      </a>
      or a crew deployed on a planet, moon, or asteroid.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is now very common for
      <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
       spacecraft
      </a>
      to harness the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">
       solar radiation
      </a>
      found
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">
        in situ
       </a>
      </i>
      , and it is likely missions to planetary surfaces will also use
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar power
      </a>
      . Beyond that, ISRU has not yet received any practical application, but it is seen by exploration proponents as a way to drastically reduce the amount of payload that must be launched from Earth in order to explore a given
      <a href="/wiki/Planetary_body" title="Planetary body">
       planetary body
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Proposals have been made for "mining"
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_gas" title="Atmospheric gas">
       atmospheric gases
      </a>
      for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_propulsion" title="Rocket propulsion">
       rocket propulsion
      </a>
      , using what is called a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propulsive_Fluid_Accumulator" title="Propulsive Fluid Accumulator">
       Propulsive Fluid Accumulator
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">
       Uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cell_production">
       Solar cell production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar cell production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It has long been suggested that
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">
       solar cells
      </a>
      could be produced from the materials present on the lunar surface. In its original form, known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_satellite" title="Solar power satellite">
       solar power satellite
      </a>
      , the proposal was intended as an alternate power source for
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      . Solar cells would be shipped to Earth orbit and assembled, the power being transmitted to Earth via microwave beams.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite much work on the cost of such a venture, the uncertainty lay in the cost and complexity of fabrication procedures on the lunar surface. A more modest reincarnation of this dream is for it to create solar cells to power future lunar bases. One particular proposal is to simplify the process by using
      <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">
       fluorine
      </a>
      brought from Earth as
      <a href="/wiki/Potassium_fluoride" title="Potassium fluoride">
       potassium fluoride
      </a>
      to separate the raw materials from the lunar rocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rocket_propellant">
       Rocket propellant
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rocket propellant">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Rocket propellant from
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
       ice
      </a>
      has also been proposed for the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      , mainly from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_ice" title="Lunar ice">
       ice that has been found at the poles
      </a>
      . The likely difficulties include working at extremely low temperatures and extraction from the
      <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
       regolith
      </a>
      . Most schemes
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">
       electrolyse the water
      </a>
      and form
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      and liquify and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">
       cryogenically
      </a>
      store them. This requires large amounts of equipment and power to achieve. Alternatively it is possible to simply heat the water in a nuclear or solar
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_rocket" title="Thermal rocket">
       thermal rocket
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which seems to give very much more
      <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
       mass
      </a>
      delivered to
      <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
       low Earth orbit
      </a>
      (LEO) in spite of the much lower
      <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">
       specific impulse
      </a>
      , for a given amount of equipment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">
       monopropellant
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">
       hydrogen peroxide
      </a>
      (H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) can be made from water on
      <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
       Mars
      </a>
      and the Moon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">
       Aluminum
      </a>
      as well as other metals have been proposed for use as rocket propellant made using lunar resources,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hepp_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hepp-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and proposals include reacting the aluminum with water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The spacecraft could use the propellant itself or supply a
      <a href="/wiki/Propellant_depot" title="Propellant depot">
       propellant depot
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_to_breathe_and_water_to_drink">
       Oxygen to breathe and water to drink
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxygen to breathe and water to drink">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Water ice could replenish a space ship's water tanks. Water is needed for drinking and hygiene, but may also be used for radiation protection in deep space (living quarters inside a double-walled cylindrical water tank). Splitting water allows the creation of rocket propellant, but can also liberate oxygen that could be used to replenish the atmosphere in a closed-loop recycling system.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Metals_for_construction_or_return_to_Earth">
       Metals for construction or return to Earth
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Metals for construction or return to Earth">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">
       Asteroid mining
      </a>
      could also involve extraction of metals for construction material in space, which may be more cost-effective than bringing such material up out of
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      's deep gravity well, or that of any other large body like the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
       Mars
      </a>
      . Metallic asteroids contain huge amounts of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siderophile_element" title="Siderophile element">
       siderophilic metals
      </a>
      , including
      <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">
       precious metals
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">
       Locations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Locations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">
       Mars
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mars">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Zubrin" title="Robert Zubrin">
       Robert Zubrin
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      ISRU research for Mars is focused primarily on providing
      <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">
       rocket propellant
      </a>
      for a return trip to Earth either for a manned or a sample return mission or for use as fuel on Mars. Many of the proposed techniques utilize the well-characterised
      <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">
       atmosphere of Mars
      </a>
      as feedstock. Since this can be easily simulated on Earth, these proposals are relatively simple to implement, though it is by no means certain that NASA or the ESA will favour this approach over a more conventional direct mission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A typical proposal for ISRU is the use of a
      <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">
       Sabatier reaction
      </a>
      , CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      + 4H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
       CH
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      + 2H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O, in order to produce methane on the Martian surface, to be used as a propellant. Oxygen is liberated from the water by
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">
       electrolysis
      </a>
      , and the hydrogen recycled back into the Sabatier reaction. The usefulness of this reaction is that only the hydrogen (which is light) need be brought from Earth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A similar reaction proposed for Mars is the reverse
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">
       water gas shift reaction
      </a>
      , CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      + H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
       CO + H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O. This reaction takes place rapidly in the presence of an iron-chrome
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">
       catalyst
      </a>
      at 400 Celsius,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has been implemented in an Earth-based testbed by NASA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Again, oxygen is recycled from the water by
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">
       electrolysis
      </a>
      , and the reaction only needs a small amount of hydrogen from Earth. The net result of this reaction is the production of oxygen, to be used as the oxidizer component of rocket fuel.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another reaction proposed for the production of oxygen and fuel
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is the electrolysis of the atmospheric carbon dioxide, 2CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      (+ energy)  2CO + O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Mars_Surveyor_2001_Lander" title="Mars Surveyor 2001 Lander">
       Mars Surveyor 2001 Lander
      </a>
      MIP (Mars ISPP Precursor) was to demonstrate manufacture of oxygen from the
      <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">
       atmosphere of Mars
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and test solar cell technologies and methods of mitigating the effect of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_dust" title="Martian dust">
       Martian dust
      </a>
      on the power systems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The proposed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_2020_rover_mission" title="Mars 2020 rover mission">
       Mars 2020 rover mission
      </a>
      might include ISRU technology demonstrator that would extract CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      from the atmosphere and produce O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      for rocket fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tap_O2_2013_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tap_O2_2013-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been suggested that buildings on Mars could be made from
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       Basalt
      </a>
      as it has good insulating properties. An underground structure of this type would be able to protect life forms against radiation exposure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      All of the resources required to make plastics exist on Mars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many of these complex reactions are able to be completed from the gases harvested from the martian atmosphere. Traces of free oxygen, carbon monoxide, water and methane are all known to exist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hydrogen and oxygen can be made by the electrolysis of water, carbon monoxide and oxygen by the electrolysis of carbon dioxide and methane by the sabatier reaction of carbon dioxide and hydrogen. These basic reactions provide the building blocks for more complex reaction series which are able to make plastics.
      <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">
       Ethylene
      </a>
      is used to make plastics such as
      <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">
       polyethylene
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">
       polypropylene
      </a>
      and can be made from carbon monoxide and hydrogen,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      2CO + 4H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
       C
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      + 2H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moon">
       The Moon
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Moon">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Footprint.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="650" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Footprint.gif/250px-Footprint.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Footprint.gif/375px-Footprint.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Footprint.gif/500px-Footprint.gif 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Footprint.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Footprint in lunar
        <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
         regolith
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Moon possesses abundant raw materials that are potentially relevant to a hierarchy of future applications, beginning with the use of lunar materials to facilitate human activities on the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      itself (i.e.
      <i>
       In Situ
      </i>
      Resource Utilization, ISRU), and progressing to the use of lunar resources to underpin a future industrial capability within the Earth-Moon system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The lunar highland material
      <a href="/wiki/Anorthite" title="Anorthite">
       anorthite
      </a>
      is similar to the Earth mineral
      <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">
       bauxite
      </a>
      , which is an
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">
       ore
      </a>
      . Smelters can produce pure aluminum, calcium metal, oxygen and silica glass from anorthite. Raw anorthite is also good for making fiberglass and other glass and ceramic products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mining_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mining-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Over twenty different methods have been proposed for
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      extraction on the Moon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hepp_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hepp-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Oxygen is often found in iron rich lunar minerals and glasses as
      <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">
       iron oxide
      </a>
      . The oxygen can be extracted by heating the material to temperatures above 900C and exposing it to hydrogen gas. The basic equation is: FeO + H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
       Fe + H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O. This process has recently been made much more practical by the discovery of significant amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      -containing
      <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
       regolith
      </a>
      near the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon#Presence_of_water" title="Moon">
       Moon's poles
      </a>
      by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_spacecraft" title="Clementine spacecraft">
       Clementine spacecraft
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Lunar materials may also be valuable for other uses. It has also been proposed to use lunar regolith as a general construction material,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      through processing techniques such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">
       sintering
      </a>
      , hot-pressing,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquification" title="Liquification">
       liquification
      </a>
      , and the cast
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       basalt
      </a>
      method. Cast basalt is used on Earth for construction of, for example, pipes where a high resistance to abrasion is required. Cast basalt has a very high
      <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">
       hardness
      </a>
      of 8
      <a href="/wiki/Mohs_scale_of_mineral_hardness" title="Mohs scale of mineral hardness">
       Mohs
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">
       diamond
      </a>
      is 10 Mohs) but is also susceptible to mechanical impact and
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_shock" title="Thermal shock">
       thermal shock
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which could be a problem on the Moon.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">
       Glass
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Glass_fiber" title="Glass fiber">
       glass fiber
      </a>
      are straightforward to process on the Moon and Mars, and it has been argued that the glass is optically superior to that made on the Earth because it can be made
      <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">
       anhydrous
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mining_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mining-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Successful tests have been performed on Earth using two
      <a href="/wiki/Lunar_regolith_simulant" title="Lunar regolith simulant">
       lunar regolith simulants
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MLS-1" title="MLS-1">
       MLS-1
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MLS-2" title="MLS-2">
       MLS-2
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basalt_fibre" title="Basalt fibre">
       Basalt fibre
      </a>
      has also been made from lunar regolith simulators.
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2005, NASA contracted for the production of 16 tonnes of simulated lunar soil, or "
      <a href="/wiki/Lunar_regolith_simulant" title="Lunar regolith simulant">
       Lunar Regolith Simulant Material
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This material, called
      <a class="external text" href="http://science.nasa.gov/headlines/y2006/28dec_truefake.htm" rel="nofollow">
       JSC-1a
      </a>
      , is now commercially available for research on how lunar soil could be utilized
      <i>
       in situ
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Martian_Moons.2C_Ceres.2C_asteroids">
       Martian Moons, Ceres, asteroids
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Martian Moons, Ceres, asteroids">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Other proposals
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      are based on
      <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
       Phobos
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
       Deimos
      </a>
      . These moons are in reasonably high orbits above Mars, have very low escape velocities, and unlike Mars have return
      <a href="/wiki/Delta-v_budget" title="Delta-v budget">
       delta-v
      </a>
      's from their surfaces to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Earth_Orbit" title="Low Earth Orbit">
       LEO
      </a>
      which are less than the return from the Moon.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">
       Ceres
      </a>
      is further out than Mars, with a higher delta-v, but launch windows and travel times are better, and the surface gravity is just 0.028 g, with a very low escape velocity of 510m/s. Researchers have speculated that the interior configuration of Ceres includes a water-ice-rich mantle over a rocky core.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2005_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Thomas2005-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Earth_Asteroids" title="Near Earth Asteroids">
       Near Earth Asteroids
      </a>
      and bodies in the
      <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
       asteroid
      </a>
      belt could also be sources of raw materials for ISRU.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Low_orbit">
       Low orbit
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Low orbit">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Gases like oxygen and
      <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
       argon
      </a>
      could be extracted from the atmosphere of planets like the Earth and Mars by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propulsive_Fluid_Accumulator" title="Propulsive Fluid Accumulator">
       Propulsive Fluid Accumulator
      </a>
      satellites in low orbit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones2010_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jones2010-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <br/>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="ISRU_classification">
       ISRU classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=In-situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: ISRU classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In October 2004, NASAs Advanced Planning and Integration Office commissioned an ISRU capability roadmap team. The team's report, along with those of 14 other capability roadmap teams, were published May 22, 2005.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The report identifies seven ISRU capabilities: (i) resource extraction, (ii) material handling and transport, (iii) resource processing, (iv) surface manufacturing with
      <i>
       in-situ
      </i>
      resources, (v) surface construction, (vi) surface ISRU product and consumable storage and distribution, and (vii) ISRU unique development and certification capabilities.
     </p>
