     <p>
      The
      <b>
       River Clyde
      </b>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">
       Scottish Gaelic
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd">
       <i>
        Abhainn Chluaidh
       </i>
      </span>
      ,
      <small>
       pronounced
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA for Scottish Gaelic">
        [avxluj]
       </a>
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
       Scots
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="sco" xml:lang="sco">
       <i>
        Watter o Clyde
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       river
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      . It is the eighth longest river in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      , and the second longest in Scotland. Flowing through the major city of
      <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">
       Glasgow
      </a>
      , it was an important river for
      <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">
       shipbuilding
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">
       trade
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
       British Empire
      </a>
      . In the early medieval
      <a href="/wiki/Cumbric_language" title="Cumbric language">
       Cumbric language
      </a>
      it was known as
      <i>
       Clud
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Clut
      </i>
      , and was central to the
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Strathclyde" title="Kingdom of Strathclyde">
       Kingdom of Strathclyde
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Teyrnas Ystrad Clut
      </i>
      ).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Course">
       Course
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Course">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Clyde is formed by the confluence of two streams, the
      <a href="/wiki/Daer_Water" title="Daer Water">
       Daer Water
      </a>
      (the headwaters of which are dammed to form the
      <a href="/wiki/Daer_Reservoir" title="Daer Reservoir">
       Daer Reservoir
      </a>
      ) and the Potrail Water. The
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Upland_Way" title="Southern Upland Way">
       Southern Upland Way
      </a>
      crosses both streams before they meet at Watermeetings (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_national_grid_reference_system" title="British national grid reference system">
       grid reference
      </a>
      <span style="white-space: nowrap">
       <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/os/coor_g/?pagename=River_Clyde&amp;params=NS953131_region%3AGB_scale%3A25000">
        NS953131
       </a>
      </span>
      ) to form the River Clyde proper. At this point the Clyde is only 10 kilometres (6 miles) from Tweed's Well, the source of the
      <a href="/wiki/River_Tweed" title="River Tweed">
       River Tweed
      </a>
      , and 13 kilometres (8 miles) from the
      <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Beef_Tub" title="Devil's Beef Tub">
       Devil's Beef Tub
      </a>
      , the source of the
      <a href="/wiki/River_Annan" title="River Annan">
       River Annan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      From there it snakes northeastward before turning to the west, its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_plain" title="Flood plain">
       flood plain
      </a>
      used for many major
      <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">
       roads
      </a>
      in the area, until it reaches the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Lanark" title="Lanark">
       Lanark
      </a>
      . On the banks of the Clyde, the industrialists
      <a href="/wiki/David_Dale" title="David Dale">
       David Dale
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Owen" title="Robert Owen">
       Robert Owen
      </a>
      , built their mills and the model settlement of
      <a href="/wiki/New_Lanark" title="New Lanark">
       New Lanark
      </a>
      . The mills harness the power of the
      <a href="/wiki/Falls_of_Clyde_(waterfalls)" title="Falls of Clyde (waterfalls)">
       Falls of Clyde
      </a>
      , the most spectacular of which is Cora Linn. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">
       hydroelectric
      </a>
      power station still generates electricity here, although the mills are now a museum and
      <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">
       World Heritage Site
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      Between the towns of
      <a href="/wiki/Motherwell" title="Motherwell">
       Motherwell
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_South_Lanarkshire" title="Hamilton, South Lanarkshire">
       Hamilton
      </a>
      the course of the river has been altered to create an artificial loch within
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strathclyde_Park" title="Strathclyde Park">
       Strathclyde Park
      </a>
      . Part of the original course can still be seen, and lies between the island and the east shore of the loch. The river then flows through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blantyre,_Scotland" title="Blantyre, Scotland">
       Blantyre
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bothwell" title="Bothwell">
       Bothwell
      </a>
      , where the ruined
      <a href="/wiki/Bothwell_Castle" title="Bothwell Castle">
       Bothwell Castle
      </a>
      stands on a defensible
      <a href="/wiki/Promontory" title="Promontory">
       promontory
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Past
      <a href="/wiki/Uddingston" title="Uddingston">
       Uddingston
      </a>
      and into the southeast of Glasgow the river begins to widen, meandering a course through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rutherglen,_Glasgow" title="Rutherglen, Glasgow">
       Rutherglen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Dalmarnock" title="Dalmarnock">
       Dalmarnock
      </a>
      . Flowing past
      <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Green" title="Glasgow Green">
       Glasgow Green
      </a>
      , the river is artificially straightened and widened through the centre, and although the new
      <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Arc" title="Clyde Arc">
       Clyde Arc
      </a>
      now hinders access to the traditional
      <i>
       Broomielaw
      </i>
      dockland area, seagoing ships can still come upriver as far as
      <a href="/wiki/Finnieston" title="Finnieston">
       Finnieston
      </a>
      where the
      <a href="/wiki/PS_Waverley" title="PS Waverley">
       PS Waverley
      </a>
      docks. From there, it flows past the shipbuilding heartlands, through
      <a href="/wiki/Govan" title="Govan">
       Govan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Partick" title="Partick">
       Partick
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Whiteinch" title="Whiteinch">
       Whiteinch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Scotstoun" title="Scotstoun">
       Scotstoun
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Clydebank" title="Clydebank">
       Clydebank
      </a>
      , all of which housed major
      <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">
       shipyards
      </a>
      , of which only two remain. The river flows out west of Glasgow, past
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renfrew,_Scotland" title="Renfrew, Scotland">
       Renfrew
      </a>
      , and under the
      <a href="/wiki/Erskine_Bridge" title="Erskine Bridge">
       Erskine Bridge
      </a>
      past
      <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">
       Dumbarton
      </a>
      on the north shore to the sandbank at Ardmore Point between
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardross,_Argyll_and_Bute" title="Cardross, Argyll and Bute">
       Cardross
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Helensburgh" title="Helensburgh">
       Helensburgh
      </a>
      . Opposite, on the south shore, the river continues past the last
      <i>
       Lower Clyde
      </i>
      shipyard at
      <a href="/wiki/Port_Glasgow" title="Port Glasgow">
       Port Glasgow
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">
       Greenock
      </a>
      where it reaches the
      <a href="/wiki/Tail_of_the_Bank" title="Tail of the Bank">
       Tail of the Bank
      </a>
      as the river merges into the
      <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">
       Firth of Clyde
      </a>
      . At the mouth of the River Clyde there has been a significant issue of oxygen depletion in the water column.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are around 72 bridges (rail, road, foot and other) that cross the Clyde, from estuary to source. The valley of the Clyde was the focus for the G-BASE project from the British Geological Survey in the summer of 2010.
     </p>

     <p>
      The success of the Clyde at the beginning of the
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">
       Industrial Revolution
      </a>
      was driven by the location of Glasgow, being a
      <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">
       port
      </a>
      facing the
      <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
       Americas
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">
       Tobacco
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">
       cotton
      </a>
      trade began the drive in the early 18th century. However, the shallow Clyde was not navigable for the largest ocean-going ships and cargo had to be transferred at
      <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">
       Greenock
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Port_Glasgow" title="Port Glasgow">
       Port Glasgow
      </a>
      to smaller ships to sail upstream into Glasgow itself.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Deepening_the_Upper_Clyde">
       Deepening the Upper Clyde
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deepening the Upper Clyde">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1768
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Golborne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Golborne (page does not exist)">
       John Golborne
      </a>
      advised the narrowing of the river and the increasing of the scour by the construction of rubble jetties and the dredging of sandbanks and shoals. A particular problem was the division of the river into two shallow channels by the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dumbuck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dumbuck (page does not exist)">
       Dumbuck
      </a>
      shoal near
      <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">
       Dumbarton
      </a>
      . After
      <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">
       James Watt
      </a>
      's report on this in 1769, a jetty was constructed at
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Longhaugh_Point&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Longhaugh Point (page does not exist)">
       Longhaugh Point
      </a>
      to block off the southern channel. This being insufficient, a training wall called the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lang_Dyke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lang Dyke (page does not exist)">
       Lang Dyke
      </a>
      was built in 1773 on the Dumbuck shoal to stop water flowing over into the southern channel. In the late 18th and early 19th centuries hundreds of jetties were built out from the banks between Dumbuck and the
      <a href="/wiki/Broomielaw" title="Broomielaw">
       Broomielaw
      </a>
      quay in Glasgow itself. In some cases this resulted in an immediate deepening as the constrained water flow washed away the river bottom; in others, dredging was required.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the mid-19th century engineers took on a much greater
      <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">
       dredging
      </a>
      of the Clyde, removing millions of cubic feet of
      <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">
       silt
      </a>
      to deepen and widen the channel. The major stumbling block in the project was a massive
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_plug" title="Volcanic plug">
       volcanic plug
      </a>
      known as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elderslie_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elderslie Rock (page does not exist)">
       Elderslie Rock
      </a>
      . As a result, the work was not completed until the 1880s. At this time, the Clyde also became an important source of inspiration for artists, such as
      <a href="/wiki/John_Atkinson_Grimshaw" title="John Atkinson Grimshaw">
       John Atkinson Grimshaw
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/James_Kay_(artist)" title="James Kay (artist)">
       James Kay
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      willing to depict the new industrial era and the modern world.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shipbuilding_and_marine_engineering">
       Shipbuilding and marine engineering
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shipbuilding and marine engineering">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime,_Glasgow,_Lanarkshire,_Scotland,_UK,_1944_D20847.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="775" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime%2C_Glasgow%2C_Lanarkshire%2C_Scotland%2C_UK%2C_1944_D20847.jpg/220px-Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime%2C_Glasgow%2C_Lanarkshire%2C_Scotland%2C_UK%2C_1944_D20847.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime%2C_Glasgow%2C_Lanarkshire%2C_Scotland%2C_UK%2C_1944_D20847.jpg/330px-Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime%2C_Glasgow%2C_Lanarkshire%2C_Scotland%2C_UK%2C_1944_D20847.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime%2C_Glasgow%2C_Lanarkshire%2C_Scotland%2C_UK%2C_1944_D20847.jpg/440px-Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime%2C_Glasgow%2C_Lanarkshire%2C_Scotland%2C_UK%2C_1944_D20847.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glasgow_Shipyard-_Shipbuilding_in_Wartime,_Glasgow,_Lanarkshire,_Scotland,_UK,_1944_D20847.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A Glasgow shipyard in 1944
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The completion of the dredging was well-timed; as
      <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
       steelworking
      </a>
      grew in the city, the channel finally became navigable all the way up to Glasgow. Shipbuilding replaced trade as the major activity on the river and shipbuilding companies were rapidly establishing themselves on the river. Soon, the Clyde gained a reputation for being the best location for shipbuilding in the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
       British Empire
      </a>
      , and grew to become the world's pre-eminent shipbuilding centre.
      <i>
       Clydebuilt
      </i>
      became an industry benchmark of quality, and the river's shipyards were given contracts for prestigious ocean-going liners as well as warships, including the
      <a href="/wiki/RMS_Queen_Mary" title="RMS Queen Mary">
       Queen Mary
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Queen_Elizabeth_2" title="RMS Queen Elizabeth 2">
       Queen Elizabeth 2
      </a>
      in later years, all built in the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Clydebank" title="Clydebank">
       Clydebank
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      From the founding of the
      <a href="/wiki/Scotts_Shipbuilding_and_Engineering_Company" title="Scotts Shipbuilding and Engineering Company">
       Scott family's
      </a>
      shipyard at Greenock in the year 1712 to the present day over 25,000 ships have been built on the River Clyde and its Firth and on the
      <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">
       tributary
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/River_Kelvin" title="River Kelvin">
       River Kelvin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/River_Cart" title="River Cart">
       River Cart
      </a>
      together with boatyards at
      <a href="/wiki/Maryhill" title="Maryhill">
       Maryhill
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kirkintilloch" title="Kirkintilloch">
       Kirkintilloch
      </a>
      on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forth_%26_Clyde_Canal" title="Forth &amp; Clyde Canal">
       Forth &amp; Clyde Canal
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Blackhill,_Glasgow" title="Blackhill, Glasgow">
       Blackhill
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Monkland_Canal" title="Monkland Canal">
       Monkland Canal
      </a>
      . In the same time frame it is estimated that over 300 firms have engaged in shipbuilding on Clydeside although probably a peak of 30-40 at any one time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The shipbuilding firms became 'household names' on Clydeside but also known around the world -
      <a href="/wiki/William_Denny_and_Brothers" title="William Denny and Brothers">
       Denny
      </a>
      of Dumbarton, Scott of Greenock,
      <a href="/wiki/Lithgows" title="Lithgows">
       Lithgows
      </a>
      of Port Glasgow, Simon and
      <a href="/wiki/Lobnitz" title="Lobnitz">
       Lobnitz
      </a>
      of Renfrew,
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Stephen_and_Sons" title="Alexander Stephen and Sons">
       Alexander Stephen and Sons
      </a>
      of Linthouse,
      <a href="/wiki/Fairfield_Shipbuilding_and_Engineering_Company" title="Fairfield Shipbuilding and Engineering Company">
       Fairfield
      </a>
      of Govan,
      <a href="/wiki/A._%26_J._Inglis" title="A. &amp; J. Inglis">
       Inglis
      </a>
      of Pointhouse,
      <a href="/wiki/Barclay_Curle" title="Barclay Curle">
       Barclay Curle
      </a>
      of Whiteinch,
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Connell_and_Company" title="Charles Connell and Company">
       Connell
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yarrow_Shipbuilders" title="Yarrow Shipbuilders">
       Yarrow
      </a>
      of Scotstoun to name but a few. Almost as famous were the engineering firms that supplied the machinery to drive these vessels, the boilers and pumps and steering gear - Rankin &amp; Blackmore, Hastie's and
      <a href="/wiki/John_G._Kincaid_%26_Company" title="John G. Kincaid &amp; Company">
       Kincaid
      </a>
      's of Greenock, Rowan's of Finnieston,
      <a href="/wiki/Weir_Group" title="Weir Group">
       Weir's
      </a>
      of Cathcart, Howden's of Tradeston and
      <a href="/wiki/Doosan_Babcock" title="Doosan Babcock">
       Babcock &amp; Wilcox
      </a>
      of Renfrew.
     </p>
     <p>
      One Clyde 'shipyard' was not even located on one of these waterways -
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alley_%26_MacLellan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alley &amp; MacLellan (page does not exist)">
       Alley &amp; MacLellan
      </a>
      's Sentinel Works in Jessie Street at
      <a href="/wiki/Polmadie" title="Polmadie">
       Polmadie
      </a>
      is around half a mile from the Clyde yet it is reputed to have constructed over 500 vessels, many of which were pre-assembled then 'knocked down' to kit form for despatch to some far distant and remote location - one such vessel being the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Chauncy_Maples" title="SS Chauncy Maples">
       SS Chauncy Maples
      </a>
      , still in service on
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Malawi" title="Lake Malawi">
       Lake Malawi
      </a>
      . Clyde Shipbuilding reached its peak in the years just before WW1 and it is estimated that over 370 ships were completed in 1913.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Yachting_and_yachtbuilding">
       Yachting and yachtbuilding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Yachting and yachtbuilding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first recorded Clyde racing yacht, a 46-ton cutter, was built by Scotts of Greenock in 1803, and the great Scottish yacht designer William Fyfe did not start designing yachts until 1807. The first yacht club on the Clyde was the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Yacht_Club" title="Northern Yacht Club">
       Northern Yacht Club
      </a>
      , which appeared in 1824 and received its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" title="Royal Charter">
       Royal Charter
      </a>
      in 1831. The club was founded to organise and encourage the sport, and by 1825 Scottish and Irish clubs were racing against each other on the Clyde. However, yachting and yacht building didn't really take off until the middle of the 19th century.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Clyde became famous worldwide for its significant contribution to yachting and yachtbuilding and was the home of many notable designers:
      <a href="/wiki/William_Fife" title="William Fife">
       William Fife III
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Mylne" title="Alfred Mylne">
       Alfred Mylne
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/George_Lennox_Watson" title="George Lennox Watson">
       G L Watson
      </a>
      ; E McGruer; David Boyd. It was also the home of many famous yacht yards.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Robertson_and_Sons_Ltd_(Yachtbuilders)" title="Alexander Robertson and Sons Ltd (Yachtbuilders)">
       Robertson's Yard
      </a>
      started repairing boats in a small workshop at Sandbank in 1876, and went on to become one of the foremost wooden boat builders on the Clyde. The 'golden years' of Robertson's yard were in the early 20th century when they started building classic 12- and 15-metre racing yachts. More than 55 boats were built by Robertson's in preparation for the First World War and the yard remained busy even during the Great Depression in the 1930s, as many wealthy businessmen developed a passion for yacht racing on the Clyde. During World War II the yard was devoted to Admiralty work, producing large, high-speed
      <a href="/wiki/Fairmile_Marine" title="Fairmile Marine">
       Fairmile Marine
      </a>
      Motor Boats (MTB's - Motor Torpedo Boats- and MGB's - Motor Gun Boats). After the war the yard built the successful one-class Loch Longs and two David Boyd designed 12-metre challengers for the America's Cup:
      <i>
       Sceptre
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (1958) and
      <i>
       Sovereign
      </i>
      (1964). Due to difficult business conditions in 1965, the yard was turned over to GRP production work (mainly Pipers and Etchells) until it closed in 1980. During its 104-year history, Robertson's Yard built 500 boats, many of which are still sailing today.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shipbuilding_decline">
       Shipbuilding decline
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shipbuilding decline">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="567" data-file-width="850" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG/220px-HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG/330px-HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG/440px-HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HMS_Daring_at_Scotstoun.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Although diminished from its early 20th century heights,
        <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">
         shipbuilding
        </a>
        remains an important industry on Clydeside
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The downfall of the Clyde as a major
      <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">
       industrial
      </a>
      centre came during and post-
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . Clydebank in particular was targeted by the
      <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">
       Luftwaffe
      </a>
      and sustained heavy damage. The immediate postwar period saw a severe reduction in warship orders which was balanced by a prolonged boom in merchant shipbuilding. By the end of the 1950s, however, the rise of other shipbuilding nations, recapitalised and highly productive, made many
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       European
      </a>
      yards uncompetitive. Several Clydeside yards booked a series of loss-making contracts in the hope of weathering the storm. However, by the mid-1960s, shipbuilding on the Clyde was becoming increasingly uneconomic and potentially faced collapse. This culminated in the closure of
      <a href="/wiki/Harland_and_Wolff" title="Harland and Wolff">
       Harland and Wolff
      </a>
      's Linthouse yard and a bankruptcy crisis facing Fairfields of Govan. The Government responded by creating the
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_Clyde_Shipbuilders" title="Upper Clyde Shipbuilders">
       Upper Clyde Shipbuilders
      </a>
      consortium. After the consortium's controversial collapse in 1971, the Labour government of
      <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">
       James Callaghan
      </a>
      later passed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_and_Shipbuilding_Industries_Act" title="Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Act">
       Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Act
      </a>
      which nationalised most of the Clyde's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipyards" title="Shipyards">
       shipyards
      </a>
      and grouped them with other major British shipyards as
      <a href="/wiki/British_Shipbuilders" title="British Shipbuilders">
       British Shipbuilders
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Today, two major shipyards remain in operation on the Upper Clyde; they are owned by the naval defence contractor,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Surface_Ships" title="BAE Systems Surface Ships">
       BAE Systems Surface Ships
      </a>
      , which specialises in the design and construction of technologically advanced warships for the Royal Navy and other navies around the world. The two yards are the former
      <a href="/wiki/Yarrow_Shipbuilders" title="Yarrow Shipbuilders">
       Yarrow
      </a>
      yard at
      <a href="/wiki/Scotstoun" title="Scotstoun">
       Scotstoun
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Fairfield_Shipbuilding_and_Engineering_Company" title="Fairfield Shipbuilding and Engineering Company">
       Fairfields
      </a>
      at Govan. There is also the
      <a href="/wiki/King_George_V_Dock,_Glasgow" title="King George V Dock, Glasgow">
       King George V Dock
      </a>
      , operated by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clyde_Port_Authority" title="Clyde Port Authority">
       Clyde Port Authority
      </a>
      . On the Lower Clyde, the privately owned
      <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Shipbuilders" title="Ferguson Shipbuilders">
       Ferguson Shipbuilders
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Port_Glasgow" title="Port Glasgow">
       Port Glasgow
      </a>
      is the last survivor of the many shipyards that once dominated Port Glasgow and
      <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">
       Greenock
      </a>
      - its mainstay being the construction of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RORO" title="RORO">
       car ferries
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Regeneration">
       Regeneration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=River_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Regeneration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Waterfront_Regeneration" title="Clyde Waterfront Regeneration">
       Clyde Waterfront Regeneration
      </a>
      project is expected to attract investment of up to 5.6bn in the area from Glasgow Green to Dumbarton.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_gardening" title="Market gardening">
       Market gardens
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Garden_centre" title="Garden centre">
       garden centres
      </a>
      have grown up on the fertile
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_plain" title="Flood plain">
       plains
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clyde_Valley" title="Clyde Valley">
       Clyde Valley
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">
       Tourism
      </a>
      has also brought many back to the riverside, especially in Glasgow where former docklands have given way to housing and amenities on the banks in the city, for instance, the
      <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Harbour" title="Glasgow Harbour">
       Glasgow Harbour
      </a>
      project, the
      <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Science_Centre" title="Glasgow Science Centre">
       Glasgow Science Centre
      </a>
      , and the creation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Exhibition_and_Conference_Centre" title="Scottish Exhibition and Conference Centre">
       Scottish Exhibition and Conference Centre
      </a>
      . With the migration of the commercial Port of Glasgow downstream to the deeper waters of the
      <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">
       Firth of Clyde
      </a>
      , the river has been extensively cleaned up, once having a very poor reputation for pollution and
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">
       sewage
      </a>
      , in order to make it suitable for recreational use.
     </p>
