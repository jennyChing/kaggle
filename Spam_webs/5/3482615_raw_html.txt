     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Metropolitan Cathedral of the Assumption of the Most Blessed Virgin Mary into Heaven
      </b>
      (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Catedral Metropolitana de la Asuncin de la Santsima Virgen Mara a los cielos
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is the largest
      <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">
       cathedral
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
       Americas
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and seat of the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Mexico" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Mexico">
       Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Mexico
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-View_of_Guadalupe_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-View_of_Guadalupe-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is situated atop the former
      <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">
       Aztec
      </a>
      sacred precinct near the
      <a href="/wiki/Templo_Mayor" title="Templo Mayor">
       Templo Mayor
      </a>
      on the northern side of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plaza_de_la_Constituci%C3%B3n" title="Plaza de la Constitucin">
       Plaza de la Constitucin
      </a>
      in Downtown Mexico City. The cathedral was built in sections from 1573 to 1813 around the original church that was constructed soon after the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest" title="Spanish conquest">
       Spanish conquest
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">
       Tenochtitlan
      </a>
      , eventually replacing it entirely. Spanish architect
      <a href="/wiki/Claudio_de_Arciniega" title="Claudio de Arciniega">
       Claudio de Arciniega
      </a>
      planned the construction, drawing inspiration from
      <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">
       Gothic
      </a>
      cathedrals in Spain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encarta_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Encarta-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Catedral_Metropolitana,_M%C3%A9xico_D.F.,_M%C3%A9xico,_2013-10-16,_DD_95.JPG" title="Enlarge">

        Organ

     <p>
      The cathedral has four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" title="Faade">
       faades
      </a>
      which contain
      <a href="/wiki/Portal_(architecture)" title="Portal (architecture)">
       portals
      </a>
      flanked with columns and statues. The two
      <a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">
       bell towers
      </a>
      contain a total of 25 bells. The tabernacle, adjacent to the cathedral, contains the
      <a href="/wiki/Baptistery" title="Baptistery">
       baptistery
      </a>
      and serves to register the parishioners. There are two large, ornate altars, a
      <a href="/wiki/Sacristy" title="Sacristy">
       sacristy
      </a>
      , and a choir in the cathedral. Fourteen of the cathedral's sixteen
      <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">
       chapels
      </a>
      are open to the public. Each chapel is dedicated to a different saint or saints, and each was sponsored by a
      <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">
       religious guild
      </a>
      . The chapels contain ornate
      <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">
       altars
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece" title="Altarpiece">
       altarpieces
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Retablo" title="Retablo">
       retablos
      </a>
      , paintings, furniture and sculptures. The cathedral is home to two of the largest 18th-century
      <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">
       organs
      </a>
      in the Americas. There is a
      <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">
       crypt
      </a>
      underneath the cathedral that holds the remains of many former archbishops.
     </p>
     <p>
      Over the centuries, the cathedral has suffered damage. A fire in 1967 destroyed a significant part of the cathedral's interior. The restoration work that followed uncovered a number of important documents and artwork that had previously been hidden. Although a solid foundation was built for the cathedral,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the soft clay soil it is built on has been a threat to its structural integrity. Dropping
      <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">
       water tables
      </a>
      and accelerated sinking caused the structure to be added to the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Monuments_Fund" title="World Monuments Fund">
       World Monuments Fund
      </a>
      list of the 100Most Endangered Sites. Reconstruction work beginning in the 1990s stabilized the cathedral and it was removed from the endangered list in 2000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sacred_Destinations_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sacred_Destinations-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Aztec_Empire" title="Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire">
       Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquistadors" title="Conquistadors">
       conquistadors
      </a>
      decided to build their church on the site of the
      <a href="/wiki/Templo_Mayor" title="Templo Mayor">
       Templo Mayor
      </a>
      of the Aztec city of
      <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">
       Tenochtitlan
      </a>
      to consolidate Spanish power over the newly conquered domain.
      <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernn Corts">
       Hernn Corts
      </a>
      and the other conquistadors used the stones from the destroyed temple of the Aztec god of war
      <a href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">
       Huitzilopochtli
      </a>
      , principal deity of the Aztecs, to build the church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sacred_Destinations_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sacred_Destinations-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Corts ordered the original church's construction after he returned from exploring what is now
      <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
       Honduras
      </a>
      . Architect Martn de Seplveda was the first director of this project from 1524 to 1532.
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Zum%C3%A1rraga" title="Juan de Zumrraga">
       Juan de Zumrraga
      </a>
      , the first
      <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">
       Bishop
      </a>
      of the first
      <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">
       See
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
       New World
      </a>
      , established in the
      <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_New_Spain" title="Viceroyalty of New Spain">
       Viceroyalty of New Spain
      </a>
      , promoted this church's completion. Zumrraga's
      <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">
       Cathedral
      </a>
      was located in the northeast portion of what is now the cathedral. It had three naves separated by three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuscan_column" title="Tuscan column">
       Tuscan columns
      </a>
      . The central roof was ridged with intricate carvings done by Juan Salcedo Espinosa and gilded by Francisco de Zumaya and
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_de_la_Concha" title="Andrs de la Concha">
       Andrs de la Concha
      </a>
      . The main door was probably of
      <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">
       Renaissance style
      </a>
      . The choir area had 48seats made of
      <a href="/wiki/Pinus_ayacahuite" title="Pinus ayacahuite">
       ayacahuite
      </a>
      wood crafted by Adrian Suster and Juan Montao. However, this church was soon considered inadequate for the growing importance of the capital of New Spain.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1544, ecclesiastical authorities in
      <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">
       Valladolid
      </a>
      ordered the creation of new and more sumptuous cathedral. In 1552, an agreement was reached whereby the cost of the new cathedral would be shared by the Spanish crown,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encomendero" title="Encomendero">
       encomenderos
      </a>
      and the Indians under the direct authority of the archbishop of New Spain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cathedral was begun by being built around the existing church in 1573. When enough of the cathedral was built to house basic functions, the original church was demolished to enable construction to continue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sacred_Destinations_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sacred_Destinations-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">
       Construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1812" data-file-width="1188" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg/170px-Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg/255px-Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg/340px-Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mexico_DF_Cathedral.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Interior of Mexico City Cathedral
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The cathedral was constructed over a period of over two centuries, between 1573 and 1813. Its design is a mixture of three architectural styles that predominated during the colonial period,
      <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">
       Renaissance
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">
       Baroque
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">
       Neo-classic
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Initial plans for the new cathedral were drawn up and work on the foundation began in 1562. The decision to have the cathedral face south instead of east was made in 1570.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the same year, construction commenced, working from the Gothic designs and models created by
      <a href="/wiki/Claudio_de_Arciniega" title="Claudio de Arciniega">
       Claudio de Arciniega
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Miguel_de_Ag%C3%BCero" title="Juan Miguel de Agero">
       Juan Miguel de Agero
      </a>
      , inspired by cathedrals found in Spanish cities such as
      <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">
       Valladolid
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Mlaga">
       Mlaga
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encarta_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Encarta-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of the muddy subsoil of the site, work on the foundation continued past the work on the walls to 1581. In 1585, work on the first of the cathedral's chapels began and by 1615, the cathedral's walls reached to about half of their final height.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Construction of the interior of the current cathedral began in 1623
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ciudadmexico_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ciudadmexico-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and what is now the vestry was where Mass was conducted after the first church was finally torn down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arsvirtual-history_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-arsvirtual-history-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1629, work was interrupted by flooding,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      over two metres in depth. Parts of the city were damaged, especially around the main plaza or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zocalo" title="Zocalo">
       Zocalo
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex2000_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex2000-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because of such damage, this site was almost abandoned and a new cathedral project was begun in the hills of the
      <a href="/wiki/Tacubaya" title="Tacubaya">
       Tacubaya
      </a>
      area to the west.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite these problems, the project continued in its current location, and under the direction of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Luis_G%C3%B3mez_de_Transmonte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Luis Gmez de Transmonte (page does not exist)">
       Luis Gmez de Transmonte
      </a>
      , the interior was finished and consecrated in 1667.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ciudadmexico_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ciudadmexico-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cathedral still lacked bell towers, the complete front facade, and many of the other features it has now at the beginning of the 18th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1787,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Damian_Ortiz_de_Castro" title="Jos Damian Ortiz de Castro">
       Jos Damian Ortiz de Castro
      </a>
      was in charge of finishing work on the cathedral. He did most of the work on the bell towers, putting in most of the
      <a href="/wiki/Fretwork" title="Fretwork">
       fretwork
      </a>
      and capping them with roofs in the shape of bells. With his death in 1793, he did not live to see the cathedral completed, and
      <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Tols%C3%A1" title="Manuel Tols">
       Manuel Tols
      </a>
      finished the cathedral by adding the
      <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">
       cupola
      </a>
      , the central front facade, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balustrade" title="Balustrade">
       balustrades
      </a>
      , and the statues of Faith, Hope and Charity at the top of the front facade. Tolsa's work was the last major construction to the cathedral and the appearance it had when he finished is the basic look the cathedral has today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cathedral faces south and is approximately 54.5 metres (179ft) wide and 110 metres (360ft) long. It consists of two
      <a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">
       bell towers
      </a>
      , a central
      <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">
       dome
      </a>
      , three main
      <a href="/wiki/Portal_(architecture)" title="Portal (architecture)">
       portals
      </a>
      , five
      <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">
       naves
      </a>
      , 51vaults, 74arches and 40columns. Inside the cathedral are five large
      <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">
       altars
      </a>
      , sixteen
      <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">
       chapels
      </a>
      , a choir area, a corridor, capitulary room, and sacristy. The cathedral has approximately 150windows.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior">
       Exterior
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exterior">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Facades_and_portals">
       Facades and portals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Facades and portals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG/150px-MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG/225px-MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG/300px-MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MainPortalCathedralDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Main portal with view of clock
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The main facade of the cathedral faces south. The main
      <a href="/wiki/Portal_(architecture)" title="Portal (architecture)">
       portal
      </a>
      is centered in the main
      <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">
       facade
      </a>
      and is the highest of the cathedral's three portals. Statues of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">
       Saint Peter
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">
       Paul the Apostle
      </a>
      stand between the columns of the portal, while
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew" title="Saint Andrew">
       Saint Andrew
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_the_Just" title="James the Just">
       James the Just
      </a>
      are depicted on the secondary doorway. In the center of this doorway is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_relief#High_relief" title="High relief">
       high relief
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assumption_of_the_Virgin_Mary" title="Assumption of the Virgin Mary">
       Assumption of the Virgin Mary
      </a>
      , to whom the cathedral is dedicated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This image is flanked by images of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Matthew" title="Saint Matthew">
       Saint Matthew
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew" title="Saint Andrew">
       Saint Andrew
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Mexico" title="Coat of arms of Mexico">
       coat of arms of Mexico
      </a>
      is above the doorway, with the eagle's wings outstretched. There is a clock tower at the very top of the portal with statues representing Faith, Hope and Charity, which was created by sculptor
      <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Tols%C3%A1" title="Manuel Tols">
       Manuel Tols
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The west facade was constructed in 1688 and rebuilt in 1804. It has a three-section portal with images of the
      <a href="/wiki/Four_Evangelists" title="Four Evangelists">
       Four Evangelists
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The west portal has high reliefs depicting
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      handing the
      <a href="/wiki/Keys_of_Heaven" title="Keys of Heaven">
       Keys of Heaven
      </a>
      to Saint Peter.
     </p>
     <p>
      The east facade is similar to the west facade. The reliefs on the east portal show a ship carrying the four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostles" title="Apostles">
       apostles
      </a>
      , with Saint Peter at the helm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The title of this relief is
      <i>
       The ship of the Church sailing the seas of Eternity.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The northern facade, built during the 16thcentury in the
      <a href="/wiki/Herrerian" title="Herrerian">
       Renaissance Herrera
      </a>
      style, is oldest part of the cathedral and was named after
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Herrera" title="Juan de Herrera">
       Juan de Herrera
      </a>
      , architect of the
      <a href="/wiki/El_Escorial" title="El Escorial">
       El Escorial
      </a>
      monastery in Spain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the eastern and western facades are older than most of the rest of the building, their third level has
      <a href="/wiki/Solomonic_column" title="Solomonic column">
       Solomonic columns
      </a>
      which are associated with the Baroque period.
     </p>
     <p>
      All the high reliefs of the portals of the cathedral were inspired by the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">
       Flemish
      </a>
      painter
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">
       Peter Paul Rubens
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bell_towers">
       Bell towers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bell towers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="353" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg/170px-Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg/255px-Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg/340px-Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mexico_City_Cathedral_bell_tower.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The west bell tower
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">
       bell towers
      </a>
      are the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Xalapa" title="Xalapa">
       Xalapan
      </a>
      artist Jos Damin Ortiz de Castro. They are capped with bell-shaped roofs made of
      <a href="/wiki/Tezontle" title="Tezontle">
       tezontle
      </a>
      covered in
      <i>
       chiluca
      </i>
      , a white stone. Ortiz de Castro was in charge of the cathedral's construction in the latter half of the 18thcentury until he died, unexpectedly.
      <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Tols%C3%A1" title="Manuel Tols">
       Manuel Tols
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">
       Valencia
      </a>
      , who had built other notable buildings in Mexico City, was hired to finish the cathedral. At this point, the cathedral had already been 240years in the making. He added the neo-Classic structure housing the clock, the statues of the three theological virtues (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faith,_Hope,_and_Charity" title="Faith, Hope, and Charity">
       Faith, Hope, and Charity
      </a>
      ), the high
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balustrade" title="Balustrade">
       balustrade
      </a>
      surrounding the building, and the dome that rises over the
      <a href="/wiki/Transept" title="Transept">
       transept
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The cathedral has 25
      <a href="/wiki/Bell_(instrument)" title="Bell (instrument)">
       bells
      </a>
      eighteen hang in the east bell tower and seven in the west tower. The largest bell is named the
      <i>
       Santa Maria de Guadalupe
      </i>
      and weighs around 13,000 kilograms (29,000lb). Other major bells are named the
      <i>
       Doa Maria
      </i>
      , which weighs 6,900 kilograms (15,200lb), and
      <i>
       La Ronca
      </i>
      ("the hoarse one"), named so because of its harsh tone. Doa Maria and La Ronca were placed in 1653 while the largest bell was placed later in 1793.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The statues in the west tower are the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Zacar%C3%ADas_Cora" title="Jos Zacaras Cora">
       Jos Zacaras Cora
      </a>
      and represent
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_VII" title="Pope Gregory VII">
       Pope Gregory VII
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine" title="Saint Augustine">
       Saint Augustine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Leander_of_Seville" title="Leander of Seville">
       Leander of Seville
      </a>
      , St.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulgentius_of_%C3%89cija" title="Fulgentius of cija">
       Fulgentius of cija
      </a>
      , St.
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Xavier" title="Francis Xavier">
       Francis Xavier
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Barbara" title="Saint Barbara">
       Saint Barbara
      </a>
      . The statues in the east tower are by
      <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Crist%C3%B3bal_Sandoval" title="Santiago Cristbal Sandoval">
       Santiago Cristbal Sandoval
      </a>
      and depict Emilio,
      <a href="/wiki/Rose_of_Lima" title="Rose of Lima">
       Rose of Lima
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">
       Mary (mother of Jesus)
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ambrogio" title="Ambrogio">
       Ambrogio
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">
       Jerome
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Jesus" title="Philip of Jesus">
       Philip of Jesus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">
       Hippolytus of Rome
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Isidore_the_Laborer" title="Isidore the Laborer">
       Isidore the Laborer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1947, a novice bell ringer died in an accident when he tried to move one of the bells while standing under it. The bell swung back and hit him in the head, killing him instantly. The bell was then "punished" by removing the clapper. In the following years, the bell was known as
      <i>
       la castigada
      </i>
      ("the punished one"), or
      <i>
       la muda
      </i>
      ("the mute one"). In 2000, the clapper was reinstalled in the bell.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eluniversal_lacastigada_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-eluniversal_lacastigada-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In October2007, a
      <a href="/wiki/Time_capsule" title="Time capsule">
       time capsule
      </a>
      was found inside the stone ball base of a cross, in the southern bell tower of the cathedral. It was placed in 1742, supposedly to protect the building from harm. The lead box was filled with religious artifacts, coins and parchments and hidden in a hollow stone ball. The ball was marked with the date of 14May1791, when the building's topmost stone was laid. A new time capsule will be placed in the stone ball when it is closed again.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timecapsule_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-timecapsule-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tabernacle">
       Tabernacle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tabernacle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG/170px-EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG/255px-EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG/340px-EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EntranceSagrariumDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Tabernacle's southern faade, opening out towards the Zcalo.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Situated to the right of the main cathedral, the Metropolitan Tabernacle (Spanish:
      <i>
       Sagrario Metropolitana
      </i>
      ) was built by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lorenzo_Rodr%C3%ADguez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lorenzo Rodrguez (page does not exist)">
       Lorenzo Rodrguez
      </a>
      during the height of the Baroque period between 1749 and 1760,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to house the archives and vestments of the archbishop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lonely_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lonely-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It also functioned and continues to function as a place to receive
      <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">
       Eucharist
      </a>
      and register parishioners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oficial_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oficial-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first church built on the cathedral site also had a tabernacle, but its exact location is unknown. During the construction of the cathedral, the
      <a href="/wiki/Tabernacle" title="Tabernacle">
       tabernacle
      </a>
      was housed in what are now the Chapels of San Isidro and Our Lady of Agony of Granada. However, in the 18thcentury, it was decided to build a structure that was separate, but still connected, to the main cathedral.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oficial_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-oficial-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is constructed of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tezontle" title="Tezontle">
        tezontle
       </a>
      </i>
      (a reddish porous volcanic rock) and white stone in the shape of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_cross" title="Greek cross">
       Greek cross
      </a>
      with its southern facade faces the Zcalo. It is connected to the main cathedral via the Chapel of San Isidro.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG/170px-MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG/255px-MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG/340px-MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MainAltarSagrariumDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Tabernacle's main altar
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The interiors of each wing have separate uses. In the west wing is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptistry" title="Baptistry">
       baptistry
      </a>
      , in the north is the main altar, the main entrance and a notary area, separated by inside corner walls made of
      <i>
       chiluca
      </i>
      stone and tezontle. Chiluca, a white stone, covers the walls and floors and the tezontle frames the doors and windows. At the
      <a href="/wiki/Crossing_(architecture)" title="Crossing (architecture)">
       crossing
      </a>
      of the structure is an octagonal
      <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">
       dome
      </a>
      framed by arches that form curved triangles where they meet at the top of the dome.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oficial_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-oficial-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The principal altar is in the ornate
      <i>
       Churrigueresque
      </i>
      style and crafted by indigenous artist
      <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Pati%C3%B1o_Ixtolinque" title="Pedro Patio Ixtolinque">
       Pedro Patio Ixtolinque
      </a>
      . It was inaugurated in 1829.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The exterior of the Baroque styled tabernacle is almost entirely adorned with decorations, such as curiously shaped niche shelves, floating drapes and many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherubs" title="Cherubs">
       cherubs
      </a>
      . Carvings of fruits such as grapes and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pomegranates" title="Pomegranates">
       pomegranates
      </a>
      have been created to in the shape of ritual offerings, symbolizing the
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_of_Christ" title="Blood of Christ">
       Blood of Christ
      </a>
      and the Church. Among the floral elements, roses, daisies, and various types of four-petalled flowers can be found, including the indigenous
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chalchihuite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chalchihuite (page does not exist)">
        chalchihuite
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The tabernacle has two main outside entrances; one to the south, facing the Zcalo and the other facing east toward Seminario Street. The southern faade is more richly decorated than the east faade. It has a theme of glorifying the Eucharist with images of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostles" title="Apostles">
       Apostles
      </a>
      , Church Fathers, saints who founded
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Consecrated_Life" title="Institute of Consecrated Life">
       religious orders
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">
       martyrs
      </a>
      as well as scenes from the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      . Zoomorphic reliefs can be found along with the anthropologic reliefs, including a rampaging lion, and the eagle from the
      <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Mexico" title="Coat of arms of Mexico">
       coat of arms of Mexico
      </a>
      . The east facade is less ambitious, but contains figures from the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
       Old Testament
      </a>
      as well as the images of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Nepomucene" title="John Nepomucene">
       John Nepomucene
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ignacio_de_Loyola" title="Ignacio de Loyola">
       Ignacio de Loyola
      </a>
      . Construction dates for the phases of the tabernacle are also inscribed here.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oficial_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-oficial-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Altars">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Altars">

     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Altar_of_Forgiveness">
       Altar of Forgiveness
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Altar of Forgiveness">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3168" data-file-width="4752" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG/220px-2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG/330px-2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG/440px-2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2013-12-22_Altar_der_Vergebung_in_der_Kathedrale_von_Mexiko-Stadt_anagoria.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Altar of Forgiveness
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Altar of Forgiveness is located at the front of the central nave. It is the first aspect of the interior that is seen upon entering the cathedral. It was the work of Spanish architect
      <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Balb%C3%A1s" title="Jernimo Balbs">
       Jernimo Balbs
      </a>
      , and represents the first use of the
      <i>
       estpite
      </i>
      column (an inverted triangle-shaped
      <a href="/wiki/Pilaster" title="Pilaster">
       pilaster
      </a>
      ) in the Americas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are two stories about how the name of this altar came about. The first states that those condemned by the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">
       Spanish Inquisition
      </a>
      were brought to the altar to ask for forgiveness in the next world before their execution. The second relates to painter
      <a href="/wiki/Simon_Pereyns" title="Simon Pereyns">
       Simon Pereyns
      </a>
      , who despite being the author of many of the works of the cathedral, was accused of
      <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy" title="Blasphemy">
       blasphemy
      </a>
      . According to the story, while Pereyns was in jail, he painted such a beautiful image of the Virgin Mary that his crime was forgiven.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This altar was damaged by fire in January 1967 but has been completely restored.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Altar_of_the_Kings">
       Altar of the Kings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Altar of the Kings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG/150px-AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG/225px-AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG/300px-AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AltarKingsCathedralDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Altar of the Kings
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Altar of the Kings was also the work of Jernimo Balbs, in Mexican
      <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">
       Baroque
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Churrigueresque" title="Churrigueresque">
       Churrigueresque
      </a>
      style.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was begun in 1718 by Balbs
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lonely_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lonely-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in cedar, and was gilded and finished by Francico Martnez, debuting in 1737.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is located at the back of the Cathedral, beyond the Altar of Forgiveness and the
      <a href="/wiki/Choir_(architecture)" title="Choir (architecture)">
       choir
      </a>
      . This altar is 13.75 metres (45.1ft) wide, 25 metres (82ft) tall and 7.5 metres (25ft) deep. Its size and depth gave rise to the nickname
      <i>
       la cueva dorada
      </i>
      ("the golden cave").
     </p>
     <p>
      It takes its name from the statues of saintly royalty which form part of its decoration,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is the oldest work in churrigueresque style in Mexico, taking 19years to complete. At the bottom, from left to right, are six female royal saints:
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Margaret_of_Scotland" title="Saint Margaret of Scotland">
       Saint Margaret of Scotland
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helena_of_Constantinople" title="Helena of Constantinople">
       Helena of Constantinople
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Hungary" title="Elisabeth of Hungary">
       Elisabeth of Hungary
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isabel_of_Portugal" title="Isabel of Portugal">
       Isabel of Portugal
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cunigunde_of_Luxemburg" title="Cunigunde of Luxemburg">
       Empress Cunegunda
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Edith_of_Wilton" title="Edith of Wilton">
       Edith of Wilton
      </a>
      . In the middle of the altar are six canonized kings, four of whom are:
      <a href="/wiki/Hermenegild" title="Hermenegild">
       Hermenegild
      </a>
      a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visigoth" title="Visigoth">
       Visigoth
      </a>
      martyr,
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor">
       Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">
       Edward the Confessor
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Casimir" title="Saint Casimir">
       Casimir
      </a>
      of Poland. Above these four are Saints
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">
       Louis of France
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_III_of_Castile" title="Ferdinand III of Castile">
       Ferdinand III of Castile
      </a>
      . In between these kings an oil painting of the
      <i>
       Adoration of the Magi
      </i>
      by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Rodriguez_Juarez" title="Juan Rodriguez Juarez">
       Juan Rodriguez Juarez
      </a>
      shows Jesus as the
      <i>
       King of kings
      </i>
      . The top portion features a painting of the
      <i>
       Assumption of Mary
      </i>
      as celestial queen flanked by oval
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bas_relief" title="Bas relief">
       bas reliefs
      </a>
      , one of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph" title="Saint Joseph">
       Saint Joseph
      </a>
      carrying the infant Jesus and the other of Saint
      <a href="/wiki/Teresa_of_%C3%81vila" title="Teresa of vila">
       Teresa of vila
      </a>
      with a quill in her hand and the Holy Spirit above her, inspiring her to write. Above this are figures of Jesus and Mary among sculptures of angels crowned with an image of God, the Father.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This altar has been under restoration since 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arsvirtual-history_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-arsvirtual-history-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sacristy">
       Sacristy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sacristy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SacristyCathedralDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/SacristyCathedralDF.JPG/170px-SacristyCathedralDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/SacristyCathedralDF.JPG/255px-SacristyCathedralDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/SacristyCathedralDF.JPG/340px-SacristyCathedralDF.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SacristyCathedralDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Dome of the Sacristy
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Herrera door opens into the
      <a href="/wiki/Sacristy" title="Sacristy">
       sacristy
      </a>
      , the oldest part of the cathedral. It is a mixture of Renaissance and Gothic styles.
     </p>
     <p>
      The walls hold large canvases painted by
      <a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_de_Villalpando" title="Cristbal de Villalpando">
       Cristbal de Villalpando
      </a>
      , such as
      <i>
       The Apotheosis of Saint Michael
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       The Triumph of the Eucharist
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       The Church Militant and the Church Triumphant
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       The Virgin of the Apocalypse
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       Virgin of the Apocalypse
      </i>
      depicts the vision of
      <a href="/wiki/John_of_Patmos" title="John of Patmos">
       John of Patmos
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epoca_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Epoca-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two other canvases,
      <i>
       Entering Jerusalem
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       The Assumption of the Virgin
      </i>
      , painted by
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Correa" title="Juan Correa">
       Juan Correa
      </a>
      , are also here.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An additional painting, attributed to
      <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Esteban_Murillo" title="Bartolom Esteban Murillo">
       Bartolom Esteban Murillo
      </a>
      , hangs in the Sacristy.
     </p>
     <p>
      On the north wall, there is a niche that holds a statue of the crucifix with a Christ image sculpted in ivory. Behind this, is another mural that depicts the
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Diego" title="Juan Diego">
       Juan Diego
      </a>
      's of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_of_Guadalupe" title="Virgin of Guadalupe">
       Our Lady of Guadalupe
      </a>
      . The Sacristy used to house Juan Diego's cloak, upon which the Virgin's image purportedly appears, but after massive flooding in 1629, it was removed from the Sacristy to better protect it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epoca_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Epoca-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A cabinet on the west wall of the Sacristy, under the
      <i>
       Virgin of the Apocalypse
      </i>
      painting, once held golden
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalice_(cup)" title="Chalice (cup)">
       chalices
      </a>
      and cups trimmed with precious stones, as well as other utensils.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1957, The wooden floor and platform around the perimeter of the Sacristy were replaced with stone.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     </div>
     <p>
      The cathedral's sixteen
      <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">
       chapels
      </a>
      were each assigned to a
      <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">
       religious guild
      </a>
      , and each is dedicated to a saint. Each of the two side naves contain seven chapels. The other two were created later on the eastern and western sides of the cathedral. These last two are not open to the public.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fourteen chapels in the east and west naves are listed below. The first seven are in the east nave, listed from north to south, and the last seven are in the west nave.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Our_Lady_of_the_Agonies_of_Granada">
       Chapel of Our Lady of the Agonies of Granada
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chapel of Our Lady of the Agonies of Granada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Our Lady of the Agonies of Granada (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de Nuestra Seora de las Angustias de Granada
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was built in the first half of the 17thcentury, and originally served as the sacristy. It is a medieval-style chapel with a ribbed
      <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">
       vault
      </a>
      and two relatively simple altarpieces. The narrow
      <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece" title="Altarpiece">
       altarpiece
      </a>
      contains an oval painting of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raphael_(angel)" title="Raphael (angel)">
        Saint Raphael, Archangel
       </a>
       and the young Tobias
      </i>
      , a 16thcentury painting attributed to Flemish painter
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_de_Vos" title="Martin de Vos">
       Martin de Vos
      </a>
      . At the top of this altarpiece is a painting of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Mount_Carmel" title="Our Lady of Mount Carmel">
        Our Lady of Mount Carmel
       </a>
      </i>
      , and above this is a painting of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">
        Last Supper
       </a>
      </i>
      . At the back of the chapel is a churrigueresque painting of
      <i>
       Our Lady of the Agonies of Granada
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Saint_Isidore">
       Chapel of Saint Isidore
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chapel of Saint Isidore">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Chapel of
      <a href="/wiki/Isidore_the_Laborer" title="Isidore the Laborer">
       Saint Isidore
      </a>
      (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de San Isidro
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was originally built as an annex between 1624 and 1627, and was once used as the
      <a href="/wiki/Baptistery" title="Baptistery">
       baptistery
      </a>
      . Its vault contains plaster casts representing Faith, Hope, Charity, and Justice, considered to be basic values in the Catholic religion. After the Tabernacle was built, it was converted into a chapel and its door was reworked in a churrigueresque style.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_the_Immaculate_Conception">
       Chapel of the Immaculate Conception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chapel of the Immaculate Conception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG/170px-ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG/255px-ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG/340px-ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ChapelImmacConceptionDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Chapel of the Immaculate Conception
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Chapel of the Immaculate Conception (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de la Inmaculada Concepcin
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was built between 1642 and 1648. It has a churrigueresque altarpiece which, due to the lack of columns, most likely dates from the 18thcentury. The altar is framed with moldinginstead of columnsand a painting of the Immaculate Conception presides over it. The altar is surrounded by paintings by
      <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_Ibarra" title="Jos de Ibarra">
       Jos de Ibarra
      </a>
      relating to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_of_Christ" title="Passion of Christ">
       Passion of Christ
      </a>
      and various saints. The chapel also contains a canvas of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Christopher" title="Saint Christopher">
       Saint Christopher
      </a>
      painted by Simon Pereyns in 1588, and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">
        Flagellation
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltasar_de_Echave_Orio" title="Baltasar de Echave Orio">
       Baltasar de Echave Orio
      </a>
      , painted in 1618. The altarpiece on the right side
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is also dedicated to the
      <a href="/wiki/Immaculate_Conception" title="Immaculate Conception">
       Immaculate Conception
      </a>
      and was donated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Nacional_Monte_de_Piedad" title="Nacional Monte de Piedad">
       College of Saints Peter and Paul
      </a>
      . This chapel holds the remains of
      <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">
       Franciscan
      </a>
      friar
      <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Margil" title="Antonio Margil">
       Antonio Margil de Jess
      </a>
      who was evangelized in what is now the north of Mexico.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Our_Lady_of_Guadalupe">
       Chapel of Our Lady of Guadalupe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Chapel of Our Lady of Guadalupe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Our Lady of Guadalupe (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de Nuestra Seora de Guadalupe
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was built in 1660. It was the first baptistery of the cathedral and for a long time was the site for the Brotherhood of the Most Holy Sacrament, which had many powerful benefactors. It is decorated in a 19thcentury neo-classic style by the architect Antonio Gonzalez Vazquez, director of the Academy of San Carlos. The main altarpiece is dedicated to the Virgin of Guadalupe and the sides altars are dedicated to
      <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">
       John the Baptist
      </a>
      and San Luis Gonzaga respectively.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Our_Lady_of_Antigua">
       Chapel of Our Lady of Antigua
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Chapel of Our Lady of Antigua">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="2304" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG/170px-Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG/255px-Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG/340px-Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora_la_Antigua.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Our Lady of Antigua
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Our Lady of Antigua (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de Nuestra Seora de La Antigua
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was sponsored and built between 1653 and 1660 by a brotherhood of musicians and organists, which promoted devotion to this Virgin. Its altarpiece contains a painting of the Virgin, a copy of one found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Seville_Cathedral" title="Seville Cathedral">
       Cathedral of Seville
      </a>
      . This copy was brought to New Spain by a merchant. Two other paintings show the birth of the Virgin and her presentation. Both were painted by Nicols Rodriguez Jurez.
     </p>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Saint_Peter">
       Chapel of Saint Peter
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Chapel of Saint Peter">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Saint Peter (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de San Pedro
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was built between 1615 and 1620, and contains three highly decorated Baroque altarpieces from the 17thcentury. The altar at the back is dedicated to Saint Peter, whose sculpture presides over the altar. It is surrounded by early 17thcentury paintings relating to his life, painted by Baltasar de Echave Orio. To the right is an altarpiece dedicated to the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Family" title="Holy Family">
       Holy Family
      </a>
      , with two paintings by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_de_Aguilera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan de Aguilera (page does not exist)">
       Juan de Aguilera
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
       Florence
      </a>
      called
      <i>
       The Holy Family in the workshop of Saint Joseph
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Birth of the Savior
      </i>
      . The altarpiece to the left of the main altarpiece is dedicated to
      <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_of_Lisieux" title="Thrse of Lisieux">
       Saint Theresa of Jesus
      </a>
      whose image also appears in the chapel's window. It includes four paintings on sheets of metal that depict scenes from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_of_Jesus" title="Birth of Jesus">
       birth of Jesus
      </a>
      . Five oil paintings illustrate scenes from the life of Saint Theresa, and above this is a semi-circular painting of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronation_of_Mary" title="Coronation of Mary">
       coronation of Mary
      </a>
      . All these works were created in the 17thcentury by Baltasar de Echave y Rioja.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This chapel is home to the
      <b>
       Nio Cautivo
      </b>
      (Captive Child) a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1o_Dios_of_Mexico" title="Nio Dios of Mexico">
       Child Jesus
      </a>
      figure that was brought to Mexico from Spain. It was sculpted in the 16th century by Juan Martnez Montaez in Spain and purchased by the cathedral. However, on its way to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">
       Veracruz
      </a>
      , pirates attacked the ship it was on and sacked it. To get the image back, a large ransom was paid. Today, the image is in the Chapel of San Pedro or De las Reliquias.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cjaramillo_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cjaramillo-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Traditionally, the image has been petitioned by those seeking release from restrictions or traps, especially financial problems or drug addiction or alcoholism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secuestrados_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-secuestrados-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cult to the Nio Cautivo is considered to be "inactive" by INAH.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arraigo_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-arraigo-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, this particular image has made a comeback since 2000 as one to petition when a family member is abducted and held for ransom.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-secuestrados_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-secuestrados-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Christ_and_of_the_Reliquaries">
       Chapel of Christ and of the Reliquaries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Chapel of Christ and of the Reliquaries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG/170px-ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG/255px-ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG/340px-ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ChapelChristConquistadorsDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Chapel of Christ of the Reliquaries
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Christ and of the Reliquaries (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla del Santo Cristo y de las Reliquias
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was built in 1615 and designed with ultra-Baroque details which are often difficult to see in the poorly lit interior.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was originally known as the
      <i>
       Christ of the Conquistadors
      </i>
      . That name came from an image of Christ that was supposedly donated to the cathedral by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Charles_V" title="Emperor Charles V">
       Emperor Charles V
      </a>
      . Over time, so many
      <a href="/wiki/Reliquary" title="Reliquary">
       reliquaries
      </a>
      were left on its main altar that its name was eventually changed. Of 17thcentury ornamentation, the main altarpiece alternates between carvings of rich
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foliage" title="Foliage">
       foliage
      </a>
      and small heads on its columns in the main portion and small sculptures of angels on its
      <a href="/wiki/Telamon" title="Telamon">
       telamons
      </a>
      in the secondary portion. Its niches hold sculptures of saints framing the main body. Its crucifix is from the 17thcentury. The
      <a href="/wiki/Predella" title="Predella">
       predella
      </a>
      is finished with sculptures of angels, and also contains small 17thpaintings of martyred saints by Juan de Herrera. Behind these paintings, hidden compartments contain some of the numerous reliquaries left here. Its main painting was done by Jose de Ibarra and dated 1737. Surrounding the altar is a series of paintings on canvas, depicting the
      <i>
       Passion of Christ
      </i>
      by Jose Villegas, painted in the 17thcentury. On the right-hand wall, an altar dedicated to the Virgin of the Confidence is decorated with numerous churrigueresque figurines tucked away in niches, columns and top pieces.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_the_Holy_Angels_and_Archangels">
       Chapel of the Holy Angels and Archangels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Chapel of the Holy Angels and Archangels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Chapel of the Holy Angels and Archangels (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de los ngeles
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was finished in 1665 with Baroque altarpieces decorated with
      <a href="/wiki/Solomonic_column" title="Solomonic column">
       Solomonic columns
      </a>
      . It is dedicated to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archangel_Michael" title="Archangel Michael">
       Archangel Michael
      </a>
      , who is depicted as a medieval
      <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">
       knight
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It contains a large main altarpiece with two smaller altarpieces both decorated by Juan Correa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lonely_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-lonely-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The main altarpiece is dedicated to the seven archangels, who are represented by sculptures, in niches surrounding images of Saint Joseph, Mary and Christ. Above this scene are the Holy Spirit and
      <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">
       God the Father
      </a>
      . The left-hand altarpiece is of similar design and is dedicated to the Guardian Angel, whose sculpture is surrounded with pictures arranged to show the angelic hierarchy. To the left of this, a scene shows Saint Peter being released from prison, and to the right, Saul, later Saint Paul, being knocked from his horse, painted by Juan Correa in 1714. The right-hand altarpiece is dedicated to the Guardian Angel of Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Saints_Cosme_and_Damian">
       Chapel of Saints Cosme and Damian
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Chapel of Saints Cosme and Damian">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>

     </h4>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Saints Cosme and Damian (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de San Cosme y San Damin
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was built because these two saints were commonly invoked during a time when New Spain suffered from the many diseases brought by the Conquistadors. The main altarpiece is Baroque, probably built in the 17thcentury. Oil paintings on wood contain scenes from physician saints, and are attributed to painter Sebastian Lopez Davalos, during the second half of the 17thcentury. The chapel contains one small altarpiece which came from the Franciscan church in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinacantepec" title="Zinacantepec">
       Zinacantepec
      </a>
      , to the west of Mexico City, and is dedicated to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_of_Jesus" title="Birth of Jesus">
       birth of Jesus
      </a>
      .

     <p>
      The Chapel of Saint Joseph (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de San Jos
       </i>
      </span>

      <i>
       Our Lord of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cacao_bean" title="Cacao bean">
        Cacao
       </a>
      </i>
      , an image of Christ most likely from the 16thcentury. Its name was inspired from a time when many indigenous worshipers would give their
      <a href="/wiki/Alms" title="Alms">
       alms
      </a>
      in the form of cocoa beans. Churrigueresque in style and containing an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graffito_(drawing_technique)" title="Graffito (drawing technique)">

         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the main altarpiece is Baroque and is from the 18thcentury. This once belonged to the Church of Our Lady of Monserrat. This altar contains statues and cubicles containing busts of the Apostles, but contains no paintings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-6">

     <p>

      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">

      <a href="/wiki/Caryatid" title="Caryatid">

      </a>
      and small angels as
      <a href="/wiki/Telamon" title="Telamon">

      <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Saint_Eligius">
       Chapel of Saint Eligius
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Chapel of Saint Eligius (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de San Eligio
       </i>
      </span>
      ), also known as the Chapel of the Lord of Safe Expeditions (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla del Seor del Buen Despacho
       </i>
      </span>
      ), was built by the first
      <a href="/wiki/Silversmith" title="Silversmith">
       silversmith
      </a>
      guild, who donated the images of the Conception and
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Eligius" title="Saint Eligius">
       Saint Eligius
      </a>
      to whom the chapel was formerly dedicated. The chapel was redecorated in the 19thcentury, and the image of
      <i>
       Our Lord of Good Sending
      </i>
      was placed here, named thus, since many supplicants reported having their prayers answered quickly. The image is thought to be from the 16thcentury and sent as a gift from
      <a href="/wiki/Infante_Carlos,_Count_of_Molina" title="Infante Carlos, Count of Molina">
       Charles V of Spain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-8">

     <p>
      The Chapel of Our Lady of Sorrows (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>

       </i>
      </span>

      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">

      <i>

      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_of_Mexico" title="Maximilian I of Mexico">
       Maximilian I of Mexico
      </a>
      . On the left-hand wall a ladder leads to a series of crypts which hold most of the remains of past archbishops of Mexico. The largest and grandest of these crypts contains the remains of Juan de Zumarraga, the first archbishop of Mexico.

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapel_of_Saint_Philip_of_Jesus">
       Chapel of Saint Philip of Jesus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Chapel of Saint Philip of Jesus">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      The Chapel of Saint Philip of Jesus (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Capilla de San Felipe de Jess
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was completed during one of the earliest stages of the construction of the cathedral. It is dedicated to
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Jesus" title="Philip of Jesus">
       Philip of Jesus
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">
       friar
      </a>
      and the only
      <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">
       martyr
      </a>
      from New Spain, who was crucified in Japan. The chapel is topped with a Gothic-style dome and has a Baroque altarpiece from the 17thcentury. A statue of the saint is located in a large niche in the altarpiece. The altar to the left is dedicated to Saint
      <a href="/wiki/Rose_of_Lima" title="Rose of Lima">
       Rose of Lima
      </a>
      , considered a protector of Mexico City. To the right is an urn which holds the remains of
      <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_de_Iturbide" title="Agustn de Iturbide">
       Agustn de Iturbide
      </a>
      , who briefly ruled Mexico in the 19thcentury.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]

       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]

       </a>
      </sup>
      Next to this chapel is a
      <a href="/wiki/Baptismal_font" title="Baptismal font">
       baptismal font
      </a>
      , in which it is believed Philip of Jesus was baptised.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forasteros_15-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-forasteros-15">

        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Organs">
       Organs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Organs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OrganCathedralDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/OrganCathedralDF.JPG/170px-OrganCathedralDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/OrganCathedralDF.JPG/255px-OrganCathedralDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/OrganCathedralDF.JPG/340px-OrganCathedralDF.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OrganCathedralDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        View of an organ case from outside the choir area.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The cathedral has had perhaps a dozen organs over the course of its history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pepe1_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pepe1-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The earliest is mentioned in a report written to the king of Spain in 1530. Few details survive of the earliest organs. Builders names begin to appear at the end of the sixteenth century. The earliest disposition that survives is for the Diego de Sebaldos organ built in 1655.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pepe2_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pepe2-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first large organ for Mexico City Cathedral was built in Madrid from 1689 to 1690 by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jorge_de_Sesma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jorge de Sesma (page does not exist)">
       Jorge de Sesma
      </a>
      and installed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tiburcio_Sanz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tiburcio Sanz (page does not exist)">
       Tiburcio Sanz
      </a>
      from 1693 to 1695.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pepe3_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pepe3-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It now has two, which were made in Mexico by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Nassarre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jos Nassarre (page does not exist)">
       Jos Nassarre
      </a>
      of Spain, and completed by 1736, incorporating elements of the 17thcentury organ. They are the largest 18thcentury organs in the Americas; they are situated above the walls of the choir, on the epistle side (east) and the gospel side (west).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Organs_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Organs-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both organs, damaged by fire in 1967, were restored in 1978. Because both organs had fallen into disrepair again, the gospel organ was re-restored from 2008-2009 by Gerhard Grenzing; the restoration of the epistle organ, also by Grenzing, was completed in 2014, and both organs are now playable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-global-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Choir">
       Choir
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Choir">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Choir_(architecture)" title="Choir (architecture)">
       choir
      </a>
      is where the priest and/or a choral group sings the
      <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">
       psalms
      </a>
      . It is located in the central nave between the main door and the high altar, and built in a semicircular fashion, much like Spanish cathedrals. It was built by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_de_Rojas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan de Rojas (page does not exist)">
       Juan de Rojas
      </a>
      between 1696 and 1697.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arsvirtual-choir_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-arsvirtual-choir-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its sides contain 59reliefs of various saints done in
      <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">
       mahogany
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">
       walnut
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cedar_wood" title="Cedar wood">
       cedar
      </a>
      and a native wood called tepehuaje. The railing that surrounds the choir was made in 1722 by Sangley Queaulo in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macao" title="Macao">
       Macao
      </a>
      , China and placed in the cathedral in 1730.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epoca_16-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Epoca-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Crypt">
       Crypt
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Crypt">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Criptacatedralmexico.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="586" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Criptacatedralmexico.jpg/170px-Criptacatedralmexico.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Criptacatedralmexico.jpg/255px-Criptacatedralmexico.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Criptacatedralmexico.jpg/340px-Criptacatedralmexico.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Criptacatedralmexico.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The crypt
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Crypt of the Archbishops is located below the floor of the cathedral beneath the Altar of the Kings. The entrance to the crypt from the cathedral is guarded by a large wooden door behind which descends a winding yellow staircase. Just past the inner entrance is a
      <a href="/wiki/Mexica" title="Mexica">
       Mexica
      </a>
      -style stone skull. It was incorporated as an offering into the base of a
      <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">
       cenotaph
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Zum%C3%A1rraga" title="Juan de Zumrraga">
       Juan de Zumrraga
      </a>
      , the first archbishop of Mexico. Zumrraga was considered to be a benefactor of the Indians, protecting them against the abuses of their Spanish overlords. There is also a natural-sized sculpture of the archbishop atop the cenotaph.
     </p>
     <p>
      On its walls are dozens of bronze plaques that indicate the locations of the remains of most of Mexico City's former archbishops, including Cardinal
      <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Corripio_y_Ahumada" title="Ernesto Corripio y Ahumada">
       Ernesto Corripio y Ahumada
      </a>
      . The floor is covered with small marble slabs covering niches containing the remains of other people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fox-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cathedral contains other crypts and niches where other religious figures are buried, including in the chapels.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">
       Restoration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Restoration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The sinking ground and seismic activity of the area have had an effect on the cathedral's construction and current appearance. Forty-two years were required simply to lay its foundation when it was first built, because even then the Spaniards recognized the danger of constructing such a huge monument in soft soil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, for political reasons, much, but not all, of the cathedral was built over the remains of pre-Hispanic structures, leading to uneven foundation from the beginning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkingTour_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WalkingTour-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_of_1967">
       Fire of 1967
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Fire of 1967">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On 17January1967 at 9pm, a fire caused by an electrical
      <a href="/wiki/Short_circuit" title="Short circuit">
       short circuit
      </a>
      caused extensive damage to the cathedral. On the Altar of Forgiveness, much of the structure and decoration were damaged including the loss of three paintings;
      <i>
       The Holy Face
      </i>
      by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alonso_L%C3%B3pez_de_Herrera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alonso Lpez de Herrera (page does not exist)">
       Alonso Lpez de Herrera
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       The Martyrdom of Saint Sebastian
      </i>
      by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_de_Zumaya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francisco de Zumaya (page does not exist)">
       Francisco de Zumaya
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       The Virgin of Forgiveness
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Simon_Pereyns" title="Simon Pereyns">
       Simon Pereyns
      </a>
      . The choir section lost 75of its 99seats as well a painting by Juan Correa along with many stored books. The two cathedral organs were severely damaged with the partial melting of their pipes. Paintings by Rafael Jimeno y Planas, Juan Correa and Juan Rodriguez Juarez were damaged in other parts of the cathedral. After the fire, authorities recorded the damage but did nothing to try to restore what was damaged. Heated discussions ensued among historians, architects and investigations centering on the moving of the Altar of Forgiveness, as well as eliminating the choir area and some of the railings. In 1972, ecclesiastical authorities initiated demolition of the choir area without authorization from the Federal government, but were stopped. The government inventoried what could be saved and named Jaime Ortiz Lajous as director of the project to restore the cathedral to its original condition. Restoration work focused not only on repairing the damage (using archived records and photographs), but also included work on a deteriorating foundation (due to uneven sinking into the ground) and problems with the towers.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Altars of Forgiveness and of the Kings were subject to extensive cleaning and restorative work. To replace the lost portions on the Altar of Forgiveness, several paintings were added;
      <i>
       Escape from Egypt
      </i>
      by Pereyns,
      <i>
       The Divine Countenance
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       The Martyrdom of Saint Sebastian
      </i>
      . The organs were dismantled with the pipes and inner workings sent to the
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Netherlands
      </a>
      for repair, while the cases were restored by Mexican craftsmen with work lasting until 1977. Reconstruction of the choir area began in 1979 using the same materials as existed before the fire. In addition, any statues in the towers that received more than 50%damage from city pollution were taken out, with replicas created to replace them. Those with less damage were repaired.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some interesting discoveries were made as restoration work occurred during the 1970s and early 1980s. 51paintings were found and rescued from behind the Altar of Forgiveness, including works by Juan and Nicolas Rodriguez Juarez,
      <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Cabrera" title="Miguel Cabrera">
       Miguel Cabrera
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_Ibarra" title="Jos de Ibarra">
       Jos de Ibarra
      </a>
      . Inside one of the organs, a copy of the nomination of
      <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernn Corts">
       Hernn Corts
      </a>
      as Governor General of New Spain (1529) was found. Lastly, in the wall of the central arch of the cathedral was found the burial place of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Miguel_Barrigan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Miguel Barrigan (page does not exist)">
       Miguel Barrigan
      </a>
      , the first governor of
      <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">
       Veracruz
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncMex_5-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncMex-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th-century_work">
       Late 20th-century work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Late 20th-century work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG/220px-ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG/330px-ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG/440px-ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ArtifactsCathdralDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Bases and other artifacts from the foundation excavation on display in front of the Cathedral
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The cathedral, along with
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City#Geography" title="Mexico City">
       the rest of the city
      </a>
      , has been sinking into the lakebed from the day it was built. However, the fact that the city is a
      <a href="/wiki/Megalopolis_(city_type)" title="Megalopolis (city type)">
       megalopolis
      </a>
      with over 18million people drawing water from underground sources has caused water tables to drop, and the sinking to accelerate during the latter half of the 20thcentury.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sections of the complex such as the cathedral and the tabernacle were still sinking at different rates,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthquake_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthquake-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the bell towers were tilting dangerously despite work done in the 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sacred_Destinations_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sacred_Destinations-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkingTour_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WalkingTour-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For this reason, the cathedral was included in the
      <a href="/wiki/1998_World_Monuments_Watch" title="1998 World Monuments Watch">
       1998 World Monuments Watch
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Monuments_Fund" title="World Monuments Fund">
       World Monuments Fund
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Major restoration and foundation work began in the 1990s to stabilize the building.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkingTour_28-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-WalkingTour-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Engineers excavated under the cathedral between 1993 and 1998.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earthquake_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Earthquake-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They dug shafts under the cathedral and placed shafts of concrete into the soft ground to give the edifice a more solid base to rest on.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These efforts have not stopped the sinking of the complex, but they have corrected the tilting towers and ensured that the cathedral will sink uniformly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalkingTour_28-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-WalkingTour-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_value">
       Cultural value
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexico_City_Metropolitan_Cathedral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Cultural value">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The cathedral has been a focus of Mexican cultural identity, and is a testament to its colonial history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ciudadmexico_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ciudadmexico-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Researcher Manuel Rivera Cambas reported that the cathedral was built on the site sacred precinct of the Aztecs and with the very stones of their temples so that the Spaniards could lay claim to the land and the people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_7-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-historic-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernn Corts">
       Hernn Corts
      </a>
      supposedly laid the first stone of the original church personally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ciudadmexico_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ciudadmexico-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It once was an important religious center, exclusive for the prominent families of New Spain. In 1864, during the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Mexican_Empire" title="Second Mexican Empire">
       Second Mexican Empire
      </a>
      , Emperor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximilian_of_Habsburg" title="Maximilian of Habsburg">
       Maximilian of Habsburg
      </a>
      and Empress
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlotte_of_Belgium" title="Charlotte of Belgium">
       Charlotte of Belgium
      </a>
      (later known as Maximiliano and Carlota of Mexico) were crowned at the cathedral, at their magnificent arrival to the head city of their reign.
     </p>
     <p>
      Located on the Zocalo it has, over time, been the focus of social and cultural activities, most of which have occurred in the 20thand 21stcenturies. The cathedral was closed for four years while President
      <a href="/wiki/Plutarco_El%C3%ADas_Calles" title="Plutarco Elas Calles">
       Plutarco Elas Calles
      </a>
      attempted to enforce
      <a href="/wiki/Cristero_War#Aftermath_of_the_war_and_the_toll_on_the_Church" title="Cristero War">
       Mexico's anti-religious laws
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">
       Pope Pius XI
      </a>
      closed the church, ordering priests to cease their public religious duties in all Mexican churches. After the Mexican government and the papacy came to terms and major renovations were performed on the cathedral, it reopened in 1930.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Time-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cathedral has been the scene of several protests both from the church and to the church, including a protest by women over the Church's exhortation for women not to wear mini-skirts and other provocative clothing to avoid rape,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xinhua_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a candlelight vigil to protest against kidnappings in Mexico.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topix_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Topix-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cathedral itself has been used to protest against social issues. Its bells rang to express the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Mexico" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Mexico">
       archdiocese's
      </a>
      opposition to the
      <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Justice_of_the_Nation" title="Supreme Court of Justice of the Nation">
       Supreme Court
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Mexico" title="Abortion in Mexico">
       upholding of Mexico City's legalization of abortion
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campanas_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Campanas-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Probably the most serious recent event occurred on 18November2007, when sympathizers of the
      <a href="/wiki/Party_of_the_Democratic_Revolution" title="Party of the Democratic Revolution">
       Party of the Democratic Revolution
      </a>
      attacked the cathedral.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNA-forgive_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CNA-forgive-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      About 150protesters stormed into Sunday Mass chanting slogans and knocking over
      <a href="/wiki/Pew" title="Pew">
       pews
      </a>
      . This caused church officials to close and lock the cathedral for a number of days.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grillo_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grillo-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cathedral reopened with new security measures, such as bag searches, in place.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNA-forgive_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CNA-forgive-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
