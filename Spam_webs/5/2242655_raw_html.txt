         <div class="entry-content">
          <div class="ndn_embed" data-config-distributor-id="90301" id="ndn-video-player-1">
          </div>
          <p>
           The Pacific Fishery Management Council has voted to close the ongoing sardine fishery along the West Coast more than two months early to prevent further decline in the collapsing sardine population.
          </p>
          <p>
           The announcement comes just
           <a href="http://www.oregonlive.com/pacific-northwest-news/index.ssf/2015/04/upcoming_sardine_season_cancel.html">
            two days after the council voted to close the upcoming sardine season
           </a>
           , which starts July 1. Wednesday's action takes the closure a step further, making it immediate instead of allowing fishermen to carry out the remaining weeks of this season.
          </p>
          <p>
           The mid-season closure highlights fishery managers' intense concern about a
           <a href="http://www.oregonlive.com/environment/index.ssf/2015/04/pacific_sardine_industry_shutd.html">
            collapse that has decimated sardine stocks
           </a>
           in recent years. Today, there are only 10 percent as many sardines in the water as there were in 2007.
          </p>
          <p>
           Council designee Michele Culver of the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife proposed the closure, calling it an "urgent action."
          </p>
          <p>
           "This is not a decision the council makes lightly," Culver said. "We understand the disruption to the sardine fishery resulting from in-season action."
          </p>
          <p>
           In Oregon alone, sardines are a multi-million dollar fishery.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ocean conservation group Oceana asked the council to consider the immediate closure, which will not include tribal fisheries.
          </p>
          <p>
           "They're fishing off spawning sardine right now off the coast of Oregon, and those are our future fish," said Ben Enticknap, a senior scientist with Oceana. "If we want this population to recover as quickly as possible, we have to stop fishing as quickly as possible.
          </p>
          <p>
           In a strange twist, the council's decision not to declare an emergency - instead asking the National Marine Fisheries Service to close the season through procedural routes - underscores fishery managers' sense of urgency about the sardine's nosedive.
          </p>
          <p>
           An emergency action would have taken several weeks to process and put into effect. Council members decided that's too long to wait.
          </p>
          <p>
           Instead, 11 of the 12 councilors voted for an alternative route to closure that could take as little as a week. One councilor voted no, avoiding a rule that requires the National Marine Fisheries Service to implement emergency action if the closure vote is unanimous.
          </p>
          <p>
           Catch limits for this season were based on numbers that significantly overestimated the size of the sardine population. Had those preseason estimates been correct, the sardine fishery's annual catch limit would have been set lower, and fishermen would have already reached it.
          </p>
          <p>
           Without council intervention fishermen would have the right to catch nearly two thousand tons of sardines before reaching this season's limit of 23,293 metric tons.
          </p>
          <p>
           West Coast sardine fishing has ramped up since the word got out that next year's season is cancelled. Enticknap called the closure a smart action that stops the "race to catch the last sardine."
          </p>
          <p>
           While no councilors went so far as to say sardines are overfished during Wednesday's meeting, Culver noted the population is in danger of being overfished if restrictions aren't enacted.
          </p>
          <p>
           "It presents a serious conservation and management concern in the fishery," she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pacific Fishery Management Council director Donald McIsaac said it could be as little as a week for the National Marine Fishery Service to put a closure in place. Fishermen would get 48-hours notice, he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           --Kelly House
          </p>
