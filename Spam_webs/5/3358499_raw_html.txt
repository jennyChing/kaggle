     <p>
      <b>
       Gerard Kitchen O'Neill
      </b>
      (February 6, 1927  April 27, 1992) was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">
       physicist
      </a>
      and space activist. As a faculty member of
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton University
      </a>
      , he invented a device called the
      <a href="/wiki/Storage_ring" title="Storage ring">
       particle storage ring
      </a>
      for high-energy physics experiments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physrev-sr_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-physrev-sr-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, he invented a magnetic launcher called the
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">
       mass driver
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 1970s, he developed a plan to build human settlements in outer space, including a
      <a href="/wiki/Space_habitat" title="Space habitat">
       space habitat
      </a>
      design known as the
      <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_cylinder" title="O'Neill cylinder">
       O'Neill cylinder
      </a>
      . He founded the
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Studies_Institute" title="Space Studies Institute">
       Space Studies Institute
      </a>
      , an organization devoted to funding research into
      <a href="/wiki/Space_manufacturing" title="Space manufacturing">
       space manufacturing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">
       colonization
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill began researching high-energy
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">
       particle physics
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton
      </a>
      in 1954, after he received his doctorate from
      <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
       Cornell University
      </a>
      . Two years later, he published his theory for a particle storage ring. This invention allowed
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics_experiments" title="Particle physics experiments">
       particle physics experiments
      </a>
      at much higher energies than had previously been possible. In 1965 at
      <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
       Stanford University
      </a>
      , he performed the first colliding beam physics experiment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While teaching physics at Princeton, O'Neill became interested in the possibility that humans could survive and live in outer space. He researched and proposed a futuristic idea for human settlement in space, the O'Neill cylinder, in "The Colonization of Space", his first paper on the subject. He held a conference on
      <a href="/wiki/Space_manufacturing" title="Space manufacturing">
       space manufacturing
      </a>
      at Princeton in 1975. Many who became post-
      <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
       Apollo
      </a>
      -era space activists attended. O'Neill built his first
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_Driver_1" title="Mass Driver 1">
       mass driver prototype
      </a>
      with professor
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kolm" title="Henry Kolm">
       Henry Kolm
      </a>
      in 1976. He considered mass drivers critical for extracting the mineral resources of the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
       asteroids
      </a>
      . His award-winning book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_High_Frontier:_Human_Colonies_in_Space" title="The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space">
        The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space
       </a>
      </i>
      inspired a generation of space exploration advocates. He died of
      <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">
       leukemia
      </a>
      in 1992.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Birth.2C_education.2C_and_family_life">
       Birth, education, and family life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Birth, education, and family life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      O'Neill was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">
       Brooklyn, New York
      </a>
      on February 6, 1927 to Edward Gerard O'Neill, a lawyer, and Dorothy Lewis O'Neill (ne Kitchen).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleAuth_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleAuth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He had no siblings. His family moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Speculator,_New_York" title="Speculator, New York">
       Speculator, New York
      </a>
      when his father temporarily retired for health reasons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For high school, O'Neill attended
      <a href="/wiki/Newburgh_Free_Academy" title="Newburgh Free Academy">
       Newburgh Free Academy
      </a>
      in Newburgh, New York. While he was a student there he edited the school newspaper and took a job as a news broadcaster at a local radio station.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He graduated in 1944, during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , and enlisted in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      on his 17th birthday.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Navy trained him as a radar technician, which sparked his interest in science.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After he was honorably discharged in 1946, O'Neill studied for an undergraduate degree in physics and mathematics at
      <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">
       Swarthmore College
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p97_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p97-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a child he had discussed the possibilities of humans in space with his parents, and in college he enjoyed working on rocket equations. However, he did not see space science as an option for a career path in physics, choosing instead to pursue high-energy physics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henson77p8_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henson77p8-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1950 he graduated with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">
       Phi Beta Kappa
      </a>
      honors. O'Neill performed his graduate studies at
      <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
       Cornell University
      </a>
      with the help of an
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">
       Atomic Energy Commission
      </a>
      fellowship, and was awarded a Ph.D. in physics in 1954.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill married Sylvia Turlington, also a Swarthmore graduate, in June 1950.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turlington50_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Turlington50-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They had a son, Roger, and two daughters, Janet and Eleanor, before their marriage ended in divorce in 1966.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of O'Neill's favorite activities was flying. He held instrument certifications in both powered and
      <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">
       sailplane
      </a>
      flight and held the
      <a href="/wiki/FAI_Gliding_Commission" title="FAI Gliding Commission">
       FAI
      </a>
      Diamond Badge, a gliding award.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During his first cross-country glider flight in April 1973, he was assisted on the ground by Renate "Tasha" Steffen. He had met Tasha, who was 21 years younger than him, previously through the
      <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">
       YMCA
      </a>
      International Club. They were married the day after his flight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleAuth_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleAuth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They had a son, Edward O'Neill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="High-energy_physics_research">
       High-energy physics research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: High-energy physics research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After graduating from Cornell, O'Neill accepted a position as an instructor at
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There he started his research into high-energy
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">
       particle physics
      </a>
      . In 1956, his second year of teaching, he published a two-page article that theorized that the particles produced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">
       particle accelerator
      </a>
      could be stored for a few seconds in a
      <a href="/wiki/Storage_ring" title="Storage ring">
       storage ring
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physrev-sr_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-physrev-sr-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The stored particles could then be directed to collide with another particle beam. This would increase the energy of the particle collision over the previous method, which directed the beam at a fixed target.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His ideas were not immediately accepted by the physics community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill became an assistant professor at Princeton in 1956, and was promoted to associate professor in 1959.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleAuth_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleAuth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He visited Stanford University in 1957 to meet with Professor
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_K._H._Panofsky" title="Wolfgang K. H. Panofsky">
       Wolfgang K. H. Panofsky
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This resulted in a collaboration between Princeton and Stanford to build the Colliding Beam Experiment (CBX).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With a US$800,000 grant from the
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Naval_Research" title="Office of Naval Research">
       Office of Naval Research
      </a>
      , construction on the first particle storage rings began in 1958 at the Stanford High-Energy Physics Laboratory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He figured out how to capture the particles and, by pumping the air out to produce a
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
       vacuum
      </a>
      , store them long enough to experiment on them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      CBX stored its first beam on March 28, 1962. O'Neill became a full professor of physics in 1965.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SLAC_long_view.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2311" data-file-width="3471" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/SLAC_long_view.jpg/220px-SLAC_long_view.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/SLAC_long_view.jpg/330px-SLAC_long_view.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/SLAC_long_view.jpg/440px-SLAC_long_view.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SLAC_long_view.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The two-mile-long
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanford_Linear_Accelerator" title="Stanford Linear Accelerator">
         Stanford Linear Accelerator
        </a>
        tunnel
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In collaboration with
      <a href="/wiki/Burton_Richter" title="Burton Richter">
       Burton Richter
      </a>
      , O'Neill performed the first colliding beam physics experiment in 1965. In this experiment, particle beams from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanford_Linear_Accelerator" title="Stanford Linear Accelerator">
       Stanford Linear Accelerator
      </a>
      were collected in his storage rings and then directed to collide at an energy of 600
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volt" title="Electron volt">
       MeV
      </a>
      . At the time, this was the highest energy involved in a particle collision. The results proved that the charge of an electron is contained in a volume less than 100
      <a href="/wiki/Metre#SI_prefixed_forms_of_metre" title="Metre">
       attometers
      </a>
      across. O'Neill considered his device to be capable of only seconds of storage, but, by creating an even stronger vacuum, others were able to increase this to hours.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1979, he, with physicist David C. Cheng, wrote the graduate-level textbook
      <i>
       Elementary Particle Physics: An Introduction
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleAuth_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleAuth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He retired from teaching in 1985, but remained associated with Princeton as
      <i>
       professor emeritus
      </i>
      until his death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_colonization">
       Space colonization
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Space colonization">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_idea_.281969.29">
       Origin of the idea (1969)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origin of the idea (1969)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S88_33646.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2876" data-file-width="4336" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/S88_33646.jpg/220px-S88_33646.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/S88_33646.jpg/330px-S88_33646.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/S88_33646.jpg/440px-S88_33646.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S88_33646.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
         NASA
        </a>
        envisioned an ambitious scientific exploration of the
        <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
         Moon
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      O'Neill saw great potential in the United States space program, especially the
      <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
       Apollo missions
      </a>
      . He applied to the Astronaut Corps after NASA opened it up to civilian scientists in 1966. Later, when asked why he wanted to go on the Moon missions, he said, "to be alive now and not take part in it seemed terribly myopic".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was put through NASA's rigorous mental and physical examinations. During this time he met
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_O%27Leary" title="Brian O'Leary">
       Brian O'Leary
      </a>
      , also a scientist-astronaut candidate, who became his good friend.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p243_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p243-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      O'Leary was selected for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Group_6" title="Astronaut Group 6">
       Astronaut Group 6
      </a>
      but O'Neill was not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill became interested in the idea of space colonization in 1969 while he was teaching freshman physics at Princeton University.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His students were growing cynical about the benefits of science to humanity because of the controversy surrounding the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p233_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p233-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overend77_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overend77-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To give them something relevant to study, he began using examples from the Apollo program as applications of elementary physics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      O'Neill posed the question during an extra seminar he gave to a few of his students: "Is the surface of a planet really the right place for an expanding technological civilization?"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His students' research convinced him that the answer was no.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1393" data-file-width="1886" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg/220px-Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg/330px-Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg/440px-Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bernal_Sphere_3.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Bernal_sphere" title="Bernal sphere">
         Bernal sphere
        </a>
        , an "inside-out planet"
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      O'Neill was inspired by the papers written by his students. He began to work out the details of a program to build self-supporting space habitats in free space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among the details was how to provide the inhabitants of a space colony with an Earth-like environment. His students had designed giant pressurized structures, spun up to approximate Earth gravity by
      <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">
       centrifugal force
      </a>
      . With the population of the colony living on the inner surface of a sphere or cylinder, these structures resembled "inside-out planets". He found that pairing counter-rotating cylinders would eliminate the need to spin them using rockets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This configuration has since been known as the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_cylinder" title="O'Neill cylinder">
        O'Neill cylinder
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_paper_.281970.E2.80.931974.29">
       First paper (19701974)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First paper (19701974)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Looking for an outlet for his ideas, O'Neill wrote a paper titled "The Colonization of Space", and for four years attempted to have it published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He submitted it to several journals and magazines, including
      <i>
       Scientific American
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Science
      </i>
      , only to have it rejected by the reviewers. During this time O'Neill gave lectures on space colonization at
      <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College" title="Hampshire College">
       Hampshire College
      </a>
      , Princeton, and other schools. Many students and staff attending the lectures became enthusiastic about the possibility of living in space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another outlet for O'Neill to explore his ideas was with his children; on walks in the forest they speculated about life in a space colony.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His paper finally appeared in the September 1974 issue of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Physics_Today" title="Physics Today">
        Physics Today
       </a>
      </i>
      . In it, he argued that building space colonies would solve several important problems:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       It is important to realize the enormous power of the space-colonization technique. If we begin to use it soon enough, and if we employ it wisely, at least five of the most serious problems now facing the world can be solved without recourse to repression: bringing every human being up to a living standard now enjoyed only by the most fortunate; protecting the biosphere from damage caused by transportation and industrial pollution; finding high quality living space for a world population that is doubling every 35 years; finding clean, practical energy sources; preventing overload of Earth's heat balance.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       Gerard K. O'Neill,
       <cite>
        "The Colonization of Space"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill74_26-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-ONeill74-26">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          26
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
     </blockquote>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lagrange_2_mass.gif">

       </a>

     </div>
     <p>
      He even explored the possibilities of flying gliders inside a space colony, finding that the enormous volume could support atmospheric thermals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He calculated that humanity could expand on this man-made frontier to 20,000 times its population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The initial colonies would be built at the Earth-Moon
      <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
       L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
       L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
       Lagrange points
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
       L
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
       L
       <sub>
        5
       </sub>
      </a>
      are stable points in the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
       Solar System
      </a>
      where a spacecraft can maintain its position without expending energy. The paper was well received, but many who would begin work on the project had already been introduced to his ideas before it was even published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The paper received a few critical responses. Some questioned the practicality of lifting tens of thousands of people into orbit and his estimates for the production output of initial colonies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p253_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p253-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While he was waiting for his paper to be published, O'Neill organized a small two-day conference in May 1974 at Princeton to discuss the possibility of colonizing outer space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The conference, titled
      <i>
       First Conference on Space Colonization
      </i>
      , was funded by
      <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Brand" title="Stewart Brand">
       Stewart Brand's
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Point_Foundation_(environment)" title="Point Foundation (environment)">
       Point Foundation
      </a>
      and Princeton University.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p249_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p249-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among those who attended were
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eric_Drexler" title="Eric Drexler">
       Eric Drexler
      </a>
      (at the time a freshman at
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
       MIT
      </a>
      ), scientist-astronaut
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Allen" title="Joseph P. Allen">
       Joe Allen
      </a>
      (from Astronaut Group 6),
      <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">
       Freeman Dyson
      </a>
      , and science reporter
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_S._Sullivan" title="Walter S. Sullivan">
       Walter Sullivan
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Representatives from NASA also attended and brought estimates of launch costs expected on the planned
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">
       Space Shuttle
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      O'Neill thought of the attendees as "a band of daring radicals".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henson77p10_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henson77p10-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sullivan's article on the conference was published on the front page of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      on May 13, 1974.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As media coverage grew, O'Neill was inundated with letters from people who were excited about living in space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To stay in touch with them, O'Neill began keeping a mailing list and started sending out updates on his progress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoEvInt_21-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-CoEvInt-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p252_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p252-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A few months later he heard
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Glaser" title="Peter Glaser">
       Peter Glaser
      </a>
      speak about
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_satellite" title="Solar power satellite">
       solar power satellites
      </a>
      at NASA's
      <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">
       Goddard Space Flight Center
      </a>
      . O'Neill realized that, by building these satellites, his space colonies could quickly recover the cost of their construction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p255_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p255-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to O'Neill, "the profound difference between this and everything else done in space is the potential of generating large amounts of new wealth".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_studies_.281975.E2.80.931977.29">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      O'Neill held a much larger conference the following May titled
      <i>
       Princeton University Conference on Space Manufacturing
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p256_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p256-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At this conference more than two dozen speakers presented papers, including
      <a href="/wiki/Keith_Henson" title="Keith Henson">
       Keith
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Meinel" title="Carolyn Meinel">
       Carolyn Henson
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">
       Tucson, Arizona
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erichsen94_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erichsen94-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIAA75_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AIAA75-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the conference Carolyn Henson arranged a meeting between O'Neill and Arizona Congressman
      <a href="/wiki/Mo_Udall" title="Mo Udall">
       Morris Udall
      </a>
      . Udall wrote a letter of support, which he asked the Hensons to publicize, for O'Neill's work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erichsen94_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erichsen94-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Hensons included his letter in the first issue of the
      <a href="/wiki/L5_Society" title="L5 Society">
       L-5 Society
      </a>
      newsletter, sent to everyone on O'Neill's mailing list and those who had signed up at the conference.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erichsen94_38-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erichsen94-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      In June 1975, O'Neill led a ten-week study of permanent space habitats at NASA Ames. During the study he was called away to testify on July 23 to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">
       House
      </a>
      Subcommittee on Space Science and Applications.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p282_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p282-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On January 19, 1976, he also appeared before the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">
       Senate
      </a>
      Subcommittee on Aerospace Technology and National Needs. In a presentation titled
      <i>
       Solar Power from Satellites
      </i>
      , he laid out his case for an Apollo-style program for building power plants in space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p264_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p264-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He returned to Ames in June 1976 and 1977 to lead studies on space manufacturing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSIHistory_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SSIHistory-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In these studies, NASA developed detailed plans to establish bases on the Moon where space-suited workers would mine the mineral resources needed to build space colonies and solar power satellites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeillAmes77_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeillAmes77-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Private_funding_.281977.E2.80.931978.29">
       Private funding (19771978)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Private funding (19771978)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although NASA was supporting his work with grants of up to $500,000 per year, O'Neill became frustrated by the bureaucracy and politics inherent in government-funded research.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleBio_4-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleBio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overend77_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overend77-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He thought that small privately funded groups could develop space technology faster than government agencies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1977, O'Neill and his wife Tasha founded the
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Studies_Institute" title="Space Studies Institute">
       Space Studies Institute
      </a>
      , a non-profit organization, at Princeton University.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      SSI received initial funding of almost $100,000 from private donors, and in early 1978 began to support basic research into technologies needed for space manufacturing and settlement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      One of
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Studies_Institute" title="Space Studies Institute">
       SSI's
      </a>
      first grants funded the development of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">
       mass driver
      </a>
      , a device first proposed by O'Neill in 1974.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutSSI_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AboutSSI-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mass drivers are based on the
      <a href="/wiki/Coilgun" title="Coilgun">
       coilgun
      </a>
      design, adapted to accelerate a non-magnetic object.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One application O'Neill proposed for mass drivers was to throw baseball-sized chunks of ore mined from the surface of the Moon into space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weintraub84p304_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weintraub84p304-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMPUTE_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-COMPUTE-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Once in space, the ore could be used as raw material for building space colonies and solar power satellites. He took a sabbatical from Princeton to work on mass drivers at MIT. There he served as the
      <a href="/wiki/Jerome_C._Hunsaker_Visiting_Professor_of_Aerospace_Systems" title="Jerome C. Hunsaker Visiting Professor of Aerospace Systems">
       Hunsaker Visiting Professor of Aerospace
      </a>
      during the 197677 academic year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At MIT, he,
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kolm" title="Henry Kolm">
       Henry H. Kolm
      </a>
      , and a group of student volunteers built their
      <a href="/wiki/Mass_Driver_1" title="Mass Driver 1">
       first mass driver prototype
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henson77p10_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henson77p10-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSIHistory_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SSIHistory-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutSSI_47-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AboutSSI-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The eight-foot (2.5m) long prototype could apply 33
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">
        g
       </a>
      </i>
      (320m/s
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) of acceleration to an object inserted into it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henson77p10_32-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henson77p10-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSIHistory_43-2">

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weintraub84p304_50-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weintraub84p304-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With financial assistance from SSI, later prototypes improved this to 1,800
      <i>
       g
      </i>
      (18,000m/s
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ), enough acceleration that a mass driver only 520 feet (160m) long could launch material off the surface of the Moon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSIHistory_43-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-SSIHistory-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_.281977.E2.80.931985.29">
       Opposition (19771985)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Opposition (19771985)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1977, O'Neill saw the peak of interest in space colonization, along with the publication of his first book,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_High_Frontier:_Human_Colonies_in_Space" title="The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space">
        The High Frontier
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erichsen94_38-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erichsen94-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He and his wife were flying between meetings, interviews, and hearings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rein77_6-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rein77-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On October 9, the
      <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">
       CBS
      </a>
      program
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">
        60 Minutes
       </a>
      </i>
      ran a segment about space colonies. Later they aired responses from the viewers, which included one from Senator
      <a href="/wiki/William_Proxmire" title="William Proxmire">
       William Proxmire
      </a>
      , chairman of the Senate Subcommittee responsible for NASA's budget. His response was, "it's the best argument yet for chopping NASA's funding to the bone .... I say not a penny for this nutty fantasy".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He successfully eliminated spending on space colonization research from the budget.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1978,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Werbos" title="Paul Werbos">
       Paul Werbos
      </a>
      wrote for the L-5 newsletter, "no one expects Congress to commit us to O'Neill's concept of large-scale space habitats; people in NASA are almost paranoid about the public relations aspects of the idea".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When it became clear that a government funded colonization effort was politically impossible, popular support for O'Neill's ideas started to evaporate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erichsen94_38-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erichsen94-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other pressures on O'Neill's colonization plan were the high cost of access to Earth orbit and the declining cost of energy. Building solar power stations in space was economically attractive when energy prices spiked during the
      <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">
       1979 oil crisis
      </a>
      . When prices dropped in the early 1980s, funding for space solar power research dried up.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His plan had also been based on NASA's estimates for the flight rate and launch cost of the Space Shuttle, numbers that turned out to have been wildly optimistic. His 1977 book quoted a Space Shuttle launch cost of $10 million, but in 1981 the subsidized price given to commercial customers started at $38 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ONeill77p134_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ONeill77p134-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Eventual accounting of the full cost of a launch in 1985 raised this as high as $180 million per flight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill was appointed by United States President
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
       Ronald Reagan
      </a>
      to the National Commission on Space in 1985.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The commission, led by former NASA administrator
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_O._Paine" title="Thomas O. Paine">
       Thomas Paine
      </a>
      , proposed that the government commit to opening the inner Solar System for human settlement within 50 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paine86_60-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Paine86-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their report was released in May 1986, four months after the
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger" title="Space Shuttle Challenger">
       Space Shuttle Challenger
      </a>
      broke up on ascent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paine86_60-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Paine86-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_career">
       Writing career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Writing career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spacecolony1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1388" data-file-width="1896" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Spacecolony1.jpg/220px-Spacecolony1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Spacecolony1.jpg/330px-Spacecolony1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Spacecolony1.jpg/440px-Spacecolony1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spacecolony1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        O'Neill cylinders as illustrated in
        <a href="/wiki/The_High_Frontier:_Human_Colonies_in_Space" title="The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space">
         <i>
          The High Frontier
         </i>
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      O'Neill's popular science book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_High_Frontier:_Human_Colonies_in_Space" title="The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space">
        The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space
       </a>
      </i>
      (1977) combined fictional accounts of space settlers with an explanation of his plan to build space colonies. Its publication established him as the spokesman for the space colonization movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It won the
      <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa_Award_in_Science" title="Phi Beta Kappa Award in Science">
       Phi Beta Kappa Award in Science
      </a>
      that year, and prompted Swarthmore College to grant him an
      <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">
       honorary doctorate
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleAuth_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleAuth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBKAwards_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PBKAwards-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       The High Frontier
      </i>
      has been translated into five languages and remained in print as of 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSIHistory_43-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-SSIHistory-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His 1981 book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/2081:_A_Hopeful_View_of_the_Human_Future" title="2081: A Hopeful View of the Human Future">
        2081: A Hopeful View of the Human Future
       </a>
      </i>
      was an exercise in
      <a href="/wiki/Futures_studies" title="Futures studies">
       futurology
      </a>
      . O'Neill narrated it as a visitor to Earth from a space colony beyond Pluto.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The book explored the effects of technologies he called "drivers of change" on the coming century. Some technologies he described were space colonies, solar power satellites,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aging" title="Anti-aging">
       anti-aging
      </a>
      drugs,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_vehicle" title="Hydrogen vehicle">
       hydrogen-propelled cars
      </a>
      , climate control, and underground
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maglev_train" title="Maglev train">
       magnetic trains
      </a>
      . He left the social structure of the 1980s intact, assuming that humanity would remain unchanged even as it expanded into the Solar System. Reviews of
      <i>
       2081
      </i>
      were mixed.
      <i>
       New York Times
      </i>
      reviewer John Noble Wilford found the book "imagination-stirring", but
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Nicol" title="Charles Nicol">
       Charles Nicol
      </a>
      thought the technologies described were unacceptably far-fetched.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GaleAuth_5-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-GaleAuth-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In his book
      <i>
       The Technology Edge
      </i>
      , published in 1983, O'Neill wrote about economic competition with Japan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weil84_63-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weil84-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He argued that the United States had to develop six industries to compete:
      <a href="/wiki/Microfabrication" title="Microfabrication">
       microengineering
      </a>
      , robotics, genetic engineering,
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_levitation" title="Magnetic levitation">
       magnetic flight
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_car_(fiction)" title="Flying car (fiction)">
       family aircraft
      </a>
      , and space science.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMPUTE_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-COMPUTE-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also thought that industrial development was suffering from short-sighted executives, self-interested unions, high taxes, and poor education of Americans. According to reviewer Henry Weil, O'Neill's detailed explanations of emerging technologies differentiated the book from others on the subject.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weil84_63-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weil84-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Entrepreneurial_efforts">
       Entrepreneurial efforts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerard_K._O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Entrepreneurial efforts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RDSS_Diagram.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="652" data-file-width="798" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RDSS_Diagram.png/220px-RDSS_Diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RDSS_Diagram.png/330px-RDSS_Diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RDSS_Diagram.png/440px-RDSS_Diagram.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RDSS_Diagram.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Design for the satellite position determination system
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      O'Neill founded Geostar Corporation to develop a satellite position determination system for which he was granted a patent in 1982.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The system, primarily intended to track aircraft, was called Radio Determination Satellite Service (RDSS).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMPUTE_51-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-COMPUTE-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In April 1983 Geostar applied to the FCC for a license to broadcast from three satellites, which would cover the entire United States. Geostar launched GSTAR-2 into
      <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">
       geosynchronous orbit
      </a>
      in 1986. Its
      <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">
       transmitter
      </a>
      package permanently failed two months later, so Geostar began tests of RDSS by transmitting from other satellites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With his health failing, O'Neill became less involved with the company at the same time it started to run into trouble.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmitGeo_66-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmitGeo-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In February 1991 Geostar filed for bankruptcy and its licenses were sold to
      <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">
       Motorola
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/Iridium_satellite_constellation" title="Iridium satellite constellation">
       Iridium satellite constellation
      </a>
      project.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the system was eventually replaced by
      <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
       GPS
      </a>
      , O'Neill made significant advances in the field of position determination.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmitGeo_66-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmitGeo-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill founded O'Neill Communications in Princeton in 1986. He introduced his Local Area Wireless Networking, or LAWN, system at the PC Expo in New York in 1989.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCExLAWN_68-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PCExLAWN-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The LAWN system allowed two computers to exchange messages over a range of a couple hundred feet at a cost of about $500 per node.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PerCompLAWN_69-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PerCompLAWN-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      O'Neill Communications went out of business in 1993; the LAWN technology was sold to Omnispread Communications. As of 2008, Omnispread continued to sell a variant of O'Neill's LAWN system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keystone06p4_70-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Keystone06p4-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On November 18, 1991, O'Neill filed a patent application for a
      <a href="/wiki/Vactrain" title="Vactrain">
       vactrain
      </a>
      system. He called the company he wanted to form
      <i>
       VSE International
      </i>
      , for velocity, silence, and efficiency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the concept itself he called
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magnetic Flight (page does not exist)">
        Magnetic Flight
       </a>
      </i>
      . The vehicles, instead of running on a pair of tracks, would be elevated using electromagnetic force by a single track within a tube (permanent magnets in the track, with variable magnets on the vehicle), and propelled by electromagnetic forces through tunnels. He estimated the trains could reach speeds of up to 2,500mph (4,000km/h)  about five times faster than a jet airliner  if the air was evacuated from the tunnels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To obtain such speeds, the vehicle would accelerate for the first half of the trip, and then decelerate for the second half of the trip. The acceleration was planned to be a maximum of about one-half of the force of gravity. O'Neill planned to build a network of stations connected by these tunnels, but he died two years before his first patent on it was granted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p98_3-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p98-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      O'Neill was diagnosed with
      <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">
       leukemia
      </a>
      in 1985.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmitGeo_66-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-SmitGeo-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He died on April 27, 1992, from complications of the disease at the
      <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_Hospital" title="Sequoia Hospital">
       Sequoia Hospital
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Redwood_City,_California" title="Redwood City, California">
       Redwood City, California
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyson93p97_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyson93p97-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was survived by his wife Tasha, his ex-wife Sylvia, and his four children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels92_7-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Daniels92-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A sample of his incinerated remains was
      <a href="/wiki/Space_burial" title="Space burial">
       buried in space
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simons97_72-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Simons97-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The vial containing his ashes was attached to a
      <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(rocket)" title="Pegasus (rocket)">
       Pegasus XL
      </a>
      rocket and launched into Earth orbit on April 21, 1997.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simons97_72-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Simons97-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It re-entered the atmosphere in May 2002.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        74
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      O'Neill directed his Space Studies Institute to continue their efforts "until people are living and working in space".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After his death, management of SSI was passed to his son Roger and colleague Freeman Dyson.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSIHistory_43-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-SSIHistory-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      SSI continued to hold conferences every other year to bring together scientists studying space colonization until 2001.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kolm" title="Henry Kolm">
       Henry Kolm
      </a>
      went on to start Magplane Technology in the 1990s to develop the magnetic transportation technology that O'Neill had written about. In 2007, Magplane demonstrated a working magnetic pipeline system to transport phosphate ore in Florida. The system ran at a speed of 40mph (65km/h), far slower than the high-speed trains O'Neill envisioned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      All three of the founders of the
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Frontier_Foundation" title="Space Frontier Foundation">
       Space Frontier Foundation
      </a>
      , an organization dedicated to opening the space frontier to human settlement, were supporters of O'Neill's ideas and had worked with him in various capacities at the Space Studies Institute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        79
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of them,
      <a href="/wiki/Rick_Tumlinson" title="Rick Tumlinson">
       Rick Tumlinson
      </a>
      , describes three men as models for space advocacy:
      <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">
       Wernher von Braun
      </a>
      , Gerard K. O'Neill, and
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">
       Carl Sagan
      </a>
      . Von Braun pushed for "projects that ordinary people can be proud of but not participate in".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grierson04_80-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grierson04-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        80
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sagan wanted to explore the universe from a distance. O'Neill, with his grand scheme for settlement of the Solar System, emphasized moving ordinary people off the Earth "en masse".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grierson04_80-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grierson04-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        80
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Society" title="National Space Society">
       National Space Society
      </a>
      (NSS) gives the Gerard K. O'Neill Memorial Award for Space Settlement Advocacy to individuals noted for their contributions in the area of space settlement. Their contributions can be scientific, legislative, and educational. The award is a trophy cast in the shape of a
      <a href="/wiki/Bernal_sphere" title="Bernal sphere">
       Bernal sphere
      </a>
      . The NSS first bestowed the award in 2007 on lunar entrepreneur and former astronaut
      <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
       Harrison Schmitt
      </a>
      . In 2008, it was given to physicist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Marburger" title="John Marburger">
       John Marburger
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSAward_81-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NSSAward-81">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        81
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In fiction, the protagonist of Stephen Baxter's
      <i>
       Manifold:Time
      </i>
      names his spaceship the
      <i>
       Gerard K. O'Neill.
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      As of November, 2013, Gerard O'Neill's papers and work are now located in the archives at the Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum, Steven F. Udvar-Hazy Center.
     </p>
