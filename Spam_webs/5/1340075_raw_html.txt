     <h1 class="entry-title">
      How to Take Great Dessert Photographs
     </h1>
     <div class="meta right">
      <time class="published" datetime="2013-11-21T00:00:00+00:00">
       November 21, 2013
      </time>
     </div>
    </header>
   </div>
   <!-- /.hero -->
   <div class="wrap">
    <div class="container" role="document">
     <div class="content row">
      <main class="main " role="main">
       <div class="entry-content col-md-offset-1 col-md-10 col-lg-offset-2 col-lg-8">
        <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
        <p>
        </p>
        <div style="margin: 10px auto;">
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="Great Dessert Photography" height="610" src="http://a1128.g.akamai.net/7/1128/497/0001/origin-d5.scene7.com/is/image/ProvideCommerce/great_dessert_photography_blog1121" title="Great Dessert Photography"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          If you love dessert the way we love dessert, then, like us, youre probably grabbing for your camera when everyone else is reaching for a fork. Theres just something about a decadent dessert (preferably dipped in chocolate) that makes many of us want to share the moment with friends and family. If not literally, at least on Instagram or Twitter.
         </p>
         <p>
          Making food look as delicious as it tastes isnt always easy. Theres quite an art to taking great dessert photos. To help you in your quest for fabulous food pics, we curated this list of tips from expert food photographers and dessert lovers.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          1. Learn About Lighting
         </p>
         <p>
          Want mouthwatering dessert photos? The most important skill to master is lighting. Its the skill that separates the edible from the exquisite.
         </p>
         <p>
          Because I dont have a fancy camera, I have to rely on other tricks to make my sweets look good, explains
          <a href="http://www.cakespy.com">
           Jessie Oleson of CakeSpy.com
          </a>
          . Lighting is key  natural light is best. I prefer a gentle natural light without strong shadows.
         </p>
         <p>
          And, whatever you do, dont use a built-in flash. Most pop-up lights will cause harsh shadows and ugly hot spots. Shoot near an open window and use a reflector to bounce extra sunlight back onto your dessert. When dining out, asked to be seated near the window or outdoors. If youre really serious, time your dinner so that dessert arrives during the 
          <a href="http://www.golden-hour.com">
           golden hour
          </a>
           when the light is just right for beautiful photos.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          2. Its All About Balance  White Balance
         </p>
         <p>
          In addition to having enough light, be sure that your camera understands the kind of light youre shooting in. Setting you camera for daylight, incandescent or fluorescent will help you achieve color fidelity by accounting for the color temperature of the light source. Without proper white balance settings, photos can end up with an orange cast or fluffy white mashed potatoes can become an unappetizing shade of blue.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="panna cotta" height="458" src="http://a1128.g.akamai.net/7/1128/497/0001/origin-d5.scene7.com/is/image/ProvideCommerce/dessert_photography_panna_cotta_blog1121" title="panna cotta"/>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: left;">
          <small>
           Proper white balance and good natural light is essential to showcase the creaminess of this Black Pepper Panna Cotta by
           <a href="http://17andbaking.com">
            Elissa Bernstein at 17 and Baking
           </a>
           .
          </small>
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          3. Find the Right Angle
         </p>
         <p>
          Be cautious about photographing food from above, which can result in flat, two-dimensional images. Instead get down, lean in or put food at eye level. Look for angles that capture varied heights to add depth to your photos.
          <a href="http://www.professionalphotography101.com/photography/best_food_angle.html">
           Professional Photography 101
          </a>
          does a good job of explaining this tip with side-by-side comparison shots.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          4. Depth of Field
         </p>
         <p>
          Selective focus is another trick of great dessert photographers. The goal with this technique is to focus on a single area and let the rest of the photo fade into a soft blur. You dont even need an expensive lens for this technique! Most point-and-shoot cameras have a Macro setting that can be used to take images with a limited depth of field. If you are lucky enough to use an SLR, set a wide aperture. Dario Milano of
          <a href="http://www.foodpixels.com/">
           Food Pixels
          </a>
          <a href="http://digital-photography-school.com/10-tips-for-mouth-watering-food-photography">
           recommends
          </a>
          using a 50mm lens at f 1.8. IPhoneographers can pick up an Olloclip to achieve similar effects.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          5. Go for Bokeh
         </p>
         <p>
          Bokeh (
          <a href="http://photographylife.com/what-is-bokeh">
           pronounced boke
          </a>
          ) is the out-of-focus area on an image with shallow depth of field. A soft, beautiful bokeh is like a backdrop for your focal point. It allows the viewer to focus on the most important part of your photo and makes your images pop. For the most part, bokeh is controlled by the lens youre using. Nevertheless, there are a few things you can do to improve the quality your bokeh. Get in close to your subject and increase the distance between your subject and your background. Without investing in fancy lenses, these two small steps offer the best chance of getting the visually-pleasing blur that top photographers aim for.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <a href="http://www.berries.com/christmas-gifts-sx1" target="new">
           <img alt="Christmas Decorated Strawberries" height="610" src="http://a1128.g.akamai.net/7/1128/497/0001/origin-d5.scene7.com/is/image/ProvideCommerce/dessert_photography_christmas_decorated_strawberries_blog1121" title="Christmas Decorated Strawberries"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: left;">
          <small>
           This sweet photo of our
           <a href="http://www.berries.com/christmas-strawberries-sx2">
            Christmas strawberries
           </a>
           has a pleasing bokeh that keeps the background berries recognizable while bringing attention to the main subject.
          </small>
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          6. Use a Tripod
         </p>
         <p>
          Bokeh is good. Blur is bad. Avoid blurry shots by using a tripod whenever possible. Shooting in natural light often requires a slow shutter speed. Even the best photographers will fall victim to camera shake in low-light conditions. If youre shooting desserts at home, take the time to set up a tripod. If youre shooting desserts with your smartphone, get a good grip on your phone and prop your arms on the table for stability.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          7. Set the Scene With a Series
         </p>
         <p>
          Rather than just taking a photo of your ready-to-eat dessert, take a series of photos that tell a story. At home, take a few shots of the preparation: an artful compilation of raw ingredients, stiff peaks of fresh whipped cream, or liquid batter ready for the oven. Dining out? Set the scene with a photo of the candle at your table, a long shot down the bar, or your honeys first bite. Then, use your favorite app to make a photo collage for sharing on social media.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          8. Play With Props
         </p>
         <p>
          Props are the supporting actors in your Academy Award-winning dessert photo. The dessert may tell the story, but the props set the scene. Place a spoon in the distance to hint at delicious bites yet to come. Include a pretty napkin with accent colors or garnish the plate with a contrasting color. Use a paper napkin or fabric placemat to add more texture to the photo.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="bundt cake slices" height="800" src="http://a1128.g.akamai.net/7/1128/497/0001/origin-d5.scene7.com/is/image/ProvideCommerce/dessert_photography_bundt_cake_slices_blog1121" title="bundt cake slices"/>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <small>
           This clever photo by
           <a href="http://www.completelydelicious.com">
            Completely Delicious
           </a>
           uses the full cake as the prop and the slice as the star.
          </small>
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          9. Pay Attention to Plating
         </p>
         <p>
          If you watch Top Chef, you know that the last few moments of competition isnt spent cooking, its spent plating. Plating is more than just putting food in a dish. Take a minute to make sure that the rim of the dish is clean, that the garnish is attractive and that all the little details are in place.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          10. Keep it Simple
         </p>
         <p>
          Although a full plate may sound appealing, visually, it can appear cluttered. Serve less or use larger dishes for a minimalist display that allows your dessert to be the star. In situations where its not possible to photograph a smaller serving, such as in a restaurant, use selective focus to make one part of the dish stand out as the hero.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          11. Take a Bite
         </p>
         <p>
          Add a sense of real life to your dessert images by photographing some shots that are mid-taste. Take a photo that says, I couldnt wait to take a bite. A cookie with a single bite mark or a missing a spoonful of pie can also add intimacy to your photos. Its as if youre inviting the viewer to share your dessert.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="mini pies" height="776" src="http://a1128.g.akamai.net/7/1128/497/0001/origin-d5.scene7.com/is/image/ProvideCommerce/dessert_photography_mini_pies_blog1121" title="mini pies"/>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <small>
           This photo by
           <a href="http://www.dessertfortwo.com">
            Desserts for Two
           </a>
           takes a bite to show off the flaky layers of crust in this mini pie.
          </small>
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          12. Dont Take a Bite
         </p>
         <p>
          Sometimes food looks best when its not meant to be eaten. That is to say, many foods lose something in the preparation. Slightly undercooked foods or even replacement ingredients may be necessary if you want to capture a recipe looking its best. Rob, the editor of Light Stalking, explains that
          <a href="http://www.lightstalking.com/how-to-photograph-food">
           food looks best full-bodied not big
          </a>
          . He also has some great tips for styling food for photography rather than for eating.
         </p>
         <p style="font-weight: bold; display: block; margin-top: 25px; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          13. Have Fun
         </p>
         <p>
          According to Oleson, adding an element of whimsy or clever cropping can help make a photo compelling even if it is not technically perfect. And of course, you can always Photoshop a face on it to make it look adorable, as in
          <a href="http://sweets.seriouseats.com/2013/10/cakespy-dulce-de-leche-pumpkin-sandwich-cookies.html">
           this post
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          While some might groan at the idea of amateur food photography, its only because carelessly snapped images can make even the most amazing dishes look unappetizing. Use these tips for better dessert photos and your friends and family will be asking for seconds!
         </p>
