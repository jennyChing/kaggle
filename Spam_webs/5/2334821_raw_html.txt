     <p>
      <b>
       Ithkuil
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">
       constructed language
      </a>
      created by John Quijada,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foer-2012_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-foer-2012-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      designed to express deeper levels of human cognition briefly yet overtly and clearly, particularly with regard to human categorization. Ithkuil is notable for its grammatical complexity and extensive phoneme inventory. The name "Ithkuil" is an anglicized form of
      <i>
       Ikul
      </i>
      , which in the original form roughly means "hypothetical representation of a language".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-intro-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ithkuil is presented as a cross between an
      <a href="/wiki/A_priori_(languages)" title="A priori (languages)">
       a priori
      </a>
      <i>
       philosophical
      </i>
      and a
      <i>
       logical
      </i>
      language. It strives to minimize the ambiguities and semantic vagueness found in natural human languages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2120_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sl2120-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The many examples from the original grammar book
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2120_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sl2120-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      show that a message, like a meaningful phrase or a sentence, can usually be expressed in Ithkuil with fewer sounds, or lexically distinct speech-elements, than in natural human languages. Quijada deems his creation too complex and strictly regular a language to have developed naturally, but nonetheless a language suited to human conversation. No person, including Quijada, is known to be able to speak Ithkuil fluently.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kozl_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kozl-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and again in 2009
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gertelmann_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gertelmann-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with its offshoot
      <a href="#Ilaksh">
       Ilaksh
      </a>
      Ithkuil was featured in the Russian-language popular science and IT magazine
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Computerra" title="Computerra">
        Computerra
       </a>
      </i>
      . In 2008, it won the Smiley Award.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The second "definitive (or 'official')" revision is a major revision that was released on 15 July 2011 and is also named Ithkuil out of convenience and continuity. A sizeable portion of this article deals with the original version of Ithkuil released in 2004, but it is indicated which sections deal with which version of Ithkuil.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <p>
      For his influences, Quijada cites the "morpho-phonology of
      <a href="/wiki/Abkhaz_language" title="Abkhaz language">
       Abkhaz
      </a>
      verb complexes, the moods of verbs of certain
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
       American Indian languages
      </a>
      , the aspectual system of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Kordofanian_languages" title="NigerKordofanian languages">
       NigerKordofanian languages
      </a>
      , the nominal case systems of
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
       Basque
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">

      </a>
      , the enclitic system of the
      <a href="/wiki/Wakashan_languages" title="Wakashan languages">
       Wakashan languages
      </a>
      , the positional orientation systems of
      <a href="/wiki/Tzeltal_language" title="Tzeltal language">
       Tzeltal
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Guugu_Yimithirr_language" title="Guugu Yimithirr language">
       Guugu Yimidhirr
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">

      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzette_Elgin" title="Suzette Elgin">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1adan" title="Ladan">

      </a>

     <p>
      Since the mention of Ithkuil in the Russian magazine
      <i>
       Computerra
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kozl_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-kozl-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      several speakers of Russian contacted Quijada and expressed enthusiasm to learn Ithkuil, with several complaining about its difficulty in pronunciation. Quijada remade Ithkuil's morphophonology and published the revision on 10 June 2007 as Ilaksh.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2120_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sl2120-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The innovation featured other amendments to grammar, like some additional
      <i>
       Levels
      </i>
      or a slight shuffling of
      <i>
       noun cases
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Ilaksh script was redesigned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has two forms, a sequential "informal" system suitable for handwriting or compact typesetting, and a "formal"
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logographic" title="Logographic">
       logographic
      </a>
      system with artistic possibilities resembling
      <a href="/wiki/Maya_script" title="Maya script">
       Maya scripts
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the "informal" writing system, several parallel sets of lines are shaped to correspond sequentially to the different parallel sets of lexemes and inflections. It is directly pronounceable. The author designed it with reserve for convenient handwriting. The overall design would permit compact, clear, black-and-white rendering.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the colorful "formal" script, a single complex glyph represents an entire sentence. Diversely shaped, shaded and superimposed "cartouches" represent the
      <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
       syntactic
      </a>
      relations of the verb and noun phrases of a sentence. The edges of the cartouches have particular shapes that indicate one set of inflections, the colors indicate another set of inflections, and the textures yet another one. On the cartouches, "letters" of
      <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">
       hexagonal
      </a>
      outline spell out the shapes of particular lexemes. The cartouches form phrases, with primary phrases overlapping subordinate phrases. The coloring system utilizes different color densities and texturing for different colors in order to be usable by color-blind people. These density conventions also allow the formal system to be inexpensively printed in black-and-white, or inscribed or imprinted on stone or other materials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As of July 2011, Quijada plans to adopt the formal script "for use as an alternative, 'ornamental' writing system for artistic purposes" to the 2011 revision of Ithkuil, which was made to be morphologically similar to Ilaksh.
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ithkuil_.282004.29_3">
       Ithkuil (2004)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ithkuil (2004)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
       lexicon
      </a>
      of Ithkuil potentially consists of 3,600
      <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
       word roots
      </a>
      ; just 900 were assigned individual translations. Each root consists of 2
      <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
       consonantal
      </a>
      <i>
       radicals
      </i>
      , and can
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derivation_(linguistics)" title="Derivation (linguistics)">
       derive
      </a>
      thousands of
      <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">
       lexemes
      </a>
      through the use of Ithkuil's complex rules of
      <a href="/wiki/Morphophonology" title="Morphophonology">
       morphophonology
      </a>
      , which involve both
      <a href="/wiki/Consonant_mutation" title="Consonant mutation">
       consonantal
      </a>
      and vocal mutation, shifts in syllabic stress and tone, and affixation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ithkuil words can be divided into just two
      <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
       parts of speech
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       formatives
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       adjuncts
      </i>
      . Formatives can function both as nouns and as verbs, depending on the morpho-semantic context.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec2o6_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec2o6-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both nominal and verbal formatives are inflected to one of the possible 3
      <i>
       stems
      </i>
      , 3
      <i>
       patterns
      </i>
      , 2
      <i>
       designations
      </i>
      (
      <i>

      </i>
      or
      <i>

      </i>
      ), 9
      <i>

      </i>
      , 4

      </i>
      , 4
      <i>

      </i>
      , 6
      <i>

      </i>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="An_example_of_morphological_analysis">
       An example of morphological analysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: An example of morphological analysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        (based entirely on the original Ithkuil grammar book
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2120_3-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-sl2120-3">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          3
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
        )
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        ikul
       </span>
      </i>
      is a
      <i>
       formative
      </i>
      derived from the
      <i>
       root
      </i>
      <i>
       k-l
      </i>
      (broadly concerning "speech", "voice", or even "interpretation") through the addition of several morphological determinants:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        -u-
       </b>
       vocalic infix
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <i>
          kul
         </i>
         is the
         <i>
          holistic
         </i>
         variety of the
         <i>
          Stem 2
         </i>
         of the three other possible stems from
         <i>
          k-l
         </i>
         . Translating roughly as "a meaningful unit of speech", i.e. "a word", it gives no emphasis on the meaning or the vocal rendering of the word.
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        u  u
       </b>
       mutation of the infix
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <i>
          Secondary
         </i>
         , as opposed to
         <i>
          primary
         </i>
         ,
         <i>
          mode
         </i>
         , means that the word
         <i>
          kul
         </i>
         refers not to a real-life phenomenon, but rather to a mental representation, or projection, of that phenomenon; to an imaginary or hypothetical object. Thus "a made-up word".
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <i>
        grade 8
       </i>
       mutation of the first radical consonant:
       <b>
        k  k
       </b>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         The
         <i>
          configuration
         </i>
         of the term is
         <b>
          composite
         </b>
         . Roughly corresponding to the
         <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
          plurality
         </a>
         concept in
         <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
          Indo-European languages
         </a>
         , it also implies the objects in question (words,
         <i>
          kul
         </i>
         ) to be diverse, while forming a "coherent emergent entity" (rather than just a collection or an array of different words), thus meaning "a
         <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
          vocabulary
         </a>
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
          lexicon
         </a>
         ".
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        i-
       </b>
       vocalic prefix, one of the 24 possible for formative roots
       <dl>
        <dd>
         The
         <i>
          extension
         </i>
         is
         <i>
          delimitive
         </i>
         , perceiving "the vocabulary" as entire, with clearly distinguished boundaries, as opposed to it being just a local manifestation such as slang or a dialect of a broader lexicon (
         <i>
          -kul
         </i>
         ).
        </dd>
        <dd>
         The
         <i>
          affiliation
         </i>
         of the set of objects in question is
         <i>
          coalescent
         </i>
         . This indicates that the individual members of the set act together toward a higher purpose by coordinating their complementary functions. Thus, "a
         <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
          vocabulary
         </a>
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
          lexicon
         </a>
         " becomes "a language".
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        Syllabic stress
       </a>
       on the penultimate syllable (
       <i>
        -u-
       </i>
       )
       <dl>
        <dd>
         The
         <i>
          perspective
         </i>
         of the noun is
         <i>
          monadic
         </i>
         , seeing "the language" as a single and specific entity, rather than a collection of many languages existing separately, the general phenomenon ("human languages") or the abstract idea of language.
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Thus, the approximate translation of
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode">
        ikul
       </span>
      </i>
      is "an idea/fantasy of a complete purposeful system of complementary speech elements", or simply "an imaginary language".
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Numeral_system">
       Numeral system
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Numeral system">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Ithkuil uses a numeral system of
      <a href="/wiki/Radix" title="Radix">
       base 100
      </a>
      . There are roots for the numbers 1 to 10 (l-s, k-s, -s, p-s, -s, t-s, n-s, x-s, f-s, and m-s), and the stem-specific derivative affix is used with a root to add a multiple of 10, providing the numerals up to 99. Ithkuil does not use the concept of
      <a href="/wiki/0_(number)" title="0 (number)">
       zero
      </a>
      . Numbers greater than 100 are expressed periphrastically in speech, whereas Ital has logograms for the numbers 1 to 100 and for even powers of 100.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ithkuil_.282011.29_2">
       Ithkuil (2011)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ithkuil (2011)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
       lexicon
      </a>
      of Ithkuil potentially consists of approximately 3,600
      <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
       word roots
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2o2_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-2o2-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      So far, just over 900 are assigned with individual translations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lexicon_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lexicon-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each root consists of a cluster of 14
      <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
       consonants
      </a>
      (five-consonant clusters are also available, but remain without an assigned meaning).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2o2_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-2o2-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From the root, word stems are formed by affixing the vocalic affix that indicates pattern, stem type, and function.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2o2_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2o2-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ithkuil words can be divided into just two
      <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
       parts of speech
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       formatives
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       adjuncts
      </i>
      . Formatives can function both as nouns and as verbs, depending on the morpho-semantic context.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec2o4_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec2o4-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are words that function as nouns and verbs, but these derive from the same root.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec2o4_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec2o4-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formatives">
       Formatives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Formatives">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roots">
       Roots
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Roots">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      Roots are Ithkuil's most basic semantic units. All Ithkuil formatives are derived from a limited number of roots. Each root consists of a consonants cluster of 1 to 5 consonants. There are approximately 3,600 roots, of which
      <a href="/wiki/Accidental_gap" title="Accidental gap">
       just over 900 have been assigned meanings
      </a>
      . No five-consonant-cluster roots have been assigned meanings.
     </p>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stems_and_pattern">
       Stems and pattern
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Stems and pattern">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <i>
       function

         ]

     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_morphology">
       Basic morphology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Basic morphology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      All Ithkuil formatives, whether functioning as nouns or verbs, inflect for various grammatical categories that are quite dissimilar from any of those in natural languages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec3-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Quantization is more or less covered by the grammatical categories
      <i>
       configuration
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       affiliation
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       perspective
      </i>
      , even though these do not technically refer to number per se.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o1_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o1-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable">
      <tr>
       <th>
        Category
       </th>
       <th>
        Indicates
       </th>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Configuration
       </td>
       <td>
        the physical similarity and relationship between the members of a set, e.g. trees may occur in a collection of the same species, of different species, or even in a patternless collection with plants that are not trees.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o1_18-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o1-18">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          18
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Affiliation
       </td>
       <td>
        the subjective purpose or function of members of a set, e.g. a group of trees may occur naturally and have no purpose, they may have the same purpose, complementary purposes, or different purposes.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o2_19-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o2-19">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          19
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Perspective
       </td>
       <td>
        the boundedness of a set, i.e. if it is viewed as a single unit, multiple disconnected units, viewed generically, or its characteristics considered abstractly.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o3_20-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o3-20">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          20
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Extension
       </td>
       <td>
        the referred part of a set, e.g. its beginning or its end.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o4_21-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o4-21">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          21
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Essence
       </td>
       <td>
        whether the referred set is in the real world or exists solely psychologically.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o5_22-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o5-22">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          22
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Context
       </td>
       <td>
        the psychological relevance of the set, e.g. merely its existence or the set as symbolic for something else.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o6_23-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o6-23">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          23
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Designation
       </td>
       <td>
        the authority or permanence of a set.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec3o7_24-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Sec3o7-24">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          24
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cases">
       Cases
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      There are 92
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
       grammatical cases
      </a>

     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_morphology">
       Verbal morphology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Verbal morphology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      Several distinct grammatical categories apply only to verbal formatives. These are listed below:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec5_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec5-27">
        <span>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adjuncts">
       Adjuncts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Adjuncts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      There are two types of adjuncts in Ithkuil: personal-reference adjuncts and verbal adjuncts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec2o4o2_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec2o4o2-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      All adjuncts are highly
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
       synthetic
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal-reference_adjuncts">
       Personal-reference adjuncts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Personal-reference adjuncts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      Personal-reference adjuncts are akin to pronouns in English. There are two types of personal-reference adjuncts in Ithkuil: Single-referent and dual-referent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec8o1_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec8o1-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_adjuncts">
       Verbal adjuncts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Verbal adjuncts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      Verbal adjuncts are adjuncts that work in conjunction with verbal formatives to provide information about the latter's
      <i>
       valence
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       level
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       phase
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       sanction
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       illocution
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       modality
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       aspect
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       bias
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec2o4o2_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec2o4o2-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Of these,
      <i>
       modality
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       level
      </i>
      can only be indicating using verbal adjuncts, whereas the others can also be expressed on the verbal formative.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sec6_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sec6-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
       Orthography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Orthography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ithkuil's native script is called Ital, the (2004) Ithkuil word for 'hypothetical writing system'. It is a
      <i>
       morphophonemic
      </i>
      script because characters convey both phonetic and morphological information. Its use is closely tied to Ithkuil's grammatical system, which allows much of the phonological aspect of words to be morpho-syntactically inferred. Those parts of an Ithkuil word whose pronunciation is predictable are not written, whereas the characters used to indicate the pronunciation of the unpredictable parts of a word also convey the grammatical information necessary to reconstruct the implicit phonetics. Words are thus written in a highly abbreviated manner, particularly useful for the highly inflected, occasionally elongated words of the Ithkuil language. The script is also used alphabetically for transliterating foreign words.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_advantages">
       Possible advantages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ithkuil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Possible advantages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_relativity" title="Linguistic relativity">
       SapirWhorf hypothesis
      </a>
      postulates that a person's language influences their perceptions and cognitive patterns. Stanislav Kozlovsky proposed,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kozl_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-kozl-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the Russian popular-scientific magazine
      <i>
       Computerra
      </i>
      , that a fluent speaker of Ithkuil, accordingly, would think "about five or six times as fast" as a speaker of a typical natural language. One
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2014)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      may also argue that, Ithkuil being an extremely precise and
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
       synthetic language
      </a>
      , its speaker would, under the SapirWhorf hypothesis, have a more discerning, deeper understanding both of everyday situations and of broader phenomena, and of abstract
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">
       philosophical
      </a>
      categories.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, strong forms of
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_relativity" title="Linguistic relativity">
       linguistic relativity
      </a>
      , of which the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis is the leading example, have been disproven within mainstream linguistics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moreover, in line with this, John Quijada (Ithkuil's creator) has stated he does not believe a speaker would think necessarily any faster, because even though Ithkuil is terse, a single word requires a lot more thought before it can be spoken than it would in a natural language.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FAQ-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "For these reasons, I believe use of Ithkuil would probably allow one to think more deeply, critically, and analytically; but think faster? I doubt it."
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Kozlovsky also likened Ithkuil to the fictional
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Speedtalk" title="Speedtalk">
        Speedtalk
       </a>
      </i>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
       Robert A. Heinlein
      </a>
      's novella
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_(Heinlein)" title="Gulf (Heinlein)">
        Gulf
       </a>
      </i>
      , and contrasted both languages with the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Newspeak" title="Newspeak">
        Newspeak
       </a>
      </i>
      of the communicationally restricted society of
      <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">
       Orwell
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four">
        Nineteen Eighty-Four
       </a>
      </i>
      . Ithkuil is by far the most complete language of the three.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      John Quijada acknowledged the similarity of Ithkuil's design goals to those of Speedtalk,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2120_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-sl2120-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      remarking that,
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "[h]owever, Heinlein's Speedtalk appears to focus only on the morpho-phonological component of language[, whereas] Ithkuil has been designed with an equal focus on [morphology, lexico-morphology, or lexico-semantics]. Additionally, the apparent purpose of Heinlein's language is simple rapidity/brevity of speech and thought, while Ithkuil is focused on maximal communication in the most efficient manner, a somewhat different purpose, in which brevity per se is irrelevant."
      </p>
     </blockquote>
