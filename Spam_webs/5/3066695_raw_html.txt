   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Bucket-brigade device
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       bucket brigade
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       bucket-brigade device
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       BBD
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discrete-time" title="Discrete-time">
       discrete-time
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Analog_delay_line" title="Analog delay line">
       analogue delay line
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      developed in 1969 by F. Sangster and K. Teer of the Philips Research Labs. It consists of a series of
      <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">
       capacitance
      </a>
      sections C
      <sub>
       0
      </sub>
      to C
      <sub>
       n
      </sub>
      . The stored
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analogue_signal" title="Analogue signal">
       analogue signal
      </a>
      is moved along the line of
      <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">
       capacitors
      </a>
      , one step at each
      <a href="/wiki/Clock_signal" title="Clock signal">
       clock cycle
      </a>
      . The name comes from analogy with the term
      <a href="/wiki/Bucket_brigade" title="Bucket brigade">
       bucket brigade
      </a>
      , used for a line of people passing buckets of water.
     </p>
     <p>
      In most signal processing applications, bucket brigades have been replaced by devices that use
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processing" title="Digital signal processing">
       digital signal processing
      </a>
      , manipulating samples in digital form. Bucket brigades still see use in specialty applications, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guitar_effects" title="Guitar effects">
       guitar effects
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A well-known
      <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">
       integrated circuit
      </a>
      device around 1980, the
      <a href="/wiki/Reticon" title="Reticon">
       Reticon
      </a>
      SAD-1024
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      implemented two 512-stage analog delay lines in a 16-pin
      <a href="/wiki/Dual_in-line_package" title="Dual in-line package">
       DIP
      </a>
      . It allowed clock frequencies ranging from 1.5kHz to more than 1.5MHz. The SAD-512 was a single delay line version. The Philips Semiconductors TDA1022
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      similarly offered a 512-stage delay line but with a clock rate range of 5500kHz. Other common BBD chips include the Panasonic MN3005,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      MN3007
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and MN3205,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with the primary differences being the available delay time.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, the guitar effects pedal manufacturer Visual Sound recommissioned production of the Panasonic-designed MN3102
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and MN3207
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      BBD chip that it offers for sale.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite being analog in their representation of individual signal voltage samples, these devices are discrete in the time domain and thus are limited by the
      <a href="/wiki/Nyquist%E2%80%93Shannon_sampling_theorem" title="NyquistShannon sampling theorem">
       NyquistShannon sampling theorem
      </a>
      ; both the input and output signals are generally
      <a href="/wiki/Low-pass_filter" title="Low-pass filter">
       low-pass filtered
      </a>
      . The input must be low-pass filtered to avoid
      <a href="/wiki/Aliasing" title="Aliasing">
       aliasing
      </a>
      effects, while the output is low-pass filtered for reconstruction. (A low-pass is used as an approximation to the
      <a href="/wiki/Whittaker%E2%80%93Shannon_interpolation_formula" title="WhittakerShannon interpolation formula">
       WhittakerShannon interpolation formula
      </a>
      .)
     </p>
     <p>
      The concept of the bucket-brigade device led to the
      <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
       charge-coupled device
      </a>
      (CCD) developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">
       Bell Labs
      </a>
      .
     </p>
