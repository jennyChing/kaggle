         <h1 style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           Attending public lectures is a great way to sharpen the
intellectual saw, as our correspondent discovered after spending a
month attending free public lectures at Toronto universities
          </span>
         </h1>
         <span style="color: #5e7a77; font-weight: normal; font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
          In the digital age of instant information, the public lecture might
seem an anachronism. Theres the travel time, the effort to locate the
right room, the strangers youll be sitting beside (with the lights
still on), the absence of a single course credit, and no salty snacks
to fill in the boring moments as you listen to a single, live human
being talk.
         </span>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           After a month attending free or nearly free public events given by
Toronto universities, I can report that the public lecture is both
plentiful and popular. My goal was to discover whether the time and
effort a member of the public devotes to this activity is time well
spent. Would the speakers engage me after a long day of work? Would I
feel out of place back in a university lecture hall?
          </span>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           <strong>
            Thursday, Oct. 2 
           </strong>
           Francis Broun is the most
entertaining of the lecturers Ive heard in my month touring the Ivory
Towers. That makes it easier to cough up the $10 it cost me as a
first-time attendee.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           Professor Broun has earned a loyal following of grown-up groupies
who delight in his knowledge, enthusiasm and irreverent sense of humour
about art history (He never changed his socks, he quips of
Michelangelo). His students at the
           <b>
            <a href="http://www.ocad.ca/home.htm" target="_blank" title="Ontario College of Art &amp; Design">
             <font color="#37A29C">
              Ontario College of Art &amp; Design
             </font>
            </a>
           </b>
           think hes great too  they have nominated him in this years
           <b>
            <a href="http://www.tvo.org/TVOsites/WebObjects/TvoMicrosite.woa?bestlecturer">
             <font color="#37A29C">
              TVOntario Best Lecturer Competition.
             </font>
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           I fall in love with his public lecturing venue, an elegant 19th
century house in Torontos tony Yorkville district that serves as
headquarters for the Womens Art Association. At break time, our group
of about 20 students, working folks and retirees is ushered to a long,
linen-covered dining table for tea, coffee and lemon pound cake.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           Dr. Brouns weekly fall lecture series covers the history of high
Renaissance art, from the techniques used by the artists to the social
and political context they worked in. On this rainy, wind-swept night,
he gives the first of two lectures on Michelangelo, using many of his
own slides taken on trips to Europe. Of Michelangelos heart-wrenching
Piet statue of Mary holding a crucified Christ in her lap, Dr. Broun
reveals it was one of the plumiest commissions of 1498. It was all
about who you knew. He also points out that if you stood the figures
up, either she is a giantess or he is a midget.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           About Michelangelos famous David, Dr. Broun directs our attention to
the fact that his thumb is bigger than another part of his anatomy,
to peals of laughter. On the four years it took Michelangelo to paint
the Sistine Chapel nearly single-handedly, it was a super-human
project. It took me far longer to do my basement, for Godssake!
          </span>
         </p>
         <p style="color: #5e7a77; font-family: arial, sans-serif; font-size: 11px; font-weight: normal; ">

         <h1>
          <img alt="" height="46" src="images/metro_canada_wt4s.bmp" width="140"/>
         </h1>
         <font color="#779d8f">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           <strong>
           </strong>
          </span>
         </font>
         <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; color: #096f6f; ">
          <strong>
           A lot of talk goes a long way
          </strong>
         </span>
         <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; color: #096f6f; ">
          <strong>
          </strong>
         </span>
         <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; color: #096f6f; ">
          <strong>
          </strong>
         </span>
         <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; color: #096f6f; ">
          <strong>
          </strong>
         </span>
         <div class="date">
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           <strong>
           </strong>
          </span>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
            December 15, 2008
            <br/>
            by Brian Coulton for Metro Canada
            <br/>
           </span>
          </p>
          <br/>
         </div>
         <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
          When
Francis Broun was an undergraduate, he swore if he ever became a
teacher, he would never bore his students like so many of his
professors did him.
          <br/>
         </span>
         <p>
          <span style="font-family: arial; font-size: 13px; ">
           Now, after teaching for more than 30 years,
the Ontario College of Art and Design professors commitment to
excitement has garnered him a Top 20 spot in TVOs 2009 Big Ideas Best
Lecturer Competition, making him the first OCAD teacher to be nominated
for the honour.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Here I am. Im probably the oldest person in
this place pulling down my pension and still somehow the students
really enjoy what I do, says Broun, who has taught in OCADs Faculty
of Liberal Studies since 1991.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Before OCAD, Scottish-born Broun
worked for 15 years in the Art Gallery of Ontarios education
department and continues to host private lectures at the Womens Art
Association of Canada. But whether hes lecturing to students or
elderly ladies, he says he does the exact same thing.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I dont
use academic language in a way, which I think helps the students. First
of all, I dont understand what most of the words mean myself so I talk
in a fairly straightforward way, he says. Plus, I cant do anything
else. Ive had several other careers and its always come down to this,
because I think Im just a talkaholic.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           He maintains humour and
that seems to be what his students seem to enjoy most about his
lectures (Im not a stuffy old fart, he quips,) Broun adds he really
tries to encourage them to travel to the source of the art they study.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Most
of them have never been to Europe or to the Louvre or one of the other
great galleries to look at the real thing, he says. Until youre
standing nose to nose with the real thing, its just never the same
somehow. Experience is everything in this business.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Nadia Carretta heard her former professors message loud and clear.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Francis
is the epitome of wisdom because he has all the education but he uses
application of actually going to these places all over Europe too,
says the third year OCAD curatorial and criticism practices student. I
think thats what he really wants to try and get across  you have to
combine the two or else were just kind of like book nerds and thats
about it.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Carretta will travel to Italy to study art history
next semester. She credits Broun, who provided recommendation and
reference letters for her, with going above and beyond to help her
overseas pursuit.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           He set me up with another professor  one
of his colleagues who lives there. That was the biggest thing a
professor has ever done for me.
          </span>
         </p>
