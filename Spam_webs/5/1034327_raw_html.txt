     <h1>
      5 adventures in Thailand youve probably never heard of
     </h1>
     <div class="post-inner clearfix">
      <div class="post-meta">
       <div class="post-meta-author">
        by
        <a href="http://matadornetwork.com/author/katesiobhanhavercroft/">
         Kate Siobhan Mulligan
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="post-meta-date">
        August 14, 2014
       </div>
       <div class="post-avatar">
        <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-100" height="100" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/authors/katesiobhanhavercroft.jpg" width="100"/>
       </div>
       <div class="post-share-wrapper post-share--vertical" data-post-id="255111" data-sticky="" data-sticky-max-width="991" data-sticky-offset-top="69" data-sticky-parent-contain="" data-sticky-parent-selector=".page-section">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="post-content">
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.statravel.com/travel-thailand.htm/">
         <noscript>
          <img alt="TAT/STA logos" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/07/tat-sta-logo.png" title=""/>
         </noscript>
         <img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-254139 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/07/tat-sta-logo.png" height="73" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <em>
         This post is proudly produced in partnership with the Tourism Authority of Thailand and STA Travel, working together to tell stories of the peoples, places, and cultures that make Thailand special.
        </em>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <h5>
        1. Being a faux-pirate / learning to dive with The Junk
       </h5>
       <p>
        <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="480" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/LHyyIx_dS7E" width="853">
        </iframe>
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.thejunk.com/">
         The Junk
        </a>
        was once a haggard sailboat lugging cargo across the Indian Ocean.  Now shes a luxury liveaboard that cruises the sparkly waters of Southern Thailand. The Junk has even been featured in a number of movies because of its pirate-y appearance and excellent restoration work.
       </p>
       <p>
        The thing with The Junk is it carries a maximum of 18 people on any given excursion  usually fewer. So you get this massive, authentically renovated sailboat pretty much to yourself and a dozen other like-minded (and therefore awesome) people.
       </p>
       <p>
        Watch the sunrise from the bow of the boat, spend the day diving in remote and picture-perfect locations, explore reefs and canyons and shipwrecks with just the small team from your boat  no more day-tripping snorkelers choking on water, stomping on coral, or leaving behind cans of Tiger beer. Just you, the group, and the wide Indian Ocean. The crew of The Junk have even perfected a quiet dropoff via a dingy, so as not to scare away fish and sea creatures by pulling the boat up too close to reef sites.
       </p>
       <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_256371" style="width: 310px">
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Thailand treehouse" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/thailand-treehouse.png" title=""/>
        </noscript>
        <img class="size-full wp-image-256371 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/thailand-treehouse.png" height="460" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="429"/>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Photo: Rabeang Pasak
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        Finish the day chowing down on the 5-star Thai and Western cooking, and then watch the sunset as you sip a bev on the sundeck and wonder if life can get any better. It probably cant.
       </p>
       <h5>
        2. Living in a treehouse
       </h5>
       <p>
        Head to Chiang Mai, but skip the ziplines and fancy cafes and go straight to Doi Saket, outside of the city. Here, youll find a world that speaks to your inner child: real, live treehouses in the dense, lush jungle.
       </p>
       <p>
        Its called
        <a href="http://www.chiangmaitreehouse.com/">
         Rabeang Pasak
        </a>
        , and its a family-run company. The owner, Lee, is an architect, so dont expect some little makeshift platform; this is a full-on village built on and around the trees.
       </p>
       <p>
        More than that, the structures are made from gorgeous cuts of wood, with porches, windows, staircases, showers, and ladders, and each is different.
       </p>
       <p>
        Theyre all as beautiful to look at as they are to live in. Some have rooftop patios, others have private pools, while still others overlook waterfalls.
       </p>
       <p>
        So hike, swim, stroll, and take in the soaring teak forest around you. At the end of the day, crack a cold one on the porch of your tree palace, channel your inner 6-year-old, and proclaim yourself king (or queen) of this jungle.
       </p>
       <h5>
        3. Moto-trekking the highways of Northern Thailand
       </h5>
       <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_256378" style="width: 950px">
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Thailand motorcycle" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/thailand-motorcycle-940x626.jpg" title=""/>
        </noscript>
        <img class="size-large wp-image-256378 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/thailand-motorcycle-940x626.jpg" height="626" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="940"/>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Photo:
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/kamgtr/">
          KamrenB Photography
         </a>
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        One of the highways up in the north of Thailand is known as the road of 10,000 turns. If youve ever ridden a motorcycle, that line probably just made you drool on your shirt a little bit.
       </p>
       <p>
        Not only do the highways weave, dip, and carve through mountainous jungles dotted with temples, and slice through valleys full of rice and soy fields, but they also happen to be some of the smoothest roads in the country (not only newer, but with less traffic than those in the south). For anyone with a passion for two wheels and the wind in their face, this is the place to be.
       </p>
       <p>
        There are a ton of operators that will take you on tours up here, so you never have to worry which way to go at that fork in the road. Tours vary from one to several days, to as much as two weeks  and costs rise accordingly. Multi-day tours include accommodation, and some throw in activities like bungee jumping, massages, elephant visits, and pretty much any other add-on you might want. Two recommended guide companies are
        <a href="http://www.bigbiketours.net/">
         Big Bike Tours
        </a>
        and
        <a href="http://thaimotorcycletouring.com/">
         Thai Motorcycle Tours
        </a>
        . A smaller outfit, but one with a lot of personality, is
        <a href="http://www.smilingalbino.com/asia-tours/thailand/">
         Smiling Albino
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        If youre not comfortable on a motorcycle and would prefer the saddle of an old-fashioned, human-powered bicycle, check out
        <a href="http://www.tigertrailthailand.com/chiang-mai-cycling-tours">
         Tiger Trail Outdoor Adventures
        </a>
        , who specialize in responsible tourism and offer one-day bike tours out of Chiang Mai. Or, for a longer cycle tour, try
        <a href="http://grasshopperadventures.com/tours.php?country=Thailand">
         Grasshopper Adventures
        </a>
        , who run trips ranging from one to 15 days and cover four categories: road, pioneering (off the beaten bath), family, and photo.
       </p>
       <h5>
        4. Arranging a homestay on a rural tropical island
       </h5>
       <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_256384" style="width: 950px">
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Koh Lanta" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/koh-lanta-940x606.jpg" title=""/>
        </noscript>
        <img class="size-large wp-image-256384 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/koh-lanta-940x606.jpg" height="606" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="940"/>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Photo:
         <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/nurpax/">
          Janne Hellsten
         </a>
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        The farthest you can get from yolo-ing Millennial tourists is inside the home of a local family. Here youll find a whole other world, so far removed from the scenes of Bangkoks Khao San Road and Ko Pha Ngans full moon parties you might wonder if its the same country. It is. Get off the
        <em>
         Lonely Planet
        </em>
        trail and into small rural towns with one road, home-cooked meals like red curry with fresh local crab, and the honor of being welcomed into someones home.
       </p>
       <p>
        There are a lot of companies offering this service  not all are legit. The last thing you want is to sign up for an authentic homestay experience only to find out the family is being ripped off. Not so authentic. Research is key here.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.andamandiscoveries.com/thailand-village-tours/">
         Andaman Discoveries
        </a>
        is a company specializing in community-based tourism on the islands of Southern Thailand  meaning the tours benefit the local communities. They have a strict policy about the fair rotation of families as guests come to stay  no playing favorites, and visitors dont get to pick their home. This translates to all participating families receiving equal treatment, and the majority of fees go to the families  which means a lot to a household that lives on roughly $6 a day.
       </p>
       <p>
        Once youre all set up in your homestay, Andaman Discoveries can facilitate dozens of local excursions and experiences  from cooking classes, to handicraft lessons, to spending a day with the local fishermen. Of course, you also can visit the innumerable beaches, cliffs, parks, and mosques and temples, and go swimming, snorkeling, hiking, and sailing  whatevers on offer in your new home.
       </p>
       <p>
        It might not be the craziest adventure youve ever heard of, but its practically guaranteed youll have stories to tell till the day you die about this amazing Thai family who took you in, treated you like a relative, taught you to cook, sew, fish, invited you to their cousins huge wedding, and showed you the nooks and crannies of their rural island that would never be found in a guidebook.
       </p>
       <h5>
        5. Going on a photojournalistic jungle trek to a hill tribe village, led by a man who rescues young girls from the sex trade
       </h5>
       <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_256374" style="width: 310px">
        <noscript>
         <img alt="Mickey Choothesa" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/mickey-choothesa.jpg" title=""/>
        </noscript>
        <img class="size-full wp-image-256374 lazy" data-original="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/blogs/1/2014/08/mickey-choothesa.jpg" height="450" src="http://cdn1.matadornetwork.com/assets/images/blank1.png" width="500"/>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Photo: Author
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        Yup, you read that right. Let me introduce a man for the ages: Mickey Choothesa.
       </p>
       <p>
        Some backstory: Mickey was a photojournalist who covered all sorts of dangerous situations and conflicts around the world. Hes been to over 70 countries and spent time in Iraq, Bosnia, Rwanda, and Somalia, to name a few hot spots. Then, one day in 1998, Mickey was assigned a story in his home country, Thailand. What Mickey didnt expect was to see young girls being trafficked and smuggled. While Mickey went on to cover some post-9/11 conflicts, Thailand was on his mind. In 2005, he moved back to his homeland permanently, gave up his still-thriving career as a journalist, and founded an organization called
        <a href="http://cosasia.org/">
         COSA
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Mickey has spent the intervening ten years rescuing young girls from the sex trade, not only through long talks with anyone wholl listen  from tribal leaders, to local families, to criminals  but also going in undercover and rescuing girls with his bare hands. Mickey has since built two homes for the girls, and while he still goes on rescue missions, the focus has shifted to long-term mental and emotional care, combined with education and safe housing.
       </p>
       <p>
        Why this story? Well, Mickeys love for photography never died. So once or twice a year, he leads an
        <a href="http://cosasia.org/get-involved/photo-expedition/">
         epic photography expedition
        </a>
        into the hills outside of Chiang Mai, taking teams on foot up to remote hill tribes where many of the girls are trafficked from. You get to document your journey  not only amazing vistas and landscapes but dense jungle, rivers, waterfalls, and hill-tribe life. This trek combines Mickeys passion for photography with the need to generate income for COSA. Its win-win. What are you waiting for?
       </p>
       <p>
        Also  Im 100% positive Mickey is Rambo. So theres that.
       </p>
