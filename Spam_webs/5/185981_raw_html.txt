     <p>
      <b>
       Class conflict
      </b>
      , frequently referred to as
      <b>
       class warfare
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       class struggle
      </b>
      , is the tension or antagonism which exists in
      <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">
       society
      </a>
      due to competing
      <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomics" title="Socioeconomics">
       socioeconomic
      </a>
      interests and desires between people of different
      <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">
       classes
      </a>
      . The view that the class struggle provides the lever for radical social change for the majority is central to the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
       Karl Marx
      </a>
      and the anarchist
      <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin" title="Mikhail Bakunin">
       Mikhail Bakunin
      </a>
      . However, the discovery of the existence of class struggle is not the product of their theories; their theories can instead be seen as a response to the existence of class struggles.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Class conflict can take many different forms: direct violence, such as wars fought for resources and cheap labor; indirect violence, such as deaths from poverty, starvation, illness or unsafe working conditions; coercion, such as the threat of losing a job or pulling an important investment; or ideology, either intentionally (as with books and articles promoting
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       capitalism
      </a>
      ) or unintentionally (as with the promotion of
      <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">
       consumerism
      </a>
      through
      <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">
       advertising
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Additionally, political forms of class conflict exist; legally or illegally lobbying or bribing government leaders for passage of partisan desirable legislation including labor laws, tax codes, consumer laws, acts of congress or other sanction, injunction or tariff. The conflict can be open, as with a
      <a href="/wiki/Lockout_(industry)" title="Lockout (industry)">
       lockout
      </a>
      aimed at destroying a
      <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">
       labor union
      </a>
      , or hidden, as with an informal slowdown in production protesting low wages or
      <a href="/wiki/Unfair_labor_practice" title="Unfair labor practice">
       unfair labor practices
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
       Usage
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Battle_strike_1934.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1106" data-file-width="1386" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Battle_strike_1934.jpg/300px-Battle_strike_1934.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Battle_strike_1934.jpg/450px-Battle_strike_1934.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Battle_strike_1934.jpg/600px-Battle_strike_1934.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Battle_strike_1934.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis_Teamsters_Strike_of_1934" title="Minneapolis Teamsters Strike of 1934">
         Teamsters wild-cat strike in Minneapolis, 1934
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the past the term
      <i>
       Class conflict
      </i>
      was a term used mostly by
      <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">
       socialists
      </a>
      , who define a class by its relationship to the
      <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">
       means of production
      </a>
       such as factories, land and machinery. From this point of view, the social control of production and labor is a contest between classes, and the division of these resources necessarily involves conflict and inflicts harm. It can involve ongoing low-level clashes, escalate into massive confrontations, and in some cases, lead to the overall defeat of one of the contending classes. However, in more contemporary times this term is striking chords and finding new definition amongst capitalistic societies in the United States and other Westernized countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      The anarchist
      <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin" title="Mikhail Bakunin">
       Mikhail Bakunin
      </a>
      argued that the class struggle of the working class, peasantry and poor had the potential to lead to a social revolution involving the overthrow of ruling elites, and the creation of
      <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_socialism" title="Libertarian socialism">
       libertarian socialism
      </a>
      . This was only a potential, and class struggle was, he argued, not always the only or decisive factor in society, but it was central. By contrast,
      <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">
       Marxists
      </a>
      argue that class conflict always plays the decisive and pivotal role in the history of class-based hierarchical systems such as
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       capitalism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">
       feudalism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Marxists refer to its overt manifestations as class war, a struggle whose resolution in favor of the
      <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">
       working class
      </a>
      is viewed by them as inevitable under plutocratic
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       capitalism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-capitalist_societies">
       Pre-capitalist societies
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Where societies are socially divided based on status, wealth, or control of social production and distribution, conflict arises. This conflict is both everyday, such as the common medieval insistence on the right of lords to control access to grain mills and baking ovens, or it can be exceptional such as the Roman
      <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_the_Orders" title="Conflict of the Orders">
       Conflict of the Orders
      </a>
      , the uprising of
      <a href="/wiki/Spartacus" title="Spartacus">
       Spartacus
      </a>
      , or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular_revolt_in_late_medieval_Europe" title="Popular revolt in late medieval Europe">
       the various popular uprisings in late medieval Europe
      </a>
      . One of the earliest analysis of these conflicts is
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">
       Friedrich Engels
      </a>
      '
      <a href="/wiki/The_Peasant_War_in_Germany" title="The Peasant War in Germany">
       The Peasant War in Germany
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of the earliest analyses of the development of class as the development of conflicts between emergent classes is available in
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">
       Peter Kropotkin
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_Aid:_A_Factor_of_Evolution" title="Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution">
        Mutual Aid
       </a>
      </i>
      . In this work, Kropotkin analyzes the disposal of goods after death in pre-class or
      <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">
       hunter-gatherer
      </a>
      societies, and how inheritance produces early class divisions and conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century_USA">
       21st century USA
      </span>
     </h3>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Capitalist_societies">
       Capitalist societies
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The typical example of class conflict described is class conflict within capitalism. This class conflict is seen to occur primarily between the
      <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">
       bourgeoisie
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">
       proletariat
      </a>
      , and takes the form of conflict over hours of work, value of wages, division of profits, cost of consumer goods, the culture at work, control over
      <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">
       parliament
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">
       bureaucracy
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">
       economic inequality
      </a>
      . The particular implementation of government programs which may seem purely humanitarian, such as disaster relief, can actually be a form of class conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the USA class conflict is often noted in labor/management disputes. As far back as 1933 representative Edward Hamilton of ALPA, the
      <a href="/wiki/Air_Line_Pilots_Association,_International" title="Air Line Pilots Association, International">
       Airline Pilot's Association
      </a>
      , used the term "class warfare" to describe airline management's opposition at the National Labor Board hearings in October of that year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Apart from these day-to-day forms of class conflict, during periods of crisis or revolution class conflict takes on a violent nature and involves repression, assault, restriction of civil liberties, and murderous violence such as assassinations or death squads. (Zinn,
      <i>
       People's History
      </i>
      )
     </p>

Cherish therefore the spirit of our people, and keep alive their attention. Do not be too severe upon their errors, but reclaim them by enlightening them. If once they become inattentive to the public affairs, you &amp; I, &amp; Congress &amp; Assemblies, judges &amp; governors shall all become wolves. It seems to be the law of our general nature, in spite of individual exceptions; and experience declares that man is the only animal which devours his own kind, for I can apply no milder term to the governments of Europe, and to the general prey of the rich on the poor.

        <span>

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Weber does recognize that there is a relationship between social status, social prestige and classes.

        </span>

      </sup>
      that controls the means of production. This ruling class is viewed to be in opposition to the remainder of society, generally considered the
      <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">
       proletariat
      </a>
      . This type of system is referred to by its detractors as
      <a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">

      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/State_socialism" title="State socialism">
       state socialism
      </a>

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/New_class" title="New class">

      </a>
      societies. (Cliff; ilas 1957) Marxism was such a predominate ideological power in what became the Soviet Union since a Marxist group known as the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party was formed in the country, prior to 1917. This party soon divided into two main factions; the Bolsheviks, who were led by
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marxist_perspectives">
       Marxist perspectives
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Karl_Marx_001.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="910" data-file-width="639" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karl_Marx_001.jpg/175px-Karl_Marx_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karl_Marx_001.jpg/263px-Karl_Marx_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karl_Marx_001.jpg/350px-Karl_Marx_001.jpg 2x" width="175"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Karl_Marx_001.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Karl Marx, 1875
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
       Karl Marx
      </a>
      (18181883) was a German born philosopher who lived the majority of his adult life in London, England. In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Communist_Manifesto" title="The Communist Manifesto">
        The Communist Manifesto
       </a>
      </i>
      , Karl Marx argued that a
      <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">
       class
      </a>
      is formed when its members achieve class consciousness and solidarity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackwell_18-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blackwell-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This largely happens when the members of a class become aware of their
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploitation" title="Exploitation">
       exploitation
      </a>
      and the conflict with another class. A class will then realize their shared interests and a common identity. According to Marx, a class will then take action against those that are exploiting the lower classes.
     </p>
     <p>
      What Marx points out is that members of each of the two main classes have interests in common. These class or collective interests are in conflict with those of the other class as a whole. This in turn leads to conflict between individual members of different classes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Marxist analysis of society identifies two main social groups:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Labour
       </i>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">
        proletariat
       </a>
       or workers) includes anyone who earns their livelihood by selling their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_power" title="Labor power">
        labor power
       </a>
       and being paid a wage or salary for their labor time. They have little choice but to work for capital, since they typically have no independent way to survive.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Capital
       </i>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">
        bourgeoisie
       </a>
       or capitalists) includes anyone who gets their income not from labor as much as from the
       <a href="/wiki/Surplus_value" title="Surplus value">
        surplus value
       </a>
       they appropriate from the workers who create wealth. The income of the capitalists, therefore, is based on their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploitation_(Marxism)" title="Exploitation (Marxism)">
        exploitation
       </a>
       of the workers (proletariat).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Not all class struggle is violent or necessarily radical, as with strikes and lockouts. Class antagonism may instead be expressed as low worker morale, minor sabotage and pilferage, and individual workers' abuse of petty authority and hoarding of information. It may also be expressed on a larger scale by support for socialist or populist parties. On the employers' side, the use of union busting legal firms and the lobbying for anti-union laws are forms of class struggle.
     </p>
     <p>
      Not all class struggle is a threat to capitalism, or even to the authority of an individual capitalist. A narrow struggle for higher wages by a small sector of the working-class, what is often called "economism", hardly threatens the status quo. In fact, by applying the craft-union tactics of excluding other workers from skilled trades, an economistic struggle may even weaken the working class as a whole by dividing it. Class struggle becomes more important in the historical process as it becomes more general, as industries are organized rather than crafts, as workers' class consciousness rises, and as they self-organize away from political parties. Marx referred to this as the progress of the proletariat from being a class "in itself", a position in the social structure, to being one "for itself",an active and conscious force that could change the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      Marx largely focuses on the capital industrialist society as the source of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">
       social stratification
      </a>
      , which ultimately results in class conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackwell_18-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blackwell-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He states that capitalism creates a division between classes which can largely be seen in manufacturing factories. The proletariat, is separated from the bourgeoisie because production becomes a social enterprise. Contributing to their separation is the technology that is in factories. Technology de-skills and alienates workers as they are no longer viewed as having a specialized skill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackwell_18-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blackwell-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another effect of technology is a homogenous
      <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">
       workforce
      </a>
      that can be easily replaceable. Marx believed that this class conflict would result in the overthrow of the bourgeoisie and that the private property would be communally owned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackwell_18-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blackwell-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The mode of
      <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">
       production
      </a>
      would remain, but communal ownership would eliminate class conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackwell_18-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blackwell-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Even after a revolution, the two classes would struggle, but eventually the struggle would recede and the classes dissolve. As class boundaries broke down, the state apparatus would wither away. According to Marx, the main task of any state apparatus is to uphold the power of the ruling class; but without any classes there would be no need for a state. That would lead to the classless, stateless communist society.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Marxist_perspectives">

      </span>
     </h2>

        </a>

     <p>
      Social commentators, historians and socialist theorists had commented on class struggle for some time before Marx, as well as the connection between class struggle, property, and law:
      <a href="/wiki/Augustin_Thierry" title="Augustin Thierry">
       Augustin Thierry
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Guizot" title="Franois Guizot">
       Franois Guizot
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Auguste_Mignet" title="Franois-Auguste Mignet">
       Franois-Auguste Mignet
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Thiers" title="Adolphe Thiers">
       Adolphe Thiers
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiocrat" title="Physiocrat">
       Physiocrats
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">
       David Ricardo
      </a>
      , and after Marx,
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_George" title="Henry George">
       Henry George
      </a>
      noted the inelastic supply of land and argued that this created certain privileges (
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_rent" title="Economic rent">
       economic rent
      </a>
      ) for landowners. According to the historian
      <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Toynbee" title="Arnold Toynbee">
       Arnold Toynbee
      </a>
      , stratification along lines of class appears only within civilizations, and furthermore only appears during the process of a civilization's decline while not characterizing the growth phase of a civilization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Proudhon, in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/What_is_Property%3F" title="What is Property?">
        What is Property?
       </a>
      </i>
      (1840) states that "certain classes do not relish investigation into the pretended titles to property, and its fabulous and perhaps scandalous history."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While Proudhon saw the solution as the lower classes forming an alternative, solidarity economy centered on cooperatives and self-managed workplaces, which would slowly undermine and replace capitalist class society, the anarchist
      <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin" title="Mikhail Bakunin">
       Mikhail Bakunin
      </a>
      while influenced by Proudhon, insisted that a massive class struggle, by the working class, peasantry and poor, was essential to the creation of
      <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_socialism" title="Libertarian socialism">
       libertarian socialism
      </a>
      . This would require a (final) showdown in the form of a social revolution.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fascists" title="Fascists">
       Fascists
      </a>
      have often opposed class struggle and instead have attempted to appeal to the working class while promising to preserve the existing social classes and have proposed an alternative concept known as
      <a href="/wiki/Class_collaboration" title="Class collaboration">
       class collaboration
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Class_vs._race_struggle">
       Class vs. race struggle
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NORTH_PHILADELPHIA_JOBLESS_BLACKS._MAN_STANDING_AT_RIGHT_IS_GERALD_%22HEAT_WAVE%22_JONES,_WHO_WORKS_FOR_%22THE_NETWORK%22,_A..._-_NARA_-_552754.tif">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NORTH_PHILADELPHIA_JOBLESS_BLACKS._MAN_STANDING_AT_RIGHT_IS_GERALD_%22HEAT_WAVE%22_JONES,_WHO_WORKS_FOR_%22THE_NETWORK%22,_A..._-_NARA_-_552754.tif" title="Enlarge">

     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">
       Michel Foucault
      </a>
      , in the 19th century the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essentialist" title="Essentialist">
       essentialist
      </a>
      notion of the "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">
       race
      </a>
      " was incorporated by
      <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">
       racists
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biologists" title="Biologists">
       biologists
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugenicists" title="Eugenicists">
       eugenicists
      </a>
      , who gave it the modern sense of "biological race" which was then integrated to "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_racism" title="State racism">
       state racism
      </a>
      ". On the other hand, Foucault claims that when
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxists" title="Marxists">
       Marxists
      </a>
      developed their concept of "class struggle", they were partly inspired by the older, non-biological notions of the "race" and the "race struggle". In a letter to Friedrich Engels in 1882 Karl Marx wrote:
      <i>
       You know very well where we found our idea of class struggle; we found it in the work of the French historians who talked about the race struggle.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For Foucault, the theme of social war provides the overriding principle that connects class and race struggle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Moses_Hess" title="Moses Hess">
       Moses Hess
      </a>
      , an important theoretician of the early socialist movement, in his "Epilogue" to "
      <a href="/wiki/Rome_and_Jerusalem" title="Rome and Jerusalem">
       Rome and Jerusalem
      </a>
      " argued that "the race struggle is primary, the class struggle secondary... With the cessation of race
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antagonism_(philosophy)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antagonism (philosophy) (page does not exist)">
       antagonism
      </a>
      , the class struggle will also come to a standstill. The equalization of all classes of society will necessarily follow the
      <a href="/wiki/Emancipation" title="Emancipation">
       emancipation
      </a>
      of all the races, for it will ultimately become a scientific question of social economics."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In modern times, emerging schools of thought in the U.S. and other countries hold the opposite to be true.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They argue that the race struggle is less important, because the primary struggle is that of class since labor of all races face the same problems and injustices.
     </p>
