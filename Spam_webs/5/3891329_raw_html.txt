           <p>
            An exoskeleton developed with a grant from the US military has
been trialed by paraplegic "test pilots" in the UK, allowing people
who are wheelchair-bound to walk.
           </p>
           <p>
            A new centre has opened at the private healthcare clinic,
            <a href="http://www.technologyinmotion.com/home">
             Technology in
Motion
            </a>
            , in Leeds and is the first in Europe where spinal cord
injury patients can use the Ekso from California-based
            <a href="http://www.eksobionics.com">
             Ekso Bionics
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.fastcompany.com/magazine/164/ekso-bionics">
             Ekso
Bionics
            </a>
            , formerly called
            <a href="http://www.eksobionics.com/blog/2011/11/2884/">
             Berkeley
Bionics
            </a>
            , was founded in 2005 and unveiled the eLegs, as the
Ekso wearable robot is also known, in 2010. They were
            <a href="/news/archive/2010-10/08/bionic-legs-paraplegics">
             number
two
            </a>
            in Wired US's "Top Ten Gadgets" last year.
           </p>
           <div class="incontentAd" data-ad-placeholder-state="not-rendered" id="incontentAd_first">
           </div>
           <p>
            The Ekso is a battery-powered, ready-to-wear exoskeleton that is
worn over clothes. It supports the patient's body and enables
"unprecedented knee flexion", says the company, "delivering the
most natural human gait available in any exoskeleton today".
           </p>
           <p>
            In February this year, the company delivered the first
commercial unit of the exoskeleton to
            <a href="http://medgadget.com/2012/03/ekso-bionics-sells-its-first-commercial-exoskeleton.html">
             Craig Hospital in Denver
            </a>
            . It has also licensed its technology
to
            <a href="http://www.wired.com/video/lockheed-martin-hulc-exoskeleton/115599816001">
             Lockheed Martin
            </a>
            for military use. The company's European
managing director, Andy Hayes, told Wired.co.uk that the volume of
enquiries from Europe led the company to the decision to open test
centres here; and the two planned could quickly be joined by more
in other European countries.
           </p>

           <p>
            The robot has been tested by three users in the UK, among whom
was Suzanne Edwards who told Wired.co.uk that she thought standing
up wearing the device "would be a lot more difficult than it was"
and "after a few steps, it just felt natural". She has now had
eight sessions using the robot and will be joined by others who
meet the criteria. They must be between 1.5m and 1.9m tall, weigh
100kg or less, and have "at least partial upper body strength and
good range of motion".
           </p>
           <p>
            Steve Mottram from Technology in Motion adds that people will
pay 350 for the initial assessment, then 200 for each session
using the exoskeleton if they are deemed suitable. This is not
available on
            <a href="/news/archive/2011-12/01/nationalfield-moves-from-obama-to-the-nhs">
             the NHS
            </a>
            as yet, as says Hayes, it seems to be something the
health service is "sceptical" of.
           </p>
           <p>
            However, two of Ekso Bionics' seven physiotherapists had to be
on hand at all times during the sessions and this is something Ekso
is looking to change in the future, says Hayes. The company is
working on a lighter, cheaper device, he says, that could be used
by patients in their own homes but this, again, will have to be
under supervision. This could arrive in the next 18 months to two
years. The issue, however, is how to prevent people falling over.
This is something that Ekso Bionics is teaming up with academic
institutions to tackle and has already visited four universities in
the UK. The ultimate hope though is for an exoskeleton that people
can use entirely by themselves.
           </p>

           <p>
            In the meanwhile, Ekso is running a pan-European study into the
physiological as well as psychological benefits of using the
exoskeleton. These effects will include improved bladder and bowel
function, increased muscle length and improved circulation. Nine
spinal injury clinics are involved. "We want to enhance
independence and freedom of movement, but we are committed to
working with our clinical partners to ensure this technology is
brought to users safely and ethically," says Hayes, "and with Ekso,
we are starting to see that future."
           </p>
