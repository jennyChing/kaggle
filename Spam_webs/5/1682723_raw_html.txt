              To Counter Effects of Online Competition, Cable TV Networks Are Speeding Up Shows To Squeeze in More Ads
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header">
              <div class="post-header-line-1">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2065450513665289697">
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-I-qv6jOnSBM/VOiAkPvmWOI/AAAAAAAAaRA/eZZ5P6jepwE/s1600/Image%2B-%2Blemon.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-I-qv6jOnSBM/VOiAkPvmWOI/AAAAAAAAaRA/eZZ5P6jepwE/s1600/Image%2B-%2Blemon.jpg" width="212"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              It's not your imagination. If you think voices of the actors are sounding squeakier
              <i>
               ,
              </i>
              your ears are not to blame. Neither is your television or computer.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Cable TV companies are using compression technology to increase the speed at which some shows and movies are being in shown in order to squeeze in more advertisements.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              They are fighting competition from digital platforms, including streaming services like Netflix and Amazon. The consumer pivot to the computer or smartphone has caused a drop in cable viewership and a resulting decline in advertising income.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              The cable companies thought they were being clever by making it up on volume, but, as the following article explains, they may actually be doing themselves even more damage.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Advertisers are concerned that packing ever more spots into traditional time slots will annoy viewers and drive even more of them to seek alternatives. They are also concerned that the impact of their ads will be negatively affected by increasing the number of them.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              As if that weren't enough, the studios that produce the shows and movies being adulterated believe it reduces the quality of the experience and maybe harming both the shows' ratings and their actors' reputations.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Desperate times call for desperate measures, but not all technological solutions produce the desired results. JL
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <i>
               Joe Flint reports in the Wall Street Journal
              </i>
              :
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <blockquote>
               Cable-TV networks use the more densely packed ads to counterbalance 
sinking viewership and a stagnant ad market. But the growing ad 
clutter is (causing) worry that oversaturation 
of commercials will reduce the effectiveness of spots and drive viewers to commercial free 
streaming services such as
               <a class="t-company" href="http://quotes.wsj.com/NFLX">
                Netflix
               </a>
               and Amazon.
               <br/>
               <a name="more">
               </a>
               When         Stephen Cox       was watching The Wizard of Oz on TBS last November, something didnt sound quite right to him about the Munchkins, who are near and dear to his heart.
               <br/>
               Their voices were raised a notch, said Mr. Cox, the author of several pop- culture books including one about the classic 1939 film. It was astounding to me.
               <br/>
               He wasnt imagining things.
               <a class="t-company" href="http://quotes.wsj.com/TWX">
                Time Warner
               </a>
               Inc.       s TBS used compression technology to speed up the movie. The purpose: stuffing in more TV commercials.
               <br/>
               As they contend with steep ratings declines, many top cable networks are jamming more ads into programming to meet audience guarantees made to advertisers and prop up revenue despite falling ad prices.Tinkering with shows to squeeze more advertising dollars out of them has been done before. Cable networks have long made room for ads by shortening the opening credits. Reruns of Law &amp; Order on TNT have a 24-second opening, in contrast to the original 1 minute, 45-second opening when it aired on NBC.
               <br/>
               <div class="hide4 textAd bodyRealtorAd realtorAdLong">
                <div class="realtorAd">
                 <div class="adSpec">
                  <span>
                   <br/>
                  </span>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
               But speeding up the actual content is a more subtle tactic TV networks use to achieve a higher volume of ads. TBS also has sped up sitcom reruns of Seinfeld and other shows, and sister network TNT has also employed the approach as well.
               <a class="t-company" href="http://quotes.wsj.com/VIAB">
                Viacom
               </a>
               Inc.       s TV Land has done the same with Friends reruns.
               <br/>
               It doesnt look like The Keystone Kops, but you can tell by the voices, said Mr. Cox of the tampered shows.
               <br/>
               Cable-TV networks use the more densely packed ads to counterbalance sinking viewership and a stagnant cable ad market. But the growing ad clutter is rubbing advertisers, content owners and Hollywoods creative community the wrong way.
               <br/>
               Marketers worry that an oversaturation of commercials will reduce the effectiveness of their spots and drive more viewers away from watching traditional TV to commercial-free streaming services such as
               <a class="t-company" href="http://quotes.wsj.com/NFLX">
                Netflix
               </a>
               and Amazon.
               <br/>
               It is important for us to consider the effect this is having on the viewer experience, said         Jackie Kulesza,       executive vice president and director of video at Starcom USA. We want to ensure our message is seen by receptive viewers.
               <br/>
               Commercial clutter increased for many cable channels in December, according to a Nielsen analysis of prime time. A&amp;E Network, which is co-owned by
               <a class="t-company" href="http://quotes.wsj.com/DIS">
                Walt Disney
               </a>
               Co.       and Hearst Corp., averaged 18 minutes and 39 seconds of commercial time per-hour in prime time, up almost three minutes from December 2013. It sister channel History had 18 minutes and 42 seconds of ad time, up two minutes from the previous year.
               <br/>
               <div class=" media-object inline " data-layout="inline">
                <div class="media-object-image enlarge-image renoImageFormat-9U img-inline" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
                 <div class="image-container responsive-media loaded" data-layout-ratio="102.8571%" data-mobile-ratio="102.8571%" style="padding-bottom: 102.85%;">
                  <img alt="" data-enlarge="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/MK-CS857_CLUTTE_16U_20150218173613.jpg" data-in-at4units-src="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/MK-CS857_CLUTTE_9U_20150218173613.jpg" data-in-base-src="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/MK-CS857_CLUTTE_9U_20150218173613.jpg" data-intent="" height="400" src="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/MK-CS857_CLUTTE_9U_20150218173613.jpg" title="" width="388"/>
                  <span class="image-enlarge">
                  </span>
                  <span class="image-enlarge">
                  </span>
                  <div class="wsj-article-caption" itemprop="caption">
                   <span class="wsj-article-caption-content">
                   </span>
                   <span class="wsj-article-credit" itemprop="creator">
                    <span class="wsj-article-credit-tag">
                    </span>
                   </span>
                  </div>
                  Other networks that added commercial time include TBS and
                  <a class="t-company" href="http://quotes.wsj.com/DISCA">
                   Discovery Communications
                  </a>
                  Inc.       s Discovery Channel and TLC. Many Viacom networks including
 MTV, TV Land and Spike all have over 20 minutes of non-programming 
content an hour.
                  <br/>
                  Cable networks havent commented publicly on the
 increased ad volume in recent months. But executives at several 
companies privately acknowledged that rating declines were the cause.
                  <br/>
                  It
 is a way to keep the revenue from going down as much as the ratings, 
said a top executive at one major cable programmer. The only way we can
 do it is to double down and stretch the unit load a little more.
                  <br/>
                  Critics say the practice is misguided.
                  <br/>
                  They
 are trying to deal with a problem in a way that is making the problem 
bigger, said         Chris Geraci,       president of national 
broadcast at media buyer Omnicom Media Group of the practice of 
increasing the commercial loads to make up for declining ratings.
                  <br/>
                  Broadcast
 networks have boosted their average commercial time per-hour as well 
but the increases arent nearly as large. Broadcast networks and local 
TV stations also typically have less commercial clutter than cable.
                  <br/>
                  Most
 of the ad clutter is in reruns and movies that are a large part of a 
typical cable networks schedule. The studios that sell the reruns are 
concerned that the glut of commercials and the lower ratings that seem 
to follow will decrease the value of their shows down the road.
                  <br/>
                  It
 has gotten completely out of control, said the distribution head of 
one major television studio. Im concerned when you look at the 
performance being diminished and hurt by their running the shows that 
way.Some studio executives say shortening TV shows through 
compression (speeding them up) could run afoul of contracts between 
networks and studios. They are not allowed to do anything to the 
content. They have to run it in the way it is delivered, the 
distribution chief said.
                  <br/>
                  A senior executive at one major cable 
programmer said the speeding up of shows, which is done by removing 
repetitive video frames, is usually a last resort. However, the 
practice, which has gone on for decades, seems to have become more 
prevalent in the past few years.
                  <br/>
                  Friends co-creator         
Marta Kauffman,       already upset at how opening and closing credits 
are squashed, doesnt like networks tinkering with the running time of
 shows to add more commercials and compares it to the colorization of 
black and white movies.
                  <br/>
                  It feels wrong, she said. It is not how
 it was shot, written or imagined. It wasnt meant to be 
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </blockquote>
