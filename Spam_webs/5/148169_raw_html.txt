      <h1 class="entry-title">
       <a href="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/solar-trends/how-to-calculate-the-30-federal-solar-investment-tax-credit-itc/" rel="bookmark">
        How to Calculate the 30% Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)
       </a>
      </h1>
     </div>
     <div class="byline">
      by Dave Llorens
     </div>
     <div class="entry-content">
      <p class="no-break">
       <span class="st_email_large" st_title="How to Calculate the 30% Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)" st_url="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/solar-trends/how-to-calculate-the-30-federal-solar-investment-tax-credit-itc/">
       </span>
       <span class="st_facebook_large" st_title="How to Calculate the 30% Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)" st_url="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/solar-trends/how-to-calculate-the-30-federal-solar-investment-tax-credit-itc/">
       </span>
       <span class="st_googleplus_large" st_title="How to Calculate the 30% Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)" st_url="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/solar-trends/how-to-calculate-the-30-federal-solar-investment-tax-credit-itc/">
       </span>
       <span class="st_twitter_large" st_title="How to Calculate the 30% Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)" st_url="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/solar-trends/how-to-calculate-the-30-federal-solar-investment-tax-credit-itc/" st_username="solarpowerrocks">
       </span>
       <span class="st_linkedin_large" st_title="How to Calculate the 30% Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)" st_url="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/solar-trends/how-to-calculate-the-30-federal-solar-investment-tax-credit-itc/">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: center">
       <img alt="MonopTaxAnnCleaver2000 " class="size-full wp-image-2259 aligncenter" height="220" src="http://solarpowerrocks.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/12/MonopTaxAnnCleaver2000.jpg" width="301"/>
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: center">
       Photo:Flickr/ AnnCleaver 2000
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Looking for the most up-to-date information about claiming the Federal Investment Tax Credit for solar?
        <a href="http://www.solarpowerrocks.com/affordable-solar/how-to-claim-the-itc/">
         Head on over to our newest post about the subject
        </a>
        (The information below may be out of date).
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       The end of the year brings the thought of the Spring yet to come. And with that Spring comes the taxes yet to pay.
      </p>
      <p>
       Me thinks this is why weve been getting a lot of emails about the 30% Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) for solar. Some claim that one should calculate the 30% tax credit
       <em>
        before
       </em>
       any state or utility rebates; i.e., the gross installed cost. Others claim one should calculate the 30%
       <em>
        after
       </em>
       deducting state rebates; i.e., the net cost paid to your installer, who typically collects the rebate on your behalf. Which is right?
      </p>
      <p>
       Before I answer that excellent question, please read the following 3 important caveats:
      </p>
      <p>
       1) We are solar geeks, not tax geeks. Please, Lord, we pray that ye will consult thy personal tax geek before following our unprofessional tax guidance. Amen.
      </p>
      <p>
       2) As of 12-15-09, the IRS and its tax lackeys have yet to write clear guidance on this issue. Instead, they have left it up to interpretation, thus far. This may be their idea of tax accounting sh$ts &amp; giggles. We are not amused (but please dont audit us.) Nevertheless, as noted below, we have consulted an authority on the subject.
      </p>
      <p>
       3) Please Re-Read #1 and #2. You have been warned. Cross your fingers and read on.
      </p>
      <p>
       So, which is right? The short answer is that both are right, but one method applies to residential solar (homes) and the other applies to commercial solar (businesses) but with a catch.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="text-decoration: underline">
        For Residents:
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Calculate your 30% Federal tax credit
        <em>
         after
        </em>
        any state or utility rebates. Thats how we do it here on SolarPowerRocks.com. In other words:
       </li>
       <li>
        If the cost of your solar power system was $35,000 before any rebate, and you receive $15,000 in state rebate money, then we suggest you calculate the 30% off $20,000.
       </li>
       <li>
        Thus, your tax credit would be $6,000, bringing your net system cost to $14,000.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Solar Fred Important Note #1:
        </strong>
        This $6,000 tax
        <em>
         credit
        </em>
        is not refundable. So, if you owe $3,000 in taxes for 2010 to the IRS, youll pay no taxes on April 15th, but Uncle Sam wont be sending you a $3,000 refund check. Instead, you must apply this to next years taxes.or the next.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Solar Fred Important Note #2:
        </strong>
        If you get your solar system through a solar lease or a solar PPA, you DO NOT get the 30% tax credit or any
        <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/affordable-solar/solar-fred-info-recs-green-tags-oh-my/" target="_blank">
         REC payments
        </a>
        , if applicable. The
        <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/los-angeles-solar/solar-ppa-vs-lease-solar/">
         solar leasing or solar PPA company
        </a>
        gets it. Too much to go into now, but this is why buying is better in the long run. That reminds me to shamelessly plug the fact that
        <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/see-your-solar-savings/" target="_blank">
         you can get a free solar quote now

        from one of our local partner installers to see if solar makes financial sense for youor not. Moving on.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <span style="text-decoration: underline">
        For Commercial Solar Installations:
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Feel free to calculate your 30% Federal ITC tax credit
        <em>
         before
        </em>
        any state or utility rebates. But theres a catch.
       </li>
       <li>
        The catch is that Uncle Sam sees you as a profit making venture. Consequently, if you get a rebate of $15,000 rebate from the utility, thats seen as income/revenue, and therefore, theyre going to tax that $15,000.
       </li>
       <li>
        So, in the same example, you can subtract $15,000 off your $35,000 gross cost, and also apply another $10,500 (30% x $35,000) to your quarterly tax bill, making your pre-taxed net cost $9,500.
       </li>
       <li>
        However, that $15,000
        <em>
         will be taxed as income
        </em>
        , and thus.could take away any gain over the residential net benefit. Bummer.
        <strong>
        </strong>
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Solar Fred Important Note (again):
        </strong>
        If you finance your solar system through a solar lease or a solar PPA, you DO NOT get the 30% tax credit or any
        <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/green-tags/solar-fred-info-recs-green-tags-oh-my/" target="_blank">
         REC payments
        </a>
        , if applicable. As with residential, the
        <a href="http://solarpowerrocks.com/los-angeles-solar/solar-ppa-vs-lease-solar/">
         solar leasing or solar PPA company
        </a>
        gets it. Once, again too much to go into now, but this is another opportunity to shamelessly plug the fact that

        <a href="http://1bog.org/solar-companies/" target="_blank" title="One project that finds solar companies - 1BOG's RFP Process">
         Solar companies
        </a>
        and developers who are creating solar products or large industrial installations for can apply for 30% grant. Yes, cash. This is part of the stimulus package, but it doesnt apply to non-solar businesses. Bummer again.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Solar Fred Important Note #2

          <p>
           Bob,
           <br/>
           RECs (Renewable Energy Credits) are not the same thing as rebates. The rebates in question are the ones that a person gets from their local and/or state government and/or their electric utility company to help decrease the initial cost of installing a system and as an incentive to install a system.
          </p>
          <p>
           An SREC (Solar Renewable Energy Credit, or Solar REC) is a credit that represents 1MWH of electricity created from a PV system. Rebates are to decrease the initial cost of installation and RECs are for production of electricity.
          </p>
          <p>
           Go to the home page of this website, click on a state and look for the section on performance payments or SRECs. Who actually owns the RECs will depend on your state laws and possibly your power company. In Florida (where I live) the RECs, by law, are the property of the system owner. From researching RECs when discussing it with a coworker from New York, I can tell you that if you have net metering with Long Island Power Authority, the RECs become the property of the power company. In some states the RECs, if you own them, can be sold for a significant amount. In Florida, however, trying to find a way to sell them is difficult and the amount you can get for them is minimal. I just wish I could sell mine in New Jersey at the going rate of around $693 per SREC. The most Ive heard about in Florida is about $30 per SREC.
          </p>
          <p>
           Back on the subject of taxes, Im not a tax expert or anything, but it would seem to me that IF a person has the choice of how to calculate the rebate, the best way would depend on your tax bracket. If you paid $30k for a system and got a $15k rebate, taking the credit after subtracting the rebate would give you a tax credit of $4500 making the system cost $10,500. ($30k-$15K=$15k, 70% of $15k= $10,500) IF you can get the credit on the entire initial cost, however, you would get a credit of $9000. You would then have to pay taxes on the $15K rebate. The only way that this wouldnt be better would be if you were in the 30% percent tax bracket or higher. If you were in a 20% tax bracket, for example, you would pay $3000 in tax on that $15k and end up with $12k of the rebate (80% of the $15k) after subtracting the tax, making the system cost $9k and leaving you  $1500 better off overall. Thats assuming that you can legally do that. (70% of $30k=$21k, 80% of $15k=12k, $21k-12K=$9K)
          </p>
