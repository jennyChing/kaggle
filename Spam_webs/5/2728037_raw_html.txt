&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Flip Flap Railway&lt;/b&gt; was the name of a &lt;a href="/wiki/Vertical_loop" title="Vertical loop"&gt;looping&lt;/a&gt; &lt;a href="/wiki/Wooden_roller_coaster" title="Wooden roller coaster"&gt;wooden&lt;/a&gt; &lt;a href="/wiki/Roller_coaster" title="Roller coaster"&gt;roller coaster&lt;/a&gt; which operated for a number of years at &lt;a href="/wiki/Paul_Boyton" title="Paul Boyton"&gt;Paul Boyton's&lt;/a&gt; &lt;a href="/wiki/Sea_Lion_Park" title="Sea Lion Park"&gt;Sea Lion Park&lt;/a&gt; on &lt;a href="/wiki/Coney_Island" title="Coney Island"&gt;Coney Island&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York"&gt;New York&lt;/a&gt;. The coaster, which opened in 1895, was notable for being the first looping roller coaster to operate in North America. It was also notable for its engineering as well as the extreme &lt;a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force"&gt;G-forces&lt;/a&gt; that this engineering inflicted on riders.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="History"&gt;History&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Flip_Flap_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The first looping roller coasters were built in Europe in the mid-1800s. These rides, known as "&lt;a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_railway" title="Centrifugal railway"&gt;centrifugal railways&lt;/a&gt;", were initially designed to be temporary installations and they achieved little success.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Scream_Machine_1-0" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Scream_Machine-1"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;1&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The Flip Flap Railway was tested in &lt;a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio"&gt;Toledo&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio"&gt;Ohio&lt;/a&gt; in 1888 by designer Lina Beecher before the coaster was moved to Coney Island.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-The_Golden_Age_of_Roller_Coasters_2-0" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-The_Golden_Age_of_Roller_Coasters-2"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;2&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt; The coaster was tested with sand bags and monkeys before human riders were allowed on the coaster.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_Roller_Coaster_3-0" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Bennett_Roller_Coaster-3"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;3&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt; Sea Lion Park developer Boyton liked Beecher's coaster and decided to move it to Sea Lion Park.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-The_Golden_Age_of_Roller_Coasters_2-1" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-The_Golden_Age_of_Roller_Coasters-2"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;2&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Flip Flap Railway was shut down along with Sea Lion Park in 1902. The Sea Lion Park was replaced with &lt;a href="/wiki/Luna_Park,_Coney_Island_(1903)" title="Luna Park, Coney Island (1903)"&gt;Luna Park&lt;/a&gt;, but the Flip Flap Railway was not retained, owing to its unpopularity.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-The_Golden_Age_of_Roller_Coasters_2-2" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-The_Golden_Age_of_Roller_Coasters-2"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;2&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt; The 2-person cars also meant that it was difficult to turn a profit on the coaster.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Roller_Coaster_Lover_4-0" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Roller_Coaster_Lover-4"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;4&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Ride_experience"&gt;Ride experience&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Flip_Flap_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ride experience"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Like the earlier centrifugal railways in Europe, Flip Flap Railway was notorious for the extreme G-forces that it produced in its riders. The circular nature of the coaster's loop, as well as its relatively small diameter of 25 feet,&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_Roller_Coaster_3-1" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Bennett_Roller_Coaster-3"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;3&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt; meant that it could produce forces of approximately 12 Gs.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Physics_for_Scientists_and_Engineers_5-0" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Physics_for_Scientists_and_Engineers-5"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;5&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Mental_Floss_6-0" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Mental_Floss-6"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;6&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt; This caused riders to often experience discomfort and neck injuries from whiplash.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_Roller_Coaster_3-2" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Bennett_Roller_Coaster-3"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;3&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Mental_Floss_6-1" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Mental_Floss-6"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;6&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt; Modern looping roller coasters use teardrop-shaped loops to greatly reduce these G-forces. For this reason, Flip Flap Railway was the last coaster to use a truly circular loop.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-Physics_for_Scientists_and_Engineers_5-1" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-Physics_for_Scientists_and_Engineers-5"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;5&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
