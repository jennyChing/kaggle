       <h2 class="lead-in">
        Mandatory maternity leave for American moms lags far behind laws in other countries.
       </h2>
      </div>
      <div class="story-asset story-metadata-asset" id="module-position-OQ5C_W62AkU">
       <div class="article-metadata-wrap">

      <p>
       Now that the duchess of Cambridge has given birth to a
       <a href="/story/life/people/2013/07/24/royal-baby-first-night-kensington-palace/2581953/">
        baby boy
       </a>
       , she faces the same question all new mothers face: What next?
      </p>
      <p>
       About a month before the baby was born, the duchess went on leave to spend the
       <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2013/06/03/kate-middleton-maternity-leave_n_3378626.html">
        last few weeks
       </a>
       of her pregnancy free from official duties. It is a leave that might stretch for a year, and this isn't just because Kate Middleton is royalty. All women in Great Britain are guaranteed
       <a href="http://www.acas.org.uk/index.aspx?articleid=1753">
        52 weeks
       </a>
       of maternity leave,
       <a href="http://www.acas.org.uk/index.aspx?articleid=1753">
        39 of them
       </a>
       paid.
      </p>
      <p>
       Compare that with what new mothers in the USA can expect. The 1993
       <a href="http://www.dol.gov/whd/regs/statutes/fmla.htm">
        federal Family and Medical Leave Act
       </a>
       requires only that companies with
       <a href="http://www.dol.gov/dol/topic/benefits-leave/fmla.htm">
        50 or more employees
       </a>
       guarantee
       <a href="http://www.dol.gov/dol/topic/benefits-leave/fmla.htm">
        12 weeks
       </a>
       of unpaid leave for new mothers. Beyond that, how much leave time a new mother gets depends on what state she lives in and for whom she works.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Where do you live?
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Women living in states such as
       <a href="http://www.paidfamilyleave.org/learn/basics.html">
        California
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://lwd.dol.state.nj.us/labor/tdi/worker/state/sp_pregnancy.html">
        New Jersey
       </a>
       and
       <a href="http://thinkprogress.org/economy/2013/07/03/2251881/rhode-island-paid-family-leave-passage/" title="http://thinkprogress.org/economy/2013/07/03/2251881/rhode-island-paid-family-leave-passage/">
        most recently
       </a>
       <a href="http://www.growingfamilybenefits.com/ri_temporary_disability/">
        Rhode Island
       </a>
       can benefit from laws requiring paid leave for new mothers. Washington has also
       <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2011/02/23/paid-parental-leave_n_826996.html">
        passed
       </a>
       mandatory paid leave, but the state has yet to implement it.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you happen to live in any of the other 46 states, you are out of luck unless you work for a big corporation with a generous parental leave policy.
      </p>
      <p>
       When it comes to federally mandated maternity leave, the U.S. is sorely out of step with the rest of the world. A few years ago,
       <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2013/02/04/maternity-leave-paid-parental-leave-_n_2617284.html">
        Australia
       </a>
       passed a parental leave law, leaving the U.S. as the
       <a href="http://www.hreonline.com/pdfs/08012009Extra_McGillSurvey.pdf">
        only industrialized nation
       </a>
       not to mandate paid leave for new mothers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Paid maternity leave usually comes down to a question of cost. One estimate puts the cost at roughly
       <a href="http://kellymom.com/blog-post/cost-maternity-leave/">
        $800 per worker
       </a>
       a year for each U.S. worker.
      </p>
      <p>
       But if you consider how these programs are being funded in states such as California and New Jersey, the cost is much lower. In these states, funding is not borne by businesses in the form of taxes. They are "
       <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/work-in-progress/2012/05/23/3-reasons-why-card-carrying-capitalists-should-support-paid-family-leave/3/">
        income replacement insurance
       </a>
       " programs, similar to unemployment insurance, which employees pay for through a small deduction in their paychecks (approximately
       <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/work-in-progress/2012/05/23/3-reasons-why-card-carrying-capitalists-should-support-paid-family-leave/3/">
        $33 a year
       </a>
       in New Jersey and
       <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/work-in-progress/2012/05/23/3-reasons-why-card-carrying-capitalists-should-support-paid-family-leave/3/">
        $36 a year
       </a>
       in California).
      </p>
      <p>
       Whatever the cost, there are well-known benefits of paid leave not only for infants and parents but also the nation. A report on childhood development from the National Scientific Council on the Developing Child at Harvard University, for instance, found that early "parent-child bonding" is critical because it helps ensure good health, well-being and success into adulthood.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Benefits outweigh costs
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       One important example of a long-term health benefit is
       <a href="http://www.babycenter.com/0_how-breastfeeding-benefits-you-and-your-baby_8910.bc">
        breastfeeding
       </a>
       , which ensures a stronger immune system for babies and a decrease in mothers' chances of getting breast cancer.
      </p>
      <p>
       A 2012 report by
       <a href="http://www.savethechildren.org/atf/cf/%7B9def2ebe-10ae-432c-9bd0-df91d2eba74a%7D/STATEOFTHEWORLDSMOTHERSREPORT2012.PDF">
        Save the Children
       </a>
       finds
       <a href="http://kellymom.com/blog-post/cost-maternity-leave/">
        that
       </a>
       "the most effective way to improve breastfeeding rates is to provide longer periods of paid maternity leave." Paid maternity leave also benefits business by helping improve the odds that new mothers will return to the workforce.
      </p>
      <p>
       So while we continue watching the royal baby, it is also a good time to ask again: When will we join the rest of the world in passing a federal mandate requiring paid maternity or parental leave?
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Jeanne Zaino is a professor of Political Science &amp; International Studies at Iona College in New Rochelle, N.Y.
       </i>
