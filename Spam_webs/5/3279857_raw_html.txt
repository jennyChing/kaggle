       <div class="head_titles">
        <h1 class="entry-title" title="Had we known, that reality would have coloured our last months together with debilitating sadness and fear">
         In hindsight, the universe was sending messages about my husbands death
         <a class="addtowatchlist add awl-article" href="#" id="atwlArticleTitle" rel="" title="Add to ...">
          Add to ...
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <p class="byline author vcard">
          Gillian Cummings
         </p>
         <p class="creditline source-org vcard">
          Contributed to The Globe and Mail
         </p>
         <div class="dateline">
          <p>
           Published
           <time datetime="2015-59-02T18:04:43Z" pubdate="">
            Thursday, Apr. 02, 2015 2:59PM EDT
           </time>
          </p>
          <p>
           Last updated
           <time class="updated" datetime="2015-08-02T19:04:01Z" pubdate="">
            Thursday, Apr. 02, 2015 3:08PM EDT
           </time>
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </header>
      <div class="entry-content fontsmall simplesans">
       <!-- This is a catch-all ASF view; only displays when an unsupported article type is put in an ASF drop zone -->
       <div class="story_tools clearfix">

       </div>
       <div class="x140x460 clearfix">
        <div class="column-1 gridcol">

        <div class="column-2 gridcol">
         <p>
          It has been one year since my husband, Gord, yelped, twice, managed his last words  My heart is beating so fast!  lost his balance, sank to the kitchen floor and proceeded to die in my arms.
         </p>
         <p>
          He was a handsome 53. It is still too painful to recall the beautiful moments with him, the touchstones of our 38 years together. When my mind does go there it ends only in tears and shock shrouds me again.
         </p>
         <!-- This is a catch-all ASF view; only displays when an unsupported article type is put in an ASF drop zone -->
         <p>
          In the weeks after his death, with the unnerving benefit of hindsight, I began to see that the loss had been coming. We just didnt know it. Things had happened, seemingly unrelated events that were flagging some sort of break like subtle tremors before a quake.
         </p>
         <p>
          But what hindsight will spotlight as clear markers are, of course, cloaked as we experience them; out of context, unrecognizable as clues. Life is full of these hints that we miss.
         </p>
         <p>
          Hindsight tells me that when Gord stopped digging a bonfire pit on a Prince Edward Island beach, that was the first warning sign, seven months before he died. At the time, he simply planted his shovel, told me he couldnt do it any more and took a long, slow walk down the beach.
         </p>
         <p>
          Had we known then that his formerly flexible heart muscle was, thanks to sarcoidosis, chemically changing, actually hardening, would it have made a difference?
         </p>
         <p>
          When we travelled to New York in early January and my delicate diamond stud earrings disappeared from our hotel room, the ones he had given me when I turned 21, I didnt see this as a piece of our history falling away like scree on a mountain trail.
         </p>
         <p>
          And when I decided later that month to dismantle a collection of dried flowers, mostly bunches of the roses hed given me every anniversary (after 26 years, it was a dusty and faintly odorous mass), I did not sense that I was ending a tradition. I didnt know that I had already received my last red roses from him. I simply wanted to hang a painting on that wall.
         </p>
         <p>
          Later, I did think it strange when my toe became mysteriously unmarked. Years before, wed been joking around in the kitchen and hed stepped on my foot to stop me from whatever annoying jabbing I was up to. My left big toenail had never been the same. But soon after Gord died, the discoloration magically disappeared, as if his mark on me left with him.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now, hindsight shows me that the bitter, snow-ridden winter of 2013-14 was preparing me for loss.
         </p>
         <p>
          While I felt empowered shovelling all the snow and chopping all the kindling  by Christmas we knew Gords heart was acting strangely  I didnt realize that those jobs would be mine for good.
         </p>
         <p>
          When I came home from work those frigid February evenings to find Gord watching TV wearing his woollen hat, a scarf circling his neck, a fur throw cozying his legs and our black cat on his lap, we didnt know that his hardening heart muscle wasnt strong enough to pump blood to his extremities. Did the cat know? Did Tango sense impending death? Certainly, shed never spent any settled time on his lap in the previous 17 years. But if this was another sign, we missed it.
         </p>
         <p>
          Hindsight is tricking me into thinking that it was obvious the great quake was coming. But when youre not looking for tremors, when you are simply doing what cardiologists tell you to do and when youre 53 and played hockey all your life and never had anything much wrong with you, youre seeing only what you know. And that is not death.
         </p>
         <p>
          When a brutally insensitive cardiologist provides no explanations, when the medical system delays your appointments and shuffles you through in no great rush, you bear it graciously because you just dont know.
         </p>
         <p>
          In the grand scheme of our sweethearts-since-high-school love story, I wonder if we were better for not knowing how death was hovering. Our ignorance, which did not prompt us to call our daughters home for goodbyes, which did not prepare us for those terrible final moments, kept us in a bubble those last two weeks.
         </p>
         <p>
          We had been summarily informed of an increased chance of cardiac failure or sudden death  parting words, a mere clause in a sentence from the cardiologist as he made for the door  but those chilling words are hard to fathom when you still dont know what is wrong.
         </p>
         <p>
          It was a floating time, a time when we could not speak of this black cloud off in the distance, when we did not know it was rumbling toward us, laden with rain that would wash away our world.
         </p>
         <p>
          It was a tender time, with only what we knew, blessedly free of the future. If there was dread in Gords heart, he bore it quietly.
         </p>
         <p>
          Our trust in a medical fix was misplaced: He would have needed a new heart and there would not be that chance. It was the autopsy that revealed the crucial truth  a heart muscle riddled with an uncommon disease. Had we known, that reality would have coloured our last months together with debilitating sadness and fear.
         </p>
         <p>
          Instead, we walked a slow road that we did not recognize in a loving haze. Hindsight shows it to me now.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Gillian Cummings lives in Toronto.
          </em>
         </p>
