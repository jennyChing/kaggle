     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Standing Stones of Stenness
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">
       Neolithic
      </a>
      monument five miles northeast of
      <a href="/wiki/Stromness" title="Stromness">
       Stromness
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Mainland,_Orkney" title="Mainland, Orkney">
       mainland
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">
       Orkney
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      . This may be the oldest
      <a href="/wiki/Henge" title="Henge">
       henge
      </a>
      site in the British Isles. Various traditions associated with the stones survived into the modern era and they form part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Neolithic_Orkney" title="Heart of Neolithic Orkney">
       Heart of Neolithic Orkney
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">
       World Heritage Site
      </a>
      . They are looked after by
      <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">
       Historic Scotland
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Layout_and_location">
       Layout and location
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Standing_Stones_of_Stenness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Layout and location">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The surviving stones are sited on a promontory at the south bank of the stream that joins the southern ends of the sea loch
      <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Stenness" title="Loch of Stenness">
       Loch of Stenness
      </a>
      and the freshwater
      <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Harray" title="Loch of Harray">
       Loch of Harray
      </a>
      . The name, which is pronounced
      <i>
       stane-is
      </i>
      in Orcadian dialect, comes from
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
       Old Norse
      </a>
      meaning
      <i>
       stone headland
      </i>
      . The stream is now bridged, but at one time was crossed by a stepping stone causeway, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">
       Ring of Brodgar
      </a>
      lies about 1.2km (0.75mi) away to the north-west, across the stream and near the tip of the isthmus formed between the two lochs.
      <a href="/wiki/Maeshowe" title="Maeshowe">
       Maeshowe
      </a>
      chambered cairn is about 1.2km (0.75mi) to the east of the Standing Stones of Stenness and several other Neolithic monuments also lie in the vicinity, suggesting that this area had particular importance.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stenness_1983.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="517" data-file-width="749" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Stenness_1983.jpg/220px-Stenness_1983.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Stenness_1983.jpg/330px-Stenness_1983.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Stenness_1983.jpg/440px-Stenness_1983.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stenness_1983.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Stenness
        <i>
         Watch Stone
        </i>
        stands outside the circle, next to the modern bridge leading to the
        <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_Brodgar" title="Ring of Brodgar">
         Ring of Brodgar
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although the site today lacks the encircling ditch and bank, excavation has shown that this used to be a henge monument, possibly the oldest in the British Isles. The stones are thin slabs, approximately 300mm (12in) thick with sharply angled tops. Four, up to about 5m (16ft) high, were originally elements of a stone circle of up to 12 stones, laid out in an ellipse about 32m (105ft) diameter on a levelled platform of 44m (144ft) diameter surrounded by a ditch. The ditch is cut into rock by as much as 2m (6.6ft) and is 7m (23ft) wide, surrounded by an earth bank, with a single entrance causeway on the north side. The entrance faces towards the
      <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">
       Neolithic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Barnhouse_Settlement" title="Barnhouse Settlement">
       Barnhouse Settlement
      </a>
      which has been found adjacent to the
      <a href="/wiki/Loch_of_Harray" title="Loch of Harray">
       Loch of Harray
      </a>
      . The Watch Stone stands outside the circle to the north-west and is 5.6m (18ft) high. Once there were at least two stones there, as in the 1930s the stump of a second stone was found. Other smaller stones include a square stone setting in the centre of the circle platform where cremated bone, charcoal and pottery were found. This is referred to as a "hearth", similar to the one found at Barnhouse. Animal bones were found in the ditch. The pottery links the monument to
      <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">
       Skara Brae
      </a>
      and Maeshowe. Based on
      <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">
       radiocarbon dating
      </a>
      , it is thought that work on the site had begun by 3100 BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monuments_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Monuments-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :41
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions_and_reception">
       Traditions and reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Standing_Stones_of_Stenness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traditions and reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       Let us imagine, then, families approaching Stenness at the appointed time of year, men, women and children, carrying bundles of bones collected together from the skeletons of disinterred corpsesskulls, mandibles, long bonescarrying also the skulls of totem animals, herding a beast that was one of several to be slaughtered for the feasting that would accompany the ceremonies.
       <p style="text-align: right;">
        
        <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Burl" title="Aubrey Burl">
         Aubrey Burl
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Rites_of_the_Gods" title="Rites of the Gods">
          Rites of the Gods
         </a>
        </i>
        , 1981.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          3
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Even in the 18th century the site was still associated with traditions and rituals, by then relating to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Norse_gods_and_goddesses" title="List of Norse gods and goddesses">
       Norse gods
      </a>
      . It was visited by
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">
       Walter Scott
      </a>
      in 1814. Other antiquarians documented the stones and recorded local traditions and beliefs about them. One stone, known as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">
       Odin
      </a>
      Stone" which stood in the field to the north of the henge,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monuments_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Monuments-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was pierced with a circular hole, and was used by local couples for plighting engagements by holding hands through the gap. It was also associated with other ceremonies and believed to have magical power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (May 2014)">
          self-published source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 1814 Captain W. Mackay, a recent immigrant to Orkney who owned farmland in the vicinity of the stones, decided to remove them on the grounds that local people were trespassing and disturbing his land by using the stones in rituals. He started in December 1814 by smashing the Odin Stone. This caused outrage and he was stopped after destroying one other stone and toppling another.
     </p>
     <p>
      The toppled stone was re-erected in 1906 along with some inaccurate reconstruction inside the circle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (May 2014)">
          self-published source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1970s, a
      <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">
       dolmen
      </a>
      structure was toppled, since there were doubts as to its authenticity. The two upright stones remain in place.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HS-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A picture of the Stones of Stenness features on the cover of Van Morrison's album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Philosopher%27s_Stone_(album)" title="The Philosopher's Stone (album)">
        The Philosopher's Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      , and the Odin stone is depicted on
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_Cope" title="Julian Cope">
       Julian Cope
      </a>
      's album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Discover_Odin" title="Discover Odin">
        Discover Odin
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_Heritage_status">
       World Heritage status
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Standing_Stones_of_Stenness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World Heritage status">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Neolithic_Orkney" title="Heart of Neolithic Orkney">
       Heart of Neolithic Orkney
      </a>
      was inscribed as a World Heritage site in December 1999. In addition to the Standing Stones of Stenness, the site includes
      <a href="/wiki/Maeshowe" title="Maeshowe">
       Maeshowe
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Skara_Brae" title="Skara Brae">
       Skara Brae
      </a>
      , the Ring of Brodgar and other nearby sites. It is managed by Historic Scotland, whose 'Statement of Significance' for the site begins:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The monuments at the heart of Neolithic Orkney and Skara Brae proclaim the triumphs of the human spirit in early ages and isolated places. They were approximately contemporary with the mastabas of the archaic period of Egypt (first and second dynasties), the brick temples of Sumeria, and the first cities of the Harappa culture in India, and a century or two earlier than the Golden Age of China. Unusually fine for their early date, and with a remarkably rich survival of evidence, these sites stand as a visible symbol of the achievements of early peoples away from the traditional centres of civilisation...Stenness is a unique and early expression of the ritual customs of the people who buried their dead in tombs like Maes Howe and lived in settlements like Skara Brae.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
