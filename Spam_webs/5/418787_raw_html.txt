          <a href="http://voices.nationalgeographic.com/2014/01/09/mystery-picket-fence-in-amazon-explained/" rel="bookmark">
           Mystery Picket Fence in Amazon Explained
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="byline vcard author">
          <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-58 photo" height="58" src="http://voices.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/01/carrie_cuckoo2-58x58.jpg" srcset="http://0.gravatar.com/avatar/31407337700aa152fe39b36758dd6ad3?s=116&amp;d=blank&amp;r=g 2x" width="58"/>
          Posted by
          <span class="fn">
           <a href="http://voices.nationalgeographic.com/author/carnold/" title="Posts by Carrie Arnold">
            Carrie Arnold
           </a>
          </span>
          in
          <a href="http://voices.nationalgeographic.com/blog/weird-wild/">
           Weird &amp; Wild
          </a>
          on
          <abbr class="published" title="2014-01-09T10:47:31+00:00">
           January 9, 2014
          </abbr>
         </div>
         <!--/byline-->
        </header>
        <!--/header-->

         <div class="comments">

         <div addthis:title="Mystery Picket Fence in Amazon Explained" addthis:url="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/2014/01/09/mystery-picket-fence-in-amazon-explained/" class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style">
          <a class="addthis_button_facebook_like" fb:like:layout="button_count" style="opacity:1;">
          </a>
          <a class="addthis_button_tweet" tw:via="NatGeo">

        <div class="main">
         <div class="content entry-content">
          <p>
           <strong>
            The animal that
            <a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/2013/09/06/what-created-this-mysterious-picket-fence-in-the-amazon/">
             built the strange picket fence structure has been found.
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Six months ago, visitors to the
           <a href="http://travel.nationalgeographic.com/travel/countries/peru-guide/">
            Peruvian
           </a>
           Amazon discovered a mysterious picket fence structure nicknamed Silkhenge. Despite watching the structure for several days, naturalists at the
           <a href="http://www.perunature.com/lodge-tambopata-research-center.html">
            Tambopata Research Center
           </a>
           couldnt figure out what type of animal (or fungus) was building it.
          </p>
          <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_120668" class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_120668" style="width: 610px">
           <a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/01/mystery-picket-fence-1.jpg">
            <img alt="mystery picket fence picture" class="size-medium wp-image-120668" height="399" src="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/01/mystery-picket-fence-1-600x399.jpg" width="600"/>
           </a>
           <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_120668">
            The odd web-like structure that has captivated scientists. Photograph by Jeff Cremer, Solent News/Rex Features via AP
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p>
           When scientist
           <a href="https://twitter.com/TroySAlexander" target="_blank">
            Troy Alexander
           </a>
           first announced his find, all he had to show for his discovery was a series of intriguing photographs. He had no idea what Amazonian critter could have created the circular hideaway with a spoke-like outer wall.
          </p>
          <p>
           After consulting with several entomologists, Alexander hypothesized that it was likely built by a type of
           <a href="http://edis.ifas.ufl.edu/in597" target="_blank">
            cribellate spider
           </a>
           , which are known for building elaborate structures.
          </p>
          <p>
           When we first posted news of the picket fence, readers flooded the blog with suggestions for what had built it and
           <a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/2013/09/10/your-best-names-for-the-odd-picket-fence-maker/">
            what scientists should name the potential new species
           </a>
           . While many of you agreed with Alexanders hypothesis that the fence was built by a spider, others of you werent so sure, guessing that a fungus or caterpillar may have built it.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Forest Architects
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           In December, Tambopata scientist
           <a href="http://therevscience.com/" target="_blank">
            Phil Torres
           </a>
           , who helped first discover the spider, along with University of Florida entomologists
           <a href="https://twitter.com/BiodiversiLary" target="_blank">
            Lary Reeves
           </a>
           and
           <a href="http://www.flmnh.ufl.edu/mcguire/kawahara/people/" target="_blank">
            Geena Hill
           </a>
           and photographer
           <a href="http://www.perunature.com/jeff-of-the-jungle-photography.html" target="_blank">
            Jeff Cremer
           </a>
           ,returned to the small island where they had first found the small picket fences built on the sides of trees and tarps. For a week, they staked out the area, taking careful notes and watching the miniature structures very closely.
          </p>
          <p>
           Along a 650-foot (200-meter) stretch of trail, the scientists identified 45 different picket fences.Their week of eagle-eyed watching paid off when they discovered spiderlings hatching from three of these fences. (
           <a href="http://video.nationalgeographic.com/video/player/animals/bugs-animals/spiders-and-scorpions">
            See National Geographics spider videos
           </a>
           .)
          </p>
          <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_120669" class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_120669" style="width: 610px">
           <a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/01/mystery-picket-fence-2.jpg">
            <img alt="A spiderling seen in December 2013 near the mysterious picket fence. Photograph by Jeff Cremer, Solent News/Rex Features via AP" class="size-medium wp-image-120669" height="399" src="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/01/mystery-picket-fence-2-600x399.jpg" width="600"/>
           </a>
           <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_120669">
            A spiderling seen in December 2013. Photograph by Jeff Cremer, Solent News/Rex Features via AP
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p>
           Although they still havent identified what species the spiderlings belong toor whether previous scientists might have already discovered itthey say the structure is intriguing for several reasons.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://blog.perunature.com/2013/12/weve-solved-amazon-rainforest-mystery.html" target="_blank">
            Torres wrote on his blog
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            For one, it is not common for spiders to lay eggs and abandon them, they typically place silken egg sacs in their own web to protect. More oddly, it appears that there was only a single egg per structure. This, as far as we can tell, would be the only known occurrence of a spider laying a single egg per egg sac.We saw a few adult spiders around that were prime suspects, but never saw any making it, so the construction and culprit remain a mystery.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Mite Meal
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Besides spiders, the area also contained large numbers of mites that were leading suspects as the fence-builders until the entomologists found the spiderlings.(Also see
           <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2010/09/photogalleries/100917-darwins-bark-spider-new-species-spider-webs-madagascar-science-pictures-strongest/">
            Photos: Worlds Biggest, Strongest Spider Webs Found.
           </a>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           The researchers have hypothesized that the picket fence either serves to protect the egg in the center from other predatory insects or it contains a chemical attractant to lure mites, ants, and other insects to the newly hatched spiders to give them an easy first meal.
          </p>
          <p>
           Plenty of work still has to be done, but the builders of Silkhenge have at long last been found.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Now that you know the picket fence is built by a spider, do you have any name suggestions for it? Share your thoughts in the comments!
           </strong>
          </p>

          </p>
