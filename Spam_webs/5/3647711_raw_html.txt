     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Wiener Zeitung
       </b>
      </i>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austrian
      </a>
      newspaper. It is one of the most famous newspapers in Europe and one of the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_oldest_newspapers" title="List of the oldest newspapers">
       oldest, still published newspapers in the world
      </a>
      . It is the official publication used by the Government of the Republic of Austria for its formal announcements.
     </p>
     <p>
      As of 2002
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung
      </i>
      was among the four Austrian quality-newspapers beside the right-liberal
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Die_Presse" title="Die Presse">
        Die Presse
       </a>
      </i>
      , the left-liberal
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Der_Standard" title="Der Standard">
        Der Standard
       </a>
      </i>
      and the catholic
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Salzburger_Nachrichten" title="Salzburger Nachrichten">
        Salzburger Nachrichten
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_profile">
       History and profile
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wiener_Zeitung&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and profile">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The newspaper was founded in 1703
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      under the name
      <i>
       Wiennerisches Diarium
      </i>
      . The first issue appeared on 8 August 1703. Victor Langer ("Obmann" and President of the Austrian Stone Masons for three decades pre WWII) writes (see the exact quote as per his words) in his documentation about the History of "Steinmetzmeistern" (Stonemason) that: "Wiennerisches Diarium" was the newspaper released by the Stonemason Community building the "Stephans Dom" Vienna's largest Cathedrale, still standing and became in 1805 the private Imperial "Wiener Zeitung". quote: (17031721) Die Steinmetzen am Dombau von St. Stephan besaen bereits eine Zeitung genannt "Wiener Diarium" aus welcher spter (1805) die Kaisliche private "Wiener Zeitung" entstand.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Wiennerisches Diarium
      </i>
      , like many papers at the time, started out by reporting regional and international news. In addition it published birth and wedding announcements as well as obituaries of the
      <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">
       aristocracy
      </a>
      and provided coverage of the imperial court. Purely local news were announced by a public
      <a href="/wiki/Announcer" title="Announcer">
       announcer
      </a>
      or drummer.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1670" data-file-width="1602" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg/220px-Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg/330px-Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg/440px-Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wiener_Zeitung_Wiedner_G%C3%BCrtel_10.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The current premises, in the centre of Vienna
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Since 1780, the paper was known as
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung
      </i>
      (meaning
      <i>
       Viennese newspaper
      </i>
      in English) and in 1810 it became the official government newspaper.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1857 the government acquired the paper and it was printed until 1997 by the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_State_Printing_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Austrian State Printing Office (page does not exist)">
       Austrian State Printing Office
      </a>
      . The first edition after
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      appeared on 21 September 1945. The number of copies sold has grown from 4,500 in 1855 to an estimated 24,000 today. In 1998, the paper was privatized, and is now owned by a
      <a href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_mit_beschr%C3%A4nkter_Haftung" title="Gesellschaft mit beschrnkter Haftung">
       GmbH
      </a>
      (Limited Liability Company), although the Government is still the publisher.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung
      </i>
      is also the official publication used by the Government of the
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Republic of Austria
      </a>
      for formal announcements. Such announcements, for example, civil service vacancies and changes in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_register" title="Commercial register">
       commercial register
      </a>
      , are printed in the Official Journal insert of
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung
      </i>
      . Until 2004 it also used to publish the official version of newly passed Austrian laws. Today the governmental version of newly passed statutes and treaties are officially published in the Internet, the law gazette thus is not available in a printed version anymore.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1776_Wiennerisches_Diarium_o_Deklaracji_Niepodleg%C5%82o%C5%9Bci_USA.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1776_Wiennerisches_Diarium_o_Deklaracji_Niepodleg%C5%82o%C5%9Bci_USA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Wienerisches Diarium
        </i>
        , 1776
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Until 2009
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung'
      </i>
      s
      <a href="/wiki/Editor-in-chief" title="Editor-in-chief">
       editor-in-chief
      </a>
      was
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andreas_Unterberger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andreas Unterberger (page does not exist)">
       Andreas Unterberger
      </a>
      , before being replaced by Reinhard Gweil. While Unterberger hired mostly outspoken conservative columnists, the paper returned to its liberal position under
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reinhard_G%C3%B6weil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reinhard Gweil (page does not exist)">
       Reinhard Gweil
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Austrian government is widely criticized among entrepreneurs because they are legally required to publish certain legal announcements, such as shareholder meeting conventions and changes of the commercial register in the Wiener Zeitung and therefore have to pay certain fees, although publications are also done through the Internet. Entrepreneurs and private newspapers argue, alleging anti-competitive measures, that the newspaper is financed through these mandatory fees. The Austrian Supreme Court dismissed claims on this matter. A decision of the European Court of Justice is pending. The issue is even more controversial since today the only authentic source of Austrian statutory law is the Internet, whereas business publications also have to be announced through
      <i>
       Wiener Zeitung
      </i>
      .
     </p>
