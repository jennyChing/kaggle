           <article class="article " xmlns:fb="http://www.facebook.com/2008/fbml" xmlns:msxsl="urn:schemas-microsoft-com:xslt" xmlns:regexp="http://exslt.org/regular-expressions">
            <div class="articlehead">
             <header>
              <span>
              </span>
              <h1>
               How to Help Someone You Love Quit Smoking
              </h1>
             </header>
             <aside class="disclaimer">
             </aside>
             <h2>
              Quitting smoking is the most important step someone with COPD can take to manage the condition.  Get tips to help someone you love start on the path toward quitting.
             </h2>
             <div class="bottom">
              <div class="byline">
               By
               <a href="/contributing-writers-and-editors.aspx">
                Chris Iliades, MD
               </a>
              </div>
              <div class="reviewedby">
               Medically reviewed by
               <a href="/medical-reviewers.aspx">
                Lindsey Marcellin, MD, MPH
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="clear">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div id="video-player-top">
            </div>
            <aside class="aside">
            </aside>
            <p>
             Having a loved one with
             <a href="/health-report/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease.aspx">
              chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder
             </a>
             (COPD) who can't or won't quit smoking can be very frustrating and frightening. Smoking is not only the main cause of COPD  quitting smoking is the most important part of
             <a href="/health-report/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/copd-treatment-tips.aspx">
              treatment
             </a>
             . COPD symptoms will continue to get worse and COPD exacerbations are unavoidable unless your loved one can quit smoking. What can you do to help?
            </p>
            <p>
             "You can do a lot," says Eleana Conway, nurse practitioner, who directs the Freedom From Smoking Program at Lahey Clinic in Burlington, Mass. "Having
             <a href="/health-report/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/caring-for-loved-one-with-copd.aspx">
              someone who supports you
             </a>
             greatly increases the chances of successfully quitting smoking. If your loved one has not quit yet, you need to avoid being negative or judgmental. Picking them up and not putting them down is the best way to get them ready to quit smoking."
            </p>
            <p>
             Nagging, scolding, and preaching are the worst approaches. "Start by recognizing how difficult and frustrating it is to quit smoking," says Conway. Accept that the decision to quit smoking has to be their decision, not yours. The best way to help is to let your loved one know that you are there for support no matter what.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Helping a Loved One Who Wants to Quit Smoking
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             "Smoking is an addiction, and just like other addictions, quitting is rarely a first-time success. That's why we prefer to think of it as smoking treatment and not smoking cessation," says Conway.
            </p>
            <p>
             Conway offers these five R's"  talking points and advice for encouragement and motivation:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Relevance.
              </strong>
              Talk about how important it is to quit, relating information to your loved ones life.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Risks.
              </strong>
              Talk about the risks of smoking with COPD.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Rewards.
              </strong>
              Talk about the benefits of quitting.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Roadblocks.
              </strong>
              Identify things that can get in the way of quitting.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Repetition.
              </strong>
              Deciding to quit is a process and can require repeated attempts, so be patient. Repeat your motivation each time your loved one tries to quit.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Other Ways You Can Help
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             People smoke to celebrate, to relieve boredom, or to relieve stress. "Recognizing these smoking triggers and planning for them can help your loved one avoid a smoking relapse," says Conway.
            </p>
            <p>
             Here are some ideas for helping your loved one quit smoking:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Spend time together doing healthy activities that keep your loved one's mind off smoking.
             </li>
             <li>
              Keep your home smoke-free and remove lighters and ashtrays that may serve as triggers.
             </li>
             <li>
              Celebrate success, recognizing that every day without smoking is a victory.
             </li>
             <li>
              Expect and accept some grumpiness during the nicotine withdrawal phase.
             </li>
             <li>
              Recognize that an occasional slip is common and doesn't mean failure.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             Part of a smoking treatment plan is behavior modification. That means avoiding situations that lead to smoking and learning other behaviors to deal with stress or boredom. "If you go out together to a place where people are smoking, plan ahead and have a signal for when the temptation is getting too high," says Conway.
            </p>
            <p>
             You can be a big part of your loved one's behavior modification by participating in healthy activities together. Smoking treatment may also include medications and support groups. Encourage your loved one to ask for help and to take advantage of smoking treatment programs that are offered at most hospitals.
            </p>
            <p>
             It's hard for your loved one to quit smoking, even though COPD symptoms and
             <a href="/health-report/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease/prevent-copd-flare.aspx">
              COPD exacerbations
             </a>
             will get worse if he or she doesnt quit. Ultimately you need to accept that only the one who smokes can make the decision to quit. Until your loved one decides to quit  as well as during the struggle to quit  the best you can do is stay positive and be supportive.
            </p>
