     <p>
      The
      <b>
       skunk ape
      </b>
      , also known as the
      <b>
       swamp ape
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       stink ape
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       Florida Bigfoot
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       myakka ape
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       swampsquatch
      </b>
      , and
      <b>
       myakka skunk ape
      </b>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">
       hominid
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Cryptid" title="Cryptid">
       cryptid
      </a>
      said to inhabit the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._states" title="U.S. states">
       U.S. states
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
       Florida
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lennon_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lennon-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">
       North Carolina
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">
       Arkansas
      </a>
      , although reports from Florida are more common. It is named for its appearance and for the unpleasant odor that is said to accompany it. According to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">
       National Park Service
      </a>
      , the skunk ape does not exist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Reports of the skunk ape were particularly common in the 1960s and 1970s. In 1974, sightings of a large, foul-smelling, hairy,
      <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">
       ape
      </a>
      -like creature, which ran upright on two legs were reported in suburban neighborhoods of
      <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">
       Dade County
      </a>
      , Florida. Skeptical investigator
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Nickell" title="Joe Nickell">
       Joe Nickell
      </a>
      has written that some of the reports may represent sightings of the
      <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">
       black bear
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Ursus americanus
      </i>
      ) and it is likely that other sightings are hoaxes or misidentification of wildlife.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Myakka_photographs">
       Myakka photographs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Skunk_ape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Myakka photographs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2000, two photographs said to be of the skunk ape were taken by an anonymous woman and mailed to the Sheriff's Department of
      <a href="/wiki/Sarasota_County,_Florida" title="Sarasota County, Florida">
       Sarasota County
      </a>
      , Florida. The photographs were accompanied by a letter from the woman in which she claimed to have photographed an ape in her backyard.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The woman wrote that on three different nights, an ape had entered her backyard to take apples left on her back porch. She was convinced the ape was an escaped
      <a href="/wiki/Orangutan" title="Orangutan">
       orangutan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The pictures have become known to
      <a href="/wiki/Bigfoot" title="Bigfoot">
       Bigfoot
      </a>
      enthusiasts as the "skunk ape photos".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newton_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Newton-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Loren_Coleman" title="Loren Coleman">
       Loren Coleman
      </a>
      is the primary researcher on the photographs, having helped track down the two photographs to an "
      <a href="/wiki/Eckerd_Corporation" title="Eckerd Corporation">
       Eckerd
      </a>
      photo lab at the intersection of Fruitville and Tuttle Roads" in
      <a href="/wiki/Sarasota,_Florida" title="Sarasota, Florida">
       Sarasota
      </a>
      , Florida.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Chester Moore, Jr., the photographs were taken in Sarasota County near the
      <a href="/wiki/Myakka_River" title="Myakka River">
       Myakka River
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colemannews_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Colemannews-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
