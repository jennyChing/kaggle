     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Grass: A Nation's Battle for Life
       </b>
      </i>
      (1925) is a silent
      <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">
       documentary film
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which follows a branch of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bakhtiari_people" title="Bakhtiari people">
       Bakhtiari
      </a>
      tribe of
      <a href="/wiki/Lurs" title="Lurs">
       lurs
      </a>
      in Iran as they and their herds make their seasonal journey to better pastures. It is considered one of the earliest ethnographic
      <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">
       documentary films
      </a>
      . It was written by
      <i>
       Richard Carver
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Terry_Ramsaye" title="Terry Ramsaye">
       Terry Ramsaye
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grass_(1925_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The film is
      <a href="/wiki/Merian_C._Cooper" title="Merian C. Cooper">
       Merian C. Cooper
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernest_Schoedsack" title="Ernest Schoedsack">
       Ernest Schoedsack
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marguerite_Harrison" title="Marguerite Harrison">
       Marguerite Harrison's
      </a>
      documentation of their journey from Angora (modern-day
      <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">
       Ankara
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
       Turkey
      </a>
      ) to the Bakhtiari lands of western Iran, in what is now the western part of
      <a href="/wiki/Chaharmahal_and_Bakhtiari_Province" title="Chaharmahal and Bakhtiari Province">
       Chaharmahal and Bakhtiari Province
      </a>
      and the eastern part of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khuzestan" title="Khuzestan">
       Khuzestan
      </a>
      . They then follow Haidar Khan as he leads 50,000 of his people and countless animals on a harrowing trek across the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karun_River" title="Karun River">
       Karun River
      </a>
      and over
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zard_Kuh" title="Zard Kuh">
       Zard Kuh
      </a>
      , the highest peak in the
      <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">
       Zagros Mountains
      </a>
      . In filming the journey, Cooper, Schoedsack, and Harrison became the first Westerners to make the migration with the Bakhtiari.
     </p>
     <p>
      The film highlights the extreme hardships faced by
      <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">
       nomadic
      </a>
      peoples, as well as the bravery and ingenuity of the Bakhtiari. At the same time, the film is also a reflection of the context out of which it emerged, that of
      <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">
       Hollywood
      </a>
      in the 1920s. The central concern of
      <i>
       Grass
      </i>
      is to present primordial human struggle with harsh environments, as in the contemporaneous ethnographic documentary
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nanook_of_the_North" title="Nanook of the North">
        Nanook of the North
       </a>
      </i>
      . According to Cooper,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      however, the filmmakers were unaware of
      <i>
       Nanook
      </i>
      until their return to New York City from filming in Iran. As in
      <i>
       Nanook
      </i>
      , the filmmakers attempt to document "timeless" and "ancient" human struggles, here still observable in this part of the oriental world. The film has an engaging but deeply
      <a href="/wiki/Orientalism" title="Orientalism">
       Orientalist
      </a>
      tone in presenting the Bakhtiari as unchanging and archaic.
     </p>
     <p>
      The documentary presents the filmmakers' travel as a narrative of a return to an ancient past: they turn the pages of history backwards until they get to "the very first page". Therefore what they present to the audience in the documentary is not a culture in the present, but a culture of the past. Mention is made of a sort of genealogical quest for so-called Aryan origins of 3000 years ago, calling the Bakhtiari, "the Forgotten People". The film highlights migratory
      <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
       Anatolian
      </a>
      and Iranian peoples as continuously in a struggle for survival: the hunter on the Taurus mountains "does not hunt for sport, he kills for food". The film presents the annual Bakhtiari migration to Iranian highlands, where several environmental difficulties stand in the way of the tribe, which over time has developed ingenious solutions to overcome them (barefoot trail cutting over snow-covered
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zard_Kuh" title="Zard Kuh">
       Zard Kuh
      </a>
      , goat-skin floats at the river crossing, etc.).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1997,
      <i>
       Grass
      </i>
      was selected for preservation in the United States
      <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">
       National Film Registry
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      as being "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant".
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, author
      <a href="/wiki/Bahman_Maghsoudlou" title="Bahman Maghsoudlou">
       Bahman Maghsoudlou
      </a>
      published his book "Grass: Untold Stories" detailing background information and historical references related to the making of the movie.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
