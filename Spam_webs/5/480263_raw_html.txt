          <h1 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="headline">
           <a href="http://thecityfarm.com/magic-rosemary/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link: The Magic of Rosemary">
            The Magic of Rosemary
            <span class="post-format-icon minor-meta">
            </span>
           </a>
          </h1>
          <span class="post-meta-infos">
           <time class="date-container minor-meta updated" datetime="2014-01-07T06:00:18+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
            January 7, 2014
           </time>
           <span class="text-sep text-sep-date">
            /
           </span>
           <span class="comment-container minor-meta">
            <a class="comments-link" href="http://thecityfarm.com/magic-rosemary/#respond">
             0 Comments
            </a>
           </span>
           <span class="text-sep text-sep-comment">
            /
           </span>
           <span class="blog-categories minor-meta">
            in
            <a href="http://thecityfarm.com/category/grow/" rel="tag">
             Grow
            </a>
           </span>
           <span class="text-sep text-sep-cat">
            /
           </span>
           <span class="blog-author minor-meta">
            by
            <span class="entry-author-link" itemprop="name">
             <span class="vcard author">
              <span class="fn">
               <a href="http://thecityfarm.com/author/rebecca/" rel="author" title="Posts by Rebecca">
                Rebecca
               </a>
              </span>
             </span>
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </header>
         <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <a class="thickbox" href="http://thecityfarm.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/rosemary.jpg">
            <img alt="rosemary" class="wp-image-5714 aligncenter" height="285" src="http://thecityfarm.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/rosemary-525x285.jpg" title="The Magic of Rosemary: An Herbal History of Greek Goddesses, Warding off Witches, and Attracting Love" width="525"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Researching rosemary took me on a journey through the origins of Aphrodite, said to havearisen from the sea wearing just a wreath of the herbs, to the original Love Potion No. 9, whenin the Middle Ages it was considered a love charm, which brides wore in their hair while thegroom and guests donned a sprig.
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: left;">
           I bought all that info with only a few fact-checking jumps around the interwebs, especially as itwas posted by
           <a href="http://libguides.ncl.ac.uk/content.php?pid=461685&amp;sid=3825202" target="_blank">
            Newcastle University
           </a>
           . I didnt bat an eye when I read that planting a bit outsideyour home warded off witches, a belief held in the 16th century. Sure. And Im down with those18th century dudes who used rosemary oil to promote hair growth  male pattern baldingdoesnt bother me, but its your scalp, guys. Im all about holistic, non-toxic beauty regimensand am personally tempted to see if it might still
           <a href="http://libguides.ncl.ac.uk/content.php?pid=461685&amp;sid=3825202" target="_blank">
            beautify my teeth.
           </a>
           Then the Universitys site notes that during the 17th century, rosemary was used medicinally tocure jaundice and to restore speech to a mute.
          </p>
          <p>
           (Record screeches to a stop.)
          </p>
          <p>
           Im sorry. What? Just like that, an herb you can grow in your garden so you might ward offwitches while you sit safely inside brushing your lush, long hair whilst you smile your gorgeousgrin can also RESTORE SPEECH TO A MUTE?
           <em>
            HOW
           </em>
           did it restore speech to a mute? Noreference for that fun fact? I posted that question on the site, and will attempt to find the story
           <br/>
           behind it. Im on it.
          </p>
          <p>
           As I read Wikipedias note that when burned, rosemary smells akin to a wood fire, my sensememorysparked the woodsy smell, my mouth watered, and I suddenly craved meat. Do youuse rosemary in your stuffing or to season lamb or pork?
          </p>
          <p>
           Known for strengthening memory, adding flavor to your food and luster to your locks, andapparently giving speech to the speechless, why not grow a little in your garden? Rosmarinusofficinalis is perennial, and though native to the Mediterranean regions, it is hardy and can growalmost anywhere, though it prefers the sun. The seedlings grow very slowly, so for instantgratification, its best to buy plants. And since the ideal time to plant rosemary is in April, checkwith your local nursery to learn when to plant in your region.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.almanac.com/plant/rosemary" target="_blank">
            The Old Farmers Almanac
           </a>
           suggests getting a head start and planting the seeds or cuttingsindoors 8 to 10 weeks before the last spring frost. If youre a city dweller in a small space, keepit in a container but give it lots of sunlight. And if you transplant it outdoors, be sure to give theplant plenty of room to grow, some reach four feet in height and spread the same distance. Andif you need a planter, check out The City Farms
           <a href="https://thecityfarm.com/product/herb-garden-boxes/" target="_blank">
            gorgeous way to grow
           </a>
           your herb garden
           <a href="https://thecityfarm.com/product/herb-garden-boxes/" target="_blank">
            here
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <a class="thickbox" href="http://thecityfarm.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/City-Farm-Garden-Boxes-1.jpeg">
            <img alt="City Farm Garden Boxes (1)" class=" wp-image-5715 aligncenter" height="285" src="http://thecityfarm.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/City-Farm-Garden-Boxes-1-491x285.jpeg" title="The Magic of Rosemary: An Herbal History of Greek Goddesses, Warding off Witches, and Attracting Love" width="491"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           There are so many ways to enjoy your new addition to your herb garden! Im thinking of startinga new slogan: Rosemary, its not just for warding off your neighborhood witch. Dry some andthrow it on the BBQ the next time youre grilling, or snag this recipe from
           <a href="http://covetgarden.com/blog/2011/7/5/covet-garden-12-sneak-peek-delicious-popcorn-snack.html" target="_blank">
            Convet Garden
           </a>
           andmake some brown butter &amp; rosemary popcorn to serve your guests a savory snack the next timeyou host movie night. Since rosemary keeps on growing and thus giving, try making anessential oil to add to your lotions, shampoo, or in your baked breads. Over at
           <a href="http://ahealthylifeforme.com/2013/01/08/how-to-make-rosemary-essential-oil/" target="_blank">
            A Healthy Life forMe
           </a>
           , Amy Stafford gives her tips for making oil with your own rosemary: all you need is a masonjar, oil, and clippings from your garden.
          </p>
          <p>
           Whats your favorite way to add a dash of rosemary to your world? Leave a note in thecomments, or tweet at us:
           <a href="https://twitter.com/TheCityFarm" target="_blank">
            @TheCityFarm
           </a>
           &amp;
           <a href="https://twitter.com/RebeccaSnavely" target="_blank">
            @RebeccaSnavely
           </a>
           .
          </p>
