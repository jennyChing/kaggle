   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Bentonite
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">

    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Bentonite
      </b>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">
       absorbent
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals#Phyllosilicates" title="Silicate minerals">
       phyllosilicate
      </a>
      , impure
      <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">
       clay
      </a>
      consisting mostly of
      <a href="/wiki/Montmorillonite" title="Montmorillonite">
       montmorillonite
      </a>
      . The absorbent clay was given the name bentonite by Wilbur C. Knight in 1898, after the
      <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">
       Cretaceous
      </a>
      Benton Shale near
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_River,_Wyoming" title="Rock River, Wyoming">
       Rock River, Wyoming
      </a>
      in America.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are different types of bentonite, each named after the respective dominant
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       element
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
       potassium
      </a>
      (K),
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">
       sodium
      </a>
      (Na),
      <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">
       calcium
      </a>
      (Ca), and
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      (Al). Experts debate a number of nomenclatorial problems with the classification of bentonite clays. Bentonite usually forms from weathering of
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">
       volcanic ash
      </a>
      , most often in the presence of water. However, the term bentonite, as well as a similar clay called
      <a href="/wiki/Tonstein" title="Tonstein">
       tonstein
      </a>
      , has been used to describe clay beds of uncertain origin. For industrial purposes, two main classes of bentonite exist: sodium and calcium bentonite. In
      <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">
       stratigraphy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tephrochronology" title="Tephrochronology">
       tephrochronology
      </a>
      , completely
      <a href="/wiki/Devitrification" title="Devitrification">
       devitrified
      </a>
      (weathered volcanic glass) ash-fall beds are commonly referred to as K-bentonites when the dominant clay species is
      <a href="/wiki/Illite" title="Illite">
       illite
      </a>
      . Other common clay species that are sometimes dominant, are
      <a href="/wiki/Montmorillonite" title="Montmorillonite">
       montmorillonite
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">
       kaolinite
      </a>
      . Kaolinite-dominated clays are commonly referred to as tonsteins and are typically associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
       Types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sodium_bentonite">
       Sodium bentonite
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sodium bentonite">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">
       Sodium
      </a>
      bentonite expands when wet, absorbing as much as several times its dry
      <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
       mass
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of its excellent
      <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">
       colloidal
      </a>
      properties,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odom_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odom-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it is often used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drilling_mud" title="Drilling mud">
       drilling mud
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">
       oil
      </a>
      and gas wells and boreholes for geotechnical and environmental investigations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The property of swelling also makes sodium bentonite useful as a sealant, since it provides a self-sealing, low permeability barrier. It is used to line the base of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">
       landfills
      </a>
      for example.
     </p>
     <p>
      Various surface modifications to sodium bentonite improve some rheological or sealing performance in geoenvironmental applications, for example, the addition of polymers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Calcium_bentonite">
       Calcium bentonite
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Calcium bentonite">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Calcium bentonite is a useful
      <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">
       adsorbent
      </a>
      of ions in solution,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as fats and oils. It is the main active ingredient of
      <a href="/wiki/Fuller%27s_earth" title="Fuller's earth">
       fuller's earth
      </a>
      , probably one of the earliest industrial cleaning agents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Calcium bentonite may be converted to sodium bentonite (termed sodium beneficiation or sodium activation) to exhibit many of sodium bentonite's properties by an
      <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">
       ion exchange
      </a>
      process. In common usage, this means adding 510% of a soluble sodium salt such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">
       sodium carbonate
      </a>
      to wet bentonite, mixing well, and allowing time for the ion exchange to take place and water to remove the exchanged calcium.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Some properties, such as viscosity and fluid loss of suspensions, of sodium-beneficiated calcium bentonite (or sodium-activated bentonite) may not be fully equivalent to those of natural sodium bentonite.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odom_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Odom-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, residual calcium carbonates (formed if exchanged cations are insufficiently removed) may result in inferior performance of the bentonite in geosynthetic liners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Potassium_bentonite">
       Potassium bentonite
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Potassium bentonite">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Also known as potash bentonite or K-bentonite, potassium bentonite is a potassium-rich illitic clay formed from alteration of volcanic ash.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The main uses of bentonite are for drilling mud, binder (e.g. foundry-sand bond, iron ore pelletizer), purifier, absorbent (e.g. pet litter), and as a groundwater barrier.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of around 1990, almost half of the US production of bentonite was used for drilling mud.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Drilling_mud">
       Drilling mud
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Drilling mud">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bentonite is used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drilling_mud" title="Drilling mud">
       drilling fluids
      </a>
      to lubricate and cool the cutting tools, to remove cuttings, and to help prevent blowouts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Much of bentonite's usefulness in the drilling and
      <a href="/wiki/Geotechnical_engineering" title="Geotechnical engineering">
       geotechnical engineering
      </a>
      industry comes from its unique
      <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">
       rheological
      </a>
      properties. Relatively small quantities of bentonite suspended in water form a viscous, shear thinning material. Most often, bentonite suspensions are also
      <a href="/wiki/Thixotropy" title="Thixotropy">
       thixotropic
      </a>
      , although rare cases of
      <a href="/wiki/Rheopecty" title="Rheopecty">
       rheopectic
      </a>
      behavior have also been reported. At high enough concentrations (~60grams of bentonite per litre of suspension), bentonite suspensions begin to take on the characteristics of a
      <a href="/wiki/Gel" title="Gel">
       gel
      </a>
      (a fluid with a minimum yield strength required to make it move). For these reasons it is a common component of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drilling_mud" title="Drilling mud">
       drilling mud
      </a>
      used to curtail
      <a href="/wiki/Drilling_fluid_invasion" title="Drilling fluid invasion">
       drilling fluid invasion
      </a>
      by its propensity for aiding in the formation of
      <a href="/wiki/Mud_cake_(oil_and_gas)" title="Mud cake (oil and gas)">
       mud cake
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Binder">
       Binder
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Binder">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bentonite has been widely used as a foundry-sand bond in iron and steel foundries. Sodium bentonite is most commonly used for large castings that use dry molds, while calcium bentonite is more commonly used for smaller castings that use "green" or wet molds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentonite is also used as a binding agent in the manufacture of iron ore (
      <a href="/wiki/Taconite" title="Taconite">
       taconite
      </a>
      ) pellets as used in the steelmaking industry.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentonite, in small percentages, is used as an ingredient in commercially designed clay bodies and ceramic glazes. Bentonite clay is also used in
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">
       pyrotechnics
      </a>
      to make end plugs and
      <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine_nozzle" title="Rocket engine nozzle">
       rocket engine nozzles
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The ionic surface of bentonite has a useful property in making a sticky coating on sand grains. When a small proportion of finely ground bentonite clay is added to hard sand and wetted, the clay binds the sand particles into a moldable aggregate known as
      <a href="/wiki/Molding_sand" title="Molding sand">
       green sand
      </a>
      used for making molds in
      <a href="/wiki/Sand_casting" title="Sand casting">
       sand casting
      </a>
      . Some river deltas naturally deposit just such a blend of clay silt and sand, creating a natural source of excellent molding sand that was critical to ancient metalworking technology. Modern chemical processes to modify the ionic surface of bentonite greatly intensify this stickiness, resulting in remarkably dough-like yet strong casting sand mixes that stand up to molten metal temperatures.
     </p>
     <p>
      The same effluvial deposition of bentonite clay onto beaches accounts for the variety of plasticity of sand from place to place for building
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_castle" title="Sand castle">
       sand castles
      </a>
      . Beach sand consisting of only silica and shell grains does not mold well compared to grains coated with bentonite clay. This is why some beaches are much better for building sand castles than others.
     </p>
     <p>
      The self-stickiness of bentonite allows high-pressure ramming or pressing of the clay in molds to produce hard, refractory shapes, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Model_rocket" title="Model rocket">
       model rocket
      </a>
      nozzles. To test whether a particular brand of cat litter is bentonite, simply ram a sample with a hammer into a sturdy tube with a close-fitting rod; bentonite will form a very hard, consolidated plug that is not easily crumbled.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Purification">
       Purification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Purification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bentonites are used for decolorizing various mineral, vegetable and animal oils. They are also used for clarifying wine, liquor, cider, beer, vinegar and others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentonite has the property of
      <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">
       adsorbing
      </a>
      relatively large amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">
       protein
      </a>
      molecules from aqueous solutions. Consequently, bentonite is uniquely useful in the process of
      <a href="/wiki/Winemaking" title="Winemaking">
       winemaking
      </a>
      , where it is used to remove excessive amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Proteins_in_wine" title="Proteins in wine">
       protein
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/White_wine" title="White wine">
       white wines
      </a>
      . Were it not for this use of bentonite, many or most white wines would precipitate undesirable
      <a href="/wiki/Flocculation" title="Flocculation">
       flocculent
      </a>
      clouds or hazes upon exposure to warmer temperatures, as these proteins
      <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">
       denature
      </a>
      . It also has the incidental use of inducing more rapid
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clarification_(wine)" title="Clarification (wine)">
       clarification
      </a>
      of both red and white wines.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Absorbent">
       Absorbent
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Absorbent">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bentonite is used in a variety of pet care items such as cat litter to absorb the odour and surround the feces. It is also used to absorb oils and grease.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Groundwater_barrier">
       Groundwater barrier
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Groundwater barrier">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The property of swelling on contact with water makes sodium bentonite useful as a sealant, since it provides a self-sealing, low permeability barrier. It is used to line the base of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">
       landfills
      </a>
      to prevent migration of
      <a href="/wiki/Leachate" title="Leachate">
       leachate
      </a>
      , for quarantining metal pollutants of groundwater, and for the sealing of subsurface disposal systems for spent nuclear fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similar uses include making
      <a href="/wiki/Slurry_wall" title="Slurry wall">
       slurry walls
      </a>
      , waterproofing of below-grade walls, and forming other impermeable barriers, e.g., to seal off the
      <a href="/wiki/Annulus_(oil_well)" title="Annulus (oil well)">
       annulus
      </a>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">
       water well
      </a>
      , to plug old wells.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentonite can also be "sandwiched" between synthetic materials to create
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geo-synthetic_clay_liners_(GCL)" title="Geo-synthetic clay liners (GCL)">
       geo-synthetic clay liners (GCL)
      </a>
      for the aforementioned purposes. This technique allows for more convenient transport and installation, and it greatly reduces the volume of bentonite required.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is also used to form a barrier around newly planted trees to constrain root growth so as to prevent damage to nearby pipes, footpaths and other infrastructure. Farmers use bentonite to seal retention ponds.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">
       Medical
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Medical">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bentonite has been prescribed as a bulk laxative, and it is also used as a base for many dermatologic formulas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Granular bentonite is being studied for use in battlefield wound dressings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentoquatam is a bentonate-based topical medication intended to act as a shield against exposure to
      <a href="/wiki/Urushiol" title="Urushiol">
       urushiol
      </a>
      , the oil found in plants such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_ivy" title="Poison ivy">
       poison ivy
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_oak" title="Poison oak">
       poison oak
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentonite can also be used as a desiccant due to its adsorption properties. Bentonite desiccants have been successfully used to protect pharmaceutical, nutraceutical and diagnostic products from moisture degradation and extend shelf life. In fact, in the most common package environments, Bentonite Desiccants offer a higher adsorption capacity than silica gel desiccants. Bentonite complies with the FDA for contact with food and drugs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Thai_farming">
       In Thai farming
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In Thai farming">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The application of clay technology by farmers in Northeast Thailand, using bentonite clay, has dramatically reversed soil degradation and resulted in greater economic returns, with higher yields and higher output prices. Studies carried out by The
      <a href="/wiki/International_Water_Management_Institute" title="International Water Management Institute">
       International Water Management Institute
      </a>
      and partners in 20022003 focused on the application of locally sourced bentonite clays to degraded soils in the region. These applications were carried out in structured field trials. Results from these studies showed that applying bentonite clays effectively improved yields of forage
      <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">
       sorghum
      </a>
      grown under
      <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">
       rain
      </a>
      -fed conditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bentonite application also influenced the
      <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">
       prices
      </a>
      that farmers received for their crops. Production costs are higher, but, due to more production and the quality of the food, clay farmers could afford to invest and grow more and better food, compared to non clay-using farmers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bentonite_slurry_walls_in_modern_construction">
       Bentonite slurry walls in modern construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bentonite slurry walls in modern construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bentonite
      <a href="/wiki/Slurry_wall" title="Slurry wall">
       slurry walls
      </a>
      (also known as diaphragm walls
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) are used in construction, where the slurry wall is a trench filled with a thick
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloidal" title="Colloidal">
       colloidal
      </a>
      mixture of Bentonite and water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GwSlurryWall1994_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GwSlurryWall1994-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A trench that would collapse due to the hydraulic pressure in the surrounding soil does not collapse as the slurry balances the hydraulic pressure. Forms for concrete, and rebar, can be assembled in a slurry filled trench, and then have concrete poured into the form. The liquid concrete being denser displaces the less-dense bentonite slurry and causes the latter to overflow from the trench. This displaced bentonite slurry is then channelled to a recycling unit from which it can subsequently be re-used in a new trench elsewhere on the construction site.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition, because the colloid is relatively impervious to water, a slurry wall can prevent the seepage of groundwater, which is useful in preventing the further spread of groundwater that has been contaminated by toxic material like industrial waste.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GwSlurryWall1994_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GwSlurryWall1994-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_natural_occurrence">
       History and natural occurrence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bentonite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History and natural occurrence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_bentonite_production" title="List of countries by bentonite production">
       List of countries by bentonite production
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 2011, U.S. was the top producer of bentonite with almost one-third world share followed by China and Greece.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Most high-grade natural sodium bentonite is produced from the western United States in an area between the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Hills" title="Black Hills">
       Black Hills
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">
       South Dakota
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Bighorn_Basin" title="Bighorn Basin">
       Bighorn Basin
      </a>
      of Wyoming and Tokat Resadiye region of Turkey.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mixed sodium/calcium bentonite is mined in
      <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
       Greece
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States, calcium bentonite is mined primarily in
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">
       Mississippi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">
       Alabama
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hosterman_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hosterman-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other major locations producing calcium bentonite include Germany, Greece, Turkey, India, and China.
     </p>
