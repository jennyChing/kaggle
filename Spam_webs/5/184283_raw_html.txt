        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.orientalwatchsite.com/10-tips-to-keep-your-automatic-mechanical-watch-in-tip-top-shape/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link: 10 Tips to Keep Your Automatic Mechanical Watches in Tip-top Shape">
          10 Tips to Keep Your Automatic Mechanical Watches in Tip-top Shape
         </a>
        </h2>
        <small>
         March 3rd, 2010
         <!-- by marlee -->
         |
         <a href="#comments" title="View all comments">
          3 Comments
         </a>
        </small>
        <p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Watch trouble got you down? Use these tips to get your automatic mechanical watch back on track, and keep it there.
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <h3>
         1. Say no to watch winders.
        </h3>
        <p>
         These convenient items are about as useful as those plastic boxes that rewound your VHS tapes as though a VCR didnt do the job well enough. Watch winders can (and probably will) wear out your watch a lot faster than wearing or manually winding your watch as needed. Of course, a more complicated watch is an exception.
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <h3>
         2. Dont touch!
        </h3>
        <p>
         The internal parts of automatic or
         <a href="http://www.orientwatchusa.com/" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outbound/article/www.orientwatchusa.com');" target="blank">
          mechanical watches
         </a>
         are very susceptible to rust, and rust is the number 1 watch killer. To decrease the chances of rust, dont touch internal watch parts with bare fingers. Wear gloves or have your watch serviced by a professional watch maker.
         <br/>
        </p>
        <center>
         <img alt="rusty watch" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4534" height="225" src="http://www.orientalwatchsite.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/03/rusty-watch.jpg" title="rusty watch" width="300"/>
        </center>
        <br/>
        Blowing on watch parts will also introduce moisture and probably result in rust, so instead, use micro tools or find a professional watch maker to remove dust or particles.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <h3>
         3. Beware of water resistance.
        </h3>
        <p>
         Even if a watch has 100m
         <a href="http://www.chronocentric.com/watches/wresist.shtml" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outbound/article/www.chronocentric.com');" target="blank">
          water resistance
         </a>
         , you should still be cautious about lengthy immersions in water.
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres a little water resistance advice to follow:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          30m= accidental splashes/spills
         </li>
         <p>
          <soft_br>
          </soft_br>
         </p>
         <li>
          50m= shallow swimming in small doses (but it should really be avoided)
         </li>
         <p>
          <soft_br>
          </soft_br>
         </p>
         <li>
          100m or 10 BAR= swimming, snorkeling
         </li>
         <p>
          <soft_br>
          </soft_br>
         </p>
         <li>
          200m or 20 BAR= swimming, snorkeling, diving
         </li>
         <p>
          <soft_br>
          </soft_br>
         </p>
         <li>
          300m or 30 BAR (or higher)= swimming, snorkeling, diving, saturation diving
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         But no matter your water resistance, watches and showers dont usually mix. Soap, shampoo or other shower accessories can cause build ups and damage in and around your watch.
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <h3>
         4. If you really love your watch, youd take it off sometimes.
        </h3>
        <p>
         Everyday activities can cause watch damage. For example, cooking, kneading or cutting up food can cause particle build ups in tiny, hard to reach watch crevices (specifically the watch band).
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <center>
         <img alt="89647060" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-4539" height="198" src="http://www.orientalwatchsite.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/03/89647060-300x198.jpg" title="89647060" width="300"/>
        </center>
        <br/>
        <br/>
        These activities can also introduce moisture or oil in or around your watch. Working with dangerous chemicals while wearing your watch is also a bad idea. So to help keep your watch clean, intact and rust-free, take it off sometimes.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <h3>
         5. Practice good watch care, get 3-7 years of watch luck.
        </h3>
        <p>
         Even though youre feeling fine, yearly check-ups are common medical practice. Same goes for your watch (sort of). Its a good idea to get your watch serviced every 3-7 years because the oils that keep the movement fresh and working do dry up even if you dont wear your watch often.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, watch service is really on an as needed basis because it can be costly, so unless its life threatening, consider holding off or doing it yourself. After all, there is no insurance company that will cover the bill, especially if your watch is old and unwarranted.
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <h3>
         6. Remember: garages are for storage, not watches.
        </h3>
        <p>
         Keeping your watch rust-free also means keeping it in a clean, dry place. This means that watches should never be stored in a garage or basement as these places are more susceptible to dirt, dampness and playful animals like cats or mice who like to break fragile things (e.g. watches or glassware).
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <center>
         <img alt="83454846" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-4536" height="194" src="http://www.orientalwatchsite.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/03/83454846-300x194.jpg" title="83454846" width="300"/>
        </center>
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <h3>
         7. Dont test your watch.
        </h3>
        <p>
         Some people like to live dangerously. Part of living dangerously is seeing how far you or your possessions can go. For example,
         <em>
          lets see how many
          <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I_ClsVjdlog" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outbound/article/www.youtube.com');" target="blank">
           doughnuts
          </a>
          my car can do on this icy parking lot
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Automatic watches (especially new ones) are built strong and durable, but that doesnt mean you should go out of your way to see what your watch can and cannot do. All watches are built and tested by the makers before theyre sold to you; all you have to do is wear it and take care of it.
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <h3>
         8. Make friends with a watch maker.
        </h3>
        <p>
         Find a good, trustworthy watchmaker before your watch needs service or repair. For a listing of certified watch makers in the U.S., visit the American Watchmakers-Clockmakers Institute
         <a href="http://www.watch-clock-makers.org/" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outbound/article/www.watch-clock-makers.org');" target="blank">
          directory
         </a>
         .
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <h3>
         9. Steer clear of magnetic fields.
        </h3>
        <p>
         This mainly applies to older mechanical watches, but its something to take note of. If you expose your watch to a strong magnetic field, it may become magnetized. In that instance, your watch will stop or become erratic. To remedy this, you must demagnetize your watch with, of course, a demagnetizer.
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
        <center>
         <img alt="72724115" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-4535" height="300" src="http://www.orientalwatchsite.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/03/magnet-225x300.jpg" title="72724115" width="225"/>
        </center>
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <h3>
         10. Wear it!
        </h3>
        <p>
         Remember
         <em>
          Bop It
         </em>
         ? Keep this in mind when it comes to your watch, and
         <em>
          Wear It
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Wearing your
         <a href="http://www.orientwatchusa.com/" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outbound/article/www.orientwatchusa.com');" target="blank">
          automatic watch
         </a>
         recharges it, and keeps it in good time-telling shape. But remember to give your watch a break every now and then (e.g. at night or when working with something that could ruin it, see #4).
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <em>
          <strong>
           Do you have a tip for automatic mechanical watch care? Share it below.
          </strong>
         </em>

         <br/>

         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
