          10 common grammar mistakes even great writers make
         </h1>
        </header>

          <p>
           Heres a secret about great writers:
           <a href="http://www.nownovel.com/blog/take-charge-plot-writing-story-beginning-end/">
            they may be wonderful at plotting
           </a>
           , imagery, building suspense and character development, but they dont all excel at the nuts and bolts and grammar. However, the cleaner your manuscript, the more professional youll look to agents and editors, so read on for common grammar mistakes you can learn to identify and avoid.
          </p>
          <p>
           Now, its true that in creative and fiction writing, there is a great deal more latitude to play with grammar and other standard English conventions than in other types of writing. As the saying goes though, you need to learn the rules before you can break them. Its one thing to make a deliberate grammatical choice versus making an error out of ignorance. Furthermore, there would be little stylistic justification for making any of the grammar errors listed below.
          </p>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Who and whom  This is an easy mistake to make, but its also easily corrected. Who is used for the subject of a sentence and whom for the object. Heres the easy part. If you can substitute he, she, or another noun, who should be used; if instead you would use her or him, whom is your word.
           <i>
            Who is the best teacher? To whom should I give the best teacher award?
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Who and that  This is a common error but even more easily corrected than who and whom. Use who for people and that for things.
           <i>
            Adam is the one who asked about the engagement ring. This is the engagement ring that he meant.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Which and that  This one is a bit more confusing. Use that when it is essential to the sentence. Use which when you are qualifying information that is not necessarily essential.
           <i>
            I dont vacation at hotels that dont allow pets. This hotel, which has a nice beach view, is not a good option for me because it doesnt allow pets.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Lay and lie  This one causes a lot of confusion because the past tense of lie is lay. In present tense, lie is something you do and does not require an object while lay is something you do to something else. The past tense of lie is lay, and the past tense of lay is laid.
           <i>
            The dog lies on the bed. She lay on the bed all day yesterday. Margaret lays the onions on the counter. She laid the onions on the counter yesterday.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Dangling modifiers  The clause at the
           <a href="http://www.nownovel.com/blog/first-sentences-first-paragraphs/">
            beginning of a sentence
           </a>
           has to have the same subject as the sentence itself.
           <i>
            Walking down the street, the trees were battered by last nights storm
           </i>
           tells us that the trees themselves were walking down the street. Corrected, it might read like this:
           <i>
            Walking down the street, we noticed that the trees were battered by last nights storm.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Fewer and less  This is another common but easily-fixed error. Fewer refers to things that can be counted; less is for things that cannot be counted.
           <i>
            Id like less milk in my coffee and fewer than three spoonfuls of sugar.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Me and I 
           <a href="http://www.nownovel.com/blog/writing-about-relationships-how-to-make-them-real/">
            Often when writing about
           </a>
           oneself and another person, writers default to I believing it to always be correct. However, I should only be used if it is the subject of the sentence, not the object. The easiest way to figure this one out is to drop the other person from the sentence and see which word fits.
           <i>
            Sharon
           </i>
           <i>
            and I flew to Los Angeles. The film director gave the script to Sharon and me.
           </i>
           Note that if Sharon and is removed from both sentences, the right choice becomes obvious.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Like and as though  These two are not interchangeable although like is frequently used improperly in place of as though. Like can only be followed by a noun or pronoun. As though can only be followed by a verb clause.
           <i>
            She looks like my mother. I cried as though I had lost my best friend.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Passive voice error I  Many people make common grammar mistakes by assuming that a passive sentence is any sentence that uses a form of the verb to be. In fact, passive voice is simply a sentence in which the object appears as the subject of the sentence.
           <i>
            The house was built in 1825.
           </i>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Passive voice error II  Many people believe passive voice is always bad. In fact, passive voice can be used effectively to convey a certain rhythm or mood. It is also unavoidable when the person or thing that performed an action is unknown as in the previous example with the house.
          </li>
         </ul>

          What kind of grammar errors drive you mad?
