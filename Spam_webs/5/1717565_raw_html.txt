the most important aspects of prevention and control is identifying the source. Researchers and public health officials work tirelessly to find the reservoir in the hopes of bringing the scourge to an end.  Sometimes, the hunt can take some surprising turns and reveal unexpected and at ties unprecedented characteristics.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EOver the last few months, these efforts have been widely publicized for three specific pathogens.  In West Africa, the hunt for the origin of the Ebola virus has led to the identification of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.msf.ca\/en\/article\/ebola-epidemic-guinea-%E2%80%9Cthere-no-treatment-and-no-vaccine-priority-isolate-suspected-cases%E2%80%9D\u0022\u003Efruit bats\u003C\/a\u003E as the likely source although \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.bushmeat.org\/bushmeat_and_wildlife_trade\/what_is_the_bushmeat_crisis\u0022\u003Ebushmeat\u003C\/a\u003E may also be the origin.  In the Gulf Peninsula, the hunt for carriers of the Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS) virus has brought a number of potential conclusions.  Studies have shown the virus may be in \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/pmid\/24964193\/\u0022\u003Ecamels\u003C\/a\u003E yet there is still \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/mbio.asm.org\/content\/5\/4\/e01430-14.long\u0022\u003Edebate\u003C\/a\u003E as to whether this means they are the true source. Most recently, a \u003Cem\u003ESalmonella\u003C\/em\u003E outbreak in the United Kingdom led officials to scour every option to find the cause of over two hundred infections.  Eventually, they found the source in \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.bbc.co.uk\/news\/health-28903195\u0022\u003Eeggs\u003C\/a\u003E and efforts began to bring the outbreak under control.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EIn all of these cases, one or a few options are identified and then tracing can proceed.  Yet, there are other situations in which the potential source is not singular or even limited to a few options.  In some cases, the microbial cause can be so ubiquitous it can be found everywhere on Earth.  Such an example was \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.plospathogens.org\/article\/info:doi\/10.1371\/journal.ppat.1004277\u0022\u003Edescribed\u003C\/a\u003E last week involving a very common fungal skin pathogen, \u003Cem\u003E\u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC358230\/\u0022\u003EMalassezia\u003C\/a\u003E\u003C\/em\u003E.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003E\u003Cem\u003EMalassezia\u003C\/em\u003E has been known for well over a century.  It is a yeast-like fungus named after the French anatomist Louis-Charles Malassez, who is best known for creating the cell-counting microscopic chamber known as a \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/futurescienceleaders.org\/researchers2012\/2013\/01\/louis-charles-malassez-inventor-of-the-hemacytometer\/\u0022\u003Ehemocytometer\u003C\/a\u003E. However, other mammalian \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.plospathogens.org\/article\/info%3Adoi%2F10.1371%2Fjournal.ppat.1003892\u0022\u003Especies\u003C\/a\u003E are susceptible to disease, including dogs, cats, horses, rabbits, pigs, goats, sheep, elephants and even primates.  The widespread nature of the fungus suggests the source, unlike Ebola, MERS and \u003Cem\u003ESalmonella\u003C\/em\u003E, had to be ubiquitous.  Unfortunately, there was no common possibility and as such, a broader search would have to proceed.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EEnter the realm of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/pmid\/16214739\/\u0022\u003EDNA barcoding\u003C\/a\u003E. The practice has become an important part of developing a catalogue of all life and has become \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.researchgate.net\/publication\/43202410_Current_state_and_perspectives_of_fungal_DNA_barcoding_and_rapid_identification_procedures\/file\/9fcfd5097ddbc80190.pdf\u0022\u003Eimportant\u003C\/a\u003E particularly in the incredibly diverse fungi.  When environmental samples were taken from a plethora of sites, including deep-sea sediments, hydrothermal vents, fish species and even Antarctic soils, one species found in humans, \u003Cem\u003EM. restricta\u003C\/em\u003E was discovered.  Closer analysis of the samples revealed the species was nearly identical in nature to the one found on human skin.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003ETaking the exploration further, the authors compared the sequences of collected fungi from the various regions.  The results were once again incredible; there was little effect of environment on the genetic similarities.  Marine and terrestrial microbes were interdigitated within the same clade suggesting they had travelled back and forth between the two environments.  This also suggested that the fungi had developed mechanisms to withstand almost any type of environment, whether the pressures of the deeps or the few minutes of dandruff shampoo.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThough the data was remarkable, it offered more questions than answers.  The fungus was no doubt one of the most widespread microbes on Earth and had adapted to thrive in almost any environment.  In many marine environments, it was a dominant species although rarely caused infection suggesting symbiosis.  How therefore it became a pathogen for mammals remained unanswered as did the means by which it found its way to the animal world.  What was clear, however, was that further study was needed to determine the impact on the world and human lives.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EWhile in the context of pathogens and epidemics, the identification of the global presence of \u003Cem\u003EMalessezia\u003C\/em\u003E may appear to be academic in nature.  However, as more emerging pathogens begin to make their way into the human realm, this study offers a unique perspective.  While we may believe we have a good understanding of where a pathogen may originate, using a wider scope may reveal new information to not only increase our knowledge but also provide direction as to Modifications: Jason Tetro\u003C\/p\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003Cp\u003EUp until recently, anyone suffering from high cholesterol had a fairly easy option to improve health. All they needed was a prescription for a group of chemicals known as statins.  For decades, this was the go-to drug to \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC3704635\/\u0022\u003Ecost-effectively\u003C\/a\u003E keep low density lipoprotein (LDL) levels in check and prevent the onset of cardiovascular disease.  Although this view has changed somewhat with the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.fda.gov\/ForConsumers\/ConsumerUpdates\/ucm293330.htm\u0022\u003EFDA\u003C\/a\u003E providing advice on the potential risks associated with their use, the number of people taking these \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/nchstats.com\/2013\/11\/14\/statistics-on-statin-use\/\u0022\u003Edrugs\u003C\/a\u003E continues to be high.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EWhile the majority of research on statins appears to be focused solely on cholesterol, researchers have examined other potential benefits.  Some include the formation of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC209366\/\u0022\u003Enew blood vessels\u003C\/a\u003E and an \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC2694580\/\u0022\u003Eimprovement\u003C\/a\u003E in immune, bone and central nervous systems .  But one of the most studied has been the antimicrobial effects of these drugs.  For some reason, their inclusion seems to help the chances of infection prevention and control.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EOne of the first mention of statins as antimicrobials came out in \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.cid.oxfordjournals.org\/cgi\/pmidlookup?view=long\u0026amp;pmid=11565076\u0022\u003E2001\u003C\/a\u003E when their use appeared to help reduce the mortality rate in people suffering from sepsis.  The effect was so pronounced, the drugs were considered as \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/circ.ahajournals.org\/cgi\/pmidlookup?view=long\u0026amp;pmid=15824210\u0022\u003Epotential therapy\u003C\/a\u003E for this life-threatening disease.  As the evidence grew, so did the experiments in the laboratory.  In 2008, the effect of statins on one of the causes of sepsis, \u003Cem\u003E\u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/jac.oxfordjournals.org\/cgi\/pmidlookup?view=long\u0026amp;pmid=18086693\u0022\u003EStaphylococcus aureus\u003C\/a\u003E\u003C\/em\u003E,      was shown.  It even had an effect on the antimicrobial resistant version, Methicillin Resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA).\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EWith the knowledge of the effects during the most critical infections, researchers sought to identify whether the drugs could help those who might not be at immediate risk. In 2014, the drug was even suggested to be an important part of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/cardiovascres.oxfordjournals.org\/content\/102\/3\/362.long\u0022\u003Ecardiovascular disease reduction\u003C\/a\u003E by simply preventing microbial-based inflammation.   The reason stemmed from the ability of the drugs to stop the development of S. aureus colonies, \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC4447369\/\u0022\u003Ebiofilms\u003C\/a\u003E, which is a critical step in the development of infections of the skin and the blood.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EBut \u003Cem\u003ES. aureus\u003C\/em\u003E wasn\u2019t the only target.  Other studies have revealed the antimicrobial effects against pathogens such as \u003Cem\u003E\u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC4187984\/\u0022\u003EMycobacterium tuberculosis\u003C\/a\u003E\u003C\/em\u003E, the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC4244940\/\u0022\u003Einfluenza virus\u003C\/a\u003E, and the worm, \u003Cem\u003E\u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC3906178\/\u0022\u003ESchistosoma mansoni\u003C\/a\u003E\u003C\/em\u003E. In each case, the drugs seemed to have a direct effect on the microbe to keep it from causing an infection or at least allowing it to worsen.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThere is another potential protective mechanism against pathogens.  This time, the target is yeast, particularly \u003Cem\u003ECandida\u003C\/em\u003E.  This particular species is known to cause both skin infections as well as the bloodborne infection called candidemia.  Without immediate intervention, the latter can be life-threatening.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe first hints of yeast-blocking activity came in \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC2894022\/\u0022\u003E2010\u003C\/a\u003E when statins appeared to help reduce the mortality rate.  This was echoed in \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC3796506\/\u0022\u003E2013\u003C\/a\u003E, although the results were not conclusive.  Though there was apparently some benefit, the actual contribution of the drug could not be determined.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003ENow there is evidence statins have the same anti-biofilm effect on yeast as they do on bacteria.  Last week, a Brazilian team of researchers \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.sciencedirect.com\/science\/article\/pii\/S1413867015001038\u0022\u003Epublished\u003C\/a\u003E their analysis of the effects of the drugs on \u003Cem\u003ECandida\u003C\/em\u003E and another genus, \u003Cem\u003ECryptococcus\u003C\/em\u003E.  Their results suggest the previous results were actually due to the drug and may offer hope for the development of novel therapies.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe group used 51 strains of \u003Cem\u003ECandida\u003C\/em\u003E and 25 strains of \u003Cem\u003ECryptococcus\u003C\/em\u003E for the test.  As for the statins, three were chosen, simvastatin, atorvastatin, and pravastatin.  The team also included antifungal drugs such as amphotericin B, itraconazole and fluconazole to act both as individual controls and also as additive for combinatorial therapy.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe testing followed to particular paths.  The first attempted to determine the effect of statins on individual cells, called planktonic.  The second focused on the formation of biofilms.  If all worked out well, the drugs on their own would both kill the yeast and consequently prevent biofilm formation.  In case this didn\u2019t work, the combination option was expected to be effective.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe results were somewhat surprising.  Out of the three statins, simvastatin came out on top.  It killed \u003Cem\u003ECandida\u003C\/em\u003E planktonic cells and also prevented biofilm formation.  The effect was solely due to the drug as combinations with antifungals had no improved effect.  The authors suggested the drug prevented the yeast from being able to properly function by both causing defects in the outside membrane as well as the internal mitochondria.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EWhen it came to \u003Cem\u003ECryptococcus\u003C\/em\u003E, the results were similar.  The only difference was a noticeable synergy between simvastatin and amphotericin B.  The authors believed this was due to the different routes of antimicrobial activity working together although an exact mechanism wasn\u2019t elucidated.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe authors concluded simvastatin was by far the best potential antifungal statin and that further research would be well worth following.  As to the reason why this particular drug was better than the other two, there was no explanation. Yet, the answer may be found in the original nature of the statins.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAlthough simvastatin, atorvastatin, and pravastatin are synthetic, they are based on a class of naturally-derived molecules found in fungus.  Simvastatin is based on a molecule \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.sciencedirect.com\/science\/article\/pii\/S1074552106003413\u0022\u003Elovastatin\u003C\/a\u003E derived from \u003Cem\u003EAspergillus terreus\u003C\/em\u003E.  Pravastatin is a \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pubmed\/9704102\u0022\u003Emodification\u003C\/a\u003E of a molecule from \u003Cem\u003EPenicillium citrinum\u003C\/em\u003E.  Both these species have the ability to fight off yeast in the wild and as a result the derived statins may still harbour the antifungal activity.  As for atorvastatin, it a completely synthetic molecule developed through \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.sciencedirect.com\/science\/article\/pii\/S0079646808700808\u0022\u003Emolecular modelling\u003C\/a\u003E.  As a result, the antimicrobial activity may not have been included in the final product.\u003C\/p\u003E  \u003C\/div\u003E\n  \u003C!--\u003C\/div\u003E--\u003E\n\n  \n  \u003C\/div\u003E\n\u003Cdiv class=\u0022field-credit\u0022\u003E\u003Cp\u003ESource: Wikipedia\u003C\/p\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003Cp\u003ECuts, scrapes, and gashes can take several days to weeks to heal. To speed up the process, history has provided a list of options.  Some are fairly appreciable, such as \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC1313538\/\u0022\u003Ealoe vera\u003C\/a\u003E and \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC3609166\/\u0022\u003Ehoney\u003C\/a\u003E. Others are somewhat more extreme such as the rather aesthetically unpleasing technique \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC3976885\/\u0022\u003Emaggot-induced wound healing\u003C\/a\u003E.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EBut one that has stood the test of time \u2013 and for some urban legend \u2013 is the use of frog skin secretions to improve regeneration.  The trick is simple.  After a wound has occurred, find a frog, rub it on the wound, and prepare to be healed. The idea hails from the apparent ability of amphibians to \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.sciencedirect.com\/science\/article\/pii\/S0301468114000061\u0022\u003Eregenerate without scarring\u003C\/a\u003E. Although this exact procedure has not yet been certified as being credible, there have been many studies in the laboratory to provide support for developing a medically-appropriate alternative.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EOne of the first revelations about the power of frog skin came in \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.pnas.org\/content\/84\/15\/5449.short\u0022\u003E1987\u003C\/a\u003E. At the time, researchers discovered a number of proteins known as antimicrobial peptides (AMPs). They came from the African clawed frog and had the ability to inhibit a variety of wound pathogens including bacteria, fungi and even amebae.  The discovery suggested frog skin secretions could improve human laceration repair.  This however was not tested in a clinical environment.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003ESince this discovery, a number of other AMPs have been found in a \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.sciencedirect.com\/science\/article\/pii\/S0005273608003118\u0022\u003Evariety of frog species\u003C\/a\u003E suggesting these molecules may be more widespread than usual.  Their abundance also offered the opportunity to initiate screening for wound-healing AMPs for future harnessing and possible production.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EBut while AMPs have been associated with frogs, their involvement in the actual healing process has until recently remained a mystery. Most of the healing properties of frogs seem to be due to the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC2918614\/\u0022\u003Elipids\u003C\/a\u003E on their skin and in the secretions.  Analysis of AMPs at the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.sciencedirect.com\/science\/article\/pii\/S0005273609000054\u0022\u003Enuclear level\u003C\/a\u003E only offered hints as to their involvement.  Yet no exact mechanism could be identified. In essence, their contribution was limited to preventing microbes from gaining the upper hand in a vulnerable cellular environment.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EBut that changed last year with the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov\/pmc\/articles\/PMC3993219\/\u0022\u003Ediscovery\u003C\/a\u003E of a separate function for these AMPs. A laboratory examination revealed two specific types, Temporin A and B, appeared to help the cellular healing process.  The specific mechanism involved the attraction of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.nature.com\/jid\/journal\/v127\/n5\/full\/5700786a.html\u0022\u003Ekeratinocytes\u003C\/a\u003E, which are needed to ensure proper skin healing.  The study suggested the key to this stimulation was an interaction with the cellular epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR).  Yet the actual process by which a frog-protein called out for human cells was not answered.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003ENow, an answer may be at hand.  Last week, the same team of researchers \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/journals.plos.org\/plosone\/article?id=10.1371\/journal.pone.0128663\u0022\u003Erevealed\u003C\/a\u003E even more about this cross-species symbiosis may occur.  Their work suggests AMPs interact with more than just microbes.  They apparently can guide and drive human cell behaviour as well.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe experiments involved a different AMP than temporin. The test molecule was from a family known as the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/onlinelibrary.wiley.com\/doi\/10.1046\/j.1432-1327.1999.00597.x\/full\u0022\u003Eesculentins\u003C\/a\u003E. The reason for the switch was due to the formation of an in-house variety called esculentin-1a(1-21)NH2.  This synthetic version of the original was already known to be highly effective at killing several bacterial pathogens as well as the formation of biofilms.  It could also be easily traced to figure out what might be happening at the molecular level.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EWith AMPs in hand, the team introduced the molecule to skin-like cell cultures.  They tested at first the toxicity of the modified esculentin \u2013 it wasn\u2019t \u2013 and then whether it would lead to migration like the temporins \u2013 it did.  With these fundamental steps completed, they could begin to identify wound-healing activities.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe means to accomplish this goal involved scratching the skin-like cells such to create a pseudo-laceration. Once the cut was made, the researchers examined the time necessary to re-close the wound.  Normally, after 12 hours, the wound would be about 85% healed.  But when the AMP was added, that jumped to 100% with nanomolecular amounts.  This was to some extent expected but showed the value of esculentin.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAs to how the AMP accomplished this task, the results were fascinating.  The molecule appeared to enter the cells and stimulate a variety of genetic changes while never actually entering the nucleus.  Though the actual events on the inside were not elucidated, the mere fact this protein somehow found its way into the cells suggested even more might be happening than previously thought.  There was definitely more room for exploration in the future to determine the intracellular targets and their contribution to overall healing.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe results of this particular study may offer more questions than answers in terms of the actual mechanism of healing promotion.  Yet the results do suggest AMPs may have more benefit than simply keeping out pathogens.                <h2>
                Under The Microscope
               </h2>
               <div class="field-subtitle">
                <p>
                 Revealing our intimate relationships with microbial life. By Jason "the Germ Guy" Tetro

           <div class="content-onecol one-col" id="mini-panel-node_header">
            <div>
             <h1>
              A Truly Global Germ: The Case of Malassezia
             </h1>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">
              <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
              <span class="author">
               <span class="label">
                By
               </span>
               <a datatype="" href="/popsci-authors/jason-tetro-0" property="prefLabel" typeof="Concept">
                Jason Tetro
               </a>
              </span>
              <span class="date" data-timestamp="1409084502">
               <span class="label">
                posted
               </span>
               Aug 26th, 2014 at 4:21pm
              </span>
              <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
          </div>
          <div class="panel-pane pane-block pane-post-social-post-social-block">
           <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
           <div class="postButtons" style="float:left;">
           </div>
           <a class="customIcon pw-widget-customIcon" href="#" name="customIcon">
            <img alt="" data-src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/all/modules/bonnier/post_social/theme/post_icon_24.png" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
            <noscript>
             <img alt="" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/all/modules/bonnier/post_social/theme/post_icon_24.png" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
            </noscript>
           </a>
           <span class="pw-counter-custom1">
            <span class="pw-button-counter_custom1">
             <b class="customCounter" style="font-weight: normal!important;">
              0
             </b>
            </span>
           </span>
           <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
          </div>
         </header>
         <article class="content-basic basic-sidebar">
          <div class="content-main basic-sidebar">
           <div class="panel-pane pane-entity-field pane-node-body">
            <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2014/images/2014/08/popsci-malassezia.jpg?itok=n-ZsRzYJ" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/images/2014/08/popsci-malassezia.jpg?itok=7uJ9XJQ0" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2014/images/2014/08/popsci-malassezia.jpg?itok=pSRrs9PJ" height="667" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="400" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2014/images/2014/08/popsci-malassezia.jpg?itok=7uJ9XJQ0" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                The Ubiquitous Malassezia and its various hosts
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                Source: Wikipedia; Modifications: Jason Tetro
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Whenever a pathogen is discovered, one of the most important aspects of prevention and control is identifying the source. Researchers and public health officials work tirelessly to find the reservoir in the hopes of bringing the scourge to an end.  Sometimes, the hunt can take some surprising turns and reveal unexpected and at ties unprecedented characteristics.
             </p>
             <p>
              Over the last few months, these efforts have been widely publicized for three specific pathogens.  In West Africa, the hunt for the origin of the Ebola virus has led to the identification of
              <a href="http://www.msf.ca/en/article/ebola-epidemic-guinea-%E2%80%9Cthere-no-treatment-and-no-vaccine-priority-isolate-suspected-cases%E2%80%9D">
               fruit bats
              </a>
              as the likely source although
              <a href="http://www.bushmeat.org/bushmeat_and_wildlife_trade/what_is_the_bushmeat_crisis">
               bushmeat
              </a>
              may also be the origin.  In the Gulf Peninsula, the hunt for carriers of the Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS) virus has brought a number of potential conclusions.  Studies have shown the virus may be in
              <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/pmid/24964193/">
               camels
              </a>
              yet there is still
              <a href="http://mbio.asm.org/content/5/4/e01430-14.long">
               debate
              </a>
              as to whether this means they are the true source. Most recently, a
              <em>
               Salmonella
              </em>
              outbreak in the United Kingdom led officials to scour every option to find the cause of over two hundred infections.  Eventually, they found the source in
              <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/health-28903195">
               eggs
              </a>
              and efforts began to bring the outbreak under control.
             </p>
             <p>
              In all of these cases, one or a few options are identified and then tracing can proceed.  Yet, there are other situations in which the potential source is not singular or even limited to a few options.  In some cases, the microbial cause can be so ubiquitous it can be found everywhere on Earth.  Such an example was
              <a href="http://www.plospathogens.org/article/info:doi/10.1371/journal.ppat.1004277">
               described
              </a>
              last week involving a very common fungal skin pathogen,
              <em>
               <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC358230/">
                Malassezia
               </a>
              </em>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              <em>
               Malassezia
              </em>
              has been known for well over a century.  It is a yeast-like fungus named after the French anatomist Louis-Charles Malassez, who is best known for creating the cell-counting microscopic chamber known as a
              <a href="http://futurescienceleaders.org/researchers2012/2013/01/louis-charles-malassez-inventor-of-the-hemacytometer/">
               hemocytometer
              </a>
              . Originally described in
              <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1016/j.femsyr.2005.05.006/full">
               1889
              </a>
              by Henri Baillon, the genus has expanded from one species,
              <em>
               M. furfur
              </em>
              , to over a
              <a href="http://eol.org/pages/32138/hierarchy_entries/36101979/overview">
               dozen
              </a>
              different species.
             </p>
             <p>
              The fungus is known to cause a few complications in humans, most notably dandruff and eczema.  However, other mammalian
              <a href="http://www.plospathogens.org/article/info%3Adoi%2F10.1371%2Fjournal.ppat.1003892">
               species
              </a>
              are susceptible to disease, including dogs, cats, horses, rabbits, pigs, goats, sheep, elephants and even primates.  The widespread nature of the fungus suggests the source, unlike Ebola, MERS and
              <em>
               Salmonella
              </em>
              , had to be ubiquitous.  Unfortunately, there was no common possibility and as such, a broader search would have to proceed.
             </p>
             <p>
              Enter the realm of
              <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/pmid/16214739/">
               DNA barcoding
              </a>
              . The practice has become an important part of developing a catalogue of all life and has become
              <a href="http://www.researchgate.net/publication/43202410_Current_state_and_perspectives_of_fungal_DNA_barcoding_and_rapid_identification_procedures/file/9fcfd5097ddbc80190.pdf">
               important
              </a>
              particularly in the incredibly diverse fungi.  When environmental samples were taken from a plethora of sites, including deep-sea sediments, hydrothermal vents, fish species and even Antarctic soils, one species found in humans,
              <em>
               M. restricta
              </em>
              was discovered.  Closer analysis of the samples revealed the species was nearly identical in nature to the one found on human skin.
             </p>
             <p>
              Taking the exploration further, the authors compared the sequences of collected fungi from the various regions.  The results were once again incredible; there was little effect of environment on the genetic similarities.  Marine and terrestrial microbes were interdigitated within the same clade suggesting they had travelled back and forth between the two environments.  This also suggested that the fungi had developed mechanisms to withstand almost any type of environment, whether the pressures of the deeps or the few minutes of dandruff shampoo.
             </p>
             <p>
              Though the data was remarkable, it offered more questions than answers.  The fungus was no doubt one of the most widespread microbes on Earth and had adapted to thrive in almost any environment.  In many marine environments, it was a dominant species although rarely caused infection suggesting symbiosis.  How therefore it became a pathogen for mammals remained unanswered as did the means by which it found its way to the animal world.  What was clear, however, was that further study was needed to determine the impact on the world and human lives.
             </p>
             <p>
              While in the context of pathogens and epidemics, the identification of the global presence of
              <em>
               Malessezia
              </em>
              may appear to be academic in nature.  However, as more emerging pathogens begin to make their way into the human realm, this study offers a unique perspective.  While we may believe we have a good understanding of where a pathogen may originate, using a wider scope may reveal new information to not only increase our knowledge but also provide direction as to how to control and even prevent future outbreaks.
             </p>
            </div>
