        </h1>

         A

     <section id="article-section-1">
      <div class="embed-wrapper" style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:75.0%;">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="" class=" lazyload" data-src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/rbDluuGS54g" frameborder="0" height="315" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;" width="420">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      Perhaps, like many people of many races, some language in some rap makes you uncomfortable. But perhaps youd nevertheless be categorically more uncomfortable with a bus full of white fraternity men chanting in favor of segregation and the extralegal murder of black people for their race.
      <p>
       On Wednesday's segment of
       <em>
        Morning Joe
       </em>
       , though, the hosts seemed to conflate these two kinds of speech.
       <a href="#mika">
        *
       </a>
       Mika Brzezinski and Joe Scarborough, along with the
       <em>
        Weekly Standard
       </em>
       editor Bill Kristol, discussed the controversy at the University of Oklahoma's chapter of Sigma Alpha Epsilon,
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/education/archive/2015/03/a-brief-and-recent-history-of-bigotry-at-fraternities/387319/">
        which my colleague Terrance Ross explains here
       </a>
       . Members of the fraternitywhose house has been closed, and two of whose members were expelledwere singing things like "There will never be a nigger in SAE! / You can hang him from a tree, but he can never sign with me." But initially, instead of focusing on the fraternity brothers, Brzezinski, Scarborough, and Kristol focused on something different: rap music.
      </p>
     </section>

          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       In particular, Brzezinski singled out Waka Flocka Flame, a goofball Atlanta MC who
       <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/music/2015/mar/10/university-oklahoma-waka-flocka-flame-cancels-show-racist-song-video">
        canceled a planned show at the school
       </a>
       , saying he was "disgusted and disappointed" in SAE's actions. "He shouldn't be disgusted with them, he should be disgusted with himself," she said, noting that Flame uses the n-word in his own lyrics. Later in the show, she backed away from this argument, but her initial comments echoed an often-repeated idea: that black musicians using racial slurs in rap music are no different than white fraternity brothers chanting racial slurs as a community ritual.
      </p>
      <p>
       There have been a few recent, disturbing cases where
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2015/03/when-rap-music-is-a-crime/386938/">
        rappers were prosecuted on the basis of their rhymes
       </a>
       , but in general, people understand that there's a distinction between what Waka Flocka Flame says in songsartistic expression in service of creating a feel or moodand discussions of lynching. If you need a refresher on what exactly lynching entails,
       <a href="http://www.dailykos.com/story/2015/03/09/1369591/-A-deeper-examination-of-the-sheer-joy-of-Oklahoma-students-chanting-about-hanging-niggers-from-trees#">
        you can look at some horrifying images here
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There is some nuance to add to the discussion, and Willie Geist triedmostly without luckto do so. "I'd love to never hear that word again ... but there is a distinction between a bunch of white kids chanting about hanging someone from a tree ... and this is a term that you hear in hip-hop that African American guys sometimes in certain contexts call each other," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Geist raises two important points, one about the use of the slur in rap, and one about the use of the term in general. On the first, it's usefulas so oftento return to the social critic and thinker Chris Rock.
      </p>
      <div class="embed-wrapper" style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:56.3157894737%;">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="" class=" lazyload" data-src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/iau-e6HfOg0" frameborder="0" height="321" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;" width="570">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
       Can white people use the word, he asks? And his answer is, "not really." A good test case and possible exception is rap:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        What are the rules when a Dr. Dre song comes on the radio or plays at a club? What is the procedure that goes into effect? 'Cause sometimes I'm with my white friends and a Dr. Dre song will come on, and there's a lot of "nigga"s in a Dr. Dre song. And they want to enjoy it, but they can't really enjoy it around me. They start taking out the "nigga"s, or mumbling the "nigga"s, and it's just a sad sight to see. I know when I'm not there, they lean into that shit. "He's not here! Turn it up!"
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Rock's rule is that if you're white you can use the word, but
       <em>
        only
       </em>
       if it's in the song. (White folks: You may want to use greater discretion if you're not in fact a friend of Chris Rock's. Kristol suggested, in the course of the
       <em>
        Morning Joe
       </em>
       discussion, that the Oklahoma students were simply repeating what they'd heard, but the Rock Rule doesn't even come into play, since what they were heard chanting is not in fact a rap lyric.)
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector2">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=15" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_entertainment&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=15"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-3">
      <p>
       Geist raised a second and more important point, which is that the context in which the word is used, and the person who does the using, makes a serious difference in the power and connotation of a word. My colleague Ta-Nehisi Coates
       <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/11/24/opinion/sunday/coates-in-defense-of-a-loaded-word.html">
        used an analogy to explain it in 2013
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="448" data-total-count="448" itemprop="articleBody">
        My fathers name is William Paul Coates. I, like my six brothers and sisters, have always addressed him as Dad. Strangers often call him Mr. Coates. His friends call him Paul. If a stranger or one of my fathers friends called him Dad, my father might have a conversation. When I was a child, relatives of my paternal grandmother would call my father Billy. Were I to ever call my father Billy, we would probably have a different conversation.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It's the context that matters, Coates wrote, leading him to conclude that "when [black NBA player] Matt Barnes used the word 'niggas' he was being inappropriate. When [white NFL players] Richie Incognito and Riley Cooper used 'nigger,' they were being violent and offensive."
      </p>
      <p>
       Of course, the SAE case is even more cut and dried than that. This wasn't just about deciding when casual use of a term was unacceptable. It wasn't even just an awful use of the slur. This was about hanging black people.
      </p>
      <p>
       It's important to make careful distinctions between different kinds of speech in conversations about race, particularly when the discussion is being held among three prominent, white media figures. It's distracting to take an obviously malicious instance of racism and compare it to a rap lyric.
      </p>
      <p>
       It's a point that Brzezinski herself
       <a href="http://www.vanityfair.com/news/2015/03/morning-joe-sae-frat-racism-rap-music" target="_blank">
        <u>
         made
        </u>
       </a>
       on MSNBC, later in the afternoon. "There is no moral equivalency between any lyrics and what happened on that bus, she said. Lyrics have nothing to do with the actions that happened on the bus.
      </p>
      <p>
       And if theres no moral equivalency, then prosecuting black culture and looking for racists in hip-hop is no way to reckon with white racism.
      </p>
      <hr/>

       <em>
        <small>
         <a id="mika" name="mika">
          *
         </a>
         This post has been updated to clarify Mika Brzezinski's comments about the racial slurs made by fraternity brothers at the University of Oklahoma.
        </small>
       </em>
      </p>

         </a>
        </li>

       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         The former president speaks out on the Confederate flag, religion, Bob Dylan, and Iran.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Im a Southerner, Jimmy Carter announced on one of his TV ads during his 1976 campaign for president, introducing himself to the nation. He was filmed walking through his peanut farm in Plains, Georgia. Today, the improbability of his election to the presidency has faded from public memory, but it ranks as one of the most remarkable success stories in national politics.
         </p>
         <p>
          Carter was governor of Georgia, but less than a year before the election, he was virtually unknown to most of the country. He was a relentless campaigner, and left other Democratic primary contenders in his wake. In the post-Watergate era, Carters promise that he would always tell the truth helped him defeat Gerald Ford, and swept the 52-year-old into the Oval Office.

        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Just how strong is Scott Walker?
         </p>
         <p>
          Theres no question for most analysts that the Wisconsin governor is in the top tier of Republican contenders, probably along with Jeb Bush and Marco Rubio. But Walker, who kicks off his campaign with a rally Monday evening in crucial Waukesha County, is probably the biggest X-factor among that elite crew.
         </p>
         <p>
          In some ways, he seems like the perfect candidate for the 2016 field. Hes made his name battling unions and enacting conservative policies in Wisconsin, making him a darling of movement conservatives and Tea Partiers alike. His promise is that he can take that brand of effective nuts-and-bolts leadership and labor-busting national. (Theres a reason the
          <a href="https://twitter.com/rebleber/status/620611041832083456">
           AFL-CIO greeted his entry with disgust
          </a>
          .) As a scrappy politician who clawed his way up through the ranks despite not having a college degree, he doesnt come across as a moderate dynasty candidate like Jeb Bush. And as an executive who won three elections in four years, thanks to an unsuccessful recall attempt, he can sidestep the charges of youthful inexperience that have dogged Rubio. He is also
          <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/04/20/us-usa-politics-kochs-idUSKBN0NB2GS20150420">

        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">

       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         <span>
          After traveling more than 3 billion miles over the past nine and a half years, NASAs New Horizons spacecraft is within hours of its rendezvous with Pluto.
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          After traveling nearly 3 billion miles over the past nine and a half years, NASAs New Horizons spacecraft is within hours of its rendezvous with Pluto. Back in 2006, when the space probe was launched, Pluto was classified as the ninth planet in the solar system, and was known to have three moons. During the long journey to this distant icy world, Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet, one of many smaller bodies orbiting the sun, and another two moons were discovered. In 2007, New Horizons flew past Jupiter and its moons on the way to Pluto, capturing many spectacular images. On the morning of July 14, 2015 (Earth time, Western Hemisphere), New Horizons will speed past the Pluto system at
          <span>
           about 9
          </span>
          <span>
           miles
          </span>
          <span>
           (14 kilometers) per second, making as many observations as possible. In the hours and days following, it will be sending the data to Earth, on its way to the
          </span>
          Kuiper belt, with plans to target another smaller body sometime around 2018. (More Pluto images will be added here as they become available.)
         </p>
