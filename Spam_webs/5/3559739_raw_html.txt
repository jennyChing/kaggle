         <h1 class="entry-title">
          No bank account in Vietnam, no new car
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="publication-logo-wrapper article-subtitle">
         Local Source:
         <span class="publication-logo" style="color:#f5423e;">
          <a href="http://vnexpress.net/tin-tuc/thoi-su/giao-thong/ha-noi-de-xuat-mua-oto-phai-mo-tai-khoan-ngan-hang-3080062.html" style="color:#f5423e;" target="_blank">
           VNExpress
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="post-date">
          September 21, 2014
          <span>
          </span>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!--&lt;br class="clear"/&gt;-->
        <div class="content-wrapper">
         <div class="wrapper-1">
          <div class="post-thumbnail">
           <img alt="No bank account in Vietnam, no new car" class="attachment-post-thumbnail wp-post-image" height="321" src="http://www.pangeatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/vietnam12.jpg" width="500"/>
          </div>
          <p>
           In an attempt to streamline traffic law enforcement in Vietnam, a law has been proposed requiring car owners to register valid bank accounts with the police.
          </p>
          <p>
           Traffic cameras will soon be installed at intersections across the country, and police officers hope to be able to fine offenders whose violations are caught on camera by directly deducting the money from their bank accounts.
          </p>
          <p>
           If the law is passed, anyone who owns or wishes to own a car will have to register his or her bank account at a police station and put a certain amount of money in the account so that fines can be automatically deducted.
          </p>
          <p>
           If the new measure proves effective, it will be applied to motorbike owners as well.
          </p>
          <p>
           Almost immediately after being introduced, the proposed law has sparked controversy across the country, with the majority of Vietnamese highly doubting its effectiveness.
          </p>
          <p>
           They argue that corruption among traffic police is common, so being able to directly withdraw the money for fines is useless as most offenders and police officers resolve violations in secret and no money goes through the official system.
          </p>
