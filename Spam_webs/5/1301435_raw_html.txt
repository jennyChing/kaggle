                <p>
                 <strong>
                  <span style="font-size: 1.6rem; line-height: 1.5;">
                   An insurance blog by Jason Galanis
                  </span>
                 </strong>
                </p>
                <p>
                 <span style="font-size: 1.6rem; line-height: 1.5;">
                  I (
                  <strong>
                   Jason Galanis
                  </strong>
                  ) had a slight case of De Ja Vu this week in the finance world. The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision announced that banks, which had been using Credit Default Swaps (CDSs) to protect their balance sheet, were no longer allowed to capitalize the expense. This is a particularly apropos discussion for my writing considering I just discussed whether CDSs were really insurance or a derivative in my column last week.
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p>
                 But first things first, the case of De Ja Vu has to do with America Online and the capitalization that went on for years with their CD mailers. Back in the olden days of the Internet (oh say 15 years ago) customers needed a CD to download the software, which then allowed for dial up modems to connect them to the world wide web. America Online capitalized these purchases; meaning they kept them as an asset on the balance sheet and wrote them off over time.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The accounting theory on this is all well and good; the CDs will benefit the company into the future so why not allow them to try and date the goodwill (sorry wrong term when we are talking the art of accounting) into the future. Eventually the regulators balked, a small scandal ensued and AOL had to rewrite their accounting numbers. Not unlike what is happening to GroupOn now; the difference is the marketplace climate, AOL had the benefit of the exuberance of the Internet bubble, GroupOn is dealing with a skeptical public in the wake of the credit crisis.
                </p>
                <p>
                 So bankers too now must deal with the same skepticism as Basel reviews their use of CDSs. I have made it clear that I think CDSs are a fascinatingly ingenious invention and I do not find them pernicious in any sense. As I made clear when talking about the lead in to the credit crisis, however, the problem is human nature not the instruments themselves. Should CDSs be allowed to be capitalized is a tough question. I have been the lonely entrepreneur trying to convince one of the Big Six (yes six existed back then before consolidation and regulator opprobrium) to see the accounting my way.
                </p>
                <p>
                 And I can tell you there is no more desperate feeling in the world than when they tell you gain on sale does not work and force accrual down your throat. You can literally destroy a business before it gets started that way. I would give that the eventual demise of AOL had nothing to do with the CD capitalization and rather the company not adapting to changing market conditions. Even their merger with Time Warner was an old school way of trying to take advantage of an inflated stock price rather than taking the internal steps of self reflection needed to keep a company dynamic.
                </p>
                <p>
                 But I digress, the banking situation is certainly one worth concern. Capital requirements are no joking matter in the days following the credit crisis where banks kept them at levels judged safe by holding loans that were not worth the paper they were written on (metaphorically of course). The fact of the matter is whether insurance or derivative CDSs have counter parties. And one of the biggest deals was Citigroup buying from Blackstone. The bigger the entities get the more correlation there is and if all their assets fall in tandem you could have the same problem that bankrupted AIG. The safest thing to do is to allow the purchases and curb the capitalization of them; although I do feel for the bankers wincing right now.
                </p>
