     <p>
      <b>
       USCGC
       <i>
        Taney
       </i>
       (WPG/WAGC/WHEC-37)
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
           t
          </span>
          <span title="/e/ long 'a' in 'base'">
           e
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ long 'e' in 'bead'">
           i
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      <span class="Unicode" title="English pronunciation respelling">
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i>
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             TAY
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -nee
        </i>
       </a>
      </span>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">
       United States Coast Guard
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USCG_high_endurance_cutter" title="USCG high endurance cutter">
       High Endurance Cutter
      </a>
      , notable as the last ship floating that fought in the
      <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">
       attack on Pearl Harbor
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      was actually moored in nearby Honolulu Harbor not Pearl Harbor itself. She was named for
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_B._Taney" title="Roger B. Taney">
       Roger B. Taney
      </a>
      (17771864), who was at various times:
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">
       US Attorney General
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_the_Treasury" title="Secretary of the Treasury">
       Secretary of the Treasury
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">
       Chief Justice of the Supreme Court
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      She is also one of two
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_class_cutter" title="Treasury class cutter">
       Treasury-class
      </a>
      (out of seven total) Coast Guard Cutters still afloat. Serving her country for 50 years, the
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      saw action in both theaters of combat in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , serving as command ship at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Okinawa" title="Battle of Okinawa">
       Battle of Okinawa
      </a>
      , and as part of fleet escort in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_(ocean)" title="Atlantic (ocean)">
       Atlantic
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">
       Mediterranean
      </a>
      . She also served in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Market_Time" title="Operation Market Time">
       Operation Market Time
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      also patrolled the seas working in drug interdiction and fisheries protection and participated in the search for
      <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">
       Amelia Earhart
      </a>
      .

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1936.E2.80.9341">
       193641
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=USCGC_Taney_(WHEC-37)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 193641">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Roger B. Taney
      </i>
      , Coast Guard Builders No. 68, was laid on 1 May 1935 at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Navy_Yard" title="Philadelphia Navy Yard">
       Philadelphia Navy Yard
      </a>
      . She was launched on 3 June 1936 and was sponsored by Miss Corinne F. Taney. She was commissioned at Philadelphia on 24 October 1936 under the command of CDR W. K. Thompson, USCG. The
      <i>
       Roger B. Taney
      </i>
      departed
      <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">
       Philadelphia
      </a>
      on 19 December, transited the
      <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">
       Panama Canal
      </a>
      from 27 to 29 December, and arrived at her home port,
      <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">
       Honolulu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Hawaii" title="Territory of Hawaii">
       Territory of Hawaii
      </a>
      , on 18 January 1937. She conducted local operations out of Honolulu through the summer of 1937. On 16 June 1937, she transferred a number of her crew for temporary duty to
      <a href="/wiki/USCGC_Itasca_(1929)" title="USCGC Itasca (1929)">
       USCGC
       <i>
        Itasca
       </i>
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       Itasca
      </i>
      was preparing to lend navigational support to
      <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">
       Amelia Earhart
      </a>
      's flight around the world. In May or June 1937
      <i>
       Roger B. Taney'
      </i>
      s name was shortened to simply
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      had arrived in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">
       Pacific
      </a>
      at a time when the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan-American_Airways" title="Pan-American Airways">
       Pan-American Airways
      </a>
      in particular, was expanding its commercial air travel capabilities. The "Clipper" flights across the Pacific to the
      <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">
       Far East
      </a>
      made islands like
      <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
       Hawaii
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">
       Midway
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">
       Guam
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Wake_Island" title="Wake Island">
       Wake Island
      </a>
      important way-stations. Other islands and islets assumed greater importance when a route across the South Pacific was mapped out to
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">
       Samoa
      </a>
      . The military benefits which accrued to the United States by its expansion onto some of the more strategic bits of land in the broad Pacific were not lost upon President Franklin D. Roosevelt, who undertook, in the late 1930s, to annex territory in the Pacific.
     </p>
     <p>
      Two such places were
      <a href="/wiki/Kanton_Island" title="Kanton Island">
       Kanton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Enderbury_Island" title="Enderbury Island">
       Enderbury Islands
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      played a role in their colonization by the United States. In early March 1938, the Coast Guard cutter loaded supplies and embarked colonists who would establish the claim of the United States upon the two islands that seemedat least to the uninitiatedto be mere hunks of coral, rock, and scrub in the Central Pacific. She disembarked four Hawaiians at Enderbury Island on 6 March 1938 and landed a second contingentof seven colonistsat Canton Island on the next day. The men, assisted by the Coast Guardsmen, erected buildings and laid the foundations for future signal towers.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Coast Guard's task over the ensuing years leading up to the outbreak of war in the Pacific was to supply these isolated way-stations along the transpacific air routes and to relieve the colonists at stated intervals.
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      performed these supply missions into 1940. Meanwhile, tension continued to rise in the Far East as
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      cast covetous glances at the American,
      <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
       British
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_colonial_empire" title="Dutch colonial empire">
       Dutch
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       French
      </a>
      colonial possessions and marched deeper into embattled
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As the Navy and Coast Guard began gradually increasing and augmenting the armament on its vessels to prepare them for the inexorably advancing war,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      underwent her first major rearmament at the
      <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor" title="Pearl Harbor">
       Pearl Harbor Navy Yard
      </a>
      in December 1940. She received her last major pre-war refit at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mare_Island_Navy_Yard" title="Mare Island Navy Yard">
       Mare Island Navy Yard
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vallejo,_California" title="Vallejo, California">
       Vallejo, California
      </a>
      , in the spring of the following year, 1941. On 25 July 1941, the Coast Guard cutter was transferred to the Navy and reported for duty with the local defense forces of the 14th Naval District, maintaining her base at
      <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">
       Honolulu
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Outside another "line island cruise" in the late summer,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      operated locally out of Honolulu into the critical fall of 1941. She conducted regular harbor entrance and channel patrols, alternating often with one of the four old destroyers of Destroyer Division 80:
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Allen_(DD-66)" title="USS Allen (DD-66)">
       USS
       <i>
        Allen
       </i>
      </a>
      (DD-66),
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Schley_(DD-103)" title="USS Schley (DD-103)">
       <i>
        Schley
       </i>
      </a>
      (DD-103),
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Chew_(DD-106)" title="USS Chew (DD-106)">
       <i>
        Chew
       </i>
      </a>
      (DD-106), and
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Ward_(DD-139)" title="USS Ward (DD-139)">
       <i>
        Ward
       </i>
      </a>
      (DD-139).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">
       World War II
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=USCGC_Taney_(WHEC-37)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The message: "Air Raid, Pearl Harbor. This is no drill" came at 07:55 on 7 December, as Japanese planes swept overhead in an attempt to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor_attack" title="Pearl Harbor attack">
       cripple the Pacific Fleet
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      , moored alongside Pier 6, Honolulu harbor, manned her
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft" title="Anti-aircraft">
       anti-aircraft
      </a>
      guns swiftly when word of the surprise attack reached her simultaneously. As no Japanese attacks were directed at Honolulu harbor, the Coast Guard cutter was only given the opportunity to fire at stray aircraft which happened to venture into her vicinity. She was firing upon unidentified aircraft as late as noon, indicating that the eager Coast Guardsmen were probably shooting at American planesnot Japanese.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANFS_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DANFS-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Coast Guardsmen from the
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      were ordered to take up defensive positions around
      <a href="/wiki/Aloha_Tower" title="Aloha Tower">
       Aloha Tower
      </a>
      and protect it from being occupied.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      patrolled the waters off Honolulu for the remainder of 1941 and into 1942, conducting many
      <a href="/wiki/Depth_charge" title="Depth charge">
       depth charge
      </a>
      attacks on suspected submarines in the wake of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor_attack" title="Pearl Harbor attack">
       Pearl Harbor attack
      </a>
      . During this time, the ship received the classification
      <b>
       WPG-37
      </b>
      . On 22 January 1942, the cutter departed Honolulu in company with USAT
      <i>
       Barbara Olson
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and arrived at
      <a href="/wiki/Kanton_Island" title="Kanton Island">
       Kanton Island
      </a>
      on 28 January. After sending a working party ashore to unload supplies,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      screened
      <i>
       Barbara Olson
      </i>
      offshore until 7 February, when both ships got underway to evacuate the American colony on
      <a href="/wiki/Enderbury_Island" title="Enderbury Island">
       Enderbury Island
      </a>
      . Embarking the four colonists at 10:15 that day,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      shelled the island and destroyed its buildings to prevent them from being used by Japanese forces.
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      subsequently escorted her merchantman consort to
      <a href="/wiki/Jarvis_Island" title="Jarvis Island">
       Jarvis Island
      </a>
      , where she evacuated the four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interior_Department" title="Interior Department">
       Interior Department
      </a>
      colonists and burned all structures to the ground before departing. Reaching
      <a href="/wiki/Palmyra_Atoll" title="Palmyra Atoll">
       Palmyra Atoll
      </a>
      on 12 February, the ships remained there until the 15 February, before
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      headed back for the Hawaiian Islands, arriving at Honolulu on 5 March. She made another voyage to Palmyra Island later that spring and when heading back to Hawaii, she received orders to search for survivors in the waters around
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midway_Island" title="Midway Island">
       Midway Island
      </a>
      after the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Midway" title="Battle of Midway">
       Battle of Midway
      </a>
      , including a stop at the island itself.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      operated locally out of Honolulu into 1943 before sailing for
      <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">
       Boston
      </a>
      late that winter. Prior to heading for the east coast, the ship received a re-gunning at
      <a href="/wiki/Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard" title="Mare Island Naval Shipyard">
       Mare Island
      </a>
      , being fitted with four single-mount, 5-inch guns, making her the only ship in her class with this modification. After making port at Boston on 14 March 1944,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      soon shifted south to
      <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">
       Hampton Roads
      </a>
      , where she arrived on 31 March. Early in April, she departed
      <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">
       Norfolk
      </a>
      as a unit of Task Force 66 (TF 66) as convoy guide for convoy UGS-38. The passage across the Atlantic proved uneventful, as the convoy made landfall off the
      <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">
       Azores
      </a>
      on 13 April.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some 35 minutes after sunset on 20 April, however, the convoy was spotted and tracked by the Germans, who launched a three-pronged attack with
      <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_88" title="Junkers Ju 88">
       Junkers Ju 88
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Heinkel_He_111" title="Heinkel He 111">
       Heinkel He 111
      </a>
      medium bombers. Each flew very low, using the shoreline as a background, thus confusing the search radar of the Allied ships. The first wave struck from dead ahead, torpedoing
      <a href="/wiki/SS_Paul_Hamilton" title="SS Paul Hamilton">
       SS Paul Hamilton
      </a>
      and SS
      <i>
       Samite
      </i>
      . The former, which had been carrying both a load of ammunition and hundreds of
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps" title="United States Army Air Corps">
       Army Air Corps
      </a>
      personnel, blew up in a shattering explosionkilling all 504 men on board.
     </p>
     <p>
      The second wave of German torpedo planes hit the SS
      <i>
       Stephen F. Austin
      </i>
      and SS
      <i>
       Royal Star
      </i>
      . During this
      <a href="/wiki/Melee" title="Melee">
       melee
      </a>
      , two torpedoes churned past
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      close aboard. The third wave mortally wounded
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Lansdale_(DD-426)" title="USS Lansdale (DD-426)">
       USS
       <i>
        Lansdale
       </i>
      </a>
      (DD-426), which later sank. All of the damaged vesselssave
      <i>
       Paul Hamilton
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Lansdale
      </i>
      reached
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bizerte,_Tunisia" title="Bizerte, Tunisia">
       Bizerte, Tunisia
      </a>
      , on 21 April.
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      later departed Bizerte with homeward-bound convoy GUS-38 and arrived at
      <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">
       New York
      </a>
      on 21 May.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      participated in two more round-trip convoy escort missions, with convoys UGS/GUS-45 and UGS/GUS-52. Detached as a unit of TF 66 on 9 October 1944, she sailed for the
      <a href="/wiki/Boston_Navy_Yard" title="Boston Navy Yard">
       Boston Navy Yard
      </a>
      soon thereafter for extensive yard work to convert her to an amphibious command ship. During this metamorphosis,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      classified as
      <b>
       WAGC-37
      </b>
      was fitted with accommodations for an embarked
      <a href="/wiki/Flag_officer" title="Flag officer">
       flag officer
      </a>
      and his staff, as well as with increased communications and
      <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
       radar
      </a>
      facilities. Her main battery also underwent change: she now sported two open-mount 5-inch guns, as well as 40- and 20-millimeter antiaircraft guns. With the work completed in early January 1945,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      departed Boston on 19 January, bound for Norfolk, Virginia.
     </p>
     <p>
      She conducted shakedown and training in her new configuration before departing the east coast and sailing, via the Panama Canal and
      <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">
       San Diego
      </a>
      , to Hawaii. Arriving at Pearl Harbor on 22 February 1945, she soon embarked Rear Admiral
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Calvin_H._Cobb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Calvin H. Cobb (page does not exist)">
       Calvin H. Cobb
      </a>
      and later underwent various minor repairs. New communications equipment was also installed before the ship departed the Hawaiian Islands for the
      <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">
       Marshalls
      </a>
      on 10 March.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      proceeded independently via
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eniwetok" title="Eniwetok">
       Eniwetok
      </a>
      and arrived at
      <a href="/wiki/Ulithi" title="Ulithi">
       Ulithi
      </a>
      on 23 March, remaining there until 7 April. Joining Task Group 51.8 (TG 51.8), the amphibious command ship proceeded to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa" title="Okinawa">
       Okinawa
      </a>
      and arrived off the
      <a href="/wiki/Hagushi" title="Hagushi">
       Hagushi
      </a>
      beaches amidst air raid alerts on 11 April. During one raid, her antiaircraft gunners scored at least three hits on a
      <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_G4M" title="Mitsubishi G4M">
       Mitsubishi G4M
      </a>
      "Betty" bomber which crossed the ship's bow 1,200 yards (1,100m) away, and later during her first day at Okinawa experienced four more "red alerts". The ship briefly shifted to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerama_Retto" title="Kerama Retto">
       Kerama Retto
      </a>
      from 13 to 15 April before returning to Hagushi on the latter date.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the end of May,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      had gone to general quarters 119 times, with the crew remaining at battle stations for up to nine hours at a stretch. During this period off Okinawa in April and May,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      downed four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicide_plane" title="Suicide plane">
       suicide planes
      </a>
      and assisted in numerous other "kills". The command ship also conducted combat information center duties, maintaining complete radar and air coverage, receiving and evaluating information on both friendly and enemy activities. On one occasion,
      <i>
       Taney'
      </i>
      s duties took her close inshore close enough to even receive fire close aboard from a Japanese shore battery.
     </p>
     <p>
      Suicide air attacks by the Japanese continued throughout June, although most were intercepted by
      <a href="/wiki/Combat_air_patrol" title="Combat air patrol">
       combat air patrol
      </a>
      (CAP) fighters and downed before they could reach their targets. Such raids took place on 18 out of 30 days that month. On 25 June, at 01:20, a float seaplane passed near
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      , provoking return fire from the command ship and batteries ashore which combined to splash the intruder. During this month-long period, at least 288 enemy planes attacked the ships in
      <i>
       Taney'
      </i>
      s vicinity, and at least 96 of these were destroyed.
     </p>
     <p>
      As if the Japanese menace alone were not enough, in mid-July a
      <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">
       typhoon
      </a>
      forced the ships at Hagushi to take evasive action.
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      led a convoy eastward on 19 July and returned the next day when the storm passed. She performed the same duties again on 1 August when she led a convoy to sea on typhoon-evasion operations. The ship returned to its anchorage on 3 August.
     </p>
     <p>
      The end of the war found
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      still off Okinawa. On 16 August, she got underway to support
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Pennsylvania_(BB-38)" title="USS Pennsylvania (BB-38)">
       USS
       <i>
        Pennsylvania
       </i>
       (BB-38)
      </a>
      as three Japanese planes were detected approaching from the northeast. One crashed 30 miles (48km) to the north, and two splashed into the sea shortly thereafter. On 25 August, TG 95.5 was dissolved, and Rear Admiral Cobb, who had been embarked during the Okinawa campaign, hauled down his flag and departed.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      soon proceeded to Japan, where she took part in the occupation of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wakayama" title="Wakayama">
       Wakayama
      </a>
      , anchoring off the port city on 11 September and sending a working party ashore the next day. While anchored there,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      weathered a typhoon on 17 September. She was, in fact, one of the few ships which stayed at her berth during the storm, her ground tackle holding well in the sticky clay bottom.
     </p>
     <p>
      Departing Wakayama on 14 October,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      returned to the west coast of the United States, via Midway, and arrived at San Francisco on 29 October. Moving on for the east coast,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      transited the Panama Canal and later arrived at her ultimate destination,
      <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">
       Charleston, South Carolina
      </a>
      , on 29 November. During the ensuing period of conversion, the Coast Guard vessel was reconfigured as a patrol cutter. She now sported a main battery of a single-mount, 5-inch gun, a
      <a href="/wiki/Hedgehog_(weapon)" title="Hedgehog (weapon)">
       hedgehog
      </a>
      , a twin 40-millimeter mount, and two 20-millimeter guns, in addition to
      <a href="/wiki/Depth_charge" title="Depth charge">
       depth charge
      </a>
      tracks and projectors and was reclassified once again as
      <b>
       WPG-37
      </b>
      .
     </p>

Their primary task was to report meteorological information, which was used in weather forecasts for the burgeoning trans-Pacific commercial air traffic as well as for surface vessels. The ocean station vessels also provided communications and navigation assistance and were always standing by for search and rescue emergencies. She also conducted dedicated law enforcement and search and rescue patrols, or stood on search and rescue standby, when she was not on ocean station duty.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1965.E2.80.9366">
       196566
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>

     </h2>
     <p>
      On 1 May 1965 the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasury_class_cutter" title="Treasury class cutter">
       Treasury-class vessels
      </a>
      were re-designated as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USCG_high_endurance_cutter" title="USCG high endurance cutter">
       High Endurance Cutters
      </a>
      or WHEC. This designation indicated a multi-mission ship able to operate at sea for 3045 days without support and
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      was then re-classified as
      <b>
       WHEC-37
      </b>
      . In March 1965 she conducted an Alaskan Patrol and on 29 March she successfully fought a fire on board the disabled fishing vessel
      <i>
       Glacier Bear
      </i>
      15 miles (24km) south of Cape Fairweather and then towed her to safety. In May 1965, off northern California, she kept the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet
      </a>
      refrigerator ship
      <i>
       Chernjakhovsk
      </i>
      under close surveillance.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1966
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      undertook a 90-day "Double VICTOR Cruise". She departed Alameda on 26 August and arrived at Pearl Harbor on 1 September, where she refueled before steaming to Honolulu, mooring at Berth 8. She departed Honolulu on 3 September en route
      <i>
       Ocean Station Victor
      </i>
      via Midway Island, arriving at the latter on 6 September, departing the same day. On 8 September 1966
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      crossed the 180th meridian and then arrived at
      <i>
       Ocean Station Victor
      </i>
      on 11 September, relieving
      <a href="/wiki/USCGC_Chautauqua_(WHEC-41)" title="USCGC Chautauqua (WHEC-41)">
       USCGC
       <i>
        Chautauqua
       </i>
      </a>
      (WHEC-41). She served on
      <i>
       Victor
      </i>
      until relieved by
      <a href="/wiki/USCGC_Winnebago_(WHEC-40)" title="USCGC Winnebago (WHEC-40)">
       USCGC
       <i>
        Winnebago
       </i>
       (WHEC-40)
      </a>
      on 1 October, then steamed towards
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Fleet_Activities_Yokosuka" title="U.S. Fleet Activities Yokosuka">
       Yokosuka, Japan
      </a>
      . Here the crew enjoyed liberty before again heading back to the ocean station. She arrived at
      <i>
       Victor
      </i>
      on 22 October, relieving
      <i>
       Winnebago
      </i>
      . On 4 November
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1950%E2%80%9359_Pacific_typhoon_seasons#1954_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="195059 Pacific typhoon seasons">
       Typhoon Marie
      </a>
      passed close aboard
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      , with winds gusting close to 70
      <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">
       knots
      </a>
      (130km/h), but she weathered the storm without damage. On 12 November 1966
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      was relieved again by
      <i>
       Winnebago
      </i>
      and she then steamed to Midway Island to refuel before heading back to Alameda, arriving there on 20 November.
     </p>

      </i>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1972.E2.80.9386">
       197286
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=USCGC_Taney_(WHEC-37)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 197286">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In February 1972
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      was shifted back to the east coast and was homeported at
      <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">
       Norfolk
      </a>
      . From 13 to 22 October 1972 she served on
      <i>
       Ocean Station Hotel
      </i>
      . From 28 October to 17 November 1972 she served on
      <i>
       Ocean Station Delta
      </i>
      . From 26 January to 15 February and 17 April to 7 May 1973 she served on
      <i>
       Ocean Station Bravo
      </i>
      . As the ocean stations were decommissioned during the early 1970s due to advances in
      <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
       radar
      </a>
      and electronic navigation,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      was assigned exclusively to the only station still operational:
      <i>
       Ocean Station Hotel
      </i>
      off the coasts of
      <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">
       Maryland
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">
       Virginia
      </a>
      . Fitted with a special
      <a href="/wiki/Radome" title="Radome">
       storm-tracking antenna
      </a>
      housed in a distinctive bulbous dome fitted atop her pilot house,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      deployed seven times yearly, conducting 21 deployments 200 miles (320km) off the coast. This last ocean station had been established to track storms threatening the middle states on the east coast which had often struck without warning. Eventually, the use of more sophisticated storm-tracking satellites and radars rendered this station obsolete. Hence,
      <i>
       Ocean Station Hotel
      </i>
      was closed down in 1977 and the
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      gained the distinction of being the last Coast Guard cutter to serve on an ocean station.
     </p>
     <p>
      The mid-1970s were a period of transition for the Coast Guard with the passage of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnuson-Stevens_Fisheries_Conservation_and_Management_Act_of_1976" title="Magnuson-Stevens Fisheries Conservation and Management Act of 1976">
       Fisheries Conservation and Management Act
      </a>
      and the nation's shift towards increased interdiction of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narcotics" title="Narcotics">
       narcotics
      </a>
      smugglers. These operations called for off-shore patrols of up to three weeks. From September 1976 through her decommissioning she was stationed at
      <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">
       Portsmouth, Virginia
      </a>
      and began law enforcement and
      <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">
       search and rescue
      </a>
      patrols.
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 1976 she assisted the sailboat Capella 200 miles (320km) off New York. In December 1979
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      helped seize the F/V
      <i>
       Eneida
      </i>
      for narcotics violations. On 15 January 1980 she seized the M/V
      <i>
       Ameila Isle
      </i>
      425 miles (684km) east of
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pierce,_Florida" title="Fort Pierce, Florida">
       Fort Pierce, Florida
      </a>
      , carrying 4 tons of contraband. In December 1980 she seized the British-flagged M/V
      <i>
       Party Doll
      </i>
      which was carrying 10 tons of contraband. Despite being the long arm of the law at sea she continued in her traditional Coast Guard humanitarian mission of search and rescue as well. On 16 November 1982 she rescued seven from the disabled ketch
      <i>
       Klarwasser
      </i>
      off the coast of North Carolina and rescued 19 migrants off the sailboat
      <i>
       Apre Dien Ni
      </i>
      . In May 1985 she assisted the disabled F/V
      <i>
       Northwind
      </i>
      300 miles (480km) off New York. She also continued nabbing drug smugglers. On 30 September 1984 she seized the P/C
      <i>
       Thriller
      </i>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatan_Channel" title="Yucatan Channel">
       Yucatan Channel
      </a>
      , carrying 1,000 pounds of
      <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">
       marijuana
      </a>
      . Her final bust occurred on 4 October 1985 when she seized the M/V
      <i>
       Sea Maid I
      </i>
      which was towing a barge that carried 160 tons of marijuana 300 miles (480km) off Virginia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firsts_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-firsts-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fate">
       Fate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=USCGC_Taney_(WHEC-37)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      She was formally decommissioned on 7 December 1986 and turned over to the city of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">
       Baltimore, Maryland
      </a>
      , for use as a
      <a href="/wiki/Museum_ship" title="Museum ship">
       museum ship
      </a>
      . Over her distinguished career,
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      received three battle stars for
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      service and numerous theater ribbons for service in World War II,
      <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">
       Korea
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1988, the USCGC
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      (WHEC-37), Structure - #88001826, was added to the National Register of Historic Places.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nris-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She was designated a
      <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">
       National Historic Landmark
      </a>
      on the same day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhl_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nhl-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      is located in the historic Baltimore
      <a href="/wiki/Inner_Harbor" title="Inner Harbor">
       Inner Harbor
      </a>
      as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Historic_Ships_in_Baltimore" title="Historic Ships in Baltimore">
       Historic Ships in Baltimore
      </a>
      collection.
      <i>
       Taney
      </i>
      is included in the
      <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_National_Heritage_Area" title="Baltimore National Heritage Area">
       Baltimore National Heritage Area
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-balto1_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-balto1-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Liberty_ship" title="Liberty ship">
       Liberty ship
      </a>
      <i>
       Roger B Taney
      </i>
      was also named for the Chief Justice. She was laid down at the
      <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem_Shipbuilding_Corporation" title="Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corporation">
       Bethlehem-Fairfield Shipyard Inc.
      </a>
      , at Baltimore Maryland on 21 June 1941 and launched 6 December 1941. She was commissioned as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Transport" title="U.S. Army Transport">
       U.S. Army Transport
      </a>
      on 9 February 1942. On 2 July 1943 she was torpedoed in the South Atlantic, and 3 crew members died.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
